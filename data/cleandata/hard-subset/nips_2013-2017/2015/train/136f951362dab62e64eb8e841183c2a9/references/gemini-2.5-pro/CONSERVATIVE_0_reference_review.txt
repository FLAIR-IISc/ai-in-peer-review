This paper proposes a new model for time-sensitive recommendation and user return-time prediction. The core idea is to model the sequence of a user's interactions with an item as a self-exciting Hawkes process. To generalize this to a collaborative filtering setting with sparse observations, the authors assume that the matrix of baseline intensities and the matrix of self-excitation parameters are both low-rank. This leads to a convex optimization problem involving the minimization of the negative log-likelihood subject to nuclear norm regularization and non-negativity constraints. To solve this problem, the authors propose a novel optimization algorithm that combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. The proposed model is evaluated on synthetic and three real-world datasets, where it is shown to outperform several baselines on item recommendation and return-time prediction tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled Model:** The application of Hawkes processes to model recurrent user activities is well-motivated and intuitive. It provides a natural way to capture both a user's long-term, static preference for an item (the baseline intensity) and the short-term, history-dependent effects of recent interactions (the self-exciting component). This continuous-time event modeling is a significant departure from common time-binned or static approaches.
2.  **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The paper effectively combines two well-established fields: temporal point processes and low-rank models for collaborative filtering. The resulting "Low Rank Hawkes Process" model is a novel contribution to the recommendation systems literature.
3.  **Optimization Contribution:** The development of a specialized optimization algorithm to handle the challenging combination of a smooth loss function, non-negativity constraints, and nuclear norm regularization is a solid technical contribution. The theoretical convergence analysis provides formal guarantees for the algorithm's performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Validation against Established Baselines:** While the paper includes several competitors, the choice and strength of these baselines are questionable, especially for the item recommendation task.
    *   The SVD baseline for implicit feedback is based on item consumption frequency, which is a fairly dated heuristic. More modern and powerful matrix factorization techniques for implicit feedback, such as those based on Bayesian Personalized Ranking (BPR) or Weighted Regularized Matrix Factorization (WRMF/ALS), are standard in the field and would have provided a much stronger and more relevant comparison.
    *   The Tensor Factorization methods are good temporal baselines, but their primary weakness (time discretization) is precisely what the proposed model is designed to overcome. The comparison feels somewhat self-serving. It would be valuable to see a comparison against other continuous-time or sequence-aware recommendation models.
    *   The STiC model is a very recent proposal and cannot be considered an "established" method. Its inability to recommend new items is a critical limitation, making the comparison on this front one-sided.

2.  **Justification of Model Complexity:** The proposed model is considerably more complex, both conceptually and computationally, than standard recommendation algorithms. The empirical results, while consistently favorable, do not always show a dramatic improvement over simpler methods like the Poisson process baseline. For instance, on the last.fm dataset, the improvement in MAE over the Poisson model appears modest. A more thorough discussion is needed to justify whether the gains in predictive accuracy are worth the significant increase in model complexity and the introduction of a new set of hyperparameters (e.g., kernel bandwidth, regularization parameters `λ` and `β`).

3.  **Methodological Soundness of the Optimization Scheme:** The paper presents an elegant solution to a difficult optimization problem. However, the proposed approach relies on solving an alternative formulation (Eq. 5) which is equivalent to the original problem (Eq. 4) only when a penalty parameter `ρ` is chosen to be larger than a threshold `ρ*` that depends on the unknown optimal solution. In practice, `ρ` is simply treated as another hyperparameter to be tuned. This is a common and pragmatic choice, but it weakens the theoretical claim of equivalence and makes the model's performance dependent on a careful tuning of this sensitive parameter. The paper provides no analysis of the model's sensitivity to the choice of `ρ`.

4.  **Experimental Rigor:**
    *   The evaluation of the item recommendation task is based on reporting the predicted rank of a single held-out test item. This is not a standard evaluation protocol. The field has established ranking metrics like NDCG@k, MAP@k, or Recall@k, which evaluate the position of relevant items within a ranked list of top-k recommendations. Using these standard metrics would make the results more comparable to the wider literature.
    *   Details on hyperparameter selection for all models are sparse. A fair comparison relies on robust tuning for all methods involved. The lack of these details makes it difficult to assess the fairness of the experimental comparison.

**Summary of evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The model is well-defined, and the theoretical analysis of the optimization algorithm appears correct. However, the claims of superiority are not fully supported due to weaknesses in the experimental comparison against established baselines.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation and model description are clear. The optimization section is dense but understandable for an expert in the area.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of combining low-rank models with Hawkes processes for recommendation is novel and interesting. The optimization algorithm, while building on existing work, is tailored innovatively to the specific problem structure.
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem of time-sensitive recommendation. The proposed approach is elegant and could inspire future work in continuous-time recommendation models. However, its practical significance is currently limited by the lack of comparison against stronger, industry-standard baselines and the model's inherent complexity.

In conclusion, this paper introduces a novel and technically interesting model for time-sensitive recommendation. The approach is principled and the optimization work is solid. However, the paper falls short in its empirical validation. To be convincing, the authors need to demonstrate significant improvements over stronger and more established baselines for recommendation, using standard evaluation metrics. Without such evidence, the benefits of this complex new model over simpler, proven methods remain unclear.
