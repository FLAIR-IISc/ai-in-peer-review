

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors establish a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models, developing a convex formulation for predicting both the next relevant item to recommend and the next returning time of users. The key innovation is using a low-rank structure for the base intensity matrix and self-exciting matrix to enable efficient learning of temporal dynamics across user-item pairs. The authors develop a new optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving optimal convergence rates. The method is evaluated on both synthetic data and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II medical dataset), demonstrating superior performance compared to baselines like Poisson processes, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for their convex formulation (Theorem 1) and establish a convergence rate analysis (Theorem 2) showing the algorithm achieves O(1/k) convergence. The experimental design is comprehensive, with both synthetic and real-world evaluations across multiple datasets. The authors appropriately validate their model assumption using the time-change theorem (Figure 3), showing their Hawkes model better explains observed data than survival analysis approaches.

The experimental results are convincing, with consistent improvements over baselines across different tasks and datasets. The synthetic data experiments demonstrate the model's ability to recover low-rank parameters with reasonable data requirements, and the real-world results confirm the method's practical utility. The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for careful tuning of hyperparameters).

One minor weakness is that the comparison with recent deep learning-based methods for temporal recommendation is limited. While the authors cite relevant work, they don't compare against state-of-the-art deep learning approaches like temporal graph networks or recurrent models that have been increasingly used in recommendation systems. This would strengthen the evaluation of their method's relative performance.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and its importance, the background section provides sufficient context on temporal point processes, and the model presentation is logically structured. The mathematical formulations are clear and consistently notated throughout the paper. The figures (Figures 1-3) are well-designed and effectively illustrate key concepts.

The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-3) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The optimization section, while technically dense, is explained with appropriate care. The paper strikes an excellent balance between mathematical rigor and accessibility for the NIPS audience.

One minor suggestion: the paper could briefly explain why the exponential triggering kernel was chosen over other options (e.g., Weibull or Log-logistic) in the context of computational efficiency versus modeling flexibility.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to the recommendation literature by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix models in a novel way. The specific contribution of using nuclear norm regularization for Hawkes processes with a convex optimization framework is original. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior work, particularly addressing the limitation of existing methods like STiC that cannot generalize to new items.

The connection between temporal point processes and low-rank models for recommendation is not common in the literature, making this a meaningful extension of existing techniques. The paper properly cites related work (Kapoor et al. [12, 11]), and the comparison clearly highlights the unique aspects of their approach. While the combination of Hawkes processes with matrix factorization is not entirely unprecedented (e.g., Du et al. [7, 8, 10]), the specific convex formulation with low-rank constraints and the efficient optimization algorithm represent novel contributions.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high for both the recommendation community and practitioners. The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is increasingly important for real-world applications, from personalized services to healthcare (as noted in the paper with the MIMIC II example). The paper successfully addresses a gap in the literature where most recommendation techniques optimize for one-time preferences rather than time-sensitive dynamics.

The practical implications are clear: the ability to predict returning times can optimize ad bidding strategies, improve healthcare interventions, and enhance user engagement in mobile services. The scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs) further increases the practical relevance. The optimization algorithm itself may have broader applications beyond recommendation systems, as noted in the conclusion.

The results demonstrate a demonstrable advance over previous methods, with consistent improvements across multiple evaluation metrics and datasets. The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence for the superiority of their approach.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of recommendation systems. The technical quality is excellent, the presentation is clear, the novelty is well-justified, and the significance is high for both researchers and practitioners.

Strengths:
- Novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation
- Solid theoretical foundation with convergence rate analysis
- Comprehensive experimental validation on multiple datasets
- Practical relevance with clear applications in advertising, healthcare, and personalized services
- Efficient optimization algorithm that scales to large datasets

Weaknesses:
- Limited comparison with recent deep learning-based temporal recommendation methods
- Real-world datasets used are relatively modest in scale (e.g., 1,000 users on last.fm)
- Could provide more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NeurIPS. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field with strong technical foundation, thorough evaluation, and practical relevance. The minor weaknesses noted above are outweighed by the paper's substantial strengths and the novelty of the approach.

The paper addresses an important gap in the literature, provides a theoretically sound solution, and demonstrates practical utility across multiple domains. The writing is clear, the experiments are well-designed, and the contributions are well-justified against related work. This work would be valuable to the NeurIPS community and could inspire further research in time-sensitive recommendation and temporal point processes.
