**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, aiming to predict not only *what* a user might like, but also *when* they might want it. The authors frame this as two tasks: (1) recommending the right item at the right moment and (2) predicting a user's return time to a specific item. The core idea is to model the sequence of a user's interactions with an item as a self-exciting Hawkes process. This elegantly captures both a static, long-term preference (the base intensity) and dynamic, short-term effects where recent activities increase the probability of future activity.

To generalize this model to all user-item pairs, including unobserved ones, the paper makes a crucial connection to collaborative filtering. It assumes that the matrices of Hawkes process parameters (the base intensities `Λ0` and self-excitation coefficients `A`) are low-rank. This allows the model to transfer learned behavioral patterns across users and items. The main technical contribution is a novel and efficient optimization algorithm to learn the model parameters. The resulting optimization problem involves minimizing a negative log-likelihood subject to non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization. To solve this, the authors propose a hybrid algorithm blending a proximal gradient method (for the smooth loss and non-negativity) and a conditional gradient method (for the nuclear norm penalty), and provide a convergence analysis showing an optimal O(1/t) rate. The approach is validated on synthetic and three real-world datasets, demonstrating superior performance over several baselines.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical development is solid. The formulation of the problem as a low-rank Hawkes process is well-motivated and provides a principled way to combine temporal dynamics with collaborative filtering. The derivation of the convex objective function (Eq. 4) is clear. The proposed optimization algorithm is a significant contribution. Recognizing that the combination of non-negativity and nuclear norm constraints makes standard solvers inefficient, the authors devise a clever alternative formulation (Eq. 5) and a tailored algorithm. The inclusion of a convergence proof (Theorem 2) that establishes an optimal rate for this class of problems substantially strengthens the paper's contribution. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using both synthetic data (to verify the algorithm's convergence and parameter recovery) and three diverse real-world datasets (music, e-commerce, healthcare) to demonstrate broad applicability. The evaluation tasks and metrics are well-chosen and directly test the paper's claims. The model validation using the time-change theorem (Figure 3) is a particularly nice touch, providing strong evidence for the suitability of the Hawkes process assumption over simpler models.

*   **Weaknesses:** One minor theoretical weakness lies in the connection between the original (4) and alternative (5) formulations. Theorem 1 establishes their equivalence under the condition `ρ > ρ*`, but `ρ*` is a problem-dependent threshold that is difficult to compute. In practice, the authors treat `ρ` as a hyperparameter to be tuned. While this is a reasonable practical approach, it slightly weakens the claim of equivalence, making the alternative formulation more of a well-performing approximation. The authors should be more explicit about this limitation.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

*   **Strengths:** The introduction provides excellent motivation for the problem. The background on temporal point processes is concise and provides the necessary foundation for readers not expert in the area. The model is built up logically, from the single-process case to the full low-rank matrix formulation. The algorithm is described clearly, and the figures are informative, particularly the convergence plots and the experimental results. The structure of the paper is logical and guides the reader effectively through the problem, model, algorithm, and results.

*   **Suggestions for Improvement:** Section 4.3, while clear to an optimization expert, could benefit from a bit more intuition. A sentence or two explaining *why* the proximal gradient is well-suited for the `X1` block (smooth loss + simple non-negativity projection) and the conditional gradient is well-suited for the `X2` block (handling the nuclear norm via an efficient linear minimization oracle) would make the algorithmic choice more accessible.

**Originality**

The paper demonstrates a high degree of originality.

*   **Strengths:** While its components (Hawkes processes, low-rank models, specific optimization techniques) are known, their synthesis into a unified framework for time-sensitive recommendation is novel and insightful. Previous work on temporal recommendations often relies on discretized time (tensor factorization) or models that cannot generalize to new items (like the cited work by Kapoor et al.). This paper’s approach of embedding the continuous-time point process parameters in a low-rank structure is a significant and original conceptual leap. Furthermore, the proposed optimization algorithm appears to be a novel contribution for solving problems with this specific structure (smooth loss + non-negativity + nuclear norm), and as the authors note, may be of independent interest. The paper does an excellent job of positioning its contributions against related work.

**Significance**

The work is highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact.

*   **Strengths:** The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is of immense practical importance in numerous domains, from e-commerce and media streaming to healthcare. This paper offers a solution that is not only principled and powerful but also scalable, as demonstrated by the experiments. The demonstrated improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art tensor factorization methods, show a clear advance in the state of the art. The framework is extensible to include other forms of side information (e.g., user profiles, item content, location), which further increases its potential for practical adoption. Researchers in recommendation systems, computational social science, and applied machine learning are likely to build upon these ideas. Practitioners could directly use this model to improve user engagement and optimize marketing strategies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Formulation:** The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for collaborative recommendation is a highly original and powerful idea.
2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The paper introduces a new, efficient optimization algorithm with a formal convergence guarantee, which is a significant contribution in its own right.
3.  **Excellent Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms a comprehensive set of strong baselines on three diverse, real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and generalizability.
4.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a crucial and practical problem, advancing the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Minor Theoretical Gap:** The practical necessity of tuning the penalty parameter `ρ` slightly undermines the formal equivalence proof between the two optimization formulations, although this is a minor point given the strong empirical results.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. It presents a novel and elegant solution to the important problem of time-sensitive recommendation. The work is technically deep, combining a principled probabilistic model with a sophisticated and novel optimization algorithm. The claims are thoroughly validated through extensive experiments. The paper is well-written, impactful, and represents a clear advance for the field.
