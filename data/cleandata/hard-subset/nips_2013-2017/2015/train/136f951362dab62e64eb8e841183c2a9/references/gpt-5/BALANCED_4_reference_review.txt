Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses two linked problems: (1) time-sensitive recommendation (ranking items for a user at a given time), and (2) predicting users’ return time to items/services. It models each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with an intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑γ(t, tj), where Λ0 encodes long-term preference and A scales self-excitation from past events via a kernel γ (exponential in experiments). To transfer knowledge to unobserved pairs, Λ0 and A are estimated jointly under low-rank (nuclear norm) regularization with nonnegativity constraints, leading to a convex formulation. Because nonnegativity and nuclear norms are coupled, the authors introduce auxiliary variables and a quadratic penalty, proving equivalence to the original problem beyond a threshold ρ*. They propose an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) and conditional gradient/LMO (for nuclear norms), with an accelerated scheme and an O(1/t) convergence rate. Experiments on large synthetic and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II) show improved ranking and return-time MAE versus Poisson, SVD, survival analysis (STiC), and Poisson-regression tensor factorization baselines. QQ plots via time-rescaling are used to compare goodness-of-fit.

This work connects several lines: temporal point processes (Hawkes) in social/temporal modeling (Hawkes 1971; Du et al.; Zhou, Zha & Song), return-time prediction with survival analysis/HSMM (Kapoor et al.), and low-rank/matrix/tensor methods for recommendation (Koren; Xiong et al.; Karatzoglou et al.; Chi & Kolda). On the optimization side, it builds on nuclear-norm minimization (Yu et al.) and conditional gradient for norm-regularized problems (Harchaoui & Juditsky), proposing a hybrid approach tailored to the nonnegativity + nuclear-norm structure.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing that unifies time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction.
- Convex formulation with nuclear norms and nonnegativity; clean mapping from Hawkes log-likelihood to a convex objective for linear-in-parameters intensity.
- Practical optimization: a well-motivated hybrid proximal/FW scheme that avoids repeated full SVDs and achieves provable O(1/t) rate; closed-form line-search within the LMO.
- Scalability demonstrated on up to ~100M events (synthetic) and large user–item spaces; linear scaling trends.
- Empirical gains on both tasks across diverse datasets; tests include challenging held-out sequences for cold-start generalization.
- Diagnostics via time-rescaling QQ plots support the Hawkes model over iid gap assumptions used in survival analysis.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Stability/branching ratio: For linear Hawkes with exponential kernel, stationarity typically requires α ∫γ < 1. The paper does not state or enforce constraints (e.g., A ⊙ σ < 1) to ensure stability and well-defined likelihoods, nor analyze the learned parameters with respect to stability. Please discuss and, if necessary, project onto a stability-feasible set or regularize to favor subcritical regimes.
- Identifiability: Both Λ0 and A can raise event rates; without additional structure there may be tradeoffs between baseline and excitation. Consider commenting on identifiability and adding priors/penalties (e.g., different strengths for Λ0 vs A, or sparsity on A) or showing ablations that fix/zero one component to demonstrate necessity.
- Modeling assumptions: The process is per user–item with only self-excitation; it ignores cross-excitation across items (e.g., similar artists, comorbid diseases), users, or contexts. The paper mentions extensions but does not evaluate them. A limited experiment with shared kernels or a low-rank mutual excitation term would strengthen the case.
- Kernel parameterization: The bandwidth σ appears to be global; heterogeneity across users/items is likely. Either learn σ per latent component or report sensitivity to σ; exploring nonparametric kernels (as cited) on at least one dataset would be instructive.
- Return-time prediction: Sampling via thinning is expensive; for exponential kernel one can compute the next-event time by root-finding on the compensator, which is faster and deterministic. Please discuss complexity/variance, and consider using time-rescaling inversion or deterministic quadrature.
- Baselines: Missing a Hawkes baseline without low-rank (fit only observed pairs) to isolate the contribution of transfer. Also, a low-rank Poisson (Λ0 only) baseline helps quantify the marginal gain from self-excitation. Reporting recall@k/NDCG@k in addition to mean rank would provide a more standard view of recommendation quality.

Clarity
- Notation is occasionally inconsistent (spacing and indices); define the observation window T once and use it consistently in likelihood and ψ terms. Clarify whether σ is global or learned, and whether Λ0 and A share rank or have distinct penalties.
- State per-iteration complexity and memory usage (e.g., cost of top singular vector via power method, dependence on number of events and nonzeros), and provide wall-clock comparisons to ADMM or pure FW.
- Theorem 1: ρ* is problem-dependent and hard to compute; provide a practical schedule/heuristic for ρ and discuss sensitivity.
- Algorithm pseudocode: define all variables (e.g., how top singular vectors are computed; stopping criteria; mini-batching over events if used).

Originality
- The combination of low-rank user–item Hawkes with a convex nuclear-norm formulation and a hybrid proximal/FW algorithm under nonnegativity constraints appears novel. Related low-rank Hawkes work in network inference is cited but addresses different structure. The optimization insight (separating constraints via auxiliary variables and using mixed oracles) is interesting and may be reusable.

Significance
- The method addresses timely problems in recommender systems and healthcare, with a principled temporal model and scalable solver. If strengthened with stability considerations, richer excitation structure, and broader metrics, it is likely to influence future work in time-sensitive recommendation and point-process-based personalization.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, principled modeling; convexity and provable convergence.
- Scalable algorithm with practical advantages over full-SVD proximal methods.
- Consistent empirical improvements on two tasks across datasets, including cold-start generalization.
- Diagnostic analysis supports model assumptions.

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of stability constraints/analysis and identifiability discussion.
- Limited modeling of cross-excitation and kernel heterogeneity.
- Baselines/metrics could be broader; no Hawkes vs low-rank ablations.
- Some clarity gaps and missing implementation details.

Recommendation: Weak accept
With added analysis of stability and identifiability, stronger ablations, and clearer complexity reporting, this would be a solid accept. Even as is, the convex formulation and hybrid optimization are technically sound and potentially impactful.
