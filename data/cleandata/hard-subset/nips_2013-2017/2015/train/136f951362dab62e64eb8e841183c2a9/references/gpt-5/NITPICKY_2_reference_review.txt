Summary
The paper tackles two related problems: (1) time-sensitive recommendation (ranking items for a user at a given time), and (2) predicting a user’s next return time for a given item. The core modeling choice is a Hawkes process per user–item pair with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) Σj γ(t, tu,ij), where Λ0 and A are nonnegative m×n matrices capturing baseline and self-excitation magnitudes, respectively. To generalize to unobserved pairs, Λ0 and A are assumed low-rank and regularized with nuclear norms, leading to a convex estimation problem. Because nonnegativity constraints complicate proximal nuclear-norm updates, the authors introduce auxiliary variables Z1,Z2 and a quadratic penalty tying Λ0↔Z1, A↔Z2. They propose an algorithm that combines projected (proximal) gradient for the nonnegativity-constrained blocks and conditional gradient over nuclear-norm atoms for the Z-blocks, with an accelerated scheme and an O(1/t) convergence guarantee. Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) report improvements over Poisson, STiC, SVD, and Poisson tensor baselines for both ranking and return-time prediction, and scalability to large numbers of events.

Strengths
- Bridges continuous-time point processes and low-rank collaborative models in a clean convex formulation; the per-pair Hawkes with low-rank sharing is a sensible way to transfer information to unobserved pairs.
- The optimization strategy (projected gradient for nonnegativity and Frank–Wolfe-style updates for nuclear-norm regularization) is well-motivated computationally and appears scalable.
- Empirical focus on both tasks (ranking and return-time prediction), including a time-change diagnostic for model fit, is thoughtful.
- Practical appeal: intensity-based ranking naturally yields time-aware recommendations.

Weaknesses and concerns (many are “small” but numerous)
Theoretical/algorithmic:
- Theorem 1 (exact equivalence of the quadratic-penalty formulation to the constrained problem for finite ρ>ρ*) is highly suspect. Quadratic penalties are not exact in general; exactness typically requires L1-type penalties or augmented Lagrangians. The given ρ* depends on unknown optimal solutions and the stated ratio does not establish exactness. This threatens the theoretical soundness of the surrogate problem.
- The Lipschitz constant L for ∇f(X) is assumed but not justified. For log-likelihoods of point processes linear in parameters, gradients can blow up as w⊤φ→0 unless intensities are bounded away from zero. No lower bound or backtracking scheme is specified; yet ηk=δk−1/L is used in the algorithm and the proof.
- Stability/branching condition for Hawkes is not discussed or enforced. For exponential kernels, α·∫γ < 1 (e.g., A(u,i)·σ < 1) is typically needed to avoid explosion. The estimation imposes only nonnegativity; without explicit stability constraints, the learned intensities can be unbounded and jeopardize both modeling and simulation.
- Conditional-gradient step: the LMO and line search are described for penalized nuclear norm (not a constrained norm ball). This is nonstandard; while related composite Frank–Wolfe variants exist (Harchaoui & Juditsky), the derivation here is terse. The linear term λδkα in the line search hinges on an atomic decomposition view; a more rigorous link to the cited framework is needed.
- Step-size/initialization and stopping criteria are unclear. No practical guidance for L (backtracking), number of rank-1 FW steps per iteration, or truncation is provided. Reproducibility is compromised.

Modeling/evaluation:
- The model assumes only self-excitation within each user–item process. This contradicts the paper’s own examples (e.g., disease–disease dependencies), where mutual excitation across items is important. At minimum, limitations should be stated; ideally, a multi-dimensional Hawkes with low-rank structure could be discussed empirically.
- Returning-time prediction is approximated by Monte Carlo with Ogata’s thinning, but the number of samples m, variance, and computational budget are not reported. For exponential kernels, closed-form survival exists; even without a closed-form mean, there are more efficient sampling/inversion strategies worth discussing.
- Hyperparameter selection is unspecified. The text says “tune to give the best performance”; there is no mention of a validation split versus test, creating potential leakage.
- Train/test splitting: for the 80% of observed item sequences, “split into a pair of training/testing subsequences” is vague. Is the split chronological? Random splits would leak future events into training for temporal models.
- Baselines: comparisons to more recent continuous-time recommenders or Hawkes-based recommenders with low-rank/sparse structure (e.g., low-rank Hawkes network estimation) are missing beyond citations. For tensor baselines, discretization choices and fairness (e.g., tuning number of bins) need more detail; STiC is applied only to return-time and the way it is extrapolated to entirely held-out pairs (averaging over observed items) seems ad hoc.
- Metrics: ranking is evaluated by the rank position of the true item, but no standard top-k metrics (Recall@k/NDCG) are given; ties/handling of unavailable items are unclear. No variance/error bars or multiple random splits are reported.

Writing/formatting/notation:
- Numerous typos and inconsistencies: “returningtime,” “Proceeedings,” missing years/pages, inconsistent author names, and many incomplete references. Several equations have indexing slips (e.g., Eq. 3, summation indices), and λ denotes both intensity and a regularization parameter. γ0 vs Λ0 and α vs A switch between scalar and matrix notations in different sections. Algorithm pseudo-code has unclear initialization (“Choose to initialize X01 and X02 = X01”) and undefined variables unless one infers intended meanings.

Quality
There is a promising idea and a plausible algorithm, but key theoretical claims (Theorem 1 exactness; Lipschitz-based rate with a practical step size) are inadequately justified. Experimental methodology has omissions (splits, tuning, sampling details), and several baselines are either weak or applied suboptimally.

Clarity
Readable at a high level, but the paper suffers from notation drift, typos, and missing details that impede reproduction. Proofs are deferred to an appendix not provided here; without them, key claims are unverifiable.

Originality
The combination of self-exciting point processes with low-rank sharing across user–item pairs, and the hybrid proximal/FW scheme under nonnegativity, is a nice synthesis. Related work on low-rank/sparse Hawkes estimation exists; the paper positions itself but could clarify distinctions more sharply.

Significance
If made rigorous and reproducible, the approach could be useful for time-aware recommendation and return-time prediction. However, the current theoretical gaps and experimental shortcomings limit confidence and impact.

Pros
- Timely problem and a coherent continuous-time formulation
- Convex estimation with shared low-rank structure
- Practical, scalable algorithmic ingredients; rank-1 updates are attractive
- Empirical gains on multiple datasets (subject to caveats)

Cons
- Questionable Theorem 1 (quadratic penalty exactness)
- Unjustified Lipschitz/step-size assumptions; missing stability constraints
- Incomplete experimental protocol and weak baseline handling
- Notation/formatting issues and missing implementation details

Recommendation: Reject
Rationale: Despite interesting ideas, the accumulation of theoretical and methodological concerns, combined with clarity and reference issues, makes the contribution too fragile for acceptance at this time. Addressing the exactness claim, stability constraints, step-size/backtracking, and strengthening/clarifying experiments would significantly improve the paper.
