Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses two related time-sensitive tasks: (1) recommending the most relevant item at any given time for a user, and (2) predicting the user’s next returning time to an item/service. It models each user–item interaction as a self-exciting Hawkes process with an intensity that is linear in two nonnegative parameters: a baseline λ0(u,i) and a self-excitation coefficient α(u,i) with an exponential triggering kernel. To transfer information across sparse pairs, it imposes low-rank structure on the m×n matrices Λ0 and A via nuclear-norm regularization, yielding a convex estimation problem over all observed sequences. Recommendations are obtained by ranking items by current intensity λu,i(t), and return-time prediction uses the conditional density via Ogata’s thinning.

The optimization contribution is an algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for the nonnegativity-constrained smooth part) with a conditional gradient/Frank–Wolfe step (for the nuclear-norm-regularized part) applied to an alternative quadratic-penalty formulation that introduces auxiliary variables Z1,Z2 for decoupling nonnegativity from nuclear norms. The algorithm uses top singular vector oracles and a line-search for scaling. The authors provide a convergence bound with O(1/t) dependence for the Frank–Wolfe part and O(1/t^2) for the proximal part in the special cases.

Empirically, the method is evaluated on synthetic data (∼100M events) and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II). Baselines include: Poisson process (constant intensity), STiC (semi-HMM survival model; return-time only), SVD (implicit feedback; ranking only), and Poisson tensor factorization with temporal bins. The paper reports improved ranking and return-time MAE, and uses a time-change theorem diagnostic to show better fit than survival models.

This work connects to: temporal point processes and Hawkes modeling (Hawkes; Daley & Vere-Jones), survival-based return-time prediction (Kapoor et al.), and time-aware/tensor factorization for recommendation. It is also related to low-rank learning for point processes and influence networks (Zhou et al.; Du et al.), though those focus on multivariate Hawkes networks rather than user–item low-rank structure.

Quality
- Modeling and convexity: Given the linear-in-parameters Hawkes intensity and nonnegativity, the negative log-likelihood is convex in Λ0 and A; adding nuclear norms yields a convex program. The reduction to a quadratic-penalty formulation (with Z1,Z2) is reasonable computationally.
- Optimization: The hybrid proximal/conditional gradient scheme is well-motivated. The LMO reduces to top singular vector extraction plus a 1D closed-form scaling. The stated convergence rate bound is plausible given the literature cited (Lan), although details are deferred to the appendix.
- Potential technical gaps:
  - Exactness of the quadratic-penalty reformulation (Theorem 1): For equality linking (Λ0=Z1, A=Z2), exact finite-ρ equivalence is not generally guaranteed with an L2 penalty. The presented threshold depends on unknown optimal quantities and appears difficult to verify or tune; in practice ρ is tuned. Clearer conditions or a proof sketch would help.
  - Hawkes stability: For exponential kernels, stability typically requires ασ<1 (branching ratio <1). No constraint or discussion is provided. Without such constraints, intensities may explode theoretically, even if the likelihood is finite on a bounded window.
  - Return-time estimation via sampling is standard but may be computationally heavy at scale; an assessment of variance and sample budget is not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The modeling section explains the construction of φ and ψ for the log-likelihood and the exponential-kernel integrals. Algorithmic pseudocode is helpful, though some implementation details are missing:
- How L (Lipschitz constant) or step sizes ηk are set in practice.
- Complexity per iteration and cost of top singular vector computations at the reported scales.
- Sensitivity to σ (kernel bandwidth) and to ρ, λ, β; only tuning for best performance is reported.

Originality
The combination of:
- a per-pair Hawkes intensity,
- low-rank structure across user–item matrices enforced via nuclear norms, and
- a hybrid proximal/Frank–Wolfe solver tailored to handle nonnegativity plus nuclear norms,
appears novel. Prior Hawkes-based works largely focus on multivariate mutually exciting processes over nodes and learning influence networks; time-aware recommendation often uses temporal factorization or survival models. This paper’s convex, low-rank Hawkes formulation specifically for recommendation and return-time prediction is a distinct contribution.

Significance
Practically, the approach provides a unified framework for two important tasks (time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction) with strong empirical performance on sizable datasets. The optimization technique could be of broader interest for problems combining nonnegativity and nuclear norms. The significance would be strengthened with comparisons to stronger temporal recommendation baselines (e.g., timeSVD++-style models, more recent tensor/point-process recommenders) and with a discussion of stability constraints.

Pros
- Clear formulation linking Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling; convex objective.
- Scalable solver with principled convergence guarantees; uses only top singular vectors.
- Unified handling of ranking over time and return-time prediction.
- Strong empirical results on both synthetic and real data; diagnostic time-change analysis.

Cons
- Theorem 1’s finite-ρ exactness for quadratic penalties is questionable; threshold is impractical to verify.
- No explicit Hawkes stability constraints; potential identifiability/explosion issues are not addressed.
- Limited baselines; missing stronger temporal recommenders and Hawkes-based collaborative models.
- Some implementation details and sensitivity analyses are omitted (step sizes, σ, ρ, computational costs).
- Return-time estimation relies on sampling without variance/runtime analysis.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Clarify Theorem 1: provide conditions under which L2 penalties yield exact equivalence at finite ρ, or use/justify an exact penalty (e.g., L1) and update the analysis. If only approximate, state so and discuss practical tuning and impact.
- Discuss and, if needed, enforce stability constraints (e.g., αu,iσ<1) and examine their effect on estimation and prediction.
- Expand baselines to include stronger temporal/point-process recommenders; report recall@k/NDCG alongside rank.
- Provide complexity per iteration, average number of power iterations for top singular vectors, and total runtime vs. data size.
- Report sensitivity to σ, ρ, λ, β, and the learned effective rank; include ablations.
- Consider approximate or analytical return-time predictors (e.g., moment approximations) to reduce sampling cost and report variance of MAE.
- Clarify step-size selection and how L is estimated in practice; consider adaptive methods.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated and largely sound contribution that brings low-rank Hawkes modeling to time-sensitive recommendation with an efficient convex solver. The empirical results are promising. Addressing the theoretical concern around the penalty reformulation, adding stability discussion, and expanding baselines would materially strengthen the paper.
