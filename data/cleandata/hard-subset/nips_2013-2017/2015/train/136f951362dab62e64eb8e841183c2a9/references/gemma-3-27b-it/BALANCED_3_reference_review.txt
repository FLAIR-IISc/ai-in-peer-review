This paper proposes a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by formulating the problem as a low-rank Hawkes process. The core idea is to model user activity as a self-exciting point process and leverage low-rank approximations to generalize across user-item pairs. The authors also develop an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, demonstrating scalability and performance on both synthetic and real datasets.

This work builds on a growing body of research recognizing the limitations of traditional collaborative filtering methods that disregard temporal dynamics. Recent NIPS papers have increasingly focused on incorporating time into recommendation systems (e.g., temporal point processes for user behavior modeling), but often focus on specific applications or limited generalization capabilities.  The connection drawn between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a noteworthy contribution. Kapoor et al. [12, 11] are correctly identified as closely related work, but the authors accurately point out their inability to generalize to unseen items, a key advantage of their proposed method.  The use of Hawkes processes, as explored in Du et al. [7], is also relevant, though the low-rank extension for scalability and generalization appears to be a novel direction.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix factorization for time-sensitive recommendation is a significant contribution. The approach effectively addresses the cold-start problem for new items.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The convex formulation and the convergence analysis of the proposed algorithm provide a strong theoretical underpinning for the work. Achieving an O(1/t) convergence rate is a positive outcome.
*   **Scalability:**  The proposed algorithm demonstrates scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events), a crucial requirement for real-world applications.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Extensive experiments on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach compared to several baselines, including SVD, tensor factorization, and STiC. The ablation studies convincingly show the benefits of the model.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the approach is clearly explained, and the mathematical notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Kernel Choice:** The choice of the exponential kernel for the triggering function is somewhat arbitrary. While justified as a starting point, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different kernel choices or exploration of more flexible kernel families.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The process of tuning parameters ρ, λ, and β is not fully detailed.  While the authors mention tuning for best performance, more specifics on the search space and methodology would be helpful for reproducibility.
*   **Complexity of Algorithm:** The algorithm, despite its claimed efficiency, appears complex with multiple steps involving proximal gradients and conditional gradients. A clearer illustration or pseudocode emphasizing the key steps could aid understanding.  The description could be streamlined.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While several baselines are used, more recent and sophisticated time-aware recommendation models (e.g., those utilizing recurrent neural networks or attention mechanisms) could be considered for a more comprehensive comparison.
*    **Real-world data:** The datasets are described, but providing more details about the scale of the datasets (e.g., the number of events per user-item pair) would be helpful.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in recommendation systems.
*   The proposed method demonstrates strong empirical performance and scalability.
*   The contribution is significant and could have a substantial impact on the field.
*   The writing is clear and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The parameter tuning process is not fully disclosed.
*   The choice of the exponential kernel is not extensively justified.
*   Comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods is limited.
*   The algorithm's complexity might hinder its practical adoption without further simplification.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The novelty of the approach, combined with the theoretical foundation and empirical validation, makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. While some weaknesses exist, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding parameter tuning details, kernel selection justification, and a discussion of algorithmic complexity.  Including a brief comparison to a more recent deep learning-based temporal recommendation model would further strengthen the paper.
