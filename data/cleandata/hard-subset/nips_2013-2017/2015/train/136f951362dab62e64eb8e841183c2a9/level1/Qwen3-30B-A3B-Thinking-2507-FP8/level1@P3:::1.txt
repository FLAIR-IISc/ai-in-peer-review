

# Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models. The authors address two critical questions in recommendation systems: how to recommend the most relevant item at the right moment, and how to predict the next returning time of users. They formulate the problem as a convex optimization problem using low-rank Hawkes processes, which allows them to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption data. The authors develop an efficient algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence rate that scales to large datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.

The work builds upon the theoretical foundation of temporal point processes (as in Daley & Vere-Jones [6]) and connects it to low-rank matrix factorization techniques. While Kapoor et al. [11,12] have explored user return time prediction using survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models, their approach cannot generalize to recommend new items, which is a key limitation addressed by the current work. The paper also relates to recent work on temporal collaborative filtering [22] and tensor factorization [2, 25], but makes a distinct contribution by explicitly modeling the temporal dynamics through Hawkes processes.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-motivated convex optimization formulation with rigorous theoretical analysis (Theorem 1 and 2). The convergence rate analysis is particularly strong, demonstrating that the algorithm achieves the best of both worlds between proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods.

2. **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate impressive scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events) through both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The linear scaling with training set size (Figure 2d) is a significant practical contribution.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments include both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), evaluating performance on both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks. The comparison with multiple baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, tensor factorization) is thorough and well-motivated.

4. **Practical Utility:** The framework's ability to incorporate contextual information (spatial, textual, user profiles) and the clear explanation of how to implement the approach make it appealing to practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Related Work Limitation:** The related work section primarily focuses on Kapoor et al. [11,12] but omits other recent temporal recommendation works. For instance, Xiong et al. [22] proposed temporal collaborative filtering with Bayesian probabilistic tensor factorization, which would have strengthened the comparison. The paper should also address the growing body of work on deep learning approaches for temporal recommendation (e.g., [23, 24]).

2. **Experimental Design:** While the experiments are comprehensive, they lack statistical significance testing for the performance improvements. The paper claims "superb predictive performance" but doesn't provide p-values or confidence intervals to support this claim.

3. **Theoretical Justification for Low-Rank Assumption:** The authors assume low-rank structures for the base intensity matrix Λ0 and self-exciting matrix A but don't sufficiently justify why this assumption is appropriate for the problem. A more detailed discussion of the connection between user-item behavior patterns and low-rank structure would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

4. **Kernel Parametrization:** The paper limits the triggering kernel to the exponential form, despite mentioning other possibilities (Weibull, Log-logistic). The impact of different kernel choices on performance isn't explored, which would have provided more insight into the model's flexibility.

5. **Practical Limitations:** The paper doesn't discuss potential limitations of the approach, such as handling extremely sparse data or the impact of the rank parameter selection. The sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β) is limited to the synthetic experiments.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to time-sensitive recommendation systems by introducing a novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models. The theoretical analysis is strong, the algorithm is efficient and scalable, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches. The work addresses an important gap in recommendation systems literature by explicitly modeling temporal dynamics rather than focusing solely on static preferences.

The main weaknesses relate to the limited comparison with recent deep learning approaches for temporal recommendation, the lack of statistical significance testing, and insufficient justification for the low-rank assumption. However, these do not undermine the core technical contribution, which is both novel and well-executed.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field with a novel framework, strong theoretical foundation, and impressive scalability. While there are minor weaknesses in the experimental design and related work, the core technical contribution is significant, well-validated, and addresses an important problem in recommendation systems. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with optimal convergence guarantees
- Demonstrated scalability to large real-world datasets
- Clear practical utility with contextual information incorporation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on multiple datasets

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison with recent deep learning approaches for temporal recommendation
- Lack of statistical significance testing for performance improvements
- Insufficient justification for low-rank assumption
- Limited exploration of different triggering kernel forms

The paper substantially advances the state of the art in time-sensitive recommendation systems and would be of interest to both researchers and practitioners in the field.
