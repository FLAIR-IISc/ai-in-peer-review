

# Peer Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework that connects self-exciting point processes (Hawkes processes) with low-rank matrix models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption data. The authors address two underexplored questions in recommendation systems: (1) how to recommend the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) how to predict the next returning time of users. The paper establishes a convex optimization formulation that leverages the connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models, develops an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and demonstrates superior performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The work builds upon recent advances in temporal point processes (e.g., Du et al., "Dirichlet-Hawkes Processes" at KDD 2015) and low-rank matrix factorization (e.g., SVD, tensor factorization), while addressing limitations of prior approaches that cannot generalize to new items or properly model temporal dynamics.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation, including a convex formulation of the problem, a convergence analysis proving O(1/t) rate, and a clear derivation of the optimization algorithm. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with careful comparison to multiple baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, tensor factorization) on both synthetic data and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II). The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for parameter tuning and the assumption of exponential triggering kernels, and provides appropriate ablation studies. The scalability claims (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events) are well-supported by experiments showing linear scaling with dataset size.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background on temporal point processes is clear and accessible, and the authors effectively build from basic concepts to their proposed model. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The experimental setup and results are described in sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the figures effectively illustrate key findings (e.g., convergence behavior, comparative performance). The writing is precise, with clear explanations of both the theoretical contributions and practical implications.

## Originality
This work makes a significant original contribution by connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation, addressing a clear gap in the literature. While Du et al. (KDD 2015) and Kapoor et al. (KDD 2014) have explored Hawkes processes for recommendation, this paper is the first to establish the low-rank structure for scalability and to develop a convex optimization approach that handles both non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization. The optimization algorithm, which blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, is novel and achieves the best of both worlds in terms of convergence rates. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work, particularly regarding the ability to generalize to new items (unlike survival analysis approaches).

## Significance
The paper addresses a meaningful problem with significant practical implications. Time-sensitive recommendation is increasingly important for modern web services, as users' preferences vary with temporal contexts. The ability to predict returning times has direct applications in marketing (e.g., targeted ad bidding), healthcare (e.g., proactive disease management), and user engagement. The demonstrated superior performance on both synthetic and real-world data, combined with scalability to large datasets, suggests this approach could have substantial impact in practice. The paper also points to potential extensions for incorporating additional contextual information, broadening its applicability.

## Strengths
- Novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation
- Theoretical foundation with rigorous convergence analysis
- Efficient optimization algorithm with O(1/t) convergence rate
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets
- Demonstrated scalability to large-scale problems
- Clear articulation of how the approach differs from previous work

## Weaknesses
- Limited exploration of alternative triggering kernels beyond the exponential form
- The real-world datasets, while diverse, could be expanded to include more domains (e.g., social media, news)
- The computational complexity analysis could be more detailed beyond the convergence rate
- The paper doesn't fully address how the model handles cold-start scenarios for new users or items
- The comparison with recent point process approaches (e.g., Zhou et al., ICML 2016) could be more thorough

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- The paper addresses a meaningful gap in recommendation systems literature with a technically sound approach
- The convex formulation and efficient optimization algorithm are novel contributions
- The experimental results demonstrate clear superiority over strong baselines
- The work has significant potential for practical applications across multiple domains
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized

**Against Acceptance:**
- The choice of exponential triggering kernel is somewhat arbitrary without thorough exploration
- The real-world evaluation could be strengthened with additional datasets
- The comparison with the most recent point process methods for recommendation (e.g., Zhou et al., ICML 2016) is insufficient
- The paper doesn't fully address how the model handles cold-start scenarios

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of recommendation systems by addressing time-sensitive recommendations through a novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models. The theoretical foundation is sound, the optimization algorithm is efficient and well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing approaches. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the literature. While the exploration of alternative triggering kernels and additional real-world datasets would strengthen the paper, these are minor concerns given the overall quality and significance of the work.

I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to NIPS. The work advances the state of the art in time-sensitive recommendation, provides a scalable solution for large-scale applications, and opens up new avenues for research at the intersection of point processes and recommendation systems.
