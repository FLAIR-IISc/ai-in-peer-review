The paper "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities" presents a novel framework for recommending the most desirable item at the right moment and predicting the next returning time of a user to a service. The authors propose a convex formulation that connects self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture the recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption pairs.

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed framework, including the background on temporal point processes and the low-rank Hawkes process model. The authors also provide a detailed description of the optimization algorithm, which blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods to learn the model efficiently.

The strengths of the paper include:

1. Novelty: The paper proposes a new framework for time-sensitive recommendation, which is a unique contribution to the field.
2. Technical soundness: The paper provides a rigorous mathematical formulation of the problem and a well-designed optimization algorithm.
3. Empirical evaluation: The authors provide an extensive evaluation of the proposed method on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating its superior scalability and predictive performance.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

1. Related work: While the authors mention some related work, they could have provided a more comprehensive review of the existing literature on time-sensitive recommendation and temporal point processes.
2. Interpretability: The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the interpretability of the proposed model, including the physical meaning of the parameters and the implications of the results.
3. Comparison to other methods: While the authors compare their method to some baselines, they could have included a more comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art methods in the field.

Arguments pro acceptance:

1. The paper proposes a novel and technically sound framework for time-sensitive recommendation.
2. The empirical evaluation demonstrates the superior scalability and predictive performance of the proposed method.
3. The paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of recommender systems and temporal point processes.

Arguments con acceptance:

1. The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive review of the existing literature.
2. The interpretability of the proposed model could be improved.
3. The comparison to other methods could be more comprehensive.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with some minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above.
