## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper addresses the important problem of time-sensitive recommendation, moving beyond traditional methods that primarily focus on static user preferences. The authors propose a novel framework combining self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interaction data. They aim to both recommend the most relevant item *at the right moment* and predict a user’s next return time to a service. The core contribution lies in a convex formulation of this problem and an efficient optimization algorithm with a provable O(1/t) convergence rate, scaling to large datasets.

The paper builds upon existing work in point processes (Cox & Isham, Daley & Vere-Jones) and collaborative filtering (Koren, Karatzoglou et al.). It directly relates to recent work by Kapoor et al. (2014, 2015) which utilized survival analysis for return time prediction, but crucially distinguishes itself by offering a framework capable of recommending *new* items, unlike the cited prior work. The use of low-rank modeling to handle scalability is also a common theme in recommender systems (e.g., Rendle, Xiong et al.), and the formulation draws on related optimization techniques (Lan, Nesterov).

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation and a well-defined optimization algorithm. The theoretical convergence guarantees are a significant plus. The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate strong performance compared to several baselines, including a recent state-of-the-art method (Kapoor et al.). The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., tuning of parameters) and point towards potential extensions (incorporating additional context information). The detailed description of the algorithm and the convergence analysis are particularly commendable.

**Weaknesses:** While the paper is strong technically, the writing could be more concise in certain sections (e.g., the background on temporal point processes feels somewhat lengthy). The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, lacks a deeper analysis of *why* the proposed method performs better.  For instance, exploring the learned parameters (e.g., the triggering kernel) could provide valuable insights. The discussion of related work, while present, could be more nuanced – for instance, briefly touching upon extensions of Hawkes processes like the inhomogeneous Hawkes process may be helpful. Finally, while the convergence analysis is rigorous, the practical impact of the O(1/t) rate is not clearly discussed in relation to the experiments.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating superior performance.
*   Provable convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithm.
*   Clear writing and detailed explanation of the method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Writing could be more concise.
*   Experimental analysis could be more insightful.
*   Limited discussion of the practical implications of the convergence rate.

**Overall:** I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work presents a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation, offering a principled and scalable solution with strong theoretical and empirical support. Addressing the minor weaknesses mentioned above would further strengthen the paper.
