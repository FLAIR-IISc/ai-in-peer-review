This paper proposes a novel framework combining self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation, specifically addressing both item recommendation at the right moment and predicting user return times. The core idea is compelling: leveraging the temporal dynamics of user-item interactions beyond simple rating predictions. The formulation is mathematically sound and the authors provide a convex optimization approach with a theoretically sound convergence rate (O(1/t)). The experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets supports the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The work builds upon a growing body of research recognizing the limitations of traditional collaborative filtering methods that ignore temporal information.  Recent NIPS papers have touched upon temporal dynamics in recommendation (e.g., modeling session-based recommendations, time-aware matrix factorization). However, the explicit connection to Hawkes processes and the simultaneous tackling of recommendation *and* return time prediction, coupled with the scalable optimization algorithm, represent a significant contribution. The related work section acknowledges Kapoor et al. [12, 11] but accurately points out their limitation in generalizing to new items.  The authors correctly position their work as addressing a broader problem.  The connection to healthcare applications (predicting disease recurrence) is a nice illustration of the method's potential.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models in this context is a novel and insightful approach.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The convex formulation and the guaranteed convergence rate are strong points. The algorithm’s scalability (handling millions of user-item pairs) is also impressive.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, comparing against several baselines, provide strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.  The ablation studies (e.g., comparing different tensor factorization variants) are helpful.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the mathematical notation is understandable.
* **Practical Relevance:**  Both the recommendation and return-time prediction tasks are practically relevant and address key challenges in modern web services.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Kernel Choice:** While the authors justify using the exponential kernel, a more thorough exploration of other kernel shapes (e.g., Weibull, Log-logistic) and a discussion of how kernel selection impacts performance would be valuable.  The sensitivity of results to the kernel bandwidth (σ) should also be investigated more rigorously.
* **Regularization Parameter Tuning:** The tuning of λ and β (nuclear norm regularization parameters) isn’t described in sufficient detail. It’s unclear how these parameters were selected and whether a cross-validation strategy was employed.  The dependence of performance on these parameters should be discussed.
* **Scalability Details:** While the authors claim scalability, more details on the computational resources used (e.g., number of cores, memory) and the runtime for different dataset sizes would strengthen the argument. A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm beyond the convergence rate would be useful.
* **Real-World Data Details:**  The descriptions of the real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) are relatively brief. Providing more details about the data characteristics (e.g., sparsity, event rates) would help readers assess the applicability of the method to other domains.  It would be useful to mention data preprocessing steps taken.
* **Comparison with State-of-the-Art Temporal Models:** The comparison to STiC [11] feels somewhat dismissive.  A more direct comparison, perhaps with modifications to STiC to allow for recommendations of new items, would be more convincing. Similarly, newer, dedicated temporal point process based recommendation systems are emerging – a comparison would strengthen the contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
* The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* The proposed algorithm is scalable and addresses a practically important problem.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited exploration of kernel choices and regularization parameter tuning.
* The lack of detailed information on computational scalability and dataset characteristics.
* The relatively superficial comparison with other state-of-the-art temporal recommendation models.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. While there are a few areas for improvement, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and experimental validation are compelling.  I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding kernel selection, regularization parameter tuning, scalability details, and a more thorough comparison with recent temporal recommendation methods.  A more detailed description of the datasets used would also be beneficial.
