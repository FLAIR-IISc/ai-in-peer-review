This paper proposes a novel framework combining self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to address time-sensitive recommendation and user returning-time prediction. The core idea is to model user-item interactions as a Hawkes process, leveraging low-rank structure to generalize across unobserved pairs. The authors present a convex formulation and an efficient optimization algorithm with a theoretical convergence guarantee, supported by experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

This work builds upon a growing body of research acknowledging the limitations of traditional recommendation systems that ignore temporal dynamics. Recent NIPS papers, such as those exploring temporal collaborative filtering (Rendle, 2014) and dynamic user preferences (Yi et al., 2014), demonstrate this trend. Specifically, the paper directly addresses limitations identified in Kapoor et al. (2014, 2015), which used survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models, by proposing a model capable of recommending *new* items, a key distinction highlighted by the authors.  The connection to Hawkes processes is also well-established (Hawkes, 1971; Daley & Vere-Jones, 1984), but applying it within a low-rank framework for recommendation is a novel contribution. Furthermore, the work connects to broader research in matrix/tensor factorization (Koren, 2009; Karatzoglou et al., 2010; Xiong et al., 2013) and optimization for large-scale problems (Lan, 2014; Ouyang et al., 2013).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation is a novel approach, extending the capabilities of existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The derivation of a convex formulation and an algorithm with an O(1/t) convergence rate is a strong technical contribution.
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm is designed to handle large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events), a crucial aspect for real-world applications.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to relevant baselines.  The comparisons to STiC, Tensor Factorization, and SVD are well-chosen.
*   **Clear Explanation:** The paper provides a clear and well-structured explanation of the proposed model and algorithm. The connection to point process theory is adequately explained, making it accessible to readers not deeply familiar with the field.
*   **Potential for Extension:** The authors explicitly mention the possibility of incorporating additional contextual information (user profiles, textual features, etc.), enhancing the model's applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Tuning:** While the experiments demonstrate good performance, the specifics of parameter tuning (ρ, λ, β, kernel bandwidth, rank) are not fully detailed. A more thorough discussion of sensitivity to these parameters would be beneficial.  How was the choice of rank determined (e.g., using cross-validation)?
*   **Kernel Choice:** The exponential kernel is used throughout the experiments.  While justified as a starting point, exploring other kernel families (Weibull, Log-logistic) and their impact on performance could further strengthen the results.
*   **Complexity of Implementation:** Implementing the algorithm, particularly the conditional gradient step with the linear minimization oracle, might be complex and require significant engineering effort. A discussion of practical implementation challenges would be valuable.
*   **Real-World Dataset Details:** More details about the real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) would be helpful. For example, what is the average number of events per user-item pair? How was the data preprocessed?
* **Limited comparison to state-of-the-art recommender systems:** Although comparisons to several relevant methods are included, they are not necessarily the most contemporary or high-performing recommender systems. Including comparisons with deep learning based approaches (e.g. recurrent neural networks with attention mechanisms) would further strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in recommendation systems.
*   The theoretical guarantees and scalability results are significant contributions.
*   The empirical validation is comprehensive and demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods.
*   The potential for future extensions is promising.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The parameter tuning details are insufficient.
*   The limited exploration of kernel choices and implementation complexity could raise concerns.
*   Comparison to more advanced recommender systems would make the results even more compelling.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The novelty, theoretical grounding, and empirical validation are all commendable. While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised regarding parameter tuning, kernel exploration, and dataset details.



**Rating:** 7/10 (Strong Accept with Minor Revisions)
