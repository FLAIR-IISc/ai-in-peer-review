This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by combining self-exciting point processes and low-rank models. The core idea is to model user-item consumption patterns as a Hawkes process, leveraging a low-rank structure to generalize to unseen user-item pairs. The authors develop a convex optimization formulation and an efficient algorithm with provable convergence guarantees, achieving strong performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets for item recommendation and returning-time prediction.

This work builds upon recent efforts in temporal recommendation, notably the work of Kapoor et al. [12, 11], which utilizes survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models. However, unlike those approaches, the proposed method aims for a more general framework capable of recommending *any* new item, not just those previously interacted with.  The connection to Hawkes processes, a well-established technique in point process analysis [4, 5, 6], offers a principled way to model temporal dependencies.  Related work in tensor factorization for recommendation [13, 23, 25] is also acknowledged and compared against.  The authors correctly position their work as addressing the limitations of traditional collaborative filtering methods that often ignore the temporal aspects of user behavior.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation is a novel contribution. It provides a flexible and theoretically sound framework for capturing temporal user behavior.
* **Technical Soundness:** The convex formulation and the proposed optimization algorithm, blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, appear well-motivated and technically sound. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is a significant strength.
* **Scalability:** The authors explicitly address scalability concerns, demonstrating that their algorithm can handle large datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.
* **Empirical Validation:** Comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, outperforming several baselines including STiC [11], SVD, and tensor factorization methods.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the technical details are presented in a reasonably accessible manner. The inclusion of a convergence analysis is appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Tuning:** While the authors mention tuning parameters ρ, λ, and β, the specifics of this process are not fully detailed. A more in-depth discussion of parameter sensitivity and selection strategies would be beneficial.
* **Kernel Choice:** The paper primarily focuses on the exponential triggering kernel. Exploring other kernel families and their impact on performance could strengthen the analysis. While extensions are mentioned, no experimental results with different kernels are presented.
* **Real-World Dataset Details:** More details about the real-world datasets would be helpful. For example, providing statistics on data sparsity and the distribution of events would give more context to the experimental results.
* **Comparison Depth:** While the comparison with baselines is comprehensive, a deeper analysis of the specific scenarios where the proposed method excels would be valuable. For instance, under what conditions does the low-rank assumption perform best?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
* The proposed algorithm is efficient and scalable.
* The empirical results demonstrate strong performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* The convergence analysis provides a strong guarantee on the algorithm’s performance.
* The work addresses an important problem in recommender systems that is often overlooked.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The parameter tuning process could be more clearly documented.
* The exploration of different kernel choices is limited.
* More detailed descriptions of the real-world datasets would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential to impact the field of recommender systems. The novelty of the approach, the technical soundness of the formulation, and the compelling empirical results strongly support acceptance. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. The paper meets the NIPS criteria of quality, clarity, originality, and significance.

**Recommendation:**

Accept.
