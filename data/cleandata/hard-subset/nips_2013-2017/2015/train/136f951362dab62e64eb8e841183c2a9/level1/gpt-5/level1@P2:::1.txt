Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models each user–item pair’s interactions as a self-exciting Hawkes process with exponential triggering kernel. The intensity λu,i(t) is the sum of a nonnegative baseline λu,i0 and a history-dependent self-excitation term scaled by a nonnegative coefficient αu,i. To enable generalization to unobserved user–item pairs, the authors posit low-rank structure on the m×n matrices Λ0 and A, enforcing nuclear-norm regularization and nonnegativity. This yields a convex estimation problem (negative log-likelihood of Hawkes processes plus nuclear norms). Because the combination of nuclear norm and elementwise nonnegativity complicates standard proximal updates, they introduce an auxiliary-variable, quadratic-penalty reformulation and design a block composite algorithm that uses a proximal step for the nonnegativity-constrained block and a conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe) step for the nuclear-norm-regularized block, with an O(1/t) convergence guarantee. Two tasks are considered: time-sensitive item recommendation (ranking items by instantaneous intensity) and return-time prediction (sampling-based estimate of the next event time given history). Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, and MIMIC II) show improved ranking and MAE for return-time prediction versus Poisson, SVD, time-tensor factorization, and a survival/HSMM baseline (STiC).

The work bridges two lines: (i) collaborative filtering and context-aware/tensor recommendation [13,16,21,22,25], and (ii) point processes/Hawkes modeling for temporal events [4–6,10,30], including related low-rank structures in Hawkes networks [29,30]. Compared to hazard/survival-based return-time prediction [11,12], the Hawkes formulation captures history dependence and supports recommendation for unseen user–item pairs via shared low-rank structure.

Quality
- Modeling and convexity: With exponential kernels and linear parameterization, the Hawkes log-likelihood is concave in (λ0, α) per pair; aggregating over pairs and adding nuclear norms yields a convex program with nonnegativity constraints. This is technically sound. Nonnegativity is appropriate for intensities and excitation parameters.
- Optimization: The hybrid proximal/conditional gradient approach is reasonable and leverages efficient linear minimization oracles (top singular vectors). The stated rate combines O(1/k^2) (prox part) and O(1/k) (CG part), so overall O(1/k), consistent with FW literature for composite objectives.
- Theorem 1 (equivalence of the quadratic-penalty formulation to the constrained problem for sufficiently large ρ) is the most delicate point. Quadratic penalties are generally not exact for equality constraints unless ρ→∞; claiming existence of a finite problem-dependent ρ* that ensures exact optimal value coincidence warrants a careful proof. As ρ* depends on unknown optimal solutions, it is also impractical to verify. In experiments the authors tune ρ, which is fine in practice, but the theoretical claim should be either softened (approximation error bound as a function of ρ) or justified more clearly.
- Stability/branching ratio: For Hawkes processes with exponential kernels, stationarity typically requires αu,i∫γ < 1. The paper does not state enforcing such a constraint; discuss whether learned parameters always satisfy stability and whether violations harm prediction or sampling (Ogata).
- Computation: The algorithm requires only top singular vectors per iteration for Z1 and Z2 and simple projections for Λ0,A, suggesting good scalability; synthetic experiments with 100M events and linear scaling plots are encouraging. It would help to report wall-clock times vs. baselines and memory footprint.
- Evaluation: The design (holding out entire user–item sequences for cold-start at the pair level and additional within-sequence splits) is sensible. Reporting mean rank and MAE is reasonable; including Recall@k/NDCG@k would strengthen the recommendation evaluation. Confidence intervals or statistical tests would add rigor.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. The derivation of the log-likelihood terms (φ, ψ) and the special case for exponential kernels is helpful. Pseudocode is compact. Some suggestions:
- Clarify the stability condition and whether it is enforced during training.
- Expand on how gradients are computed efficiently given long histories (for exponential kernels, recursive accumulation is possible).
- Explain the choice of kernel bandwidth σ and sensitivity analysis.
- Temper the “equivalence” claim for the penalty method or provide an intuition/proof sketch in the main text.

Originality
The connection of Hawkes processes with collaborative filtering via low-rank matrices on per-pair base and excitation parameters is novel in the recommendation context. Prior Hawkes work with low-rank structure focuses on network infectivity [29,30], not user–item recommendation or return-time prediction. The hybrid optimization scheme for nonnegativity plus nuclear norms via auxiliary variables is a practical twist on composite Frank–Wolfe/prox methods; related ideas exist (e.g., conditional gradient for norm-regularized objectives), but the particular block design and application are new to the best of my knowledge.

Significance
Time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction are important for many applications (media, e-commerce, healthcare). The approach is general, scalable, and shows consistent gains over strong baselines, including temporal tensor factorization. The method can be extended to include features or nonparametric kernels. If the theoretical aspects are clarified and stability addressed, the contribution is likely to influence follow-up work on continuous-time recommendation.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled modeling of recurrent temporal behavior with Hawkes processes.
- Convex estimation with a scalable algorithm and convergence guarantee.
- Strong empirical results on large synthetic and multiple real datasets.
- Addresses two relevant tasks (ranking at the right time and return-time prediction) within one framework.
- Potentially impactful for time-sensitive personalization.

Cons for acceptance
- Theorem 1’s exactness with finite quadratic penalty is questionable and depends on unknown ρ*; needs clarification or a weaker, approximate statement.
- No explicit stability constraints for Hawkes parameters; implications not discussed.
- Evaluation lacks standard ranking metrics (Recall@k/NDCG@k) and statistical significance reports.
- Limited comparison to other continuous-time temporal recommenders (e.g., time-aware factorization beyond Poisson-tensor; though continuous-time alternatives were scarce in 2015).

Questions and suggestions
- Do learned αu,i satisfy αu,i∫γ < 1? If not, how does that affect sampling and predictions?
- Provide ablations showing the benefit of low-rank vs. per-pair MLE and of including/excluding self-excitation.
- Report Recall@k/NDCG@k and variance estimates.
- Discuss sensitivity to ρ and σ; provide guidance for setting ρ without relying on unknown ρ*.
- Consider comparing against survival-based models that allow history features (if available), and timeSVD++ as a discrete-time baseline for ranking.

Overall recommendation
A strong paper with a novel and well-motivated method, solid experiments, and good potential impact. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying the penalty equivalence claim and addressing stability and evaluation details.
