

# Review: Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities

This paper proposes a novel framework that integrates self-exciting point processes (specifically Hawkes processes) with low-rank matrix models to address two time-sensitive recommendation tasks: recommending relevant items at the right moment and predicting users' next return times. The authors establish a connection between temporal point processes (previously used in survival analysis and event prediction) and low-rank models (common in collaborative filtering), creating a unified framework that captures recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption data.

The work builds upon and differentiates from Kapoor et al.'s [11, 12] time-sensitive recommendation approaches, which use survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models but cannot generalize to recommend new items. The paper also relates to tensor factorization methods for temporal recommendations (e.g., Karatzoglou et al. [22], Xiong et al. [23]), but addresses their limitation in capturing the full history dependence of events.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach. The convex formulation (Equation 4) is well-motivated as a relaxation of the low-rank problem, and the authors provide a rigorous convergence analysis (Theorem 2) showing the proposed algorithm achieves O(1/t) convergence rate. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using both synthetic data (with ground-truth parameters) and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II medical data). The comparison with multiple strong baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, tensor factorization) demonstrates clear improvements, particularly on the more challenging "held-out" sequences. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, including the need for parameter tuning.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, the algorithms (1-3) are clearly described, and the experimental setup is thoroughly explained. The connection between the Hawkes process intensity function and the two recommendation tasks is particularly well articulated. The figures effectively illustrate convergence behavior, scalability, and performance comparisons. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution by establishing a novel connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for recommendation systems. While Hawkes processes have been used in event prediction (e.g., Du et al. [7, 8]), and low-rank models are standard in collaborative filtering, their integration for time-sensitive recommendation represents a meaningful advance. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from previous work, particularly noting that their model can recommend new items (unlike Kapoor et al.'s [11] approach), and provide a theoretically grounded convex formulation. The optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods appears novel for this specific problem.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important gap in recommendation systems research: most approaches focus on predicting one-time preferences rather than considering temporal dynamics. The applications in music streaming, e-commerce, and healthcare are compelling and demonstrate the practical relevance of the work. The scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events makes the approach applicable to real-world systems. The ability to incorporate additional contextual information (user profiles, spatial features) further enhances its practical utility.

**Strengths:**
- Novel integration of Hawkes processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendations
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with optimal convergence rate
- Comprehensive experimental validation on both synthetic and multiple real-world datasets
- Clear demonstration of scalability to large-scale problems
- Practical applicability to real-world recommendation scenarios

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of how the approach handles cold-start problems for new users/items (a major challenge in recommendation systems)
- The paper claims "superb predictive performance" but doesn't provide statistical significance tests to confirm the improvements over baselines are meaningful
- The exponential triggering kernel is assumed throughout, with only brief mention of alternative parametric forms (Weibull, Log-logistic)
- The connection between the intensity function and specific recommendation decisions could be made more concrete with additional examples

**Recommendation:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation systems. The technical quality is excellent, the approach is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method. While the cold-start limitation is notable, it's a common challenge in recommendation systems rather than a specific weakness of this paper. The work clearly advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and has significant potential for practical impact.

I recommend acceptance. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria exceptionally well, with particular strengths in originality and significance. The proposed framework represents a meaningful step forward in modeling temporal dynamics for recommendation systems, and the efficient optimization algorithm makes it practical for real-world applications.
