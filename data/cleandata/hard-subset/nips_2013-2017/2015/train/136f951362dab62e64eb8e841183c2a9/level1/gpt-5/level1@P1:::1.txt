Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process with an intensity composed of a nonnegative baseline term and a history-dependent excitation via a parametric triggering kernel (exponential by default). To enable knowledge transfer across the many unobserved user–item pairs, the baseline matrix Λ0 and excitation matrix A are constrained to be low-rank via nuclear norm regularization, yielding a convex estimation problem. The authors develop an efficient hybrid optimization algorithm that combines proximal gradient (to handle the nonnegativity constraints) with conditional gradient (to handle nuclear norms) through a penalty-based splitting with auxiliary variables. They prove that for sufficiently large penalty ρ, the penalized problem is equivalent to the original constrained problem and establish an O(1/t) convergence rate. Empirically, on synthetic data (∼100M events) and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II), the method outperforms Poisson, SVD, tensor factorization variants, and an HMM/survival-based return-time predictor (STiC) on two tasks: time-sensitive item recommendation and next return-time prediction.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Hawkes processes [10] and convex learning of point processes. It connects to recent work on learning triggering kernels and influence networks [8, 30], but differs by imposing low-rank structure across user–item pairs to transfer information and enable recommendations for unseen pairs. Compared to survival analysis and semi-Markov approaches for return-time prediction [11, 12], the Hawkes-based approach captures dependence on the entire event history and supports recommending new items via shared low-rank structure. In recommendation, the work contrasts with temporal MF [16] and context-aware tensor factorizations [3, 21, 25], which typically discretize time and do not model recurrent, continuous-time self-excitation. On the optimization side, the hybrid proximal/conditional gradient scheme relates to work on conditional gradient for norm-regularized smooth problems [27] and accelerated composite methods [17, 18], but the penalty-splitting to decouple nonnegativity and nuclear norms is a useful engineering of these ideas.

Strengths
- Well-motivated problem: unifies time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction in a single continuous-time, history-aware framework.
- Convex formulation with nuclear norms and nonnegativity; clear log-likelihood derivation for linear Hawkes intensity with exponential kernels; parameters have interpretable roles (long-term preference vs. short-term recency effects).
- Optimization: practical algorithm that avoids full SVDs; per-iteration cost relies on top singular vectors and simple projections; theoretical convergence bound and an equivalence theorem for the penalty reformulation.
- Scalability demonstrated on large synthetic data; consistent improvements on three real datasets for both tasks.
- Diagnostics using time-change theorem to validate Hawkes assumptions on real data are thoughtful and informative.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Modeling limitations: treating each (user, item) as an independent 1-D Hawkes process omits mutual-excitation across items (substitutes/complements) and across users (social/context spillovers). While low-rank sharing helps parameter estimation, it does not capture cross-pair triggering. Extending to a (sparse/low-rank) mutually-exciting structure or adding latent components that can excite multiple items would strengthen the modeling.
- Stability/identifiability: there is no explicit constraint ensuring subcriticality (e.g., α·∫γ < 1) for each pair, which is standard for Hawkes stability. Please clarify whether constraints or regularization enforce this implicitly, and report empirical branching ratios. Also discuss identifiability between Λ0 and A, as both can explain higher intensities.
- Penalized reformulation: Theorem 1 depends on an unknown threshold ρ*. In practice ρ is tuned. Provide a sensitivity analysis showing performance and recovered rank versus ρ; discuss numerical stability and the tradeoff between constraint satisfaction and conditioning.
- Baselines and metrics: For time-sensitive ranking, include stronger temporal/sequential baselines such as timeSVD++ [16], FPMC/Markov-chain-based next-item models, or dynamic Poisson factorization over time (if feasible). Report ranking metrics used commonly in recommendation (Recall@k/NDCG@k) in addition to “rank of the true item.” For return-time, include calibration plots or proper scoring rules (e.g., log-likelihood or CRPS) rather than only MAE.
- Experimental details: More detail on hyperparameter selection, kernel bandwidth tuning, and computational costs for prediction (Monte Carlo sampling via thinning can be nontrivial at scale). It would help to report wall-clock times, memory footprint, and the number of iterations/SVD computations per epoch.
- Contextual extensions: The paper mentions the ability to incorporate side information; a small ablation that demonstrates gains from user/item/context features would bolster the claim.

Quality
The modeling and convex formulation appear technically sound; the likelihood is concave in the nonnegative parameters when intensity is linear, making the overall objective convex with nuclear norms. The optimization method is reasonable and theoretically justified. Some claims about being “first of its kind” on optimal rates may be somewhat strong given related hybrid proximal/Frank-Wolfe literature, but the adaptation to this setting is solid. Empirical evaluation is thorough in scale but could include stronger baselines and broader metrics.

Clarity
Overall clear and well-organized. The notation around Algorithm 1–3 can be streamlined; define L and Lipschitz constants precisely, and state per-iteration complexity. Minor typos and spacing around formulas. Include pseudocode for the return-time sampling/prediction step and discussion on the number of samples needed.

Originality
The low-rank Hawkes formulation for user–item recommendation and a convex, scalable solver are novel. The hybrid optimization with penalty-based splitting is a useful contribution, though related ideas exist.

Significance
Addresses an important and timely problem; likely to influence both recommendation and temporal modeling communities. The ability to predict both what and when is compelling for real systems.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled continuous-time model capturing recurrent behaviors with shared low-rank structure.
- Convex estimation with an efficient, scalable algorithm and convergence guarantees.
- Strong empirical results on large-scale synthetic and multiple real datasets.
- Practical diagnostic validating model fit.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited modeling of mutual excitation across items/users; could miss important dynamics.
- Dependence on penalty parameter ρ with no principled selection; lack of stability constraints.
- Baselines and evaluation metrics could be stronger/more standard in recommendation; limited ablations.

Questions for the authors
- Do you enforce any stability constraints on A and the kernel to guarantee subcritical Hawkes processes? What fraction of learned pairs have branching ratio < 1?
- How sensitive are results to ρ, λ, β and kernel bandwidth? Please provide a sensitivity plot.
- What are the per-iteration and total runtimes and memory usage on the largest dataset?
- Can you report Recall@k/NDCG@k and negative log-likelihood for return-time predictions?
- Have you explored adding simple time-of-day/weekly seasonality to Λ0(t)?

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a principled, scalable method and convincing results. Addressing the noted empirical and modeling limitations would further strengthen the contribution.
