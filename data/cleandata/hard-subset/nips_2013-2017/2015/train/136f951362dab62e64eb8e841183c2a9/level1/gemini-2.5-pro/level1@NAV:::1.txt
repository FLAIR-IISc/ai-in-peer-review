This paper addresses two key problems in time-sensitive recommendation: recommending the right item at the right moment and predicting a user's next return time. The authors propose a novel framework that elegantly combines self-exciting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models. The core idea is to model each user-item interaction sequence as a Hawkes process, where the base intensity and self-excitation parameters for all pairs are captured in two matrices. By assuming these matrices are low-rank, the model can generalize to unobserved user-item pairs, effectively transferring knowledge. This work builds upon the extensive literature of matrix factorization in recommendation systems (e.g., Koren, KDD '09) and recent temporal models like tensor factorization (e.g., Chi & Kolda), but its unique contribution lies in integrating the sophisticated, event-driven dynamics of point processes into a collaborative filtering setting.

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Quality and Originality:** The formulation of the problem as a low-rank Hawkes process is both novel and principled. The primary technical contribution is the development of an efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. This approach cleverly circumvents the need for expensive SVD computations at each iteration, making it scalable. The inclusion of a convergence rate analysis (Theorem 2) provides strong theoretical backing for the proposed algorithm.
**Significance:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The model is tested on synthetic and three real-world datasets, consistently outperforming strong and relevant baselines, including tensor factorization and hidden semi-Markov models, on both recommendation and return-time prediction tasks. The use of the time-change theorem to validate the Hawkes process assumption (Figure 3) is a particularly strong piece of analysis that lends credibility to the model's design.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses.
**Clarity:** While the method is well-described, the process for tuning hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β) is not discussed. A brief analysis of the model's sensitivity to these parameters would improve the paper's practical utility and reproducibility.
**Scope:** The experiments are limited to a simple exponential triggering kernel. The paper mentions the possibility of using more flexible or non-parametric kernels in Section 3.3, but an empirical comparison, even on a small scale, would have strengthened this claim. Similarly, the ability to incorporate contextual information is a key stated advantage, but it is not demonstrated experimentally.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and elegant synthesis of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for a crucial and under-explored problem in recommendation.
2.  Offers a significant technical contribution with a scalable optimization algorithm backed by theoretical convergence guarantees.
3.  Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance through a comprehensive and rigorous experimental evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The empirical validation does not cover all claimed model extensions (e.g., flexible kernels, contextual features).

Overall, the strengths of this paper—its novelty, technical depth, and strong empirical results—far outweigh its minor limitations. It is a high-quality contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in temporal recommendation.
