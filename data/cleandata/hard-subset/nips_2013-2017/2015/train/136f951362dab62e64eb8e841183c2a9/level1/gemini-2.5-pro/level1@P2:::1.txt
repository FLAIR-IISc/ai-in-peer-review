This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, focusing on two key tasks: (1) recommending the right item at a specific moment, and (2) predicting when a user will next interact with an item/service. The authors propose a novel and elegant framework that models the sequence of user-item interactions as a self-exciting Hawkes process. The core idea is that past interactions with an item increase the probability of future interactions in the short term. To handle the sparsity of user-item data and to generalize to unobserved pairs, the model assumes that the parameters of the Hawkes processes—specifically, the baseline intensity matrix (capturing long-term preference) and the self-excitation matrix (capturing influence strength)—are low-rank. This connects the continuous-time point process model to the well-established low-rank paradigm of collaborative filtering.

The authors formulate the parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem involving the maximization of the log-likelihood with nuclear norm regularization and non-negativity constraints. To overcome the computational challenges of this formulation, they propose an equivalent penalized formulation that decouples the constraints. They then develop a novel and efficient algorithm that combines proximal gradient steps (for the non-negative part) with conditional gradient steps (for the nuclear norm part), thereby avoiding expensive full SVDs at each iteration. The paper provides a theoretical convergence analysis for this algorithm. This approach is evaluated on synthetic and three real-world datasets, showing superior performance against several baselines, including static matrix factorization, tensor factorization, and other time-aware models.

**Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The modeling approach is well-justified, providing a principled way to capture both long-term static preferences (via the baseline intensity) and short-term, history-dependent dynamics (via the self-exciting kernel). The formulation of the learning problem as a convex optimization program is sound. The development of a custom optimization algorithm that is both efficient (scaling linearly and avoiding full SVDs) and theoretically grounded (with an O(1/t) convergence guarantee) is a significant contribution.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and supports the paper's claims. The authors use one large-scale synthetic dataset to validate the algorithm's convergence, scalability, and ability to recover ground-truth parameters. The evaluation on three diverse real-world datasets (music, e-commerce, and healthcare) demonstrates the model's broad applicability. I particularly appreciated the use of the time-change theorem (Figure 3) to empirically validate the core assumption that a Hawkes process fits the data better than simpler survival models; this adds significant confidence in the modeling choices.

A minor weakness is the practical handling of the penalty parameter ρ from the alternative formulation in Eq. 5. Theorem 1 guarantees equivalence for a sufficiently large ρ, but its theoretical value is intractable. The paper notes it is tuned via cross-validation, which is standard practice, but a discussion on the model's sensitivity to this hyperparameter would have strengthened the paper.

**Clarity**
The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, and the necessary background on temporal point processes is provided concisely. The model is developed logically, starting from a single user-item pair and building up to the full low-rank formulation. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the high-level intuition behind the combination of proximal and conditional gradient methods is easy to follow. The figures and tables are informative and effectively communicate the key results. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand the contributions, though replicating the exact experimental setup might require access to the code due to the number of hyperparameters involved.

**Originality**
The paper's primary contribution is the novel synthesis of two distinct fields: recommender systems (specifically low-rank collaborative filtering) and temporal point processes (specifically Hawkes processes). While Hawkes processes have been used to model other sequential phenomena like information diffusion (e.g., Zhou et al., AISTATS 2013), their application to the general time-sensitive recommendation problem in this low-rank matrix framework is new and insightful. This approach provides a continuous-time alternative to tensor factorization methods, which typically require discretizing time into arbitrary intervals. The optimization algorithm, which blends proximal and conditional gradient methods for a problem with both non-negativity and nuclear norm constraints, also appears to be a novel contribution of potential independent interest. Related work is adequately cited and distinguished from the proposed approach.

**Significance**
The paper addresses a problem of high practical and research importance. As services become more integrated into users' daily routines, moving beyond static "top-N" recommendations to predict *what* and *when* is a critical next step. The proposed "Hawkes-Recommender" provides a powerful and flexible framework for this. The demonstrated scalability to hundreds of millions of events suggests it is viable for real-world systems. The results are strong and convincingly advance the state-of-the-art on the tasks of time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction. This work is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research at the intersection of point processes and recommender systems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Formulation:** The combination of low-rank models and Hawkes processes is an elegant and powerful new approach for time-sensitive recommendation.
2.  **Strong Technical Contributions:** The paper contributes a novel model, a convex learning formulation, an efficient custom optimization algorithm with convergence guarantees, and a thorough empirical validation.
3.  **High Significance:** The paper tackles an important, under-explored problem with a practical and scalable solution that significantly outperforms relevant baselines.
4.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and technically sound.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The practical choice of the key parameter ρ is based on tuning rather than the provided theory, and its sensitivity is not discussed.
2.  **Baseline Comparison:** The discussion could have benefited from a deeper analysis of why the simple Poisson baseline performs competitively on the ranking task, which would better delineate the scenarios where the added complexity of the Hawkes model is most crucial.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a complete and compelling piece of research with novel contributions in modeling, optimization, and application. I recommend it for acceptance.
