Summary
The paper addresses two time-sensitive recommendation problems: (1) ranking items for a user at a particular time, and (2) predicting a user’s next return time to an item/service. It models each observed user–item interaction sequence as a univariate self-exciting Hawkes process with exponential triggering kernel. It transfers information to unseen user–item pairs by assuming the matrices of base intensities and self-excitation coefficients (Λ0 and A) are low-rank and learned via nuclear norm regularization. The resulting likelihood is convex in the nonnegative parameters, leading to a convex optimization with nuclear-norm penalties.

To solve the problem at scale, the authors introduce an auxiliary-variable formulation and an algorithm blending proximal gradient (for nonnegativity-constrained smooth part) and conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe) steps for the nuclear norms. They establish an O(1/t) convergence rate for the penalized problem and claim an equivalence to the original constrained formulation when a penalty parameter ρ exceeds a problem-dependent threshold. Empirically, they show improved performance on both tasks over Poisson, STiC (survival analysis/HMM), SVD, and Poisson tensor factorization baselines, on large synthetic data and three real datasets (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC II).

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on temporal point processes and Hawkes processes (Hawkes; Daley & Vere-Jones), survival analysis-based return time prediction (Kapoor et al.), and low-rank models for recommendation (Koren; tensor approaches). It is also related to low-rank/multivariate Hawkes estimation (Zhou, Zha, Song) but focuses on self-excitation per user–item pair and low-rank structure across the dyadic parameter matrices for recommendation.

Quality
Strengths:
- Modeling: The log-likelihood for Hawkes with exponential kernel is concave in the nonnegative parameters, so the proposed estimation with nuclear norms is a principled convex program. The use of intensity for ranking and survival density for return-time prediction is coherent.
- Algorithmics: The proximal/conditional-gradient hybrid avoids full SVDs, requiring only top singular vectors per iteration, and has a clear computational advantage for large matrices. The convergence analysis for the penalized objective is sound and yields an O(1/t) rate.
- Empirics: Large-scale experiments (up to 100M events) demonstrate scalability and improved predictive performance on both tasks, with meaningful datasets across domains.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Exact-penalty claim (Theorem 1): The paper asserts that the auxiliary quadratic-penalty formulation becomes equivalent to the original constrained problem for sufficiently large finite ρ. Quadratic penalties are generally not exact; equivalence typically holds only asymptotically as ρ→∞ or within augmented Lagrangian frameworks. The stated threshold depends on unknown optimal solutions, and without convincing proof this is a notable theoretical gap. The convergence theorem pertains to the penalized problem; the leap to the original objective is contingent on Theorem 1.
- Stability/branching ratio: Hawkes stability (e.g., α∫γ<1 per pair, for exponential kernel α·σ<1) is not discussed or enforced. Without this, processes can be supercritical, affecting interpretability and potentially estimation.
- Limited temporal dependencies: The model captures self-excitation within a user–item pair but not cross-excitation across items or within-user activity types, which can be important in recommendation (e.g., substitutable/complementary items). Prior Hawkes network work could serve as a stronger comparator or an extension baseline.
- Return-time prediction: Using Ogata’s thinning to sample from f(t|history) for each test case can be computationally heavy; there is no analysis of test-time cost or calibration (e.g., QQ plots beyond single sequences, metrics like CRPS or likelihood).
- Baselines: Comparisons omit stronger time-aware recommenders prevalent at NIPS and RecSys (e.g., timeSVD++, time-varying MF with smooth temporal factors) and point-process-based recommenders (if available). Tensor baselines rely on discretization; renewal or autoregressive temporal models are not included.
- Clarity and presentation: There are typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., φ and ψ definitions, stray references), the step-size/Lipschitz constant selection is not detailed, and hyperparameter tuning/validation protocol is under-specified. The held-out protocol tests unseen pairs but not true item or user cold-start.

Clarity
Overall understandable with clear motivation, model, and algorithm structure. However, the exposition would benefit from:
- A careful, rigorous statement and proof (or correction) of Theorem 1, and practical guidance for choosing ρ.
- Discussion of Hawkes stability and how it is handled.
- More explicit computational complexity per iteration and at test time for return-time prediction.
- Improved notation consistency and correction of typos.

Originality
The coupling of Hawkes processes with low-rank nuclear-norm regularization for time-sensitive recommendation, together with a scalable hybrid optimization scheme, is novel relative to the cited literature. While related ideas exist in low-rank Hawkes estimation and time-aware recommendation, the specific formulation and application are sufficiently distinct.

Significance
The problems addressed are important for modern recommender systems. If the optimization is indeed convex and scalable as claimed, and the empirical gains generalize, the work could influence temporal recommendation modeling and spur further integration of point processes into recommendation. The theoretical flaw around the penalty equivalence tempers the impact unless resolved.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and relevant problem formulation linking recommendation and temporal point processes.
- Convex learning objective with a practical, scalable solver and nontrivial convergence analysis.
- Strong empirical performance and scale on multiple datasets.
- Potentially useful general optimization template for nonnegative low-rank models with convex losses.

Cons for acceptance
- Questionable exact-penalty theorem; the connection back to the original constrained problem is not convincingly established.
- Missing stability constraints for Hawkes processes.
- Limited comparisons to strong time-aware recommendation baselines and lack of cross-excitation modeling.
- Some clarity issues and missing details on hyperparameter selection and test-time efficiency.

Suggestions for improvement
- Revisit Theorem 1: either provide a rigorous proof of exactness or position the method as solving a smoothed surrogate (Moreau envelope) and adjust claims accordingly; alternatively, consider an augmented Lagrangian/ADMM variant with provable convergence that avoids full SVD via randomized or partial proximal steps.
- Enforce or discuss Hawkes stability (e.g., constraints on α and σ) and its practical implications.
- Add stronger baselines (e.g., timeSVD++, time-varying MF) and, if possible, a mutually exciting Hawkes variant to assess the value of cross-item excitation.
- Provide ablations on kernel choice/bandwidth, effect of ρ, λ, β, and report test-time cost for return-time prediction and calibration metrics.
- Improve notation and proofreading.
