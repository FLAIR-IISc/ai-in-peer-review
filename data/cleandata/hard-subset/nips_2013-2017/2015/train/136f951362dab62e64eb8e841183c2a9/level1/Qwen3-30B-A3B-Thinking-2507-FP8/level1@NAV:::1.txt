

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendations by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption. The authors address two key questions: how to recommend the most relevant item at the right moment, and how to predict the next returning time of users. They establish a convex optimization formulation that combines Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix constraints, and develop an efficient algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods with O(1/t) convergence rate. The approach significantly differs from prior work like Kapoor et al. (STiC), which uses survival analysis but cannot generalize to recommend new items, and from standard matrix factorization models that ignore temporal dynamics.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with clear theoretical analysis, including convergence proofs and a thorough empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC II). The experiments convincingly show superior predictive performance for both recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks compared to Poisson processes, SVD, and tensor factorization baselines. The scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events is particularly impressive.

The work is highly original in connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank models for recommendation systems, addressing an important gap in the literature. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work and adequately reference relevant NIPS proceedings (e.g., Du et al. on Hawkes processes, Zhou et al. on tensor factorization).

The paper is well-written with clear organization and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The only minor weakness is that the cold-start problem for new users/items isn't explicitly addressed, though the authors mention the framework can incorporate additional contextual information.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in time-sensitive recommendation systems. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates significant practical impact. The proposed optimization algorithm is particularly noteworthy for its theoretical guarantees and scalability.

Strengths: Novel framework, strong theoretical foundation, excellent empirical results, scalable to large datasets.
Weaknesses: Limited discussion of cold-start scenarios, some technical details could be more accessible.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level and makes a clear, important contribution to the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
