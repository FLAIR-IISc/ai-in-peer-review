- Proposes a novel framework connecting self-exciting (Hawkes) point processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive item recommendation and user return-time prediction.  
- Formulates the problem as a convex optimization using low-rank Hawkes processes to capture recurrent temporal patterns.  
- Develops an efficient algorithm combining proximal and conditional gradient methods with O(1/t) convergence, scalable to millions of pairs and hundreds of millions of events.  
- Builds on temporal point process theory and low-rank factorization, extending beyond Kapoor et al. by enabling recommendation of new items.  
- Strength — Technical soundness: rigorous convex formulation and convergence analysis (Theorems 1 & 2).  
- Strength — Scalability: theoretical and empirical evidence of linear scaling to very large datasets.  
- Strength — Comprehensive evaluation: experiments on synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) for recommendation and return-time tasks with multiple baselines.  
- Strength — Practical utility: supports incorporating contextual information and provides clear implementation guidance.  
- Weakness — Related work limited: omits recent temporal recommendation and deep learning approaches (e.g., Xiong et al., other DL papers).  
- Weakness — Experimental design: lacks statistical significance testing (no p-values or confidence intervals).  
- Weakness — Low-rank assumption: insufficient justification for assuming low-rank structure in base intensity and excitation matrices.  
- Weakness — Kernel choice limited: only uses exponential triggering kernel and doesn’t explore alternatives (Weibull, log-logistic).  
- Weakness — Practical limitations under-discussed: handling extreme sparsity, rank selection, and limited hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.  
- Overall assessment: significant and novel contribution with strong theory, scalability, and empirical gains; minor weaknesses do not undermine core contribution.  
- Recommendation: Accept — valuable addition to the conference.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel Hawkes–low-rank connection, rigorous guarantees, demonstrated scalability, practical utility, and comprehensive experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited comparisons to deep learning, no significance testing, weak low-rank justification, and limited kernel exploration.
