- Proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework modeling each user–item pair as a 1-D Hawkes process with baseline + history-dependent excitation (exponential kernel by default).  
- Imposes low-rank structure on baseline Λ0 and excitation A via nuclear-norm regularization, yielding a convex estimation problem for transfer across unobserved pairs.  
- Develops a hybrid optimization algorithm combining proximal gradient (nonnegativity) and conditional gradient (nuclear norm) through penalty-based splitting with auxiliary variables.  
- Proves that for sufficiently large penalty ρ the penalized problem is equivalent to the constrained one and establishes an O(1/t) convergence rate.  
- Empirical results: outperforms Poisson, SVD/tensor factorization variants, and STiC on time-sensitive item recommendation and next return-time prediction across synthetic (~100M events) and three real datasets.  
- Connects to prior Hawkes and point-process convex learning work but differs by enforcing low-rank sharing across user–item pairs to enable recommendation for unseen pairs.  
- Contrasts with survival/semi-Markov and temporal MF/tensor methods by modeling continuous-time recurrent self-excitation and full event history dependence.  
- Optimization novelty: penalty-splitting to decouple nonnegativity and nuclear norms is a practical engineering adaptation of proximal/Frank-Wolfe ideas.  
- Strength: well-motivated unified model for ranking and return-time prediction in continuous time.  
- Strength: convex formulation with interpretable parameters separating long-term preference and short-term recency.  
- Strength: scalable algorithm avoiding full SVDs, using top singular vectors and simple projections, with theoretical guarantees.  
- Strength: demonstrated scalability on large synthetic data and consistent improvements on multiple real datasets.  
- Strength: thoughtful use of time-change diagnostics to validate Hawkes assumptions on real data.  
- Weakness: modeling treats each pair independently and omits mutual-excitation across items/users, limiting capture of substitutes/complements or social spillovers.  
- Weakness: no explicit stability/subcriticality constraint (branching ratio <1) or discussion of identifiability between Λ0 and A.  
- Weakness: Theorem 1 depends on unknown ρ*; paper lacks sensitivity analysis for ρ and discussion of numerical stability/conditioning.  
- Weakness: baselines and metrics could be stronger—missing temporal baselines (timeSVD++, FPMC), standard ranking metrics (Recall@k, NDCG@k), and proper scoring rules for return-time (log-likelihood, CRPS).  
- Weakness: experimental details lacking on hyperparameter selection, kernel bandwidth, computational/prediction costs (thinning/Monte Carlo), wall-clock times, memory, and iteration counts.  
- Weakness: claims about incorporating side information lack supporting ablations demonstrating gains from contextual features.  
- Quality: modeling and convex formulation are technically sound, but some claims about novelty/rates may be overstated relative to existing hybrid methods.  
- Clarity: overall clear and organized; notation and algorithm presentation can be streamlined and per-iteration complexity/Lipschitz constants should be defined.  
- Originality: low-rank Hawkes formulation and convex scalable solver are novel, though related optimization ideas exist.  
- Significance: addresses an important problem of predicting both what and when; likely to impact recommendation and temporal modeling communities.  
- Arguments for acceptance: principled continuous-time model, convex scalable algorithm with convergence guarantees, strong large-scale empirical results, and practical diagnostics.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited mutual-excitation modeling, dependence on tuned penalty ρ without principled selection, and weaker baselines/ablations.  
- Questions for authors: do you enforce stability constraints or report branching ratios; how sensitive are results to ρ, λ, β, and kernel bandwidth; what are runtimes/memory on largest dataset; can you report Recall@k/NDCG@k and return-time NLL; have you tried time-of-day/weekly seasonality in Λ0(t)?  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept.
