- Proposes a novel framework connecting Hawkes (self-exciting) point processes with low-rank matrix models for time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction.  
- Addresses two problems: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and predicting users' next returning time.  
- Formulates a convex optimization problem leveraging the Hawkes–low-rank connection and enforces non-negativity and nuclear-norm regularization.  
- Develops an efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods.  
- Provides theoretical convergence analysis proving an O(1/t) rate.  
- Demonstrates superior empirical performance on synthetic data and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II).  
- Shows scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events with linear scaling behavior.  
- Paper is technically sound with well-supported claims and thorough derivations.  
- Writing is clear, well-organized, and reproducible; notation and experimental details are well presented.  
- Original contribution: first to establish low-rank structure for Hawkes-based recommendation and a convex scalable formulation.  
- Optimization approach is novel in combining proximal and conditional gradient methods to balance convergence and constraints.  
- Significant practical implications for marketing, healthcare, and user engagement through time-sensitive recommendations and return-time prediction.  
- Strengths include theoretical rigor, efficient algorithm, comprehensive experiments, scalability, and clear contrast with prior work.  
- Weaknesses: reliance on exponential triggering kernel without exploring alternatives.  
- Weaknesses: limited diversity of real-world datasets; could include social media or news domains.  
- Weaknesses: computational-complexity analysis lacks detail beyond convergence rate.  
- Weaknesses: insufficient treatment of cold-start scenarios for new users/items.  
- Weaknesses: comparisons with the most recent point-process methods (e.g., Zhou et al., ICML 2016) are incomplete.  
- Arguments for acceptance: meaningful gap addressed, novel convex formulation and algorithm, strong empirical results, practical impact, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: arbitrary kernel choice, need for broader datasets and more recent-method comparisons, and unresolved cold-start handling.  
- Recommendation: recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to NIPS, with minor concerns about kernels and evaluations.
