Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models each user–item interaction stream as a self-exciting Hawkes process with an intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) Σj γ(t, tu,ij), where Λ0 encodes a long-term propensity and A(u,i) scales self-excitation. To generalize beyond observed pairs and enable recommendations at arbitrary times, the authors impose low-rank structure on Λ0 and A via nuclear-norm regularization, yielding a convex estimation problem. They introduce an optimization method that combines proximal gradient (for nonnegativity constraints) with a conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe) step for nuclear norms via an auxiliary-variable penalty that decouples constraints and regularizers. They prove an O(1/t) convergence rate and demonstrate scalability to large synthetic data, with additional experiments on last.fm, tmall, and MIMIC II. Two tasks are considered: (a) item ranking at a given time using current intensities; (b) return-time prediction from the conditional time-to-event density.

The work connects temporal point process modeling—e.g., Hawkes processes (Hawkes 1971; Daley & Vere-Jones)—with collaborative filtering via low-rank structure. Prior Hawkes work in machine learning has focused on multivariate/mutually-exciting processes and learning influence networks (e.g., Zhou et al., AISTATS/ICML), and survival analysis-based return-time prediction (Kapoor et al., KDD/WSDM). In recommender systems, time-aware matrix/tensor factorization and Poisson-based implicit feedback models are common baselines. The paper’s formulation is novel in casting time-sensitive recommendation as a low-rank Hawkes estimation problem with a convex program and scalable optimization.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear modeling rationale: Λ0 captures long-term preference; self-excitation captures recency/repeat usage. The likelihood admits a convex formulation when parameters are constrained to be nonnegative.
- Optimization contribution: the penalty-based decoupling and the hybrid proximal/conditional gradient scheme is elegant and practical; the oracle uses only top singular vectors and has closed-form linesearch scalars. The convergence bound is carefully stated.
- Large-scale synthetic evaluation and three real datasets. The synthetic setup tests convergence and sample efficiency; the real-data Q–Q analysis via time-change is appropriate to justify Hawkes vs. i.i.d. gap models.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Modeling independence across items: λu,i(t) depends only on the history of (u,i). This ignores cross-excitation among items (e.g., listening to artist A increases intensity for similar artist B) or competition/inhibition. Multidimensional Hawkes with cross-kernels is a natural and known extension; not considering it limits recommendation power and undercuts claims of “at the right moment,” since recent activity on other items does not affect current ranking.
- Stability and identifiability: For Hawkes with exponential kernels, stability requires A(u,i)·∫γ < 1 (branching ratio < 1). The paper does not impose or discuss this constraint; without it, the fitted processes can be supercritical. Also, the scale tradeoff among A, Λ0, and kernel bandwidth σ can cause identifiability issues; using a single global σ further aggravates misspecification. At minimum, add explicit stability constraints or a projection, and discuss identifiability.
- Return-time evaluation: MAE is not a proper scoring rule for time-to-event prediction; likelihood or CRPS would be preferable. The Monte Carlo approximation (Ogata thinning) for expected return time is computationally heavy and unnecessary—median-based or mode-based approximations, or direct likelihood evaluation, could be used. Report runtime and variance for the MC estimates.
- Baselines: Missing strong time-aware recommenders and point-process baselines. In particular: (i) dynamic/factorized Poisson or gamma-Poisson implicit-feedback models; (ii) timeSVD++ style dynamic MF; (iii) multivariate Hawkes (with item-item coupling) as a predictor of item rankings and return times. Comparing only to Poisson, tensor Poisson regression, SVD, and STiC is insufficient for a NIPS audience circa 2015. Also report standard ranking metrics (Recall@k, NDCG@k), not just “rank of ground-truth item.”
- Optimization theory: The equivalence theorem between the constrained and penalized formulations requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima. In practice ρ is tuned. Please discuss a continuation strategy or bounds on suboptimality if ρ < ρ*, and provide sensitivity analyses.
- Practicalities: The Lipschitz assumption for ∇f may fail near zero intensity (log(w⊤φ)); numerical stability requires lower-bounding λ by ε. This is not discussed. Also report wall-clock times and hardware for large-scale runs; top singular vector computations per-iteration may be a bottleneck.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and the main ideas are clear. Some notation/typo issues (e.g., Section 3.3 references “feature ψu,i in 3” and has formatting glitches) should be fixed. The experimental protocol should be described more precisely (how many negatives per user, how rankings are computed across all items, cold-start setup). Provide hyperparameter choices, kernel bandwidth selection strategy, and standard deviations over multiple splits.

Originality
The core idea of combining Hawkes processes with low-rank structure for recommendation and return-time prediction is original and timely, as is the convex formulation. The optimization scheme is a neat contribution, though related to known proximal and Frank–Wolfe hybrids; positioning relative to recent conditional gradient variants would help. The modeling choice to restrict to self-excitation per user–item pair is conservative relative to prior multivariate Hawkes work.

Significance
If strengthened, this line can impact time-sensitive recommendation and retention modeling. The current empirical gains are promising but the limited baselines and metrics weaken the case. The optimization method could be useful beyond this application.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled convex formulation linking Hawkes processes and low-rank recommendation.
- Scalable algorithm with a theoretically grounded convergence rate and simple oracles.
- Addresses two important tasks (time-aware ranking and return-time prediction) with one model.
- Solid synthetic study and persuasive Q–Q diagnostics on real data.

Cons for acceptance
- Modeling limitations (no cross-excitation; no stability constraints; global kernel) reduce realism and potentially performance.
- Incomplete experimental evaluation: limited baselines and metrics; lack of likelihood-based assessments and runtime details.
- Theoretical equivalence relies on an unobservable ρ*; practical guidance is missing.
- Clarity issues and minor typos; limited discussion of identifiability and numerical stability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Extend to a low-rank multivariate Hawkes with item–item (or latent-topic) cross-excitation, or at least analyze whether current-history-only suffices.
- Enforce stability (e.g., A(u,i) ≤ c) and discuss identifiability and the role of σ; consider learning pair-specific kernels or mixtures.
- Add strong baselines and standard ranking metrics (Recall@k/NDCG@k); report log-likelihood for time-to-event predictions.
- Provide runtime/hardware details and sensitivity to ρ, λ, β; consider a continuation scheme for ρ.
- Add an ε-floor to intensities and discuss numerical stability.

Overall, this is a promising paper with a solid optimization contribution and a clear problem formulation, but it needs stronger empirical comparisons and attention to modeling details to reach NIPS standards.
