

**Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"**

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation that models time-varying user preferences and predicts users' next-return time by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models. The authors develop a convex optimization formulation and an efficient algorithm with O(1/t) convergence rate, demonstrated to scale to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events. The method is evaluated on synthetic data and two real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, and MIMIC II medical data).

The paper builds on related work by Kapoor et al. (KDD'14, WSDM'13) who used survival analysis for return-time prediction, but correctly identifies the limitation that their approach cannot recommend new items. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models represents a novel combination of techniques for recommendation systems.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with well-justified convergence guarantees (Theorem 2). The optimization algorithm effectively blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving optimal convergence rates. The scalability claims are well-supported by experimental results on large datasets. The method demonstrates clear improvements over several baselines in both recommendation and return-time prediction tasks.

**Weaknesses:** The paper suffers from several critical shortcomings. First, it fails to convincingly justify why time-varying preferences are widespread (a key assumption) or provide dataset analysis to identify causes (e.g., whether new popular songs drive patterns). Second, the motivation for predicting next-return time is unclear - the "ad bidding" argument is superficial and lacks concrete examples of practical implementation in web services. Third, the paper omits simple baselines for return-time prediction (e.g., last time delay, most frequent delay, coarser delay categories) that would establish a meaningful baseline for comparison. Fourth, the experimental results lack clarity on the practical significance of improvements over SVD and popular-item baselines - it's unclear whether the gains are modest or substantial enough to be user-perceivable.

Additional issues include a typo ("abel"→"able") and confusing, unsupported examples of retweets/link-creation in Section 2 without references. The paper claims to tackle two important problems but lacks real-world motivation, sufficient baselines, and deeper analysis to demonstrate their practical value.

**Quality:** The technical content is sound with strong theoretical guarantees for the optimization algorithm. However, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient baselines and deeper analysis to fully support the claims about practical significance.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured but contains confusing elements (Section 2 examples) and a typo that undermine clarity. The motivation for the return-time prediction task is particularly unclear.

**Originality:** The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation is novel. The optimization algorithm blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods in a non-trivial way, representing a genuine contribution.

**Significance:** The significance is questionable due to the lack of convincing real-world use cases and insufficient analysis of practical impact. The paper fails to answer "why should practitioners care" for either of its two proposed tasks.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models
- Strong theoretical guarantees for optimization algorithm
- Good scalability demonstrated on large datasets
- Clear comparison to relevant baselines (Poisson, SVD, Tensor factorization)

*Con:*
- Lack of real-world motivation for return-time prediction task
- Missing simple baselines for return-time prediction
- Insufficient analysis of time-varying preferences' prevalence
- Unclear practical impact of improvements over baselines
- Presentation issues (typo, confusing examples)

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a technically sound approach with a novel convex optimization framework for time-sensitive recommendation. However, it falls short in addressing the practical relevance of its contributions. The lack of convincing real-world motivation, insufficient baselines, and unclear demonstration of practical significance prevent it from making a compelling contribution to the field. While the technical approach is interesting, the paper fails to demonstrate why this method should be adopted over existing approaches in practice.

I recommend rejection as the paper is technically sound but lacks the real-world relevance and thorough analysis expected for NIPS. The marginal improvements demonstrated in experiments, without clear practical significance, do not justify acceptance at this venue.
