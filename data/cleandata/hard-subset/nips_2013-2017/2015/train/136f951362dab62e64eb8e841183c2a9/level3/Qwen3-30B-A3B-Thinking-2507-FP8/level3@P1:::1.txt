

The paper proposes a novel framework combining self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix models to address two time-sensitive recommendation tasks: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) predicting users' next-return time. The authors establish a connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models, developing a convex optimization formulation with an efficient algorithm achieving O(1/t) convergence rate. The approach is evaluated on synthetic data and two real-world datasets (last.fm music streaming logs and tmall.com shopping data).

Strengths include a technically sound theoretical contribution with a well-justified convex formulation and convergence analysis. The optimization algorithm, which blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, is novel and well-motivated, with demonstrated scalability to large datasets (hundreds of millions of events). The experimental results show superior predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art baselines on both synthetic and real data.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses in motivation, evaluation, and significance. The practical motivation for predicting next-return time is insufficiently justified; while the paper mentions "marketers could bid on time slots," it fails to provide concrete business cases demonstrating measurable value. For example, how much would a web company save by predicting return times, and how does this compare to simpler alternatives? The paper lacks analysis showing why time-varying preferences are widespread in the datasets—does the temporal pattern stem from new popular songs, user habits, or other factors? Without such analysis, the necessity of the approach remains unproven.

The experimental evaluation is weakened by missing baselines. For return-time prediction, the paper omits simple baselines like "last time delay" or "most frequent delay," making it impossible to assess whether the improvements over SVD and Poisson process are meaningful. The paper also fails to address whether the predictive improvements are substantial enough to be user-perceivable in real applications. The performance gains over SVD appear modest in the results, but the paper doesn't quantify whether these improvements translate to tangible user engagement benefits.

Presentation issues further undermine the paper. A typo ("abel" instead of "able") appears in the abstract, and section 2 introduces confusing examples about retweets/link-creation without proper references or justification. The transition to these examples feels abrupt and unsupported.

While the technical contribution is solid—connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank models is novel—the paper's significance is not convincingly demonstrated. The related work section adequately distinguishes from Kapoor et al.'s STiC work but doesn't sufficiently address why their approach is superior beyond the technical framework.

The paper's strengths lie in its theoretical novelty and computational efficiency, but its weaknesses in practical motivation, baseline comparisons, and significance analysis prevent it from being a strong contribution to NIPS. The authors should provide more concrete evidence of business impact, include missing baselines for return-time prediction, and conduct deeper analysis of the datasets to justify the necessity of time-sensitive recommendations.

PROS: Novel theoretical connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models, efficient optimization algorithm with strong convergence guarantees, good scalability demonstrated on large datasets.

CONS: Weak practical motivation with insufficient business case for return-time prediction, missing simple baselines for return-time prediction, unclear significance of performance improvements over SVD and Poisson process, presentation issues including typo and unsupported examples.

Overall, this is a technically competent paper with a solid theoretical contribution, but it lacks sufficient justification for its practical value. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the motivation and evaluation to make a compelling case for significance. As currently written, the paper makes an incremental contribution without clearly demonstrating its real-world impact.
