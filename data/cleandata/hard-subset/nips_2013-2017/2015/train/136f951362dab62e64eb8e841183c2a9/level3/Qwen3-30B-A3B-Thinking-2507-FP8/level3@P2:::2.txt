

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel framework that combines self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to address time-sensitive recommendation problems. The authors model user-item consumption patterns as low-rank Hawkes processes, where the intensity function captures both baseline user preferences (Λ₀) and self-exciting effects from past interactions (A). The key contribution is a convex optimization formulation with an efficient algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence rate. The approach addresses two important questions: (1) how to recommend the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) how to predict the next returning time of users.

The paper builds on recent work in temporal recommendation, particularly extending the Hawkes process literature (Hawkes, 1971; Du et al., 2015) to the recommendation setting. It relates to Kaplan et al. (2014, 2015) who use survival analysis for user return time prediction, but the current work is more general as it can recommend new items rather than being limited to observed items. The paper also connects to temporal collaborative filtering methods (Koren, 2008; Xiong et al., 2012) but with a fundamentally different point process perspective.

**Quality:** The theoretical foundation is sound, with a well-motivated convex optimization formulation and solid convergence analysis (Theorem 2). The algorithm is well-designed and scales to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs). However, the empirical evaluation lacks important details: the paper doesn't explain how the bandwidth parameter σ for the triggering kernel is set, and there's no sensitivity analysis for this key parameter. Hyperparameter tuning details for both the proposed method and baselines are missing, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the results. The authors should include a sensible baseline that combines STiC (which handles temporal dynamics) and SVD (which captures user/item preferences), as these methods have complementary strengths.

**Clarity:** The paper contains numerous language issues, typos, and grammatical errors that impede readability. Key sections, particularly the low-rank modeling in Section 3.2, are unclear. The paper also lacks sufficient dataset characterization relevant to temporal modeling (e.g., number of recurring events per user/item pair, temporal irregularity). These omissions make it challenging to fully understand the problem setting and the model's applicability.

**Originality:** The combination of self-exciting point processes with low-rank models for recommendation is novel and well-motivated. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work, particularly highlighting how their method overcomes the limitations of survival analysis-based approaches (Kapoor et al., 2014, 2015). The optimization algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods is also a noteworthy contribution.

**Significance:** The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is increasingly important for modern web services, as the authors correctly note. The method has potential applications in areas like music streaming, retail, and healthcare. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the incomplete experimental evaluation and unclear hyperparameter settings, which limit the confidence in the claimed superior performance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel integration of point processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation
- Solid theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees
- Demonstrated scalability to large datasets
- Clear problem statement with important real-world applications

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Missing hyperparameter tuning details and sensitivity analysis
- Insufficient baseline comparison (no ensemble of STiC and SVD)
- Poor clarity due to language issues and missing dataset characterization
- Experiments lack thoroughness in evaluating key aspects of the model

The paper presents an interesting approach to time-sensitive recommendation with a solid theoretical foundation. However, the current presentation lacks clarity and sufficient experimental details to fully assess the contribution. The authors should address the missing hyperparameter details, provide sensitivity analysis for key parameters like σ, include the suggested baseline comparison, and improve the paper's language and structure. With these improvements, this could be a valuable contribution to the field.

Overall, the paper has reasonable quality but falls short of the high standards expected at NIPS due to clarity issues and incomplete experimental evaluation. I recommend rejection, but with the strong encouragement that the authors address these concerns in a revised version. The core idea has merit, but the current presentation does not sufficiently demonstrate its significance and novelty.
