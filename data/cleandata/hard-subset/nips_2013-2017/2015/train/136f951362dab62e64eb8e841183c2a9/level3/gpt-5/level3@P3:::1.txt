Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models (i) time-varying user-item affinities and (ii) users’ next-return time. Each user–item pair is modeled as a self-exciting Hawkes process whose intensity is the sum of a nonnegative base rate and a history-dependent trigger, and knowledge is shared across pairs via low-rank structure imposed on the base-intensity and self-excitation parameter matrices (Λ0 and A). The resulting negative log-likelihood with nuclear-norm penalties is convex. The authors develop a blended proximal/conditional-gradient algorithm with O(1/t) convergence to handle the nonnegativity constraints and nuclear norms efficiently, and evaluate on one large synthetic dataset and two modest real datasets (music listening and e-commerce; a brief medical example is discussed). Compared to Poisson, tensor factorization, SVD, and survival analysis baselines, the method improves item ranking at time t and reduces MAE of return-time prediction.

The work connects point-process modeling (Hawkes; e.g., Hawkes 1971; Zhou, Zha, Song AISTATS/ICML) with low-rank structure (nuclear norm; e.g., Yu et al., NIPS) in a recommender setting. Return-time prediction has been studied with survival models for streaming services (Kapoor et al., KDD/WSDM). The paper’s novelty lies in the convex, low-rank Hawkes formulation for user–item dynamics and the tailored optimization scheme.

Quality
Strengths
- Sound modeling: with exponential kernels the log-likelihood is concave in linear parameters, so the overall objective with nuclear norms is convex.
- Optimization is well-motivated and appears efficient; the separation of nonnegativity and nuclear-norm terms via auxiliary variables and LMO is neat, with clear convergence guarantees.
- The framework naturally yields both time-aware ranking and a return-time predictive density.
- Synthetic experiments demonstrate scalability and recovery under low-rank ground truth.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation on modest-scale real data; impact versus simple popularity/SVD baselines is unclear in magnitude and user-perceivability.
- The claim that user preferences are meaningfully time-varying and self-exciting is not substantiated by dataset analysis. The QQ-plot style diagnostic is a good start but does not quantify prevalence or causes across the population.
- Return-time motivation is underspecified; how will web companies operationalize a per-pair return-time predictor?
- Missing simple heuristics for return-time as baselines (last observed delay; mode of delays; circadian Poisson), and no exploration of coarser delay categories that may be more actionable and easier to predict.
- Statistical significance and effect sizes are not reported.

Clarity
- Generally clear, with helpful background on point processes. Some presentation issues: a typo “abel”→“able”; section 2 abruptly switches to retweets/link-creation examples without references or tying back to the recommender setting; some notation is dense (e.g., block variables X1/X2) and constants in Theorem 2 (L, D1, D2) are not instantiated.
- The return-time estimation via thinning would benefit from details on sample size, variance, and computational cost; numerical quadrature or inversion alternatives could be discussed.

Originality
- The combination of low-rank user–item parameterization with Hawkes processes in a convex formulation for recommender tasks is novel to my knowledge circa NIPS 2015.
- The blended proximal/conditional-gradient scheme under nonnegativity and nuclear norm is a useful contribution.

Significance
- Potentially significant if gains over strong practical baselines are demonstrated and if the return-time component has clear applications. As presented, gains vs. popularity/SVD/tensor are not contextualized (e.g., lift at k, fraction of users helped), and real-world use cases are not tied to business metrics.

Detailed comments and suggestions
On time-varying preferences and self-excitation
- Provide population-level evidence: for each user, compute Kendall’s tau or Spearman correlation between item rankings across time windows; report distribution of drift. Measure fraction of pairs where αu,i>0 significantly (with CIs via bootstrap) and the magnitude relative to λu,i0.
- Disentangle drivers: include item-age/novelty covariates (e.g., time since release) to test if recency of new popular items explains effects; analyze daily/weekly seasonality (add time-of-day/day-of-week baselines).
- Conduct ablations: (i) base-only (Poisson), (ii) self-excitation-only, (iii) full model; report deltas.

On return-time motivation and evaluation
- Clarify concrete use cases: push notification timing, re-engagement campaigns, ad auction time-slot bidding, content prefetch/cache, medical follow-up scheduling. For at least one domain, map MAE (or categorical accuracy) to a plausible business metric (e.g., notification sent within ±Δ hours).
- Add simple baselines: last inter-arrival; median/mode per (u,i); user-level typical delay; circadian inhomogeneous Poisson (sinusoidal λ(t)); kernel density on gaps; renewal (Weibull/Rayleigh) per (u,i).
- Consider discretizing return-time into 3–4 bins (short/medium/long/very long) and evaluate as classification with accuracy/F1, calibration (Brier score), and expected utility under a simple cost model.

On recommendation evaluation
- Report standard top-k metrics (Hit@k, NDCG@k, Recall@k) at multiple k and per-user improvements; include a Popularity baseline and SVDpop (SVD + popularity prior).
- Provide statistical significance (paired bootstrap/Wilcoxon) and effect sizes; show the distribution over users, not only means.
- Clarify if improvements are user-perceivable: e.g., lift@k over Popularity.

On methodology
- Returning-time estimation: report number of thinning samples m and CIs; discuss computational burden vs. a numerical integration approach for exponential kernels.
- Optimization: include wall-clock vs. ADMM/Frank–Wolfe baselines, memory footprint, and sensitivity to ρ with a principled selection rule.
- Consider evaluating a nonparametric Hawkes kernel baseline (e.g., Zhou et al., ICML) to test robustness to kernel misspecification.

Presentation and minor issues
- Fix “abel”→“able”; smooth grammar (e.g., “Choose to initialize…”).
- Provide references when switching to retweet/link-creation examples; otherwise keep focus on the recommender setting for coherence.
- Define all constants in Theorem 2 and give practical values; add pseudocode comments and consistent notation; specify time units for each dataset.

Pros
- Convex, principled formulation linking Hawkes processes with low-rank user–item structure.
- Efficient algorithm with clear convergence guarantees and strong scalability on synthetic data.
- Addresses two practically relevant tasks within one framework.

Cons
- Limited real-world motivation for return-time and lack of actionable evaluation.
- Missing simple and strong baselines; unclear statistical and practical significance of gains.
- Insufficient analysis to justify prevalence and causes of time-varying preferences/self-excitation.
- Presentation issues and abrupt cross-domain examples without references.

Overall assessment
Borderline (OK/marginal). The paper presents a solid, original modeling and optimization contribution, but the empirical section needs stronger baselines, clearer practical motivation (especially for return time), statistical significance, and deeper analysis of temporal effects. Strengthening these aspects would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
