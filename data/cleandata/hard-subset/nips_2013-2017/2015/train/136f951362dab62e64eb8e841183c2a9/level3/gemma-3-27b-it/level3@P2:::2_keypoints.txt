- Proposes a novel framework combining self-exciting Hawkes point processes with low-rank matrix factorization to model recurrent user-item temporal interactions.
- Models user activity as a Hawkes process with low-rank factors for generalization across users and items.
- Derives a convex optimization formulation and an efficient algorithm with provable convergence guarantees.
- Demonstrates scalability to large datasets (millions of pairs, hundreds of millions of events).
- Shows strong empirical performance on synthetic and real-world datasets, outperforming several baselines including STiC and tensor methods.
- Framework can incorporate additional contextual information, increasing applicability.
- Writing and presentation suffer from clarity issues, typos, and vague phrasing that hinder readability.
- Lacks a thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (e.g., latent dimension, kernel bandwidth σ) and details on how parameters were chosen.
- Baseline comparisons are incomplete; an ensemble of STiC and SVD would be a stronger benchmark.
- Dataset descriptions omit important temporal statistics (e.g., recurring event counts per pair, temporal irregularity) needed to assess model fit.
- Implementation details are insufficient: interaction between proximal gradient and conditional gradient steps and their computational costs are not fully explained.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, scalable approach with strong empirical results and flexibility to include context.
- Arguments against acceptance: poor clarity, insufficient hyperparameter analysis, limited dataset characterization, and missing stronger baselines.
- Overall assessment: promising and solid contribution but not outstanding; requires improvements in clarity, analysis, and empirical validation.
- Recommendation: Weak Accept contingent on fixing clarity issues, adding hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, stronger baseline comparisons, and more dataset/implementation details.
