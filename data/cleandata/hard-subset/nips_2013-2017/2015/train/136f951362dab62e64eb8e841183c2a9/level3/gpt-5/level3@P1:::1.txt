This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models recurrent user-item interactions with self-exciting Hawkes processes and transfers information across all user–item pairs via low-rank structure (nuclear-norm regularization) on the base intensity Λ0 and the self-excitation scale A. The method addresses two tasks: (1) ranking items for a user at any time by the instantaneous intensity λu,i(t) and (2) predicting the next return time via the conditional density implied by the point process (approximated by thinning). The authors derive a convex surrogate and introduce an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient (for nuclear norms), proving an O(1/t) convergence rate and demonstrating scalability to large synthetic data. Experiments on synthetic and multiple real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) compare against Poisson, SVD, tensor factorization with Poisson regression, and a survival/HMM baseline (STiC), reporting improved ranking and return-time MAE. The work relates to prior point-process modeling in NIPS/AISTATS (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song; Du et al.) by focusing on recommender settings and to survival/time-to-event modeling for return time (Kapoor et al.), with the key novelty being the low-rank Hawkes parameterization and an efficient solver.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing around two practically relevant time-sensitive tasks; well-motivated mathematically via Hawkes processes.
- Technically solid convex formulation with nuclear-norm surrogates; clean separation of nonnegativity and rank penalties via a penalty method and auxiliary variables.
- Algorithmic contribution is practical: proximal step for nonnegativity, linear minimization for nuclear norms via top singular vectors, and an accelerated scheme; convergence analysis is provided.
- Scalability appears strong on large synthetic data; real datasets cover diverse domains (media, commerce, clinical).
- The time-rescaling check is a good diagnostic of model fit and appropriately compares Hawkes vs survival assumptions.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Practical utility of next-return-time prediction is under-motivated. Please articulate concrete use cases (e.g., notification timing, ad pacing, inventory allocation, campaign scheduling, clinician alerting) and how prediction error translates into measurable gains (CTR, retention, revenue, intervention timeliness).
- Baselines for return-time prediction are incomplete. Add simple, strong heuristics:
  - Last inter-arrival time (user–item level).
  - Median/most frequent inter-arrival (user–item; user-only; item-only).
  - Seasonality-aware baseline (e.g., time-of-day/week histogram).
  - Category prediction of delay buckets (short/medium/long/very long) with accuracy/F1; many applications need robust coarse timing more than exact MAE.
- Report statistical significance and practical effect sizes. For recommendation, include Top-k hit rate/Recall@k/NDCG and absolute differences vs a popular-item baseline; for return time, report MAE/median AE, calibration plots, and coverage of prediction intervals.
- Provide ablations: base intensity only (Λ0), self-excitation only (A), different kernels (exponential vs Weibull), and a nonparametric kernel (as discussed) to show the source of gains and kernel robustness.
- The penalty parameter ρ is central to equivalence with the constrained problem; while Theorem 1 states a threshold ρ*, guidance for choosing ρ in practice is unclear. Add sensitivity curves and a practical selection heuristic.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized, but some claims and examples are abrupt. In Section 2, examples like retweets/link-creation are introduced without citations; please add references and connect them to the presented intensity forms.
- Define all symbols on first use and keep consistent block notation. Clarify computational cost per iteration: complexity of top singular vector computations, memory footprint for event histories, and how λu,i(t) is computed for ranking across all items at test time (any caching/incremental updates?).
- Fix typos: “abel”→“able”; in the abstract “O(1/ )” presumably “O(1/t)”; ensure all figures/axes are legible and labeled with units.

Originality
- The integration of low-rank structure with Hawkes for recommendation is a useful, moderately novel step beyond prior Hawkes network inference and survival-based return-time models. However, the current model treats each user–item pair as self-exciting only. Many domains exhibit cross-excitation (e.g., artist-to-artist or disease-to-disease influence). Discuss and, if possible, experiment with a low-rank mutually-exciting extension (low-rank factorization of influence tensors or user–item–item kernels).

Significance
- Improvements over strong popularity/SVD/tensor baselines are reported but the paper does not make clear whether the gains are substantial and user-perceivable. Provide absolute metrics and confidence intervals, and an analysis of when time variation matters most (e.g., cold-start, highly seasonal users/items, bursts).
- Include dataset analyses to justify the prevalence of time-varying preferences: quantify seasonality, burstiness/self-excitation (e.g., distribution of inter-arrivals, autocorrelation), and separate effects from global popularity shocks (new song releases) vs individual repeat consumption.

Additional suggestions
- Cold-start analysis: show performance vs number of historical events per user/item, to highlight low-rank transfer benefits.
- Calibration and PIT/time-rescaling: show aggregate results across many sequences, not just a single example.
- Medical domain: caution about clinical use and clarify scope; avoid overstating intervention readiness without prospective validation.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, general methodology with a scalable solver and convergence guarantees.
- Clear advance over survival-only models and static recommenders for time-sensitive tasks.
- Empirical results suggest benefits across domains; diagnostic fit checks are welcome.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical impact and use cases for return-time prediction are underspecified.
- Missing simple but strong baselines and ablations; limited analysis of when and why the model helps.
- Some clarity/presentation issues and minor typos.

Overall assessment: a technically competent paper with promising ideas and an efficient algorithm. With stronger empirical grounding (baselines, ablations, practical metrics) and clearer motivation for return-time prediction, this would be a solid accept; in its current form, I judge it marginal.
