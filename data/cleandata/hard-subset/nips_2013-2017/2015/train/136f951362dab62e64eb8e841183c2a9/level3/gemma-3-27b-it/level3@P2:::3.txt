This paper presents a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation, connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions. The core idea is to model user activity as a Hawkes process, leveraging low-rank approximations to handle scalability and generalize to unseen items. The authors develop a convex optimization formulation and an efficient algorithm with provable convergence guarantees, demonstrating strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The work builds upon prior research in recommender systems, particularly addressing the limitations of traditional methods that often neglect temporal dynamics. It explicitly acknowledges and attempts to overcome the issues of data sparsity and the need to model user engagement beyond simple ratings. Related work in temporal point processes, specifically Hawkes processes, is well-cited and contextualized. The authors rightly point out the limitations of existing approaches like survival analysis [12, 11] in generalizing to new item recommendations. Comparisons are made to matrix factorization [3, 25, 13, 21] and tensor factorization methods [3], positioning the proposed approach as a significant advancement.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling for time-sensitive recommendation is a genuinely novel contribution. This approach offers a principled way to model the complex temporal dependencies in user behavior.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The authors provide a convex formulation of the problem and an algorithm with a proven O(1/t) convergence rate. This adds significant rigor to the work.
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm is designed to scale to large datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events, a crucial factor for real-world applicability.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that the proposed model outperforms state-of-the-art competitors in both item recommendation and returning-time prediction.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details. The motivation and contribution are clearly stated.
*   **Potential Impact:** The ability to predict user returning times has significant implications for applications beyond recommendation, such as ad bidding and proactive healthcare intervention, as discussed in the introduction.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Tuning and ρ:** The discussion around the parameter ρ in Theorem 1, and its subsequent practical handling, feels somewhat unresolved. The paper acknowledges the difficulty of finding the exact threshold ρ* and resorts to empirical tuning, diminishing the practical impact of the theoretical result. The relevance of Theorem 2 is weakened by the fact that the prescribed parameter selection is not implemented in the experiments.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** While the comparisons to existing methods are solid, the inclusion of SVD++ [23] would strengthen the evaluation. SVD++ is a widely used and highly effective collaborative filtering algorithm that explicitly models implicit feedback and temporal dynamics, making it a more challenging and relevant baseline.
*   **Triggering Kernel Simplification:** The choice to focus on the exponential kernel form may limit the flexibility of the model. While justified as a starting point, exploring alternative kernel functions or even learning the kernel directly could potentially improve performance.
*   **Equation Clarity:** Some equations could benefit from further clarification, such as equation (2). Defining each term explicitly would increase readability.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in recommender systems.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*   The algorithm is scalable and has practical implications for real-world applications.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical application of the theoretical results regarding parameter ρ is limited.
*   The lack of comparison to SVD++ weakens the strength of the empirical evaluation.
*   The simplification of the triggering kernel may limit the model's flexibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The proposed approach is novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. While the issues regarding parameter tuning and the baseline comparison are valid concerns, they do not outweigh the overall strengths of the work. I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation and warrants acceptance to NIPS 2015.

**Rating:** Accept (Strongly consider)
