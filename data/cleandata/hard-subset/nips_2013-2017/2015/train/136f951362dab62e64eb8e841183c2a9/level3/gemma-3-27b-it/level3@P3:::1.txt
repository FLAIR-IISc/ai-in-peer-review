This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation method leveraging Hawkes processes and low-rank models to capture user preference dynamics and predict return times. The approach is accompanied by a convex optimization formulation and an efficient algorithm with a claimed O(1/t) convergence rate, evaluated on a synthetic dataset and two real-world datasets. While the problem setting is interesting and the technical development appears substantial, the paper suffers from several shortcomings regarding motivation, baselines, experimental clarity, and presentation.

The primary strength lies in the attempt to model temporal aspects of user behavior, a historically under-explored area in recommendation systems. Connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank models is a potentially valuable contribution. The reported convergence rate of the optimization algorithm is also a positive aspect, suggesting scalability. However, the originality of this connection requires more thorough contextualization within related work beyond Kapoor et al. (2014, 2015). While these papers are cited, a broader discussion of point process applications in recommendation, or similar temporal modeling techniques, would strengthen the contribution's novelty claim.

A major weakness is the lack of compelling motivation. The paper argues that predicting return time is useful for ad bidding and healthcare, but these justifications feel superficial. A deeper exploration of practical use cases, perhaps with concrete examples of potential revenue gains or clinical benefits, is necessary to establish significance. The motivation feels disconnected from common industry practices and the actual problems faced by web companies.

Furthermore, the experimental setup is lacking. The paper omits essential baselines for return-time prediction. Simple methods like predicting the last observed time delay or using the most frequent delay would provide a crucial benchmark for evaluating the proposed method’s improvement. Similarly, for recommendation, considering coarser delay categories (e.g., short/medium/long/very long) might simplify the prediction task and offer a competitive comparison point.

The experimental results, while demonstrating improvements over baselines, lack clarity regarding statistical significance and practical impact. The improvements over SVD and simpler baselines are not convincingly demonstrated as substantial or user-perceptible. Providing confidence intervals or effect sizes would be essential. A more detailed analysis of prediction errors, beyond simple MAE, would also be beneficial. Specifically, the paper needs to address *how much* better the proposed method performs, not just that it performs better.

The presentation also contains issues. The typo “abel” should be corrected to “able.” More seriously, the abrupt switch to retweets and link-creation examples in Section 2 is jarring and unsupported without proper referencing. This digression detracts from the paper's focus and leaves the reader confused. 

In summary, the paper tackles two interesting problems but falls short of demonstrating significant impact. The lack of strong real-world motivation, insufficient baseline comparisons, unclear experimental results, and presentation flaws hinder its overall quality. While technically sound to a degree, the paper does not convincingly advance the state of the art or address a difficult problem in a demonstrably superior way.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel attempt to combine Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation.
*   Efficient optimization algorithm with a favorable convergence rate.
*   Empirical validation on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating some performance gains.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak motivation and lack of compelling use cases.
*   Missing essential baselines for comparison.
*   Unclear experimental results lacking statistical significance and practical impact assessment.
*   Presentation issues and confusing digressions.
*   Insufficient contextualization of originality within the broader literature.



**Overall Assessment:** OK/Marginal. The paper demonstrates technical competence but requires significant revisions to address the identified weaknesses before being considered for acceptance. A stronger focus on practical relevance, more thorough experimentation, and a clearer presentation would substantially improve its quality.
