### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by combining self-exciting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix factorization. The goal is to address two key problems: recommending the right item at the right moment and predicting a user's next return time to a service/item. The model represents the intensity of user-item interaction events using a Hawkes process, where a baseline intensity (long-term preference) is augmented by a self-exciting component that captures short-term influence from past activities. To handle data sparsity and generalize to unobserved user-item pairs, the baseline intensity and self-exciting parameter matrices are assumed to be low-rank, regularized via the nuclear norm. The authors formulate this as a convex optimization problem and propose an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, providing a convergence analysis. The method is evaluated on one synthetic and three real-world datasets, demonstrating improvements over several baselines including SVD, tensor factorization, and a hidden semi-Markov model. This work relates to previous research in temporal recommendation, particularly tensor factorization methods and survival analysis models, but distinguishes itself by jointly modeling recurrent dynamics and providing a collaborative filtering framework that can recommend new items.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel Model Formulation:** The core contribution is the elegant integration of Hawkes processes with low-rank models for recommendation. This provides a principled way to model both static, long-term user preferences (via the baseline intensity matrix) and dynamic, history-dependent preferences (via the self-exciting matrix). This unification is a novel and promising direction for capturing temporal dynamics in user behavior.

2.  **Efficient Optimization Algorithm:** The paper presents a technically sound and novel optimization algorithm to solve the proposed convex formulation. By combining proximal and conditional gradient methods, the algorithm efficiently handles the non-negativity constraints and the non-smooth nuclear norm regularization. The provided convergence analysis, which shows the algorithm achieves optimal rates, is a strong technical contribution that could be of independent interest to the optimization community.

3.  **Comprehensive Problem Scope:** The framework is ambitious in that it simultaneously tackles two important and challenging problems: time-sensitive item ranking and return-time prediction. This dual focus provides a more holistic view of user engagement compared to traditional recommendation models that focus solely on rating prediction or static ranking.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Motivation and Justification:**
    *   The central premise that user preferences exhibit widespread, self-exciting temporal patterns is asserted but not sufficiently justified with data. The paper would be much stronger if it included an exploratory analysis of the datasets to demonstrate the prevalence of these dynamics. For instance, is the "self-exciting" behavior primarily driven by external factors like new popular songs becoming trendy (an effect better modeled by item-side dynamics), or are there genuine user-specific recurrent patterns? This analysis is crucial to motivate the choice of a Hawkes process over simpler temporal models.
    *   The motivation for predicting the *next return time* is unclear and lacks compelling practical use cases. The introduction mentions ad bidding and proactive healthcare, but these connections feel tenuous and are not elaborated upon. How exactly would a web company leverage a prediction that a user will listen to a specific song in 3.7 hours? The practical utility of such a granular prediction needs to be clearly explained to justify its inclusion as a primary task.

2.  **Inadequate Experimental Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is missing several simple, yet crucial, baselines that would help contextualize the model's performance.
    *   For return-time prediction, the most obvious baselines are absent: e.g., predicting the user's last inter-event delay for that item, or predicting the mean/median/most frequent inter-event delay for that user-item pair. Without these, it's hard to assess if the complex model provides a meaningful advantage over simple heuristics.
    *   The paper might also consider whether predicting the exact return time is the right formulation. A potentially more practical and robust approach would be to predict coarser time categories (e.g., short, medium, long delay). Comparing against a model trained for this classification task would provide valuable insight.

3.  **Lack of Clarity on Practical Impact:** The experimental results demonstrate numerical improvement but fail to convey the practical significance of these gains.
    *   For the item recommendation task, it is unclear whether the reported improvements in mean rank over baselines like SVD or a popularity-based Poisson model are substantial. A lower mean rank is better, but is the difference large enough to be perceived by a user? Including standard ranking metrics like NDCG@k or Precision@k would make the impact more tangible.
    *   The paper concludes that its model performs better, but a deeper discussion is needed. Is the improvement uniform across all users/items, or does it excel for specific types of temporal patterns? Quantifying the practical lift is essential for establishing the significance of the work.

4.  **Presentation Issues:**
    *   There is a typo on page 1 ("abel" should be "able").
    *   The transition in Section 2 (Background) to examples about retweets and link creation is abrupt and confusing, as the rest of the paper focuses on user-item consumption (music, shopping, etc.). These examples are introduced without supporting references or a clear connection to the main application, which disrupts the narrative flow.

### **Overall Assessment**

This paper introduces an interesting and technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation. The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling is novel, and the proposed optimization algorithm is a solid contribution. However, the work is weakened by a lack of clear, real-world motivation, particularly for the return-time prediction task. The experimental evaluation, while showing positive results, is not entirely convincing due to the absence of key simple baselines and a lack of analysis regarding the practical significance of the improvements. The paper tackles two challenging problems but feels like two separate, underdeveloped ideas packaged together. Overall, it is a borderline paper with promising ideas that require more thorough justification and empirical validation to realize their full potential.
