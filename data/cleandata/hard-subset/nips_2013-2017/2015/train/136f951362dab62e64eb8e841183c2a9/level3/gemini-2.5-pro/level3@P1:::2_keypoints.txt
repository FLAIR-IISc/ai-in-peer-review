- Problem & approach: Proposes a time-sensitive recommender that models user-item interaction sequences with self-exciting Hawkes processes and enforces low-rank structure on base intensity and excitation matrices to enable generalization.

- Theoretical contribution: Presents a convex formulation with nuclear-norm regularization and a novel optimization algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, with near-optimal convergence guarantees (Theorem 2).

- Algorithmic novelty: The hybrid proximal + conditional gradient approach for handling non-negativity and nuclear-norm constraints is clever and may be of independent interest.

- Evaluation tasks: Evaluated on recommending the right item at a given time and predicting users’ next return time.

- Claimed empirical performance: Authors claim strong predictive results, but those claims are undermined by weaknesses in the experimental design.

- Missing baselines: A critical baseline—an ensemble hybrid of a temporal model for seen items (e.g., STiC) and SVD for unseen items—is not included, making it hard to judge the benefit of the unified model.

- Hyperparameter transparency: Key hyperparameter selection (rank, regularization λ, β, ρ, and kernel bandwidth σ) and tuning procedures are not described, harming fairness and reproducibility.

- Dataset description: Datasets lack essential temporal statistics (recurrence counts, inter-event time distributions, temporal irregularity), which are necessary to understand when the model should work well.

- Clarity and presentation: Paper has grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing; organization is logical but experimental details are insufficient for replication.

- Originality: The synthesis of self-exciting point processes with low-rank collaborative filtering is novel and provides a principled continuous-time extension to CF.

- Significance: Addresses an important practical problem and has potential impact, but empirical shortcomings limit the strength of the claims.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, well-motivated model; solid convex formulation and optimization theory; addresses limitations of prior temporal and CF models.

- Arguments against acceptance: Weak empirical evaluation, missing obvious baselines, omitted experimental details, and poor writing impede confidence in the results.

- Overall verdict: Interesting and theoretically strong work that requires substantially improved experiments and clearer presentation to convincingly demonstrate practical significance.
