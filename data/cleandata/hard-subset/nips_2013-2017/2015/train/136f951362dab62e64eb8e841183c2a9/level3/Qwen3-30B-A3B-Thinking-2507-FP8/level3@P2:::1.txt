

**Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"**

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendations by modeling user-item interactions as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors connect self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user behavior. They develop a convex optimization formulation with an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence. The method is evaluated on two tasks: recommending relevant items at the right moment and predicting users' next return time. The approach extends previous work on temporal point processes (e.g., Du et al. 2015) and low-rank recommendation models (e.g., Rendle 2012, Xiong et al. 2012), addressing a limitation in Kapoor et al.'s work that cannot generalize to new items.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The convex optimization formulation is well-motivated and theoretically grounded. The convergence analysis is rigorous, establishing the O(1/t) rate for the proposed algorithm, which combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods—a novel combination that achieves optimal rates for both components.
2. **Scalability:** The algorithm scales efficiently to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events), which is crucial for real-world applications.
3. **Novel Model:** The connection between low-rank models and Hawkes processes is original and addresses a gap in existing recommendation systems that primarily focus on static preferences.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of Clear Motivation:** The paper fails to convincingly justify the practical need for predicting next-return time. While the authors mention applications like marketing strategies and healthcare, they do not provide concrete examples of how these predictions would translate into tangible business value or user experience improvements. Web companies typically optimize for engagement metrics like click-through rates or session duration, not return time prediction.
2. **Insufficient Baselines:** The experimental evaluation omits critical simple baselines for return-time prediction (e.g., last time delay, most frequent delay, or categorical delay prediction). Without these, it's unclear whether the proposed method's improvements over SVD and Poisson baselines are significant or merely marginal. The paper should compare against these simpler, more interpretable approaches.
3. **Limited Dataset Analysis:** The claim that time-varying preferences are "widespread" lacks supporting evidence. The authors should analyze the real datasets to identify whether the observed temporal patterns are driven by new popular items (e.g., trending songs) versus more complex recurrent behaviors. Without this, the motivation for the proposed method remains speculative.
4. **Unclear Practical Impact:** The paper does not quantify the practical significance of the improvements. For instance, do the predictive performance gains translate to meaningful improvements in user engagement or business metrics? The results in Figure 3 show modest improvements over baselines, but the paper fails to contextualize whether these are user-perceivable or just statistically significant.
5. **Presentation Issues:** The paper contains a typo ("abel" instead of "able" in Section 2) and introduces unsupported examples (e.g., retweets/link-creation in Section 2 without citations), which undermines clarity and credibility.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to time-sensitive recommendations with solid theoretical and experimental results. However, the lack of clear motivation, insufficient baselines, and limited analysis of practical significance prevent it from making a compelling contribution to the field. The experimental results, while showing competitive performance on limited datasets, do not convincingly demonstrate that the method solves a meaningful problem or provides substantial improvements over simpler alternatives.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel connection between low-rank models and Hawkes processes.
- Rigorous optimization algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees.
- Demonstrated scalability to large datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Weak practical motivation for predicting return times.
- Missing simple baselines for return-time prediction.
- Insufficient analysis to justify the need for time-varying preference modeling.
- Unclear practical impact of the proposed method.

**Recommendation:** Marginal. While the technical contribution is solid, the paper lacks sufficient justification for its problem formulation and fails to demonstrate meaningful practical impact. The authors should strengthen the motivation by providing concrete use cases, add missing baselines, and conduct deeper analysis of the datasets to validate their claims before being suitable for publication at NIPS.
