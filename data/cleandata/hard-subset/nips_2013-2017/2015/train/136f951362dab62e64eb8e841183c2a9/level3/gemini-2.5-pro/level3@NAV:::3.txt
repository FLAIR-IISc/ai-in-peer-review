This paper presents a novel approach for time-sensitive recommendation by combining a low-rank user-item model with a self-exciting Hawkes process. The goal is to address two key problems: recommending the right item at the right time and predicting a user's next return time. This work connects the well-established field of collaborative filtering with the principled stochastic modeling of temporal point processes, extending previous work on temporal recommendation (e.g., Koren, 2009) by adopting a more expressive, continuous-time event model. The proposed formulation results in a convex optimization problem, for which the authors develop an efficient, scalable algorithm.

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Originality & Significance:** The combination of low-rank models and Hawkes processes for this problem is a novel contribution to the relatively new area of time-sensitive recommendation. The ability to model recurrent user activities in continuous time is a clear advantage over static or discrete-time models. The work is potentially impactful due to its strong empirical performance and the development of a distributed algorithm that can scale to large datasets.
**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-articulated, and the technical details are presented logically.

Despite these strengths, there are a few weaknesses that should be addressed.
**Quality (Theoretical):** The theoretical justification for the alternative formulation has some ambiguities. The definition of ρ* in Theorem 1 appears to be a maximization over the optimal parameters of the original problem, which is circular. It is also unclear if ρ* is guaranteed to be bounded. Furthermore, the relevance of the convergence analysis in Theorem 2 is diminished because the value of ρ is tuned empirically in the experiments rather than being set according to the theoretical prescription, creating a disconnect between theory and practice.
**Quality (Experimental):** The experimental evaluation is solid but could be strengthened. The baselines are reasonable, but the absence of SVD++, a powerful and widely-used method that also incorporates temporal dynamics (albeit simpler ones), is a notable omission. Including it would provide a more robust comparison and lend greater confidence to the reported performance gains.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting an original and potentially significant contribution. The core idea is elegant, and the empirical results are promising. While the theoretical claims could be clarified and the experimental comparison broadened, the overall quality of the work is high.
