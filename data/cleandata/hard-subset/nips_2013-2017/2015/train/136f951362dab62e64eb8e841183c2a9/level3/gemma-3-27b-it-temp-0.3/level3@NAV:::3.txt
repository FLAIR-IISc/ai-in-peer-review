## Review of “Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities”

This paper presents a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by combining low-rank models with Hawkes processes. The core idea – modeling user-item interactions as a self-exciting point process – is a compelling way to capture temporal dynamics often overlooked in traditional recommendation systems. The authors address both item recommendation and returning-time prediction, framing them within a unified convex optimization problem and developing an efficient algorithm with a provable convergence rate. This work builds upon recent efforts to incorporate temporal information into recommendations (e.g., Kapoor et al., [12, 11]) but distinguishes itself by offering a more generalizable approach capable of recommending *new* items, rather than being limited to previously observed interactions.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The approach is original and tackles a significant problem. The theoretical analysis, including the convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithm, is a strong point. The authors demonstrate scalability through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showcasing the practical applicability of their method. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the paper easy to follow. The potential impact is high, as the modeling choice and distributed solvability could lead to improved recommendation performance in various applications.

However, there are some weaknesses. The explanation surrounding Equation (8) and the parameter ρ is unclear; the paper needs to explicitly state what is being maximized with respect to and address the potential for ρ* to be unbounded. While Theorem 2 is theoretically interesting, its relevance is diminished by the fact that the prescribed ρ is not utilized in the experiments. A more robust comparison would include SVD++ as a baseline, given its strong performance in time-aware recommendation scenarios. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of existing baselines, a direct comparison to SVD++ would strengthen their claims of superior performance. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
*   Demonstrated scalability and improved empirical performance.
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
*   Potential for significant impact in the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity regarding Eq. (8) and the parameter ρ.
*   Limited relevance of Theorem 2 due to experimental setup.
*   Missing comparison to a strong time-aware baseline (SVD++).



Overall, this is a strong paper with a promising approach. Addressing the minor concerns regarding clarity and baseline comparison would significantly enhance its contribution and justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance contingent on these revisions.
