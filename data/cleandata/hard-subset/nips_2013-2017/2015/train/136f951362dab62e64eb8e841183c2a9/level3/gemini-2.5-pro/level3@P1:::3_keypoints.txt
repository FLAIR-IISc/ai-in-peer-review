- Paper proposes a novel low-rank multivariate Hawkes process for time-sensitive recommendation, jointly modeling item recommendation and user return-time prediction.  
- Low-rank structure on base intensity (Λ0) and self-excitation (A) enables generalization to unobserved user-item pairs and links collaborative filtering with temporal point processes.  
- Parameter estimation is cast as a convex problem with nuclear-norm regularization and solved by a scalable algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods.  
- Modeling choice is principled: captures long-term preferences via base intensity and short-term history via self-excitation.  
- Technical formulation and optimization are sound and the custom algorithm effectively handles non-negativity and nuclear-norm constraints.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides adequate background and explanations.  
- Empirical results on synthetic and real datasets show consistent improvement over the chosen baselines for both recommendation and return-time prediction.  
- Time-change analysis (Figure 3) convincingly supports Hawkes processes as a better fit than simpler alternatives.  
- Theorem 1 is unclear: definition and maximization domain for the threshold ρ* are unspecified, potentially undermining the equivalence claim.  
- Theorem 2’s convergence guarantee depends on ρ, but experiments treat ρ as a tuned hyperparameter, creating a practical-theoretical disconnect.  
- Experimental baselines are incomplete—missing strong time-aware CF methods (e.g., TimeSVD++/SVD++), weakening the claim of state-of-the-art performance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: timely and important problem, novel and elegant model, scalable algorithm with theoretical analysis, and strong empirical performance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: theoretical ambiguities around ρ* and incomplete experimental comparison with standard time-aware baselines.  
- Recommendation: Strong candidate for acceptance provided the authors clarify ρ* in Theorem 1 and add comparisons to state-of-the-art time-aware baselines (e.g., TimeSVD++).
