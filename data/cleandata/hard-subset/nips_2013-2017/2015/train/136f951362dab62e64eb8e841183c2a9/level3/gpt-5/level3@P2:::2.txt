Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles two time-sensitive recommendation problems: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) predicting a user’s next return time to a service. The authors model each user–item interaction as a self-exciting Hawkes process with a baseline intensity γ0 (long-term preference) and an additive history-dependent term via a triggering kernel γ(t, ti), instantiated as an exponential kernel with bandwidth σ. To enable knowledge transfer across sparse user–item pairs, they impose low-rank structure on the user–item matrices of base intensities (Λ0) and self-excitation coefficients (A) via nuclear-norm regularization, yielding a convex estimation problem. They introduce a penalty reformulation with auxiliary variables to decouple non-negativity and nuclear norms, and propose an accelerated algorithm that blends proximal and conditional gradient steps, with an O(1/t) convergence rate and top singular-vector oracles instead of full SVDs. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) demonstrate improvements on both time-aware ranking and return-time prediction against Poisson, tensor factorization, SVD, and STiC/survival baselines.

The work builds on point process foundations [Daley & Vere-Jones] and Hawkes processes [Hawkes’71], connects to survival analysis baselines used in return-time prediction [Kapoor et al., WSDM’13; KDD’14], and relates to low-rank CF and temporal MF/tensor models [Koren’09; Xiong et al.; Chi & Kolda; Karatzoglou et al.]. It is also related to work on learning Hawkes triggering kernels and low-rank structures [Du et al.; Zhou et al.].

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and practical relevance: jointly modeling “what” and “when” is important in modern services and healthcare.
- Modeling contribution: principled combination of Hawkes processes (to capture recency and recurrence) with low-rank structure over user–item pairs for generalization, preserving convexity.
- Optimization contribution: a practical proximal/conditional-gradient hybrid that avoids full SVDs, with a clean convergence analysis and demonstrated scalability.
- Empirical breadth: evaluates both time-aware recommendation and return-time prediction on synthetic and multiple real datasets; includes Poisson, STiC, SVD, and tensor baselines.
- Evidence that Hawkes fits sequences better than i.i.d. gap models via time-change/QQ diagnostics.

Weaknesses
- Hyperparameter transparency and sensitivity: bandwidth σ, nuclear-norm weights (λ, β), penalty ρ, and latent ranks for baselines lack clear selection procedures; no sensitivity analysis, especially for σ and ρ.
- Baselines: missing a sensible ensemble baseline that combines static preference (e.g., SVD) with return-time modeling (e.g., STiC), which could be competitive given complementary strengths.
- Dataset characterization for temporal modeling is thin: no distributions of events per user–item pair, inter-event irregularity, or recurrence rates to contextualize the task difficulty.
- Cold-start/generalization claims are not directly validated: no experiments on new items/users with no past events or on content-feature extensions.
- Evaluation details: limited information on train/test splitting protocol to avoid temporal leakage, choice of top-k metrics, confidence intervals, and statistical significance.
- Clarity and writing: notable language issues, typos, and inconsistent notation make the manuscript harder to follow.

Quality
The technical development appears sound: the convex formulation, penalty decoupling, and hybrid optimizer are coherent, and the convergence result is appropriate for the composite structure. Theorem 1’s equivalence requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* is unknown; in practice ρ is tuned—this should be acknowledged more explicitly with guidance or sensitivity plots. The use of top singular vectors via linear minimization oracles is reasonable. The experimental setup is relevant, but (i) selection and tuning of σ, λ, β, ρ (and sample size m in Ogata-based return-time Monte Carlo) are not described in enough detail; (ii) fairness of baseline tuning (e.g., latent dimensions, time discretization for tensor models) is unclear; (iii) runtime/memory breakdowns and hardware details are missing, despite scalability claims.

Clarity
The high-level idea is clear, but exposition suffers from grammatical errors, typos (e.g., “returningtime”), and occasional notation jumps. Important implementation details (hyperparameter search spaces, stopping criteria, power-iteration tolerance for top singular vectors) are missing. The evaluation section would benefit from precise definitions of ranking metrics (recall@k/HR@k/mean rank), reporting of standard errors, and exact split procedures to prevent leakage.

Originality
The paper presents a novel and timely combination: self-exciting point processes for sequence timing fused with low-rank user–item structure in a convex program, plus an efficient solver. While Hawkes processes and low-rank CF are individually well-studied, their integration for time-sensitive recommendation at this scale and with convex estimators is original.

Significance
Addressing both time-aware recommendation and return-time prediction is practically important. If made more reproducible and better compared, the approach could influence subsequent work on time-sensitive CF and point-process recommenders. Current empirical gains are promising but would be more convincing with stronger baselines, ablations, and clearer methodology.

Questions for the authors
- How is the kernel bandwidth σ chosen? Please report the search space, selection criterion, and sensitivity of performance to σ.
- How are λ, β, and ρ tuned? Any heuristic for ρ beyond grid search? Please include sensitivity curves for ρ to support Theorem 1’s practicality.
- How many Monte Carlo samples m are used for return-time prediction, and how does MAE vary with m?
- What are the latent ranks or regularization settings for SVD and tensor baselines, and how were time bins chosen for tensor models?
- Can you quantify cold-start performance for new items/users, and/or include experiments with content/profile features as suggested in the text?
- What hardware, wall-clock time, memory usage, and iteration counts are required for the largest runs? How many power-iteration steps per LMO?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a baseline that ensembles SVD (for static preference) with STiC/survival (for timing), e.g., re-rank SVD’s top-k by predicted hazard/return-time.
- Provide comprehensive dataset characterizations relevant to temporal modeling: events per (u, i), inter-event time distributions, fraction of recurrent pairs, activity burstiness.
- Include ablations: no-history Poisson vs Hawkes; alternate kernels (Weibull, Rayleigh); effect of σ; effect of nuclear-norm weights; removal of non-negativity.
- Clarify evaluation: time-aware train/test splits (strictly past-to-future), report Recall@k/HitRate@k and confidence intervals, and conduct significance tests.
- Add cold-start and context-feature experiments to substantiate generalization claims.
- Improve writing and fix typos; add a schematic that maps model components to tasks (what vs when).

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled model and convex estimator connecting Hawkes and low-rank CF.
- Efficient algorithm with solid theory and practical advantages (no full SVD).
- Addresses impactful, underexplored tasks; promising empirical results.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient hyperparameter and sensitivity reporting, particularly for σ and ρ.
- Missing strong ensemble baseline combining static preference and timing.
- Limited dataset characterization and lack of cold-start/context experiments.
- Clarity and reproducibility concerns.

Overall evaluation
Reasonably strong and timely contribution with solid modeling/optimization ideas and promising results, but the paper needs clearer experimental methodology, stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses, and improved presentation. I lean toward borderline accept if the above issues can be addressed; otherwise borderline reject due to empirical clarity gaps.
