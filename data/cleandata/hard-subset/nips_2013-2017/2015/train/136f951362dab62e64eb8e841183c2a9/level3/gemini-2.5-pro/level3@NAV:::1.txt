This paper proposes a unified framework for time-sensitive recommendation, addressing two tasks: recommending items at the right moment and predicting a user's next return time. The core contribution is a novel model that connects low-rank factorization with self-exciting Hawkes processes to capture both static preferences and history-dependent temporal dynamics. To fit this model, the authors develop an efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. This work builds upon prior research in temporal collaborative filtering and point process modeling by integrating these areas into a single convex formulation.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality:** The primary strength is the novel formulation integrating low-rank models with Hawkes processes to simultaneously model item preference and return-time. This is a creative and promising research direction.
*   **Technical Contribution:** The development of a scalable optimization algorithm with a formal convergence analysis is a solid technical contribution that could be of independent interest.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Motivation and Justification:** The paper's motivation is not fully convincing. It assumes time-varying preferences are a significant phenomenon without providing data analysis to support this claim or disentangle it from simpler effects like changing item popularity. Furthermore, the practical utility of predicting the precise next-return time is not well-articulated with concrete use cases for web services.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation lacks the rigor to convincingly demonstrate superiority. For return-time prediction, crucial baselines (e.g., predicting the last inter-event time or the most frequent delay) are missing, making it hard to assess the value of the complex model. For recommendation, while the method shows quantitative improvements, the practical significance of these gains over strong baselines like SVD or popularity is not discussed. It is unclear if the improvements are substantial enough to be meaningful in a real-world system. The authors could also consider evaluating the prediction of coarser time categories (e.g., short/medium/long delay), which may be more practical.
*   **Clarity:** The presentation has minor issues that detract from readability, such as an unsupported switch to retweet examples in Section 2 and a typo (“abel” for “able”).

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper tackles an interesting problem with a novel technical approach. However, its contributions are undermined by weak motivation, insufficient baselines, and a lack of analysis regarding the practical impact of its results. While the core idea is promising, the paper in its current form is a marginal contribution that requires substantial revision to address these weaknesses.
