This paper proposes a novel point-process collaborative filtering model that combines constant and self-exciting intensities via user/item latent vectors. The model is designed to capture the temporal dynamics of user behaviors and recommend relevant services at any given moment. The authors also develop an efficient optimization algorithm to learn the model parameters.

The main idea of the paper is to model the occurrences of user-item pairs as a self-exciting Hawkes process, which captures the temporal dependencies between past events. The intensity function of the Hawkes process is designed to depend on the history of past events, making it more expressive than traditional Poisson processes. The authors also propose a low-rank model to transfer knowledge from observed user-item pairs to unobserved pairs.

The paper presents an efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods to learn the model parameters. The algorithm is shown to converge to the optimal solution, and its performance is evaluated on both synthetic and real datasets. The results demonstrate that the proposed model outperforms other state-of-the-art methods in terms of predictive performance.

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement in the paper. For example, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the γ function bandwidth parameter σ is set, and there is no analysis of sensitivity to σ. Additionally, the selection and tuning of other key hyperparameters, such as the latent dimension, are not well explained. The paper could also benefit from more dataset characterization relevant to temporal modeling, such as the number of recurring events per user/item pair and temporal irregularity.

In terms of clarity, the paper contains language issues, typos, and grammar errors, which detract from the overall quality of the presentation. However, the originality of the proposed method and its significance in terms of practical value are notable.

Overall, I would rate the quality of the paper as reasonably good but not great. The paper presents an interesting and novel combination of point processes and latent factor models, but the experiments and presentation require improvement.

Here are some specific arguments for and against acceptance:

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting combination of point processes and latent factor models.
* The proposed model is designed to capture the temporal dynamics of user behaviors, which is a practical and important problem.
* The authors develop an efficient optimization algorithm to learn the model parameters, which is a significant contribution.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper lacks clarity in some areas, including the explanation of the γ function bandwidth parameter σ and the selection and tuning of other key hyperparameters.
* The paper could benefit from more dataset characterization relevant to temporal modeling.
* The presentation contains language issues, typos, and grammar errors, which detract from the overall quality of the paper.

Recommendation:

I would recommend that the authors revise the paper to address the limitations and areas for improvement mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should:

* Provide a clear explanation of how the γ function bandwidth parameter σ is set and analyze its sensitivity.
* Explain the selection and tuning of other key hyperparameters, such as the latent dimension.
* Provide more dataset characterization relevant to temporal modeling.
