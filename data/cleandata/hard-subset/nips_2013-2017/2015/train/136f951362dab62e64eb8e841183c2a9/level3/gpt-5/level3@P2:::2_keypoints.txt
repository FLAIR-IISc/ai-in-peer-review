- Paper addresses two time-sensitive recommendation tasks: next-item recommendation at the right moment and return-time prediction using Hawkes point processes.  
- Model: user–item interactions as Hawkes processes with exponential kernel (σ) and low-rank structure on base intensities (Λ0) and excitation coefficients (A) via nuclear-norm regularization.  
- Optimization: penalty reformulation with auxiliary variables and a proximal/conditional-gradient hybrid solver using top singular-vector oracles, O(1/t) convergence.  
- Experiments: synthetic + three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) showing improvements over Poisson, SVD, tensor, and STiC/survival baselines for ranking and return-time prediction.  
- Novelty: original integration of self-exciting point processes with low-rank CF in a convex formulation and an efficient solver.  
- Strength — practical relevance: jointly modeling “what” and “when” is important for services and healthcare.  
- Strength — modeling: principled combination capturing recency/recurrence while preserving convexity for generalization.  
- Strength — optimization: avoids full SVDs, has clean convergence analysis and demonstrated scalability.  
- Strength — empirical breadth: evaluates both tasks across multiple datasets and baselines; Hawkes fit supported by time-change/QQ diagnostics.  
- Weakness — hyperparameter transparency: selection procedures and sensitivity analysis for σ, λ, β, ρ, and baseline ranks are missing.  
- Weakness — missing ensemble baseline: no combined static-preference + timing baseline (e.g., SVD re-ranked by STiC hazard).  
- Weakness — dataset characterization: lacks distributions of events per (u,i), inter-event irregularity, and recurrence rates to contextualize difficulty.  
- Weakness — cold-start/generalization claims unvalidated: no experiments for new users/items or content-feature extensions.  
- Weakness — evaluation details lacking: unclear train/test splits to avoid temporal leakage, metric definitions (top-k), confidence intervals, and significance tests.  
- Weakness — clarity: grammatical errors, typos (e.g., “returningtime”), inconsistent notation, and missing implementation details hinder readability.  
- Theory note: Theorem 1 requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* is unknown; authors tune ρ in practice and should acknowledge/tune-sensitize this choice.  
- Experimental methodology concerns: unclear tuning procedures for σ, λ, β, ρ, m (Monte Carlo samples), and fairness of baseline hyperparameter selection.  
- Reproducibility gap: missing runtime/memory/hardware details, iteration counts, and power-iteration tolerances for the LMO.  
- Suggested experiments: add ablations (Poisson vs Hawkes, alternate kernels, effect of σ, nuclear-norm weights, non-negativity removal).  
- Suggested analyses: provide sensitivity curves for σ and ρ, MAE vs. Monte Carlo samples m, and cold-start/context-feature experiments.  
- Suggested dataset reporting: include events per (u,i), inter-event time distributions, recurrence fractions, and activity burstiness.  
- Suggested baseline: ensemble SVD + STiC to combine static preference and timing as a competitive baseline.  
- Suggested evaluation fixes: use strict past-to-future splits, report Recall@k/HitRate@k with standard errors, and perform statistical tests.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled, novel model; efficient theory-backed algorithm; impactful tasks and promising empirical gains.  
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient hyperparameter/sensitivity reporting, missing strong ensemble baseline, limited dataset characterization, and reproducibility/clarity issues.  
- Overall: solid and timely contribution with promising results but needs clearer experimental methodology, stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses, and improved presentation; reviewer leans borderline accept pending fixes.
