

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel framework that combines self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption data. The authors model user-item interactions as a low-rank Hawkes process, where the intensity function combines a constant baseline component (representing long-term user preferences) and a self-exciting component (capturing short-term influences from past events). They develop a convex optimization formulation and an efficient algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence rate. The method is evaluated on synthetic and real datasets for two time-sensitive recommendation tasks: item recommendation and returning-time prediction.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes an interesting and novel contribution by connecting point processes with low-rank collaborative filtering models, which is a fresh perspective on temporal recommendation systems. The theoretical analysis is sound, with a clear convergence rate analysis demonstrating optimal performance. The scalability claims (handling millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events) are compelling for practical applications. The problem addressed is timely and relevant to modern recommendation systems, where temporal dynamics are increasingly important.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. The paper fails to include a sensible baseline that would properly contextualize the proposed method's performance. A simple ensemble of STiC [12] (which models returning time prediction) and SVD (which provides static item recommendations) would be a natural baseline given their complementary strengths, as suggested in the review criteria. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed method.

The paper lacks crucial details about hyperparameter selection and sensitivity analysis. The bandwidth parameter σ for the triggering kernel γ(t, ti) is used but its selection is unclear, and no sensitivity analysis is provided. Similarly, the selection of latent dimensions for both the proposed method and baselines is not discussed, making it hard to evaluate the robustness of the results.

The dataset characterization is insufficient for temporal modeling. The paper fails to provide key metrics relevant to temporal analysis, such as the average number of recurring events per user-item pair, temporal irregularity statistics, or the distribution of inter-event times. These details would help contextualize the results and understand the challenges of the data.

The paper suffers from significant clarity issues, including language problems, typos, and grammatical errors that make it difficult to follow. For instance, the description of the optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) is confusing, and key mathematical expressions lack proper exposition.

**Quality Assessment:**
The technical content is sound, but the experimental evaluation is incomplete. The paper claims "superb predictive performance" without providing adequate comparison to meaningful baselines. The synthetic experiments are well-designed, but the real-world evaluation lacks the necessary depth to fully validate the claims. The theoretical contribution is strong, but the practical contribution is undermined by the insufficient experimental validation.

**Clarity Assessment:**
The paper contains numerous language issues, typos, and grammatical errors that hinder readability. Some key concepts (like the role of the γ function bandwidth parameter) are not properly explained. The organization could be improved to better guide the reader through the technical content.

**Originality Assessment:**
The combination of point processes with low-rank collaborative filtering models for time-sensitive recommendation is novel and well-motivated. The authors clearly position their work relative to related work, particularly STiC [12] and survival analysis approaches. This represents a meaningful contribution to the literature.

**Significance Assessment:**
The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is highly relevant to practical applications in modern web services. The method has potential for real-world deployment, but the significance is limited by the incomplete experimental evaluation. The paper would have greater impact if the claims about performance were better supported.

**Recommendation:**
This paper has interesting ideas and a sound theoretical foundation, but the experimental evaluation is insufficient to fully support the claims. The lack of a sensible baseline, missing hyperparameter analysis, and inadequate dataset characterization significantly weaken the paper's contribution. The clarity issues further diminish its impact.

*Arguments for acceptance:*
- Novel combination of point processes and low-rank models
- Strong theoretical contribution with convergence analysis
- Practical relevance to modern recommendation systems

*Arguments against acceptance:*
- Missing sensible baseline (ensemble of STiC and SVD)
- Unclear parameter selection and no sensitivity analysis
- Insufficient dataset characterization for temporal modeling
- Significant clarity issues with language and organization

Overall, this is a reasonably good paper with interesting ideas but not great due to the experimental shortcomings. I recommend rejection as presented, but the authors could substantially improve the paper by addressing the missing experimental details and improving clarity. The core idea has potential, but the current version does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims.
