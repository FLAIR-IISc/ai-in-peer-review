This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation, connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interaction data. The authors aim to address two key questions: recommending the most desirable item at the right moment, and predicting a user’s next return time to a service. They present a convex formulation for these problems, develop an efficient optimization algorithm with O(1/t) convergence, and demonstrate performance gains on synthetic and real datasets.

The work builds upon existing research in recommender systems, particularly moving beyond traditional rating-based approaches to incorporate temporal dynamics. Recent work, such as Kapoor et al. [12, 11], has explored temporal aspects, often using survival analysis or hidden semi-Markov models. However, the authors rightly point out a limitation of these approaches – their inability to generalize to recommending *new* items. The proposed method, by leveraging low-rank models, aims to overcome this limitation. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a novel contribution, offering a principled way to model both individual user preferences and shared patterns across users and items. The theoretical convergence analysis of the proposed optimization algorithm is also a positive aspect.

The paper's strengths lie in its technical soundness and the novelty of its approach. The formulation appears well-motivated from a probabilistic perspective, and the optimization algorithm's convergence guarantees are a significant achievement, particularly given the scale of the problem. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms several baselines, although the magnitude of these improvements requires further scrutiny (discussed below). The ability to incorporate additional contextual information, as mentioned by the authors, is a valuable feature, adding to the model's flexibility.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, the motivation for focusing on time-varying preferences requires stronger justification. While the introduction alludes to varying activities (morning vs. evening), the paper lacks analysis of the datasets to confirm the prevalence and nature of these temporal patterns. Is the observed time variation primarily driven by external factors (e.g., new popular items entering the system) or genuine shifts in user preferences? Providing such an analysis would strengthen the argument for the proposed approach. Second, the practical utility of accurately predicting a user's *exact* return time is unclear. The paper mentions benefits for ad bidding, but this feels somewhat speculative. Would predicting coarser time categories (e.g., short/medium/long delay) be more practical and potentially easier to achieve? 

The experimental evaluation is also lacking in several respects.  Simple, yet important, baselines are missing. For the return-time prediction task, comparing against methods like predicting the next return time as the last observed time delay, or the most frequent delay observed for that user-item pair, would establish a clearer baseline for performance. For the recommendation task, a simple popularity-based recommendation algorithm (always recommending the most popular items) would provide a useful point of comparison. Furthermore, the presentation of experimental results lacks sufficient detail regarding statistical significance. Are the observed improvements statistically significant, and what is the effect size? More importantly, the practical impact of the improvements needs to be discussed. Are the gains in predictive accuracy substantial enough to translate into a meaningful improvement in user experience or business metrics? The discussion regarding the datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) is also somewhat cursory; more details about their characteristics would be helpful. 

Minor issues include a typo (“abel” instead of “able”) and a somewhat jarring transition in Section 2 to examples involving retweets and link creation, lacking clear connection or citation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and convergence guarantees for the optimization algorithm.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Potential for incorporating additional contextual information.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak justification for the prevalence of time-varying user preferences.
*   Unclear practical utility of predicting precise return times.
*   Lack of crucial baseline comparisons.
*   Insufficient analysis of experimental results (statistical significance, practical impact).
*   Minor presentation issues.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation. However, the lack of strong justification for the problem, missing baselines, and insufficient analysis of results raise concerns about its practical significance. The paper is currently borderline. I recommend acceptance with major revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding motivation, baseline comparisons, and experimental analysis. The authors should provide a more compelling case for why this work is important and how it advances the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way.
