- Paper models time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by combining Hawkes temporal point processes with low-rank collaborative modeling.
- Each user–item pair uses a Hawkes process (baseline + exponential self-excitation) to capture long-term affinity and short-term recency.
- Low-rank structure is imposed on nonnegative base-intensity (Λ0) and excitation-magnitude (A) matrices, yielding a convex MLE with nuclear-norm regularization.
- Authors introduce an equivalent penalty formulation with auxiliary variables (under a ρ threshold) to decouple nonnegativity and nuclear norms.
- Optimization algorithm uses proximal projection for nonnegativity and conditional-gradient (Frank–Wolfe) steps on nuclear-norm blocks to avoid full SVDs.
- Convergence analysis is provided with O(1/t)-style rate components.
- Method scales to millions of pairs and ~100M events; Q–Q diagnostic supports Hawkes model fit.
- Empirical evaluation on synthetic data and three real domains (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC-II) shows improved ranking-at-time and return-time MAE versus Poisson, SVD, temporal tensor factorization, and STiC.
- Extensions to contextual features and nonparametric kernels are discussed.
- Distinct from discretized temporal MF/tensor methods by operating in continuous time and modeling history dependence explicitly.
- Differs from survival-analysis return-time models by handling recurrent events and generalizing to unseen items via low-rank sharing.
- Novel relative to Hawkes-based network inference due to recommender focus and convex low-rank estimation.
- Optimization contrasts with ADMM-based rank minimization by avoiding full SVDs and using a proximal–conditional-gradient hybrid.
- Strength: coherently addresses both recommendation timing and user return in a probabilistic framework.
- Strength: sound convex formulation with penalty-equivalence insight clarifying ρ’s role.
- Strength: well-motivated, scalable optimization with nontrivial convergence guarantees.
- Strength: realistic-scale experiments and useful Q–Q model diagnostics.
- Strength: broad empirical coverage including held-out-item settings.
- Weakness: missing ensemble baseline combining SVD and STiC-style time scores; recommend inclusion and tuning.
- Weakness: unclear selection and sensitivity of kernel bandwidth σ; request selection protocol, sensitivity curves, and multi-kernel experiments.
- Weakness: insufficient hyperparameter/tuning details for ρ, λ, β and baselines; request search ranges and time-aware validation policy.
- Weakness: dataset characterization missing per-pair recurrence stats, inter-event distributions, sparsity, held-out definition, and time units.
- Weakness: presentation issues—typos, unclear Alg.1 notation, missing per-iteration cost/memory/stopping criteria, and practical guidance for ρ.
- Weakness: evaluation lacks confidence intervals/statistical tests, wall-clock/memory comparisons, ablations (no nuclear norm, no self-excitation), and dedicated cold-start evaluation.
- Quality: technically sound but limited reproducibility due to missing sensitivity analyses, ablations, and tuning details.
- Clarity: core ideas clear but need editing, better pseudocode, complexity analysis, and experimental protocols.
- Originality: novel combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank modeling with a tailored optimization scheme.
- Significance: promising and scalable approach with potential high impact if experimental rigor and presentation are improved.
- Pros for acceptance: continuous-time modeling, convex scalable algorithm with guarantees, positive multi-dataset results and diagnostics.
- Cons for acceptance: incomplete baselines/tuning, missing sensitivity analyses and ablations, and clarity/reproducibility issues.
- Recommendation: borderline accept conditional on stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses, ablations, and substantial improvements in clarity and reproducibility.
