This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that couples low-rank structure with Hawkes processes to model time-varying user-item intensities, enabling both “what to recommend now” and “when the user will return” predictions. The authors formulate parameter learning as a convex program with nuclear-norm regularization and nonnegativity constraints, and develop a blended proximal–conditional gradient algorithm with O(1/t) convergence. Experiments on one large synthetic dataset and two modest real datasets (last.fm, tmall; with a smaller medical case) suggest improvements over Poisson, tensor factorization, and an SVD baseline. The work relates to temporal CF (e.g., Koren 2009; Rendle), survival/return-time prediction for recommender systems (Kapoor et al., WSDM’15; KDD’14), and Hawkes learning (Du et al., AISTATS/ICML/AISTATS), but differs by tying low-rank transfer to self-excitation for recommendation and return-time tasks.

Quality: The modeling and optimization are technically solid, and the algorithmic decomposition is neat. However, the empirical section lacks critical baselines and clearer measures of practical effect size. The claim that time-varying preferences are pervasive is not substantiated by data analysis.

Clarity: Generally understandable, but with presentation issues: a typo (“abel”→“able”), and an abrupt, unsupported switch to retweets/link-creation examples in Section 2 without references.

Originality: Combining low-rank sharing with Hawkes for recommendation and return-time prediction is novel relative to prior temporal CF and survival approaches.

Significance: Potentially useful, but real-world impact is unclear without stronger motivation for return-time prediction and clearer gains over simple baselines.

Major weaknesses and requests:
- Justify the prevalence and causes of time variation via dataset analysis (e.g., seasonal/diurnal effects, change-points, popularity shocks like new songs vs. other patterns).
- Clarify the practical use cases for next-return time (notifications, throttling, inventory/ads pacing), expected business metrics moved, and how predictions would be acted upon.
- Add simple return-time baselines: last inter-arrival time, median/most frequent delay, user- or user-item seasonal inhomogeneous Poisson, renewal models; also consider coarse delay categories (short/medium/long/very long) as an easier and actionable target.
- For recommendation, report strong time-aware popularity baselines (time-of-day/week popularity), SVD++, and quantify top-k lift/precision and user-level deltas; provide statistical significance.
- Clarify the magnitude of improvements vs. SVD and popular-item heuristics; are gains modest or user-perceivable? Include CIs, per-user breakdowns, and ablations (self-excitation off; low-rank off; kernel choices).

Pros:
- Clean convex formulation and efficient algorithm.
- Conceptually unified treatment of item-at-time and return-time tasks.
- Scalability demonstrated on large synthetic data.

Cons:
- Limited real-world motivation and missing simple baselines.
- Insufficient analysis of time-variation and its drivers.
- Practical impact and statistical significance unclear.
- Presentation nits and unsupported examples.

Overall: OK/marginal. Leaning borderline reject unless the empirical section and motivation are substantially strengthened.
