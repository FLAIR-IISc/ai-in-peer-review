This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interaction data. The core idea is to model user activity as a Hawkes process, leveraging low-rank matrix factorization to generalize across users and items. The authors develop a convex optimization formulation and an efficient algorithm with provable convergence guarantees, demonstrating strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

This work builds upon a growing body of research recognizing the importance of temporal dynamics in recommender systems. Traditional collaborative filtering techniques often overlook the sequential nature of user interactions, focusing instead on static preferences. Recent efforts, such as those by Kapoor et al. [12, 11], have begun to address this limitation using survival analysis and hidden Markov models. However, these approaches often struggle with generalization to new items. This paper distinguishes itself by proposing a generative model capable of both recommending new items and predicting user return times, a crucial distinction highlighted by the authors. Furthermore, the connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models appears novel, offering a principled approach to scalability. Related work in tensor factorization [23, 3, 13, 25, 21] is acknowledged, but the paper effectively positions itself as addressing the specific challenges of point process modeling within a collaborative filtering context.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of self-exciting point processes and low-rank matrix factorization is a conceptually appealing and original approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide a rigorous convex optimization formulation and establish a convergence rate for their proposed algorithm, demonstrating a strong theoretical understanding.
* **Scalability:** The developed algorithm is designed to handle large-scale datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events, a significant practical advantage.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that the proposed model outperforms several state-of-the-art baselines, including STiC [12] and tensor factorization methods.
* **Generality:** The formulation allows for the incorporation of additional contextual information, broadening the applicability of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper suffers from clarity issues. The writing is sometimes convoluted, and numerous typos and grammatical errors detract from the reading experience. For example, phrases like “the most desirable item” are vague.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the model’s performance to key hyperparameters, such as the latent dimension and the bandwidth parameter σ of the triggering kernel (γ function). While the authors mention tuning these parameters, the details of the tuning process are missing. It is unclear *how* σ was chosen and if a sensitivity analysis was conducted.
* **Baseline Comparison:** While the inclusion of STiC [12] is valuable, the suggestion in the key points regarding an ensemble of STiC and SVD appears logical and would provide a stronger benchmark.  This would allow a direct comparison against methods capitalizing on both temporal dynamics *and* collaborative filtering.
* **Dataset Characterization:** The description of the datasets lacks sufficient detail regarding properties relevant to temporal modeling. For example, providing statistics on the number of recurring events per user-item pair and the degree of temporal irregularity would help readers assess the appropriateness of the chosen modeling approach.
* **Algorithm Detail:** While convergence is proven, the practicality of implementation is unclear. The interplay between proximal gradient and conditional gradient isn’t fully elucidated, and the computational cost of each step relative to other methods isn't discussed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem in recommender systems.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model in a variety of settings.
* The scalability of the algorithm is a significant advantage for real-world applications.
* The model's ability to incorporate contextual information increases its flexibility and applicability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The clarity of the paper is significantly hindered by writing quality issues.
* The lack of a thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis raises concerns about the robustness of the results.
* The limited dataset characterization makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the findings.
* The absence of a stronger baseline (e.g., an ensemble of STiC and SVD) weakens the empirical validation.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising approach to time-sensitive recommendation. The core ideas are innovative, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are encouraging. However, the paper’s clarity and presentation need significant improvement.  Addressing the weaknesses related to hyperparameter tuning, dataset characterization, and baseline comparison would substantially strengthen the contribution. I rate this as a reasonably good paper, but not a great one.

**Recommendation:**

Weak Accept. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity issues and providing a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and a stronger baseline comparison.  A revised version should include more details about the dataset characteristics and the implementation of the algorithm.
