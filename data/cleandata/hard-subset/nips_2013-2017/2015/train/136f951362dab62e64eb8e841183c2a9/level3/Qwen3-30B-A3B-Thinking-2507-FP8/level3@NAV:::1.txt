

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption. The authors develop an efficient optimization algorithm with O(1/t) convergence rate for learning parameters and evaluate their approach on synthetic data and two modest real-world datasets (last.fm and tmall.com). The work addresses two complementary problems: recommending items at the right moment and predicting users' next-return time.

The paper demonstrates technical soundness through a clear convex formulation and convergence analysis. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the optimization algorithm appears well-designed. The model's scalability to large datasets is a notable strength.

However, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses. The motivation for predicting next-return time is underdeveloped; the authors fail to convincingly explain practical applications for web companies beyond vague statements about "marketing strategies." The claim about time-varying preferences lacks empirical justification—no dataset analysis examines whether these patterns are widespread or identifies specific causes (e.g., new popular songs versus other patterns). 

The experimental design is problematic. The authors omit simple baselines for return-time prediction (e.g., last time delay, most frequent delay) and fail to consider coarser delay categories (short/medium/long/very long) that might be more practical. The results lack clarity on whether improvements over SVD and popular-item baselines are substantial or merely statistical, and it's unclear if these gains would be user-perceivable.

Additionally, the paper contains presentation issues: a typo ("abel"→"able"), and Section 2 introduces unsupported examples about retweets/link-creation without references. The connection between the proposed method and practical recommendation systems remains tenuous.

While the technical approach is novel and the optimization algorithm is well-justified, the paper lacks sufficient real-world motivation, adequate baselines, and deeper analysis to demonstrate significant practical impact. The marginal improvements over baselines on modest datasets do not strongly justify the method's complexity or the claimed importance of the problem. The paper would benefit from stronger empirical justification of the time-varying preference assumption, clearer practical motivation, and more comprehensive baseline comparisons.
