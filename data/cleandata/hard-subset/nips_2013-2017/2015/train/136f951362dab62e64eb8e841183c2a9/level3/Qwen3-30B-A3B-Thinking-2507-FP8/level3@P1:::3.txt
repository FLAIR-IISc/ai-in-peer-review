

The paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by integrating low-rank models with self-exciting Hawkes processes to model recurrent user activities and predict next-return times. It addresses two underexplored challenges in recommendation systems: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the optimal time, and (2) predicting user return intervals. The authors connect temporal point processes (via intensity functions) with low-rank matrix structures to capture user-item dynamics, framing the problem as convex optimization with nuclear norm regularization. Their algorithm blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence and scalability to 100M+ events. Experiments on synthetic data and real-world datasets (last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC II) show superior performance over Poisson baselines, STiC, and tensor factorization methods for both item ranking and return-time prediction.

**Strengths**  
The work demonstrates strong technical quality. The convex formulation (Eq. 4) is well-motivated, and the optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) efficiently handles non-negativity and low-rank constraints, with rigorous convergence analysis (Theorem 2). The scalability claims are validated empirically: Figure 2(d) confirms linear scaling with data size, and the method handles 100M events—far exceeding prior work like STiC [11] or tensor factorization [3]. The problem formulation is novel for NIPS: while Hawkes processes have been used in seismology or social networks [8, 10], their application to *time-sensitive recommendation* with low-rank user-item structures (unlike prior work focused on single-item prediction [11]) represents a significant advance. The real-world impact is compelling: predicting return times enables better ad bidding (e.g., "bid only on high-probability return slots") and proactive healthcare interventions (e.g., anticipating disease relapses in MIMIC II). The paper is exceptionally clear, with well-structured exposition and intuitive figures (e.g., Fig. 1(a) illustrating temporal context).

**Weaknesses**  
The primary theoretical concern is the unresolved practicality of Theorem 1’s threshold ρ*. The paper states ρ must exceed ρ* (a problem-dependent bound) for equivalence between formulations (4) and (5), but ρ* is undefined for real data (e.g., Eq. 8 ambiguously suggests maximization over ρ, yet ρ* could be unbounded). Crucially, the experiments use ρ tuned empirically (not ρ*), weakening the relevance of Theorem 1. This is a notable gap in theoretical grounding. Second, the baseline comparisons lack SVD++ [16, 23], a dominant time-aware collaborative filtering method that explicitly models temporal dynamics. Omitting SVD++ (a standard in NIPS recsys literature [2, 15]) weakens the empirical significance claim; the paper states SVD is "not designed for return-time prediction," but SVD++ extends this to time-sensitive tasks [15, 23], making its absence a significant omission. Finally, while the Hawkes model’s fit to real data (Fig. 3) is convincing, the paper does not rigorously test whether the exponential kernel (γ(t, t_i) = exp(-(t-t_i)/σ)) is necessary or if nonparametric alternatives [8] would improve performance.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments**  
*Pro*: Novel framework bridging temporal point processes and low-rank models; strong scalability (100M+ events); clear problem definition addressing a gap in time-sensitive recommendation; compelling real-world applications (ad bidding, healthcare); technically sound optimization with provable convergence.  
*Con*: Theoretical gap in ρ*’s practical implementation; missing SVD++ baseline undermines comparative significance; exponential kernel assumption lacks ablation study.

**Recommendation**  
The paper makes a high-quality, original contribution to NIPS with strong empirical results and a principled approach to time-sensitive recommendation. While the theoretical concern about ρ* and the omission of SVD++ are significant, they do not invalidate the core innovation. The work advances the state of the art in modeling temporal user dynamics, and its scalability and impact potential justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions: (1) clarify ρ*’s practical estimation in the appendix, (2) add SVD++ to experiments, and (3) include a short ablation on kernel choices. The paper’s clarity, novelty, and significance outweigh the identified weaknesses.
