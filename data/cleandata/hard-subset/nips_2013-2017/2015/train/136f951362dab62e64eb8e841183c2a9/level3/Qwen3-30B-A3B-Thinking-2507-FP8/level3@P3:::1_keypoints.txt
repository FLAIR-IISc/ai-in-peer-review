- Proposes a novel framework connecting self-exciting (Hawkes) point processes with low-rank recommendation models for time-sensitive recommendation and next-return-time prediction.
- Develops a convex optimization formulation and an efficient algorithm combining proximal-gradient and conditional-gradient methods with O(1/t) convergence (Theorem 2).
- Demonstrates scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events; tested on synthetic data and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II).
- Shows improvements over several baselines (Poisson, SVD, tensor factorization) on both recommendation and return-time prediction tasks.
- Strong theoretical contribution with well-justified convergence guarantees for the optimization algorithm.
- Novelty: the Hawkes process + low-rank modeling combination is an original contribution to recommendation systems.
- Scalability claims are well-supported by experiments on large datasets.
- Fails to convincingly justify that time-varying preferences are widespread or analyze dataset causes (e.g., new popular items driving changes).
- Motivation for predicting next-return time is weak and superficial (ad bidding example lacks concrete, practical implementation details).
- Omits simple, meaningful baselines for return-time prediction (e.g., last delay, most frequent delay, coarse delay categories).
- Experimental results lack clarity on the practical significance of gains over SVD and popular-item baselines—unclear if improvements are user-perceivable.
- Presentation issues: typo ("abel"→"able") and confusing, unsupported examples in Section 2 (retweets/link-creation) without references.
- Claims to address two important problems but lacks real-world motivation, sufficient baselines, and deeper analysis to demonstrate practical value.
- Quality: technically sound but experimental evaluation insufficient to fully support practical claims.
- Clarity: generally well-structured but undermined by confusing examples and weak motivation for return-time prediction.
- Significance: questionable due to insufficient demonstration of real-world impact and practitioner relevance.
- Pro accept points: novel modeling connection, strong optimization guarantees, demonstrated scalability, appropriate baseline comparisons.
- Con accept points: weak real-world motivation, missing simple baselines, insufficient analysis of time-varying preference prevalence, unclear practical impact, presentation flaws.
- Overall assessment/recommendation: technically interesting but lacking in practical relevance and thorough analysis; recommends rejection for NIPS.
