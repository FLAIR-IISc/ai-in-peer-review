

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by combining self-exciting point processes (Hawkes processes) with low-rank matrix models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions. The authors model user-item consumption events as a low-rank Hawkes process, where the intensity function captures both long-term user preferences (via a base intensity matrix) and short-term temporal dependencies (via a self-exciting matrix). The key innovation lies in connecting these two modeling paradigms to create a convex optimization problem that can be efficiently solved using a distributed algorithm with O(1/t) convergence rate. This approach addresses two important time-sensitive recommendation tasks: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and (2) predicting the next returning time of users.

The paper builds upon related work in temporal recommendation systems, such as the work by Kapoor et al. [11, 12] which uses survival analysis to predict user return times, but correctly identifies that such approaches cannot generalize to recommend new items. It also relates to matrix factorization approaches for recommendation (e.g., SVD++ [16, 23]) but extends them to explicitly model temporal dynamics.

Strengths:

1. Quality: The paper presents a principled modeling approach using Hawkes processes to capture temporal dynamics, which is theoretically sound. The convex optimization formulation (Equation 4) is well-motivated, and the authors provide a detailed convergence analysis (Theorem 2) for their proposed algorithm. The experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) demonstrates superior performance compared to several strong baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, tensor factorization).

2. Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The technical concepts are clearly explained, and the algorithmic details are presented in a structured manner with pseudocode (Algorithm 1).

3. Originality: The combination of low-rank matrix models with Hawkes processes for time-sensitive recommendation appears novel. While there is related work on Hawkes processes for event prediction (e.g., Du et al. [7, 8, 9]), and matrix factorization for recommendation (e.g., Koren [16], Karatzoglou et al. [22]), the specific integration proposed here for time-sensitive recommendation is new.

4. Significance: The work is significant because it addresses two important but under-explored questions in recommendation systems: how to recommend the most desirable item at the right moment, and how to predict the next returning time of users. The distributed nature of the algorithm makes it scalable to large datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events, which is crucial for real-world applications.

Weaknesses:

1. Theoretical concern: Equation (8) is unclear about which parameter is being maximized (is it over ρ?). The paper claims ρ* is a threshold, but it's not clear if ρ* is bounded or how it scales with problem size. If ρ* is unbounded, the practical utility of the approach could be limited.

2. Theorem 2's relevance is weakened because the prescribed ρ from the theorem is not used in the experiments. The paper tunes ρ empirically, but doesn't show how the theoretically prescribed ρ compares to the empirically chosen value, undermining the theoretical contribution.

3. Experimental baselines: The authors do not include SVD++ as a baseline, which is a strong, time-aware recommendation method that would strengthen the comparison. SVD++ [16] explicitly models temporal dynamics through implicit feedback, making it a relevant baseline for time-sensitive recommendation tasks.

4. The paper mentions that "our model can be readily generalized to incorporate other contextual information," but doesn't demonstrate this with experiments, which would have strengthened the practical significance.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to time-sensitive recommendation that combines Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results demonstrate superior performance on both synthetic and real datasets. The theoretical concerns are relatively minor and could be addressed with minor revisions, and including SVD++ as a baseline would further strengthen the experimental evaluation.

Given the novelty of the approach, the strong empirical results, and the potential impact on real-world recommendation systems, I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. The theoretical concerns can be addressed with minor revisions, and including SVD++ as a baseline would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

Recommendation: Accept
