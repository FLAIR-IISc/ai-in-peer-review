This paper presents a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation, addressing two key tasks: recommending the right item at the right time, and predicting a user's next return time to a service. The core idea is to model the sequence of user-item interactions as a multivariate Hawkes process. To handle the inherent sparsity of recommendation data and enable generalization to unobserved user-item pairs, the authors propose a low-rank structure for the Hawkes process parameters—specifically, for the base intensity matrix (Λ0) and the self-excitation matrix (A). This establishes an elegant connection between collaborative filtering (via low-rank models) and temporal point processes. The authors formulate the parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization and propose an efficient algorithm that combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. The model is evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating superior performance against several baselines on both item recommendation and return-time prediction tasks.

The work is situated in the growing area of temporal dynamics in recommender systems. While many classic methods like matrix factorization are static, and some extensions (e.g., tensor factorization, time-aware SVD) discretize or add simple time-decay factors, this paper adopts a more principled, continuous-time, stochastic process approach. It is most closely related to the work of Kapoor et al., which uses survival analysis, but the proposed model is more expressive by capturing self-excitation effects and can generalize to new items, a crucial feature for recommendation.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The proposed combination of low-rank models and multivariate Hawkes processes is a novel and powerful approach for time-sensitive recommendation. It addresses a significant and practical problem: moving beyond static "what" recommendations to dynamic "what and when" recommendations. The modeling choice is well-justified, capturing both long-term user preferences (via the base intensity) and short-term, history-dependent patterns (via the self-exciting kernel). This work has the potential to be highly impactful for researchers and practitioners working on user engagement and dynamic personalization.

2.  **Technical Quality (Modeling and Optimization):** The formulation is technically sound. Modeling user activities as a point process is a principled way to handle event data. The introduction of low-rank constraints to share statistical strength across users and items is a standard and effective technique in collaborative filtering, applied here in a novel context. The development of a custom, scalable optimization algorithm is a significant contribution. The algorithm cleverly handles the non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization separately, achieving a good convergence rate.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and generally easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient background on point processes before introducing their model, and the problem formulation, algorithm, and experimental setup are clearly explained.

4.  **Empirical Results:** The experiments, though with some caveats mentioned below, demonstrate strong performance. The proposed method consistently outperforms competitors on both synthetic and real-world datasets for both recommendation and return-time prediction. The analysis of the model's fit using the time-change theorem (Figure 3) provides compelling evidence that the Hawkes process is a more suitable underlying model for this data than simpler alternatives.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Theoretical Gaps and Clarity:** There are some concerns regarding the theoretical justification of the optimization algorithm.
    *   The definition of the threshold ρ* in Theorem 1 is unclear. The theorem states ρ* = max{...}, but it is not specified what the maximization is over. If it is over all possible parameters, ρ* could potentially be unbounded, which would weaken the claim of equivalence between the original problem (4) and the penalized formulation (5). A clarification on the domain of this maximization is needed to fully assess the soundness of this theorem.
    *   There is a disconnect between the convergence analysis in Theorem 2 and the experimental procedure. Theorem 2 provides a convergence rate that depends on ρ, implying a specific way to set this parameter. However, in the experiments, ρ is treated as a hyperparameter and tuned via cross-validation to achieve the best performance. This makes it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical convergence guarantee.

2.  **Experimental Baselines:** The set of competitors could be strengthened to make a more conclusive claim of state-of-the-art performance. The paper compares against Poisson processes, SVD, and tensor factorization. However, it omits several highly relevant and strong baselines from the time-aware recommendation literature. A particularly notable omission is SVD++ or its time-aware variants (e.g., TimeSVD++ by Koren), which incorporate temporal dynamics by modeling parameter drift over time. These models are widely recognized as powerful baselines for time-sensitive rating prediction and recommendation. Comparing against such a method would provide a much stronger benchmark for the proposed model's performance on the item recommendation task.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper addresses a timely and important problem in recommender systems.
*   The core idea of a low-rank Hawkes process is novel, elegant, and well-suited to the problem.
*   The work includes both a novel model and a scalable, custom optimization algorithm with theoretical analysis.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the model's effectiveness over the chosen baselines.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   The theoretical analysis contains some ambiguities (the definition of ρ*) that affect the soundness of the claims.
*   The experimental evaluation is not complete, as it misses comparisons against strong, standard time-aware collaborative filtering baselines like TimeSVD++.

### Recommendation

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a novel and significant contribution. The strengths in modeling, originality, and empirical performance are compelling. The weaknesses, particularly the missing experimental baselines and the ambiguity in the theory, are addressable. I believe the paper is a strong candidate for acceptance, but I strongly recommend that the authors address the weaknesses in the final version. Specifically, they should clarify the definition of ρ* in Theorem 1 and, most importantly, strengthen their experimental section by including a comparison with a state-of-the-art time-aware baseline such as TimeSVD++. This would make the paper's contribution even more solid and convincing.
