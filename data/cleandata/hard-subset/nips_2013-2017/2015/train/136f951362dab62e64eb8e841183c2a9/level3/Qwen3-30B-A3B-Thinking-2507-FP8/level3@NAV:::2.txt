

This paper proposes a novel framework combining self-exciting point processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation, modeling user-item interactions as a Hawkes process with low-rank intensity matrices. The authors claim to address two key questions: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and predicting users' next return times. The work builds on recent point process approaches like those by Kapoor et al. [12, 11], but extends them by incorporating latent factor models to enable generalization to new items, which is a significant limitation of previous survival analysis-based methods.

The paper demonstrates interesting theoretical contributions, including a convex optimization formulation with O(1/t) convergence rate and a novel algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. The empirical evaluation on synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) shows scalability to large datasets and promising predictive performance on both recommendation and return time prediction tasks.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that prevent it from being compelling. First, the experimental design lacks a sensible baseline: a simple ensemble of STiC and SVD should be included given their complementary strengths (STiC captures temporal dynamics but cannot generalize to new items, while SVD handles new item recommendations but ignores temporal dynamics). Second, the paper fails to explain how the γ function bandwidth parameter σ is set, and provides no sensitivity analysis to this crucial parameter. Third, the paper omits critical details about hyperparameter tuning for both the proposed method and baselines, making reproducibility difficult. Fourth, the dataset characterization is insufficient for temporal modeling - the paper lacks metrics like number of recurring events per user-item pair or temporal irregularity measures. Finally, the paper suffers from poor clarity with language issues, typos, and grammatical errors that hinder readability.

While the core idea represents an interesting combination of point processes and latent factor models (originality), and the problem has practical significance, the experimental shortcomings and presentation issues significantly undermine the contribution. The authors would strengthen this work substantially by addressing these weaknesses, particularly by including a proper baseline comparison and providing complete experimental details.
