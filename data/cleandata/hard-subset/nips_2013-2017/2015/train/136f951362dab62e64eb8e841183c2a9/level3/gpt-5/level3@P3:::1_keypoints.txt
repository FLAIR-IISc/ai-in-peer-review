- Proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework modeling time-varying user–item affinities and per-pair next-return time using low-rank Hawkes processes with convex nuclear-norm regularization.  
- Introduces a blended proximal/conditional-gradient optimization algorithm handling nonnegativity and nuclear norms with O(1/t) convergence.  
- Evaluated on one large synthetic dataset and two modest real datasets (music, e-commerce) and shows improvements in item ranking and return-time MAE versus Poisson, tensor factorization, SVD, and survival baselines.  

Strengths:
- Convex formulation: with exponential kernels the objective is convex and well-founded.  
- Well-motivated, efficient optimization with clear separation of constraints and convergence guarantees.  
- Produces both time-aware ranking and return-time predictive densities.  
- Synthetic results demonstrate scalability and recovery under low-rank ground truth.  

Weaknesses:
- Empirical validation is limited on modest-scale real data and practical impact versus simple baselines is unclear.  
- No population-level evidence that user preferences are broadly time-varying or self-exciting; diagnostic plots are insufficient.  
- Return-time motivation and operational use cases are underspecified for industry deployment.  
- Missing simple, strong baselines for return-time (e.g., last gap, mode, circadian Poisson) and no exploration of coarse categorical delays.  
- Statistical significance and effect sizes are not reported.  

Clarity and presentation issues:
- Generally clear but contains typos, abrupt example switches (retweets/link-creation) without references, and dense notation in places.  
- Theorem constants (L, D1, D2) are not instantiated and return-time thinning details (sample size, variance, cost) are missing.  

Originality:
- Novel combination of low-rank user–item parameterization with Hawkes processes in a convex recommender formulation.  
- The blended proximal/conditional-gradient scheme under nonnegativity and nuclear norms is a useful algorithmic contribution.  

Significance:
- Potentially significant if gains over strong practical baselines are contextualized and return-time has clear business applications; current contextualization is lacking.  

Suggested analyses and experiments:
- Provide population-level metrics of temporal drift (e.g., Spearman/Kendall across windows) and quantify prevalence/magnitude of self-excitation with CIs.  
- Disentangle drivers by controlling for item age/novelty and daily/weekly seasonality; add ablation studies (base-only, self-excitation-only, full).  
- Map return-time MAE to concrete use cases (notifications, re-engagement, ad bidding, caching) and show utility under a business metric.  
- Add simple baselines for return-time, consider discretizing delays into bins and evaluate classification metrics and calibration.  
- Report standard top-k metrics (Hit@k, NDCG@k) at multiple k, per-user distributions, paired significance tests, and effect sizes versus Popularity/SVD.  
- Provide optimization baselines (ADMM/Frank–Wolfe), wall-clock comparisons, memory footprint, sensitivity to ρ, and thinning sample details; test nonparametric kernel robustness.  

Minor fixes:
- Correct typos (e.g., “abel”→“able”), smooth grammar, keep focus on recommender setting or add references for cross-domain examples, and define constants/time units and pseudocode details.  

Pros (summary):
- Convex, principled Hawkes + low-rank formulation, efficient algorithm with convergence guarantees, and scalability on synthetic data.  

Cons (summary):
- Limited real-world motivation and actionable evaluation for return-time, missing simple baselines, unclear statistical/practical significance, and insufficient analysis of temporal effects.  

Overall assessment:
- Borderline (OK/marginal): solid modeling and optimization contributions but needs stronger empirical validation, clearer return-time use cases, statistical significance, and deeper temporal analyses to justify acceptance.
