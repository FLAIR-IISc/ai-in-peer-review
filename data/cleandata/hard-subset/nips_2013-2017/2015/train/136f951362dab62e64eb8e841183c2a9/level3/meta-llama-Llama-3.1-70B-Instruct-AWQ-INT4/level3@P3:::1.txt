This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation that models time-varying user preferences and predicts users' next-return time. The authors connect self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture the recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption pairs. The framework is trained with an efficient optimization algorithm and evaluated on one synthetic and two modest real datasets.

The paper tackles two important problems in recommendation systems: recommending the most desirable item at the right moment and predicting the next returning time of users to existing services. The authors provide a convex formulation of the problems and develop a new optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. The algorithm achieves the optimal O(1/t) convergence rate and scales up to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events.

The experimental results show that the proposed method achieves superb predictive performance on both synthetic and real datasets, outperforming state-of-the-art competitors such as Poisson process, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization. The results also demonstrate that the history of user activities helps in improving the predictive performance, and the proposed method can better capture the temporal dynamics of the observed sequences of events.

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. The paper lacks real-world motivation and sufficient baselines for return-time prediction. The authors only compare their method with simple baselines such as last time delay and most frequent delay, but do not consider more advanced baselines such as survival analysis or machine learning-based methods. Additionally, the presentation has some issues, including a typo and a confusing switch to retweets/link-creation examples in section 2 without references.

The paper also raises some questions about the widespread nature of time-varying preferences and the practical use cases for web companies. The authors assume that users' preferences change over time, but it is unclear how common this phenomenon is in real-world scenarios. Moreover, the motivation for predicting next-return time is not well-explained, and it is unclear how this prediction can be used in practice.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their framework and algorithm. The experimental results are also well-presented, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the results. However, the paper could benefit from more real-world motivation, additional baselines, and a deeper analysis of the results.
