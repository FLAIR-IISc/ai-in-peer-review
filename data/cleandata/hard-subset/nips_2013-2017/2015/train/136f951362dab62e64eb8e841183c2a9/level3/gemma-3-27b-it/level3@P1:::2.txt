This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by combining self-exciting point processes with low-rank models. The core idea is to model user-item interactions as a Hawkes process, capturing both inherent user preferences (baseline intensity) and temporal dependencies through a low-rank structure. The authors develop a convex optimization formulation and an efficient algorithm with an O(1/t) convergence rate, capable of scaling to large datasets. Experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate superior performance compared to several baselines.

The work builds upon a growing body of research recognizing the limitations of traditional recommendation systems that neglect temporal dynamics.  Prior work, such as survival analysis approaches (Kapoor et al., 2014; 2015) and hidden semi-Markov models, have begun to address this. However, these approaches often struggle with recommending new items, a key limitation addressed by the proposed model.  The use of low-rank models to capture underlying user and item characteristics is also common in collaborative filtering (Koren, 2009), but the integration with point processes represents a novel contribution. Related work in tensor factorization for collaborative filtering (e.g., Karatzoglou et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2013) provides a different avenue for incorporating contextual information, but doesn't directly address the temporal aspects as effectively as the Hawkes process formulation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of self-exciting point processes and low-rank models is a unique and promising approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The convex optimization formulation and convergence analysis provide a strong theoretical grounding for the method. The claimed O(1/t) convergence rate is a significant achievement.
* **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate the scalability of their algorithm to large datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.
* **Empirical Performance:**  The experimental results show that the proposed model outperforms several baseline methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* **Generality:** The framework's ability to incorporate additional contextual information (user profiles, spatial features) is a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Baseline Comparison:**  The comparison to existing baselines could be strengthened. A more sensible baseline would be an ensemble of STiC (Kapoor et al., 2014) and SVD, leveraging their complementary strengths.  STiC excels at temporal modeling, while SVD captures overall user-item affinities.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning. Specifically, the choice of the triggering kernel bandwidth (σ) is not adequately explained. An analysis of the model's sensitivity to σ and other key hyperparameters (e.g., latent dimension) is crucial. The tuning method for these parameters should be clarified.
* **Dataset Characterization:** A more in-depth characterization of the datasets used would be beneficial.  Metrics such as the number of recurring events per user/item pair, and measures of temporal irregularity (e.g., coefficient of variation of inter-event times) would provide valuable context for evaluating the model's performance.
* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from clarity issues. The writing contains language imperfections, typos, and grammatical errors that hinder understanding. A careful revision to improve readability is necessary.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the overall performance is promising, a more detailed error analysis would be helpful.  Identifying the types of recommendation errors the model makes (e.g., recommending items too early, too late, or inappropriate items) could provide insights for future improvements.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in recommender systems.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* The scalability of the algorithm is a significant advantage.
* The potential for incorporating additional contextual information enhances the practical value of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a strong baseline comparison and a detailed hyperparameter analysis weaken the empirical evaluation.
* The clarity and presentation of the paper need improvement.
* The limited dataset characterization makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the findings.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The proposed framework is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically promising. However, the weaknesses related to baseline comparison, hyperparameter tuning, dataset characterization, and clarity detract from the overall quality of the work.  With revisions addressing these concerns, this paper could be a strong addition to NIPS.  

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the concerns outlined above, particularly focusing on strengthening the baseline comparison, providing a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, and improving the clarity of the writing.
