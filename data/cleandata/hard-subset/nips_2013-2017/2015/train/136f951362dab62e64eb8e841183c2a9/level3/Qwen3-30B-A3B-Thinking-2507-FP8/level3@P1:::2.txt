

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption. The authors address two key problems: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and predicting the next returning time of users to services. The model represents the intensity function for each user-item pair as a combination of a baseline intensity (capturing long-term preferences) and a self-exciting component (modeling the influence of past events), with both components assumed to have low-rank structures. The authors formulate the problem as a convex optimization with nuclear norm regularization and develop an efficient algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence rate.

The paper makes an interesting contribution by bridging two distinct areas: temporal point processes (specifically Hawkes processes) and collaborative filtering. This connects to prior work on temporal recommendation like [12, 11] but differs significantly in that their approach can recommend new items (unlike survival analysis approaches) and captures the full history of events rather than just the last one. The approach also relates to low-rank matrix factorization methods [2, 23, 25] but extends them to incorporate temporal dynamics.

Strengths include the clear technical contribution of the convex formulation and optimization algorithm, which is well-motivated and theoretically grounded. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is rigorous and demonstrates the algorithm achieves optimal convergence rates for both components of the problem. The empirical results on synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) demonstrate scalability to large problems (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events) and superior performance compared to baselines on both recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks. The authors also provide useful insights about how their Hawkes model better explains observed temporal patterns than i.i.d. assumptions used in prior work.

However, several weaknesses limit the paper's impact. The experimental section lacks a sensible baseline: a simple ensemble of STiC [12] (which handles temporal dynamics but only for observed items) and SVD (which captures user-item preferences but not dynamics) would provide a better comparison point to demonstrate the value of the proposed joint modeling approach. The paper is unclear about how the bandwidth parameter σ for the triggering kernel is set, with no analysis of sensitivity to this key hyperparameter. Similarly, there's insufficient detail on hyperparameter tuning for both the proposed method and baselines (e.g., latent dimension, λ, β). The dataset characterization is also inadequate for temporal modeling—critical details like number of recurring events per user-item pair, temporal irregularity, and event density are missing.

The clarity of the paper is problematic, with multiple language issues, typos, and grammatical errors that make it difficult to follow some technical arguments. For example, the description of the triggering kernel parametrization in Section 3.3 contains confusing notation and incomplete explanations.

Regarding the NIPS review criteria: Quality is reasonably good with solid theoretical analysis and experiments, but the lack of proper baselines and hyperparameter details weakens the empirical validation. Clarity is poor due to language issues. Originality is strong—the combination of point processes and low-rank models is novel and well-motivated. Significance is moderate; while the problem is practically important, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how the approach advances the state of the art beyond incremental improvements.

The main contribution is the convex formulation and optimization algorithm, which has potential applications beyond recommendation (e.g., to general nonnegative matrix rank minimization problems). However, the paper's presentation and experimental evaluation prevent it from making a strong case for acceptance. The core technical contribution is sound and interesting, but the presentation issues and incomplete experimental analysis prevent it from being a compelling contribution to NIPS.

I recommend rejection due to the following reasons: 1) Insufficient baseline comparison (missing sensible ensemble baseline), 2) Incomplete hyperparameter analysis (especially for σ), 3) Poor clarity with language/grammar issues, and 4) Inadequate dataset characterization for temporal modeling. While the core idea is novel and the technical contribution is sound, these weaknesses significantly undermine the paper's impact and scientific contribution. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be considered for NIPS.
