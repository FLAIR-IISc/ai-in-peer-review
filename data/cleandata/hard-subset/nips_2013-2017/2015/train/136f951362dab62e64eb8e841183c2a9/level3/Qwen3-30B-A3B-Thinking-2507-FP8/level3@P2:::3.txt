

# Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes (Hawkes processes) with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption data. The authors address two key questions: (1) how to recommend the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) how to predict the next returning time of users to services. They formulate the problem as a convex optimization with nuclear norm regularization for low-rank structure, develop an efficient algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and demonstrate superior performance on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper builds upon the growing body of work on temporal recommendation systems. While previous approaches like SVD [23] and tensor factorization [3] have considered temporal aspects, they either treat time as a separate dimension or fail to model the self-exciting nature of user activities. The closest related work is Kapoor et al. [11, 12], who use survival analysis for return time prediction but cannot generalize to recommend new items. The authors correctly identify this limitation and position their work as addressing the critical gap of recommending new items while modeling temporal dynamics.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The convex formulation with nuclear norm regularization provides a principled approach to low-rank matrix estimation. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 2) establishes an optimal O(1/t) convergence rate for the proposed algorithm, which is a significant contribution to the optimization literature.

2. **Empirical Performance:** The experiments on synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) demonstrate clear advantages over baselines including Poisson processes, SVD, tensor factorization, and STiC. The ability to handle millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events is particularly impressive.

3. **Practical Impact:** The work directly addresses a critical gap in recommendation systems where temporal dynamics are increasingly important for user engagement. The ability to predict return times has clear applications in marketing and healthcare (as noted in the paper).

4. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, with clear mathematical formulations and well-organized exposition.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Theoretical Concerns:** Equation (8) is unclear about which parameter is being maximized (it appears to be over ρ, but this is not specified). More critically, the theoretical threshold ρ* may be unbounded in some cases, which could undermine the convergence guarantees. Theorem 2's prescribed ρ is not used in the experiments, weakening the connection between theory and practice.

2. **Experimental Baselines:** The paper omits SVD++, a strong time-aware baseline that explicitly incorporates temporal dynamics. Including this would strengthen the empirical comparison and better demonstrate the novelty of the approach.

3. **Practical Implementation:** While the paper claims the algorithm scales to large problems, the experimental details about runtime and memory requirements for the largest datasets are insufficient. The paper should provide more concrete evidence of scalability.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation
- Strong theoretical guarantees with optimal convergence rate
- Superior empirical performance on multiple datasets
- Clear practical significance for real-world recommendation systems
- Well-written and technically sound paper

*Con:*
- Theoretical threshold ρ* is not properly addressed in experiments
- Missing comparison with SVD++ as a strong time-aware baseline
- Unclear definition of ρ* in Equation (8) may be problematic
- Insufficient detail on scalability metrics for large-scale deployment

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The technical approach is novel, well-motivated, and theoretically sound, with compelling empirical results. The main weaknesses are the theoretical concern about ρ* and the omission of SVD++ as a baseline. However, these issues do not undermine the core contribution, which is a principled framework for time-sensitive recommendation that outperforms existing methods.

The paper clearly advances the state of the art in recommendation systems by explicitly modeling temporal dynamics in a way that enables both time-sensitive item recommendations and return time predictions. The scalability of the proposed algorithm to large datasets further enhances its practical relevance.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical insights, empirical results, and practical significance make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. With minor revisions addressing the theoretical concerns and adding the SVD++ baseline, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the conference.
