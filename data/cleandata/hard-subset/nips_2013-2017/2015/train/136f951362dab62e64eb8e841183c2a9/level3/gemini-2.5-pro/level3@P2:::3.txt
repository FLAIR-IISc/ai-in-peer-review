This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, focusing on two key tasks: recommending the right item at the right moment and predicting a user's next return time to a service. The authors propose a novel model that combines a low-rank matrix factorization approach with self-exciting Hawkes point processes. For each user-item pair, user activity is modeled as a Hawkes process, where the intensity function (the instantaneous rate of an event) is composed of a constant baseline intensity and a self-exciting term that depends on the history of past events.

To handle data sparsity and enable generalization, the baseline intensities and self-excitation coefficients for all user-item pairs are collected into two matrices, Λ0 and A, which are assumed to be low-rank. The model parameters are learned by optimizing the log-likelihood of the observed event data, regularized by the nuclear norms of the parameter matrices to enforce the low-rank structure. To solve this convex optimization problem efficiently, the authors propose a novel algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving an O(1/t) convergence rate and demonstrating linear scalability. The proposed model is compared to several baselines, including Poisson processes, a hidden semi-Markov model (STiC), SVD, and tensor factorization, on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing superior performance on both recommendation and return-time prediction tasks.

This work relates to a growing body of literature on temporal dynamics in recommender systems. It significantly extends the work of Kapoor et al. [11, 12], which uses survival analysis on individual user-item streams, by creating a unified model that can transfer knowledge across users and items and recommend items a user has not previously interacted with. It also provides a more fine-grained temporal model than tensor factorization methods [3, 25], which typically operate on discretized time intervals.

### **Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a very important and practical problem in modern recommender systems. The proposed combination of low-rank models and multi-dimensional Hawkes processes is a novel and elegant approach. It provides a principled, continuous-time framework for modeling user activities that captures both long-term static preferences (via the base intensity) and short-term, history-dependent dynamics (via the self-exciting kernel). The ability to address both item recommendation and return-time prediction within a single model is a significant strength.

*   **Technical Quality:** The formulation of the problem as a convex optimization problem is sound. The development of a custom, scalable optimization algorithm that cleverly combines proximal and conditional gradient steps is a strong technical contribution. The algorithm's design addresses the challenge of handling non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization simultaneously and may be of independent interest. The empirical evaluation is thorough, including experiments on both synthetic and large-scale real-world datasets, and demonstrates the model's effectiveness and scalability.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-articulated, the model is introduced logically from its basic components, and the experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Clarity of Theoretical Claims:** The theoretical analysis has some points that require clarification. In Theorem 1, the condition for the equivalence of the two optimization problems depends on a threshold ρ*. This threshold is defined as a maximum, but it is unclear what set the maximization is over. The authors should explicitly state this and discuss whether ρ* is guaranteed to be finite. Without this clarification, the soundness of the claim in Theorem 1 is difficult to fully assess.

*   **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The convergence guarantee in Theorem 2 is derived for a specific setting of the algorithm's parameters, including the penalty parameter ρ. However, in the experiments, ρ is treated as a hyperparameter and tuned to achieve the best performance. This disconnect weakens the practical relevance of the theoretical result. The authors should comment on this choice and perhaps report on the sensitivity of the model to the choice of ρ.

*   **Experimental Baselines:** While the experimental comparison is solid, it could be strengthened by including other strong, time-aware baselines from the recommendation literature. A particularly relevant comparison would be against models like SVD++, which incorporates implicit feedback, or other factorization-based methods that explicitly model temporal dynamics (e.g., Koren's work on "Collaborative Filtering with Temporal Dynamics," cited as [16]). Comparing against such established methods would provide a more robust benchmark for the claimed performance improvements.

### **Summary of Arguments**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and timely problem with a novel and principled modeling approach.
*   The combination of low-rank models and Hawkes processes is elegant and powerful.
*   The paper includes a novel and scalable optimization algorithm with theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   Empirical results are strong and demonstrate superior performance over several relevant baselines on multiple datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Some aspects of the theoretical analysis lack full clarity and rigor (specifically regarding Theorem 1).
*   The experimental evaluation is missing comparisons to some key time-aware recommendation baselines, which makes it harder to situate the contribution with respect to the state of the art.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and significant contribution to the field of recommender systems. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the model and the strong empirical results, outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses are addressable and would further strengthen an already compelling paper.
