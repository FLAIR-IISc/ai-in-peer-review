## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel point-process collaborative filtering model that leverages both constant and self-exciting intensities via user/item latent vectors. The authors aim to address the often-overlooked temporal dynamics of user-item interactions, tackling both time-sensitive recommendation and next-visit prediction. The approach is theoretically grounded, with a convex optimization formulation and an algorithm designed for scalability, and empirically evaluated on synthetic and real datasets. 

The core idea of combining Hawkes processes with low-rank models is interesting and novel. The problem addressed is certainly significant, as incorporating temporal aspects into recommendation systems is crucial for improving user engagement and system efficacy. The formulation offers a potentially powerful way to capture complex user behavior patterns beyond simple static preferences.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the algorithm is claimed to scale well, the experimental section lacks a crucial baseline: a sensible ensemble of STiC [12] and SVD, given their complementary strengths as pointed out by the authors themselves. The justification for selecting the bandwidth parameter σ for the triggering kernel (γ function) is unclear, and a sensitivity analysis is absent. This is a significant oversight, as σ directly impacts the model’s ability to capture temporal dependencies. Furthermore, details on the selection/tuning of other key hyperparameters (e.g., latent dimension) are missing for both the proposed method and the baselines, hindering reproducibility and a fair comparison. 

The dataset characterization is also lacking. More details on the temporal properties of the datasets—such as the number of recurring events per user/item pair and the irregularity of the event timings—would provide valuable context for interpreting the results. The paper’s clarity is hindered by numerous language issues, typos, and grammatical errors, making it difficult to follow the technical details at times. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of point processes and latent factor models.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem with a theoretically sound approach.
*   Demonstrated scalability on large datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Missing key baseline comparison.
*   Lack of analysis regarding the sensitivity of the γ function bandwidth.
*   Insufficient details on hyperparameter tuning.
*   Limited dataset characterization.
*   Poor clarity and numerous language errors.



**Overall Assessment:** Reasonably good but not great. The paper presents a promising approach, but the lack of thorough experimentation and clarity prevent it from being a strong contribution at this time. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the outlined weaknesses and resubmit. The core idea is valuable, but requires substantial refinement and more rigorous validation.
