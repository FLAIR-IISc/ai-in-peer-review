This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by combining self-exciting temporal point processes (specifically, Hawkes processes) with low-rank models. The goal is to address two key questions: what item to recommend at a given moment, and when a user is likely to return to a service. The core idea is to model the event intensity for each user-item pair as a combination of a static baseline intensity and a self-exciting component that depends on the history of past interactions. The baseline intensity matrix and the self-exciting influence matrix are both assumed to be low-rank, allowing the model to generalize to unobserved user-item pairs, a key limitation of prior work like STiC [11, 12]. The authors formulate parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization and propose an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods to solve it. The model's effectiveness is evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets for item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem in recommender systems: incorporating temporal dynamics to provide timely recommendations. The combination of Hawkes processes for modeling recurrent activities and low-rank matrix factorization for collaborative filtering is novel and elegant. This unified framework for simultaneously predicting "what" and "when" is a valuable contribution that moves beyond static rating prediction.

2.  **Modeling Approach:** The model's formulation is intuitive. It decomposes the event intensity into a static base preference (`Λ0`) and a dynamic, history-dependent component (`A`). This allows the model to capture both long-term user interests and short-term, self-exciting behavioral patterns (e.g., repeated listening to a song or rebuying a product).

3.  **Technical Contribution:** The proposed optimization algorithm, which combines proximal and conditional gradient methods to handle non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm regularization simultaneously, is a solid technical contribution. The theoretical analysis showing an O(1/t) convergence rate could be of independent interest to the optimization community.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper's clarity is hampered by several issues that also affect reproducibility.
    *   The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing that make it difficult to read. A thorough proofreading by a native English speaker is strongly recommended.
    *   Crucial experimental details are missing. The selection of the triggering kernel's bandwidth parameter `σ` is not explained. This is a critical hyperparameter that likely has a significant impact on performance, as it controls the decay rate of past events' influence. A sensitivity analysis for `σ` would be necessary to understand its effect.
    *   The paper states that hyperparameters (e.g., `λ`, `β`, `ρ`, latent dimension) for all models were "tuned to give the best performance," but the methodology for this tuning is not described. The lack of detail on the validation procedure, search space, and strategy makes it impossible to reproduce the results and difficult to assess the fairness of the comparisons.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate promising results, the evaluation could be strengthened in several ways.
    *   **Missing Baseline:** The method is designed to be effective at both item ranking (a strength of SVD) and returning-time prediction for previously seen items (a strength of STiC). A powerful and practical baseline would be a simple ensemble of these two methods: use a standard matrix factorization model for the ranking task and STiC for the returning-time prediction task on items with a history. Comparing against such an ensemble would provide a more challenging and realistic benchmark.
    *   **Dataset Characterization:** The paper focuses on a temporal problem, but the datasets are described with little temporal context. To better understand the results and the nature of the problem, it would be very helpful to include statistics on the temporal dynamics, such as the distribution of the number of events per user-item pair, the average and variance of inter-event times, and the overall sequence lengths. This would help justify the need for a sophisticated point process model.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem in recommender systems.
*   The proposed model, combining low-rank factorization with Hawkes processes, is novel, well-motivated, and elegant.
*   The optimization algorithm and its theoretical analysis are a solid technical contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper's clarity is poor due to numerous language issues.
*   The experimental section lacks crucial details on hyperparameter selection (especially the kernel bandwidth `σ`), which harms reproducibility and weakens the empirical claims.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more convincing with the inclusion of a stronger ensemble baseline and more detailed characterization of the datasets.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and novel approach to a significant problem. The core ideas are strong, and the technical contributions are valuable. However, the paper is held back by a lack of clarity in its writing and insufficient detail in its experimental methodology. If the authors can thoroughly revise the manuscript to improve its readability and provide the missing experimental details (especially regarding hyperparameter tuning and a stronger baseline), this would be a strong contribution to the conference.
