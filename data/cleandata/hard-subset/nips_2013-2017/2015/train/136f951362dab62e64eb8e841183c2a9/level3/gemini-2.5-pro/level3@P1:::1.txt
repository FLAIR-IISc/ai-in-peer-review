This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models. The work addresses two central problems: (1) recommending the most relevant item at a specific moment in time, and (2) predicting a user's next return time to a service or item. The core idea is to model the sequence of user-item interactions (e.g., listening to a song, purchasing a product) as a Hawkes process, where the intensity function `λ_ui(t)` for a user-item pair has a static baseline component and a dynamic, history-dependent component triggered by past events. To handle data sparsity and enable generalization to unobserved pairs, the parameters governing these components for all users and items are assumed to reside in low-rank matrices (Λ0 and A), which are learned via nuclear norm regularization. The authors propose a new, efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods to solve the resulting convex optimization problem, and they provide a convergence analysis showing an O(1/t) rate. The model is evaluated on one synthetic and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) for both the recommendation and return-time prediction tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its novel formulation. The unification of Hawkes processes for modeling temporal event dynamics with low-rank matrix models for collaborative filtering is an elegant and powerful idea. It directly addresses the temporal dimension of user behavior, an aspect often overlooked or simplified in traditional recommendation systems. This approach provides a principled way to model both long-term (baseline intensity) and short-term (self-exciting) user preferences.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem as a convex optimization objective with nuclear norm regularization is well-justified. The proposed optimization algorithm is a non-trivial contribution that efficiently handles the combination of non-negativity constraints and nuclear norm penalties, which is a challenging problem. The accompanying convergence analysis further strengthens the paper's technical contribution and could be of independent interest to the optimization community.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The background on temporal point processes and the step-by-step development of the Low Rank Hawkes Process model are clear and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Motivation and Problem Framing:** The motivation for both tasks could be significantly strengthened.
    *   The paper claims that time-varying preferences are a central challenge, but it does not provide data-driven evidence from the used datasets to support this claim. Is this variation primarily driven by new popular items appearing (a trend effect), or are there more complex, recurring patterns at the individual user level? An exploratory analysis of the datasets to justify the need for such a sophisticated temporal model would make the motivation more compelling.
    *   The practical utility of predicting the *exact* next return time is not clearly articulated. While ad bidding is mentioned, the link is tenuous. How would a web company concretely use this prediction? Are there specific marketing strategies or resource allocation plans that depend on knowing a user will return at a precise time, versus simply knowing they are likely to return soon? The motivation here feels speculative.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup is missing key baselines and deeper analysis.
    *   For the return-time prediction task, the evaluation lacks simple but important heuristic baselines. For example, how does the model compare against predicting the next delay as the *same as the last delay*, or the *median/most frequent delay* for that user-item pair? Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the true value added by the model's complexity.
    *   The experiments focus on predicting the exact return time, but it might be more practical and tractable to predict a coarser time category (e.g., user will return within an hour, a day, a week). Exploring this alternative framing could lead to more robust and useful results.
    *   For the item recommendation task, while the proposed model shows improvement over SVD and a Poisson (popularity) baseline, the practical significance of these gains is not discussed. Is the improvement in rank substantial enough to be perceived by a user? A more qualitative analysis or a discussion of the practical impact would be beneficial. The Poisson baseline is noted as being a "hard baseline," suggesting that simple popularity is already a strong performer, which may diminish the perceived impact of the proposed model's gains.

3.  **Presentation:** There are minor issues with the presentation. For example, the "Related Work" section introduces the model of Kapoor et al. but does not clearly position the current work against it until later in the paper. A more direct comparison of methodological differences and advantages in the related work section itself would improve clarity.

In summary, the paper presents a technically solid and novel approach to an important problem. The core contribution—the Low Rank Hawkes Process model and its efficient optimization algorithm—is significant. However, the work is let down by underdeveloped motivation for the specific tasks it addresses and an experimental evaluation that, while positive, lacks the simple baselines and deeper analysis needed to fully convince the reader of its practical superiority and impact. The paper is therefore on the borderline. With a strengthened motivation and more thorough experimental comparison, it would be a very strong contribution.
