- Proposes a novel time-sensitive recommendation model that predicts both what and when by modeling user-item interaction sequences as self-exciting Hawkes processes with low-rank baseline and excitation matrices for collaborative generalization.  
- Uses a convex formulation with nuclear-norm regularization and an efficient optimization algorithm combining proximal-gradient and conditional-gradient methods, with an O(1/t) convergence guarantee.  
- Novel direct integration of continuous-time point processes with latent factor models, distinguishing it from STiC and tensor factorization approaches.  
- Strength: original and elegant synthesis of Hawkes processes and low-rank collaborative filtering, enabling both static preferences and dynamic history-dependent behavior.  
- Strength: addresses a practically important problem (predicting return times and timing recommendations) with potential impact on engagement and marketing.  
- Strength: technically solid convex formulation and a non-trivial optimization algorithm supported by convergence analysis.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is incomplete—missing a crucial baseline (e.g., ensemble of STiC for known pairs and SVD for held-out items) that could be a strong competitor.  
- Weakness: lacks transparency on hyperparameter selection and sensitivity, notably the triggering kernel bandwidth σ and regularization/latent-dimension settings, hindering reproducibility and robustness assessment.  
- Weakness: insufficient dataset characterization (e.g., inter-event time distributions, recurrence counts, burstiness) needed to motivate Hawkes modeling and interpret results.  
- Weakness: paper clarity suffers from numerous language, grammar, and presentation issues; descriptions of the algorithm and experiments need clearer exposition.  
- Argument for acceptance: introduces a well-motivated, novel framework that combines point processes and collaborative filtering and could spur future research.  
- Argument for acceptance: presents a non-trivial optimization contribution of independent interest with theoretical guarantees.  
- Argument for rejection: empirical shortcomings (missing baselines, hyperparameter opacity) and inadequate dataset analysis reduce confidence in claimed advantages.  
- Argument for rejection: poor presentation and language issues degrade readability and overall quality.  
- Overall: technically interesting and potentially impactful but currently undermined by incomplete experiments and poor presentation; reviewer would reconsider after substantial experimental revision and improved clarity.
