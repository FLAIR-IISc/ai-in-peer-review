## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper tackles the problem of time-sensitive recommendation by proposing a framework that combines self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture temporal patterns in user-item interactions, along with an efficient optimization algorithm. The authors aim to address two related questions: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and predicting a user’s next return time to a service. This builds upon existing work in recommender systems that traditionally focus on static preferences, and connects to recent research leveraging temporal point processes for modeling user behavior (e.g., Kapoor et al., 2014, 2015).

The primary strength of the paper lies in its attempt to unify two interesting problems – recommendation and return-time prediction – within a single, theoretically grounded framework. The proposed convex optimization formulation and the resulting algorithm, with its claimed O(1/t) convergence rate, demonstrate technical merit. The authors also acknowledge potential for incorporating additional contextual information, expanding the model’s applicability.

However, several weaknesses limit the contribution. First, the justification for the core premise – that time-varying preferences are widespread and necessitate this complexity – is weak. A more detailed analysis of the datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) characterizing the *types* of temporal preference shifts (e.g., novelty seeking, seasonal effects, changing needs) would strengthen this claim. It’s unclear if the observed dynamics warrant a model explicitly predicting return times. The practical motivation for predicting next-return time remains underdeveloped; the marketing application feels somewhat speculative without more concrete examples or analysis.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient baselines. Simple baselines for return-time prediction, such as using the last time delay or the most frequent delay, are missing. Considering coarser delay categories (e.g., short/medium/long/very long) might also be more realistic. The clarity regarding the significance of the improvements over SVD and the Poisson process baseline is lacking. It's difficult to assess if the observed gains are substantial and user-perceivable, or merely statistically significant but practically negligible.

Finally, the paper suffers from minor presentation issues: a typo ("abel" to "able") and a somewhat jarring, unsupported jump to examples of retweets and link-creation in Section 2.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation.
*   Efficient optimization algorithm with theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   Potential for incorporating additional contextual information.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Weak justification for the prevalence of time-varying preferences.
*   Unclear practical motivation for return-time prediction.
*   Insufficient baseline comparisons.
*   Lack of clarity regarding the significance of experimental results.

**Overall:** The paper addresses two interesting problems, but the lack of strong motivation, sufficient baselines, and deeper analysis leads to a judgment of **OK/Marginal**. More compelling justification and clearer demonstration of practical impact are needed for acceptance.
