This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation method that models time-varying user preferences and predicts users' next-return time. The authors develop an efficient optimization algorithm and evaluate their method on one synthetic and two modest real datasets.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a novel convex formulation that connects self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture the recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption pairs. The authors show that their model can be efficiently estimated via a convex optimization problem, and they develop an efficient algorithm that maintains an O(1/t) convergence rate.

The paper's strengths include its ability to model time-varying user preferences and predict users' next-return time. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their model and algorithm, and they demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on both synthetic and real datasets.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. Firstly, the authors do not provide sufficient justification for their claim that time-varying preferences are widespread. They should provide a more detailed analysis of their datasets to identify the causes of time-varying preferences, such as new popular songs or other patterns.

Secondly, the motivation for predicting next-return time is unclear. The authors should explain practical use cases for web companies and how predicting next-return time can be useful in these scenarios.

Thirdly, the paper lacks simple baselines for return-time prediction, such as last time delay or most frequent delay. The authors should consider coarser, easier-to-predict delay categories, such as short/medium/long/very long.

Fourthly, the experimental results lack clarity on significance and practical impact compared to SVD and simple popular-item baselines. The authors should provide more detailed analysis to demonstrate the effectiveness of their method.

Lastly, there are some presentation issues, such as a typo "abel"→"able" and a confusing, unsupported switch to retweets/link-creation examples in section 2 without references.

In terms of originality, the paper's connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models is novel and interesting. However, the idea of modeling time-varying user preferences is not new, and the authors should provide more context and comparison to existing work in this area.

In terms of significance, the paper's results are modest and may not have a substantial impact on the field. The authors should provide more analysis and justification for their method's effectiveness and practical applications.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but there are some issues with presentation and organization. The authors should provide more clear and concise explanations of their model and algorithm and avoid unnecessary switches to unrelated examples.

Overall, I would rate this paper as OK/marginal. While it tackles two interesting problems, it lacks real-world motivation, sufficient baselines, and deeper analysis. The authors should address these issues to improve the paper's quality and impact.

* The paper proposes a novel convex formulation that connects self-exciting point processes and low-rank models.
