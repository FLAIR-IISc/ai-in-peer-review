Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles time-sensitive recommendation by modeling each user–item interaction as a self-exciting point process whose intensity is the sum of a constant (baseline) component and a self-exciting component triggered by past events. Concretely, for pair (u, i), λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) Σj γ(t, tu,ij), with γ often taken as an exponential kernel exp(−(t − tj)/σ). To generalize across sparse dyads, the matrices Λ0 and A are assumed low-rank and are estimated via a convex program with nuclear-norm penalties and nonnegativity constraints. The authors develop a scalable optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient/Frank–Wolfe (for nuclear norms), and prove an O(1/t) convergence rate. The approach is evaluated on both synthetic data and several real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) for two tasks: (a) time-aware item ranking and (b) return-time prediction.

This work connects two influential lines of research: (i) temporal point processes, especially Hawkes processes for self-excitation and triggering effects (e.g., Zhou et al., AISTATS/ICML), and (ii) low-rank collaborative filtering and tensor approaches (e.g., timeSVD++, Poisson/tensor factorization). It is also related to survival analysis/hidden semi-Markov approaches for return-time prediction (Kapoor et al., STiC and hazard-based models), but unlike those, it yields generalization to unseen items via shared latent structure.

Strengths
- Interesting and novel combination: a point-process CF model that cleanly decomposes long-term affinity and short-term excitation, tied together via low-rank structure. This is a meaningful advance over pairwise survival/HMM approaches that do not generalize across items.
- Convex estimation with a practical, scalable algorithm that avoids full SVDs per iteration; the prox–FW blend is well-motivated, and the convergence analysis is clear at a high level.
- Tasks and metrics are relevant: time-aware ranking and return-time prediction. The time-change (QQ) diagnostic is a nice touch showing better fit than i.i.d. gap models.
- Results suggest improved accuracy over Poisson, SVD, tensor baselines, and STiC on the tasks where each applies.

Weaknesses
- Baselines: missing an obvious, competitive ensemble that exploits complementary strengths of STiC (per-pair temporal dynamics) and SVD (global generalization). A simple, sensible baseline is to re-rank SVD scores by a function of STiC’s predicted hazard/return-time (e.g., linearly combine z-scored SVD scores with −E[return time] or instantaneous hazard at t). This would directly test whether the proposed model is more than the sum of established components.
- Kernel bandwidth σ: unclear how σ is chosen and no sensitivity analysis. Since σ strongly controls short-term excitation, the lack of ablation raises concerns about robustness and potential overfitting.
- Hyperparameter selection: insufficient detail on tuning λ, β (nuclear-norm weights), ρ (penalty parameter), latent rank for baselines (SVD/tensor), stopping criteria, and step-size/L estimation. Reproducibility and fairness across methods are hard to assess.
- Dataset characterization is thin for temporal modeling. Readers need distributions of events per (u,i), inter-event time statistics (mean/variance/CV, burstiness), seasonality/periodicity indicators, and cold-start proportions. These affect both identifiability and task difficulty.
- Theoretical details: The equivalence between the constrained and penalized formulations depends on ρ > ρ*, but ρ* is problem-dependent and not estimable in practice; guidance or empirical evidence on choosing ρ is missing.
- Limited ablations: no study of the contribution of baseline vs excitation (e.g., α=0 vs Λ0=0), kernel choice (exponential vs Weibull/Log-logistic), or low-rank strength. 
- Clarity and writing quality: the paper contains numerous language issues and typos, and notation is sometimes inconsistent, which obscures important details. Examples: “O(1/ )” in the abstract (missing t), mixing γ0 and Λ0 for baseline intensity, αu,i vs A(u,i), inconsistent use of T for horizon vs history, “en present,” “Proceeedings,” spacing/formatting issues in equations.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a reasonable convex formulation and a scalable algorithm. However, the empirical evaluation falls short of best practice: missing a strong ensemble baseline, incomplete hyperparameter/sensitivity reporting, and limited dataset statistics. The claims would be more convincing with ablations and robustness analyses, and clearer guidance on ρ and σ.

Clarity
The high-level ideas are understandable, but the paper needs significant editing for grammar, notation consistency, and completeness of experimental details. Re-structuring the modeling and optimization sections to define all symbols before use and unifying notation would help. Reproducibility would benefit from a clear hyperparameter tuning protocol and resource/time reporting.

Originality
The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank collaborative filtering in a convex framework is original and interesting relative to prior work at NIPS and elsewhere. The optimization blend is a useful engineering contribution.

Significance
The problem is important, and the approach is practically valuable. If the empirical evaluation is strengthened and the presentation improved, this line of work could be adopted by practitioners. As it stands, significance is moderate due to evaluation gaps.

Questions for the authors
- How is σ chosen on each dataset? Please provide a sensitivity analysis over a reasonable grid and report the impact on both tasks.
- How are λ, β, ρ tuned? Any heuristics for setting ρ to approximate the constrained problem? How sensitive are results to ρ?
- What latent ranks and regularization settings were used for SVD and tensor baselines, and how were they tuned?
- Please report dataset statistics: distribution of events per (u,i), fraction of repeated dyads, inter-event CV and burstiness, seasonality (diurnal/weekly), and cold-start proportions.
- What are the stopping criteria and runtime/memory on each dataset? How are step sizes (ηk) and Lipschitz constants estimated?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add the STiC+SVD ensemble baseline; consider also a Poisson CF + STiC variant to isolate the benefit of excitation.
- Provide ablations: (i) baseline-only vs excitation-only; (ii) σ sensitivity; (iii) kernel family alternatives; (iv) effect of nuclear-norm strength.
- Expand metrics: calibration plots (time-change QQ) with confidence bands; top-k precision/recall/MAP for ranking; time-aware NDCG; median absolute error.
- Improve dataset characterization and describe train/validation/test protocols precisely (including entirely held-out sequences), along with compute budgets and seeds.
- Substantially edit for clarity and fix typos/notation inconsistencies.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and relevant modeling contribution; principled and scalable optimization; promising results on multiple datasets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete experimental design (missing ensemble baseline), scarce hyperparameter/sensitivity details, weak dataset characterization, and clarity issues reduce confidence in the empirical claims.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is compelling and likely impactful, but the evaluation and presentation need a solid round of strengthening to meet NIPS standards.
