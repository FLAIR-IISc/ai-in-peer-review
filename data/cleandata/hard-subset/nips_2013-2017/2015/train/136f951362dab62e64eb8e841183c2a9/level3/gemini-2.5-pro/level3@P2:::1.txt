This paper addresses two key problems in time-sensitive recommendation: recommending the right item at the right moment and predicting a user's next return time to a service. The authors propose a novel framework that models recurrent user activities by connecting self-exciting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models. For each user-item pair, a Hawkes process is used to model the event timeline, where a baseline intensity captures long-term preference and a self-exciting component captures short-term influence from past activities. To generalize across all user-item pairs and handle sparsity, the baseline intensity and self-excitation coefficient matrices are assumed to be low-rank. The authors formulate parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization and develop an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving a fast convergence rate. The approach is evaluated on one synthetic and three real-world datasets, showing superior performance against several baselines, including static matrix factorization and recent temporal models. This work relates to recent efforts in modeling temporal dynamics in recommendations, such as those using survival analysis [11, 12], but distinguishes itself by being able to recommend new items, not just those a user has previously interacted with.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty and Originality:** The combination of self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to solve time-sensitive recommendation problems is novel and provides an elegant mathematical framework for a challenging problem. This moves beyond traditional static rating prediction and tackles the more nuanced temporal aspects of user engagement.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It presents a convex formulation for the learning problem and provides a new, efficient optimization algorithm with theoretical convergence guarantees. The algorithm's ability to scale to large datasets is a significant strength and could be of independent interest.
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem. Understanding and predicting when a user will return and what they will want at that specific moment is highly valuable for improving user engagement and for applications like targeted marketing.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Motivation and Problem Formulation:**
    *   The paper's core assumption is that user preferences are continuously time-varying, but the motivation for this is not sufficiently supported with data. An exploratory analysis of the datasets to show *how* and *why* preferences change would significantly strengthen the paper. For instance, are the variations due to new popular items, seasonality, or other identifiable patterns?
    *   The motivation for predicting the *exact* next return time is unclear from a practical standpoint. The paper mentions applications like ad bidding, but does not explain how a precise prediction would be used. It is not obvious what a web company would do with this information. It would be more compelling to either provide concrete use cases or consider predicting coarser, more actionable time intervals (e.g., short, medium, long delays).

*   **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The evaluation is missing simple but crucial baselines for the return-time prediction task. Strong baselines would include predicting the next time delay based on the *last observed delay* for that user-item pair, or the *most frequent/mean delay*. Without these, it is difficult to assess the true contribution of the sophisticated Hawkes process model.
    *   The practical impact of the performance gains is not well-articulated. While the proposed model outperforms SVD and other baselines on metrics like rank and MAE, it is not clear if these improvements are substantial enough to be perceived by a user. For example, is the improvement in recommendation ranking significant enough to change the top-k list in a meaningful way compared to a simple popularity-based recommendation?

*   **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The paper's presentation could be improved. At times, the examples used to illustrate concepts feel disconnected from the core recommendation task (e.g., switching to retweets or link-creation examples, as mentioned in Section 2 of the full paper, can be confusing without a clear bridge to the main problem).
    *   There appear to be minor typos in the manuscript (e.g., "abel" should be "able"), which should be corrected in a final version.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for time-sensitive recommendation, combining Hawkes processes with low-rank models in an interesting way.
2.  It introduces an efficient and scalable optimization algorithm with theoretical guarantees, which is a valuable contribution.
3.  The problem of modeling temporal dynamics in user behavior is significant and of high interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The practical motivation for the specific problems being solved (especially precise return-time prediction) is not well-established.
2.  The experimental evaluation lacks simple, intuitive baselines, making it hard to judge the true effectiveness and complexity trade-off of the proposed model.
3.  The paper does not provide sufficient analysis to clearly demonstrate the practical significance of its results over simpler alternatives.

Overall, this paper tackles an important problem with a novel technical approach. However, its contributions are undermined by weaknesses in motivation and experimental validation. The work is promising but feels incomplete in its current form. I am currently on the borderline but lean towards rejection unless the authors can better motivate the problem and strengthen their experimental comparison.
