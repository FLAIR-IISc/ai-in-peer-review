- Paper proposes a time-sensitive recommender that couples low-rank structure with Hawkes processes to predict both what to recommend now and when a user will return.  
- Learning is posed as a convex program with nuclear-norm and nonnegativity constraints; authors present a blended proximal–conditional gradient algorithm with O(1/t) convergence.  
- Experiments on one large synthetic and two modest real datasets (last.fm, tmall, plus a small medical case) show improvements over Poisson, tensor factorization, and an SVD baseline.  
- Related to temporal collaborative filtering, survival/return-time prediction, and Hawkes learning, but novel in tying low-rank transfer to self-excitation for recommendation and return-time tasks.  
- Technical quality is solid and the algorithmic decomposition is neat.  
- Empirical evaluation is weak: missing critical baselines and clearer measures of practical effect size.  
- The claim that time-varying preferences are pervasive is unsupported by dataset analysis.  
- Presentation issues: minor typos and an abrupt, unsupported switch to retweet/link-creation examples without references.  
- Originality: combining low-rank sharing with Hawkes processes for these tasks is novel.  
- Significance: potentially useful but real-world impact unclear without stronger motivation and clearer gains versus simple baselines.  
- Major request: justify prevalence and causes of time variation via dataset analyses (seasonality, change-points, popularity shocks, etc.).  
- Major request: clarify practical use cases for next-return time predictions, expected business metrics, and how predictions will be acted upon.  
- Major request: add simple return-time baselines (last inter-arrival, median/mode delay, seasonal inhomogeneous Poisson, renewal models) and consider coarse delay categories.  
- Major request: for recommendation, include strong time-aware popularity baselines (time-of-day/week), SVD++, and report top-k lift/precision, user-level deltas, and statistical significance.  
- Major request: clarify magnitude of improvements vs. SVD and popularity heuristics; provide CIs, per-user breakdowns, and ablation studies (no self-excitation, no low-rank, kernel choices).  
- Pros: clean convex formulation, conceptually unified treatment of item-at-time and return-time, scalability shown on large synthetic data.  
- Cons: limited real-world motivation, missing simple baselines, insufficient analysis of time-variation drivers, unclear practical impact and statistical significance, presentation nits.  
- Overall recommendation: OK/marginal — leaning borderline reject unless empirical section and motivation are substantially strengthened.
