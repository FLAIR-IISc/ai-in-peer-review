## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper presents a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by combining low-rank models with Hawkes processes. The core idea – modeling user-item interactions as a recurrent event process – is a principled and promising direction, especially given the limitations of traditional recommendation systems that largely ignore temporal dynamics. The authors successfully demonstrate a convex formulation and an efficient algorithm for estimation, reporting improved performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets. This work builds upon recent interest in temporal point processes for recommendation (e.g., Kapoor et al., [12, 11]) but distinguishes itself by offering a more generalizable approach capable of recommending new items, rather than solely predicting return times. 

The paper is technically sound and well-written, making the concepts accessible. The authors clearly articulate the motivations and contributions of their work, and the proposed method appears novel in its combination of techniques and focus on distributed solvability. The significance of the work is potentially high, given the increasing importance of personalization and the limitations of existing approaches in handling temporal data. The scalability of the algorithm, achieving O(1/t) convergence and handling large datasets, is a particularly strong point.

However, several areas could benefit from further attention. The explanation surrounding Equation (8) is unclear; specifying *which* parameter (ρ) is being maximized would greatly improve understanding, and the authors need to address the potential for ρ* to be unbounded. While Theorem 2 is interesting, its practical relevance is diminished by the fact that the prescribed value of ρ is not utilized in the experiments.  Crucially, the experimental evaluation lacks a strong, time-aware baseline. Including SVD++ would provide a more robust comparison, as it's a well-established method incorporating temporal effects. Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge limitations, a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm compared to existing methods would be valuable. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
*   Technically sound with a convex formulation and efficient algorithm.
*   Empirically demonstrates improved performance on multiple datasets.
*   Scalable solution applicable to large-scale problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in the explanation of Equation (8) and the unboundedness of ρ*.
*   Limited relevance of Theorem 2 due to unutilized parameter.
*   Missing a strong baseline (SVD++) in the experimental evaluation.



**Overall:** Despite some minor concerns regarding clarity and experimental completeness, this paper represents a significant contribution to the emerging field of time-sensitive recommendation. The authors present a compelling approach with strong theoretical foundations and empirical validation. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the concerns outlined above.
