Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that (i) recommends items “at the right moment” and (ii) predicts users’ next return time. The core modeling choice is a per user–item self-exciting Hawkes process with an exponential triggering kernel, where the base intensity and self-excitation coefficients across all user–item pairs are tied via two low-rank nonnegative matrices (Λ0 and A). Parameter estimation is cast as a convex problem with nuclear-norm regularization; to avoid expensive SVD-based proximal steps, the authors introduce an equivalent penalty formulation and develop an algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for the nonnegativity-constrained smooth block) with conditional gradient/LMO (for the nuclear-norm-regularized block). They provide a convergence guarantee and evaluate on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) for two tasks: time-aware top-k recommendation and return-time prediction.

The work relates to temporal recommendation/dynamics (e.g., Koren 2009; Koenigstein et al. 2011; tensor factorization and Poisson regression for counts: Xiong et al. 2010; Karatzoglou et al. 2010; Wang et al. 2015) and to point-process modeling in social/temporal data (Hawkes 1971; Zhou, Zha, Song 2013/2014; Du et al. 2015). It contrasts with survival-analysis-based return-time prediction (Kapoor et al. KDD’14; WSDM’13), arguing survival models lack recurrence handling and generalization to new items.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation that connects Hawkes processes with low-rank structure to transfer information across sparse user–item pairs.
- Convex estimation objective and a thoughtful optimization design that separates nonnegativity and nuclear norms; the accelerated proximal/conditional-gradient hybrid is interesting and practically appealing.
- Scalable implementation demonstrated on millions of pairs and 100M events in synthetic experiments; convergence plots are informative.
- The model addresses two practically relevant tasks within one framework; evaluating both ranking and return-time is a plus.
- Extensions to side information, nonparametric kernels, and spatiotemporal tensors are well-motivated.

Weaknesses
- Practical motivation for return-time prediction is underdeveloped. The paper mentions advertising/marketing at a high level but does not provide concrete use cases, design choices (e.g., notification throttling, budgeted bidding), or offline–online impact proxies.
- Missing simple and strong baselines for return-time: last inter-event gap, median/most-frequent gap per (user,item), per user, per item; time-of-day/day-of-week seasonal inhomogeneous Poisson; renewal/Weibull models. For recommendation, stronger popularity/recency baselines and a seasonality-aware popularity over short windows are needed.
- The claim that time-varying preferences are widespread is not substantiated by data analysis. There is no exploratory study of temporal drift, periodicity, or the proportion of variance explained by recency vs. base preference. It is unclear how much gains come from self-excitation vs. popularity/seasonality.
- Experimental reporting under-emphasizes practical effect sizes. Improvements over SVD/popularity are not contextualized (e.g., hit@k/NDCG lifts, MAE in minutes/hours, bucketed accuracy), nor are statistical significance tests provided.
- Modeling choices need more discussion: stability/branching ratio conditions for Hawkes; identifiability between α and kernel bandwidth; sensitivity to σ, λ, β, ρ; whether cross-excitation across items (e.g., artist → related artist) matters.
- Presentation issues: a typo (“abel”→“able”); Section 2 abruptly references retweets/link creation without supporting citations or continuity with the recommender setting.

Detailed evaluation
Quality: The formulation is technically sound and the optimization algorithm is carefully designed with a convergence bound. However, empirical validation lacks critical baselines and significance testing, making it hard to assess how much the Hawkes mechanism, versus simpler temporal heuristics, drives the gains. The Monte Carlo return-time estimator is reasonable but computationally heavy; a discussion of sample sizes, runtime, and variance would help.

Clarity: Generally well organized with useful background. The optimization section is clear. The exposition would benefit from: (i) a brief stability discussion for Hawkes (branching ratio <1) and whether constraints enforce it; (ii) a concise complexity analysis per iteration including the cost of top singular vectors in the LMO; (iii) clearer motivation and operationalization for the two tasks; (iv) fixing typos and the confusing example shift in Section 2.

Originality: The low-rank Hawkes formulation for user–item events and the hybrid optimization approach are novel in this context. Prior Hawkes work often addresses network inference or mutual excitation; low-rank transfer across dyads for recommender-style tasks is a nice contribution. The penalty reformulation enabling separate proximal/LMO steps is a useful trick.

Significance: Potentially significant if the temporal modeling yields robust, practically meaningful gains. As presented, the real-data experiments are on modest scale and do not convincingly show user-perceivable improvements. Providing stronger baselines, ablations (e.g., Λ0-only Poisson; Hawkes without low-rank; low-rank Poisson with seasonality), and practical metrics would strengthen the case.

Suggestions for improvement
- Motivation and use cases: Explain how return-time predictions drive concrete decisions (notification timing, ad retargeting windows, replenishment reminders, clinical monitoring thresholds). Consider evaluating categorical delay prediction (e.g., short/medium/long/very long) to reflect actionable policies.
- Baselines: Add last-gap, median-gap, and mode-gap predictors at (user,item)/(user)/(item) levels; seasonal inhomogeneous Poisson (time-of-day/week); Weibull renewal; recency-weighted popularity; time-bucketed popularity. For ranking, include a time-windowed popularity@t baseline.
- Analysis: Quantify temporal nonstationarity and recurrence (ACF of inter-arrival times, seasonality strength, proportion of sequences exhibiting self-excitation). Provide ablations disentangling Λ0 vs. A contributions.
- Reporting: Include absolute effect sizes and statistical tests (paired t-test/Wilcoxon) on hit@k/NDCG and MAE; show bucketed return-time accuracy. Discuss runtime and memory; sensitivity to ρ, λ, β, σ; guidance on setting ρ relative to Theorem 1.
- Modeling: Discuss Hawkes stability and how training avoids supercritical regimes; comment on learning/tying σ; consider cross-item excitation within users; clarify if cold-start users/items can leverage Λ0 side features.
- Presentation: Fix typos; remove or properly cite/justify the retweet/link-creation examples in Section 2; ensure consistent notation and spacing.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, convex, and scalable formulation connecting low-rank structure with point processes.
- Novel hybrid optimizer with clear practical benefits.
- Addresses two time-sensitive tasks in a unified model; promising synthetic and some real-data gains.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient practical motivation and evidence that gains are meaningful for deployed systems.
- Missing simple but strong temporal baselines; lack of ablations and significance tests.
- Limited analysis of when and why time-varying/self-exciting behavior matters.
- Presentation glitches and some unclear example framing.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The paper has solid technical ideas and could become impactful with stronger empirical grounding, clearer motivation, and more comprehensive baselines and analysis.

Confidence: High (familiar with temporal recommendation, point processes, and large-scale convex optimization).
