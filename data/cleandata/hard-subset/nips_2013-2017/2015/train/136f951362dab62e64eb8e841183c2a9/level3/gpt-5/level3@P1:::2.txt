This paper tackles time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by coupling self-exciting temporal point processes with low-rank collaborative modeling. For each user–item pair, events are modeled via a Hawkes process with a baseline rate and a self-excitation term (exponential kernel), capturing both long-term affinity and short-term recency. To generalize beyond observed pairs, the authors impose low-rank structure on two nonnegative matrices: Λ0 (base intensities) and A (self-excitation magnitudes), leading to a convex maximum-likelihood estimation problem with nuclear-norm regularization. Because nonnegativity and nuclear norms are entangled, they introduce an equivalent penalty formulation with auxiliary variables (under a threshold condition on ρ), enabling an algorithm that applies a proximal step on the nonnegative parameters and a conditional-gradient (Frank–Wolfe) step on the nuclear-norm blocks. They prove a convergence bound with O(1/t) rate components and demonstrate scalability to millions of pairs and 100M events. Extensions to contextual features and nonparametric kernels are discussed. Empirically, they evaluate on synthetic data and three domains (last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC-II), showing improved ranking-at-time and return-time MAE versus Poisson, SVD, temporal tensor factorization [3], and STiC survival models [11,12]. Q–Q analyses using the time-change theorem support the Hawkes assumption.

Relation to prior work: Unlike classic temporal MF (e.g., Koren [16]) and tensor models [3,25], which discretize time and often ignore self-excitation, this work operates in continuous time and models history dependence via Hawkes processes. Compared to survival-analysis approaches for return time [11,12], it handles recurrent events and generalizes to new items via low-rank sharing. It is also distinct from Hawkes-based network inference [30] in its recommender focus and convex low-rank estimation with nuclear norms. On the optimization side, it contrasts with ADMM-based rank minimization [29] by avoiding full SVDs via a proximal–conditional gradient hybrid.

Strengths
- Addresses two practically important tasks in one coherent probabilistic framework: when to recommend and when the user returns.
- Sound convex formulation with nonnegativity and nuclear-norm regularization; penalty-equivalence result clarifies the role of ρ.
- Algorithmic contribution is well-motivated: proximal projection for nonnegativity is cheap; conditional gradient with top singular vectors avoids expensive full SVDs; convergence analysis is nontrivial and useful.
- Scalability demonstrated at realistic scales; the Q–Q diagnostic for model fit is insightful.
- Broad empirical evaluation over diverse domains, with both held-out-item and standard settings.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Baselines: Given complementary strengths of SVD (item ranking) and STiC (return-time), an ensemble baseline (e.g., SVD ranks modulated by STiC-derived time scores, or a simple weighted product) would be a stronger and fairer comparator. Please include such a baseline and tune the weight by validation.
- Kernel bandwidth σ: The paper does not explain how σ is selected nor how sensitive performance is to σ. Provide: (i) the selection protocol (CV, held-out likelihood, or grid search), (ii) sensitivity curves for ranking and MAE, and (iii) results with a small basis of exponential kernels or learned kernels (cf. [30]) to reduce misspecification risk.
- Hyperparameters and tuning: Important details are missing for ρ, λ, β, and for baselines (e.g., latent dimensions, number and width of tensor time bins, regularization strengths). Report search ranges, selection criteria, and sensitivity; ensure all methods use the same validation policy (time-aware splits).
- Dataset characterization: For temporal modeling, report per-pair recurrence statistics (events per pair, proportion of singletons), inter-event time distributions, sparsity patterns, and the fraction/definition of “entirely held-out sequences.” Clarify time units and any time normalization.
- Clarity and presentation: The manuscript has typos and grammar issues that impede readability. Algorithm 1 could be clearer: unify notation for X, Y, U; specify per-iteration cost, memory footprint, and stopping criteria; detail how top singular vectors are computed (power iterations, tolerance). Theorem 1 gives existence of ρ*, but practical guidance for choosing ρ is needed.
- Evaluation rigor: Report confidence intervals or statistical tests; include wall-clock and memory comparisons; ablate the low-rank constraints (e.g., remove nuclear norms) and the self-excitation (reduce to Poisson) to quantify each component’s contribution. Since the method claims to generalize to new items, include a dedicated cold-start evaluation.

Quality
- Technically sound with appropriate likelihood and convexity; the optimization method and convergence guarantees are credible. Missing sensitivity analyses, ablations, and clearer hyperparameter/tuning details limit reproducibility and the conclusiveness of the empirical claims.

Clarity
- Core ideas are understandable, but the paper needs editing for language and organization. Provide clearer algorithmic pseudocode, complexity analysis, and experimental protocols.

Originality
- A novel and interesting combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank matrix modeling for recommender systems, with a tailored optimization scheme. To my knowledge, this is new relative to NIPS-era work.

Significance
- The problems are important; the approach is promising and scalable. With stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses, and improved presentation, the impact could be substantial for time-sensitive recommendation and engagement modeling.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid modeling contribution in continuous time, beyond discrete-time tensor/MF approaches.
- Convex estimation with a practical, scalable algorithm and convergence guarantees.
- Positive results on multiple datasets and tasks; useful diagnostics.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental baselines and tuning details are insufficient; no ensemble comparator.
- No sensitivity analysis for σ or other hyperparameters; limited ablations.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; some theoretical choices (ρ) lack practical guidance.

Recommendation
- Borderline accept. The paper presents a timely and technically solid contribution with promising empirical results. I would support acceptance if the authors strengthen the experimental section (baselines, sensitivity, ablations) and significantly improve clarity and reproducibility in the final version.
