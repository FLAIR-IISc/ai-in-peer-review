This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interaction data. The core idea is to model user activity as a Hawkes process, leveraging its ability to represent temporal dependencies, while using low-rank factorization to generalize across users and items. The authors develop a convex optimization formulation and an efficient algorithm with provable convergence guarantees to estimate the model parameters. Extensive experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate superior performance compared to several state-of-the-art methods.

The work builds upon existing research in both recommender systems and temporal point processes. Traditional recommender systems primarily focus on static preferences, often represented by ratings, overlooking the dynamic nature of user interests. Recent approaches have begun to incorporate temporal dynamics, such as Kapoor et al.’s work using survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models [11, 12]. However, these methods are limited in their ability to generalize to new items. This paper addresses this limitation by proposing a more flexible framework based on Hawkes processes and low-rank factorization, allowing recommendations for previously unseen items. The paper also acknowledges related work on matrix and tensor factorization [3, 13, 25], but argues its approach is more suitable for capturing the time-varying nature of user behavior.  The connection to Hawkes processes draws on foundational work in the field [4, 5, 6] and extends it to the context of recommendation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation appears to be a novel approach. The theoretical justification and convex formulation are well-presented.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper demonstrates a solid understanding of the underlying theory of temporal point processes and optimization. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) provides a theoretical guarantee for the proposed algorithm.  The efficient optimization algorithm addressing scalability is a significant strength.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets consistently show that the proposed model outperforms existing baselines, including STiC [11] and tensor factorization methods [3].
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the technical details and understand the key contributions.
* **Scalability:** The authors emphasize the scalability of their algorithm and demonstrate its ability to handle large datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.
* **Generalizability:** The authors explicitly point out the potential to incorporate additional contextual information (user profiles, textual features, etc.) into the model, increasing its flexibility and applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Tuning:** The process for choosing ρ, λ, and β is not clearly explained. While the authors mention tuning for best performance, more details on the search strategy would be beneficial.  The claim about the difficulty of directly estimating ρ* raises concerns about the practical relevance of Theorem 1.
* **Baseline Comparison:** While the selected baselines are reasonable, the inclusion of SVD++ would have strengthened the empirical evaluation. SVD++ is a widely used and strong baseline for collaborative filtering that explicitly models implicit feedback and time dynamics.
* **Theoretical Connection to Experiments:** The paper establishes a theoretical convergence rate (Theorem 2) with a specific parameter setting (ρ), but the experiments do not explicitly demonstrate the algorithm’s performance under those conditions. The relevance of Theorem 2 feels somewhat disconnected from the empirical validation.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach, such as potential sensitivity to the choice of the triggering kernel function or the assumptions of low-rank structure.  A brief discussion of scenarios where the approach may *not* perform well would increase credibility.


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem (time-sensitive recommendation).
* The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The clarity of the writing and the thoroughness of the experimental evaluation are commendable.
* The work has the potential to impact the field of recommender systems by providing a more principled and scalable framework for modeling temporal user behavior.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical results (Theorem 2) may not directly translate to practical implementation due to the difficulty in selecting the appropriate parameter ρ.
* The lack of comparison to a stronger baseline like SVD++ weakens the empirical evaluation.
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of its limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The novelty of the approach, the technical soundness of the methodology, and the compelling empirical results outweigh the shortcomings. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the concerns about parameter tuning and the inclusion of SVD++ as a baseline. A more detailed discussion of the limitations would also be valuable.
