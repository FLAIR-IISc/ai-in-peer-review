This paper proposes a novel time-sensitive recommendation method that models time-varying user preferences and predicts users' next-return time. The authors develop an efficient optimization algorithm to train the model and evaluate its performance on one synthetic and two modest real datasets. The method is based on a low-rank self-exciting Hawkes process, which captures the temporal dependencies in user-item interactions.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and its components. The authors provide a thorough review of related work and highlight the advantages of their approach over existing methods. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in both item recommendation and return-time prediction tasks.

However, there are some concerns and suggestions for improvement. Firstly, the reviewer questions whether time-varying preferences are widespread and asks for dataset analysis to justify the claim and identify causes (e.g., new popular songs vs. other patterns). The authors should provide more insights into the datasets used and the underlying patterns that their method is trying to capture.

Secondly, the motivation for predicting next-return time is unclear, and the paper needs to explain practical use cases for web companies. The authors should provide more context on how this feature can be useful in real-world applications and what benefits it can bring to users and companies.

Thirdly, the experimental results lack clarity on significance and practical impact compared to SVD and simple popular-item baselines. The authors should provide more detailed analysis and comparisons to help readers understand the magnitude of the improvements achieved by their method.

Lastly, there are some presentation issues, such as a typo "abel"→"able" and a confusing, unsupported switch to retweets/link-creation examples in section 2 without references. The authors should proofread the paper carefully and ensure that all examples and references are consistent and well-supported.

In terms of the four criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on Quality and Clarity. The proposed method is technically sound, and the paper is well-organized and easy to follow. However, the paper could improve on Originality and Significance. While the method is novel and effective, the authors could provide more insights into the underlying patterns and practical use cases to justify the significance of the work.

Here is a list of arguments for and against acceptance:

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective method for time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction.
* The method is based on a sound theoretical framework and is well-evaluated on multiple datasets.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and its components.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper lacks real-world motivation and sufficient baselines for comparison.
* The experimental results lack clarity on significance and practical impact.
* The paper could benefit from more insights into the underlying patterns and practical use cases to justify the significance of the work.

Overall, the paper is judged as OK/marginal, and the reviewer recommends minor revisions to address the concerns and suggestions mentioned above.
