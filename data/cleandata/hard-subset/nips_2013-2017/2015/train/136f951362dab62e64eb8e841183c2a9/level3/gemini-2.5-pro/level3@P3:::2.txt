This paper proposes a novel model for time-sensitive recommendations, which aims to predict not only *what* a user might like but also *when* they are likely to engage with an item. The core idea is to model the sequence of user-item interactions as a self-exciting Hawkes process. The intensity function for each user-item pair is composed of a constant baseline intensity and a self-exciting term that captures the influence of past events. To enable collaborative filtering and generalize to unobserved user-item pairs, the paper assumes that the matrices representing the baseline intensities and self-excitation coefficients for all users and items are low-rank. The authors propose a convex formulation to learn the model parameters, regularized by the nuclear norm, and develop an efficient optimization algorithm that combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. This approach is novel in its direct integration of continuous-time point processes with latent factor models for recommendation, distinguishing it from prior work like STiC [12], which models temporal dynamics for individual series but cannot generalize to new items, and tensor factorization methods [3, 13, 25], which typically rely on discretizing time.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and elegant synthesis of two powerful modeling paradigms: self-exciting point processes and low-rank latent factor models. By parameterizing the Hawkes process intensity with user and item latent vectors learned collaboratively, the model provides a principled way to capture both static user preferences and dynamic, history-dependent behaviors, while also allowing for generalization to unseen user-item pairs. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field of time-aware recommender systems.
*   **Significance and Potential Impact:** The problem addressed is of high practical importance. Predicting user return times and recommending items at the right moment can significantly improve user engagement and optimize marketing strategies. The proposed method directly tackles these challenges within a unified framework. Furthermore, the optimization algorithm developed for the non-negative nuclear norm regularized problem may be of independent interest to the broader machine learning community.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a solid technical contribution. The problem is framed as a convex optimization problem, and a non-trivial algorithm is developed to solve it efficiently. The authors provide a convergence analysis for their algorithm, establishing an O(1/t) rate, which adds to the work's technical quality.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation, while showing promising results, has several weaknesses that limit the confidence in the claimed superiority of the model.
    1.  **Missing Baselines:** The comparison is missing a crucial and sensible baseline. Given that STiC [12] is strong at modeling temporal patterns for known user-item pairs and SVD is a classic collaborative filtering method strong at predicting preferences for new items, a simple ensemble of the two would be a very strong competitor. Such an ensemble could, for instance, use STiC's prediction for items with history and SVD's prediction for entirely held-out items. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the complex unified model is truly superior to a simpler combination of existing specialized models.
    2.  **Hyperparameter Details and Sensitivity:** The paper lacks transparency regarding hyperparameter selection. A critical parameter is the triggering kernel bandwidth σ, which dictates the timescale of self-excitation. The authors state it was "tuned to give the best performance" but provide no details on the range of values tested or the final value chosen. A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with σ is essential for understanding the model's robustness and applicability. Similarly, crucial details on the tuning of other hyperparameters (e.g., latent dimension, regularization parameters λ and β) for both the proposed method and the baselines are omitted, which hinders reproducibility and fair comparison.
    3.  **Dataset Characterization:** For a paper focused on modeling temporal event dynamics, the dataset descriptions are insufficient. Beyond basic statistics (users, items, events), the authors should provide characterization relevant to the problem, such as the distribution of inter-event times, the number of recurring events per user-item pair, and measures of temporal irregularity (e.g., burstiness). This would better motivate the need for a Hawkes process model and help interpret the results across different datasets.

*   **Clarity:** The clarity of the paper could be significantly improved. The manuscript suffers from numerous language issues, including typos, grammatical errors, and awkward phrasing. While the core technical ideas are discernible, these issues make the paper difficult to read and detract from its professionalism. A thorough proofreading by a native English speaker is highly recommended. For instance, the description of the optimization algorithm and the setup of the experiments could be made much clearer.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and well-motivated combination of Hawkes processes and collaborative filtering for an important practical problem.
2.  The proposed optimization algorithm is non-trivial and supported by a theoretical convergence analysis.
3.  The core idea has the potential to inspire future research at the intersection of point processes and recommender systems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The empirical evaluation is not fully convincing due to the omission of key baselines and a lack of transparency and analysis regarding hyperparameter tuning.
2.  Insufficient dataset analysis makes it difficult to understand the conditions under which the proposed model excels.
3.  The paper's poor presentation and numerous language errors hinder readability and reduce its overall quality.

In summary, this paper introduces an interesting and original approach to time-sensitive recommendations. The technical foundation is solid. However, its significance is currently undermined by an incomplete experimental evaluation and poor presentation. While the core contribution is valuable, the paper in its current form falls short of the high standards for NIPS. I would be willing to reconsider my rating if the authors were to substantially revise the experiments and improve the clarity of the manuscript.
