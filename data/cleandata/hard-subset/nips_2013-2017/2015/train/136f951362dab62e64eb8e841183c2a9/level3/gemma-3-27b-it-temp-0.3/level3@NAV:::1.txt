## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel framework combining self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to address time-sensitive recommendation and next-return time prediction. The authors aim to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions, offering a potentially more nuanced approach than traditional methods focused on static preferences. The proposed model is accompanied by a convex optimization algorithm with an O(1/t) convergence rate, and evaluated on a synthetic dataset and two real-world datasets.

The work builds upon recent advancements in temporal point processes for recommendation, particularly referencing Kapoor et al.’s work on survival analysis. However, the authors differentiate their approach by aiming for a more generalizable recommendation system capable of suggesting *new* items, rather than solely predicting return times. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a central contribution, allowing for scalability and knowledge transfer across user-item pairs. 

Despite these strengths, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The core motivation for predicting next-return time remains unclear. While the authors mention potential applications in ad bidding and healthcare, these are briefly touched upon and lack a compelling justification for why this prediction is crucial for web companies. Furthermore, the paper lacks baseline comparisons for return-time prediction. Simple heuristics like using the last time delay or the most frequent delay would provide a valuable benchmark. Considering coarser delay categories (short/medium/long) might also simplify the prediction task and offer a more practical comparison. 

The experimental results are also presented with insufficient clarity. The significance of improvements over SVD and simple popularity baselines is not adequately demonstrated. It’s unclear whether the observed gains are substantial enough to be user-perceptible or practically meaningful. Additionally, there are presentation issues – a typo ("abel" should be "able") and a somewhat jarring, unsupported switch to examples involving retweets and link creation in Section 2. 

In summary, the paper tackles two interesting problems with a technically sound approach. However, it lacks strong real-world motivation, sufficient baselines for comparison, and a deeper analysis of the experimental results. While the proposed method shows promise, the current presentation doesn’t convincingly demonstrate its practical significance. I would rate this paper as **OK/marginal** for NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models.
*   Efficient optimization algorithm with theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrated performance on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Unclear motivation for next-return time prediction.
*   Missing simple baselines for return-time prediction.
*   Lack of clarity regarding the significance of experimental results.
*   Presentation issues and unsupported examples.
