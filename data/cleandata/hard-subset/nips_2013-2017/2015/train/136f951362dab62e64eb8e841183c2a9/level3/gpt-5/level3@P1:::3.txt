This paper addresses time-sensitive recommendation by modeling recurrent user-item interactions as Hawkes point processes coupled with low-rank user–item parameter matrices. The core idea is to (i) use self-exciting processes to capture short-term, history-dependent bursts in activity and (ii) transfer signal across sparse user–item pairs via nuclear-norm-regularized low-rank matrices for baseline intensity and self-excitation strength. The authors propose a convex formulation with nonnegativity constraints and nuclear-norm penalties, then introduce a penalty-based reformulation that separates these constraints. They develop an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient/LMO (for nuclear norms), prove an O(1/t) convergence rate, and demonstrate scalability to millions of pairs and hundreds of millions of events. Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) show improvements in two tasks: ranking items at the right time (using instantaneous intensity) and predicting user return times (via sampling from the conditional density). The approach is related to prior work on temporal point processes and Hawkes processes (Hawkes; Du et al.; Zhou et al.) and to temporal recommender models (Koren; Karatzoglou et al.), but differs by directly modeling continuous-time event dynamics and making them transferable through low-rank structure, rather than discretizing time or modeling only terminal events as in survival analysis (Kapoor et al.).

Quality
- Soundness: The Hawkes modeling choice is well-motivated for recurrent behaviors, and the low-rank assumption is standard for transfer in sparse settings. The convex likelihood with nuclear-norm penalties is appropriate, and the algorithmic design (proximal step for nonnegativity, LMO with top singular vectors for nuclear norms) is technically solid and practical at scale.
- Theory: The equivalence between the original constrained problem and the penalized reformulation hinges on choosing ρ above a problem-dependent threshold ρ*. As presented, the definition of ρ* (in the theorem following the penalty reformulation) depends on unknown optimal quantities and may be unbounded if the denominator approaches zero, leaving no actionable guidance for selecting ρ. Moreover, the experiments tune ρ as a hyperparameter rather than using a principled scheme, which weakens the practical relevance of the equivalence guarantee. The convergence analysis for the blended algorithm is clear and yields an O(1/t) rate; however, constants (L, D1, D2) are not instantiated, and it would help to discuss how L is computed or bounded for the Hawkes likelihood and how the cost of top singular-vector computations scales with matrix size per iteration.
- Empirics: The evaluation protocol is thoughtful (both held-out sequence prediction and standard splits; two tasks). The method outperforms Poisson, tensor factorization variants, and STiC (for time prediction). For ranking, reporting precision@k/NDCG@k in addition to median/mean rank would strengthen the claims. For time prediction, calibration diagnostics (QQ plots are shown qualitatively for one sequence) and aggregate calibration metrics would be valuable. Ablations isolating the contributions of (a) Hawkes vs Poisson with low rank and (b) low rank vs per-pair Hawkes without sharing would clarify where gains come from. Sensitivity to kernel bandwidth σ and to the number of thinning samples for return-time prediction would improve reproducibility.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, with clear problem formulation, algorithm description, and experimental setup. Two issues merit clarification:
  1) The equation/definition for ρ* in the equivalence theorem should clearly state over which variables the maximization is taken and under what conditions ρ* is finite; providing a computable lower bound or a practical selection rule (e.g., increasing ρ schedule with a stopping criterion) would help.
  2) The relationship between the theoretical settings (step sizes, ρ) and the actual hyperparameters used in experiments should be made explicit. Minor suggestions: specify how many thinning samples are drawn for return-time prediction, and provide pseudocode-level complexity per iteration (including SVD computations).

Originality
- Combining Hawkes processes with low-rank user–item structure for recommender tasks, optimizing a convex likelihood with nuclear norms, and designing a hybrid proximal/conditional-gradient method for this setting is, to my knowledge, novel. Prior work on temporal recommendations tends to discretize time (tensor factorization) or to model only survival/return-time without transferable item recommendation. Prior Hawkes work on network inference is related but targets different problems.

Significance
- Time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction are important in several domains. The method is practically relevant (scales well, continuous-time modeling) and empirically strong. The optimization approach is of independent interest to the broader community working on structured low-rank estimation with side constraints.

Missing/weak baselines and analyses
- Include stronger time-aware recommenders: SVD++/TimeSVD++ (Koren) and other temporal MF baselines that capture user/item dynamics, at least for the ranking task. For time prediction, compare to renewal-process baselines (e.g., Weibull, log-logistic) fitted per user–item pair with hierarchical sharing.
- Report top-k metrics (precision/NDCG) and coverage/novelty to complement rank.
- Provide ablations (Hawkes-only, low-rank-only) and sensitivity to σ and ρ.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled formulation linking Hawkes processes with low-rank transfer.
- Scalable, elegant algorithm with convergence guarantees.
- Strong empirical results on multiple datasets for both ranking and return-time tasks.
- Clear writing; addresses an important, timely problem; extensible to contextual features.

Cons against acceptance
- Theoretical gap around ρ*: unclear, potentially unbounded threshold; experiments rely on tuning rather than a principled selection consistent with the theorem.
- Missing strong temporal baselines (TimeSVD++, SVD++) for the ranking task.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses; limited evaluation metrics for ranking and calibration for time prediction.

Overall assessment
- A solid, original contribution with practical impact and good empirical performance. Addressing the clarity and baseline gaps, and providing guidance on ρ selection, would further strengthen the paper. I lean positive.
