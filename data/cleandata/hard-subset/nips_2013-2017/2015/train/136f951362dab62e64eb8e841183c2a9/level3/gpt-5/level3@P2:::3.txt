Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles two coupled problems in time-sensitive recommendation: (1) recommending the right item at the right time; and (2) predicting a user’s next return time to a service. The key modeling choice is to represent each user–item pair’s event sequence as a self-exciting Hawkes process, thus capturing both a long-term baseline preference and short-term recency/repeat effects via a triggering kernel. To share statistical strength across the many unobserved user–item pairs, the per-pair baseline and excitation parameters are constrained to be low rank over the user–item matrix, using nuclear norms to keep the estimation convex. The authors derive a convex objective (negative log-likelihood plus nuclear norms and non-negativity constraints) and propose an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient (to handle non-negativity) with conditional gradient/LMO (to handle nuclear norms) and prove an O(1/t) convergence rate, with recovery of O(1/t^2) for the smooth case without nuclear norms.

Empirically, the method scales to millions of pairs and hundreds of millions of events and outperforms Poisson, survival-analysis-based and tensor factorization baselines on synthetic and three real datasets (last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC-II) for both item ranking at time t and return-time prediction. The work relates to (i) survival/hazard-based return-time models (e.g., Kapoor et al., WSDM’15/KDD’14) but goes beyond their single-terminal-event and i.i.d. gap assumptions; (ii) time-aware matrix/tensor factorization (e.g., Koren 2009; Xiong et al., SDM’10; Chi & Kolda, 2012) by using continuous-time event modeling rather than discretization; and (iii) low-rank Hawkes/network estimation (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song, AISTATS’13/ICML’13) by tying many univariate self-exciting processes through low-rank user–item structure rather than learning a mutually exciting network.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The convexity of the parameterization (log of an affine positive intensity minus linear term), the low-rank sharing via nuclear norms, and the accelerated hybrid optimization are well-motivated. The convergence analysis is careful and recovers known optimal rates as special cases. The experimental protocol and metrics are appropriate for the two tasks.
- Theoretical concerns:
  - The equivalence between the penalized formulation and the original constrained one hinges on ρ > ρ*. As written (Equation (8)/Theorem 1), it is unclear precisely over which variables the outer max is taken and under what conditions ρ* is finite. Please clarify the definition and boundedness of ρ*, and provide guidance on how to choose ρ in practice beyond tuning.
  - The convergence theorem is stated for a fixed ρ satisfying the required threshold, but experiments tune ρ for predictive performance without verifying the condition. This weakens the practical relevance of the theoretical guarantees; a robustness discussion or empirical sensitivity to ρ would help.
  - For Hawkes stability with an exponential kernel, a sufficient condition is α(u,i) ∫0∞ γ(τ) dτ = α(u,i) σ < 1 (subcritical branching ratio). The paper does not state whether such constraints are enforced or how supercritical parameterizations are avoided; please discuss identifiability and stability (trade-offs between Λ0 and A) and whether projections/penalties ensure a well-behaved process.
- Empirics: The datasets and scale are compelling, and the proposed method performs strongly. However, the baseline set could be strengthened with widely used implicit-feedback and time-aware factorization models:
  - SVD++/TimeSVD++ for implicit feedback and temporal dynamics (Koren 2008/2009), and Hu–Koren–Volinsky (KDD’08) for implicit data. These are strong baselines for top-k recommendation and often perform well with temporal effects.
  - Additional continuous-time baselines (e.g., renewal/ACD models) and nonparametric Hawkes kernel learning (Zhou et al., ICML’13) would further contextualize gains.
- Practicality: Sampling-based return-time prediction via Ogata’s thinning is reasonable, but please report the computational overhead and whether a faster approximation (e.g., moment matching, root-finding on the integrated hazard) is feasible at scale.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The modeling assumptions, optimization method, and experiments are presented coherently. Specific points to clarify:
- Precisely define Equation (8) / ρ* and the domain of the max; explain how ρ is selected and whether the equivalence condition is checked.
- State stability/regularity conditions for the Hawkes processes and whether they are enforced during learning.
- Briefly outline how the linear minimization oracle obtains top singular vectors in large-scale settings (e.g., power/Lanczos, warm starts) and the cost per iteration.

Originality
The connection between low-rank user–item structure and Hawkes-process-based continuous-time modeling for recommendation is novel, as is the hybrid proximal/conditional-gradient scheme tailored to non-negativity plus nuclear norms with proven rates. While related pieces exist in Hawkes network learning and time-aware factorization, this combination for time-sensitive recommendation appears new.

Significance
The work advances the state of the art on two practically important tasks with a principled stochastic-process approach, scalable convex estimation, and strong empirical results on large real datasets. The framework is extensible to additional context (profiles, text, location), which increases its potential impact for recommender systems and healthcare event modeling.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled continuous-time model capturing recurrent behavior; convex estimation.
- Efficient algorithm with theoretical guarantees and practical scalability.
- Strong empirical performance on large-scale, diverse datasets.
- Clear exposition and useful analysis (e.g., time-change theorem diagnostics).

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguity and practical relevance of the ρ* threshold and convergence guarantees; lack of stability discussion.
- Missing strong time-aware recommender baselines (SVD++/TimeSVD++, implicit feedback).
- Limited analysis of computational cost for return-time sampling and sensitivity to kernel bandwidth σ and ρ.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Equation (8)/ρ*, provide selection guidance, and discuss boundedness.
- Enforce or analyze Hawkes stability (e.g., α σ < 1) and identifiability between Λ0 and A.
- Add SVD++/TimeSVD++ and Hu–Koren–Volinsky baselines; consider nonparametric Hawkes kernel learning as a comparator.
- Report runtime per iteration, SVD computations, and inference cost for return-time prediction; include scalability plots versus rank/λ/β.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a compelling, well-executed and scalable approach with clear novelty and promising results, but would benefit from clarifications on the theoretical conditions and stronger baselines to solidify the empirical claims.

Reviewer confidence
High on the modeling/optimization aspects; moderate on the breadth of recommender baselines.
