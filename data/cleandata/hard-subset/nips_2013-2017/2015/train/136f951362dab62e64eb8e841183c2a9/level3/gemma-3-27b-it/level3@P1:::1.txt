This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation, connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions. The core contribution lies in addressing two related questions: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and accurately predicting the next returning time of a user. The authors present a convex formulation, an efficient optimization algorithm with O(1/t) convergence, and demonstrate performance on synthetic and real datasets.

The work builds upon existing research in recommender systems, particularly addressing the limitations of traditional methods that often ignore temporal dynamics. Recent works like Kapoor et al. [12, 11] have explored temporal aspects using survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models. However, the proposed approach distinguishes itself by aiming for a more generalizable solution capable of recommending *new* items, a limitation of prior work. The use of Hawkes processes, rooted in point process theory [4, 5, 6], provides a framework for modeling self-exciting events, while the low-rank structure offers scalability and generalization.  Related work in tensor factorization [23, 25, 13, 3] is also acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation is a promising approach.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The convex formulation and convergence analysis provide a solid theoretical grounding for the proposed algorithm. The O(1/t) convergence rate is a significant achievement.
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm is designed to handle large-scale datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.
*   **Potential for Extensibility:** The framework can incorporate additional contextual information, enhancing its applicability to diverse scenarios.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The paper includes experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Motivation & Real-World Relevance:** The justification for the importance of predicting next-return time is weak. While mentioned in the introduction regarding ad bidding and healthcare, the practical implications require further elaboration and a convincing demonstration of real-world value.  The paper needs to clearly articulate *why* accurately predicting return time is crucial for web services beyond the observation that it might be useful.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient baseline comparisons. Simple yet effective baselines for return-time prediction, such as predicting the next return based on the average or last observed delay, are missing. Similarly, for the recommendation task, a simple “most popular item” baseline would provide a crucial point of reference. Considering coarser categories for return-time prediction (e.g., short/medium/long delay) might also simplify the task and reveal if the gains are due to fine-grained prediction accuracy or simply capturing general trends.
*   **Dataset Analysis:** The paper assumes time-varying preferences are prevalent without strong supporting evidence. An analysis of the datasets used, demonstrating the existence and nature of these temporal patterns (e.g., seasonal trends, evolving item popularity, etc.) would strengthen the motivation.  Are the observed effects driven by the introduction of genuinely new, popular items, or genuine shifts in user preferences?
*   **Experimental Clarity & Significance:**  The experimental results presented are not sufficiently clear regarding the statistical significance of the improvements.  The paper should include confidence intervals or p-values to demonstrate whether the observed gains over baselines are statistically meaningful. Furthermore, the practical impact of the improvements should be discussed – are the gains substantial enough to be user-perceptible or translate into measurable business value?
*   **Presentation & Clarity:** A minor point, but the typo (“abel” instead of “able”) detracts from the polish of the work. Additionally, the abrupt transition to examples about retweets and link-creation in section 2 feels disconnected and lacks proper referencing.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important and challenging problem – integrating temporal dynamics into recommender systems.
*   The proposed method is novel and theoretically well-grounded.
*   The algorithm demonstrates good scalability and potential for real-world application.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The motivation for the next-return-time prediction task is weak.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks comprehensive baseline comparisons and analysis of statistical significance.
*   The real-world impact of the improvements is unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of recommender systems. However, the lack of strong motivation, inadequate baseline comparisons, and insufficient analysis of the experimental results weaken its impact. While the technical aspects are well-executed, the paper needs to better demonstrate the practical relevance and significance of its findings. I rate this paper as **OK/Marginal**.  Addressing the concerns raised above, particularly strengthening the motivation and providing a more thorough experimental evaluation, could significantly improve its quality.
