- Models time-sensitive recommendation by combining Hawkes point processes with low-rank user–item matrices to capture short-term bursts and transfer across sparse pairs.
- Proposes a convex likelihood with nonnegativity constraints and nuclear-norm regularization, and a penalty-based reformulation that separates constraints.
- Develops a hybrid algorithm mixing proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) and conditional gradient/LMO (for nuclear norms) with an O(1/t) convergence rate.
- Demonstrates scalability to millions of pairs and hundreds of millions of events, with experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II).
- Empirically improves ranking at the right time (using instantaneous intensity) and predicting user return times (via conditional density sampling) over several baselines.
- Soundness: Hawkes choice and low-rank transfer are well-motivated; convex formulation and algorithmic design are technically solid and practical at scale.
- Theory concern: equivalence guarantee depends on ρ being above a problem-dependent ρ*, whose definition uses unknown optima and may be unbounded, offering no actionable selection rule.
- Practical gap: experiments tune ρ as a hyperparameter rather than using a principled scheme tied to the theorem, weakening the equivalence claim.
- Convergence analysis is clear but lacks instantiated constants (L, D1, D2); the paper should discuss how L is bounded for the Hawkes likelihood and SVD cost per iteration.
- Empirics: evaluation protocol is thoughtful and the method outperforms Poisson, tensor variants, and STiC, but reporting only rank metrics is insufficient.
- Recommendation metrics: should include precision@k and NDCG@k (and coverage/novelty) to strengthen ranking claims.
- Time-prediction diagnostics: need aggregate calibration metrics and more quantitative calibration analyses beyond qualitative QQ plots.
- Ablations needed: isolate contributions of (a) Hawkes vs Poisson with low rank and (b) low rank vs per-pair Hawkes without sharing to clarify sources of gains.
- Sensitivity analyses required for kernel bandwidth σ and number of thinning samples used in return-time prediction for reproducibility.
- Clarity: definition of ρ* must state optimization variables, conditions for finiteness, and provide a computable lower bound or practical selection rule (e.g., increasing-ρ schedule).
- Clarity: explicitly relate theoretical settings (step sizes, ρ) to hyperparameters used in experiments and provide pseudocode-level per-iteration complexity including SVD costs and number of thinning samples.
- Originality: the combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank transfer for continuous-time recommendation and the hybrid optimization method is novel relative to prior temporal recommendation and Hawkes work.
- Significance: addresses an important practical problem with a scalable, continuous-time, transferable model and an optimization approach of independent interest.
- Missing/weak baselines: include TimeSVD++/SVD++ and other temporal MF methods for ranking, and hierarchical renewal-process baselines (Weibull, log-logistic) for time prediction.
- Pros for acceptance: novel principled formulation, scalable algorithm with guarantees, strong multi-dataset results, and clear writing.
- Cons against acceptance: theoretical gap around ρ*, reliance on hyperparameter tuning for ρ, missing strong temporal baselines, and limited ablations and sensitivity analyses.
- Overall assessment: solid, original, and practically impactful work that leans positive but should address clarity about ρ selection, include stronger baselines, and add ablations/sensitivity studies.
