- Introduces a novel framework combining self-exciting point processes (Hawkes) and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation and next-return-time prediction.
- Provides a convex formulation and an efficient optimization algorithm with O(1/t) convergence.
- Demonstrates scalability to large datasets and potential for incorporating additional contextual information.
- Empirically validated on synthetic and real datasets.
- Novelty lies in generalizing to recommend new items, addressing limitations of prior temporal recommendation work.
- Motivation for predicting next-return time is weak and needs stronger real-world justification and demonstration.
- Experimental baselines are insufficient—simple heuristics (e.g., average/last delay, most-popular item) are missing.
- Suggests evaluating coarser return-time categories (short/medium/long) to clarify whether gains are fine-grained or general trends.
- Dataset analysis is lacking evidence that time-varying preferences truly drive the results (seasonality, new-item effects, or preference shifts).
- Statistical significance of improvements is not reported—confidence intervals or p-values are needed.
- Practical impact of the reported gains is unclear—need discussion of user-perceptible or business-relevant effects.
- Minor presentation issues (typo “abel” and abrupt, poorly referenced examples) reduce polish and clarity.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem, method is novel and theoretically sound, and shows scalability.
- Arguments against acceptance: weak motivation for return-time task, inadequate baseline comparisons, and unclear real-world impact.
- Overall assessment: technically solid but marginal acceptance due to motivational and experimental-evaluation shortcomings; addressing these would strengthen the paper.
