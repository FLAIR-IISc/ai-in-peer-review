This paper presents a novel distributed algorithm for time-sensitive recommendation, combining low-rank matrix factorization with a Hawkes process to model user-item interactions. The approach aims to address the limitations of traditional recommendation systems that ignore temporal dynamics, and offers both item recommendation and returning-time prediction capabilities. The work is technically sound, clearly written, and demonstrates originality in applying Hawkes processes to this context, offering potentially significant improvements over existing methods.

The core idea of modeling user activity as a self-exciting point process, coupled with a low-rank structure to handle scalability, is compelling. The authors effectively connect established techniques from different areas – temporal point processes and matrix factorization – in a novel way. This builds upon recent work acknowledging the importance of temporal dynamics in recommendation (e.g., [11, 12]), but crucially moves beyond those approaches by offering a generative model capable of recommending *new* items, rather than being limited to observed interactions. The distributed solvability is also a key strength, enabling application to large-scale datasets.

However, several points warrant consideration. First, the explanation surrounding Equation (8) and the parameter ρ is unclear. The paper states ρ* may be unbounded, which raises concerns about the practical application of the theoretical results and the algorithm's stability. Clarifying which parameter is being maximized and providing a more detailed justification for the unboundedness (or addressing how it’s mitigated) would be beneficial.

Second, while Theorem 2 provides theoretical grounding, its relevance is diminished by the fact that the prescribed ρ is not utilized in the experiments. This disconnect weakens the practical impact of the theoretical contribution. If the chosen value of ρ in experiments differs significantly from the theoretically derived one, the authors should explain why and discuss the implications for the theorem’s applicability.

A significant weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. While comparisons are made to several baselines, the absence of SVD++ – a strong and well-established time-aware collaborative filtering algorithm – is a notable omission. Including SVD++ would provide a more rigorous benchmark and strengthen the claim of improved performance. Similarly, the performance of Tensor factorization methods is reported with two variants, but the reasons for choosing these specifically are not clearly articulated. A more thorough justification for the selected baselines would improve the evaluation’s robustness.

The clarity of the paper is a major strength. The writing is concise and well-organized, making it easy to follow the methodology and understand the key contributions. The inclusion of Figure 1(a) effectively illustrates the motivation behind considering temporal dynamics in recommendation. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is also well-presented, although, as noted above, its connection to the experimental setup is weak.

In terms of originality, the approach appears novel for the relatively new field of time-sensitive recommendation. While Hawkes processes have been applied in other domains, their integration with low-rank models for this specific problem is a valuable contribution. The ability to incorporate additional contextual information further enhances the model's flexibility.

The significance of this work is considerable. The combination of a principled stochastic-process modeling approach, distributed solvability, and improved empirical performance positions this research as potentially impactful. If the limitations identified above can be addressed, this work has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in time-sensitive recommendation and influence future research in this area. The potential for application in various domains, as highlighted in the introduction (e.g., mobile services, healthcare), further strengthens its significance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
*   Strong empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Scalable distributed algorithm.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Potential for significant impact in various application domains.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Unclear explanation of Equation (8) and ρ.
*   Limited relevance of Theorem 2 to the experiments.
*   Lack of comparison to SVD++ and insufficient justification of baseline choices.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. Addressing the concerns regarding the theoretical clarity and the completeness of the experimental evaluation would significantly strengthen the contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should clarify the discussion around Equation (8), explain the choice of ρ in the experiments, and include SVD++ as a baseline.
