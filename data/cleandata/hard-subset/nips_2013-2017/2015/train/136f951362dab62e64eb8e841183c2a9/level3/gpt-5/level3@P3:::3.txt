Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models each user–item interaction stream as a self-exciting Hawkes process, while tying parameters across all user–item pairs through low-rank structure. Concretely, it combines a base intensity matrix Λ0 and a self-excitation matrix A with nuclear-norm penalties to promote low rank; the triggering kernel is exponential by default, but the framework accommodates other kernels and contexts. The authors derive a convex surrogate formulation, introduce an auxiliary-variable exact-penalty variant, and solve it with a hybrid algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient (for nuclear-norm-regularized blocks). They prove an O(1/t) convergence bound for the composite scheme and show strong empirical performance for two tasks: (1) time-sensitive item recommendation (ranking at a time t); and (2) return-time prediction.

Relative to prior work, the paper is well positioned. It goes beyond classic static collaborative filtering (SVD, Poisson/tensor factorization) and beyond time-aware latent-factor models (e.g., TimeSVD++) by modeling the full temporal point process rather than discretized counts or time-varying biases. It is also distinct from survival-analysis approaches for return-time prediction that assume independent inter-event times, and from sequence models such as FPMC that capture short-order transitions but not self-exciting temporal dependence with continuous time. Hawkes processes have been widely used in diffusion and event analysis (e.g., Zhou et al., Du et al.), but coupling them with low-rank structures for recommendation and return-time prediction appears novel for NeurIPS at the time.

Strengths
- Principled stochastic-process modeling: Using Hawkes processes to capture self-excitation provides a coherent, continuous-time likelihood that matches the phenomena under study, and the time-change diagnostics are appropriate and convincing.
- Novel modeling and algorithmic combination: The low-rank Hawkes formulation enables transfer across sparse user–item pairs. The hybrid proximal + conditional-gradient solver is elegant, avoids full SVDs, and is scalable; the analysis articulates how each block contributes to the overall rate.
- Empirical performance and scale: The method outperforms existing approaches on synthetic and multiple real datasets for both ranking-at-time and return-time prediction, and scales to hundreds of millions of events.
- Clarity and readability: The paper is well written and easy to follow, with sensible experimental protocols and ablations demonstrating convergence and scaling.

Weaknesses and questions
- Exact-penalty theory and Eq. (8): The definition of ρ* in Eq. (8) (the threshold ensuring equivalence of the constrained and penalized problems) is unclear: it is not explicit what variables are being optimized over in the max, and the expression can be unbounded or ill-posed if the denominator vanishes. As written, ρ* depends on unknown optimal solutions (Λ0*, A*, Z1*, Z2*), making it impractical. Please (i) clarify the precise statement (variables and set over which the max is taken), (ii) provide a finite, computable lower bound for ρ* (e.g., in terms of Lipschitz constants or dual norms via standard exact-penalty results), or (iii) adopt a continuation scheme with a guarantee that increasing ρ yields equivalence in the limit.
- Theorem 2 relevance: The convergence result is for the penalized problem with a prescribed ρ, but in experiments ρ is tuned for best performance rather than set per the theory, and the theorem’s ρ is not used to select hyperparameters. This weakens the connection between theory and practice. Please either (a) use the theoretically valid ρ (or a provable bound) in at least one experiment to demonstrate consistency with Theorem 1–2, or (b) provide sensitivity analyses showing the algorithm’s robustness to ρ and when equivalence empirically holds.
- Baselines: Time-aware collaborative filtering baselines are incomplete. In particular, TimeSVD++/SVD++ (Koren 2009) is a strong time-aware baseline; even though it models discrete time effects differently, it remains a standard comparator for temporal recommendation and should be included. Sequential recommenders such as FPMC, and dynamic Poisson factorization would further strengthen comparisons.
- Evaluation metrics: For recommendation, please report Recall@k/NDCG@k besides rank of the true item. For return-time prediction, add calibrated probabilistic metrics (negative log-likelihood on held-out events, CRPS, or quantile coverage) and confidence intervals to assess statistical significance.
- Modeling scope: The model captures self-excitation within each user–item pair but not cross-excitation (e.g., listening to artist A increasing intensity for similar artist B). While extensions are discussed, noting the limitations and potential multi-dimensional Hawkes extensions would be valuable.
- Minor: The abstract mentions O(1/·) without the rate symbol; Theorem 2 shows a composite bound dominated by O(1/k). Please reconcile the claimed rate in the abstract with the proven bound. Also clarify computational complexity per-iteration (cost of top singular vectors and gradient computations) and any distributed/parallel implementation details.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling and optimization are solid and well motivated. The concerns around Eq. (8)/ρ* and the gap between theory and hyperparameter selection should be addressed. Empirical evidence is strong.
- Completeness: The work is a complete piece with modeling, algorithm, theory, and experiments, but would benefit from stronger baselines and more diagnostic metrics.
- Honesty: The paper is generally careful; please be more explicit about the practical handling of ρ and the conditions under which Theorem 1 holds in practice.

Clarity
- Very clear and well organized. Suggested additions: a brief intuitive explanation of the exact-penalty equivalence; a table of symbols; details on how top singular vectors are computed at scale (e.g., power iterations, warm starts).

Originality
- High. Combining Hawkes processes with low-rank user–item structure for joint time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction is novel for this setting.

Significance
- High potential impact: The approach targets an important and growing problem, is scalable, and shows improved results. The modeling choice and the solver could be reused in other continuous-time recommendation or EHR applications.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled continuous-time formulation with strong empirical gains.
- Scalable, elegant algorithm with convergence analysis.
- Clear writing; interpretable model; extensible to contexts.

Cons against acceptance
- Theoretical clarity issues around Eq. (8)/ρ*; theory–practice mismatch for ρ in experiments.
- Missing strong time-aware baselines (TimeSVD++/SVD++, FPMC).
- Limited evaluation metrics for ranking and return-time calibration.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Eq. (8) and provide a computable bound or continuation scheme for ρ; include a sensitivity study and at least one experiment with a theoretically valid ρ.
- Add SVD++/TimeSVD++ and, if possible, FPMC/dynamic PF baselines.
- Report Recall@k/NDCG@k and probabilistic time-forecast metrics with confidence intervals.
- Discuss potential multi-dimensional/cross-excitation extensions and limitations.
- Provide per-iteration complexity, memory footprint, and any distributed implementation details.

Overall recommendation
- Leaning accept. The paper offers a novel and well-executed contribution to time-sensitive recommendation with strong potential impact. Addressing the theoretical clarity of the penalty parameter and strengthening baselines would make it an excellent submission.
