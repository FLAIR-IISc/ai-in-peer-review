This review assesses the paper "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities".

### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendations by modeling recurrent user activities. The work addresses two key problems: (1) recommending the most relevant item to a user at a specific moment in time, and (2) predicting the user's next return time to a service or item. The core contribution is a model that elegantly connects self-exciting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models. For each user-item pair, a Hawkes process is used to capture the temporal dynamics, where past activities excite future ones. To generalize across all user-item pairs and handle data sparsity, the base intensity and self-excitation parameters are represented as low-rank matrices.

To learn the model parameters, the authors formulate a convex optimization problem involving the negative log-likelihood with nuclear norm regularization and non-negativity constraints. A key technical contribution is a novel and efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving a convergence rate of O(1/t). The proposed method is evaluated on one large synthetic dataset and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), demonstrating superior performance against several baselines, including Poisson processes, SVD, and tensor factorization methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for recommendation is a significant and novel contribution. This approach provides a principled way to model the rich temporal dynamics of user behavior, moving beyond static models that ignore when events occur. The unified framework to handle both time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction is a particular strength.

2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The paper presents a solid technical contribution in the form of a new optimization algorithm. Solving a convex problem with both non-negativity constraints and non-smooth nuclear norm regularization is non-trivial. The proposed algorithm is efficient, scalable, and comes with a theoretical convergence guarantee. This optimization method could be of independent interest to researchers working on related matrix and tensor problems.

3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough. The authors demonstrate the algorithm's fast convergence and scalability on a large synthetic dataset. On three real-world datasets from different domains, the proposed model consistently outperforms various relevant baselines on both recommendation and return-time prediction tasks. The use of the time-change theorem to validate the Hawkes process assumption against real data (Figure 3) is a nice addition that strengthens the modeling choices.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Motivation and Problem Framing:** The motivation for the problems, while intuitive, could be more empirically grounded.
    *   The central claim is that user preferences are time-varying. It would significantly strengthen the introduction to include a preliminary analysis of the real datasets to showcase this phenomenon. For instance, demonstrating how item popularity or user activity patterns change over different time scales (e.g., diurnal, weekly) would make a more compelling case for the proposed model.
    *   The motivation for predicting the exact return time could be elaborated. While ad bidding is mentioned, it is not fully clear how a company would leverage a precise time prediction versus a coarser-grained one (e.g., predicting the day of return).

2.  **Completeness of Experimental Baselines:** The evaluation of the return-time prediction task could be strengthened by including simpler, intuitive baselines. It is unclear if the proposed complex model is necessary compared to simple heuristics. I suggest comparing against:
    *   **Last Inter-event Time:** Predicting the next time gap will be the same as the most recent one.
    *   **Average/Median Inter-event Time:** Predicting based on the historical average or median time gap for a given user-item pair.
    These baselines are easy to implement and would provide a clearer picture of the value added by the Hawkes process formulation.

3.  **Return-Time Task Formulation:** Predicting the exact continuous time of a user's return is an extremely challenging regression task, where small errors can be penalized heavily. A potentially more practical and robust evaluation would be to frame this as a classification or binned regression problem. For example, predicting whether a user will return within a short (e.g., 1-5 hours), medium (5-24 hours), or long (>24 hours) time window. Success on such a task might be more directly actionable for a service provider.

4.  **Practical Significance of Performance Gains:** For the item recommendation task, the results show improved ranking metrics over baselines like SVD and Tensor factorization. However, it is difficult to assess the practical impact of this improvement. A qualitative discussion or analysis of what these improved rankings mean from a user's perspective would be valuable. Furthermore, a comparison to a strong, time-aware heuristic, such as "recommend the most popular items from the last 24 hours" (recalculated daily), would provide important context, as such methods are often hard to beat in practice.

### **Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The background on temporal point processes is helpful, and the proposed model is presented logically. The description of the optimization algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings is also clear.

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and technically sound method for an important problem in recommender systems. The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank models is an excellent contribution, and the scalable optimization algorithm is impressive. The primary weaknesses lie in the need for stronger empirical motivation and a more comprehensive set of baselines for the return-time prediction task. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper's claims and impact. The work is original and significant, and it is likely to inspire future research in time-aware recommender systems.
