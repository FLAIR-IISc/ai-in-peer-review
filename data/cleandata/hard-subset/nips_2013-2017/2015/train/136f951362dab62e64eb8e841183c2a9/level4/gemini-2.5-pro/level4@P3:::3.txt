This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation by proposing a novel framework that models recurrent user activities. The central idea is to combine self-exciting temporal point processes with low-rank models to simultaneously predict which item a user will be interested in and when they are likely to return to the service. The authors model the sequence of a user's interactions with an item as a Hawkes process, where the base intensity is governed by a user-item preference matrix and past interactions can excite future ones. To handle data sparsity and enable generalization to unobserved user-item pairs, they assume that the matrices representing the base intensity and self-excitation parameters are low-rank.

The parameter estimation is formulated as a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization. To solve this efficiently, the authors develop a novel algorithm that hybridizes proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, which is shown to be scalable and has a guaranteed convergence rate. The proposed model, named Hawkes-Recommender, is evaluated on two tasks—item recommendation at a specific time and return-time prediction—using both synthetic and three real-world datasets. The results demonstrate that the proposed method significantly outperforms existing baselines, including Poisson process models, survival analysis, and tensor factorization methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and principled approach to time-sensitive recommendation. The connection established between self-exciting point processes and low-rank collaborative filtering models is a significant conceptual contribution. This framework moves beyond static rating prediction and tackles more dynamic and practical problems like predicting the timing of user engagement, which is of great importance to both researchers and practitioners. The model is well-motivated, as user behavior often exhibits self-exciting patterns (e.g., listening to an artist makes one more likely to listen to them again soon).

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically strong. The formulation of the problem as a convex optimization problem is elegant. A key contribution is the development of an efficient optimization algorithm tailored for this problem, which cleverly handles the non-negativity constraints and the nuclear norm regularization separately. The inclusion of a convergence analysis (Theorem 2) provides solid theoretical grounding for the proposed algorithm.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient background on temporal point processes, which makes the paper accessible even to readers not deeply familiar with the area. The model, optimization procedure, and experimental setup are all explained in a clear and logical manner.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors use both synthetic data, which allows for controlled evaluation of the model's recovery properties and scalability, and three diverse real-world datasets (music, e-commerce, and clinical). The choice of evaluation tasks is well-aligned with the paper's goals. The analysis using the time-change theorem (Figure 3) is a particularly nice touch, providing empirical evidence that the Hawkes process assumption is a better fit for the data than alternatives.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Guarantees:** There is a slight disconnect between the theoretical analysis and its practical application. Theorem 1 establishes the equivalence of the original and alternative optimization problems under the condition that `ρ > ρ*`, where `ρ*` is a problem-dependent threshold. However, the definition of `ρ*` appears to depend on the optimal solution itself, making it non-trivial to set `ρ` in practice to satisfy this condition. The paper acknowledges this by treating `ρ` as a hyperparameter to be tuned in the experiments. This diminishes the practical utility of the theoretical bound on `ρ`. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion on whether `ρ*` can be bounded or estimated, or to clarify how the choice of `ρ` impacts the solution quality in practice.

2.  **Experimental Comparisons:** While the set of baselines is reasonable, the comparison could be strengthened by including other prominent time-aware recommendation models. For instance, models like timeSVD++ (Koren, KDD 2009) directly incorporate temporal dynamics into matrix factorization and represent a very strong and relevant baseline in this area. Comparing against such an established method would provide a more complete picture of where the proposed model stands in the landscape of time-sensitive collaborative filtering.

**Summary of Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound contribution to the important problem of time-sensitive recommendation. The proposed model is elegant, and the custom optimization algorithm is efficient and well-analyzed. The extensive empirical results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the approach. The weaknesses identified are minor and can be addressed with additional discussion and experiments in a final version. The work is a significant advancement and is likely to inspire future research in this direction. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and significant combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation.
*   Addresses important and practical problems beyond static rating prediction.
*   Strong technical contributions in both model formulation and the development of an efficient optimization algorithm with theoretical guarantees.
*   Comprehensive and convincing experimental validation on diverse datasets.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against Acceptance (minor):**
*   The practical utility of a key theoretical result (Theorem 1) is unclear due to the difficulty in setting the hyperparameter `ρ`.
*   The experimental comparison could be made more robust by including other strong time-aware factorization-based baselines.
