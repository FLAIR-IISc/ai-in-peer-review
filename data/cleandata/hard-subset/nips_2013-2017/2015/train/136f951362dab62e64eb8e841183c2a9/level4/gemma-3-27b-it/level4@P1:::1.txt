This paper proposes a novel method for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities, differing from existing approaches by explicitly addressing the problem of time-varying user preferences and tackling the related challenge of predicting a user's next returning time. The core contribution lies in a new convex formulation connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models, coupled with an efficient optimization algorithm for learning. The method is evaluated on a synthetic dataset demonstrating scalability, and two real-world datasets of modest size.

The paper identifies a significant gap in existing recommendation techniques, which often overlook the temporal dynamics of user behavior. While the importance of considering these dynamics is intuitively appealing – as highlighted by the example of context-dependent mobile assistant usage – the extent to which user preferences truly *vary* over time warrants further investigation. A more thorough analysis of the real datasets, perhaps quantifying the degree of temporal preference shift, would strengthen this claim. It would also be helpful to discuss whether observed variations are primarily driven by external factors like emerging popular items, or by intrinsic changes in user tastes.

The motivation for predicting a user’s next returning time could be more clearly articulated *before* presenting it as a core contribution. A concrete explanation of how a web company could leverage this prediction—for instance, in optimizing ad bidding strategies as suggested—would significantly enhance the paper's impact. 

The experimental setup could be improved by including several simple baselines for returning time prediction, such as predicting the next return time based on (1) the last observed time delay and (2) the most frequent time delay. Furthermore, predicting the *exact* returning time may be an overly ambitious goal. Framing the problem as a classification task—predicting whether a user will return within a short, medium, long, or very long timeframe—could yield more tractable and interpretable results.

The results section would benefit from a clearer quantification of the performance improvements. Is the lift over SVD substantial (order of magnitude) or incremental? Would a user perceive a noticeable difference in recommendation quality? A comparison to the performance of simply recommending consistently popular items – a strong and often difficult-to-beat baseline – is also missing.

Minor comments:

1) Typo in the last paragraph of page 3: "and is abel to capture", should be "and is able to capture".
2) In the first paragraph of Section 2, the introduction of retweets and link creation in social networks feels abrupt and lacks context. Rephrasing this section would improve clarity.

Overall, this is an OK/marginal paper. The authors identify an important problem, propose a novel approach, and demonstrate promising initial results. However, strengthening the motivation, including stronger baselines, and providing a more nuanced analysis of the experimental results would significantly enhance the paper’s contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach combining self-exciting point processes and low-rank models.
*   Development of an efficient optimization algorithm with theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrated scalability on a synthetic dataset.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in recommendation systems – incorporating temporal dynamics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical validation on relatively small real-world datasets.
*   Lack of comparison to strong baselines (e.g., popularity-based recommendations, simple returning time predictors).
*   Motivation for the returning time prediction task could be more compelling.
*   The extent to which user preferences truly vary over time is not convincingly demonstrated.
