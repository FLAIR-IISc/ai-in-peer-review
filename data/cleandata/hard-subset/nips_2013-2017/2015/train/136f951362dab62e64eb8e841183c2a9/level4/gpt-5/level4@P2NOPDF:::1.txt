This paper introduces a time-sensitive recommendation method that models time-varying user preferences from activity data and also predicts a user’s next return time. The authors propose an efficient optimization algorithm and report experiments on one large synthetic dataset (to demonstrate scalability) and two real, moderate-sized datasets.

Strengths
- Tackles temporal dynamics in user preferences, an important challenge in recommendation.
- Includes a return-time prediction component that could, in principle, be useful operationally.
- Presents an efficient learning algorithm and shows scalability on synthetic data.

Major comments
- Prevalence and nature of temporal drift: The paper asserts that user preferences vary over time, but does not quantify how common or strong this effect is in the evaluated datasets. Please analyze the real datasets to characterize temporal drift (e.g., distribution and magnitude of preference changes). Is drift primarily driven by global trends (e.g., new popular songs) or by user-specific shifts? This distinction matters for modeling choices and baselines.
- Motivation for return-time prediction: The paper lists next-return-time prediction as a contribution but provides little motivation. Clarify why this prediction is valuable and how it would be used (e.g., scheduling notifications, pacing recommendations, resource allocation, or session-level ranking). Without a clear use case, it is difficult to assess the practical impact of this component.
- Baselines for return-time prediction: Some simple and competitive baselines seem missing, such as predicting the last observed inter-arrival time or the most frequent (modal) inter-arrival time for the user. Please include these to contextualize gains.
- Task formulation for return-time: Predicting exact return times can be noisy and brittle. Consider a coarser, arguably more actionable formulation: classify the delay into bins such as short (1–5 days), medium (5–14), long (14–33), and very long (>33). This may be easier to learn and evaluate, and more directly linked to downstream decisions.
- Experimental clarity and baselines for recommendations: It is hard to judge how large the performance lift is over SVD. Please report absolute metrics and relative improvements, with statistical significance where applicable, and discuss whether the gains are likely perceptible to users. Also include a strong popularity baseline (e.g., re-computed daily or session-aware) since popularity is often hard to beat in practice. A head-to-head comparison would clarify the real-world value of the proposed method.
- Scope and narrative: The paper addresses two distinct problems—time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction—without a unifying motivation or clear interaction between them. Tighten the narrative: explain why both are needed and how they reinforce one another, or consider focusing on one to provide deeper analysis.

Experimental setup
- The synthetic dataset demonstrates scalability, but the real datasets are modest in size. Please discuss how well results might transfer to larger, noisier, real-world settings, and provide runtime/complexity analyses on real data where possible.

Clarity and presentation
- There is an abrupt context shift in Section 2 when introducing retweets and link creation without references. Provide citations and motivate their relevance to the recommendation and return-time tasks to avoid confusion.
- Typo on page 3, last paragraph: “and is abel to capture” → “and is able to capture.”

Overall assessment
Interesting direction with a potentially useful temporal component and an efficient learning algorithm. However, the paper currently lacks a clear motivation for return-time prediction, omits straightforward baselines, and provides limited analysis of temporal drift and practical impact. With stronger empirical baselines, clearer motivation and use cases, improved experimental reporting, and a tighter narrative, this work could be substantially strengthened. In its current form, I consider it borderline/OK.
