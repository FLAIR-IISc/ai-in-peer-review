This paper introduces a novel algorithm for time-sensitive recommendation that models user-item interactions by combining a low-rank matrix factorization approach with a Hawkes process. This formulation elegantly captures the self-exciting nature of user activity over time. A key contribution is a scalable, distributed algorithm for model estimation. The authors demonstrate empirically that their proposed method outperforms several existing approaches on real-world datasets.

**Quality and Significance**

The primary strength of this work lies in its principled approach. Modeling user ratings as a stochastic process, specifically using a Hawkes process to capture temporal dependencies, is a well-motivated and elegant way to address the dynamics inherent in recommendation systems. The development of a distributed optimization algorithm is also a significant practical contribution, making the method applicable to large-scale datasets. The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is highly relevant, and by providing a scalable and high-performing algorithm, this paper makes a solid contribution to the field with clear potential for practical impact.

While the overall approach is sound, I have two main points of feedback that could further strengthen the paper:

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Analysis:** My main concerns relate to the theoretical analysis, specifically regarding Equation (8) and Theorem 2.
    *   In Equation (8), the maximization appears to be with respect to the parameter ρ. It is unclear if the resulting optimal value, ρ*, could be unbounded. Could the authors clarify the optimization space for ρ and discuss the conditions under which ρ* is guaranteed to be finite?
    *   Furthermore, there seems to be a disconnect between the theoretical contribution of Theorem 2 and its practical application. The theorem provides a bound related to ρ, yet this result is not leveraged for hyperparameter selection in the experiments. This raises questions about the practical utility of the theorem. Clarifying how this theoretical result could inform or guide the experimental setup would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2.  **Experimental Baselines:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including a more comprehensive set of baselines. Notably, SVD++ [17], a well-known and highly effective model from the Netflix Prize that also incorporates temporal dynamics, is a natural point of comparison. While the current baselines are reasonable, including SVD++ would provide a more robust benchmark and better contextualize the performance gains of the proposed method.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the model and the derivation of the learning algorithm.

**Originality**

The work is novel in its synthesis of low-rank matrix models and point processes for the time-sensitive recommendation task. While the problem domain itself is an active area of research, the specific combination of these powerful techniques represents a distinct and valuable contribution.
