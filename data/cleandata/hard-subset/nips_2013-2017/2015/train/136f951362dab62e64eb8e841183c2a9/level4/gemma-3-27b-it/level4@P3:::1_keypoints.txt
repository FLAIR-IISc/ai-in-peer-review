- Proposes a novel framework combining self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions.  
- Targets two tasks: recommending the most relevant item at the right time and predicting a user's next return.  
- Presents a convex formulation and an efficient optimization algorithm with O(1/t) convergence guarantees.  
- Demonstrates scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.  
- Shows strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real datasets against multiple baselines.  
- Novel connection between Hawkes/self-exciting processes and low-rank matrix factorization; can recommend new items and leverage low-rank structure for generalization.  
- Framework is flexible and can incorporate additional contextual information (profiles, text, spatial features).  
- Motivation for return-time prediction is underdeveloped and needs more concrete, real-world examples beyond ad bidding.  
- Missing simple baselines for return time prediction (e.g., last-time delay, most frequent delay) and a popularity-based recommendation baseline.  
- Experimental results do not clearly report magnitude or statistical significance of improvements or user-perceptible gains.  
- Lacks preliminary dataset analysis to demonstrate true time-varying user preferences versus capturing popularity trends.  
- Minor clarity issues in writing (abrupt context shift in Section 2) and a typo on page 3.  
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, novel, strong theory, empirical validation, and real-world scalability.  
- Arguments for rejection: weak motivation/baselines, unclear practical significance of gains, and insufficient dataset justification.  
- Overall assessment: solid, novel, above-average/borderline accept; recommending a rebuttal period and revisions addressing baselines, motivation, and significance.
