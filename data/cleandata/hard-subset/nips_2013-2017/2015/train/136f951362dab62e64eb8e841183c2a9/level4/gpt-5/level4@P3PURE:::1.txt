Summary
- The paper proposes a new method for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities. It differs from existing methods by explicitly addressing time-varying user preferences. The paper also addresses prediction of the next return time of a user. Learning is performed using an efficient optimization algorithm proposed by the authors. The experimental section reports results on one synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability and on two real datasets of modest size.

Major comments and questions
- Prevalence and nature of time-varying preferences: Is the problem of time-varying user preferences truly widespread? It would be valuable to analyze the real datasets used in the experiments to substantiate this claim. If it is prevalent, is the variation driven mostly by the emergence of new popular songs, or are there other dominant patterns?
- Motivation for return-time prediction: What is the motivation for predicting the next return time of a user? Please motivate this task before presenting it as a contribution. How could a web company use this prediction, and for what concrete purposes?
- Baselines and problem formulation for return-time prediction: There are simple baselines for predicting the next return time that appear to be missing: (1) the last inter-visit time (last time delay) and (2) the most frequent inter-visit time (most occurred time delay). In addition, I think predicting the exact return time is too challenging. Instead, I would focus on a simpler task that is easier to predict—for example, classifying whether the user will return after a short delay (1–5 days), medium delay (5–14 days), long delay (14–33 days), or very long delay (>33 days).
- Experimental significance: It was hard to gauge how significant the performance lift is compared to SVD. Is the improvement groundbreaking (order of magnitude) or modest? Would a user perceive the difference?
- Popular-items baseline: How would these recommendations compare to recommending popular items, especially if popularity is recalculated daily? This is often a hard baseline to beat.

Presentation and clarity
- In the first paragraph of Section 2, you start mentioning retweets and link creation in social networks without any references. The abrupt change of context is confusing; it would be best to rephrase and add appropriate citations.
- Typo: last paragraph on page 3: “and is abel to capture” → “and is able to capture.”

Overall assessment
- The paper addresses two problems—time-sensitive recommendations and prediction of user return time—without clear motivation for their relevance and practical impact. Several baselines and analyses are missing (as detailed above). Other than that, overall this reads as an OK/marginal paper.
