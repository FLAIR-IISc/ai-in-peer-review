This paper proposes a novel method for time-sensitive recommendations that models recurrent user activities. The core idea is to connect self-exciting point processes (specifically, Hawkes processes) with low-rank matrix models to capture both long-term user preferences and short-term, history-dependent temporal dynamics. This unified framework is applied to two main tasks: (1) recommending the most relevant item at a given moment, and (2) predicting a user's next return time to a service. The authors formulate parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem and develop an efficient algorithm that blends proximal and conditional gradient methods, providing theoretical convergence guarantees. The approach is evaluated on a large synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability and on three real-world datasets to show predictive performance.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed model, which connects low-rank models with self-exciting point processes, is a novel and principled approach for modeling temporal dynamics in recommender systems.
*   The formulation of the learning problem as a convex optimization problem is a significant advantage, as it provides guarantees for finding a global optimum.
*   The paper presents a technically sound and efficient optimization algorithm with a clear convergence analysis demonstrating optimal rates. The scalability of the algorithm is convincingly demonstrated on synthetic data.
*   The work addresses an important and challenging problem area in recommender systems, moving beyond static, one-time preference prediction to model continuously time-varying user behavior.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper's motivation could be significantly strengthened. While the introduction posits that modeling time-varying preferences and predicting return times are important, these claims would be more compelling with supporting analysis from the datasets used.
    *   For time-varying preferences, an exploratory analysis of the real-world datasets could demonstrate the prevalence and nature of this phenomenon. For example, are preferences changing due to global trends (e.g., new popular songs appearing) or are there more subtle, user-specific patterns that the model is designed to capture?
    *   The motivation for predicting the exact user return time needs to be more clearly established. A more detailed discussion of the practical applications and how a company would leverage this specific prediction would help clarify the significance of this task as a primary contribution.

2.  **Experimental Baselines for Return-Time Prediction:** The evaluation of the return-time prediction task could be made more rigorous by including simpler, yet potentially strong, heuristic baselines. For instance, it would be valuable to compare against predictions based on:
    *   The duration of the user's most recent time delay between activities.
    *   The most frequently occurring (modal) time delay for that user.
    Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the practical gains of the complex model proposed.

3.  **Return-Time Prediction Task Formulation:** Predicting the precise, continuous return time of a user is an exceptionally challenging task. The paper could potentially have a greater impact by also considering a simplified, and perhaps more practical, version of this problem. For example, framing the task as a classification problem—predicting whether a user will return in a short (e.g., 1-5 days), medium (5-14 days), or long (>14 days) interval—could be more robust and immediately useful for applications like targeted marketing or churn prevention.

4.  **Clarity of Experimental Results for Recommendation:** While the proposed model shows performance gains over baselines like SVD, the practical significance of these improvements could be better contextualized.
    *   It is difficult to interpret the magnitude of the improvement from the reported metrics alone. A discussion of what the performance lift means from a user's perspective would be beneficial. For example, is the improvement modest, or is it substantial enough that a user would clearly notice a difference in recommendation quality?
    *   The recommendation experiments should include a comparison against a strong, time-aware popularity baseline. Recommending the most popular items (with popularity recalculated daily or weekly) is often a very difficult baseline to beat in practice. While the Poisson process model captures static popularity, a dynamic popularity baseline would provide a more challenging and realistic benchmark for the time-sensitive recommendation task.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces a novel and technically sound method for time-sensitive recommendation. The fusion of low-rank models and Hawkes processes is compelling, and the development of an efficient, provably convergent optimization algorithm is a solid technical contribution. However, the paper's impact could be substantially improved by strengthening the motivation with data-driven analysis. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation would be more convincing with the inclusion of stronger and simpler baselines for both the recommendation and return-time prediction tasks, alongside a clearer discussion of the practical significance of the results. Overall, this is a borderline paper containing interesting ideas that require revision to fully demonstrate their contribution to the field.
