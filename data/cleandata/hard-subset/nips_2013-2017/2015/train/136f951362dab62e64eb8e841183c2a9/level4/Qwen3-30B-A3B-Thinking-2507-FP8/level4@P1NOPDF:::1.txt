

The paper introduces a novel method for time-sensitive recommendations that accounts for time-varying user preferences and predicts user return intervals. While the approach addresses two distinct problems—personalized recommendations with temporal dynamics and user return time forecasting—the paper lacks sufficient motivation for both contributions and omits key baselines.  

The claim that time-varying preferences are widespread requires stronger empirical support. The authors should analyze the real-world datasets used in experiments to demonstrate how preferences evolve (e.g., due to seasonal trends, new content releases, or shifting user interests). Without this, the premise feels speculative. Similarly, the motivation for predicting return times is unclear. Why would a service provider care about exact return intervals? How would this prediction directly improve user experience or business metrics (e.g., retention, engagement)? The paper must explicitly justify this as a meaningful contribution before presenting it.  

For the return time prediction task, the authors overlook simple yet effective baselines. Predicting the *last observed time interval* (e.g., how long the user waited between their last two sessions) or the *most frequent interval* across all users are natural baselines that should be tested. More critically, predicting exact return times appears overly ambitious. A more practical and achievable goal would be to classify return intervals into coarse categories (e.g., short: 1–5 days, medium: 5–14 days, long: 14–33 days, very long: >33 days). This aligns better with real-world use cases where service providers might act on probabilistic insights (e.g., sending a targeted notification for users likely to return in 3–5 days).  

The experimental evaluation has significant gaps. The performance gains over SVD (or other baselines) are described as "modest" but lack quantitative context. Is the improvement statistically significant? Does it translate to a noticeable user experience difference (e.g., higher engagement rates)? Crucially, the paper fails to compare against the strong baseline of *daily recalculated popular items*—a common, hard-to-beat heuristic in recommendation systems. Without this, it’s impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers meaningful gains beyond standard practice.  

Minor issues:  
1. Page 3, last paragraph: "and is abel to capture" → "and is able to capture."  
2. Section 2, first paragraph: The abrupt shift to discussing "retweets and link creation in social networks" without context or references is jarring. Rephrase to clarify the connection to the paper’s core problems.  

Overall, the paper tackles relevant problems but falls short in justification, baseline comparison, and experimental rigor. The method’s novelty is diluted by insufficient motivation and unaddressed practical constraints. While the optimization algorithm appears efficient, the broader contribution lacks impact without clearer alignment to real-world needs. The results on two modest-sized datasets and one synthetic set are insufficient to demonstrate scalability or practical utility. The paper would benefit significantly from deeper analysis of real data, stronger baselines, and explicit discussion of how the proposed solutions address tangible business or user challenges. As presented, it is a marginal contribution at best.
