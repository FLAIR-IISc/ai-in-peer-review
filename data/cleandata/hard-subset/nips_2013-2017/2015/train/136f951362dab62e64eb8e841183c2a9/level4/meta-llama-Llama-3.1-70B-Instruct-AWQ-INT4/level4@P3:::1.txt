This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities. The framework connects self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption pairs. The authors develop an efficient optimization algorithm to learn the model parameters, which achieves a fast convergence rate and scales up to problems with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events.

The paper addresses two related questions: how to recommend the most desirable item at the right moment and how to predict the next returning time of a user to a service. The proposed model is a significant departure from existing recommendation algorithms, which mainly focus on predicting users' one-time preferences. By incorporating temporal dynamics and recurrent activities, the model can capture users' time-varying preferences and provide more accurate recommendations.

The experimental section demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model on both synthetic and real datasets. The results show that the model outperforms state-of-the-art competitors, including Poisson process, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods. The authors also provide an analysis of the convergence rate of the optimization algorithm and demonstrate its scalability.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel framework that addresses a crucial aspect of recommendation systems, namely, time-sensitive recommendations.
* The model is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the underlying assumptions and mathematical formulations.
* The experimental section is comprehensive, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model.

Weaknesses:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the real datasets used in the experiments. Specifically, it would be helpful to understand the characteristics of the data, such as the distribution of user preferences and item popularity.
* The motivation for predicting the next returning time of a user could be more clearly explained. How would a web company use this prediction, and what would be the benefits of accurate predictions?
* Some simple baselines for predicting the next returning time, such as the last time delay and the most occurred time delay, were not tested. It would be helpful to include these baselines in the experimental section.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed model and potential avenues for future research.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel framework that addresses a crucial aspect of recommendation systems.
* The model is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the underlying assumptions and mathematical formulations.
* The experimental section is comprehensive, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the real datasets used in the experiments.
* The motivation for predicting the next returning time of a user could be more clearly explained.
* Some simple baselines for predicting the next returning time were not tested.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed model and its underlying assumptions. The experimental section is comprehensive, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the real datasets and a clearer explanation of the motivation for predicting the next returning time of a user. With some revisions to address these issues, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of recommendation systems.
