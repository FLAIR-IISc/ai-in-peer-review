Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommender based on low-rank Hawkes processes. For each user–item pair, the event sequence is modeled with a self-exciting point process whose intensity is the sum of a nonnegative baseline term and a history-dependent triggering component. To share statistical strength and enable generalization to unseen pairs, the baseline and self-excitation parameters form two low-rank nonnegative matrices learned via a convex formulation with nuclear-norm regularization. The authors introduce an optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient (to handle nonnegativity) with conditional gradient/LMO (to handle nuclear norms), prove an O(1/t) convergence rate for the composite problem, and show (via a penalty argument) equivalence to the constrained formulation for sufficiently large penalty. The learned model supports (a) time-sensitive top-k recommendation by ranking items using instantaneous intensities, and (b) prediction of a user’s next return time via sampling from the conditional density.

The work builds on temporal point processes (Daley & Vere-Jones), Hawkes processes (Hawkes), and recent machine learning applications of Hawkes with low-rank/sparse structure (e.g., Du et al.; Zhou, Zha & Song). It contrasts with survival-analysis approaches to return-time prediction (Kapoor et al.) and with temporal collaborative filtering (Koren’s timeSVD++, Koenigstein et al.) and tensor factorization methods for context-aware recommendation. The authors also compare to Poisson-process and tensor Poisson baselines.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: A clear and useful connection between low-rank models and Hawkes processes for recommender systems. The convex surrogate with nuclear norms and nonnegativity is well motivated, and the penalty-based reformulation with separate handling of constraints and norms is elegant.
- Algorithmic contribution: An efficient hybrid of proximal and conditional gradient with an explicit convergence guarantee for this composite constraint/regularizer structure. The per-iteration updates rely on projections and top singular vectors, making the method scalable.
- Scope: A unified framework that addresses both time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction, with a principled way to incorporate side information through the intensity function.
- Empirical scale: Synthetic experiments with hundreds of millions of events demonstrate favorable scaling; real-data experiments span music, e-commerce, and healthcare.

Weaknesses
- Motivation and problem framing: The paper asserts the prevalence of time-varying preferences and the importance of return-time prediction, but does not quantify these phenomena on the real datasets. Stronger descriptive analyses (e.g., drift in inter-event distributions, seasonality, or novelty effects) would substantiate the claims and guide model design.
- Baselines for return-time: Simple and competitive baselines are missing, such as (i) last inter-event time (LIET), (ii) median/mode inter-event time per user–item, (iii) user- or item-level renewal/Poisson models, and (iv) time-varying popularity. These would better calibrate the MAE improvements.
- Evaluation metrics: For recommendation, reporting only average rank obscures practical impact. Standard metrics (Recall@k, NDCG@k, MAP) and a strong popularity baseline (updated frequently) are needed to judge user-perceived gains. For return-time, consider also RMSE, relative error, and calibration plots.
- Empirical depth: The real datasets are moderate in size, and the magnitude of improvement over SVD/tensor baselines is not clearly quantified (confidence intervals, effect sizes). Ablations (e.g., baseline-only Λ0, excitation-only A, kernel choices) and sensitivity (to σ, rank surrogates, penalty ρ) are lacking.
- Clarity and details: Some notation is heavy, and algorithmic details are missing (how top singular vectors are computed, stopping criteria, step-size/Lipschitz estimation, memory footprint). The motivation for sampling-based return-time estimation could be strengthened (are there tractable alternatives for the exponential kernel?).

Quality
The modeling and optimization components are technically sound, and the theoretical statements are plausible given the structure of the likelihood and penalties. The convergence analysis is a nice contribution. The empirical section demonstrates scalability and promising accuracy, but would benefit from stronger baselines, ablations, and standard ranking metrics to fully support the claims.

Clarity
Overall the paper is readable, but certain sections could be tightened. Clearly define evaluation protocols and metrics; explicitly motivate the two tasks with concrete use cases and dataset statistics; and provide more algorithmic detail (per-iteration cost, computation of top singular vectors, and memory usage). Minor typos are listed below.

Originality
The application of low-rank Hawkes processes to recommender systems with a convex estimation procedure, plus the hybrid optimization scheme with a provable rate under nonnegativity and nuclear norms, appears novel relative to NIPS and related venues.

Significance
If validated with stronger baselines and metrics, this approach could influence time-sensitive recommendation and user modeling, particularly where recurrent behaviors and temporal context matter (media, commerce, healthcare). The optimization technique may be of independent interest for other nonnegative low-rank problems.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually compelling integration of Hawkes processes and low-rank structure for recommendation and return-time prediction.
- Efficient algorithm with convergence guarantees; demonstrated scalability.
- Broad applicability and extensibility to side information and contexts.
- Promising results on multiple domains.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient motivation and empirical evidence for the prevalence/structure of time-varying preferences on real data.
- Missing strong, simple baselines for return-time and popularity-based recommendation; unclear practical lift over SVD/popularity.
- Limited evaluation metrics and lack of ablations/sensitivity analyses.
- Some clarity and presentation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Motivation and data analysis: Quantify temporal drift and recurrence patterns (e.g., Q–Q plots, seasonality, novelty decay) on the real datasets; report how often LIET or popularity baselines succeed.
- Baselines: Add LIET, median/mode inter-event time, user-level and item-level Poisson/renewal models, and a daily-updated popularity recommender. For tensors, include timeSVD++-style baselines if feasible.
- Metrics: Report Recall@k/NDCG@k@{5,10,20} and compare to popularity; include statistical significance tests. For return-time, add RMSE and calibration; consider a discretized classification task (short/medium/long/very long gaps) as a more actionable alternative for deployment.
- Ablations and sensitivity: Evaluate Λ0-only vs A-only models; vary kernel bandwidth σ, penalty ρ, and nuclear-norm weights; show how gains depend on the amount of history.
- Algorithmic details: Describe computation of top singular vectors (e.g., power method, number of iterations), stopping criteria, per-iteration complexity, and memory usage; discuss how ρ is chosen and its effect (given Theorem 1).
- Practical deployment: Elaborate on use cases for return-time prediction (e.g., notification timing, budgeted ad bidding, proactive clinical monitoring) to clarify impact.

Minor comments
- Typo: page 3, last paragraph: “abel” → “able”.
- Section 2: The shift in examples to retweets/link creation (if retained) should be supported with citations and a clearer tie-in to the two target tasks.
- Notation: Clearly define all vectors/matrices when first introduced; consider a notation table in the appendix.

Overall assessment
A promising and technically solid paper with a novel formulation and scalable algorithm. I lean toward acceptance if the authors strengthen the empirical evaluation with the suggested baselines, metrics, ablations, and clearer motivation; otherwise, it remains borderline due to evaluation gaps rather than technical flaws.
