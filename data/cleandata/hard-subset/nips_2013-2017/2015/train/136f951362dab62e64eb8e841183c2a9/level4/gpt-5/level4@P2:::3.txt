Summary
The paper tackles two coupled problems: recommending the right item at the right time and predicting a user’s next return time. The authors model each user–item pair as a self-exciting Hawkes process whose intensity combines a baseline term and history-dependent triggering from past events. To enable generalization to unseen pairs, they impose low-rank structure on the user–item baseline and excitation matrices via nuclear-norm regularization and nonnegativity constraints, yielding a convex estimation problem. They propose an efficient optimization method that blends proximal gradient (to enforce nonnegativity) with a conditional gradient step (to handle nuclear norms through linear oracles), facilitated by an auxiliary-variable penalty reformulation. They establish an O(1/t) convergence rate and show scalability to millions of pairs and hundreds of millions of events. Experiments on synthetic data and multiple real datasets (e.g., last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) report strong performance for both time-aware recommendation (ranking at a given moment) and return-time prediction, outperforming Poisson, tensor factorization variants, SVD, and survival-analysis baselines (e.g., STiC). The approach extends naturally to include contextual features.

Relation to prior work
The paper contrasts its continuous-time, self-exciting formulation with survival models for return-time prediction (e.g., Kapoor et al.), which often assume independent inter-event times and cannot recommend new items. It also compares with matrix/tensor factorization approaches that discretize time or ignore recurrent temporal dependencies. This work’s connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank transfer across user–item pairs is, to my knowledge, new in the recommendation literature. It would be valuable to relate more explicitly to time-aware matrix factorization (e.g., Koren’s temporal dynamics, SVD++) as strong baselines for item ranking.

Quality
- Strengths: The modeling choice is principled: Hawkes processes naturally capture recurrent behavior and recency effects, while low-rank structure transfers information across sparse user–item pairs. The convex formulation with nuclear norms and nonnegativity is well motivated. The optimization algorithm is carefully designed to exploit problem structure, with a clear convergence guarantee and convincing scalability in practice. Empirical results are thorough across tasks and datasets.
- Concerns: The definition of the threshold rho* in the penalty reformulation needs to be clearer. In the expression for rho* (the “max” ratio), over which variables is the maximum taken, and under what conditions is rho* finite? As written, it is not obvious whether rho* can be unbounded. Moreover, the experiments tune rho, lambda, and beta for best performance without an explicit link to the theoretical threshold or guidance from Theorem 2, which diminishes the practical role of the analysis. Clarifying how rho was selected, and whether the theoretical choice is ever used or approximated in practice, would strengthen the contribution.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The problem setup, model, and algorithms are presented cleanly, and the empirical protocol is clear. Minor suggestions: explicitly state the domain of the maximization in the rho* expression and provide brief practical guidance for choosing rho. A short complexity summary (per-iteration cost and dependence on rank/sparsity) would also help readers.

Originality
The integration of low-rank transfer with Hawkes processes for recommendation and return-time prediction, along with the proximal–conditional gradient algorithm for the resulting convex problem with nonnegativity and nuclear norms, appears novel. The continuous-time perspective distinguishes it from common time-aware/discretized approaches.

Significance
The work addresses impactful tasks where temporal dynamics matter. The model’s ability to capture recurrent behavior and transfer knowledge across pairs, coupled with strong scalability, makes it potentially useful for large-scale recommender systems and event modeling in other domains (e.g., health).

Pros
- Principled continuous-time model (Hawkes) with low-rank transfer across user–item pairs.
- Convex formulation; efficient algorithm with O(1/t) convergence and strong scalability.
- Strong performance on both time-aware ranking and return-time prediction across datasets.
- Flexible to include context (profiles, text, location).

Cons
- Ambiguity around the definition and boundedness of rho*; unclear practical use of the theory when tuning rho.
- Missing strong time-aware baselines for ranking such as SVD++/temporal dynamics; adding them would strengthen the empirical case.
- Minor clarity issues on parameter selection and computational complexity summary.

Recommendation
A solid and timely contribution. Addressing the rho* clarification and adding a strong time-aware MF baseline (e.g., SVD++) in the ranking evaluation would further improve the paper, but the core ideas, theory, and empirical results are compelling.
