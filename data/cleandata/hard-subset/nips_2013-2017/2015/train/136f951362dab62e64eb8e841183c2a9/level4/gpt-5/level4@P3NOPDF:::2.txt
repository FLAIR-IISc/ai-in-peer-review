Summary
The paper proposes a collaborative filtering model based on temporal point processes to capture both long-term preferences and recurrent user–item interactions. For each user–item pair, the event intensity is modeled as the sum of a constant background rate and a self-exciting component driven by past events. These two components are parameterized by separate sets of user and item latent vectors. The authors derive an optimization algorithm for parameter estimation and report empirical gains over commonly used alternatives.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant problem: incorporating temporal and recurrent structure into recommendation is practically important.
- Methodological contribution: a principled combination of point processes with latent factor models. The separation between background and self-exciting intensities is intuitive and interpretable.
- Optimization: the paper provides a concrete training procedure rather than a purely conceptual model.
- Empirical promise: reported improvements over baselines suggest the approach is competitive.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Baselines and cold-start: Since the paper argues that prior temporal models such as STiC [12] and [13] cannot recommend entirely new items, a strong and fair baseline would be a simple ensemble that combines a temporal model (e.g., STiC) with a static latent-factor model (e.g., SVD). A straightforward approach is to linearly combine scores or to fall back to SVD for items unseen in the temporal component. Please include this comparison and describe how the ensemble is tuned.
- Kernel specification and sensitivity: The self-exciting term relies on a gamma function with bandwidth σ. How is σ chosen (e.g., cross-validation, heuristics, per-user/item vs. global)? How sensitive are the results to σ? A brief sensitivity analysis (e.g., performance vs. σ) would clarify robustness and the temporal scale the model exploits.
- Hyperparameter transparency: Important details are missing for both the proposed method and baselines, including:
  - Latent dimensionality for each component (background vs. self-exciting), and whether these dimensions are shared or distinct.
  - Regularization strengths, learning rates, optimization schedules, initialization, and early stopping criteria.
  - Validation protocol used to select hyperparameters for all methods. Please ensure baselines receive comparable tuning.
  Without these details, reproducibility and the fairness of comparisons are hard to assess.
- Dataset characterization: Since the contribution centers on temporal dynamics, the paper would benefit from descriptive statistics of the datasets:
  - Distribution of the number of recurring events per user–item pair (and the fraction of singletons).
  - Inter-event time distributions and measures of irregularity (e.g., coefficient of variation).
  - Temporal coverage, event density over time, and prevalence of cold-start items/users.
  - The proportion of training vs. test events and the temporal split protocol.
  These statistics would contextualize when and why the self-exciting component helps.
- Evaluation details: Please clarify metrics, how predictions are generated over time (e.g., next-event prediction vs. time-to-event), and whether confidence intervals or statistical tests are reported. Reporting variability would strengthen claims of improvement.
- Clarity and writing: The presentation can be improved by correcting typos and grammar, defining notation early (especially the two sets of latent vectors and the kernel), and providing more intuition for the intensity decomposition. A small figure illustrating how past events modulate intensity over time would help.

Originality
The paper offers a meaningful integration of temporal point processes with latent factor models. While both ingredients are known, their joint use to separate background preference from self-excitation at the user–item level is interesting and practically motivated.

Significance
Modeling recurrence and timing in recommendations is highly relevant, and the proposed framework has practical value. With stronger baselines (including an STiC+SVD ensemble), clearer hyperparameter reporting, and dataset characterization, the empirical evidence would be substantially more compelling.

Quality
Overall technically sound with a reasonable optimization approach. The experimental section is promising but currently under-documented; additional analyses as suggested above would improve credibility and impact.

Clarity
Readable but needs polishing. Addressing language issues, tightening notation, and adding implementation details will improve accessibility.

Questions for the authors
- How is σ selected, and how sensitive are results to it? Is σ global or parameterized (e.g., per item or per category)?
- Are the latent dimensions for background and self-exciting components the same? Did you explore tying or sharing parameters?
- How does the method handle completely new items at test time? If it relies on background factors, what information initializes them?
- What is the computational complexity per event, and how does training scale with the number of users, items, and events?

Overall assessment
A solid and timely contribution with clear potential. I recommend acceptance contingent on strengthening the empirical evaluation (particularly the ensemble baseline), providing hyperparameter and dataset details, and improving the writing.
