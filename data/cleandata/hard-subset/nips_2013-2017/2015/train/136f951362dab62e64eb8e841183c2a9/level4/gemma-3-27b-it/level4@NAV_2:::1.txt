This paper introduces a novel framework connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to address the challenges of time-sensitive recommendation and predicting user returning times. The core idea is to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions, moving beyond traditional methods that primarily focus on static preferences. The authors present a convex formulation for the problem and develop an efficient optimization algorithm with provable convergence guarantees, demonstrating scalability to large datasets. Empirical evaluations on synthetic and real-world datasets showcase the superior performance of the proposed model compared to state-of-the-art approaches.

The paper tackles an important problem: incorporating temporal dynamics into recommendation systems. Existing techniques often overlook the evolving nature of user preferences and the impact of timing on recommendation effectiveness. This work directly addresses these limitations by modeling user behavior as a temporal point process. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a key novelty, enabling knowledge transfer across user-item pairs and efficient parameter estimation. The theoretical analysis of the optimization algorithm, establishing an O(1/t) convergence rate, adds to the technical strength of the paper. The consideration of incorporating additional contextual information (profile, textual, spatial features) enhances the potential applicability of the framework.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough motivation of the problem and a more detailed analysis of the experimental results. While the authors mention the inadequacy of traditional methods, a stronger argument for the prevalence of time-varying user preferences would be valuable. Investigating the underlying reasons for these variations – whether driven by external factors like new item releases or intrinsic shifts in user interests – would provide further context. The motivation for predicting the exact returning time of a user could be strengthened with concrete examples of how this prediction could be used in practical applications for service providers (e.g., optimized ad bidding). 

The experimental evaluation would be improved by including several baselines. Specifically, considering the last observed inter-event time and the most frequent inter-event time as simple predictors of returning time would provide a useful point of comparison.  Furthermore, framing the returning time prediction task as a classification problem (e.g., predicting short, medium, long, or very long delays) might be more realistic and yield more interpretable results than predicting the exact time. A more detailed discussion of the performance gains over SVD, quantifying the magnitude of the improvement and its practical significance, is needed.  Additionally, comparing the proposed method against recommending simply the most popular items (dynamically updated) would provide a valuable benchmark.

Minor points: A typo exists on page 3, paragraph 1: "and is abel to capture" should read "and is able to capture." The introduction of social network concepts (retweets, link creation) in the first paragraph of Section 2 feels abrupt and lacks context; rephrasing is recommended.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach combining Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation.
*   Strong theoretical foundations with provable convergence guarantees for the optimization algorithm.
*   Demonstrated scalability to large datasets.
*   Empirically outperforms existing methods on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   Potential for incorporating additional contextual information.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Motivation for the problem and the specific focus on exact returning time prediction could be stronger.
*   Missing baseline comparisons (e.g., last time delay, most frequent delay, popularity-based recommendations).
*   Lack of detailed analysis of the experimental results to quantify the significance of the performance gains.
*   Some minor clarity issues in the presentation.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a promising approach. Addressing the aforementioned weaknesses would significantly strengthen the contribution and increase its impact. I rate this as a **marginal accept**, contingent upon addressing the concerns raised above.
