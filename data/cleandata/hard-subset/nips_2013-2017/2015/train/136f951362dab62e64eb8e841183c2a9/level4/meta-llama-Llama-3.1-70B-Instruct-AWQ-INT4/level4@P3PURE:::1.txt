This paper proposes a novel method for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities, distinguishing itself from existing methods by addressing the challenge of time-varying user preferences. The authors also tackle the prediction of the next returning time of a user, employing an efficient optimization algorithm for learning. The experimental section presents results on one synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability and two real datasets of modest size.

However, it is unclear how prevalent the problem of time-varying user preferences truly is. Analyzing the real datasets from the experiments would strengthen this claim. If time-varying preferences are indeed widespread, it would be beneficial to investigate the underlying causes. Are new popular releases driving these variations, or are there other patterns at play?

Furthermore, the motivation behind predicting the next returning time for users is not clearly established. It would be helpful to provide a justification for this contribution and explain how a web company could utilize this prediction. What benefits would this prediction bring to the company, and how would it be integrated into their operations?

In terms of baseline comparisons, two simple yet relevant methods for predicting the next returning time were overlooked: 1) the last time delay, and 2) the most occurred time delay. Moreover, predicting the exact returning time of a user may be too ambitious. A more feasible approach might be to focus on predicting the likelihood of a user returning within a specific timeframe, such as short (1-5 days), medium (5-14 days), long (14-33 days), or very long (>33 days) delays.

The experimental section could be improved by providing a clearer sense of the performance lift compared to SVD. Is the improvement substantial (order of magnitude) or modest? Would the end-user notice a significant difference? Additionally, it would be valuable to compare these recommendations to a simple yet effective baseline: recommending popular items, which are often recalculated daily.

Minor comments include a typo in the last paragraph of page 3 ("and is abel to capture" should be "and is able to capture"). The first paragraph of Section 2 abruptly shifts context by mentioning retweets and link creation in social networks without proper references, causing confusion. Rephrasing this section would enhance clarity.

Overall, while the paper addresses two distinct problems – time-sensitive recommendations and returning time of users – it lacks concrete motivation for tackling these issues and explaining their relevance. Several baselines and analyses are missing, which detracts from the paper's impact. Nevertheless, the paper is generally acceptable, albeit marginal.
