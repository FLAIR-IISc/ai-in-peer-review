This paper proposes a new method for time-sensitive recommendations based on the temporal dynamics of user activities. A key contribution is its focus on modeling time-varying user preferences, which distinguishes it from many existing approaches. Additionally, the model predicts the next return time of a user. The authors propose an efficient optimization algorithm for model learning. The experimental evaluation includes a synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability and two real-world datasets of modest size to assess performance.

While the paper addresses an interesting problem, its contributions are undermined by a lack of motivation, missing baselines, and insufficient analysis.

**Major Comments**

1.  **Motivation for Time-Varying Preferences:** The central premise of the paper is the importance of modeling time-varying user preferences. However, the motivation for this problem could be significantly strengthened. To substantiate the claim that this is a widespread and critical issue, an analysis of the provided real-world datasets would be beneficial. For instance, does the variance in user preference stem primarily from the emergence of new popular items (e.g., new songs), or are there other underlying user-specific temporal patterns? Providing this evidence would better ground the paper's contributions.

2.  **Motivation and Formulation of Return Time Prediction:** The paper presents the prediction of user return times as a key contribution, but the practical motivation for this task is not clearly articulated. The authors should elaborate on potential applications. For example, how could a company leverage this prediction? Possible use cases might include targeted promotions, managing computational resources, or churn prevention, but this needs to be specified to justify its inclusion as a core contribution.

    Furthermore, the evaluation of the return time prediction is missing some simple yet crucial baselines. Comparing against a model that predicts either the user's most recent time delay or the modal (most common) time delay would provide essential context for the proposed method's performance.

    Predicting the exact return time is also an ambitious and potentially overly difficult task. A more practical and perhaps more impactful formulation would be to frame it as a classification problem: predicting whether the user will return within a short, medium, or long time window (e.g., 1-5 days, 5-14 days, >14 days). This binned prediction is often more actionable for businesses and may be easier to model accurately.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation and Baselines:** The reported performance gains over baselines like SVD are difficult to interpret in terms of practical significance. Are the improvements modest, or are they substantial enough to be perceptible to an end-user? Providing some qualitative examples or user-centric metrics could help clarify the real-world impact.

    A critical baseline is missing from the recommendation task: a simple popularity-based recommender. In many real-world systems, recommending popular items—especially when popularity is recalculated frequently (e.g., daily)—is a very strong baseline. Comparing against such a model is essential to demonstrate the value of a personalized, time-aware approach.

**Conclusion**

In summary, the paper tackles two interesting problems: time-sensitive recommendations and user return time prediction. However, its current contributions are weakened by insufficient motivation for the problems, a lack of crucial baseline comparisons in the experiments, and missing data analysis to support its core premises. While the proposed technical approach appears sound, these omissions currently make the paper marginal. Addressing the points above would substantially strengthen the work.
