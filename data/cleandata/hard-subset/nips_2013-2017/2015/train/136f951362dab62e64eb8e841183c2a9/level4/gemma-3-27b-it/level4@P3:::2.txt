This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions. The core idea is to model user activity as a Hawkes process, where past interactions influence future ones, and to learn the intensity function using low-rank matrix factorization. This allows for both recommending relevant items at the right moment and predicting user return times. The authors present a convex formulation of the problem and develop an efficient optimization algorithm with a provable convergence rate, demonstrating scalability to large datasets.

This work builds upon a growing body of research recognizing the limitations of traditional collaborative filtering methods that ignore temporal dynamics. Recent approaches, such as those by Kapoor et al. [12, 11], have begun to address this by applying survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models. However, the authors rightly point out a key limitation of these methods: an inability to generalize to recommending new items. This paper’s strength lies in overcoming this limitation through the use of low-rank models, enabling recommendations beyond the observed user-item interactions.  Related work in tensor factorization [23, 3] also explores temporal dynamics, but the proposed Hawkes process approach offers a different perspective and, as demonstrated in the experiments, competitive performance.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation is a novel and promising approach. It effectively addresses the limitations of existing methods that struggle with recommending new items and capturing complex temporal dependencies.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The formulation as a convex optimization problem and the development of an efficient algorithm with a provable convergence rate (O(1/t)) are significant contributions. The scalability demonstrated in the experiments further strengthens this aspect.
* **Practical Significance:** The problems addressed – time-sensitive recommendation and predicting user return times – are directly relevant to numerous web-services and have substantial practical implications for personalization and marketing.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The inclusion of multiple baselines allows for a fair comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion regarding hyperparameter tuning. While the authors mention tuning ρ, λ, and β, more details about the selection process and sensitivity analysis (e.g., varying σ, the bandwidth of the triggering kernel) would be beneficial. The choice of latent vector dimensions for all methods is also not discussed.
* **Baseline Comparison:** While a range of baselines are included, a simple ensemble of STiC [12] and SVD would provide a strong and insightful comparison.  The authors' argument that SVD can cover the recommendation of new items alongside STiC provides a good motivation for this baseline.
* **Dataset Characterization:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough characterization of the real-world datasets. Providing statistics like the average number of recurring events per user/item pair and measures of temporal irregularity would give readers a better understanding of the data's properties and the challenges it presents.
* **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper contains several instances of awkward phrasing, typos, and grammatical errors that detract from clarity. While the technical content is sound, improvements to the writing quality would significantly enhance readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in recommendation systems.
* The proposed method demonstrates superior performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets compared to several strong baselines.
* The efficient optimization algorithm and convergence analysis are valuable contributions.
* The potential impact of this work on personalized services and marketing strategies is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a more detailed discussion on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis.
* The absence of a potentially strong baseline (ensemble of STiC and SVD).
* The limited characterization of the real-world datasets.
* The need for improved clarity and polish in the writing.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. While there are areas for improvement, particularly in the experimental details and presentation, the contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, especially regarding hyperparameter sensitivity, baseline comparison, and dataset characterization. The authors should also carefully proofread the manuscript for clarity and grammatical accuracy.
