This work introduces a novel method for generating time-sensitive recommendations derived from user activity. Its key novelty lies in explicitly modeling temporally dynamic user preferences. A secondary contribution is a model for predicting the next time a user will return to the service. The authors propose a custom, efficient optimization algorithm for model training. The experimental evaluation is conducted on a large-scale synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability, and on two moderately-sized real-world datasets to assess practical performance.

The paper's core premise rests on the significance of time-varying user preferences. The manuscript would be strengthened by a data-driven analysis of the experimental datasets to empirically establish the prevalence and magnitude of this phenomenon. Furthermore, an analysis into the nature of these preference shifts would be insightful. For instance, are they driven primarily by external factors like the emergence of new popular items, or do they follow other, more user-specific temporal patterns?

The motivation for predicting a user's next return time is not clearly articulated. The paper claims this as a contribution but lacks a discussion of its practical applications. How would this prediction be leveraged by a service provider, and what specific business objectives would it support (e.g., targeted promotions, resource management)?

The evaluation of the return time prediction model is missing several simple yet crucial baselines. For example, comparing against heuristics such as predicting the same delay as the user's most recent visit or using the user's modal inter-session time would provide a better context for the proposed model's performance. Additionally, predicting the exact return timestamp is an exceptionally difficult regression task. The authors might consider reframing this as a more tractable classification problem, such as predicting the time delay bin (e.g., 1-5 days, 5-14 days, 14-33 days, >33 days) for the user's next session. This simplification could yield more robust and practically useful results.

Regarding the experimental results, the practical significance of the performance improvements over baselines like SVD is unclear. The paper should provide more context to help the reader understand whether the reported gains are substantial or incremental, and whether they would translate to a perceivable improvement in the user experience. A critical baseline that is missing from the comparison is a non-personalized "most popular" recommender, particularly a temporally-aware version where popularity is recalculated frequently (e.g., daily). This is often a surprisingly strong baseline in many real-world systems.

Minor Comments:
1)  There is a typo in the last paragraph of page 3: "abel" should be "able".
2)  The first paragraph of Section 2 abruptly introduces concepts like retweets and social network link creation without references or a clear connection to the paper's main topic. This contextual shift is confusing and should be rephrased or better integrated.

In summary, this paper tackles two interesting problems but suffers from a lack of clear motivation for their practical relevance and an experimental evaluation that is missing key analyses and competitive baselines. While the proposed ideas have merit, the paper in its current form is borderline.
