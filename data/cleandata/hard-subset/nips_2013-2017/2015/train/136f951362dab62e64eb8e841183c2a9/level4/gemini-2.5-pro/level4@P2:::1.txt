### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendations by addressing two key problems: 1) recommending the most relevant item at a specific moment, and 2) predicting the next time a user will return to a service or item. The core contribution is a model that connects self-exciting temporal point processes with low-rank matrix factorization. Specifically, the sequence of a user's interactions with an item is modeled as a Hawkes process, which captures how past events excite future ones. To generalize this model across all user-item pairs, including unobserved ones, the authors impose a low-rank structure on the matrices representing the Hawkes process parameters (the base intensity and the self-excitation strength).

To learn the model parameters, the paper presents a convex optimization problem regularized by the nuclear norm. A key technical contribution is a new, scalable optimization algorithm that efficiently solves this problem by blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this algorithm achieves an O(1/t) convergence rate. The proposed model is evaluated on one large synthetic dataset and three real-world datasets, demonstrating its scalability and superior predictive performance on both recommendation and return-time prediction tasks compared to several existing methods.

### **Strengths**

*   **Originality:** The combination of low-rank models and multivariate Hawkes processes for recommendation is a novel and principled approach. It provides an elegant way to capture the recurrent, self-exciting nature of user activities while simultaneously leveraging collaborative filtering principles to generalize to sparse data. This connection between two previously disparate fields is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The convex formulation for parameter estimation is well-motivated, and the development of a custom hybrid optimization algorithm is a strong point. The accompanying convergence analysis (Theorem 2) adds significant theoretical weight to the work and may be of independent interest to the optimization community.
*   **Scalability and Efficiency:** The proposed optimization algorithm is designed for large-scale settings. The experiments on synthetic data convincingly demonstrate its efficiency and linear scalability with the size of the training data, which is a crucial feature for real-world recommender systems.

### **Weaknesses**

The main weaknesses of the paper lie in the motivation for the problem and the comprehensiveness of the experimental evaluation.

*   **Motivation and Significance:** While the paper presents plausible application scenarios, the practical significance of the two addressed problems could be more strongly motivated and empirically supported.
    *   The central premise is that user preferences are time-varying, but the paper does not include an analysis of the experimental datasets to demonstrate the prevalence or nature of these dynamics. A brief exploratory analysis showing how user-item interaction patterns change over time would substantially strengthen the motivation.
    *   The utility of predicting the precise return time of a user is not fully established. The ad-bidding example is mentioned, but a more detailed discussion of how a system would use this specific prediction would be beneficial. It is unclear why predicting the exact time is superior to predicting a time window, which may be an easier and more robust task.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments show positive results, the evaluation could be made more convincing by including stronger baselines and providing more context for the results.
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The evaluation is missing some simple but powerful baselines. For the return-time prediction task, performance should be compared against simple heuristics like predicting the **last observed inter-event time** or the **user's average inter-event time** for that item. For the time-sensitive recommendation task, a crucial baseline is a **dynamic popularity model** that recommends items with the highest consumption frequency over a recent time window (e.g., the last day or week). This is often a very difficult baseline to outperform in practice.
    *   **Interpretation of Results:** The practical significance of the reported performance gains is difficult to assess. For item recommendation, what is the practical impact of the rank improvement shown in Figure 3? Providing top-N precision/recall metrics would make the results more interpretable. For return-time prediction, reporting Mean Absolute Error (MAE) in hours or weeks is hard to interpret without knowing the typical scale and variance of the inter-event times in each dataset. Reporting a relative error metric or providing summary statistics of the ground-truth return times would add valuable context.

*   **Problem Formulation:** Predicting a continuous value for the return time is an ambitious and difficult regression problem. A potential weakness is that this formulation might be overly sensitive to noise and outliers. It is worth considering whether a simpler formulation might be more practical and robust. For example, framing the problem as a classification task—predicting whether a user will return within a short, medium, or long time window—could be more directly actionable for applications like marketing or churn prevention.

### **Detailed Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide a brief analysis of the real-world datasets to empirically support the claim that user preferences are significantly time-varying? This would help motivate the need for a dynamic model like the one proposed.
2.  For the return-time prediction task, please consider comparing your model against simpler baselines, such as predicting based on the last inter-event duration or the user's historical average inter-event duration.
3.  For the item recommendation task, how does your model's performance compare to a strong, non-personalized baseline like a dynamic popularity model (e.g., recommending the most-consumed items in the last 24 hours)?
4.  To help readers better interpret the MAE results for return-time prediction, could you provide summary statistics (e.g., mean, median, standard deviation) for the ground-truth inter-event durations in each real-world dataset?
5.  Have you considered framing the return-time prediction problem as a classification task into discrete time bins (e.g., predicting return in <1 day, 1-7 days, >7 days)? This formulation might be more robust and practical for certain applications. What are your thoughts on the trade-offs between this and your continuous prediction approach?
