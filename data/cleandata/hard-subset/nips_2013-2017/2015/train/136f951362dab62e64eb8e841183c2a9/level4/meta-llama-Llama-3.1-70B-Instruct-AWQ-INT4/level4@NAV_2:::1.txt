Paper proposes a novel approach for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities, addressing the problem of time-varying user preferences. The authors also tackle the prediction of the next returning time of a user. Learning is performed using an efficient optimization algorithm proposed by the authors. Experimental results are presented on one synthetic data set to demonstrate the efficiency of the learning process on large-scale data, as well as on two real data sets of modest size.

The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, highlighting the differences between their approach and existing methods. They also present a detailed description of their model, including the low-rank Hawkes processes and the triggering kernel parametrization. The optimization algorithm is well-explained, and the convergence analysis is rigorous.

The experimental section is comprehensive, with results on both synthetic and real data sets. The authors compare their approach with several baselines, including Poisson process, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization. The results show that their approach outperforms the baselines in terms of predictive performance.

However, there are a few areas that require further attention. Firstly, it would be beneficial to analyze the real data sets from the experiments to better understand the problem of time-varying user preferences. This could involve examining the patterns of user behavior and identifying the factors that contribute to the variation in preferences.

Secondly, the motivation for predicting the next returning time of a user could be more clearly explained. How would a web company use this prediction, and for what purpose? What are the potential benefits of accurately predicting the returning time of a user?

Additionally, some simple baselines for predicting the next returning time were not tested, such as the last time delay and the most occurred time delay. It might be useful to include these baselines in the comparison to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the approach.

Furthermore, predicting the actual returning time of the user might be too challenging. Instead, the authors could focus on a simpler task, such as predicting whether the user will have a short, medium, long, or very long delay before using the service again.

Finally, the experimental section could be improved by providing more context about the significance of the performance lift compared to SVD. Is the improvement groundbreaking, or is it modest? How would the user perceive the difference in recommendations? How do the recommendations compare to recommending popular items, which is often a hard baseline to beat?

Minor comments include a typo in the last paragraph on page 3, where "abel" should be replaced with "able." Additionally, the first paragraph of section 2 could be rephrased to avoid mentioning retweets and link creation in social networks without any references.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the approach is novel and interesting. However, addressing the above concerns could strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the approach.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities.
* The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work and a detailed description of their model.
* The optimization algorithm is well-explained, and the convergence analysis is rigorous.
* The experimental section is comprehensive, with results on both synthetic and real data sets.

Arguments con acceptance:
