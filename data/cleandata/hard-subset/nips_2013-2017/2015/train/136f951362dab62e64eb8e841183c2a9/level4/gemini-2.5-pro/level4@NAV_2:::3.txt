This paper addresses two critical and less-explored problems in time-sensitive recommendation: (1) recommending the right item at the right time, and (2) predicting a user's next return time to a service. The core contribution is a novel framework that models recurrent user activities by connecting low-rank matrix models with self-exciting Hawkes processes. This approach elegantly captures both a user's static, long-term preferences through a low-rank base intensity matrix and the dynamic, history-dependent nature of their actions through a low-rank self-excitation matrix. This work extends prior art in the area, such as survival analysis models, by creating a model that can generalize to recommend new items, which is a crucial aspect of recommendation. To learn the model parameters, the authors present a convex formulation and develop an efficient and scalable optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, proving its convergence properties.

**Quality:** The paper presents a high-quality, complete piece of work. The proposed approach is principled, and the claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and extensive experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets. The authors are careful in their evaluation and present a solid contribution. A couple of points could further improve the work:

*   There is a minor disconnect between the theoretical analysis of the penalty parameter ρ in Theorem 1 and its practical use. The theorem provides a condition on ρ for the equivalence between the original and alternative optimization problems. However, in the experiments, this threshold is not calculated, and ρ is instead tuned as a hyperparameter. While this is a pragmatic choice, it slightly reduces the practical impact of this theoretical result. A brief discussion on the model's sensitivity to ρ would be a valuable addition.
*   The experimental comparison is good, but could be strengthened by the inclusion of other strong time-aware recommendation baselines. For instance, a model like Time-SVD++ (Koren, KDD 2009), which incorporates temporal dynamics directly into matrix factorization, would serve as a very relevant competitor for the item recommendation task.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the technical details of the model and the optimization algorithm are presented logically and are easy to follow, and the experimental setup and results are described lucidly.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The central idea of modeling the parameters of a multivariate Hawkes process (the base intensity and self-excitation matrices) as low-rank is a novel concept in the recommendation space. This provides an effective mechanism for transferring learned temporal patterns across user-item pairs, which is a key challenge that previous point process models for recommendation have not addressed in this manner. The work is clearly differentiated from prior contributions.

**Significance:** The results are important and address a problem of high practical value. As services increasingly focus on user engagement over time, methods that can accurately model temporal dynamics for recommendation and return-time prediction are highly significant. The proposed model is demonstrated to be effective and scalable, making it potentially useful for practitioners. Furthermore, the novel optimization algorithm developed for this specific problem structure could be of independent interest to the machine learning community.
