This paper presents a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation, connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interaction data. The core idea is to model user behavior using a Hawkes process, leveraging low-rank approximations to enable scalability and generalization. A convex optimization formulation is proposed, alongside an efficient algorithm with an O(1/t) convergence rate, demonstrated to scale to large datasets. The results on both synthetic and real-world datasets show superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods. The authors also highlight the model's extensibility to incorporate additional contextual information.

The paper addresses an important problem in recommender systems – incorporating temporal dynamics and recurrent user activities – which are often overlooked in traditional approaches. The use of Hawkes processes is a principled way to model the self-exciting nature of user interactions, and the low-rank approximation is crucial for scalability. The theoretical analysis, including the convergence guarantee for the proposed optimization algorithm, adds to the rigor of the work.

Regarding quality, the technical approach appears sound, and the experimental results are compelling. However, a few points warrant further clarification. In the discussion surrounding Equation (8) and Theorem 1, it would be beneficial to explicitly state the variable over which the maximum is taken (specifically, ρ) and to address whether ρ* could potentially be unbounded. Furthermore, the practical relevance of Theorem 2 is diminished as the authors do not utilize the derived ρ* value in their experiments. While the convergence analysis is valuable, its connection to the experimental setup could be strengthened. It would be helpful to compare against stronger baselines, such as SVD++ [17], a highly successful algorithm in collaborative filtering and a strong contender for time-sensitive scenarios. Although the current baseline selection is reasonable, including SVD++ would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the contribution within the existing literature. The mathematical notation is generally clear, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.  The inclusion of ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of individual components (e.g., the low-rank approximation, the specific kernel function) would further enhance clarity.

The originality of this work lies in the novel combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation. While Hawkes processes have been applied in related domains, their application to this specific recommendation setting, along with the efficient optimization algorithm, represents a significant contribution. The authors adequately reference related work, including Kapoor et al. [12, 11], and clearly differentiate their approach.

The significance of this research is substantial. Time-sensitive recommendation has the potential to significantly improve user engagement in various web services. The demonstrated improvements in predictive performance, combined with the scalability of the proposed algorithm, suggest that this work could have a real-world impact. The ability to incorporate additional contextual information further broadens the applicability of the model.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
*   Compelling experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Scalable algorithm suitable for large-scale applications.
*   Potential for significant impact on user engagement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarification needed regarding the practical use of Theorem 2 and the potential unboundedness of ρ*.
*   Comparison to a stronger baseline (e.g., SVD++) would strengthen the evaluation.
*   Ablation studies could provide further insights into the contribution of individual components.
