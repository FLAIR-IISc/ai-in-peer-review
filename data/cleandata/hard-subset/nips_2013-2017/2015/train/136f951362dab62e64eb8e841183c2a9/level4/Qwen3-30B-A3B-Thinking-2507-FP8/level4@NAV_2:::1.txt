

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation that connects self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption. The authors address two key problems: recommending the most desirable item at the right moment and predicting the next returning time of users to services. The work builds upon previous research in temporal point processes and recommendation systems, particularly extending the work of Kapoor et al. [11, 12] by incorporating low-rank structures to handle large-scale user-item pairs. While related work has explored user return time prediction using survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models, the current paper's key innovation is its ability to generalize to recommend new items, which is a significant limitation of prior approaches.

Strengths:
- The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-motivated convex optimization formulation that addresses both time-sensitive recommendation and returning time prediction.
- The proposed algorithm efficiently combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving the optimal O(1/t) convergence rate and scaling to millions of user-item pairs.
- The experimental evaluation demonstrates strong performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), with meaningful comparisons to relevant baselines including Poisson process, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods.
- The paper provides a clear theoretical analysis of the optimization algorithm with convergence guarantees, which is a significant contribution to the field.

Weaknesses:
- The motivation for predicting exact returning times could be strengthened by providing more concrete examples of how web companies would operationalize these predictions (beyond the general statement about ad bidding). A more specific discussion of practical applications would better demonstrate the significance of the work.
- The paper does not sufficiently analyze why time-varying user preferences are widespread in the datasets used. It would be valuable to include analysis showing patterns of temporal variation in the real data beyond just the experimental results.
- Several simple baselines for returning time prediction were not tested, such as using the last time delay or the most frequently occurring time delay. The authors should compare against these to establish the baseline performance.
- The paper would benefit from addressing whether predicting exact returning times is necessary or if a simpler categorical prediction (e.g., short/medium/long delay) would be more practical and effective for real-world applications.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a well-justified convex optimization approach. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are comprehensive. The authors carefully consider both strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, though some sections (such as Section 2) could be improved by removing the confusing reference to "retweets and link creation" without proper context. The algorithm descriptions are clear, but a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup would help readers better evaluate the results.

Originality: The connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendations represents a novel contribution. The paper clearly differentiates from previous work, particularly the limitations of survival analysis approaches in the related work section.

Significance: The work has potential significance for recommendation systems, particularly in contexts where timing of recommendations matters (e.g., music streaming, e-commerce). However, the significance could be strengthened by providing more concrete examples of how the approach would improve real-world systems compared to existing methods.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-motivated convex optimization formulation, demonstrates scalability to large datasets, and provides rigorous theoretical analysis. The problem addressed is relevant to modern recommendation systems where timing of suggestions matters.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper would benefit from stronger motivation for predicting exact returning times, more detailed analysis of temporal patterns in the real data, and comparison with simpler baselines for returning time prediction. The significance claims could be better supported with concrete examples of practical impact.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation systems. With some revisions to strengthen the motivation, analysis, and baseline comparisons, this paper would make a valuable addition to the conference proceedings. The current version is acceptable but not exceptional, and would benefit from addressing the concerns raised above.
