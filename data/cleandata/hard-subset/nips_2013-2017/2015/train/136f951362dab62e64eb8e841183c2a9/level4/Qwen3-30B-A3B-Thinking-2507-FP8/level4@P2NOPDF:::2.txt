

Summary: This paper introduces a point process-based collaborative filtering model that captures temporal and recurrent user-item interactions by modeling event density as the sum of a constant intensity and a self-exciting intensity dependent on historical events. The approach employs distinct latent user and item vectors to compute two intensity components, with a derived optimization algorithm for parameter estimation. Empirical results demonstrate the method's superiority over standard baselines. However, the experimental evaluation requires significant strengthening.  

The authors claim that STiC [12] and SVD cannot recommend new items, yet omit a critical baseline: a simple ensemble of STiC and SVD (which would inherently handle new items via SVD). Comparing against this ensemble would robustly validate their claim about handling novel items. Additionally, the choice of the bandwidth parameter σ in the γ function for the self-exciting process is unexplained. Sensitivity analysis of results to σ variations (e.g., across a range of σ values) is essential to assess model robustness. Crucially, hyperparameter selection for both baselines and the proposed method lacks detail—particularly the latent vector dimension, which significantly impacts performance.  

The paper also omits key dataset characteristics relevant to its temporal focus. For both real-world datasets, the authors should report: (1) statistics on recurrence frequency (e.g., mean number of interactions per user-item pair), (2) metrics quantifying temporal irregularity (e.g., variance in inter-event times, temporal sparsity), and (3) overall event density patterns. Such details would contextualize the model’s design and justify its suitability for the data.  

Quality: The technical approach is sound but the evaluation is incomplete and lacks depth. The absence of the suggested ensemble baseline and insufficient hyperparameter justification weaken the empirical claims.  

Clarity: The writing contains grammatical errors and awkward phrasing (e.g., "empirical studies have been done to demonstrate the advantage of the proposed methods to other commonly used alternatives"). Key methodological descriptions are repetitive and could be more concise. The presentation requires careful editing to improve precision and flow.  

Originality: The integration of point processes with latent factor models for temporal collaborative filtering is novel and well-motivated. However, the core idea—combining self-exciting processes with latent factors—is not entirely unprecedented (e.g., related to Hawkes processes in CF literature). The contribution lies in the specific application to collaborative filtering with the proposed optimization.  

Significance: The problem of modeling temporal user-item dynamics is highly relevant to real-world recommendation systems. The method’s practical utility is promising, particularly for scenarios where user-item interactions exhibit strong temporal dependencies and recurrence. However, the significance is partially offset by the underdeveloped experimental validation.  

Overall, the paper presents an interesting methodology with clear practical potential. To strengthen it, the authors must:  
1) Add the STiC+SVD ensemble as a baseline,  
2) Provide a sensitivity analysis for σ and justify all hyperparameters (including latent dimensions),  
3) Report detailed temporal dataset statistics,  
4) Correct grammatical errors and streamline the presentation.  
Without these revisions, the empirical evidence supporting the method’s advantages remains incomplete, and the paper’s contribution is not fully substantiated. The current quality is modest due to these gaps, though the core idea holds promise.
