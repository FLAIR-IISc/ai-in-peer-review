This paper introduces a novel collaborative filtering model leveraging temporal point processes to capture recurrent user-item interactions. The authors propose a framework connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models, aiming to predict both the most relevant item to recommend at a given moment and the user’s next returning time. The model estimates parameters via convex optimization, with a developed algorithm achieving O(1/t) convergence and scalability to large datasets. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

This work builds upon existing research in recommendation systems by explicitly addressing temporal dynamics, a component often overlooked in traditional approaches. While collaborative filtering has historically focused on static preferences (e.g., ratings), this paper acknowledges the importance of understanding evolving user behavior and the timing of interactions. Recent work, such as that by Kapoor et al. [12, 11], has begun to explore temporal aspects, but primarily through survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models. The key distinction highlighted by the authors is the ability of their model to generalize recommendations to new items, unlike the approaches in [12, 11]. Furthermore, the connection between point processes and low-rank models represents a novel combination of techniques. Related work in tensor factorization [23, 13, 25] provides a foundation for handling multi-dimensional data, but doesn’t explicitly model the point process nature of user interactions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The integration of self-exciting point processes and low-rank models offers a fresh perspective on temporal recommendation.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The convex formulation and convergence guarantees (O(1/t)) provide a strong theoretical basis for the method.
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm’s demonstrated scalability to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events is a significant practical advantage.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The results on both synthetic and real datasets showcase the model's predictive power.
*   **Generality:** The formulation allows for the incorporation of additional contextual information (user profiles, text, location).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Baseline:** The comparison lacks a simple, yet potentially strong, baseline: an ensemble of STiC [12] and SVD. Given the authors' argument regarding STiC’s limitation in recommending new items, combining it with SVD could provide a more robust benchmark.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks detail regarding the selection of key hyperparameters, such as the latent vector dimensions and the bandwidth parameter σ for the triggering kernel. A sensitivity analysis varying σ would be valuable.
*   **Dataset Characterization:** More comprehensive descriptions of the real-world datasets are needed. Statistics like the number of recurring events per user/item pair and measures of temporal irregularity would provide important context.
*   **Presentation:** The paper contains instances of awkward phrasing and could benefit from more careful editing for clarity and grammatical correctness.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in recommendation systems.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*   The scalability and generality of the model are significant strengths.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with the inclusion of a more comprehensive baseline and a sensitivity analysis.
*   The clarity of the presentation could be improved through more careful editing.
*   The lack of detail regarding hyperparameter selection raises concerns about reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of recommendation systems. The proposed model offers a novel and effective way to capture temporal dynamics in user-item interactions. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding experimental baselines, hyperparameter sensitivity, and presentation clarity. Addressing these points will significantly enhance the impact and reproducibility of this work.
