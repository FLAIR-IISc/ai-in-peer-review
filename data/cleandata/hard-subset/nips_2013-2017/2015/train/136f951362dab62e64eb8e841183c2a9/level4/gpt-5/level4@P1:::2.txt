— Summary —
This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models recurrent user–item interactions with self-exciting temporal point processes (Hawkes processes) coupled with low-rank structure across users and items. For each user–item pair, the event intensity is the sum of a baseline term and a history-dependent self-exciting term triggered by past events. The model ties these intensities across all pairs through two low-rank nonnegative matrices, capturing shared user/item structure and enabling generalization. The authors formulate parameter estimation as a convex problem with nuclear-norm regularization and nonnegativity constraints, and develop an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) and conditional gradient (for nuclear norms), achieving an O(1/t) convergence rate. Two tasks are addressed: (1) time-sensitive item recommendation by ranking items at time t using λu,i(t), and (2) returning-time prediction via the conditional density f(t|history), approximated by Ogata’s thinning. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, and MIMIC II) show strong accuracy and scalability compared to Poisson, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization baselines. The authors argue their approach can be extended to incorporate contextual features (profiles, text, location) via the intensity function.

Relation to prior work: The paper contrasts with survival-analysis-based return-time models (Kapoor et al.) that assume i.i.d. inter-event gaps and are constrained to previously seen items, and with tensor factorization methods that discretize time and optimize Poisson or squared-loss objectives. It also relates to prior Hawkes-process work and low-rank modeling, but contributes a convex formulation with nonnegativity, nuclear norms, and a practical algorithm with convergence guarantees.

— Strengths —
- Unified modeling of two time-sensitive tasks (ranking at the right moment and return-time prediction) within a principled Hawkes-process framework.
- Convex estimation with nuclear-norm regularization and nonnegativity, plus a clean equivalence via a penalty formulation; clear theoretical convergence analysis with optimal rates for the constituent steps.
- Scalability to millions of pairs and hundreds of millions of events, with empirical evidence of linear scaling and fast convergence on synthetic data.
- Competitive to superior performance across diverse domains (music, e-commerce, clinical visits).
- Clear path to incorporating additional context features into the intensity.

— Weaknesses and questions —
- Baselines: A sensible additional comparison is a simple ensemble of STiC and SVD. The paper argues that STiC-like approaches cannot recommend unseen items, while SVD can handle new items via factorization; an ensemble could cover both aspects and would be a useful reference against the proposed method.
- Triggering kernel bandwidth: For the exponential γ(t, tj) = exp(−(t − tj)/σ), how is σ chosen? Please report the selection procedure (e.g., validation grid), and provide sensitivity analyses that vary σ to assess robustness of both ranking and return-time predictions.
- Hyperparameter details: The paper lacks details on how key hyperparameters are selected for both the proposed method and baselines: nuclear-norm weights (λ, β), penalty ρ, latent dimension for matrix/tensor factorizations, number of samples m used for Ogata’s thinning, stopping criteria, and step-size choices. Please provide selection ranges, validation protocols, and final values per dataset.
- Dataset characterization: Because temporal structure is central, it would help to include simple descriptive statistics of each real dataset, such as:
  - Distribution of events per user–item pair and fraction of pairs with multiple events.
  - Inter-event time irregularity (e.g., coefficient of variation) and evidence of seasonality/periodicity.
  - Train/test split details by dataset (time units, horizon, number of held-out sequences).
These would contextualize the difficulty and clarify when Hawkes effects are likely to be beneficial.
- New-item generalization: The paper claims to generalize beyond previously observed items via low-rank structure. Please clarify how truly new items with no historical events are handled in practice in the experiments (e.g., cold-start via shared latent factors vs. content features), and whether any item/user had zero history at training time.
- Return-time estimation: Since expectations under Hawkes do not have closed forms, the paper uses sampling. Please specify the number of samples, computational overhead, and whether variance reduction or calibration checks (e.g., time-change theorem diagnostics) were applied at test time. This would help assess runtime and reliability for the return-time task.

— Quality —
Technically sound overall, with a solid convex formulation and a practically motivated algorithm that achieves provable rates. The empirical section is promising but would be stronger with the additional baseline (STiC+SVD), sensitivity analyses, and fuller hyperparameter and dataset details.

— Clarity —
The paper is generally understandable, but the presentation can be improved. There are noticeable typos and grammatical issues; careful proofreading would help. Please also improve exposition of:
- Notation for matrices/vectors and kernel integrals (consolidate subscripts/superscripts).
- Algorithmic steps (expand pseudo-code, define inputs/outputs, and report complexity per iteration).
- Experimental setup (explicit validation protocol, metrics definition, and error bars where applicable).

— Originality —
The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank nonnegative matrices in a convex framework, together with a blended proximal/conditional gradient algorithm and convergence analysis, is interesting and, to my knowledge, novel in this specific form.

— Significance —
The problem is important and the proposed approach is practically valuable. The method’s scalability and extensibility make it relevant to real systems. Strengthening the experimental section as suggested would further substantiate its impact.

— Arguments for acceptance —
- Principled, unified model for time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction.
- Convex formulation with an efficient algorithm and theoretical guarantees.
- Demonstrated scalability and strong empirical performance.

— Arguments against acceptance —
- Missing a key ensemble baseline and sensitivity analyses.
- Insufficient detail on hyperparameters and dataset temporal characteristics.
- Presentation and language issues that impede clarity.

Overall, this is a solid contribution; with improved experimental rigor and presentation, it could be a strong paper.
