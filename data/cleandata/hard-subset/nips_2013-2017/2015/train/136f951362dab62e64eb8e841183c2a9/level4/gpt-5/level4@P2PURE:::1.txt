The paper proposes a new method for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activity. It differs from existing approaches by explicitly modeling time-varying user preferences and also by predicting a user’s next return time. Learning is performed via an efficient optimization algorithm proposed by the authors. The experiments include one synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability and two real-world datasets of modest size.

First, how prevalent are time-varying user preferences in practice? It would help to analyze the real datasets used in the experiments to substantiate this claim. If preferences do vary, is the effect primarily driven by the release of new popular songs, or are there other recurring patterns?

Second, what is the concrete motivation for predicting a user’s next return time? Please justify this before presenting it as a contribution. How would a web company use such predictions, and for what specific purposes?

I believe some simple baselines for return-time prediction were omitted:
- The last observed inter-arrival time
- The most frequent (modal) inter-arrival time
Also, predicting the exact return time may be unnecessarily difficult. A simpler and likely more reliable alternative would be to predict delay categories, e.g., short (1–5 days), medium (5–14 days), long (14–33 days), or very long (>33 days).

Experimental section: I found it hard to judge how substantial the improvement over SVD is. Is it an order-of-magnitude gain or a modest lift? Would users notice the difference? How does your method compare to a strong popularity baseline (e.g., recommending globally popular items refreshed daily), which is often difficult to beat?

Small comments:
1) Typo in the last paragraph of page 3: “and is abel to capture” → “and is able to capture.”
2) In the first paragraph of Section 2, retweets and link creation in social networks are mentioned without references. The abrupt context shift is confusing; please rephrase and provide citations.

Overall, the paper tackles two problems—time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction—without adequately motivating their practical relevance and potential impact. Several baselines and analyses are missing (as noted above). Other than that, this is an OK/marginal paper.
