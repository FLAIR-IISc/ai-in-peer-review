

This paper proposes a novel framework that combines self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions for time-sensitive recommendations. The authors address two key challenges: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and predicting the next user return time. The approach models each user-item pair using a Hawkes process with a low-rank structure, where the intensity function combines a constant baseline (capturing long-term preferences) with a self-exciting component (modeling short-term dependencies). The authors derive a convex optimization formulation and develop an efficient algorithm with O(1/t) convergence rate, demonstrating scalability to large datasets with millions of user-item pairs.

The work builds on recent advances in point processes for recommendation systems (e.g., Kapoor et al. [12, 11]) but addresses a significant limitation of existing approaches: their inability to recommend new items beyond those already observed. While Kapoor et al. focus on predicting return times for observed items, the proposed model can generalize to recommend new items by learning a low-rank representation of user-item interactions. The paper also relates to collaborative filtering approaches like SVD and tensor factorization [2, 3, 25, 13, 21], but distinguishes itself by explicitly modeling temporal dynamics.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, with a well-motivated convex optimization formulation and a theoretically grounded algorithm with convergence guarantees. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple baselines on both synthetic and real datasets. However, the experimental section could be strengthened by including more details about hyperparameter selection. The paper does not explain how the bandwidth parameter σ for the triggering kernel is chosen or how sensitive results are to this parameter. Additionally, the latent dimension for the low-rank factorization is not clearly justified, and the authors should provide more details on how these hyperparameters were selected and their impact on performance.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-structured and readable, but contains several grammatical errors and awkward phrasings that detract from the clarity. For example, the sentence "the corresponding optimization algorithm is derived for parameter estimation and empirical studies have been done" is grammatically incorrect. The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading to correct these issues. Additionally, the presentation of the experimental results could be improved by providing more detailed statistics about the real-world datasets (e.g., number of recurring events per user-item pair, distribution of inter-event times) to better contextualize the temporal patterns being modeled.

Originality: The combination of point processes with low-rank matrix factorization for time-sensitive recommendations is novel. While point processes have been used in recommendation contexts [12, 11, 19], and low-rank models are common in collaborative filtering [2, 3, 25], the authors' specific integration of these approaches to address both item recommendation and return time prediction represents a meaningful contribution. The work extends the literature by providing a convex formulation and an efficient optimization algorithm for this problem.

Significance: The problem addressed has clear practical importance for modern web services, where understanding user temporal patterns can improve engagement and marketing strategies. The ability to recommend items at the right moment and predict user return times has direct applications in domains like music streaming, e-commerce, and healthcare. The empirical results demonstrate superior performance over baselines, particularly on the challenging task of predicting return times for entirely held-out sequences. The paper's framework is also flexible enough to incorporate additional contextual information (e.g., user profiles, spatial features), enhancing its potential applicability.

Strengths:
- Novel integration of point processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendations
- Theoretical guarantees for the optimization algorithm with O(1/t) convergence rate
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets
- Practical significance with clear applications in multiple domains

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection, particularly for the triggering kernel bandwidth
- Missing details about dataset characteristics (e.g., recurrence patterns, temporal irregularity)
- Grammar and clarity issues that could be addressed with better proofreading
- Limited discussion of how the proposed method compares to a simple ensemble of STiC and SVD (which would cover both observed and new items)

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation systems. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the empirical results are compelling. With minor improvements to the experimental details and writing quality, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The authors should address the hyperparameter selection details and provide more context about the datasets to strengthen the paper.
