This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities, addressing the problem of time-varying user preferences. The authors also tackle the prediction of the next returning time of a user. The learning is performed using an efficient optimization algorithm, which is a notable contribution.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere by combining self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption pairs. This approach is distinct from existing methods, which often focus on predicting users' one-time preferences.

The authors' rationale for addressing time-varying user preferences is well-motivated, as they highlight the importance of delivering personalized user experiences in modern web services. However, it would be beneficial to analyze the real data sets from the experiments to further support this claim. For instance, the authors could investigate whether the time-varying user preferences are primarily driven by new popular items or other patterns.

The motivation for predicting the next returning time of a user is not entirely clear. The authors could provide more context on how web companies could utilize this prediction and what benefits it would bring. For example, predicting the returning time could help companies optimize their marketing strategies or improve customer retention.

The experimental section demonstrates the efficiency of the proposed algorithm on large-scale data and its superior predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art competitors on both synthetic and real data sets. However, it is challenging to gauge the significance of the performance lift compared to the SVD baseline. The authors could provide more insight into whether the improvements are substantial enough to make a noticeable difference to the user.

Some potential baselines for predicting the next returning time, such as the last time delay or the most occurred time delay, are not evaluated in the paper. Additionally, predicting the actual returning time of the user might be too challenging, and the authors could consider focusing on a simpler task, such as predicting the delay before the user uses the service again (e.g., short, medium, long, or very long delay).

The paper is generally well-written, but there are a few minor issues. For instance, there is a typo in the last paragraph on page 3 ("and is abel to capture" should be "and is able to capture"). In the first paragraph of Section 2, the mention of retweets and link creation in social networks seems out of context and could be rephrased for clarity.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendations and returning time prediction.
* The authors develop an efficient optimization algorithm for learning the model.
* The experimental section demonstrates the algorithm's efficiency and superior predictive performance.

Weaknesses:

* The motivation for predicting the next returning time of a user is not entirely clear.
* Some potential baselines for predicting the next returning time are not evaluated.
* The significance of the performance lift compared to the SVD baseline is not well-explained.
* The paper could benefit from more analysis of the real data sets to support the claim of time-varying user preferences.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and efficient framework for time-sensitive recommendations and returning time prediction.
* The experimental section demonstrates the algorithm's efficiency and superior predictive performance.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The motivation for predicting the next returning time of a user is not entirely clear.
* Some potential baselines for predicting the next returning time are not evaluated.
* The significance of the performance lift compared to the SVD baseline is not well-explained.

Overall, the paper is well-written and proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendations and returning time prediction. However, it could benefit from more analysis and evaluation of potential baselines to strengthen its contributions.
