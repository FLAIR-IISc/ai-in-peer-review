

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption data. The authors address two key problems: recommending the most relevant item at the optimal moment, and predicting the next user return time to a service. Their approach extends recent work on temporal modeling (e.g., Kapoor et al. [11, 12]), which focuses on return time prediction but cannot generalize to recommend new items. The paper develops a convex optimization formulation with a novel algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence rate and scaling to large datasets with millions of user-item pairs.

Quality: The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-motivated theoretical foundations. The convex formulation (Equation 4) and convergence analysis (Theorem 2) are rigorous, though I have two concerns. First, Theorem 1 establishes that the approximate formulation (Equation 5) yields the same optimum as the original problem when ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optimal parameters (Λ*₀, A*), making its practical implementation somewhat ad hoc. Second, the authors do not directly use Theorem 2 in their experiments to guide ρ selection, which weakens the connection between the theoretical contribution and empirical results. While the optimization algorithm is well-described and the scalability claims are supported by experiments, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how ρ is selected in practice.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The technical concepts are clearly explained, with logical progression from background to model formulation, optimization, and experiments. The figures effectively illustrate key results, and the algorithm descriptions are precise and implementable. The writing style is professional and accessible to readers familiar with machine learning and point processes.

Originality: The paper makes a significant original contribution by establishing a novel connection between low-rank matrix models and self-exciting point processes for time-sensitive recommendation. While previous work has considered temporal aspects of user behavior (e.g., [11, 12, 16, 23]), the authors present a unified framework that simultaneously addresses both item recommendation and return time prediction. This is particularly novel because most existing approaches focus on one problem or the other, and the authors demonstrate how their framework can generalize to incorporate additional contextual information (e.g., user profiles, spatial features).

Significance: Time-sensitive recommendation addresses an important practical problem with applications in personalized marketing, health monitoring, and user engagement. The proposed framework demonstrates superior predictive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), suggesting potential for real-world impact. The ability to scale to large datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events makes this approach particularly promising for industrial applications. The work also contributes to the broader literature on point processes by introducing a novel optimization approach for low-rank Hawkes processes.

The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive baseline comparison. While the authors evaluate against Poisson processes, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods, they do not include SVD++ [17], which is one of the most successful approaches for time-sensitive recommendation (a winner of the Netflix challenge). Adding SVD++ as a baseline would provide a more complete evaluation of the proposed method's relative performance against state-of-the-art techniques.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The theoretical foundation is sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the proposed framework is both novel and practically applicable. The minor concerns regarding the theoretical analysis and baseline comparison do not substantially undermine the paper's technical contributions.

Strengths:
- Novel connection between low-rank models and self-exciting point processes
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees
- Effective algorithm that scales to large datasets
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real data
- Clear writing and well-organized presentation

Weaknesses:
- Theoretical threshold ρ* depends on unknown optimal parameters
- Theorem 2 is not directly used in experiments
- Baseline comparison could be strengthened with SVD++ and other recent methods

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of recommender systems and temporal modeling, addressing an important problem with a technically sound and practically applicable solution. I recommend acceptance.
