This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation, connecting self-exciting point processes (Hawkes processes) with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions. The core idea is to model user activity as a Hawkes process and leverage low-rank factorization to scale to large datasets. The authors also develop an efficient optimization algorithm with a provable convergence rate and demonstrate strong performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper addresses an important problem – moving beyond static preference prediction to account for the temporal dynamics of user behavior. While traditional recommender systems often focus on predicting overall preferences, this work tackles the related, but less explored, questions of *when* to recommend an item and *when* a user will next interact with the service. This is particularly relevant in modern web services where context and timing are crucial. The proposed approach builds upon existing work in temporal point processes and matrix factorization, offering a potentially powerful combination. This work relates to prior NIPS publications on recommender systems (e.g., [25, 13, 21]) by extending them to explicitly model temporal dependencies. Recent NIPS work by Kapoor et al. [12, 11] is most closely related, but this paper distinguishes itself by offering a more general approach capable of recommending *new* items, unlike the survival analysis techniques used in that work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation appears to be novel. The authors effectively bridge two distinct areas of research.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, including a convex formulation of the problem and a convergence analysis for the proposed optimization algorithm.
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm is designed for scalability, demonstrated by its performance on datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets show that the proposed model outperforms several state-of-the-art baselines.
*   **Generality:** The authors point out the potential for incorporating additional contextual information (user profiles, textual features, etc.), making the framework quite flexible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Clarification:** The discussion surrounding Equation (8) and Theorem 2 could be clarified. Specifically, specifying over which parameter (ρ) the maximum is taken and addressing the practical relevance of the theorem given its non-use in parameter selection during experimentation would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** While the chosen baselines are reasonable, including SVD++ [17], a strong performer in collaborative filtering, would provide a more comprehensive comparison and further highlight the benefits of the proposed approach.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper could benefit from a discussion of the sensitivity of the model's performance to the choice of key parameters, such as the rank of the low-rank matrices and the bandwidth of the triggering kernel.
*   **Implementation Details:** Providing more implementation details (e.g., specific hardware used, tuning strategies) would improve reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging and important problem.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The algorithm is scalable and offers the potential for real-world application.
*   The writing is clear and concise, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis could be further clarified.
*   The baseline comparison could be more comprehensive.
*   The lack of discussion regarding parameter sensitivity and implementation details might hinder reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of recommender systems. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong theoretical and empirical results. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above regarding theoretical clarification, baseline comparison, and discussion of parameter sensitivity/implementation details.
