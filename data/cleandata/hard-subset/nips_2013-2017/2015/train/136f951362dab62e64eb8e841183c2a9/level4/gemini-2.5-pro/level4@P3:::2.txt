This paper proposes a point process-based collaborative filtering model to capture the temporal dynamics and recurrent nature of user-item interactions. Specifically, for each user-item pair, the event stream is modeled as a self-exciting Hawkes process. The intensity function for an event consists of two components: a constant baseline intensity, representing a user's long-term, static preference for an item, and a self-exciting term, which captures the short-term, history-dependent influence of past interactions.

To generalize across the sparse user-item matrix and enable recommendations for unobserved pairs, the authors assume that the matrices representing the baseline intensities (Λ0) and the self-excitation magnitudes (A) are low-rank. This allows knowledge transfer by learning latent factor representations for users and items for both preference components. The authors frame parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization to enforce the low-rank structure. A novel and efficient optimization algorithm is developed that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, which is shown to be scalable and has a convergence rate of O(1/t). The model is evaluated on two key tasks: time-sensitive item recommendation (ranking items at a specific moment) and returning-time prediction. Empirical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the model's superiority over several baselines.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Problem Formulation:** The primary strength of this work lies in its novel and elegant synthesis of self-exciting point processes (specifically, Hawkes processes) and low-rank models for collaborative filtering. It addresses two significant and practical problems in recommender systems: recommending the right item at the right time and predicting user return times, which are often overlooked by traditional rating-prediction models.

2.  **Technical Contribution:** The development of an efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal and conditional gradient methods to handle the non-negative and nuclear norm constraints is a valuable technical contribution. The authors provide a theoretical convergence analysis, and the algorithm itself may be of independent interest for other problems involving non-negative matrix rank minimization.

3.  **Modeling Power:** The Hawkes process formulation is well-suited for capturing the "bursty" or self-reinforcing nature of user activity, where one interaction with an item makes another interaction more likely in the near future. The model gracefully balances a user's static, long-term interests with dynamic, short-term influences.

4.  **Scalability:** The paper demonstrates that the proposed algorithm scales linearly with the size of the training set, making it applicable to the large-scale datasets typical in recommender systems.

### Weaknesses

The main weaknesses of the paper lie in the experimental evaluation and presentation, which could be improved to make the results more convincing and reproducible.

1.  **Missing Baseline Comparison:** The experimental comparison could be strengthened. A key claim is that the model can recommend *new* items, which is a stated limitation of STiC [11]. However, a simple but strong baseline would be an ensemble of a temporal model like STiC and a standard collaborative filtering model like SVD. Such an ensemble could handle both returning-time prediction for known items (via STiC) and recommendation for new ones (via SVD), providing a more direct and challenging comparison to the proposed unified model. It would be valuable to see how the proposed method performs against such a practical hybrid approach.

2.  **Lack of Details on Hyperparameters:** The paper lacks sufficient detail on hyperparameter selection, which is critical for reproducibility. For instance, the bandwidth parameter `σ` for the exponential triggering kernel is crucial for modeling the rate of temporal decay of influence. It is unclear how this was set (e.g., fixed, cross-validated), and a sensitivity analysis would be beneficial to understand its impact on performance. Similarly, details on how the latent rank/dimension was chosen for the proposed method and the factorization-based baselines are missing.

3.  **Insufficient Dataset Characterization:** Given the paper's focus on temporal dynamics, the description of the real-world datasets could be enhanced. Providing statistics such as the distribution of the number of recurring events per user-item pair, the average and variance of inter-event times, or other measures of temporal regularity would help readers better understand the nature of the challenges in the data and contextualize the model's performance.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's presentation could be improved. There are several grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrases throughout the manuscript that occasionally hinder readability. A thorough proofreading would significantly strengthen the paper.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Section 3.3 (Triggering Kernel):** The authors stick with the exponential kernel for simplicity. It would be interesting to discuss the potential benefits or challenges of using more complex kernels (e.g., power-law) and whether the choice of kernel is dataset-dependent.
*   **Section 5.2 (Real data):** In the time-change theorem analysis (Figure 3), the results convincingly show the Hawkes model is a better fit. Could the authors comment on the characteristics of the datasets that might explain the different degrees of fit (e.g., why does the model fit `last.fm` so much better than the Poisson model, compared to `MIMIC II`)? This could provide deeper insights into when such a model is most needed.

### Summary of Evaluation

*   **Quality:** The technical quality of the work is solid, particularly the model formulation and the optimization algorithm. The claims are generally well-supported, though the experimental validation could be more rigorous, as noted above.
*   **Clarity:** The core ideas are conveyed, but the paper would benefit from significant proofreading to improve language, grammar, and overall polish. Key experimental details are missing, which affects clarity for reproducibility.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank collaborative filtering to solve time-sensitive recommendation is a novel and clever approach.
*   **Significance:** The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is of high practical and research significance. The proposed framework is promising and likely to inspire future work in modeling dynamic user behavior.

**Recommendation:**
This paper introduces a novel and valuable contribution to the field of recommender systems. The strengths in its formulation and originality are significant. While the experimental section and overall presentation have room for improvement, the core ideas are strong and well-executed. The weaknesses are largely addressable. I lean towards acceptance.
