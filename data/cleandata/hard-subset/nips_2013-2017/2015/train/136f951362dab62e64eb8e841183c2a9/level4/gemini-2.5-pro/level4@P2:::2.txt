### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling recurrent user activities as a low-rank Hawkes process. The work addresses two key questions: (1) how to recommend the most relevant item at a specific moment in time, and (2) how to predict when a user will next return to a service or item.

The core idea is to model the sequence of consumption events for each user-item pair using a self-exciting Hawkes process. The intensity function for each pair is composed of a constant baseline intensity, capturing long-term preference, and a history-dependent, self-exciting term that models the influence of past events. To generalize across all users and items and handle data sparsity, the paper assumes that the matrices representing the baseline intensities (Λ0) and the self-exciting coefficients (A) are low-rank. This elegantly connects the temporal dynamics of point processes with the collaborative filtering capabilities of latent factor models.

The authors formulate the parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization to enforce the low-rank structure. To solve this efficiently, they propose a novel algorithm that combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, providing theoretical convergence guarantees. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), where it outperforms several baseline methods on item recommendation and return-time prediction tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The primary strength of this work is its novel synthesis of two powerful modeling paradigms: self-exciting point processes and low-rank latent factor models. This provides a principled and unified framework for capturing both long-term static preferences and short-term temporal dynamics in user behavior, a significant step beyond traditional collaborative filtering.

2.  **Addresses Important Practical Problems:** The paper tackles two commercially and clinically relevant problems that are often overlooked by standard recommendation models: time-sensitive item ranking and user return-time prediction. These capabilities have direct applications in areas like targeted advertising, user engagement optimization, and proactive healthcare.

3.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The paper presents a solid technical contribution. The formulation of the learning problem as a convex optimization is sound, and the development of a scalable custom optimization algorithm with a formal convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is a valuable contribution in itself. The algorithm is particularly noteworthy for efficiently handling both the non-negativity constraints and the nuclear norm regularization.

4.  **Convincing Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed model consistently outperforms a range of relevant baselines, including static factorization models (SVD), tensor factorization, and a method based on hidden semi-Markov models (STiC). The model's ability to better explain the underlying event generation process, as shown in the quantile-quantile plots in Figure 3, is particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is strong overall, but the experimental evaluation and presentation could be improved in a few areas to make the contribution even more impactful.

1.  **Comparison to a Stronger Hybrid Baseline:** The authors correctly argue that a major limitation of STiC [11, 12] is its inability to recommend completely new items. However, a natural and powerful baseline would be a simple ensemble of STiC and a standard collaborative filtering model like SVD. For instance, for items with a history, one could use STiC's prediction, and for new items, one could fall back to SVD's prediction. Comparing against such a hybrid model would provide a more challenging and informative benchmark, clarifying the practical advantage of the proposed integrated approach.

2.  **Lack of Hyperparameter Details and Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper states that hyperparameters (e.g., ρ, λ, β, latent rank) were "tuned to give the best performance." This is insufficient for reproducibility.
    *   It is crucial to detail the method used for hyperparameter selection (e.g., grid search on a validation set) and report the ranges considered.
    *   The bandwidth parameter `σ` of the exponential triggering kernel is a critical component of the model, as it dictates the timescale of self-excitation. How was this parameter set for each dataset? A sensitivity analysis showing how performance changes with different values of `σ` would significantly strengthen the paper by providing insight into the model's robustness and the characteristic timescales of user behavior in each domain.

3.  **Insufficient Dataset Characterization:** Given the paper's focus on temporal dynamics, the description of the real-world datasets could be enhanced. It would be highly beneficial to include key statistics that characterize the temporal patterns, such as:
    *   The distribution of the number of events per user-item sequence.
    *   The mean, median, and variance of inter-event times.
    *   A measure of "burstiness" or irregularity in the event sequences.
    This information would help readers better understand the nature of the challenges present in each dataset and provide a clearer context for the model's performance.

**Detailed Comments on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The model is well-motivated, the mathematical formulation is correct, and the proposed optimization algorithm is supported by a convergence analysis. The primary weakness in quality relates to the experimental evaluation, which could be made more rigorous with the inclusion of stronger baselines and a more transparent discussion of hyperparameter tuning, which is essential for reproducibility.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core ideas are conveyed effectively in the introduction and methods sections. However, the presentation of the optimization algorithm in Section 4 is quite dense. A brief, high-level paragraph motivating the alternative formulation (Eq. 5) and the choice to split the optimization into proximal and conditional gradient steps *before* presenting the algorithm details would improve readability and accessibility for a broader audience.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix/tensor models for collaborative filtering is a novel and powerful contribution. It clearly differentiates itself from prior work by providing an integrated solution that models fine-grained temporal dependencies while retaining the ability to generalize to unobserved user-item pairs.

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a significant and increasingly important problem in recommender systems and beyond. As online services focus more on long-term user engagement rather than one-off interactions, methods that can model and predict *when* a user will return and *what* they will want are of great practical value. The proposed model is a strong step in this direction and is likely to be influential, inspiring further research at the intersection of temporal point processes and large-scale machine learning.
