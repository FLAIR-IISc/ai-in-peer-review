The paper introduces a framework for time-sensitive recommendation based on recurrent user activities. It differs from prior work by explicitly modeling temporally varying user preferences and by addressing prediction of a user’s next return time. The approach links self-exciting point processes (Hawkes processes) with low-rank models, estimating parameters via a convex objective with nuclear-norm regularization and nonnegativity constraints. The authors propose an efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal-gradient and conditional-gradient steps with O(1/t) convergence and demonstrate scalability. Experiments include a large synthetic dataset illustrating efficiency and two real-world datasets of modest size.

First, it would help to substantiate the claim that time-varying preferences are widespread. An analysis of the deployed real datasets could clarify the magnitude and nature of temporal variation. For instance, are changes primarily driven by the emergence of new popular items (e.g., new songs), or are there other recurrent patterns?

What is the practical motivation for predicting a user’s next return time? Please motivate this use case before positioning it as a core contribution. How would a web service concretely leverage such predictions, and for which operational decisions?

There appear to be simple baselines for return-time prediction that are not included: (1) using the last observed inter-event time; and (2) using the most frequent inter-event time for that user-item pair (or user). Also, predicting exact return times may be unnecessarily difficult; a coarser yet actionable task might be more appropriate, such as classifying the next gap into short (1–5 days), medium (5–14 days), long (14–33 days), or very long (>33 days).

Experimental section: it is difficult to gauge the practical significance of the improvement over SVD. Is the gain large (order-of-magnitude) or modest? Would end users notice? How does the method perform relative to recommending globally popular items, updated frequently (e.g., daily), which is often a strong baseline?

Small comments:
1) Typo in the last paragraph on page 3: “and is abel to capture” → “able.”
2) In the first paragraph of Section 2, the shift to discussing retweets and link creation in social networks lacks references and is abrupt; consider rephrasing to avoid a confusing change of context.

The paper tackles two problems—time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction—without fully motivating their relevance or impact. Several baselines and analyses are missing (as noted above). Otherwise, the work is overall OK/marginal.
