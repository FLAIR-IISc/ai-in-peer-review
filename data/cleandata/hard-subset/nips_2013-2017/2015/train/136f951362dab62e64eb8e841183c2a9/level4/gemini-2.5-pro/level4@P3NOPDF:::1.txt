This paper introduces a novel method for time-sensitive recommendations that models user activities over time. The key contribution is a model designed to capture time-varying user preferences, a factor often overlooked in traditional recommendation systems. In addition to recommendations, the proposed framework jointly models and predicts the time until a user's next interaction with the service. The authors propose an efficient optimization algorithm for model learning and evaluate the method on one large-scale synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability and on two real-world datasets of modest size to assess performance.

While the paper addresses an interesting set of problems, the current manuscript is weakened by a lack of clear motivation for its core contributions and an experimental section that could be significantly strengthened.

**Major Concerns / Weaknesses**

1.  **Motivation for Time-Varying Preferences:** The central premise of the paper—that user preferences are significantly time-varying—is asserted but not sufficiently motivated or empirically validated within the paper. It would greatly strengthen the submission to include an analysis of the real-world datasets to demonstrate this phenomenon. For instance, is the variance in preferences primarily driven by global trends, such as new popular songs emerging, or are there more subtle, user-specific patterns at play? Quantifying the extent and nature of this temporal drift would provide a much stronger foundation for the proposed model.

2.  **Motivation for Predicting User Return Time:** The motivation for predicting a user's exact next return time is not clearly articulated. Before this is claimed as a primary contribution, the paper needs to explain its practical relevance. How would a web company or service provider leverage this specific prediction? Potential applications might include managing server load, timing marketing campaigns, or identifying users at risk of churn, but these need to be explicitly discussed to justify the complexity of this sub-problem.

3.  **Inadequate Baselines and Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is missing several crucial baselines and lacks context for interpreting the results.

    *   **Return Time Prediction Baselines:** For the task of predicting return time, the paper overlooks simple yet effective baselines. For example: a) predicting the user's last observed return delay, or b) predicting the most frequently observed return delay for that user. Without comparing against these, it is difficult to assess the added value of the proposed model.
    *   **Recommendation Baselines:** The comparison against standard SVD is a good start, but a stronger baseline would be a time-aware popularity model (e.g., recommending the most popular items from the last day or week). In many real-world systems, such a model is a surprisingly hard baseline to beat, especially when item popularity changes rapidly.
    *   **Significance of Performance Gains:** The paper reports performance lifts over SVD, but it is difficult to gauge their practical significance. Is the reported improvement groundbreaking (e.g., an order-of-magnitude change) or a more modest, incremental gain? It would be helpful to translate these metrics into a more intuitive understanding of the user experience. Would a user actually perceive a noticeable improvement in the quality of their recommendations?

**Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Alternative Formulation for Return Time:** Predicting the *exact* return time of a user is an extremely challenging regression task. A potentially more practical and tractable alternative would be to frame this as a classification problem. For instance, predicting whether the user's return delay will fall into predefined bins—such as short (1-5 days), medium (5-14 days), long (14-33 days), or very long (>33 days)—could be more robust and still highly valuable for business applications.

**Minor Comments**

1.  There is a typo in the last paragraph of page 3: "and is abel to capture" should be "and is able to capture".
2.  In the first paragraph of Section 2, the text abruptly mentions retweets and link creation in social networks without providing context or citations. This sudden shift is confusing and should be rephrased or better integrated into the paper's narrative.

**Overall Assessment**

In summary, the paper addresses two interesting and relevant problems. However, the work in its current form feels preliminary. The core contributions are not sufficiently motivated, and the experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over simpler, well-established alternatives. While the technical approach is interesting, the paper's impact is not yet clear. Addressing the issues of motivation, baselines, and evaluation context would be necessary to make a compelling case for publication. As it stands, this is a marginal paper.
