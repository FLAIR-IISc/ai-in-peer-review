

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption. The authors address two related problems: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) predicting the next returning time of users to services. The paper builds on recent work by Kapoor et al. [11, 12] who focused on returning time prediction but did not address item recommendation for new items, which is a critical limitation for practical applications. The authors present a convex optimization formulation with theoretical guarantees and an efficient algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence.

The paper demonstrates good technical quality with a clear theoretical foundation in temporal point processes and low-rank matrix optimization. The experimental section includes synthetic data to showcase scalability (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events) and two real-world datasets (last.fm and tmall.com) for evaluation. The proposed method outperforms several baselines including Poisson processes, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods on both recommendation and returning time prediction tasks.

The main strengths of the paper include:
1. A theoretically sound approach with convergence guarantees
2. Demonstrated scalability to large datasets
3. Clear connection between temporal point processes and recommendation systems
4. Good experimental design with appropriate baselines

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before acceptance:

First, the significance of the work is not well established. The paper claims that "most existing recommendation techniques do not explicitly take into account the temporal behavior and recurrent activities of users" but fails to provide convincing evidence from the real datasets used in the experiments. It would be valuable to analyze the real data to show how user preferences actually vary over time (e.g., through temporal patterns in user-item interactions) rather than assuming this is a widespread problem. Without this analysis, the motivation for addressing time-varying preferences remains speculative.

Second, the motivation for predicting the exact returning time is unclear. The paper mentions that "predicting the returning time not only allows a service to keep track of evolving user preferences but also helps improve marketing strategies," but does not elaborate on how this specific prediction would be used in practice. For instance, how would a web company use the exact returning time (e.g., 3.7 days) rather than a more actionable metric? The authors should clarify the practical impact of this prediction and provide a concrete business use case.

Third, the paper lacks appropriate baselines for the returning time prediction task. The authors should compare with simple but effective baselines such as: (1) using the last observed time interval as the prediction, (2) using the most frequently observed time interval, and (3) predicting the median time interval. These baselines would provide a more meaningful benchmark for evaluating the proposed method's performance.

Fourth, the paper would benefit from a more practical approach to the returning time prediction task. Predicting the exact returning time is challenging and may not provide significant practical value. Instead, the authors could focus on a classification task (e.g., predicting whether the next return will be within 1-5 days, 5-14 days, 14-33 days, or >33 days), which would be more actionable for business applications and potentially easier to predict.

Fifth, the significance of the performance improvements over SVD is unclear. The paper claims "superb predictive performance" but does not quantify how much better the proposed method performs. Is the improvement order of magnitude or modest? How would this translate to user experience? The paper should compare the method's performance to the simple but effective baseline of recommending popular items (re-calculated daily), which is a strong baseline in practice.

The paper is generally well-written with clear organization, though there are minor issues: a typo on page 3 ("abel" instead of "able") and an abrupt mention of "retweets and link creation" in Section 2 without context or references.

In terms of the four NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees, but missing analysis of real-world temporal patterns and appropriate baselines.
- Clarity: Good organization and writing, but some sections lack sufficient motivation.
- Originality: Novel connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models, though the novelty could be better articulated by comparing with more relevant baselines.
- Significance: Limited by unclear practical motivation and lack of evidence showing the problem is widespread in real applications.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendations with good scalability, but the significance and practical relevance need to be strengthened through additional analysis and clearer motivation. The authors should provide evidence that time-varying preferences are widespread in real data, clarify the practical impact of predicting exact returning times, and compare with more appropriate baselines. With these improvements, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, the work is technically sound but lacks sufficient justification for its significance in the context of real-world recommendation systems.
