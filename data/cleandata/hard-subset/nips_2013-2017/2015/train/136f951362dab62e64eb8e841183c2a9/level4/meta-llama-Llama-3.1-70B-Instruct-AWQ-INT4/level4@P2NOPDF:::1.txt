The paper proposes a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities, which differentiates itself from existing methods by addressing the challenge of time-varying user preferences. Additionally, the paper tackles the prediction of the next returning time of a user, utilizing an efficient optimization algorithm developed by the authors. The experimental section presents results on one synthetic dataset to demonstrate the efficiency of the approach on large-scale data, as well as two real datasets of modest size.

However, there are several concerns and suggestions that can improve the paper. Firstly, it is essential to verify the prevalence of time-varying user preferences using real-world data from the experiments. Analyzing the patterns behind these variations, such as the impact of new popular content, would strengthen the claim. Furthermore, it is crucial to provide a clear motivation for predicting the next returning time of a user. Explaining how a web company can utilize this prediction and its potential benefits would enhance the contribution of the paper.

Regarding the prediction task, simple baselines such as the last time delay and the most occurred time delay were not evaluated. Moreover, predicting the exact returning time of a user might be too ambitious. A more feasible approach would be to focus on a simpler task, such as predicting the delay before a user returns to the service, categorized into short (1-5 days), medium (5-14 days), long (14-33 days), or very long (>33 days) delays.

The experimental section lacks a clear indication of the significance of the performance improvement compared to SVD. It is unclear whether the difference is substantial (order of magnitude) or modest, and whether the user would perceive the difference. Additionally, comparing the proposed recommendations to recommending popular items, which are often recalculated daily, would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

Some minor comments include a typo in the last paragraph of page 3, where "and is abel to capture" should be corrected to "and is able to capture." The first paragraph of section 2 abruptly introduces retweets and link creation in social networks without references, causing confusion. Rephrasing this section would improve clarity.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses two problems – time-sensitive recommendations and returning time of user – it lacks real motivation and impact analysis. Several baselines and analyses are missing, which are discussed above. Overall, the paper is acceptable but marginal.
