Summary
The paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models recurrent user–item interactions with Hawkes processes and couples them with low-rank structure across the user–item matrix. The intensity λu,i(t) combines a baseline preference and self-excitation via a triggering kernel (exponential in the main experiments), enabling time-aware item ranking and prediction of users’ next return time. Parameter estimation is cast as a convex problem with nuclear-norm regularization and nonnegativity constraints. The authors develop a scalable algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) and conditional gradient (for nuclear norms), with an O(1/t) convergence rate. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, and MIMIC II) show improved performance over Poisson, tensor factorization variants, SVD (for ranking), and STiC (for return-time), along with good calibration via time-change tests.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against survival analysis and semi-Markov models for return-time prediction (Kapoor et al.), which assume independent inter-event intervals and do not naturally recommend unseen items. It also relates to temporal factorization approaches (matrices/tensors) that discretize time and to learning in Hawkes processes (including low-rank or networked variants). The main novelty is a convex, low-rank Hawkes formulation for user–item events and an efficient hybrid optimization scheme.

Strengths
- Clear, unified formulation that captures both long-term preferences and short-term recency via Hawkes processes.
- Convex objective with a practical algorithm that scales to large event logs; solid convergence analysis.
- Empirical improvements on two tasks (time-aware ranking and return-time prediction) across synthetic and real data.
- Sensible model diagnostics (time-change QQ plots), and a path to incorporate context (profiles, text, location).

Weaknesses and questions
- Motivation for return-time prediction is underdeveloped in the introduction. Please provide concrete scenarios (e.g., scheduling notifications, inventory/staffing, ad pacing, proactive care in EHR) and clarify how accuracy translates into measurable gains.
- Missing simple baselines for return-time: last inter-event time, median/mode inter-event time per pair, exponential/Rayleigh MLE per pair, and a Hawkes model without low-rank coupling (or α = 0 Poisson with recency features). These would contextualize the gains.
- Popularity baselines for ranking are not directly reported. A time-varying popularity model (global item intensity by hour/day, or exponentially decayed counts) is often strong; how does your method compare to such baselines?
- Practical lift is unclear. Please report absolute and relative improvements in top-k metrics (e.g., Recall@k/NDCG@k) versus SVD and popularity to judge user-perceived impact.
- When and where temporal variation matters: analyze the real datasets to quantify the prevalence and strength of self-excitation (e.g., estimated α relative to γ0; kernel half-life), and whether improvements concentrate in certain users/items or contexts.
- Return-time evaluation is potentially high-variance. Report calibration (e.g., PIT histograms), robust metrics (median absolute error, quantile errors), and sensitivity to the number of thinning samples m. Consider complementary evaluation by binning delays into short/medium/long/very-long classes and reporting accuracy/AUC.
- Ablations and sensitivity: effect of (i) removing self-excitation, (ii) removing low-rank regularization, (iii) different kernels (exponential vs. Weibull/Log-logistic), (iv) the penalty parameter ρ and its impact on solution quality and speed.
- Cold-start: The model transfers across user–item pairs via low rank, but cannot score brand-new items/users without context features. Please clarify and, if feasible, include a context-augmented variant or a held-out new-item experiment.
- Computational details: provide wall-clock comparisons vs. ADMM proximal SVD approaches, memory footprint, and per-iteration costs on real datasets.

Quality
The formulation and algorithm appear technically sound, with appropriate convexity, well-defined likelihood, and a clear hybrid optimization scheme. The convergence result is convincing, though additional empirical comparisons (ablation and baselines above) would strengthen the evidence. Clarifying expected-time computation versus sampling (and variance) would improve the methodological section.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, but the narrative can be improved:
- Introduce and motivate both tasks (time-aware recommendation and return-time prediction) clearly in the introduction with concrete use cases.
- Improve the transition in Section 2; avoid abrupt shifts of domain (e.g., retweets/link creation) without citations or explanation.
- Define notation early and keep symbols consistent (Λ0, A, α, γ).
- Provide a brief intuition for the penalty formulation with Z1, Z2 and how ρ is chosen in practice.

Originality
The link between low-rank user–item coupling and Hawkes-based intensities in a convex formulation, coupled with a proximal–conditional gradient hybrid and its convergence analysis, is novel to my knowledge. The work is distinct from survival analysis and discretized temporal factorization.

Significance
If validated against stronger baselines and with clearer application-driven metrics, the approach could be impactful for real-time recommendation, marketing timing, and healthcare monitoring. The ability to scale to millions of pairs is a practical advantage.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add simple return-time baselines (last-gap, median-gap, exponential/Rayleigh MLE per pair) and time-varying popularity for ranking.
- Report top-k ranking metrics and absolute gains versus SVD and popularity; analyze user/item segments where gains are largest.
- Provide calibration and variance analyses for return-time predictions; include binned delay classification as a complementary evaluation.
- Include ablations on self-excitation, low-rank regularization, kernel family, and sensitivity to ρ.
- Clarify cold-start capabilities and, if possible, add a context-augmented experiment.

Pros
- Convex, scalable framework with convergence guarantees.
- Strong empirical results across two tasks; good calibration checks.
- Broad applicability and extensibility to contextual features.

Cons
- Motivation and practical impact of return-time prediction need strengthening.
- Several strong but simple baselines are missing.
- Limited ablation/sensitivity analysis; unclear magnitude of user-visible lift.

Minor comments
- Typo: page 3 last paragraph “abel” → “able.”
- Section 2: the move to retweets/link creation lacks references and is confusing; rephrase or add citations to maintain focus on the recommender setting.

Overall: promising and potentially significant, but borderline without the additional baselines, analyses, and clearer motivation outlined above.
