### **Summary**

This paper introduces a collaborative filtering model based on point processes to capture the temporal dynamics and recurrent patterns in user-item interactions. For each user-item pair, the model defines an event intensity function as the sum of a constant baseline intensity and a self-exciting component that depends on past events. The model represents both the baseline and self-exciting intensities using two distinct sets of latent vectors for users and items, effectively combining the strengths of latent factor models with temporal point processes. The authors derive an optimization algorithm for parameter estimation and present empirical results to demonstrate the proposed model's advantages over several alternatives.

### **Strengths**

*   **Originality:** The primary strength of this work lies in its novel synthesis of temporal point processes and latent factor models for collaborative filtering. This is an interesting and well-motivated approach to capturing the rich temporal structure often present in user interaction data.
*   **Significance:** The problem of modeling time-sensitive user preferences is of significant practical importance. The proposed method offers a principled way to address this challenge, potentially leading to more timely and relevant recommendations.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is promising, but the experimental evaluation and presentation could be significantly strengthened.

1.  **Missing Baseline Comparison:** The experimental evaluation would be more convincing with the inclusion of a stronger, more pragmatic baseline. The authors correctly argue that temporal models like STiC [12] and the one in [13] cannot recommend completely new items. However, a standard latent factor model (e.g., SVD/matrix factorization) excels at this task. Therefore, a simple yet powerful baseline would be an ensemble of STiC and SVD. This hybrid approach would leverage the temporal strengths of STiC and the generalization ability of SVD. Comparing the proposed model against such an ensemble would provide a clearer and more compelling assessment of its unique advantages.

2.  **Lack of Hyperparameter Details and Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper lacks crucial details regarding hyperparameter selection, which hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparisons.
    *   For the proposed model, how was the bandwidth parameter `σ` for the self-exciting kernel function chosen? This is a critical parameter, and a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with different values of `σ` would be highly valuable.
    *   The paper does not specify how other key hyperparameters, such as the dimensionality of the latent vectors, were selected. This information is essential for both the proposed method and the baseline models to ensure a fair and rigorous comparison.

3.  **Insufficient Dataset Characterization:** Given that the paper's central contribution is modeling temporal structure, the description of the datasets is insufficient. To better contextualize the results and understand the problem setting, it is important to provide statistics that characterize the temporal nature of the data. For instance, what is the distribution of recurring events per user-item pair? How irregular are the time intervals between interactions (e.g., mean, median, variance of inter-event times)? Providing such details would help readers understand the specific temporal challenges posed by the datasets and better appreciate the model's performance.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The overall presentation of the paper could be improved. There are several typos and grammatical errors throughout the manuscript that detract from its readability. A thorough round of proofreading would significantly enhance the paper's clarity and professionalism.

### **Overall Assessment**

This paper presents an interesting and valuable contribution by combining point processes with latent factor models for temporal recommendation. The core idea is sound and addresses an important problem. However, the work in its current form is weakened by an incomplete experimental evaluation and a lack of detail regarding methodology and data. Addressing the issues outlined above—particularly by adding the suggested baseline, providing a thorough hyperparameter analysis, and better characterizing the datasets—would substantially strengthen the paper and more clearly demonstrate the merits of the proposed approach.
