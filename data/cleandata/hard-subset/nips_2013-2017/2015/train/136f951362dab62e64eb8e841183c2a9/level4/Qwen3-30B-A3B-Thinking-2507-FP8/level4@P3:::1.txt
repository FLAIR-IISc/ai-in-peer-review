

**Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"**

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation that connects self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption. The authors address two key problems: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and predicting the next returning time of users to a service. They formulate a convex optimization problem, develop an efficient algorithm with O(1/t) convergence rate, and demonstrate scalability to large datasets. The work extends the literature on temporal recommendation systems by introducing a unified approach to both recommendation and returning time prediction.

The paper's main contribution lies in connecting self-exciting point processes (Hawkes processes) with low-rank matrix factorization, a novel combination that enables both time-sensitive recommendation and returning time prediction. This differs from related work such as Kapoor et al. (2014, 2015), who focused on returning time prediction using survival analysis but could not generalize to new item recommendations. The paper also relates to recent NIPS work on temporal recommendation (e.g., Du et al., 2015; Zhou et al., 2013), though it would benefit from more comprehensive comparison to these works.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Quality:** The convex formulation and optimization algorithm are well-motivated and theoretically sound. The convergence analysis (Theorem 2) is rigorous and demonstrates the algorithm's efficiency. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from standard Poisson processes and Hawkes processes.
2. **Scalability:** The experimental results demonstrate impressive scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events), which is a significant practical contribution.
3. **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and explanations of the proposed model. The experimental design is appropriate for evaluating the main claims.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Significance and Motivation:** The paper lacks convincing motivation for why time-sensitive recommendation is more important than existing approaches. The introduction claims that "most existing recommendation techniques are mainly optimized at predicting users' onetime preference," but does not provide empirical evidence that time-varying preferences are a significant issue in practice. The paper would be stronger if it analyzed the real data to show that preferences genuinely change over time (e.g., not just due to new popular items appearing).
2. **Baselines for Returning Time Prediction:** The paper misses key baselines for returning time prediction, such as the last time delay and most common time delay. As noted by the draft review, predicting exact returning times is challenging; a more practical approach would be to predict return time intervals (e.g., short, medium, long). The paper should address this by comparing to more relevant baselines.
3. **Comparison to SVD:** The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate how much better the proposed method is compared to SVD, which is a strong baseline for recommendation systems. The performance gains should be quantified more precisely (e.g., "the proposed method reduces MAE by 30% compared to SVD") and contextualized in terms of user experience impact.
4. **Practical Impact:** The paper claims that returning time prediction "helps service providers improve marketing strategies," but does not provide concrete examples of how this would be implemented. It would strengthen the paper to include a specific use case (e.g., "marketers could bid on time slots with 20% higher conversion rates when using our predictions").

**Specific Comments:**
- Page 3, last paragraph: "and is abel to capture" should be "and is able to capture."
- Section 2: The introduction of "retweets and link creation in social networks" without context or references is confusing and should be removed or better integrated.
- The paper claims to address "two related questions" but does not clearly articulate why these two tasks are fundamentally connected or how solving one informs the other.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents an interesting technical contribution with a sound theoretical foundation and good experimental results. However, it falls short in demonstrating the significance of the problem it addresses and in comparing to relevant baselines. The connection between the theoretical contribution and practical impact is underdeveloped, which weakens the overall significance.

**Recommendation:**
The paper has technical merit and addresses an interesting problem, but requires substantial revision to strengthen its significance and baseline comparisons. Specifically, the authors should:
1. Provide empirical evidence that time-varying preferences are a significant issue in practice (not just due to new content)
2. Compare to more relevant baselines for returning time prediction (e.g., last time delay, most common time delay)
3. Quantify performance improvements over SVD and popular items baselines more precisely
4. Elaborate on the practical impact of returning time prediction with concrete examples

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these points. As it stands, the contribution is novel but its significance is not fully demonstrated. With these revisions, it could be a strong contribution to the field.

**Final Recommendation:** Weak Accept (with major revisions)
