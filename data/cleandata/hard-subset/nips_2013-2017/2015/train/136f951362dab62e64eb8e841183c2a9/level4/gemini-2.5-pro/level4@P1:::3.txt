This paper presents a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation, addressing two primary tasks: recommending the most relevant item at a specific moment and predicting a user's next return time to a service. The core idea is to model recurrent user activities by unifying self-exciting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models. This approach allows the model to capture both long-term, static user preferences (through a base intensity matrix) and short-term, history-dependent dynamics (through a self-exciting component). The authors assume the parameter matrices have a low-rank structure and formulate the model estimation as a convex optimization problem regularized by the nuclear norm. To solve this problem, they develop an efficient and scalable algorithm that combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, demonstrating its effectiveness on large-scale synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Quality**

The paper's approach of modeling user event sequences with stochastic processes is principled and technically sound. The formulation as a convex optimization problem is a significant strength, providing theoretical guarantees. The development of a custom optimization algorithm that is both scalable and has a strong convergence rate is a solid contribution.

However, I have a few concerns regarding the theoretical analysis and the experimental setup:

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Results:** In Theorem 1, the definition of the threshold `ρ*` could be clarified. The theorem states that for the alternative formulation (5) to be equivalent to the original problem (4), `ρ` must be greater than `ρ*`. However, the expression for `ρ*` involves a `max` operator, and it is not specified what this maximum is taken over. Furthermore, if the optimal solution has `Λ*_0 = Z*_1` and `A* = Z*_2`, the denominator in the expression for `ρ*` would be zero, which raises the question of whether `ρ*` can be unbounded. A more detailed explanation would strengthen this part of the analysis.

2.  **Theory-Practice Gap:** The practical utility of Theorem 1 is somewhat disconnected from the experimental methodology. The authors state that `ρ*` is difficult to compute, so `ρ` is instead tuned as a hyperparameter. While this is a pragmatic choice, it weakens the link between the theoretical analysis and the practical application of the model, making the theoretical result less impactful for practitioners.

3.  **Experimental Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, but it could be made stronger by including a comparison with SVD++. As one of the winning models of the Netflix Prize, SVD++ and its temporal variants are well-known for effectively modeling temporal dynamics in user feedback. Including it as a baseline would provide a more robust assessment of the proposed model's performance against the state-of-the-art in time-aware collaborative filtering. The current baselines are reasonable, but this addition would significantly improve the quality of the comparison.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-established, the model is presented logically, and the experimental setup and results are explained clearly.

**Originality**

The paper's primary contribution—the synthesis of low-rank models and Hawkes processes for time-sensitive recommendation—is novel. While both techniques are established in their respective fields, their integration to solve these specific recommendation problems is original and well-motivated. This work clearly distinguishes itself from prior art by simultaneously modeling temporal recurrence and generalizing to unobserved user-item pairs.

**Significance**

This work addresses a critical and often overlooked aspect of modern recommender systems: the temporal dimension of user engagement. The ability to predict not only *what* a user might like but also *when* has significant practical implications for applications such as dynamic ad bidding, proactive customer engagement, and resource planning. The model's strong empirical performance and demonstrated scalability suggest it could be a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners. The proposed optimization algorithm may also be of independent interest for other problems involving non-negative matrix rank minimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and principled model for an important and challenging problem in recommendation systems.
*   The technical contributions, including the convex formulation and the efficient optimization algorithm, are solid.
*   Demonstrates superior performance and scalability on large-scale synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The theoretical analysis regarding the parameter `ρ*` in Theorem 1 lacks clarity and its practical relevance is limited as it is tuned empirically.
*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by comparing against a stronger state-of-the-art baseline like SVD++.
