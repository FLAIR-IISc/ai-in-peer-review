The paper proposes a novel method for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities, distinguishing itself from existing approaches by addressing the problem of time-varying user preferences. Additionally, the paper tackles the prediction of the next returning time of a user. The authors employ an efficient optimization algorithm for learning, which is demonstrated to be effective on both synthetic and real-world data sets.

However, there are several concerns and suggestions for improvement. Firstly, the paper claims that time-varying user preferences are a widespread problem, but this assertion would be strengthened by an analysis of the real data sets used in the experiments. It would be beneficial to investigate the underlying causes of these time-varying preferences, such as the impact of new popular items or other patterns.

Furthermore, the motivation behind predicting the next returning time of a user is not clearly explained. It would be helpful to provide a justification for this prediction task and explore its potential applications in a web-company setting. For instance, how would this prediction be utilized, and what benefits would it bring to the company?

Regarding the prediction of the next returning time, the authors may want to consider including additional baselines for comparison. Two simple yet effective baselines that were not tested are: 1) the last time delay, and 2) the most occurred time delay. Moreover, predicting the actual returning time of the user may be too challenging, and a more feasible approach might be to focus on a simpler task, such as predicting the delay category (e.g., short, medium, long, or very long) before the user returns to the service.

In the experimental section, it is difficult to gauge the significance of the performance lift compared to SVD. The authors should provide more context to help readers understand the magnitude of the improvement. Is it groundbreaking, or is it a modest gain? Would the user notice a difference? Additionally, it would be beneficial to compare the proposed recommendations to a popular item-based approach, which is often a challenging baseline to beat, especially when popular items are updated daily.

Minor comments include a typo in the last paragraph of page 3, where "abel" should be corrected to "able." In the first paragraph of section 2, the sudden introduction of retweets and link creation in social networks without references is confusing. It would be better to rephrase this section to provide a clearer connection to the rest of the paper.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses two problems – time-sensitive recommendations and returning time of user – it lacks a clear motivation for why these problems are relevant and how solving them would have an impact. Several baselines and analyses are missing, which detracts from the overall quality of the paper. Nevertheless, the paper is overall acceptable, albeit marginal.
