

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a point process-based collaborative filtering model to capture temporal and recurrent user-item interactions. The core idea models event intensity for each user-item pair as the sum of a constant base rate and a self-exciting component driven by historical events. The self-exciting term is parameterized via two sets of user and item latent vectors, enabling the model to encode both user preferences and item characteristics. The authors derive an optimization algorithm for parameter estimation and validate the approach on real-world datasets, demonstrating superior performance over standard baselines.  

**Strengths**  
The integration of point processes with latent factor models is conceptually sound and addresses a meaningful gap in modeling temporal dynamics in recommendation systems. The problem formulation is well-motivated, and the method’s practical relevance is clear given the prevalence of time-sensitive user behaviors. The empirical results, while requiring refinement, suggest the model captures complex temporal patterns effectively.  

**Major Concerns**  
1. **Baseline Comparison**: The experimental evaluation lacks a critical baseline: a simple ensemble of STiC [12] (which handles recurrence) and SVD (which mitigates cold-start issues for new items). The authors argue STiC/SVD cannot recommend new items, but an ensemble would test whether their method *truly* integrates these capabilities better than a naive combination. Omitting this comparison weakens the claim of superiority.  

2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper does not specify how key hyperparameters are set. For the gamma kernel (used in the self-exciting process), the bandwidth parameter $\sigma$ is critical yet unaddressed—how is $\sigma$ chosen? How sensitive are results to its variation? Similarly, the dimensionality of latent vectors (a standard hyperparameter in such models) is not justified. Sensitivity analyses or ablation studies on these parameters are essential for credibility.  

3. **Dataset Characterization**: The temporal nature of the problem demands deeper dataset analysis. The paper should report key temporal statistics:  
   - Mean/median recurrence intervals per user-item pair (e.g., "users interact with items every 2.3 days on average").  
   - Temporal irregularity metrics (e.g., coefficient of variation of inter-event times).  
   Without this, it is unclear whether the model’s strength stems from capturing *actual* temporal patterns or merely fitting noise.  

**Clarity and Presentation**  
The writing contains grammatical errors and ambiguous phrasing (e.g., "event density is the sum" should be "intensity function is modeled as"). Figures/tables lack sufficient detail (e.g., error bars in results, hyperparameter settings in tables). A clearer exposition of the optimization algorithm’s convergence properties would also strengthen the technical contribution.  

**Originality and Significance**  
The method is an interesting fusion of point processes and latent factor models, offering a principled way to model user-item interactions with temporal dependencies. Its significance lies in addressing a practical limitation of existing CF methods (e.g., treating time as static), with potential applications in dynamic recommendation scenarios. However, the novelty is incremental; the core technique (combining point processes with latent vectors) builds directly on prior work [12, 13].  

**Recommendation**  
The paper presents a promising approach with practical value but requires substantial revisions to address experimental weaknesses. The proposed method’s advantages over baselines are not convincingly demonstrated due to missing comparisons and inadequate hyperparameter analysis. The authors should:  
- Add the STiC+SVD ensemble baseline and report results.  
- Conduct sensitivity analyses for $\sigma$ and latent dimensions.  
- Provide temporal statistics for both datasets.  
- Correct grammatical errors and clarify technical exposition.  

**Overall Assessment**  
*Quality*: Moderate. The technical foundation is sound, but experimental rigor is lacking.  
*Clarity*: Needs significant improvement (language, figures, hyperparameter transparency).  
*Originality*: Moderate (incremental advancement).  
*Significance*: High (temporal dynamics are crucial for real-world CF).  

**Final Recommendation**  
**Weak Reject** (with major revisions). The core idea has merit, but the current experimental evaluation fails to substantiate the claimed advantages. Addressing the baseline, hyperparameter, and dataset characterization gaps would transform this into a strong contribution. Without these changes, the paper’s claims remain unsubstantiated.
