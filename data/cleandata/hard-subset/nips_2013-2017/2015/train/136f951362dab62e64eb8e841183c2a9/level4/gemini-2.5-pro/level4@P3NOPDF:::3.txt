**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for time-sensitive recommendation. The proposed method models the user-item rating matrix by combining a low-rank factorization approach with a Hawkes process to capture the temporal dynamics of user behavior. A key advantage of the algorithm is its amenability to distributed optimization, enabling scalability. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing techniques on several datasets.

**Review and Justification**

**Quality and Contributions**

The paper's main strength lies in its principled, stochastic process-based approach to modeling user ratings over time. The synthesis of low-rank models for collaborative filtering with the self-exciting properties of Hawkes processes is a compelling way to capture how past ratings influence future ones. The development of a scalable, distributed algorithm is also a significant practical contribution.

However, I have a few concerns regarding the theoretical analysis and the experimental setup that could be addressed to strengthen the paper:

1.  **Theoretical Clarity:** There is an ambiguity in the theoretical presentation concerning Equation (8) and its connection to Theorem 2.
    *   In Equation (8), the maximization that defines ρ* is underspecified. It is unclear over which parameter(s) the maximum is taken. If the maximization is over ρ, it is not immediately obvious whether the optimal value, ρ*, is guaranteed to be bounded. Clarification on this point is necessary.
    *   There appears to be a disconnect between the theoretical result in Theorem 2 and its application. The method for choosing ρ in the experiments does not seem to leverage Theorem 2. This lessens the practical impact of the theorem and raises questions about its role in the overall framework. If the theorem provides a guideline that was not strictly followed, an explanation would be helpful.

2.  **Experimental Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is sound, but it could be made more comprehensive. While the current baselines are reasonable, the inclusion of SVD++ [17], a widely recognized and high-performing model from the Netflix Prize competition, would significantly strengthen the empirical claims. SVD++ effectively incorporates temporal dynamics and implicit feedback, making it a highly relevant and competitive baseline for this task. Comparing against such a strong benchmark would provide a more convincing demonstration of the proposed method's advantages.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The core ideas are presented in a logical sequence that is easy to follow, and the notation is largely consistent. The motivation for the problem and the description of the proposed model are articulated effectively.

**Originality**

The work is novel. While time-sensitive recommendation is an active area of research, the proposed combination of low-rank matrix factorization with Hawkes processes appears to be a unique contribution. This synthesis of collaborative filtering techniques and temporal point process models offers a new and promising direction for modeling dynamic user preferences.

**Significance**

The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is of high practical and academic importance, with direct applications in many online systems. By providing a novel, high-performing, and scalable algorithm, this work makes a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed approach is likely to be impactful and could inspire future research at the intersection of recommender systems and temporal machine learning.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and well-motivated algorithm for an important problem. The core idea is elegant and the results are promising. My primary reservations are the need for clarification in the theoretical analysis and the suggestion to include a stronger baseline like SVD++ in the experiments. Addressing these points would substantially improve the paper and solidify its contributions. I lean towards acceptance.
