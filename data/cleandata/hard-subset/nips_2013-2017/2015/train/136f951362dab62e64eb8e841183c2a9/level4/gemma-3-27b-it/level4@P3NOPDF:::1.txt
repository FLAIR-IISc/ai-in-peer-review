## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission

This paper proposes a new method for time-sensitive recommendations, motivated by the observation of time-varying user preferences. The approach also introduces a model for predicting a user’s next return time to the platform. Learning is performed via an efficient optimization algorithm developed by the authors. The experimental evaluation uses one synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability and two real-world datasets of modest size to assess performance.

While the problem of adapting recommendations to evolving user tastes is important, the paper lacks a compelling justification for the *prevalence* of rapidly changing preferences. The authors claim that user preferences are time-varying, but this claim isn’t supported by analysis of the provided datasets. It would significantly strengthen the paper to present such an analysis. Specifically, investigating the nature of these shifts – are they driven by the introduction of new popular items, or do underlying patterns of preference change exist? – would be valuable.

Furthermore, the motivation for predicting a user’s next return time is unclear. Before positioning this as a core contribution, the paper needs to articulate *why* this prediction is useful. A concrete explanation of how a web-company could leverage this information (e.g., targeted re-engagement campaigns, resource allocation) would greatly enhance the impact of this work.

The proposed method for predicting return time also requires further consideration. The evaluation lacks comparison to simple, intuitive baselines, such as: 1) predicting the next return time based on the last observed time delay, and 2) predicting the next return time based on the most frequent observed time delay. Moreover, predicting the *exact* return time appears overly ambitious. A more practical approach might be to frame the problem as a classification task – predicting whether the user will return within a short (1-5 days), medium (5-14 days), long (14-33 days), or very long (>33 days) timeframe. This simplification could lead to more robust and interpretable results.

The experimental results are difficult to interpret in terms of practical significance. The performance gains relative to SVD are not clearly quantified. Is the improvement substantial (e.g., an order of magnitude), or modest? Would a user perceive a meaningful difference in recommendation quality? Additionally, the paper should include a comparison against a non-personalized “popular items” baseline, ideally one that dynamically updates daily. This is a strong competitor, especially in many real-world scenarios, and serves as an important benchmark. The absence of this comparison makes it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach.

Finally, several minor issues require attention:

1.  On page 3, the last paragraph contains a typo: "and is abel to capture" should be "and is able to capture."
2.  In the first paragraph of Section 2, the introduction of concepts like retweets and link creation in social networks feels abrupt and lacks context or citation. This sudden shift in focus is confusing and should be rephrased to maintain clarity and focus on the core problems being addressed. 
3. The paper lacks a strong motivational narrative. While it addresses time-sensitive recommendations and return time prediction, it doesn't fully articulate *why* solving these problems is important or what the real-world impact would be.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of demonstrating a compelling contribution. The lack of justification for the central problem, missing baselines, ambiguous experimental results, and minor presentation issues lead me to rate this paper as OK/marginal. Addressing the concerns outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper.
