Summary
The paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models recurrent user behavior using low-rank Hawkes processes. For each user–item pair, the conditional intensity is the sum of a nonnegative base rate λ0 and a self-exciting term that aggregates past events through a triggering kernel (exponential in the experiments). To share information across pairs and enable generalization to unobserved user–item combinations, the base-rate matrix Λ0 and the excitation matrix A are learned under nuclear-norm regularization (i.e., low-rank structure), subject to nonnegativity constraints. The resulting estimation problem is convex. The authors introduce an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient updates (to handle nonnegativity) with conditional gradient steps (to handle nuclear norms), achieving an O(1/t) convergence rate. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (Last.fm, Tmall, MIMIC II) evaluate two tasks: (1) time-sensitive recommendation via ranking by intensity at time t, and (2) return-time prediction via sampling from the conditional distribution. The proposed method outperforms Poisson, survival analysis/HSMM-style baselines (STiC), SVD, and tensor factorization variants.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Hawkes processes for modeling self-excitation and recurrent activity, extending prior work on learning Hawkes parameters and kernels (e.g., Zhou, Zha, Song; Du et al.) by introducing a low-rank structure at the user–item level for recommendation. It contrasts with survival analysis approaches to return-time prediction (Kapoor et al.) that assume i.i.d. inter-event times and cannot recommend novel items. Compared to standard collaborative filtering and temporal tensor factorizations (e.g., Koren; Karatzoglou et al.; Xiong et al.), the proposed model explicitly accounts for event timing and history dependence.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation that unifies time-sensitive ranking and return-time prediction.
- Convex estimation with a principled low-rank inductive bias; nonnegativity maintains interpretability.
- Algorithmic contribution combining proximal and conditional gradients with a provable O(1/t) rate and efficient per-iteration steps.
- Strong scalability demonstrated on large synthetic data and competitive performance on real datasets.
- Sensible qualitative validation via time-rescaling checks that support Hawkes assumptions.

Weaknesses and questions
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: It is unclear how the kernel bandwidth σ is chosen (global vs. per user/item) and how sensitive the results are to σ. Please include selection protocol and a sensitivity analysis for σ, nuclear-norm weights (λ, β), rank surrogates, and the penalty parameter ρ.
- Stability/branching ratio: For exponential kernels, classical Hawkes stability requires α·∫γ < 1 (e.g., α·σ < 1 in the scalar case). Are such constraints enforced or checked post hoc? A discussion of stability across user–item pairs and its empirical impact would strengthen the paper.
- Choice and strength of baselines: Consider adding a simple hybrid baseline that combines preference from SVD with timing from a point process (e.g., SVD score × time-varying intensity from STiC or a renewal model), which would test whether the proposed joint model beats a modular ensemble. Also consider a renewal process baseline (history via last inter-arrival only) and a Hawkes variant without low-rank sharing.
- Evaluation metrics: For the ranking task, please report standard retrieval metrics (MRR, Recall@k, NDCG@k) in addition to mean rank. For return-time prediction, complement MAE with distributional calibration (e.g., time-rescaling KS statistics) beyond a single illustrative QQ plot.
- Dataset characterization: Provide summary statistics relevant to temporal modeling: per-user/item event counts, proportion of recurrent pairs, inter-event time distributions, seasonality/diurnal effects, and sparsity patterns. These will help readers assess where Hawkes assumptions hold.
- Practical details and reproducibility: Include details on initialization, power-method parameters for top singular vectors, stopping criteria, wall-clock time, memory usage, and the effect of ρ. Theorem 1 gives a problem-dependent threshold ρ*, but in practice it is tuned; a continuation or adaptive scheme would be useful.
- Identifiability: Please discuss potential trade-offs between Λ0 and A (e.g., absorbing low-frequency history into baseline) and whether regularization or constraints mitigate identifiability issues.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The modeling and estimation framework appears technically sound, with a careful convex formulation and a reasonable optimization strategy. The convergence analysis is clear and consistent with the algorithm’s structure. Experimental design is generally solid, but the baseline set can be strengthened and more details on hyperparameter selection and stability would improve rigor.
- Clarity: The paper is readable, but the presentation can be improved. Suggestions: unify notation early (Λ0, A, λu,i(t)), clearly distinguish matrices vs. implicit factors (since low-rank is induced via nuclear norm, not explicit factorization), define all symbols upon first use, and proofread for typos and grammar. More detailed algorithm pseudocode (including line-search and SVD computations) and a complexity table would help.
- Originality: The connection between low-rank matrix learning and Hawkes-based recommendation is novel and timely. The penalty reformulation and the blended proximal/conditional-gradient solver tailored to nonnegativity plus nuclear norms are interesting and of potential independent interest.
- Significance: Addressing “what and when” jointly is important for modern recommendation and healthcare applications. The approach is likely to be adopted or extended, especially given its convexity and scalability.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and well-motivated model linking recommender systems with self-exciting processes.
- Convex, scalable learning with nontrivial algorithmic contribution and theory.
- Strong empirical results on both recommendation and return-time prediction.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing details on hyperparameter selection, kernel bandwidth sensitivity, and stability constraints.
- Baselines could be stronger; no hybrid or renewal models considered.
- Limited reporting of standard ranking metrics and dataset temporal characteristics.

Suggestions for revision
- Add sensitivity analyses for σ, λ, β, ρ and discuss stability.
- Include hybrid and renewal baselines; report MRR/Recall@k/NDCG@k.
- Provide temporal statistics of datasets and additional training/runtime details.
- Improve clarity and notation, and proofread for language issues.

Overall, this is a solid and promising submission. Addressing the above points would considerably strengthen the paper’s empirical and practical impact.
