This paper proposes a new method for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities. The approach distinguishes itself from existing methods by explicitly addressing the problem of time-varying user preferences. In addition to recommendations, the paper introduces a model for predicting a user's next return time. The model is trained using an efficient optimization algorithm, also proposed by the authors. The experimental section demonstrates the algorithm's efficiency on a large-scale synthetic dataset and evaluates its performance on two real-world datasets of modest size.

While the paper addresses an interesting problem, there are several key areas that require improvement.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Motivation and Problem Framing:**
    *   The paper's central premise—that time-varying user preferences are a widespread and significant problem—needs to be better substantiated. The authors could strengthen this claim by providing an analysis of the real-world datasets used in their experiments. For instance, do preferences primarily shift due to the emergence of new popular items (e.g., new songs), or are there other underlying temporal patterns at play?
    *   The motivation for predicting a user's next return time is not clearly articulated. The authors should provide a stronger justification for why this is a significant contribution. What are the practical applications for a web company? How might this prediction be leveraged to improve user experience or business outcomes? Without this context, the contribution feels disconnected.

2.  **Return Time Prediction Baselines and Task Formulation:**
    *   The evaluation of the return time prediction model is missing some simple yet crucial baselines. It would be insightful to compare the proposed model against: 1) predicting the next return interval to be the same as the user's most recent one ("last time delay"), and 2) predicting the most frequently observed return interval for that user ("most occurred time delay").
    *   Furthermore, predicting the *exact* return time is an extremely challenging regression task. A more practical and perhaps more useful approach would be to frame this as a classification problem. For example, predicting whether a user will return within a short (e.g., 1-5 days), medium (5-14 days), long (14-33 days), or very long (>33 days) interval could be more tractable and actionable for a recommendation system.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The significance of the performance improvement over the SVD baseline is difficult to assess from the results presented. Is the improvement substantial (e.g., an order of magnitude) or merely incremental? It is unclear whether this performance lift would translate into a noticeably better experience for the end-user.
    *   The experiments lack a comparison against a "most popular" baseline. Recommending popular items is often a very strong competitor in real-world systems, especially if popularity is recalculated frequently (e.g., daily). Including this baseline would provide a much clearer picture of the proposed method's practical value.

**Minor Comments:**

1.  There is a typo in the last paragraph on page 3: "and is abel to capture" should be "and is able to capture".
2.  The first paragraph of Section 2 abruptly introduces concepts like retweets and link creation in social networks without providing context or references. This sudden change of context is confusing and should be rephrased or better integrated into the paper's narrative.

**Summary:**

The paper addresses two potentially interesting problems: time-sensitive recommendations and user return time prediction. However, it falls short in motivating the relevance and potential impact of solving these problems. The experimental evaluation is weakened by the absence of several important baselines and a clearer analysis of the performance gains. Due to these issues, the paper is marginal in its current form.
