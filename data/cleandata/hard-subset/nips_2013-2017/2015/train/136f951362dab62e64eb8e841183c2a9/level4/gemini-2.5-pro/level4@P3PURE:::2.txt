**Summary**

This paper proposes a collaborative filtering model based on point processes to capture the temporal dynamics and recurrent patterns in user-item interactions. For each user-item pair, the model defines an event intensity function as the sum of a constant base intensity and a self-exciting term that depends on the history of past events. These two intensity components are parameterized by two distinct sets of user and item latent vectors. The authors derive an optimization algorithm for parameter estimation and present empirical results suggesting that the proposed model outperforms several common alternatives.

While the core idea is interesting and the problem is significant, the paper's contribution is undermined by shortcomings in the experimental evaluation and a lack of clarity in its presentation.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality:** The main strength of this work is the novel combination of point process theory with latent factor models for collaborative filtering.
*   **Significance:** This approach is well-motivated and addresses the important practical problem of modeling temporal dynamics in recommendation systems.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The experimental evaluation requires significant improvement to be convincing.

1.  **Baselines:** The choice of baselines could be strengthened. The authors argue that models like STiC [12] cannot recommend entirely new items. While this is true, a simple yet strong baseline would be an ensemble of STiC and a standard matrix factorization model (e.g., SVD), which explicitly handles this cold-start scenario. A comparison against such an ensemble would provide a more compelling demonstration of the proposed model's advantages.

2.  **Hyperparameter Details:** The paper lacks crucial details regarding hyperparameter selection, which hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparisons.
    *   For the kernel function used in the self-exciting process, it is unclear how the bandwidth parameter σ was chosen. A sensitivity analysis with respect to σ would be valuable to understand its impact on performance.
    *   Details on how other important hyperparameters, such as the dimension of the latent vectors, were selected are missing for both the proposed model and the baseline methods.

3.  **Dataset Characterization:** Given the paper's focus on temporal structure, the description of the datasets could be more informative. The authors should provide statistics that characterize the temporal nature of the data, such as the distribution of recurring events per user-item pair or metrics quantifying the irregularity of interaction timings. This would help readers better understand the problem setting and the challenges addressed by the model.

**Clarity**

The presentation of the paper could be improved. There are several typos and grammatical errors that detract from the readability of the work.

**Overall Assessment**

The paper introduces an interesting method for an important problem. However, its overall quality is currently limited by the weaknesses in the experimental validation and a lack of key methodological details. Addressing the issues raised above, particularly by strengthening the experimental comparisons and providing a more thorough description of the methodology and datasets, would significantly improve the paper.
