- Paper proposes time-sensitive recommendations and return-time prediction with an efficient learning algorithm and experiments on one synthetic and two modest real datasets.  
- No evidence or analysis is provided to justify the prevalence of rapidly changing user preferences in the datasets.  
- Authors should analyze whether preference shifts are due to new popular items or true changes in underlying user tastes.  
- Motivation for predicting users’ next return time is unclear and needs concrete real-world uses (e.g., re-engagement campaigns, resource allocation).  
- Return-time prediction evaluation lacks simple baselines (e.g., last observed delay, most frequent delay).  
- Predicting exact return times seems overly ambitious; framing as classification into short/medium/long/very-long windows may be more practical.  
- Experimental results lack clear practical significance: improvements over SVD are not quantified or contextualized for user impact.  
- Missing comparison to a dynamic non-personalized “popular items” baseline, which is a strong real-world competitor.  
- Presentation issues: typo on page 3 (“abel” → “able”), abrupt/uncited introduction of retweets and link creation, and weak motivational narrative.  
- Overall assessment: interesting idea but falls short—OK/marginal due to weak justification of problem, missing baselines, ambiguous results, and presentation flaws.
