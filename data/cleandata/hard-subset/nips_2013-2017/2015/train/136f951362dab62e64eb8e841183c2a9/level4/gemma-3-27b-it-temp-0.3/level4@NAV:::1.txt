The paper introduces a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendations, grounded in modeling user activity, and distinguishes itself by explicitly addressing the challenge of evolving user preferences. It also proposes a method for predicting a user’s next return time. Learning is facilitated by an optimization algorithm developed by the authors. The experimental evaluation demonstrates the efficiency of the learning process on a large-scale synthetic dataset, alongside results on two real-world datasets of limited size.

However, the prevalence of time-varying user preferences warrants further investigation. A deeper analysis of the experimental datasets could substantiate this claim. Specifically, it would be valuable to understand the nature of this variation – is it primarily driven by the introduction of new popular items, or are other patterns at play? 

The motivation behind predicting user return times requires clarification. Establishing the practical utility of this prediction – how a company might leverage this information – would strengthen the contribution. Furthermore, several straightforward baseline methods for predicting return time were not considered, such as utilizing the last observed time delay or the most frequent time delay. Given the inherent difficulty of predicting precise return times, a simplification of the task might be beneficial. For instance, categorizing future delays into intervals (e.g., short, medium, long, very long) could offer a more tractable and potentially more impactful prediction target.

Regarding the experimental results, the magnitude of performance improvement over SVD is unclear. Quantifying this lift – is it substantial (an order of magnitude) or incremental? – and considering its practical significance from a user perspective is crucial. A comparison against a popularity-based recommendation strategy, updated regularly, would also provide valuable context, as this often represents a strong baseline.

Minor points:

1) There is a typographical error on page 3, last paragraph: “and is abel to capture” should read “and is able to capture.”
2) The introduction of retweets and link creation in social networks in the first paragraph of section 2 lacks supporting references and creates an abrupt shift in context. Rephrasing this section is recommended. The paper currently lacks a strong articulation of the relevance and potential impact of addressing both time-sensitive recommendations and user return time prediction. Several baselines and analyses are missing (as detailed above). Overall, the paper is currently assessed as OK/marginal.
