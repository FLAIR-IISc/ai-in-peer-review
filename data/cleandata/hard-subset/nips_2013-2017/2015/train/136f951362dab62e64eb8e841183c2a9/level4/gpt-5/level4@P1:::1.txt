Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models recurrent user activities with low-rank Hawkes processes. For each user–item pair, the event sequence is modeled by a self-exciting point process whose intensity combines a baseline (capturing long-term preference) and a history-dependent triggering kernel (capturing short-term recency effects). To generalize across sparse user–item interactions, the model ties parameters across pairs via low-rank structure on the base-intensity and self-excitation matrices, enforced through nuclear-norm penalties.

The authors formulate parameter estimation as a convex problem with nonnegativity and nuclear-norm constraints, and solve it using a new algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for the nonnegativity-constrained block) and conditional gradient (for the nuclear-norm-regularized block). They prove an O(1/t) convergence rate and show an equivalence between a penalized auxiliary-variable formulation and the original problem when the penalty parameter exceeds a threshold. The learned model supports two tasks: (a) time-aware item recommendation by ranking items via current intensity, and (b) next-return-time prediction using the conditional intensity with Ogata’s thinning for sampling.

Empirically, the paper reports results on one large synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability and on two real datasets of modest size. Baselines include Poisson processes, STiC-style survival/HMM methods for return-time prediction, SVD-based collaborative filtering, and tensor factorization with Poisson regression. The proposed method reportedly achieves superior performance on both ranking and return-time prediction.

Relative to prior work at NIPS and elsewhere: the paper connects low-rank recommendation with temporal point processes, addressing gaps in methods that model ratings but not continuous-time behavior [e.g., SVD/matrix factorization, time-varying factorizations, tensor methods], and in survival-analysis approaches to return-time prediction [Kapoor et al.], which assume i.i.d. inter-event gaps and do not generalize to unseen items.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated modeling choice: Hawkes processes capture self-excitation and recency, while the baseline handles persistent preferences.
- Principled low-rank transfer across user–item pairs via convex nuclear-norm regularization.
- An efficient optimization algorithm that neatly separates nonnegativity and nuclear-norm components, with a convergence guarantee and practical subroutines (projection and top-SVD calls).
- The framework jointly addresses two important tasks: time-aware ranking and return-time prediction.
- Scalability demonstrated on a large synthetic setup.

Weaknesses and questions
- Prevalence of time-varying preferences: The paper asserts that time-varying user preferences are widespread, but the real-data analysis does not quantify this. Please analyze the real datasets to show, for example, diurnal/weekly seasonality, popularity drift, and self-excitation effects. An ablation (e.g., setting the self-excitation α to zero) would help quantify the added value over a Poisson baseline.
- Motivation for return-time prediction: The contribution would be stronger with concrete, data-driven use cases. How exactly would a web company use return-time predictions (e.g., scheduling notifications, budgeting ad bids by time slots, churn prevention)? What are the operational objectives and constraints?
- Missing simple baselines for return-time prediction: Please add (i) last inter-event time, and (ii) most frequent inter-event time (mode) per user–item or per user. These often perform surprisingly well. Also consider a coarser, more actionable classification task (e.g., short/medium/long/very-long return windows) which may be both more stable and practically useful.
- Comparison to popularity-based recommendation: Popularity is a hard baseline, especially if refreshed daily. How does your time-aware ranking compare to recommending currently popular items? Please report this baseline. Relatedly, quantify the lift over SVD and Poisson in absolute and relative terms so readers can judge whether the improvement is modest or substantial and whether users would likely notice.
- Experimental scope: Real datasets are modest in size compared to the claimed scalability. It would be helpful to include larger real-world logs if available, or at least provide detailed runtime/memory profiling and wall-clock comparisons to baselines on the existing data.
- Clarity issues: The paper occasionally shifts context abruptly. In particular, in the first paragraph of Section 2 you start mentioning retweets and link creation in social networks without references; this was confusing and should be rephrased to maintain focus. More generally, the introduction should better motivate why solving these two tasks matters and how impact would be measured.

Quality
- The modeling and optimization components are technically sound, and the convexity and convergence claims are supported by clear derivations. The sampling-based return-time predictor is reasonable given the intractable expectation.
- However, the empirical section would benefit from stronger baselines (last/mode delay, popularity), clearer quantification of gains, and ablation studies to isolate contributions (baseline vs. self-excitation, kernel bandwidth sensitivity).

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but could be improved by tightening the motivation for return-time prediction, smoothing transitions (avoid abrupt shifts to social-network examples without references), and reporting effect sizes and confidence intervals for the empirical gains.

Originality
- The linkage between low-rank recommendation and Hawkes processes in a convex framework is novel and interesting. The algorithmic combination of proximal and conditional gradient under nonnegativity and nuclear norms is also a nice contribution.

Significance
- If the empirical gains over strong, practical baselines (dynamic popularity, last/mode delay) are significant, the work could meaningfully influence time-sensitive recommender systems. Current results suggest promise, but stronger baselines and clearer effect sizes are needed to assess impact.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and principled framework combining Hawkes processes with low-rank structure.
- Efficient optimization with theoretical guarantees.
- Addresses both time-aware ranking and return-time prediction.

Cons:
- Insufficient motivation and analysis of the prevalence and drivers of time variation.
- Missing simple, strong baselines for return-time and popularity-based ranking.
- Lack of ablation studies and effect-size reporting; unclear practical significance of improvements.
- Real-data experiments are modest in scale relative to claims.

Minor comments
- Typo: last paragraph on page 3, “abel” → “able”.
- Please add references when introducing examples such as retweets and link creation, and ensure the narrative stays aligned with the two main tasks.
- Consider reporting calibration and Q–Q plots for the return-time models to support the time-change validation.

Overall, this is an interesting and potentially impactful paper, but in its current form I would consider it OK/marginal. Addressing the missing baselines, strengthening motivation and analysis of temporal effects, and quantifying lift more clearly would substantially improve it.
