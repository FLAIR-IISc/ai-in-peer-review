This paper proposes a novel method for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities, distinguishing itself from existing methods by addressing time-varying user preferences. The paper also introduces a model for predicting the next return time of a user. The authors propose an efficient optimization algorithm for learning the model parameters. The experimental section presents results on one large-scale synthetic dataset to demonstrate efficiency and on two real-world datasets of modest size.

While the paper addresses an interesting problem, several aspects require clarification and further investigation:

First, the central premise that time-varying user preferences are a widespread problem needs stronger justification. An analysis of the real-world datasets used in the experiments would be valuable to substantiate this claim. If preferences do vary significantly over time, what are the underlying patterns? Is this variation primarily driven by external factors, such as newly popular items emerging, or does it reflect more complex, user-specific behavioral shifts?

Second, the motivation for predicting a user's next return time is not well-established. The paper would be strengthened by a clearer explanation of why this is a significant contribution. For instance, how could a web-based company practically leverage this prediction, and for what specific applications?

Third, the evaluation of the return-time prediction model is weakened by the absence of simple yet important baselines. For example, the model should be compared against predictions based on 1) the user's most recent return interval and 2) the user's most frequent return interval. Furthermore, predicting the exact return time is an inherently challenging task. A more practical and perhaps more tractable approach would be to frame this as a classification problem, such as predicting whether a user will return within a short (e.g., 1-5 days), medium (5-14 days), long (14-33 days), or very long (>33 days) interval.

Finally, regarding the experimental results for recommendations, it is difficult to gauge the practical significance of the performance improvement over the SVD baseline. Are the gains modest or substantial (e.g., an order of magnitude)? From a user's perspective, would the improved recommendations be perceptibly better? The experiments are also missing a comparison to a strong baseline of recommending globally popular items, particularly a dynamic version where popularity is recalculated daily. This is often a difficult baseline to outperform in practice.

Minor Comments:
1.  Typo in the last paragraph on page 3: "and is abel to capture" should be "and is able to capture".
2.  The first paragraph of Section 2 abruptly mentions retweets and link creation in social networks without context or references. This shift is confusing and should be rephrased for clarity.

In summary, the paper addresses two problems—time-sensitive recommendations and user return time prediction—but does not sufficiently motivate their relevance or potential impact. Key baselines and analyses are missing from the evaluation, which makes it difficult to assess the contributions. For these reasons, the paper is marginal in its current form.
