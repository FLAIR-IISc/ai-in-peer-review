- Proposes a novel framework connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns for time-sensitive recommendation.
- Addresses two problems: recommending the most desirable item at the right moment and predicting users’ next return time.
- Builds on prior temporal point process and recommendation work (extending Kapoor et al.) by incorporating low-rank structure to scale to many user-item pairs.
- Key innovation: can generalize to recommend new items, overcoming a limitation of prior return-time models.
- Strength: technically sound convex optimization formulation that jointly handles recommendation and return-time prediction.
- Strength: efficient algorithm combining proximal and conditional gradient methods with O(1/t) convergence, scalable to millions of pairs.
- Strength: strong empirical performance on synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) with comparisons to relevant baselines.
- Strength: clear theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees for the optimization algorithm.
- Weakness: motivation for predicting exact returning times is weak—needs concrete operational examples beyond generic ad bidding.
- Weakness: lacks analysis demonstrating that time-varying user preferences are widespread in the used datasets.
- Weakness: missing comparisons to simple baselines for return-time prediction (e.g., last delay, most frequent delay).
- Weakness: does not address whether categorical delay prediction (short/medium/long) might be more practical than exact time prediction.
- Quality: overall technically rigorous with comprehensive empirical results and solid theoretical grounding.
- Clarity: generally well-organized and clear algorithm descriptions, but some confusing references (e.g., “retweets and link creation”) and sparse experimental setup details.
- Originality: novel and clearly differentiated contribution by linking self-exciting point processes with low-rank recommendation models.
- Significance: potential practical importance for timing-aware recommender systems, but impact claims need stronger, concrete real-world examples.
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, scalable, rigorous analysis, and relevant problem formulation.
- Arguments against acceptance: needs stronger motivation for exact return-time prediction, additional temporal-pattern analysis, and simpler baseline comparisons.
- Overall: solid and acceptable contribution that would improve with revisions to motivation, analysis, and baseline experiments.
