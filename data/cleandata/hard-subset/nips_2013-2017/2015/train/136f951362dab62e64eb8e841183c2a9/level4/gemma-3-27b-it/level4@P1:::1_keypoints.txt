- Proposes a novel time-sensitive recommendation method linking self-exciting point processes with low-rank models and an efficient learning algorithm.
- Evaluated on a synthetic dataset (scalability) and two modest real-world datasets.
- Identifies a gap: many recommenders ignore temporal dynamics of user behavior.
- Claims temporal preference variation is important but lacks thorough empirical evidence quantifying how much preferences change over time.
- Suggests authors should analyze whether observed variations arise from external trends (popular items) or intrinsic changes in user tastes.
- Motivation for predicting next returning time should be stated earlier and justified with concrete use cases (e.g., ad bidding).
- Experimental setup should include simple baselines for return-time prediction (e.g., last observed delay, most frequent delay).
- Predicting exact return time may be unrealistic; consider framing as classification into short/medium/long/very long return windows.
- Results need clearer quantification of improvements over baselines (is the lift over SVD substantial or incremental?) and whether users would notice the difference.
- Missing comparison to a popularity-based recommendation baseline, which is a strong practical baseline.
- Minor comments: fix typo "abel" → "able" (p.3); rephrase Section 2 intro on retweets/link creation, which is abrupt and lacks context.
- Overall: OK/marginal paper—novel and promising but needs stronger motivation, baselines, and more nuanced empirical analysis.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel modeling approach, efficient algorithm with convergence guarantees, demonstrated synthetic scalability, tackles an important temporal-dynamics problem.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited real-world validation on small datasets, lack of strong baselines, weak justification for return-time prediction, insufficient evidence that preferences truly vary over time.
