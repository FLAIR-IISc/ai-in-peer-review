This paper introduces a novel method for time-sensitive recommendations driven by user activity, explicitly addressing the challenge of evolving user preferences over time. The work also proposes a model for predicting a user’s next return time to the system. Learning is facilitated by an efficient optimization algorithm developed by the authors. The experimental evaluation utilizes one synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability and two real-world datasets of limited size.

The central claim regarding the prevalence of time-varying user preferences requires further justification. While the paper posits this as a key motivation, it would be strengthened by an analysis of the experimental datasets themselves to quantify the extent of this variation. Specifically, exploring *why* preferences change – is it primarily due to the introduction of new popular items, or are there other discernible patterns? – would be valuable.

The motivation for predicting a user’s next return time is currently unclear. Before presenting this as a contribution, the paper should articulate the practical applications and benefits of such a prediction for a web-based service. Providing concrete examples of how a company might utilize this information would significantly enhance the paper's impact.

Regarding the return time prediction task, the proposed approach appears ambitious. While predicting the precise return time is challenging, I suggest refocusing on a more manageable, yet still informative, task: classifying return time into discrete intervals. For example, predicting whether a user will return within a short timeframe (1-5 days), medium timeframe (5-14 days), long timeframe (14-33 days), or very long timeframe (>33 days) may be more feasible and provide actionable insights. Furthermore, several simple baselines for return time prediction were not considered, specifically: 1) using the user’s last observed time delay as the prediction, and 2) predicting the most frequently observed time delay for that user. 

The experimental results are difficult to interpret regarding the magnitude of improvement over SVD. Is the performance lift substantial (e.g., an order of magnitude improvement), or more modest? The paper needs to clarify whether the observed differences are likely to be perceptible to users.  Importantly, a comparison to a "recommend popular items" baseline is crucial. Recommending popular items – especially if these are updated daily – is a surprisingly strong baseline that is often difficult to surpass in practice. Establishing performance relative to this baseline would provide a more realistic assessment of the proposed method’s effectiveness.

Several minor issues should be addressed:

1.  A typographical error appears in the last paragraph of page 3: “and is abel to capture” should be “and is able to capture.”
2.  In the first paragraph of Section 2, the discussion of retweets and link creation in social networks lacks appropriate citations. The sudden shift in context is jarring and should be either removed or properly referenced.
3.  The paper lacks a compelling motivation for addressing both time-sensitive recommendations *and* return time prediction. It’s unclear why these problems are important or how solving them will have a significant impact. The omission of several key baselines and analyses (as detailed above) further weakens the evaluation. 

Overall, this paper is acceptable but currently marginal. Addressing the concerns raised regarding motivation, baseline comparisons, task simplification, and result interpretation would significantly improve its quality.
