This paper addresses the important and relatively underexplored problem of time-sensitive recommendation. The authors tackle two central questions: (1) how to recommend the most relevant item at a specific moment, and (2) how to predict when a user will next return to a service. The core contribution is a novel framework that models recurrent user activities by connecting self-exciting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models. Specifically, each user-item interaction sequence is modeled as a Hawkes process, whose parameters (a baseline intensity and a self-excitation magnitude) are organized into matrices. To handle sparsity and generalize to unobserved user-item pairs, these matrices are assumed to be low-rank, a concept familiar from collaborative filtering.

The authors propose a convex formulation for estimating the model parameters, regularized by the nuclear norm to enforce the low-rank assumption. To solve this optimization problem efficiently, they develop a novel algorithm that combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, which avoids costly full SVDs at each iteration and achieves a convergence rate of O(1/t). The proposed model is evaluated on both synthetic and three real-world datasets, demonstrating superior performance on both the item recommendation and return-time prediction tasks compared to several relevant baselines.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and principled approach to time-sensitive recommendation. The combination of self-exciting point processes (to model the temporal dynamics of individual user-item interactions) and low-rank models (to share statistical strength across all users and items) is elegant and well-motivated. This work moves beyond traditional static rating prediction and addresses a more nuanced and practical problem that is highly significant for modern web services, from e-commerce to healthcare.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The formulation of the problem as a convex optimization task with nuclear norm regularization is well-founded. The development of a custom, efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal and conditional gradient methods is a significant technical contribution that may be of independent interest. The authors provide a theoretical convergence analysis for their algorithm, strengthening the paper's contributions.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The background on temporal point processes is helpful, and the motivation for the model is well-explained. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering both synthetic data (to verify model properties and scalability) and diverse real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II). The evaluation on two distinct tasks—item ranking and return-time prediction—effectively demonstrates the utility of the proposed model. The comparison against appropriate baselines, including Poisson factorization and tensor-based methods, is convincing. The analysis in Figure 3, which uses the time-change theorem to show the model's superior fit to the underlying event data, is a particularly strong piece of evidence supporting the modeling choices.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Practicality of Theoretical Guarantees:** A minor weakness lies in the connection between the theoretical results and the practical implementation of the optimization. Theorem 1 introduces a threshold `ρ*` that guarantees the equivalence of the original and alternative optimization formulations (Eq. 4 and 5). However, the definition of `ρ*` depends on the optimal solutions, making it non-constructive for selecting a value for `ρ` *a priori*. The authors rightly note that they tune `ρ` as a hyperparameter in the experiments. While this is standard practice, it slightly diminishes the practical impact of Theorem 1. It would strengthen the paper if the authors could include a brief discussion on the model's sensitivity to the choice of `ρ` or provide some practical intuition on setting this parameter.

2.  **Comparison with Additional Baselines:** The set of experimental baselines is reasonable, but the evaluation could be made even stronger by including a comparison to other prominent time-aware recommendation models. For example, latent factor models that explicitly incorporate temporal dynamics, such as timeSVD++ (a key model from the Netflix Prize competition by Koren), are strong and widely-recognized baselines in this area. Comparing against such a method would provide a more complete assessment of the proposed model's standing relative to the state-of-the-art in time-aware collaborative filtering.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the field of recommender systems. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by strong empirical evidence. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the core contributions of the work. I recommend acceptance.
