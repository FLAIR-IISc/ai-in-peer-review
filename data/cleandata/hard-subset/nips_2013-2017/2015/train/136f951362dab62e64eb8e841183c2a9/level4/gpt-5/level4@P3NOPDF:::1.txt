Summary
The paper proposes a method for time-sensitive recommendation that models time-varying user preferences and also predicts a user’s next return time. The learning procedure is based on an efficient optimization algorithm designed to scale. Experiments are conducted on one large synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability and on two real, modest-sized datasets to demonstrate effectiveness.

Strengths
- Addresses temporal dynamics in user preferences, a relevant and challenging aspect of recommendation.
- Jointly models recommendation and return-time prediction, which could be valuable if the second task is well-motivated and actionable.
- Claims an efficient optimization approach and demonstrates scalability on synthetic data.

Weaknesses and questions
- Prevalence and nature of temporal drift are not demonstrated. The paper claims users’ preferences vary over time but does not analyze the real datasets to quantify the frequency, magnitude, or structure of this drift. Are changes driven by global popularity shifts (e.g., new releases) or by user-specific cycles and routines? Without such evidence, the core motivation remains speculative.
- The motivation for predicting the next return time is unclear. The paper lists return-time prediction as a contribution without explaining concrete applications. How would a web company operationalize these predictions (e.g., notification timing, content scheduling, cache prefetching, churn intervention, ad pacing)? Clarifying this is important to justify the additional task and modeling complexity.
- Missing simple baselines for return-time prediction. At a minimum, include:
  1) Predict the last observed inter-arrival time.
  2) Predict the mode (most frequent) inter-arrival time for the user, or a global/user-segment mode.
  3) A simple recency-/seasonality-aware baseline (e.g., weekday/weekend, hour-of-day).
- Task formulation for return time may be unnecessarily hard. Exact return-time regression is often noisy; a more stable and actionable formulation is to predict buckets (e.g., short: 1–5 days, medium: 5–14, long: 14–33, very long: >33). This would also make evaluation clearer and potentially more useful for operations (e.g., when to re-engage a user).
- Experimental clarity and baselines for recommendation are insufficient. The paper compares to SVD but does not convey the magnitude or practical significance of the lift. Please quantify absolute and relative gains with confidence intervals and/or significance tests, and discuss whether the differences are likely to be perceptible to users. A popularity-based recommender (recomputed daily) is a strong and widely used baseline that appears to be missing; beating it consistently would strengthen the claims.
- Dataset limitations. The real datasets are modest in size and may not stress-test the method’s scalability or robustness to real-world nonstationarity. The synthetic experiment demonstrates scaling but does not substitute for large, real temporal datasets with known drift.
- Positioning and related work. The introduction shifts to retweets and link creation without context or citations, which is confusing. The paper should more clearly situate itself relative to time-aware recommendation, temporal matrix/tensor factorization, and survival/hazard models for return-time or inter-arrival prediction.

Requested analyses and experiments
- Empirical evidence of temporal drift in the real datasets:
  - Per-user and global drift measures over time (e.g., changes in top-k item distributions, time-varying NDCG when training on old vs recent data).
  - Decomposition into global popularity shifts vs user-specific changes.
- Clear use cases and metrics for return-time prediction:
  - Define operational thresholds (e.g., when to trigger a notification).
  - Evaluate both regression (if retained) and a bucketed classification variant; report calibration where relevant.
  - Include simple baselines: last delay, modal delay (user/global/segment), and a seasonality-aware baseline.
- Recommendation baselines and reporting:
  - Add popularity and time-aware baselines (e.g., time-decayed MF) where appropriate.
  - Report absolute metrics (e.g., Recall@k, NDCG@k, MAP), relative improvements, and statistical significance.
  - Provide ablations isolating the impact of temporal components vs static factors and the coupling (if any) between recommendation and return-time modules.
- Robustness and practicality:
  - Sensitivity to time granularity and window size.
  - Cold-start analysis for new users/items and sparse-history users.
  - Training/inference time and memory use on real datasets; include learning curves.

Clarity and presentation
- Tighten the motivation: clearly state why time-varying preferences matter in your domains and how much they change in practice (with evidence).
- Before listing return-time prediction as a contribution, motivate its product relevance (e.g., engagement, resource allocation) and define success criteria.
- Improve the flow in Section 2: avoid abrupt context switches (e.g., to retweets/link creation) unless directly tied to the problem and supported by references.

Minor comments
- Typo on page 3 (last paragraph): “and is abel to capture” → “and is able to capture.”
- In Section 2’s first paragraph, references are needed when mentioning retweets and link creation; rephrase to maintain a consistent narrative focused on time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important direction—temporal dynamics in recommendation—and proposes a seemingly scalable method. However, the current submission lacks (a) empirical evidence that the targeted temporal effects are prevalent and user-specific on the evaluated datasets, (b) clear motivation and actionable framing for return-time prediction, and (c) several strong but simple baselines and analyses that would calibrate the claimed gains. As written, the contribution is promising but not yet fully substantiated. I would view this as a marginal/weak reject in its current form. With stronger motivation, clearer evaluation against strong baselines (including popularity), added analyses of temporal drift, and a more actionable formulation of return-time prediction, the work could become significantly more compelling.
