This paper proposes a point process collaborative filtering model to capture the temporal and recurrent structure between users and items. Specifically, for each user-item pair, the event density is modeled as the sum of a constant intensity and a self-exciting intensity that depends on past events. Two sets of intensities are computed using latent vectors for users and items, respectively. The authors derive an optimization algorithm for parameter estimation and demonstrate the advantages of their method over commonly used alternatives through empirical studies.

However, the experimental studies could be strengthened. For instance, a sensible baseline would be a simple ensemble of STiC [12] and SVD. Since the authors argue that [12, 11] are unable to recommend entirely new items – a limitation SVD can address – it would be valuable to compare the proposed method against this ensemble. Additionally, regarding the triggering kernel’s bandwidth parameter σ, the paper would benefit from detailing how it was set and assessing the sensitivity of the results to variations in σ. Furthermore, the paper lacks details on how other important hyperparameters were chosen for both baseline methods and the proposed method, such as the dimensionality of the latent vectors. 

Providing more details about the characteristics of the two real-world datasets would also be helpful. Given the paper’s focus on temporal structure, discussing simple statistics like the number of recurring events per user/item pair and the irregularity of the temporal patterns would enhance the analysis.

**Quality:** The overall quality is reasonably good, but there’s room for improvement. The technical aspects appear sound, but the lack of detail in the experiments and parameter tuning weakens the claims.

**Clarity:** The paper’s presentation could be improved in terms of language, with attention to typos and grammatical errors. While understandable, refining the writing would enhance readability.

**Originality:** The proposed method represents an interesting and novel combination of point process models and latent factor models. Connecting these approaches is a valuable contribution.

**Significance:** Both the problem addressed and the proposed method have practical value. Accurately modeling temporal user behavior is crucial for improving recommender systems and offers potential benefits for various applications, including personalized marketing and healthcare. The model's ability to predict both relevant items and returning times demonstrates its potential impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of point process and low-rank models.
*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in recommendation systems.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Efficient optimization algorithm with theoretical convergence guarantees.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, including a stronger baseline (e.g., ensemble of STiC and SVD).
*   Insufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning.
*   Limited analysis of dataset characteristics.
*   Writing quality could be improved for better clarity.
