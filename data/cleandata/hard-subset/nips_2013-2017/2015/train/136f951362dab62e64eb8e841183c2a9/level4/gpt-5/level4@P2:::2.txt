Summary
The paper introduces a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models recurrent user–item interactions via self-exciting Hawkes processes with low-rank structure. For each user–item pair, the intensity is the sum of a baseline rate and a history-dependent component driven by past events through a triggering kernel (e.g., exponential). To transfer information to unobserved pairs and enable recommendation of new items, the baseline and self-excitation parameters are organized as nonnegative low-rank matrices with nuclear-norm regularization, yielding a convex estimation problem. The authors develop an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient (for nuclear norms), achieving O(1/t) convergence and scaling to millions of pairs and hundreds of millions of events. They evaluate two tasks: (1) ranking items at a given time based on instantaneous intensity, and (2) predicting users’ next return time via sampling from the conditional density. Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II) show improvements over Poisson baselines, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization.

Relation to prior work: Compared with survival analysis approaches for return-time prediction (Kapoor et al.), this work captures self-excitation and allows generalization to unseen user–item pairs. It is related to time-aware factorization (Koren), tensor models for temporal recommendation, and learning Hawkes processes and kernels (Du et al.; Zhou et al.), but differs by coupling Hawkes intensities with low-rank structure and providing a convex estimator with a scalable solver.

Strengths
- Unified, principled approach to time-aware recommendation and return-time prediction using point processes.
- Convex formulation via nuclear-norm regularization with nonnegativity, and a practical algorithm that avoids full SVDs.
- Theoretical convergence analysis and clear scalability claims with large-scale experiments.
- Demonstrates applicability across diverse domains (music, e-commerce, clinical).
- Sensible use of time-rescaling diagnostics supporting the Hawkes assumption.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Baselines: Since STiC does not recommend new items, a simple, strong baseline would be an ensemble that combines STiC (for seen items/return-time) with SVD (for unseen items). This would directly test the value of the proposed unified model over a pragmatic two-model pipeline.
- Hyperparameters: Please specify how you choose key hyperparameters (for both your method and baselines), including the latent dimensions for SVD/tensor baselines, nuclear-norm weights (λ, β), and penalty ρ. Report sensitivity curves or ranges. Since Theorem 1 relies on ρ > ρ*, practical guidance on ρ and robustness to its choice would strengthen the claims.
- Kernel bandwidth: Clarify how the exponential kernel bandwidth σ is set (global vs per user/item; cross-validated vs fixed) and provide sensitivity analysis. Given σ directly shapes recency effects, its tuning is crucial.
- Dataset characterization: Provide descriptive statistics on the real datasets that relate to temporal complexity, e.g., distribution of events per user–item pair, recurrence rates, inter-event-time dispersion/irregularity, and cold-start proportions. This will help interpret where Hawkes modeling yields gains.
- Ablations: Include ablations that isolate (i) Hawkes vs Poisson (self-excitation off), and (ii) low-rank vs unconstrained or diagonal structure, to quantify which components drive the improvements.
- Metrics and evaluation details: For the recommendation task, report standard top-k metrics (Recall@k/Precision@k/NDCG@k) in addition to rank of the ground-truth item. For return-time prediction, complement MAE with calibration checks (e.g., PIT/time-rescaling across users) and consider censored-evaluation protocols if any events are truncated.
- Clarity and presentation: Several typos/grammar issues and notation inconsistencies (e.g., α vs A, λ vs Λ0) hinder readability. A careful pass to standardize notation and improve language would help. Clarify the sampling-based return-time estimator and number of samples used.

Quality
The technical approach is generally sound: a convex objective with well-defined gradients, a solver that leverages proximal updates for nonnegativity and Frank–Wolfe for nuclear norms, and a convergence guarantee. However, the practical equivalence claim between the constrained and penalized formulations depends on an unknown threshold ρ*. Since ρ is tuned, reporting robustness to ρ and any observed dual gaps would bolster the empirical validity of this equivalence. Experimental comparisons are broad but can be strengthened with the baseline ensemble, ablations, and richer metrics.

Clarity
Overall organization is clear, but the manuscript would benefit from improved grammar, consistent notation, and more complete experimental details (hyperparameters, dataset stats, sensitivity analyses). The algorithm description is helpful; adding pseudocode annotations and clarifying the line-search step would further improve accessibility.

Originality
The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank recommendation with a convex estimator is a novel and interesting combination. The optimization scheme that mixes proximal and conditional gradients under nonnegativity and nuclear norms is also a noteworthy contribution.

Significance
The ability to jointly recommend “what” and “when” is practically important, and the method appears scalable and adaptable (e.g., to additional context). Assuming the experimental gaps are addressed, the work is likely to influence time-aware recommendation and event modeling.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, unified model for time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction.
- Convex formulation with a scalable solver and theoretical guarantees.
- Strong performance across multiple domains; meaningful diagnostics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing strong pragmatic baselines (e.g., STiC+SVD ensemble) and ablations to isolate contributions.
- Limited transparency on hyperparameter selection and sensitivity (σ, λ/β, ρ).
- Incomplete characterization of datasets and evaluation metrics, limiting interpretability of gains.

Questions for authors
- How are σ, λ, β, ρ selected? Please provide sensitivity analyses and practical guidance.
- What latent dimensions were used for all baselines, and how were they tuned?
- How does performance change when self-excitation is disabled, or when replacing nuclear norms with fixed-rank factorizations?
- Can you report top-k recommendation metrics and training/runtime comparisons to ADMM-based methods?
- How does the model handle cold-start users/items, and what are the observed effects on performance?
