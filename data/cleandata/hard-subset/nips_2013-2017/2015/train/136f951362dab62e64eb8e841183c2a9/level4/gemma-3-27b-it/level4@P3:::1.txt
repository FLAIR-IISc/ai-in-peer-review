This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation systems, connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions. It addresses two key questions: recommending the most relevant item at the right time and predicting a user's next return to a service. The authors present a convex formulation of the problem and an efficient optimization algorithm with provable convergence guarantees, demonstrating strong performance on both synthetic and real datasets. 

This work builds upon a growing body of research recognizing the limitations of traditional recommendation systems that primarily focus on static user preferences. Recent NIPS papers, such as those exploring contextual bandits (e.g., [reference a relevant NIPS contextual bandit paper]) and dynamic graph embeddings (e.g., [reference a relevant NIPS dynamic graph embedding paper]), also highlight the importance of incorporating temporal dynamics. The use of point processes for modeling user behavior is related to work by Kapoor et al. [12, 11], who employ survival analysis and hidden semi-Markov models for return time prediction. However, the proposed approach distinguishes itself by its ability to recommend *new* items and by leveraging a low-rank structure for scalability and generalization. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank matrix factorization is a novel contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of self-exciting point processes and low-rank models is a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide a convex formulation and demonstrate an efficient optimization algorithm with an O(1/t) convergence rate, crucial for handling large-scale datasets.
* **Scalability:** The algorithm is shown to scale to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate superior performance compared to several baselines.
* **Generality:** The formulation is flexible and can incorporate additional contextual information (user profiles, textual data, spatial features).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Motivation:** The initial motivation for predicting user return times feels somewhat underdeveloped. A more concrete example of how a web company could benefit from this prediction (beyond ad bidding) would strengthen the paper.
* **Baseline Comparison:** While several baselines are included, the inclusion of simpler baselines for return time prediction (e.g., last time delay, most frequent time delay) is missing. These provide important context for evaluating the performance gains. Furthermore, a comparison to simply recommending the most popular items at any given time would be valuable.
* **Performance Significance:** The experimental results should clearly articulate the *magnitude* of the performance improvements. Is the gain statistically significant? Would a user perceive a noticeable difference in recommendation quality?
* **Dataset Analysis:** A preliminary analysis of the real datasets to demonstrate the existence of significant time-varying user preferences would bolster the paper’s central premise. It's unclear if observed performance gains are simply due to capturing inherent popularity trends or genuinely identifying nuanced temporal dynamics.
* **Clarity (Minor):** The shift in context in the first paragraph of Section 2 (mentioning social networks) is jarring and could be rephrased for clarity.  Also, a typo exists on page 3 ("and is abel to capture" should be "and is able to capture").



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to a challenging problem in recommender systems.
* The theoretical results and empirical validation are strong.
* The algorithm’s scalability makes it applicable to real-world scenarios.
* The work has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in time-sensitive recommendation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of stronger motivation and baseline comparisons weakens the paper's overall impact.
* The performance gains are not convincingly demonstrated to be practically significant.
* The assumption of time-varying user preferences could benefit from further justification through dataset analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a novel contribution. While the motivation could be strengthened and the experimental analysis refined with additional baselines and a clearer articulation of performance gains, the technical soundness and theoretical guarantees are compelling. I believe the paper is currently **above average/borderline accept**. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the baseline comparison and the discussion of performance significance, would significantly increase its chances of acceptance.  I recommend requesting a rebuttal period where the authors can address these points.
