The paper proposes a novel approach for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities, addressing the problem of time-varying user preferences. Unlike existing methods, this approach explicitly considers the temporal behavior and recurrent activities of users. The authors also tackle the prediction of the next returning time of a user, a problem that has received limited attention in the literature.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of existing recommendation algorithms. They argue that traditional user feedback signals, such as ratings and click-through rates, are often ineffective in representing real user engagement. Instead, they propose using the temporal patterns of user activities as a more relevant metric for evaluating user satisfaction.

The authors present a comprehensive framework that combines self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture the recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption pairs. They develop an efficient algorithm that scales up to problems with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both synthetic and real datasets.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to incorporate additional context information, such as user profiles, textual features, and spatial features, into the recommendation model. This allows for a more personalized and context-aware recommendation system.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors could have provided more analysis on the real data sets to support their claim that time-varying user preferences are a widespread phenomenon. Additionally, the motivation for predicting the next returning time of a user could be more clearly explained. How would a web company use this prediction, and what benefits would it bring?

The experimental section could also be improved. The authors could have included more baselines, such as the last time delay and the most occurred time delay, to provide a more comprehensive comparison. Furthermore, predicting the actual returning time of the user may be too challenging, and the authors could have focused on a simpler task, such as predicting the delay category (short, medium, long, or very long).

Overall, the paper presents a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendations and returning time prediction. While there are some limitations, the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both synthetic and real datasets. With some additional analysis and improvements to the experimental section, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendations and returning time prediction.
* The authors provide a comprehensive framework that combines self-exciting point processes and low-rank models.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both synthetic and real datasets.
* The paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The authors could have provided more analysis on the real data sets to support their claim.
* The motivation for predicting the next returning time of a user could be more clearly explained.
* The experimental section could be improved with more baselines and a simpler task.
* The paper may benefit from additional analysis and improvements to the experimental section.

Rating: 7/10
