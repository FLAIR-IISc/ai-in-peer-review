This paper presents a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation by connecting low-rank matrix factorization with Hawkes processes. The authors propose an algorithm that can be efficiently solved and demonstrate improved performance compared to existing methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Quality:** Overall, the paper takes a principled approach to modeling user behavior using stochastic processes. However, I have a few comments regarding the theoretical aspects and experimental evaluation. 

Regarding the theoretical analysis, there’s a question about Eq. (8) and Theorem 2. Specifically, over what parameter is the maximum taken in Eq. (8)? If it's over ρ, could ρ* be unbounded? Furthermore, Theorem 2 wasn't utilized when choosing ρ in the experiments, diminishing its practical impact in this work.

Concerning the experimental setup, while the chosen baselines are reasonable, incorporating SVD++ [17] – a strong performer and winner of the Netflix challenge – would provide a more comprehensive comparison and strengthen the evaluation. However, I do not find the absence of SVD++ to be a critical flaw.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient detail to understand the proposed method and its implementation.

**Originality:** The work appears novel. Time-sensitive recommendation is a relatively recent and increasingly important problem, and the combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models represents a new direction in this area.

**Significance:** I believe time-sensitive recommendation has the potential to be impactful, and this paper presents a promising algorithm to address this challenge. The ability to capture temporal dynamics and predict future user behavior could lead to significant improvements in recommender systems.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
*   Strong empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Efficient algorithm with demonstrated scalability.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   Minor theoretical concerns regarding Eq. (8) and the application of Theorem 2.
*   Lack of comparison to a strong baseline such as SVD++.
