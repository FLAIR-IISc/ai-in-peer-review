This paper presents a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation and user return-time prediction. The core idea is to model the sequence of a user's interactions with an item as a self-exciting Hawkes process. To generalize across all user-item pairs and handle data sparsity, the parameters of these processes (the base intensity and the self-excitation term) are assumed to form low-rank matrices. This establishes an interesting connection between temporal point processes and low-rank models. The authors formulate parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization. To solve this, they propose an efficient algorithm that combines proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, providing a theoretical convergence analysis. The method is evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets for both item recommendation at a specific time and predicting the next interaction time.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and relatively under-explored problem of modeling the continuous-time dynamics of user activities for recommendation. The proposed connection between low-rank matrix models and multivariate Hawkes processes is novel and provides an elegant way to capture both long-term user preferences (base intensity) and short-term, history-dependent effects (self-excitation).
*   **Technical Quality:** The model is well-grounded in the theory of temporal point processes. The formulation as a convex optimization problem is a significant strength, ensuring a principled estimation procedure.
*   **Algorithmic Contribution:** The development of a scalable optimization algorithm that blends proximal and conditional gradient methods to handle non-negativity and nuclear norm constraints simultaneously is a solid contribution. The accompanying convergence analysis, demonstrating an O(1/t) rate, is rigorous and suggests the algorithm could be of independent interest for related problems.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation, model formulation, and algorithm are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Motivation and Problem Framing:** The motivation for the second task, predicting the exact user return time, could be strengthened. While the introduction mentions applications like ad bidding, it is not fully clear how a precise point estimate of return time would be used in practice. A discussion of alternative, perhaps more robust, formulations would be valuable. For instance, predicting a time interval (e.g., short, medium, long delay) might be more practical and easier to evaluate.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The set of baselines for the return-time prediction task is limited. It would be beneficial to compare against simpler heuristics that are often quite effective. For example: 1) predicting the next delay to be the same as the user's most recent inter-event delay, or 2) predicting the modal inter-event delay for that user-item pair.
    *   For the item recommendation task, the practical significance of the performance gains is difficult to assess from the reported metrics. It is unclear if the improvement in rank over baselines like SVD would translate to a noticeable difference for the end-user. The evaluation would be much stronger with the inclusion of a time-aware popularity baseline (e.g., recommending the most popular items within the last day or week), which is often a very competitive baseline in real-world systems.
*   **Dataset Analysis:** The central claim is that user preferences are time-varying. This claim could be substantiated with a preliminary analysis of the real-world datasets. Demonstrating how user activity patterns change over different timescales (e.g., time of day, day of week) would make the problem statement more compelling.

In summary, the paper introduces a novel and technically sound approach for time-sensitive recommendations. The core modeling idea and the proposed optimization algorithm are strong contributions. However, the work could be improved by strengthening the motivation and practical framing of the return-time prediction task, and by expanding the experimental evaluation to include more robust and practical baselines to better contextualize the significance of the results.
