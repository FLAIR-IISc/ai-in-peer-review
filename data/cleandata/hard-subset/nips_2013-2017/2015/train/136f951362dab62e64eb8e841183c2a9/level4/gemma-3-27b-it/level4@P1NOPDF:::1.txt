This paper proposes a novel method for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activity, differentiating itself from existing approaches by explicitly addressing the problem of time-varying user preferences. The paper also introduces a method for predicting a user’s next return time. Learning is performed using an efficient optimization algorithm developed by the authors. The experimental section presents results on one synthetic dataset to demonstrate the algorithm’s scalability, and on two real-world datasets of modest size.

However, the prevalence of rapidly time-varying user preferences warrants further investigation. It would be beneficial to analyze the real-world datasets used in the experiments to substantiate this claim. If such variation exists, understanding its root cause is crucial – is it primarily driven by the introduction of new popular items, or are there other underlying patterns?

The motivation for predicting a user’s next return time is currently unclear. Before positioning this as a key contribution, the paper should articulate *why* this prediction is valuable. How could a web company leverage this prediction, and for what specific applications? 

Regarding the return time prediction task, I believe several simple baselines were overlooked: 1) predicting the return time based on the last observed delay, and 2) predicting it based on the most frequently observed delay. Furthermore, predicting the precise return time appears quite challenging. I suggest focusing on a simpler, more tractable task, such as predicting whether a user will return within a short delay (1-5 days), medium delay (5-14 days), long delay (14-33 days), or very long delay (>33 days).

The experimental section lacks clarity regarding the significance of the performance gains over SVD. Is the improvement substantial (on the order of magnitude) or modest? Would a user perceive a noticeable difference in recommendation quality? Crucially, how do these recommendations compare to simply recommending popular items? The latter often represents a strong baseline, particularly when popularity is dynamically updated daily.

Minor comments:

1) Typo on page 3, last paragraph: "and is abel to capture" should be "and is able to capture."
2) The first paragraph of section 2 abruptly introduces concepts like retweets and link creation in social networks without any supporting references. This change in context is jarring and should be rephrased. More broadly, the paper introduces two problems – time-sensitive recommendations and return time prediction – without adequately motivating their relevance or outlining the potential impact of solving them. Several baselines and analyses are missing (as detailed above). 

Overall, this is an OK/marginal paper.
