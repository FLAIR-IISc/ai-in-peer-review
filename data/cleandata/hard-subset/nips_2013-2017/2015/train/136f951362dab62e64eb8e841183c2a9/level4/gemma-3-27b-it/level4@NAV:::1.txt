The paper introduces a novel approach to time-sensitive recommendations, leveraging user activity to address the challenge of evolving user preferences. A key distinction from existing work lies in its explicit consideration of these temporal shifts in preference, alongside a prediction component for user return times. The authors employ a newly proposed optimization algorithm to facilitate learning, and demonstrate its scalability on a synthetic dataset, with further evaluation on two real-world datasets of limited size.

However, the central premise regarding the prevalence of time-varying user preferences requires stronger justification. A more detailed analysis of the experimental datasets could substantiate this claim, and it would be beneficial to investigate the underlying drivers of this variability – is it primarily driven by the introduction of new items, or are other patterns present? 

Furthermore, the motivation for predicting user return times is unclear. The paper would benefit from a clear articulation of the utility of this prediction, outlining potential applications for a web-based service and the value it could provide. The evaluation of this component also appears incomplete. Simple baselines, such as predicting return time based on the previous delay or the most frequent delay, were not considered. Additionally, directly predicting precise return times may be overly ambitious; a more tractable formulation could involve categorizing delays into broader intervals (e.g., short, medium, long, very long), which might be more readily predicted.

Regarding the experimental results, the magnitude of performance improvement over SVD is not readily apparent. Clarification is needed to determine whether the gains are substantial enough to be practically significant and noticeable to users. A comparison to a popularity-based recommendation strategy, regularly updated, would also provide valuable context, as this represents a strong and frequently effective baseline.

Minor points:
1) A typographical error exists on page 3, last paragraph: “and is abel to capture” should be “and is able to capture.”
2) The introduction of retweets and link creation in the first paragraph of section 2 feels abrupt and lacks appropriate citation. This contextual shift is disorienting and should be rephrased. Overall, the paper lacks a compelling motivation for tackling the proposed problems and demonstrating their real-world impact. Several key baselines and analyses are missing (as detailed above). Despite these shortcomings, the paper is currently rated as OK/marginal.
