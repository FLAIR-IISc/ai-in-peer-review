- Proposes a novel time-sensitive recommendation framework linking self-exciting point processes (Hawkes) with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal user-item patterns.  
- Targets two tasks: recommending the most relevant item at the right time and predicting users' next return times.  
- Models interactions as a low-rank Hawkes process combining baseline user preferences and self-exciting effects of past interactions.  
- Develops an efficient optimization algorithm blending proximal and conditional gradient methods with O(1/t) convergence and scalability to millions of pairs.  
- Extension over prior work: can both predict return times and generalize to recommend new items, addressing limitations of methods like Kapoor et al.  
- Strength: well-structured theoretical foundation connecting point processes and recommendation systems.  
- Strength: technically sound optimization with strong convergence guarantees (Theorem 2).  
- Strength: empirical validation on synthetic and real datasets showing scalability and superior performance.  
- Strength: clear presentation of model and experiments.  
- Weakness: insufficient evidence that time-sensitive recommendation is broadly needed—authors should analyze real datasets to show meaningful time-varying preferences.  
- Weakness: underdeveloped practical motivation for return-time prediction—needs concrete use cases (e.g., how it aids ad bidding or marketing).  
- Weakness: experiments omit simple, practical baselines for return-time prediction (e.g., last delay, most frequent delay).  
- Weakness: recommendation performance lacks context—needs quantified comparisons to static baselines (SVD) and popular-item baselines and discussion of effect size on UX.  
- Weakness: evaluation uses absolute error for return-time prediction; predicting time intervals (short/medium/long) might be more meaningful.  
- Minor issues: typo ("abel" → "able") and unclear mention of "retweets and link creation in social networks" without context/references.  
- Overall: technically solid and novel convex formulation, but significance is limited by weak motivation and insufficient baselines; reviewer recommends borderline acceptance contingent on revisions.
