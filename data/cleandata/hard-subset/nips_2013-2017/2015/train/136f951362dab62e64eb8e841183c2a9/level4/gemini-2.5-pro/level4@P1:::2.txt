This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation by proposing a novel collaborative filtering model based on temporal point processes. Specifically, for each user-item pair, the model uses a Hawkes self-exciting process to capture recurrent activities. The intensity function for each pair is composed of a constant baseline intensity and a self-exciting term that depends on the history of past events. To generalize across users and items and handle sparsity, the baseline intensity and self-excitation parameters are represented by two low-rank matrices. This effectively combines a latent factor model with the point process framework. The authors formulate the parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem and develop an efficient algorithm that blends proximal and conditional gradient methods. The model is evaluated on two tasks: recommending the right item at a given time and predicting a user's next return time, showing improvements over several baselines on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The primary strength of this work lies in its novel combination of two powerful modeling paradigms: low-rank matrix factorization for collaborative filtering and Hawkes processes for modeling self-exciting temporal event sequences. This provides a principled way to incorporate the rich temporal dynamics of user activity, which is often ignored in standard recommendation models.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is of significant practical importance. Moving beyond static ratings to predict *when* a user might be interested in an item and when they will return is a valuable contribution for improving user engagement and optimizing marketing strategies. The proposed optimization algorithm, which combines proximal and conditional gradient methods to handle non-negativity and nuclear norm constraints, may also be of independent interest to the optimization community.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising results, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened in several areas.

*   **Baselines:** A key weakness is the choice of baselines. The authors correctly argue that a major limitation of STiC [11] is its inability to recommend new items. However, a natural and strong baseline would be a simple ensemble of STiC and a standard matrix factorization method like SVD. Such a hybrid model would handle both the temporal prediction for known items (via STiC) and the recommendation of new items (via SVD), providing a more challenging benchmark for the proposed unified model. It would be valuable to see a comparison against such an ensemble.

*   **Experimental Details:** The paper lacks crucial details regarding hyperparameter selection, which hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparisons. For instance, the bandwidth parameter σ for the exponential triggering kernel is a critical component of the Hawkes process model. The paper does not explain how this was set or provide a sensitivity analysis to show how robust the model's performance is to this choice. Similarly, details on the tuning of other important hyperparameters, such as the latent vector dimensions for both the proposed method and the factorization-based baselines, are omitted.

*   **Dataset Analysis:** The analysis of the real-world datasets would also benefit from more depth. Given the paper's focus on capturing temporal structure, providing basic statistics about the datasets' temporal characteristics would be very insightful. For example, statistics on the number of recurring events per user-item pair, the distribution of inter-event times, or measures of burstiness would help readers understand the nature of the problem and better appreciate the model's effectiveness.

*   **Clarity:** While the core ideas are clear, the manuscript could benefit from a thorough proofread to correct minor grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing to improve overall readability.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and valuable approach by combining point processes with latent factor models for recommendation. The problem is significant, and the proposed method is novel. However, the paper's contribution would be stronger with a more thorough experimental evaluation, including a more competitive baseline, a detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning, and a deeper characterization of the datasets.
