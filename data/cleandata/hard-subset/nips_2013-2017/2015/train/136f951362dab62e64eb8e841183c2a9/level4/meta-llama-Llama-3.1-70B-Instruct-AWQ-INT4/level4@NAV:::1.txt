The proposed method for time-sensitive recommendations, based on user activities, presents a novel approach by addressing the challenge of time-varying user preferences. This method differs from existing techniques and also explores the prediction of a user's next returning time. The authors utilize an efficient optimization algorithm for learning, which is demonstrated through experiments on one synthetic and two real data sets of varying sizes.

However, it would be beneficial to analyze the real data sets used in the experiments to substantiate the claim that time-varying user preferences are a widespread issue. If this phenomenon is indeed prevalent, it would be insightful to investigate whether the primary cause is the emergence of new popular content or other underlying patterns.

The motivation behind predicting a user's next returning time is unclear, and further justification is needed to establish this as a significant contribution of the paper. Additionally, it would be helpful to explain how a web company could utilize this prediction and what benefits it would provide.

Regarding the prediction of returning time, simple baselines such as the last time delay and the most occurred time delay were not evaluated. Moreover, predicting the exact returning time of a user may be overly ambitious. Instead, focusing on a more manageable task, such as predicting the delay before a user utilizes the service again (e.g., short: 1-5 days, medium: 5-14 days, long: 14-30 days, or very long: >30 days), might be more feasible.

The experimental section could be improved by providing a clearer understanding of the performance gain compared to SVD. Specifically, it would be helpful to know whether the improvement is substantial (order of magnitude) or modest, and whether the difference would be noticeable to the user. Furthermore, it would be valuable to compare these recommendations to recommending popular items, which can be a challenging baseline to surpass, especially when popular items are updated daily.

Minor comments include a typo in the last paragraph of page 3 ("and is abel to capture" should be "and is able to capture"). Additionally, the first paragraph of section 2 abruptly introduces retweets and link creation in social networks without proper context or references, which may cause confusion. Rephrasing this section would improve clarity.

Overall, while the paper addresses two problems – time-sensitive recommendations and returning time of user – it lacks clear motivation for why these issues are relevant and what impact solving them would have. Furthermore, several baselines and analyses are missing, which are discussed in more detail below. Nevertheless, the paper is generally acceptable, albeit marginal.
