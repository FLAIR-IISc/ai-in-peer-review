

Summary: This paper introduces a point process-based collaborative filtering model designed to capture temporal and recurrent user-item interactions. The model defines event intensity for each user-item pair as the sum of a constant base intensity and a self-exciting component driven by past events. Two sets of user and item latent vectors parameterize these intensity components, and the authors derive an optimization algorithm for parameter estimation. Empirical results demonstrate the method's superiority over several established baselines.  

However, the experimental evaluation requires strengthening. First, the authors claim that STiC [12] and SVD [13] cannot recommend completely new items (which SVD handles), yet they omit a simple baseline ensemble combining STiC and SVD. Comparing against this ensemble would better validate their claims. Second, the choice of bandwidth parameter σ for the γ function in the self-exciting process is unclear. The authors should report how σ is selected and assess sensitivity to its value. Third, critical hyperparameters—such as latent vector dimensions for both the proposed method and baselines—are not justified or detailed, undermining reproducibility.  

Additionally, the paper lacks sufficient characterization of the real-world datasets. Given the focus on temporal dynamics, it would be valuable to include statistics like the average recurrence rate per user-item pair and the irregularity of event timing (e.g., inter-event time distributions). These details would contextualize the model's applicability to the data.  

Quality: The technical foundation is sound but the experimental rigor is moderate.  

Clarity: The paper contains grammatical errors, inconsistent terminology (e.g., "event density" vs. standard "intensity"), and minor phrasing issues that impede readability. These should be addressed in revision.  

Originality: The integration of point processes with latent factor models presents a novel approach to modeling temporal user-item interactions.  

Significance: The problem addressed—capturing dynamic, recurrent preferences—is highly relevant for practical recommendation systems. The method offers tangible value, though its incremental contribution relative to ensemble baselines requires clearer justification.  

Overall, the core idea is promising but the empirical validation and presentation need substantial improvement to support the claimed advantages. Addressing the baseline, parameterization, and dataset characterization gaps would significantly strengthen the paper.
