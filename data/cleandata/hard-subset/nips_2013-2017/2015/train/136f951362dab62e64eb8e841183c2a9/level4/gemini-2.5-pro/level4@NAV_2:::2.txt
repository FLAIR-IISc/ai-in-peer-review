This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models. The goal is to address two key problems: recommending the right item at the right time and predicting a user's next return time to a service. The core idea is to model the sequence of consumption events for each user-item pair as a Hawkes process, where the event intensity is a combination of a constant baseline intensity and a self-exciting term that depends on the history of past events. To enable generalization and knowledge transfer across the sparse user-item matrix, the paper assumes that the matrices containing the baseline intensities (Λ0) and self-exciting coefficients (A) for all pairs are low-rank. The authors derive a convex optimization objective to estimate these matrices and develop an efficient algorithm that blends proximal and conditional gradient methods to solve it, providing a formal convergence analysis. Experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that the proposed model outperforms several state-of-the-art methods in both item recommendation and return-time prediction tasks.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles two important and practical problems in recommender systems that are often overlooked by traditional models focused on static ratings. The proposed method, which unifies Hawkes processes and low-rank collaborative filtering, is an elegant and novel combination of familiar techniques to capture the recurrent temporal dynamics of user behavior.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The formulation of the problem as a convex optimization task with nuclear norm regularization is well-justified. The development of a custom optimization algorithm that efficiently handles the non-negativity constraints and the non-smooth regularization is a notable contribution, especially with the provided theoretical convergence guarantees. This algorithm may be of independent interest for related matrix rank minimization problems.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors validate their model on a combination of synthetic and large-scale real-world datasets. The results convincingly show the superiority of the proposed approach over several relevant baselines, including static factorization models and temporal tensor-based methods. The analysis showing a better fit of the Hawkes process to the data compared to other survival models (Figure 3) provides strong support for the modeling assumptions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Missing Comparative Baseline:** The authors argue that a key limitation of related work like STiC [11, 12] is its inability to recommend completely new items. While this is a valid point, it also suggests a powerful and straightforward baseline is missing from the comparison: a simple ensemble of STiC and a standard matrix factorization model like SVD. Such an ensemble could use STiC for return-time prediction on known items and SVD for ranking new items. Comparing against this combined approach would provide a more compelling argument for the benefits of the proposed unified model.
*   **Lack of Experimental Details:** The paper could be strengthened by providing more details about the experimental setup, which is crucial for reproducibility. For instance:
    *   The self-exciting kernel `γ` contains a bandwidth parameter `σ`. It is unclear how this critical parameter was set. A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with `σ` would be highly valuable.
    *   Details on how other important hyperparameters were selected for both the proposed method (e.g., latent dimensions, regularization weights `λ` and `β`) and the baseline methods are not provided. This makes it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison.
*   **Insufficient Dataset Characterization:** Given the paper's focus on modeling temporal structure, the description of the real-world datasets would benefit from more detailed statistics. Providing information such as the distribution of recurring events per user-item pair, the average and variance of inter-event times, and other measures of temporal irregularity would better contextualize the results and further motivate the need for a sophisticated point process model.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** While the core ideas are well-conveyed, the manuscript would benefit from a thorough proofreading to address minor typos and grammatical errors, which would improve the overall clarity and presentation quality.
