The paper proposes a novel approach for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities, distinguishing itself from existing methods by addressing the issue of time-varying user preferences. Additionally, it tackles the prediction of the next returning time of a user, utilizing an efficient optimization algorithm developed by the authors. Experimental results are presented on one synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability and two real datasets of modest size.

However, it is unclear how widespread the problem of time-varying user preferences is. Analyzing the real datasets used in the experiments would help substantiate this claim. If the phenomenon is indeed prevalent, it would be beneficial to investigate whether this variation is primarily driven by factors such as the release of new popular content or other underlying patterns.

The motivation behind predicting the next returning time for a user is not explicitly stated. It would be helpful to provide a clear justification for this contribution and explain how a web company could utilize this prediction and what benefits it would bring.

Regarding the prediction of the next returning time, simple baselines such as the last time delay and the most frequently occurring time delay were not evaluated. Furthermore, predicting the exact returning time may be overly challenging. A more feasible approach might be to focus on a simpler task, such as predicting the delay before a user utilizes the service again, categorized into short (1-5 days), medium (5-14 days), long (14-33 days), or very long (>33 days) delays.

The experimental section could be improved by providing a clearer sense of the significance of the performance lift compared to SVD. It is unclear whether the improvement is substantial (order of magnitude) or modest, and whether the difference would be noticeable to the user. Additionally, a comparison with recommending popular items, which are often recalculated daily, would be a valuable baseline to include.

Minor comments include a typo in the last paragraph of page 3 ("and is abel to capture" should be "and is able to capture"). The first paragraph of section 2 abruptly introduces retweets and link creation in social networks without references, causing confusion. Rephrasing this section would improve clarity.

Overall, the paper addresses two problems – time-sensitive recommendations and returning time of user – without adequately motivating their relevance and potential impact. Several baselines and analyses are missing, as detailed above. Nevertheless, the paper is generally acceptable, albeit marginally so.
