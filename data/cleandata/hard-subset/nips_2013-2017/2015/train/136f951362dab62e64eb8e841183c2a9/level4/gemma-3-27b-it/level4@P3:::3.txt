This paper presents a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation, connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions. The authors address the important questions of recommending the most desirable item at the right moment and predicting a user’s next return time to a service. They propose a convex formulation, an efficient optimization algorithm with provable convergence guarantees, and demonstrate strong performance on both synthetic and real datasets. This work builds upon recent interest in modeling user behavior as temporal point processes, particularly extending the work of Kapoor et al. [12, 11], but importantly overcomes the limitations of those methods by enabling recommendations for *new* items.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates a technically sound approach to modeling user behavior. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a novel and principled way to address the problem of time-sensitive recommendations. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation and a convergence analysis for their proposed algorithm (Theorem 2). However, some aspects could be clarified. Specifically, the discussion surrounding Eq. (8) and Theorem 2 needs refinement. It’s unclear what parameter space the maximum is taken over when defining ρ*, and whether ρ* could be unbounded. Furthermore, while Theorem 2 establishes a convergence rate, it appears the authors did not utilize the theoretical guidance provided by this theorem when selecting ρ in their experiments, potentially diminishing the practical impact of the theoretical result. The choice of the exponential kernel for the triggering function (γ(t, ti)) is reasonable, but a brief discussion of why other kernel choices were not considered could strengthen the justification.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-explained, and the technical details are presented in a logical manner. The inclusion of Figure 1(a) provides a useful intuitive example of the problem. The notation is consistent, and the equations are generally easy to follow. However, the algorithmic description (Algorithm 1) could benefit from more detailed comments explaining the purpose of each step and the role of the various parameters. Providing pseudocode for the proximal operator and LMOψ would also enhance readability.

**Originality:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation represents a novel contribution. While Hawkes processes have been applied to user behavior modeling [12, 11], this work provides a distinct and more generalizable approach.  The formulation allows for the incorporation of additional contextual information (user profiles, textual features, etc.), which is a valuable extension.

**Significance:** Time-sensitive recommendation is a significant problem with broad applicability across various domains, including e-commerce, mobile applications, and healthcare (as illustrated in the introduction). The proposed method has the potential to improve user engagement and provide more relevant recommendations. The demonstrated scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events) further enhances the practical significance of this work. The ability to predict user return times also provides valuable insights for marketing strategies and resource allocation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach combining Hawkes processes and low-rank models.
*   Strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Scalable algorithm with provable convergence guarantees.
*   Potential for significant impact in various application domains.
*   Addresses a relevant and increasingly important problem.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The discussion surrounding Theorem 2 and its application in the experiments is weak.
*   The justification for the choice of the exponential kernel could be strengthened.
*   The inclusion of a stronger baseline like SVD++ [17] would provide a more comprehensive comparison. (Though the current baselines are acceptable)
*   Algorithm 1 lacks sufficient inline comments to fully elucidate its steps.



**Overall Assessment:** I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of recommender systems. The novelty of the approach, the strong empirical results, and the potential for significant impact outweigh the minor weaknesses identified. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding the theoretical discussion and providing more detailed explanations in the algorithmic description. I suggest a score of 7/10.
