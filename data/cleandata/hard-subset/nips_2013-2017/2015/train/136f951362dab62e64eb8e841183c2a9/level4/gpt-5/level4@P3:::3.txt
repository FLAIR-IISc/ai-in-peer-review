Summary
The paper addresses time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction by modeling recurrent user-item interactions with Hawkes point processes and transferring information across user–item pairs via low-rank structure. For each user–item pair, the intensity is a baseline plus a self-exciting component (exponential kernel by default). To share statistical strength and generalize to unseen pairs, the baseline matrix Λ0 and the self-excitation matrix A are assumed low-rank and constrained to be nonnegative. The authors propose a convex estimation framework with nuclear-norm regularization. To handle the combination of nuclear norms and nonnegativity efficiently, they introduce an equivalent penalty formulation (with auxiliary variables Z1, Z2 and parameter ρ) and optimize with a hybrid algorithm that combines proximal updates (for nonnegativity) and conditional gradient updates (for nuclear norms). They provide a convergence guarantee with O(1/t) rate. Empirically, on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II), the method outperforms Poisson, SVD-based, tensor, and survival-model baselines on both ranking at the right time and predicting return times, while scaling to millions of pairs and hundreds of millions of events.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects low-rank collaborative filtering (e.g., Koren 2009; tensor factorization for temporal/context-aware recommendation) with continuous-time point processes (Hawkes processes; Du et al., Zhou et al.). Unlike survival-analysis approaches for return-time prediction (e.g., Kapoor et al.), which assume independent inter-event gaps or focus on terminal events, this work explicitly models recurrence and history dependence. Compared to multivariate Hawkes work that learns excitation networks, the novelty here lies in per-pair Hawkes processes coupled through low-rank structure and a convex estimation procedure tailored to recommendation tasks.

Quality
- Theory: The alternative penalty formulation is natural, and the hybrid proximal/conditional-gradient scheme is well-motivated. The convergence analysis is clear for the composite structure. However, two points need clarification:
  1) Equivalence threshold ρ*: Theorem 1 defines ρ* via a max ratio involving unknown optima. Please specify over what set the maximum is taken, argue boundedness (can ρ* be unbounded?), and, if possible, provide a computable upper bound or a practical rule for setting ρ that ensures near-equivalence. Currently, ρ is tuned empirically, which diminishes the practical value of the theorem.
  2) Step-size/Lipschitz constant: Theorem 2 assumes knowledge of a Lipschitz constant L to set ηk. In practice, how is L obtained (backtracking, bounds), and how sensitive is performance to these choices?
- Modeling: The self-exciting structure is appropriate for recurrent engagement. Two modeling concerns:
  1) Stability/identifiability: For Hawkes processes, stability typically requires the excitation mass to be below 1 (branching ratio). What conditions are enforced on αu,i and σ to ensure stability and distinguish baseline from excitation (identifiability)? A brief discussion or constraint would be helpful.
  2) Return-time prediction: Using Ogata’s thinning to Monte Carlo the expectation can be computationally heavy and high-variance. For exponential kernels, the survival function is tractable; could you compute the mean numerically (quadrature) or report median/mode, which can be more robust?
- Experiments: The setup (including cold-start-on-item by holding out entire item sequences) is thoughtful, and results are strong. Suggestions to strengthen the empirical section:
  1) Baselines: Please add TimeSVD++ (Koren 2009) or related temporal matrix factorization baselines. SVD++ without temporal dynamics is a weaker comparator for time-sensitive tasks. Also consider renewal or seasonally modulated Poisson baselines, and, if feasible, a multivariate Hawkes baseline with low-rank or clustering.
  2) Metrics: Besides “rank of the ground-truth item at event time,” report standard top-k metrics (HR@k, NDCG@k) and calibration metrics for return-time predictions (negative log-likelihood on held-out events, or coverage of prediction intervals). Confidence intervals/variability would help assess significance.
  3) Runtime/memory: Since scalability is a key claim, please include wall-clock times, peak memory, and details on SVD computations for the linear oracles (e.g., power/Lanczos iterations, tolerances). The method appears amenable to parallelization; if used, summarize the setup.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. Minor issues:
  - Define all quantities when first introduced (e.g., explicitly define the maximization set in the ρ* expression).
  - A few typos: “returningtime” → “returning time”; “unit time for both dataset is hour” → “The time unit for both datasets is hours”; “Acknowledge” → “Acknowledgments.”
  - Slight redundancy in the introduction could be trimmed.

Originality
The coupling of Hawkes processes with low-rank, nonnegative matrices within a convex framework for time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction is novel. The hybrid optimization approach that separates nonnegativity and nuclear norms with provable rate is also a nice algorithmic contribution.

Significance
Time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction are important in many applications (media, e-commerce, health). The approach is principled, extensible (to contextual features), and empirically strong. If the theoretical clarifications are addressed and stronger temporal baselines are included, this work would likely influence future research on continuous-time recommendation and event modeling.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled continuous-time modeling of recurrent behavior with clear motivation.
- Convex low-rank formulation with nonnegativity; efficient hybrid optimization and convergence guarantees.
- Strong empirical performance on large-scale synthetic and real datasets; good cold-start-like evaluation.
- Clear path to incorporate additional context (text, location, profiles).

Cons against acceptance
- The equivalence condition (ρ*) is defined via intractable quantities; practical guidance is limited.
- Missing key temporal baselines (TimeSVD++/temporal MF); baseline set skews in favor of proposed method.
- Limited discussion of Hawkes stability/identifiability and practical step-size selection.
- Return-time estimation by sampling may be computationally heavy; alternatives not explored.

Overall assessment
A strong, well-motivated paper with solid algorithmic and empirical contributions. I recommend acceptance after addressing the questions on ρ*, step-size selection, stability, and by adding stronger temporal baselines and standard evaluation metrics.
