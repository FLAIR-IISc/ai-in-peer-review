

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interaction sequences as low-rank Hawkes processes. The authors connect self-exciting point processes with low-rank matrix models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user activities, addressing two key challenges: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) predicting users' next return time to services. The approach combines the expressive power of Hawkes processes for modeling temporal dependencies with low-rank matrix structures to enable scalable learning across millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the NIPS community by addressing a meaningful gap in recommendation systems research. While traditional approaches focus on predicting user preferences (e.g., ratings), this work recognizes that understanding temporal dynamics—how users return to services over time—is crucial for delivering timely, relevant recommendations. This aligns with recent NIPS work on temporal modeling (e.g., Zhou et al., 2013; Du et al., 2015) but extends it by incorporating low-rank structure for scalability and generalization to unseen user-item pairs, unlike Kapoor et al.'s (2014, 2015) survival analysis approach that cannot recommend new items.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-justified. The convex optimization formulation (Equation 4) provides a principled way to estimate model parameters, and the theoretical analysis (Theorem 2) establishes an O(1/t) convergence rate. However, Theorem 1's practical implications need clarification: the threshold ρ* depends on unknown optimal parameters (Λ*0, A*), making it difficult to determine ρ in practice. The paper tunes ρ empirically but doesn't explain how this choice affects the theoretical guarantees. Additionally, the authors could strengthen the empirical evaluation by comparing against SVD++ (the Netflix challenge winner), which has demonstrated strong performance in time-sensitive recommendation tasks (Koren, 2008).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The connection between Hawkes processes and recommendation is clearly explained, and the optimization algorithm is presented with sufficient mathematical detail. The experimental setup is transparent, with clear descriptions of synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II). The figures effectively illustrate convergence behavior and predictive performance.

**Originality:** The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by establishing a connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation. While related work (e.g., Kapoor et al., 2014; Du et al., 2015) has explored temporal dynamics in recommendation, none has combined Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix factorization to achieve both scalability and generalization to unseen items. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from prior work by explicitly addressing the limitation of previous methods in recommending new items.

**Significance:** The research addresses a critical gap in recommendation systems, where temporal dynamics are increasingly recognized as vital for user engagement. The ability to predict both the right item at the right time and users' next return time has direct applications in e-commerce, streaming services, and healthcare (e.g., predicting patient disease progression). The scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events) makes the approach practical for real-world deployment. The authors demonstrate superior performance over strong baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, tensor factorization) across multiple datasets, confirming the significance of their approach.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Novel framework connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation
- Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets
- Demonstrated scalability to large-scale problems
- Clear practical applications across multiple domains

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- The practical implementation of Theorem 1 requires empirical tuning of ρ, which isn't fully justified
- Comparison against SVD++ would strengthen the empirical evaluation
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the model handles cold-start scenarios for new users/items

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of recommendation systems by addressing a significant gap in temporal modeling. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the applications are compelling. The minor concerns about ρ selection and SVD++ comparison don't detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to NIPS.
