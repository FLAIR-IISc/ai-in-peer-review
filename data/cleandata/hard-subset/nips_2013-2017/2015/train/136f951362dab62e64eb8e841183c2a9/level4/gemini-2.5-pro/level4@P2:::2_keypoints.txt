- Proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework modeling recurrent user-item events as a low-rank Hawkes process to recommend items at specific times and predict return times.  
- Models intensity as a baseline (long-term preference) plus history-dependent self-excitation, and imposes low-rank structure on baseline and excitation matrices to handle sparsity.  
- Casts parameter estimation as a convex optimization with nuclear-norm regularization and introduces a scalable algorithm combining proximal and conditional gradient methods with convergence guarantees.  
- Empirical results on synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) show improvements over several baselines for item recommendation and return-time prediction.  
- Novel and elegant synthesis of self-exciting point processes with low-rank latent factor models capturing both long-term preferences and short-term dynamics.  
- Addresses practically important problems: time-sensitive item ranking and user return-time prediction with applications in advertising, engagement, and healthcare.  
- Strong technical contribution: convex formulation and a scalable algorithm that handles non-negativity and nuclear-norm regularization with formal convergence analysis.  
- Convincing empirical evidence, including QQ plots showing better fit to event generation, and outperformance of static and temporal baselines.  
- Lacks comparison to a stronger hybrid baseline (e.g., STiC combined with SVD for new items), which could clarify practical advantages over ensemble approaches.  
- Insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection and sensitivity—must report selection methodology, ranges, and sensitivity, particularly for the kernel bandwidth σ.  
- Dataset descriptions lack temporal characterization; should report events-per-sequence distribution, inter-event time statistics (mean/median/variance), and burstiness measures.  
- Quality: technically sound overall but experimental evaluation needs stronger baselines and greater reproducibility in hyperparameter tuning.  
- Clarity: generally well-written but the optimization algorithm presentation is dense; add a high-level motivation paragraph before algorithm details.  
- Originality: highly original combination of Hawkes processes with low-rank collaborative models.  
- Significance: addresses an important, timely problem in recommender systems and is likely to inspire further research.
