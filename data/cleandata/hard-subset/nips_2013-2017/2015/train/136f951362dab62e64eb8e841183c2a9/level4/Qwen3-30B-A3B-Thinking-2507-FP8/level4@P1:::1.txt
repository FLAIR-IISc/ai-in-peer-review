

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption. The authors address two key problems: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) predicting the next returning time of users to services. The approach models user-item interactions as a low-rank Hawkes process, where the intensity function captures both baseline user preferences and the self-exciting effects of past interactions. The authors develop an efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence rate and scaling to large datasets with millions of user-item pairs.

The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing time-sensitive recommendation, which is less explored compared to traditional recommendation systems that focus on predicting one-time user preferences. While some recent work (e.g., Kapoor et al.) has explored user return time prediction, their approach cannot generalize to recommend new items, which is a key limitation addressed by this paper. The connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models is novel and well-motivated, providing a unified framework for both recommendation and return time prediction.

Strengths of the paper include:
- A well-structured theoretical foundation that clearly connects point processes with recommendation systems
- A technically sound optimization algorithm with strong convergence guarantees (Theorem 2)
- Empirical validation on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating scalability and superior performance
- Clear presentation of the model and experimental setup

However, several weaknesses need to be addressed:

1. The motivation for time-sensitive recommendation is not sufficiently justified. The paper claims that "most existing recommendation techniques do not explicitly take into account the temporal behavior and recurrent activities of users," but doesn't provide convincing evidence from real-world data that this is a widespread problem. The authors should analyze the real datasets used in their experiments to demonstrate that user preferences are indeed time-varying in meaningful ways (e.g., not just due to new popular items). Without this, the problem's relevance remains questionable.

2. The motivation for predicting user return times is underdeveloped. The paper states that "predicting the next returning time not only allows a service to keep track of evolving user preferences but also helps a service provider to improve marketing strategies," but doesn't elaborate on how this prediction would be practically used. For example, how would a company actually use this prediction to improve ad bidding or marketing strategies? More concrete examples would strengthen the contribution.

3. The experimental section lacks sufficient comparison with simple baselines for return time prediction. The authors claim that "predicting the actual returning time is too challenging," but then don't test simple baselines like "last time delay" or "most frequent time delay," which are commonly used in practice. Instead, the paper focuses on comparing with more complex methods like STiC and tensor factorization. The authors should address these simple baselines to demonstrate their method's relative advantage.

4. The evaluation of recommendation performance lacks context. The paper states that their method "achieves superb predictive performance," but doesn't quantify how much better it is compared to SVD (the static baseline). Is the improvement substantial (e.g., order of magnitude) or modest? How would this difference impact user experience? The paper should provide more concrete comparisons to popular items, which is often a strong baseline in practice.

5. The paper mentions that "predicting the next returning time" is challenging, yet the evaluation focuses on absolute error. The authors should consider whether predicting time intervals (e.g., short, medium, long delays) would be more meaningful and achievable than predicting exact return times. This would also align better with the claimed practical applications.

Minor issues:
- Typo on page 3: "and is abel to capture" → "and is able to capture"
- In Section 2, the mention of "retweets and link creation in social networks" is confusing without proper context or references.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting technical contribution that advances the state of the art in time-sensitive recommendation. However, the lack of strong motivation for the problems addressed and insufficient baseline comparisons weaken the significance of the work. The authors should strengthen the paper by providing evidence that time-varying preferences are a meaningful problem in real-world applications, clarifying the practical utility of return time prediction, and including more relevant baselines in their evaluation.

The paper is technically sound with a novel convex formulation and efficient optimization algorithm, but the significance could be substantially improved with the suggested revisions. Given the limited evidence for the practical importance of the problems addressed, I recommend a borderline acceptance, contingent on addressing the concerns raised above.
