- Proposal: Time-sensitive recommendation framework using Hawkes processes per user–item pair with baseline plus self-exciting history term.  
- Generalization: Baseline and excitation parameters constrained to be low-rank via nuclear-norm regularization across the user–item matrix.  
- Optimization: Parameter learning posed as a convex problem; algorithm blends proximal gradient (nonnegativity) with conditional gradient (nuclear norm) and achieves O(1/t) convergence.  
- Evaluation: Tested on item recommendation and return-time prediction over large synthetic and real datasets, showing strong performance and scalability.  
- Positioning: Compared against survival-analysis return-time models, matrix/tensor factorization, and Hawkes-process literature including low-rank/networked variants.  
- Quality (positive): Principled point-process modeling of temporal engagement and a well-motivated, efficient optimization method.  
- Concern 1: Ambiguity in Eq. (8) about which variable the “max” is taken over and whether ρ* can be unbounded; needs clarification for existence/fin

iteness.  
- Concern 2: Theorem 2 gives a convergence rate but experiments tune ρ heuristically, weakening the practical role of the theoretical guarantee; authors should discuss how theory informs ρ or why it cannot.  
- Baseline recommendation: Include a strong time-aware baseline such as SVD++ (Netflix Prize winner) to strengthen empirical comparisons.  
- Clarity: Paper is clearly written and well organized, aside from the ambiguity around Eq. (8)/ρ*.  
- Originality: Novel connection of Hawkes processes with low-rank modeling for recommendation and a convex formulation with an efficient solver.  
- Significance: Addresses impactful, underexplored time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction with a scalable method amenable to distributed implementation.  
- Pro: Clear, well-motivated formulation linking Hawkes processes and low-rank structure.  
- Pro: Convex learning objective with an efficient combined proximal/conditional gradient algorithm and solid convergence analysis.  
- Pro: Strong scalability and empirical performance for ranking and return-time prediction on large datasets.  
- Pro: Flexible to incorporate contextual features.  
- Con: Ambiguity in Eq. (8) regarding definition of the “max” and conditions ensuring finiteness of ρ*.  
- Con: Theorem 2’s guidance does not match experimental hyperparameter selection (especially ρ), reducing practical impact.  
- Con: Missing strong time-aware baseline (e.g., SVD++), limiting empirical completeness.  
- Overall: Strong submission with a principled model, practical algorithm, and convincing results; clarify ρ* and add stronger baselines to further strengthen the paper.
