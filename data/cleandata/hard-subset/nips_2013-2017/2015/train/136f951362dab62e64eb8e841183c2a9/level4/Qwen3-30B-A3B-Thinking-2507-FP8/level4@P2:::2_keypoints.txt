- Proposes a novel framework combining self-exciting (Hawkes) point processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendations.  
- Targets two challenges: recommending the most relevant item at the right time and predicting the next user return time.  
- Models each user-item pair with a Hawkes process whose intensity is baseline (long-term preference) plus self-exciting component (short-term dependency).  
- Provides a convex optimization formulation and an efficient algorithm with O(1/t) convergence, scalable to millions of user-item pairs.  
- Extends prior point-process recommendation work by enabling recommendation of new (unobserved) items via low-rank representations.  
- Distinguishes itself from SVD/tensor factorization approaches by explicitly modeling temporal dynamics.  
- Technical approach is sound and theoretically grounded with convergence guarantees.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple baselines on synthetic and real datasets.  
- Lacks sufficient detail on hyperparameter selection, notably the triggering-kernel bandwidth σ.  
- Does not justify or analyze sensitivity to the latent dimension for low-rank factorization.  
- Contains grammar and awkward phrasing that require thorough proofreading.  
- Experimental presentation should include more dataset statistics (e.g., recurrence counts per user-item, inter-event time distributions).  
- Original contribution: novel integration of point processes and low-rank factorization with a convex formulation and efficient optimization.  
- Significant practical relevance for domains like streaming, e-commerce, and healthcare; shows superior performance, including on held-out sequences.  
- Strengths: novelty, theoretical guarantees, scalable algorithm, and comprehensive empirical results.  
- Weaknesses: insufficient hyperparameter/dataset detail, clarity/grammar issues, and limited comparison to a simple STiC+SVD ensemble.  
- Overall: strong paper with minor revisions recommended to address hyperparameter reporting, dataset context, and writing.
