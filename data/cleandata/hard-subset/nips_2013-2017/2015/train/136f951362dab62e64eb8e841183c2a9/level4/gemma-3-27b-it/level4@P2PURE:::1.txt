This paper introduces a novel method for time-sensitive recommendations, leveraging user activity to address the challenge of time-varying user preferences. The work also explores prediction of a user’s next returning time. Learning is achieved through an efficient optimization algorithm developed by the authors. The experimental evaluation demonstrates efficient learning on a large-scale synthetic dataset and presents results on two real-world datasets of moderate size.

However, the prevalence of significant time-varying user preferences warrants further investigation. Analyzing the experimental datasets to support this claim would be beneficial. Specifically, it would be valuable to understand the underlying causes of this variation – is it primarily driven by the introduction of new popular items, or are other patterns present?

The motivation for predicting a user’s next returning time is currently unclear. Please provide a rationale for this contribution and discuss its potential applications for a web-based company. What specific use cases would benefit from this prediction?

Regarding the returning time prediction, I believe several simple baselines were overlooked: 1) predicting the next delay based on the last observed delay, and 2) predicting the next delay based on the most frequent observed delay. Furthermore, predicting the precise returning time appears overly ambitious. I suggest focusing on a more tractable task, such as classifying the delay into categories – for example, short (1-5 days), medium (5-14 days), long (14-33 days), or very long (>33 days).

The experimental results lack clarity regarding the magnitude of performance improvement over SVD. Is the lift substantial (on the order of magnitude), or more modest? Would a user perceive a noticeable difference? Additionally, it is important to benchmark the proposed recommendations against a popular item recommendation strategy, which can be a strong baseline, especially with daily re-calculation of popular items.
