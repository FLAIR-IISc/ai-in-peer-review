

Summary: This paper proposes a novel framework that integrates self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions. The authors model user-item consumption events using Hawkes processes, where the intensity function combines a baseline component representing users' inherent preferences and a self-exciting component reflecting the influence of past events. The key innovation lies in formulating the problem as a convex optimization with nuclear norm regularization, enabling efficient learning that scales to large datasets. The paper demonstrates the model's effectiveness in two time-sensitive recommendation tasks: predicting the next item to recommend at a given moment and forecasting users' returning times. The authors compare their approach against several baselines, including Poisson processes, STiC, matrix factorization, and tensor factorization methods, on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing two underexplored questions in recommendation systems: how to recommend the most relevant item at the right moment and how to predict users' returning times. While the connection between point processes and collaborative filtering is interesting, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened in several ways. First, the authors claim that STiC [12] cannot recommend new items, but it would be informative to compare against a simple ensemble of STiC (for historical patterns) and SVD (for new item recommendations), which would provide a more complete baseline. Second, the paper lacks details about the selection of the bandwidth parameter σ for the exponential triggering kernel and its sensitivity analysis. Third, the hyperparameter selection process for both the proposed method and baselines is not sufficiently described, particularly regarding the dimension of latent vectors. Finally, the paper would benefit from more detailed characteristics of the real-world datasets, such as the distribution of recurring events per user-item pair and the irregularity of temporal patterns.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, with a clear convex formulation and well-justified optimization algorithm. The convergence analysis provides theoretical guarantees, and the empirical results demonstrate scalability and effectiveness on both synthetic and real data. However, the experimental design could be improved to provide stronger evidence for the proposed method's superiority.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-structured and contains sufficient mathematical detail. However, the writing could be significantly improved for better flow and precision. There are several grammatical errors, awkward phrasings, and inconsistent terminology that disrupt the reading experience. For example, "two sets of intensity are computed by two sets of user and item latent vectors" is unclear, as the paper actually uses a low-rank factorization of two matrices (Λ₀ and A) rather than "two sets of latent vectors." The authors should revise the presentation for greater clarity and professionalism.

Originality: The integration of self-exciting point processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendations represents a novel combination of techniques. While point processes have been used in recommendation systems before, the specific application to recurrent user activities with a low-rank convex optimization approach appears to be new. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work, particularly Kapoor et al. [12, 11], and clearly articulate how their method differs by enabling recommendations for new items.

Significance: The problem addressed—time-sensitive recommendations and returning time prediction—has substantial practical importance for modern web services, from personalized advertising to healthcare applications. The ability to predict when users will return to services can significantly improve marketing strategies and user engagement. The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets, suggesting the approach could be valuable for practitioners. The optimization algorithm also has broader applicability to nonnegative matrix rank minimization problems, which adds to the paper's significance.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful approach to time-sensitive recommendation systems. While the core idea is promising and technically sound, the experimental evaluation would be strengthened by addressing the specific points raised above, and the presentation would benefit from careful revision to improve clarity and professionalism. The authors make a valuable contribution to the field, but the current version could be improved to better demonstrate the full potential of their method.
