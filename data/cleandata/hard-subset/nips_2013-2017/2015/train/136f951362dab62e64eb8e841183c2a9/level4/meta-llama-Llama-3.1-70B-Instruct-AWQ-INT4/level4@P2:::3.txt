This paper proposes a novel algorithm for time-sensitive recommendation by leveraging low-rank structure in a user-item matrix and utilizing Hawkes processes. The algorithm is designed to be solved in a distributed fashion and demonstrates better performance compared to existing methods.

The paper's approach to modeling observations using stochastic processes is principled and well-executed. However, there are a few concerns regarding the theoretical aspects of the paper. Specifically, in Equation (8) and Theorem 2, it is unclear over which parameter the maximum is taken. Is it over ρ? If so, can ρ* be unbounded? Furthermore, Theorem 2 is not utilized when choosing ρ in the experiments, which diminishes its significance.

In terms of experiments, the baselines used are adequate, but the addition of SVD++ (the winner of the Netflix challenge) would have strengthened the paper's quality. SVD++ is a well-known and highly effective algorithm for time-sensitive recommendation, and its inclusion would have provided a more comprehensive comparison.

The paper's clarity is one of its strengths, as it is well-written and easy to follow. The authors have done an excellent job of presenting their ideas and methodology in a clear and concise manner.

The originality of the paper lies in its novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation, which is a relatively new problem in the field. The use of Hawkes processes and low-rank structure in the user-item matrix is a unique contribution.

The significance of the paper is substantial, as time-sensitive recommendation has the potential to make a significant impact in various applications. The proposed algorithm's ability to solve this problem in a distributed fashion and achieve better results than existing methods makes it a valuable contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel algorithm for time-sensitive recommendation.
* The algorithm leverages low-rank structure in the user-item matrix and utilizes Hawkes processes.
* The paper demonstrates better performance compared to existing methods.
* The approach is principled and well-executed.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The theoretical aspects of the paper could be improved, specifically regarding Equation (8) and Theorem 2.
* The inclusion of SVD++ as a baseline would have strengthened the paper's quality.
* The paper's contribution, while significant, may not be substantial enough to warrant acceptance.
