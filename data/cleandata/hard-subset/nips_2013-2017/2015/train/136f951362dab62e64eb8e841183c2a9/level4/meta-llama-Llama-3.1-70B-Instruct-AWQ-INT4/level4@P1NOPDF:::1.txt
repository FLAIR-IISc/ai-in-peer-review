Paper proposes a novel method for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities, distinguishing itself from existing approaches by addressing the challenge of time-varying user preferences. The paper also tackles the prediction of the next returning time of a user, employing an efficient optimization algorithm developed by the authors. The experimental section presents results on one synthetic dataset to demonstrate the efficiency of the learning process on large-scale data, as well as on two real datasets of modest size.

Firstly, it is unclear whether the problem of time-varying user preferences is widespread enough to warrant the proposed approach. Analyzing the real datasets used in the experiments would strengthen this claim. If time-varying preferences are indeed prevalent, it would be helpful to investigate the underlying causes. For instance, are new popular songs primarily responsible for these variations, or are there other patterns at play?

Furthermore, the motivation behind predicting the next returning time for a user is not clearly established. Before presenting this as a contribution, it would be beneficial to provide a more detailed explanation of its significance. How might a web company utilize this prediction, and what benefits would it bring?

In addition, there are simple baselines for predicting the next returning time that were not tested, such as 1) the last time delay and 2) the most frequently occurring time delay. Predicting the actual returning time of the user may be too ambitious, and I suggest focusing on a simpler task that is easier to predict. For example, predicting whether a user will have a short delay (1-5 days), medium delay (5-14 days), long delay (14-33 days), or very long delay (>33 days) before using the service again could be a more feasible and practical approach.

The experimental section could be improved by providing a clearer sense of the significance of the performance lift compared to SVD. Is the improvement substantial (on the order of magnitude) or modest? Would the user notice the difference? How do these recommendations compare to recommending popular items, which is often a challenging baseline to surpass, especially if popular items are recalculated daily?

Small comments:
1) Typo in the last paragraph on page 3: "and is abel to capture" should be corrected to "and is able to capture".
2) The first paragraph of Section 2 abruptly shifts context by mentioning retweets and link creation in social networks without providing any references. Rephrasing this section would improve clarity.

The paper addresses two problems: time-sensitive recommendations and returning time of the user, but lacks a clear motivation for why these problems are relevant and what impact solving them would have. Several baselines and analyses are missing (detailed below). Overall, the paper is acceptable but marginal.
