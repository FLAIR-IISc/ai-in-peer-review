- Proposes a novel framework linking self-exciting Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models for time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction.  
- Models each user-item pair with a Hawkes process and represents base intensity and excitation parameters as low-rank matrices to handle sparsity.  
- Learns parameters via a convex negative log-likelihood with nuclear-norm regularization and non-negativity constraints.  
- Introduces a new optimization algorithm (proximal + conditional gradient) with O(1/t) convergence, scalable and theoretically grounded.  
- Empirically evaluated on one large synthetic and three real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II), outperforming baselines (Poisson, SVD, tensor methods).  
- Strength: elegant, principled formulation that captures temporal dynamics and unifies recommendation and return-time prediction.  
- Strength: strong technical contribution — an efficient, scalable optimization method with convergence guarantees that is of independent interest.  
- Strength: comprehensive experiments, including validation of the Hawkes assumption via the time-change theorem.  
- Weakness: motivation could be more empirically grounded; include dataset analyses showing time-varying user/item behavior (e.g., diurnal/weekly patterns).  
- Weakness: unclear practical need for exact continuous return-time prediction; clarify real-world use cases versus coarser predictions.  
- Weakness: return-time baselines incomplete — should include simple heuristics (last inter-event, average/median inter-event) for comparison.  
- Suggestion: consider reframing return-time prediction as classification or binned regression (short/medium/long windows) for practicality and robustness.  
- Weakness: practical significance of ranking improvements is unclear; provide qualitative analysis and compare to strong time-aware heuristics (e.g., last-24h popularity).  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized; background and optimization exposition are clear.  
- Conclusion: strong, original, technically solid work likely to inspire future research, but would benefit from stronger empirical motivation and more complete baselines.
