This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation tasks, which involves capturing the recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption pairs using self-exciting point processes and low-rank models. The authors develop an efficient algorithm that achieves a convergence rate of O(1/t) and scales up to millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it clear that the authors have made a significant effort to ensure the clarity of their work. The use of Hawkes processes to model the temporal dynamics of user behavior is a novel approach that sets this work apart from existing methods. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their algorithm on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its superior scalability and predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art competitors.

One of the strengths of this paper is its ability to balance theoretical soundness with practical considerations. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of their algorithm's convergence properties and demonstrate its efficiency in solving the proposed optimization problem. At the same time, they also consider the practical implications of their work, such as the ability to incorporate additional context information and the potential applications of their framework in real-world scenarios.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a thorough discussion of existing methods for time-sensitive recommendation tasks, including survival analysis, hidden semi-Markov models, and tensor factorization. They also highlight the limitations of these approaches and demonstrate how their proposed framework addresses these limitations.

One potential concern is the choice of baselines in the experimental evaluation. While the authors compare their algorithm to several state-of-the-art competitors, they do not include SVD++ [17], which is widely considered one of the best algorithms for time-sensitive recommendation tasks. Including SVD++ as a baseline would have strengthened the evaluation and provided a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

Another minor comment is regarding the presentation of Theorem 2. The theorem states that the convergence rate of the algorithm is O(1/t), but it is not entirely clear how this theorem is used in the experimental evaluation. Specifically, the value of ρ is chosen empirically, and it is not clear how this choice relates to the theorem. Providing more context or explanation for how the theorem is used in practice would have strengthened the paper.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and impactful approach to time-sensitive recommendation tasks. The authors' use of Hawkes processes and low-rank models provides a unique perspective on the problem, and their algorithm's scalability and predictive performance make it a compelling solution for real-world applications.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and impactful approach to time-sensitive recommendation tasks.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their algorithm on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* The paper balances theoretical soundness with practical considerations, making it a compelling solution for real-world applications.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The choice of baselines in the experimental evaluation could be improved by including SVD++.
* The presentation of Theorem 2 could be clarified to provide more context for how it is used in practice.
