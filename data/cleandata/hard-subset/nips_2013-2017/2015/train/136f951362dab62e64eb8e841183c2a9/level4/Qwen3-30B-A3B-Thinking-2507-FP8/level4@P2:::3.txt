

This paper addresses the important but underexplored problem of time-sensitive recommendation by modeling user-item interactions through a novel combination of low-rank models and self-exciting point processes (specifically Hawkes processes). The authors propose a convex optimization framework that captures recurrent temporal patterns in user behavior, allowing them to solve two key time-sensitive recommendation tasks: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) predicting the next returning time of users to a service. The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a principled connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models, (2) an efficient optimization algorithm with O(1/t) convergence rate that scales to large datasets, and (3) a comprehensive evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper builds upon previous work in temporal recommendation systems, particularly the recent work by Kapoor et al. [11, 12] who used survival analysis to predict user return times. However, the authors correctly identify a key limitation in existing approaches: most methods cannot generalize to recommend new items, which is crucial for practical recommendation systems. The paper also relates to work on matrix factorization for recommendation (e.g., [13, 15, 23, 25]) but extends it by incorporating temporal dynamics explicitly.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with solid theoretical foundations. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is well-motivated, and the convex optimization framework is carefully developed. The convergence analysis in Theorem 2 is a strong theoretical contribution. However, there are some concerns about the theoretical analysis: Theorem 1 states that the optimal values of the two formulations coincide when ρ > ρ*, but it's not clear what the maximum is taken over in the definition of ρ*. The paper doesn't specify whether ρ* could be unbounded, which would make the theorem less useful in practice. Additionally, the paper doesn't explain why ρ was chosen as a fixed value in the experiments rather than using the theoretical threshold.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical formulations are presented logically, and the technical details are accessible to readers with a background in machine learning and optimization. The explanation of the Hawkes process and its connection to low-rank models is particularly clear. The authors provide sufficient detail for the expert reader to reproduce their results, including algorithm pseudocode and implementation details.

**Originality:** The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by addressing the time-sensitive recommendation problem through a novel combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models. While the use of Hawkes processes for modeling event sequences has been explored in other domains, applying them to recommendation systems with a low-rank structure is innovative. The authors also address a significant gap in the literature by explicitly modeling the temporal dynamics of user behavior rather than treating it as static. This represents a meaningful advancement over previous work in recommendation systems.

**Significance:** The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is highly relevant to modern web services, where understanding the timing of user interactions is crucial for engagement. The proposed approach has the potential to significantly improve recommendation systems by enabling more timely and relevant suggestions. The paper demonstrates that their method outperforms strong baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets, suggesting practical applicability. The ability to scale to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of temporal events) further enhances the significance of the work.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:*
- Novel combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation
- Strong theoretical foundations with convergence analysis
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on synthetic and real data
- Demonstrated scalability to large datasets
- Clear writing and well-organized presentation

*Con:*
- Theoretical analysis of ρ* could be more precise
- The paper could have included SVD++ [17] as a baseline, as it's a strong time-sensitive recommendation algorithm (though the authors do mention SVD as a baseline, which is related but not specifically designed for time-sensitive tasks)
- The connection to related work could be strengthened (e.g., more clearly distinguishing from recent work on temporal matrix factorization)

**Overall Recommendation:** This is a strong paper with meaningful contributions to the field of recommendation systems. The technical quality is high, the problem is important, and the results are convincing. The minor theoretical concerns and baseline suggestions do not detract from the paper's overall quality. I recommend acceptance.
