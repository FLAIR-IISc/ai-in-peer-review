This paper proposes a new method for time-sensitive recommendations based on user activities, differentiating itself from existing approaches by directly addressing the problem of time-varying user preferences. The paper also introduces prediction of a user’s next returning time. Learning is performed using an efficient optimization algorithm developed by the authors. The experimental section presents results on one synthetic dataset to demonstrate scalability, and on two real-world datasets of modest size.

A central claim is that user preferences are time-varying. However, the extent to which this is true warrants further investigation. It would strengthen the paper to analyze the real datasets used in the experiments to substantiate this claim. If preferences *do* vary significantly, understanding the underlying causes – for example, the introduction of new popular items – would provide valuable insight. 

The motivation for predicting a user’s next returning time is unclear. Before presenting this as a contribution, the paper should articulate *why* this prediction is useful and how a web company might leverage it. What specific applications would benefit from knowing when a user is likely to return?

Regarding the returning time prediction task, several simple baselines appear to be missing from the comparison. These include: 1) predicting the next delay as equal to the last observed delay, and 2) predicting the next delay as the most frequently observed delay. Furthermore, predicting the precise returning time seems inherently challenging. A potentially more tractable and informative task would be to categorize the delay into discrete intervals – for example, short (1-5 days), medium (5-14 days), long (14-33 days), and very long (>33 days).

The experimental section makes it difficult to assess the significance of the performance improvements over SVD. Is the lift substantial (on the order of magnitude), or merely modest? Would a user perceive a noticeable difference in recommendation quality? Moreover, the results should be contextualized by comparison to a strong, dynamic popularity-based baseline, where popular items are recalculated daily – a challenging benchmark in many scenarios.

Minor comments:
1) Typo in the last paragraph on page 3: "and is abel to capture" should read "and is able to capture."
2) In the first paragraph of section 2, the introduction of retweets and link creation in social networks lacks necessary references and creates a jarring shift in context. This section should be carefully rephrased. 

Overall, the paper lacks sufficient motivation for the relevance of the addressed problems and the potential impact of solving them. Furthermore, several important baselines and analyses are missing (as detailed above). Despite these issues, the paper is overall OK/marginal.
