This paper proposes a novel point process collaborative filtering model designed to capture the temporal and recurrent structure inherent in user-item interactions. Specifically, the authors model the event density for each user-item pair as the sum of a baseline intensity and a self-exciting intensity, modulated by past events, and estimate model parameters via a convex optimization framework with a provably efficient algorithm. Empirical evaluations demonstrate performance improvements over several state-of-the-art methods.

The core idea of connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture temporal dependencies is a compelling one, building upon the growing recognition that static preference modeling is insufficient for many recommendation scenarios [26]. This work extends prior research by explicitly addressing both item recommendation and returning-time prediction within a unified framework. Related work in temporal recommendation, such as Kapoor et al. [12, 11], often relies on survival analysis or hidden semi-Markov models, but the current approach offers the advantage of being able to generalize to recommend new items, a significant limitation of prior work. The authors appropriately acknowledge this distinction and position their contribution accordingly. The theoretical guarantees on the optimization algorithm (O(1/t) convergence) and its scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events) are also important contributions.

The strengths of the paper lie in its technically sound approach, the clear formulation of the problem, and the theoretical results regarding convergence and scalability. The experimental setup includes both synthetic and real-world datasets, which strengthens the validity of the findings. The authors also acknowledge potential extensions, such as incorporating additional contextual information (user profiles, textual data, spatial features), demonstrating foresight and a broader understanding of the problem space.

However, there are several areas for improvement. The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including a more comprehensive comparison to baseline methods. Specifically, a simple ensemble of STiC [12] and SVD would provide a valuable point of comparison, particularly given the authors’ argument that STiC struggles with new item recommendation, a weakness potentially addressed by SVD. The sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters would also enhance the robustness of the results. The choice of the bandwidth parameter σ in the exponential triggering kernel γ(t, tu,ij) is not fully justified, and exploring its impact on performance would be beneficial. Furthermore, detailed reporting of hyperparameter tuning procedures (e.g., latent vector dimensions) for all methods is necessary for reproducibility and fair comparison. Providing more descriptive statistics for the real-world datasets – such as the distribution of recurring events per user/item pair and the irregularity of temporal patterns – would provide valuable context and strengthen the justification for using a point process model.

**Quality:** Technically sound with a well-defined optimization framework and proven convergence properties. However, the empirical evaluation could be more thorough.
**Clarity:** Generally clear, but minor improvements in language and grammar would enhance readability.
**Originality:** The combination of point processes and low-rank models is novel and offers a promising approach to temporal recommendation.
**Significance:** The problem addressed and the proposed method have significant practical implications for improving user engagement and personalization in web services. The ability to predict returning times has clear benefits for marketing and resource allocation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to temporal recommendation.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees regarding convergence and scalability.
*   Promising empirical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Addresses a significant problem with practical applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and rigorous.
*   Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is lacking.
*   Limited details on dataset characteristics.
*   Minor clarity issues in presentation.



Overall, this is a promising paper with a solid technical foundation. Addressing the suggested improvements would significantly strengthen the contribution and increase its impact on the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
