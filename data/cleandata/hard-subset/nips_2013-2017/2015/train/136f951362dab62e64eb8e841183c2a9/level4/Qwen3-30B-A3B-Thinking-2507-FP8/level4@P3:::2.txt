

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions. The authors model the intensity of user-item consumption events as a combination of a constant baseline intensity and a self-exciting component dependent on past events, where the parameters are constrained to be low-rank to enable efficient estimation. The approach addresses two key challenges: (1) recommending the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) predicting the next user returning time. The paper establishes a convex optimization formulation for parameter estimation, develops an efficient algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods with O(1/t) convergence, and demonstrates scalability to millions of user-item pairs. While related to Kapoor et al.'s work on user return time prediction [12, 11], this paper differs by enabling recommendations for *new* items (not just previously viewed ones), which is critical for practical applications. The approach also generalizes to incorporate additional contextual information like user profiles and spatial features.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-motivated convex formulation. The theoretical analysis of the optimization algorithm (Theorem 2) is rigorous and establishes a novel convergence rate for the combined proximal-conditional gradient method. The empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) demonstrates superior performance over multiple baselines (Poisson, STiC, SVD, Tensor factorization), with strong scalability to large-scale problems.

*Originality*: The combination of self-exciting point processes with low-rank structure for collaborative filtering is novel. While point processes have been used in recommendation (e.g., [11]), this paper's key innovation is the low-rank formulation that enables efficient learning and generalization to new items, which previous methods cannot achieve. The connection to convex optimization for large-scale settings is particularly noteworthy.

*Significance*: The problem of time-sensitive recommendation is highly relevant to modern web services (e.g., music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare), where predicting both *what* to recommend and *when* to recommend it significantly impacts user engagement. The demonstrated scalability to hundreds of millions of events makes the approach practically applicable to real-world systems.

*Clarity*: The paper is generally well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and a logical flow from problem statement to solution. The experimental design is thorough, and the ablation studies (e.g., Figure 2) effectively demonstrate the model's behavior.

**Weaknesses**  
*Quality*: While the theoretical contribution is strong, the experimental section would benefit from more detailed hyperparameter analysis. The paper does not adequately explain how key parameters (e.g., the bandwidth parameter σ for the exponential triggering kernel, the latent rank, and the regularization parameters λ, β, ρ) were selected. Sensitivity analysis for σ would strengthen the empirical claims. Additionally, the comparison to a simple ensemble of STiC (for temporal dynamics) and SVD (for cold-start items) would provide a more meaningful baseline, as the authors acknowledge STiC cannot handle new items.

*Clarity*: The manuscript contains several grammatical errors and unclear phrasing (e.g., "the two matrices of coefficients Λ0 and A contain too many parameters" should be "the two matrices Λ0 and A contain too many parameters"). The description of the triggering kernel parametrization (Section 3.3) could be more precise. The paper also lacks a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm relative to baselines.

*Experimental Rigor*: The paper does not provide sufficient details about the real-world datasets (e.g., average number of events per user-item pair, temporal irregularity metrics), which would help contextualize the results. For instance, it would be valuable to know how many users have multiple events per item (indicating recurrent behavior) versus those with only one event (which would not benefit from the temporal model).

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of temporal modeling and recommendation systems. The technical approach is sound, the problem is significant, and the empirical results are promising. However, the experimental section would be substantially strengthened by addressing the gaps in hyperparameter analysis and baseline comparisons. With minor revisions to improve clarity, provide more experimental details, and address the baseline comparison, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Pro/Con Summary**  
*Pros*: Novel combination of point processes and low-rank models; rigorous theoretical analysis; strong empirical results on large-scale datasets; practical relevance to real-world recommendation systems.  
*Cons*: Limited hyperparameter sensitivity analysis; insufficient baseline comparison (e.g., STiC+SVD ensemble); missing details on dataset characteristics; grammatical issues and unclear phrasing in parts of the manuscript.
