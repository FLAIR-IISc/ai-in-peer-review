This paper proposes a time-sensitive recommendation framework that models recurrent user activities with Hawkes processes and transfers statistical strength across user–item pairs via low-rank structure. For each user–item pair, the intensity is a sum of a baseline term and a history-dependent self-exciting component. To generalize across the matrix of user–item pairs, the baseline and excitation parameters are constrained to be low-rank via nuclear norms. The authors formulate parameter learning as a convex problem and introduce an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient (to handle nonnegativity) with conditional gradient (for nuclear norms), achieving an O(1/t) convergence rate. They evaluate two tasks—item recommendation at a given time and return-time prediction—on large synthetic and real datasets, reporting strong performance and scalability. The approach is positioned against survival-analysis-based return-time models (e.g., [11,12]), traditional matrix/tensor factorization, and a Hawkes-process literature that includes low-rank and networked variants.

Quality: The paper takes a principled approach to modeling temporal engagement signals with point processes, and the optimization method is well-motivated and efficient. I do have two concerns:

- Regarding Eq. (8) and Theorem 2: In Eq. (8), over which variables is the “max” taken? Is it over ρ? If so, can ρ* be unbounded? The current statement is ambiguous and could be clarified to avoid interpretational issues about existence and finiteness.

- Theorem 2 establishes a convergence rate, but in the experiments ρ is tuned heuristically rather than set according to the theorem. This weakens the practical role of the theory. A discussion on how the theorem informs the choice of ρ, or why it cannot, would help.

Baselines: Including SVD++ [17] (winner of the Netflix Prize) would strengthen the empirical comparison. To my knowledge, SVD++ is among the strongest baselines for time-sensitive recommendation. While I’m not dissatisfied with the current baselines, adding SVD++ would improve completeness.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. Aside from the ambiguity around Eq. (8)/ρ*, the exposition is easy to follow.

Originality: The work is novel in connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank modeling for recommendation and in formulating a convex program with an efficient solver for this setting. Time-sensitive recommendation remains relatively underexplored compared to rating prediction.

Significance: Time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction are impactful problems. The proposed method is scalable, achieves strong empirical results, and appears amenable to distributed implementation, which increases its practical relevance.

Pros:
- Clear and well-motivated formulation connecting Hawkes processes with low-rank structure.
- Convex learning objective with an efficient algorithm combining proximal and conditional gradients; solid convergence analysis.
- Strong scalability and performance on large synthetic and real datasets for both ranking and return-time prediction.
- Flexible to incorporate contextual features.

Cons:
- Ambiguity in Eq. (8) (definition of the “max” and conditions ensuring ρ* is finite).
- Theorem 2 is not tied to the experimental hyperparameter selection (especially ρ), reducing its practical impact.
- Missing a strong time-aware baseline such as SVD++ [17], which would better situate the gains.
- Minor mismatch between the theoretical guidance and empirical tuning strategy.

Overall, this is a strong submission with a principled model, a practical algorithm, and convincing results. Addressing the ambiguity around ρ* and expanding the baseline set (e.g., adding SVD++) would further strengthen the paper.
