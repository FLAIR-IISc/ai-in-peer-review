This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation and predicting user returning times, leveraging self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to capture temporal patterns in user-item interactions. The core contribution lies in connecting these two areas and developing a convex formulation with an efficient optimization algorithm scaling to large datasets. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate competitive performance.

This work builds upon a growing interest in modeling sequential user behavior, moving beyond traditional collaborative filtering approaches that primarily focus on static preferences. Recent NIPS work, such as [11, 12], has explored temporal dynamics in recommendation, but often with limitations in generalization or specific assumptions about event types. This paper distinguishes itself by proposing a unified framework applicable to both recommendation and return-time prediction, and by emphasizing scalability through a novel optimization algorithm. The connection to Hawkes processes is also well-established in related fields like social network analysis [4, 6], providing a solid theoretical foundation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for this problem appears novel. The convex formulation and scalable optimization algorithm are valuable contributions.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation and convergence analysis for the proposed algorithm (Theorem 2).
*   **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to handle large-scale datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.
*   **Unified Framework:** Addressing both time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction within a single model is a significant strength.
*   **Potential for Extensibility:** The authors correctly point out the ability to easily incorporate additional contextual information, expanding the model’s applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Motivation & Problem Relevance:** The paper lacks a strong initial motivation for the importance of modeling time-varying user preferences or predicting return times. The benefits of accurate return-time prediction for web companies are stated but could be elaborated upon with concrete examples.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** The experimental evaluation would be significantly strengthened by including simpler baselines for return-time prediction, such as predicting based on the last observed time delay or the most frequent time delay. The comparison to SVD, while present, isn't clearly framed in terms of the magnitude of the performance gain. It’s unclear if the observed differences are practically significant.
*   **Evaluation Task Complexity:** Predicting the *exact* returning time is a very challenging task. The authors might consider a more manageable task, such as classifying return times into discrete intervals (e.g., short, medium, long delay), which could provide a clearer evaluation of the model’s predictive power.
*   **Data Analysis:** The paper asserts the presence of time-varying user preferences but lacks analysis to support this claim on the real datasets. Investigating the nature of this variability (e.g., driven by external factors like seasonality or trends) would strengthen the argument.
*   **Clarity & Context:** The paragraph beginning "Alternatively, for an inhomogeneous..." in Section 2 contains a sudden shift in context mentioning "retweets and link creation," which is unclear and unrelated to the preceding discussion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to a challenging problem in recommendation systems.
*   The scalable optimization algorithm is a valuable contribution, enabling application to real-world datasets.
*   The potential for incorporating additional contextual information enhances the model’s flexibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of strong motivation and thorough baseline comparisons weakens the impact of the experimental results.
*   The overly ambitious return-time prediction task makes it difficult to assess the model’s true performance.
*   The absence of analysis supporting the claim of significant time-varying user preferences reduces confidence in the problem formulation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to time-sensitive recommendation. However, the lack of robust motivation, comparison to essential baselines, and clear analysis of the data limits its current impact. I rate this as **marginal accept**. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by strengthening the experimental evaluation and providing a more compelling justification for the problem, would significantly increase the paper’s value.
