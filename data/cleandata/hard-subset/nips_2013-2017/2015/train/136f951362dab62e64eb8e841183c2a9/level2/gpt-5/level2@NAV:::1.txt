This paper tackles two time-sensitive recommendation problems—ranking items at the right moment and predicting user return times—by linking self-exciting point processes (Hawkes) with low-rank models over user–item pairs. The authors propose a convex formulation with nuclear-norm regularization on base intensities and self-excitation parameters, and design a scalable algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient (for nuclear norms), with an O(1/t) rate and per-iteration costs avoiding full SVDs. Empirically, on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC-II), the method outperforms Poisson, SVD, survival-model STiC, and Poisson tensor factorization baselines on (i) time-sensitive ranking of the ground-truth item and (ii) return-time MAE. The work relates to prior lines on temporal point processes and Hawkes modeling in ML (e.g., Du/Zhou/Song) and to tensor/matrix factorization for recommendation; its novelty lies in a convex, low-rank Hawkes formulation for user–item dynamics with a tailored, scalable optimizer.

Quality: The modeling choices are technically sound and well-motivated; the likelihood and convex relaxation are standard and correctly used. Theorems on equivalence under a penalty parameter and convergence are plausible, though the threshold condition for the penalty (ρ*) is not operationalized. Experiments are extensive and suggest scalability and accuracy gains; however, statistical significance, uncertainty, and ablations (e.g., kernel choices, rank sensitivity) are missing.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized, with useful algorithm sketches. Some notation is dense, and the text alternates between O(1/t) and O(1/t^2) claims—clarify when each applies. Practical guidance for choosing ρ, λ, β, step sizes, and bandwidth is limited.

Originality: The integration of low-rank nuclear-norm regularization into Hawkes-based user–item modeling with a composite proximal/conditional-gradient solver is novel relative to survival/tensor baselines and prior Hawkes network estimation.

Significance: The ability to time recommendations and predict return times is practically valuable; results suggest the approach advances the state of the art and should interest both recommender-systems and TPP communities.

Limitations and suggestions:
- The model assumes self-excitation per user–item pair; consider mutual excitation (item–item, user–user) and/or shared kernels.
- Report variance/confidence, calibration of return-time predictions, and statistical tests.
- Provide sensitivity analyses (rank, σ, ρ) and ablations (nuclear norm vs. factorization).
- Compare with stronger recent TPP baselines (e.g., nonparametric Hawkes; learning kernels).
- Detail memory/throughput and provide code for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear problem focus with strong practical relevance.
- Convincing formulation and scalable algorithm.
- Solid empirical gains across diverse datasets.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited analysis of design choices and statistical rigor.
- Missing comparisons to some contemporary TPP methods.
- Practical hyperparameter selection and reproducibility details are sparse.
