This paper proposes a novel framework connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation, addressing the intertwined problems of recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and predicting user return times. The core idea is to model user-item interactions as a Hawkes process with low-rank parameterizations, enabling knowledge transfer between items and efficient optimization. The authors develop an algorithm with provable convergence guarantees and demonstrate superior performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets compared to several baselines. 

The work is technically sound and addresses a relevant problem in the recommender systems literature. Existing methods often overlook the temporal dynamics and recurrent patterns of user behavior, a gap this paper effectively attempts to fill. The convex formulation and the proposed optimization algorithm – blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods – are significant contributions. The theoretical analysis establishing the O(1/t) convergence rate is a strength, and the empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The scalability of the algorithm to large-scale datasets is also a positive aspect. The ability to incorporate additional contextual information, as mentioned by the authors, broadens the applicability of the framework. 

Several areas could benefit from further clarification and refinement. While the paper mentions related work, specifically Kapoor et al. [12, 11], it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of their limitations and how the proposed approach overcomes them beyond simply stating that their model “cannot generalize to recommend any new item in future time”. A deeper comparison with methods leveraging recurrent neural networks (RNNs) for sequence modeling, which are increasingly prevalent in recommendation tasks, would also strengthen the positioning of the work.  The discussion of the choice of the exponential kernel for the triggering function could be expanded. Justifying this choice, or exploring the performance sensitivity to other kernel functions, would be valuable. 

The experimental section is well-structured, but the explanations for the parameter tuning and model selection are brief. Providing more details on the criteria used for choosing hyperparameters (ρ, λ, β, kernel bandwidth, rank) would enhance reproducibility. The presentation of results could also be improved. While the figures are informative, providing statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) for the performance comparisons would increase confidence in the findings. The description of the datasets, while present, could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding the characteristics of the real-world data (e.g., sparsity, distribution of event times). 

The paper’s clarity is generally good, but some sections are dense with mathematical notation. Adding more intuitive explanations alongside the equations could improve readability for a broader audience. The use of consistent notation throughout the paper would also be helpful.

Originality: The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation is novel.  The proposed optimization algorithm and the theoretical convergence analysis also contribute to the originality of the work. 

Significance:  The results suggest that incorporating temporal dynamics and recurrent patterns can significantly improve recommendation performance. The potential applications in areas like personalized mobile services, ad bidding, and electronic health records are substantial, indicating the significance of this work. 

Arguments for acceptance:
* Novel and technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
* Strong theoretical guarantees (O(1/t) convergence rate).
* Superior performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* Scalability to large-scale problems.

Arguments against acceptance:
* Limited discussion of related work, particularly RNN-based approaches.
* Lack of statistical significance tests in the experimental results.
* Dense mathematical notation in some sections could hinder readability.
* Brief explanation of parameter tuning and model selection.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential to impact the field of recommender systems. The authors address an important problem with a novel and well-supported approach. Addressing the minor concerns outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. A recommendation for acceptance is warranted, contingent on minor revisions to address the points raised.
