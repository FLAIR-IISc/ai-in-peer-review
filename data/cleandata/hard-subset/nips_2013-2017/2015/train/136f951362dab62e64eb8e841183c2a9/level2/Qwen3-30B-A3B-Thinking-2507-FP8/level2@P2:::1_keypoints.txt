- Proposes a novel framework linking Hawkes (self-exciting) point processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation.  
- Targets two tasks: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and predicting users' next return times.  
- Introduces a convex optimization formulation (Eq.4) solvable by a new algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods.  
- Optimization algorithm achieves O(1/t) convergence and is supported by theoretical guarantees (Theorem 2).  
- Demonstrates scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of events).  
- Empirical results on synthetic and real datasets show improved predictive performance over state-of-the-art baselines.  
- Main strength: rigorous theoretical contribution connecting Hawkes processes and low-rank matrix models.  
- Strength: well-motivated convex formulation and an efficient algorithm with proven convergence.  
- Strength: thorough empirical evaluation (convergence, scalability, and multiple real-world datasets).  
- Claims ability to incorporate contextual information, enhancing applicability (but not empirically demonstrated).  
- Weakness: lacks statistical significance testing to support claims of superior predictive performance.  
- Weakness: related work discussion is insufficient, not clearly differentiated from other Hawkes-based recommendation work (e.g., Du et al.).  
- Weakness: claim of generalization to new items is supported only by synthetic experiments, lacking real-data evidence.  
- Weakness: limited empirical validation of the low-rank assumption for Λ0 and A on real datasets.  
- Weakness: no experiments showing the effect of incorporating extra contextual information.  
- Suggestions: add statistical significance tests, expand related work comparison, validate low-rank assumption (e.g., singular values), and provide experiments demonstrating contextual feature benefits.  
- Overall: theoretically sound, practically relevant, scalable approach; promising candidate for acceptance if empirical claims are made more rigorous.
