**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation that addresses two key problems: recommending the right item at a given moment and predicting a user's next return time to a service. The core idea is to model the sequence of a user's interactions with an item as a self-exciting Hawkes process. This captures both a user's baseline interest in an item and the short-term influence of past interactions. To generalize across all users and items and handle data sparsity, the parameters of these Hawkes processes (the base intensity and the self-excitation strength) are organized into matrices which are assumed to be low-rank. This insightfully connects the temporal point process literature with classical low-rank models from collaborative filtering. The authors formulate parameter estimation as a convex optimization problem with nuclear norm regularization. A major technical contribution is a novel and efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods to solve this problem, which is difficult due to the combination of non-negativity constraints and non-smooth nuclear norm penalties. The authors provide a theoretical convergence guarantee for their algorithm. The proposed model is evaluated on both synthetic and large-scale real-world datasets, demonstrating superior performance over several state-of-the-art baselines on both recommendation and return-time prediction tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and elegant synthesis of two distinct fields: temporal point processes and collaborative filtering. By framing the parameters of user-item specific Hawkes processes as entries in low-rank matrices, the authors provide a principled and powerful way to model rich, continuous-time user dynamics at scale. This addresses an important and relatively under-explored area in recommender systems, moving beyond static ratings to dynamic user engagement. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically very strong. The model formulation is well-motivated and leads to a convex objective function. The development of the optimization algorithm is a significant contribution in its own right. Recognizing the difficulty of the original formulation, the authors propose a clever alternative formulation and an efficient hybrid algorithm that achieves an optimal convergence rate. The inclusion of a convergence proof (Theorem 2) provides strong theoretical support for the proposed method.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   The use of both synthetic and three diverse real-world datasets (music, e-commerce, and healthcare) demonstrates the broad applicability of the model.
    *   The experiments directly test the two claimed contributions: time-sensitive item ranking and return-time prediction.
    *   The chosen baselines are appropriate and represent strong competitors, including static matrix factorization, a hidden semi-Markov model approach, and a state-of-the-art tensor factorization method.
    *   The model validation using the time-change theorem (Figure 3) is an excellent addition, providing empirical evidence that the Hawkes process assumption is a better fit for the data than simpler Poisson or survival models.
    *   The scalability experiments (Figure 2d) effectively demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed algorithm.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While the paper is generally well-written, some details regarding the optimization algorithm could be clarified to improve reproducibility. For instance, explicitly writing out the form of the gradients `∇f` used in Algorithm 1 would be helpful for readers wishing to implement the method. Furthermore, Theorem 1 establishes the equivalence of the two optimization problems based on a threshold `ρ*` that seems difficult to compute in practice. The paper states `ρ` is tuned; a brief discussion on the model's sensitivity to this hyperparameter would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Model Assumptions:** The paper assumes an exponential triggering kernel for simplicity in the experiments. While the framework allows for other kernels, the performance with only this simple kernel is reported. It would be interesting to discuss the potential impact of using more flexible, or even non-parametrically learned, kernels, and whether the optimization would remain as tractable. The model also focuses on self-excitation (an interaction with item *i* makes a future interaction with item *i* more likely) but does not model mutual-excitation (an interaction with item *i* influences a future interaction with item *j*). While this is a reasonable simplification, acknowledging this as a potential direction for future work would be valuable.

3.  **Comparison Details:** The comparison with the tensor factorization baseline could be elaborated upon. Tensor methods require time discretization, and the choice of interval length can significantly affect performance. It would be useful to know if this was tuned for the baseline and how the chosen discretization might compare to the continuous-time nature of the proposed model.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper presenting a novel, significant, and technically sound contribution. It tackles an important problem with a creative and effective solution, backed by strong theoretical analysis and a thorough empirical evaluation. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and represents a clear advance in the field of recommender systems and temporal data modeling. I strongly recommend acceptance.
