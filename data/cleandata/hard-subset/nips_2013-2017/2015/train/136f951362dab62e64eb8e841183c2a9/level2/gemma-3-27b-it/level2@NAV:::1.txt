## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel framework combining self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to address time-sensitive recommendation and user returning-time prediction. The core idea of leveraging Hawkes processes to model user activity recurrence, coupled with low-rank factorization for scalability, is a promising approach to capturing temporal dynamics often ignored in traditional recommendation systems. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods—particularly their inability to handle temporal behavior and sparseness of data—and position their work as a solution.

The strengths of the paper lie in its technical soundness and potential significance. The convex formulation of the problem and the developed optimization algorithm, with its O(1/t) convergence rate and scalability claims, are substantial contributions. The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate superior performance compared to several baselines, including STiC and tensor factorization methods. The authors also acknowledge limitations and suggest potential extensions, such as incorporating additional contextual information. 

However, the paper has weaknesses. While the authors compare to relevant work, the description of some baselines (e.g., Tensor factorization) feels cursory.  A more detailed comparison, particularly highlighting specific implementation details, would strengthen the results. Furthermore, the discussion of the real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) lacks depth regarding data preprocessing, parameter tuning strategies, and the specific characteristics impacting the results. The convergence analysis, while mathematically sound, could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the constants involved and their impact on practical performance.  The connection to existing work on Hawkes processes is mostly limited to citation and a brief discussion of differences; a more comprehensive review, especially concerning recent advances in scalable Hawkes process estimation, would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating improved performance.
*   Scalable optimization algorithm with theoretical guarantees.
*   Clear problem definition and motivation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited detail in baseline comparison and dataset description.
*   Convergence analysis could be more accessible.
*   Missing broader contextualization within the Hawkes process literature.

**Overall:**

This is a solid paper with a well-defined contribution. While some areas could be strengthened with more detail and discussion, the novelty, technical rigor, and promising results warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions to address the points raised above. The authors should consider expanding on the baseline comparisons and providing more comprehensive details about their experiments and the limitations of their approach.
