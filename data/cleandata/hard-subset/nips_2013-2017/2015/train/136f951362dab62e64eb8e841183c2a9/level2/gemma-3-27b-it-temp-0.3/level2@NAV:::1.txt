## Review of "Time-Sensitive Recommendation From Recurrent User Activities"

This paper proposes a novel framework combining self-exciting point processes and low-rank models to address time-sensitive recommendation and user returning-time prediction. The core idea is to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item interactions, moving beyond traditional preference-based recommendation. The authors present a convex formulation of the problem and develop an efficient optimization algorithm with a provable O(1/t) convergence rate, demonstrating scalability to large datasets.

The paper builds upon recent work in temporal recommendation, specifically citing Kapoor et al. [12, 11], but distinguishes itself by offering a more generalizable approach capable of recommending *new* items, unlike the survival analysis-based methods. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a key contribution, allowing for knowledge transfer across user-item pairs. The theoretical analysis of the optimization algorithm is a strong point, and the empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) supports the effectiveness of the proposed method. The ablation studies and comparisons against baselines (Poisson process, STiC, SVD, Tensor Factorization) are well-conducted and demonstrate clear improvements.

A strength of this work is its completeness. The paper provides a detailed mathematical formulation, a clear algorithmic description, and a thorough experimental evaluation. The authors also acknowledge limitations, such as the choice of the exponential kernel and the need for parameter tuning. The discussion of potential extensions, including incorporating additional contextual information, is also valuable. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the computational cost of the proposed algorithm, particularly concerning the SVD steps within the conditional gradient method. While scalability is claimed, a more detailed analysis of runtime performance with varying dataset sizes would be beneficial. Furthermore, the presentation of results could be streamlined; the numerous figures and tables, while comprehensive, can feel overwhelming. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees (O(1/t) convergence).
*   Empirically validated performance on multiple datasets.
*   Clear and comprehensive presentation of the method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity analysis could be more detailed.
*   Presentation of results could be more concise.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of temporal recommendation. The technical soundness, clarity, and empirical results justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above.
