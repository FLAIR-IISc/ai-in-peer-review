This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by combining self-exciting point processes and low-rank models. The core idea is to model user-item interactions as a Hawkes process, leveraging its ability to capture temporal dependencies, and then use low-rank factorization to generalize across users and items. The authors provide a convex formulation of the problem, an efficient optimization algorithm with a provable convergence rate, and demonstrate strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial gap in traditional recommendation systems that often overlook temporal dynamics. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a novel and potentially powerful approach. The proposed optimization algorithm, blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, appears to be a significant contribution, especially given the scale of the problems considered (millions of user-item pairs). The experiments are reasonably thorough, comparing against several relevant baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The inclusion of theoretical convergence guarantees further strengthens the work.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is present, the derivation of the convergence rate (O(1/t)) is not fully detailed and relies on references to prior work. Expanding on this would increase clarity. The experimental section, while showing strong results, could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the model's behavior. For example, a visualization of the learned low-rank factors or an ablation study to assess the contribution of different components (e.g., the self-exciting component versus the low-rank component) would be valuable. 

The comparison to related work is mostly adequate, but some relevant papers could be considered. For instance, work on dynamic graph embeddings or recurrent neural networks for sequential recommendation (e.g., GRU4Rec, SASRec) that also address temporal dependencies might be worth discussing. While the authors address Kapoor et al. (2014), a broader consideration of related approaches beyond just survival analysis would strengthen the positioning of the work.  The connection to related work on temporal point processes beyond Hawkes processes could be elaborated upon.  

Clarity could be improved in a few places. The notation is dense, and a glossary or more detailed explanation of some symbols would be helpful. The discussion of the algorithm’s parameter settings could be more transparent. For example, how were ρ, λ, and β chosen in the experiments? Providing more insight into these choices would increase reproducibility. The writing quality is generally good, but some sentences are overly complex and could be simplified.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation is a novel and promising approach.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The convergence analysis provides strong theoretical support for the proposed algorithm.
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm is shown to scale to large datasets with millions of user-item pairs.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experiments demonstrate superior performance compared to several baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Detail in Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence rate derivation lacks full details.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Analysis:** More in-depth analysis of model behavior and ablation studies are needed.
*   **Related Work Coverage:** The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive.
*   **Clarity:** Some notation and algorithmic details could be explained more clearly.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in recommendation systems.
*   The theoretical analysis and empirical results are strong.
*   The algorithm is scalable and demonstrates promising performance on real-world data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of detailed explanations in the theoretical analysis and the limited experimental analysis could be concerns.
*   The related work section could benefit from a more thorough review of existing literature.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution and should be accepted. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the strong empirical performance outweigh the minor weaknesses. Addressing the suggestions for improvement, particularly regarding the clarity of the theoretical analysis and the depth of the experimental analysis, would further strengthen the paper.
