

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes (specifically Hawkes processes) with low-rank matrix models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption data. The work addresses two key challenges: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and predicting the next returning time of users to services. The authors formulate the problem as a convex optimization task with nuclear norm regularization, develop an efficient algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and demonstrate its scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs, hundreds of millions of temporal events). The approach is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II), showing superior performance compared to several baselines including Poisson processes, SVD, tensor factorization, and STiC.

The paper makes several strong contributions. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models for recommendation represents a meaningful novel approach to time-sensitive recommendation, which has been largely overlooked in the literature. The convex formulation with nuclear norm regularization is well-motivated and properly justified, addressing the scalability challenges of previous approaches. The convergence analysis in Theorem 2 is rigorous and demonstrates the algorithm achieves optimal convergence rates, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The empirical results are comprehensive, with thorough comparisons across multiple datasets and evaluation metrics (item recommendation rank, returning-time prediction error), and the ablation studies effectively demonstrate the value of the temporal modeling.

The paper excels in clarity, with a logical structure that gradually builds the technical foundation. The background on temporal point processes is concise yet sufficient, and the model formulation is well-explained with appropriate mathematical notation. The experimental setup is transparent, and the results are presented in a way that clearly demonstrates the advantages of the proposed approach. The discussion of how the model can incorporate contextual information (user profiles, spatial features) provides a strong foundation for future extensions.

Regarding originality, the paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly Kapoor et al. [11,12], by addressing the critical limitation that their approach cannot generalize to recommend new items. The paper also properly contextualizes its work within the broader literature on temporal recommendation systems, including recent advances in tensor factorization and survival analysis. The convex formulation and optimization algorithm represent substantial technical innovations beyond previous approaches.

The significance of this work is evident in its potential applications across multiple domains: personalized recommendations in e-commerce, music streaming, and healthcare. The ability to predict returning times has direct implications for marketing strategies (e.g., more economical ad bidding) and proactive healthcare interventions. The scalability to large datasets makes the approach practically relevant for real-world applications.

One minor weakness is the limited discussion of computational complexity in the experimental section. While the paper claims scalability to "millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events," a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's runtime performance would strengthen the empirical evaluation. Additionally, while the paper mentions the ability to incorporate contextual information, the experimental section does not demonstrate this explicitly, leaving this aspect somewhat underexplored.

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The convex formulation is properly justified, the convergence analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results consistently demonstrate superior performance across multiple evaluation metrics. The authors are appropriately careful in discussing both the strengths and limitations of their approach.

The main strength of the paper is its novel integration of Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix models for time-sensitive recommendation, which addresses an important gap in the literature. The theoretical contributions (convex formulation, convergence analysis) and practical scalability make this work particularly valuable for the recommendation systems community.

The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field by providing a principled approach to time-sensitive recommendation that is both theoretically sound and practically scalable. The work advances the state of the art in temporal recommendation systems by effectively capturing recurrent user activities through a unified framework that enables both item recommendation and returning-time prediction. The proposed algorithm achieves optimal convergence rates while maintaining computational efficiency, which is a significant practical contribution. The connections to real-world applications in e-commerce, music streaming, and healthcare further enhance the significance of this work. The paper would be strengthened by more detailed experimental analysis of the algorithm's runtime characteristics and explicit demonstration of contextual information incorporation, but these are reasonable suggestions for future work rather than critical weaknesses. Overall, the paper is of high quality, makes significant contributions, and is well-suited for publication at NIPS.
