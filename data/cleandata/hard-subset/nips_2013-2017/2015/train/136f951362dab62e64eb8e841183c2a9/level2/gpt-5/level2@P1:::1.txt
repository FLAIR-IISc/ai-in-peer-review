Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses two time-sensitive recommendation tasks: (1) recommending the right item at the right time and (2) predicting users’ next return times. The core idea is to model each user–item interaction stream as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j γ(t, tu,ij), where Λ0 captures long-term preference and A the self-excitation strength. To enable generalization across sparse user–item pairs, Λ0 and A are endowed with low-rank structure via nuclear norm regularization, yielding a convex estimation problem for the negative log-likelihood plus nuclear norms under nonnegativity. The authors propose an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient (for nonnegativity) with conditional gradient (for nuclear norms) using a penalty-based splitting; they prove an O(1/t) convergence bound. Empirical results on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) indicate strong performance on both ranking-at-time and return-time prediction, and a time-change diagnostic suggests Hawkes better matches real sequences than survival baselines.

This connects lines of work in temporal point processes and recommender systems: Hawkes processes [Hawkes’71; Daley & Vere-Jones] for self-excitation; low-rank estimation via nuclear norms in recommendation; and comparison to tensor factorization with Poisson loss. Related continuous-time return-time prediction via survival models [Kapoor et al.] and low-rank/multidimensional Hawkes (e.g., Zhou et al., AISTATS’13; ICML’13) are cited. The formulation differs by coupling per-pair Hawkes with a low-rank parameterization to transfer across unobserved pairs and support time-sensitive recommendation.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and unification: the framework handles both time-sensitive item ranking and return-time prediction.
- Convex learning objective with a principled, scalable algorithm that decouples nonnegativity and nuclear norms; practical O(1/t) convergence guarantee.
- Solid empirical evaluation at scale (millions of pairs, 100M events in synthetic) and across domains; meaningful diagnostics (time-change plots) to validate modeling assumptions.
- Demonstrates benefits over strong baselines, including Poisson process, tensor factorization with Poisson regression, and survival/HMM-based return-time methods.
- Flexible extensions discussed (context features, tensorization, nonparametric kernels).

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Stability/regularity of Hawkes processes is not addressed formally. In the self-exciting case, a standard sufficient condition is A(u,i) ∫ γ < 1 to avoid explosion; enforcing or monitoring this (e.g., via constraints or reparameterization) would strengthen the modeling.
- Identifiability between Λ0 and A may be problematic (e.g., a higher base vs. stronger excitation can trade off). Some discussion or diagnostics (learned distributions, sensitivity) would help.
- The equivalence theorem for the penalty formulation requires ρ > ρ*, but ρ* depends on unknown optima. In practice ρ is tuned. A practical recipe (e.g., continuation schedule, bounds) or sensitivity analysis would be valuable.
- The LMO requires top singular vectors of gradient matrices of size m×n; a runtime/memory breakdown and comparison to ADMM or proximal-SVD with partial SVDs would clarify the actual gains.
- The model uses only self-excitation at the user–item level. Cross-item influence (within-user) and mutual excitation (across users/items) are not modeled; this may be crucial in music or shopping. Even a brief experiment with a block-structured or factorized cross-excitation would be informative.
- Evaluation metrics for recommendation are limited to the rank of the true item; reporting recall@k/NDCG@k and calibration of return-time predictions (e.g., coverage of prediction intervals) would increase practical relevance.
- Cold-start: while the model can, in principle, incorporate context, experiments do not evaluate new users/items, where the temporal structure is most beneficial.

Quality
- The formulation is technically sound, with correct likelihood and convexity under the chosen parameterization and constraints. The algorithmic splitting is plausible and the convergence proof (sketched) yields clear rates.
- Some missing technical details: how L (Lipschitz constant) and step sizes are set; enforcement of Hawkes stability; complexity of Ogata sampling for return-time; memory footprint at scale.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the modeling and algorithm are explained cleanly. The connection between the accelerated scheme and the two blocks is helpful.
- Suggest clarifying: (i) stability constraints; (ii) precise computational costs per iteration and wall-clock comparisons; (iii) a brief derivation of ψ for general kernels; (iv) details of hyperparameter tuning and early stopping.

Originality
- The coupling of per-pair Hawkes with low-rank transfer for time-sensitive recommendation appears novel. The algorithmic blend of proximal and conditional gradient for this specific constrained/regularized composite is a nice contribution.

Significance
- Addresses an important and timely problem; results suggest improvements over competitive baselines on multiple datasets. The approach is likely to influence further work on continuous-time recommendation and temporal modeling.

Questions for the authors
- How do you ensure or check Hawkes stability (non-explosion) during training and prediction? Did you observe pathological intensities?
- How is L chosen in practice, and how sensitive is convergence to step-size choices?
- What are the per-iteration and total training times vs. ADMM and proximal-SVD with truncated SVD? Any GPU use?
- How many samples are drawn via Ogata’s thinning for return-time prediction, and what is the runtime per prediction?
- Can you report recommendation metrics at multiple k, and performance on true cold-start with available side features?
- Did you try alternative kernels (Weibull, Log-logistic) or nonparametric kernels, and how did this affect performance?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, convex, and scalable continuous-time recommendation model with strong empirical gains.
- Solid theoretical treatment of optimization with convergence rates.
- Clear applicability across domains and meaningful diagnostics.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited treatment of stability/identifiability and lack of constraints ensuring valid Hawkes dynamics.
- Evaluation could be more comprehensive (metrics, baselines with cross-excitation, cold-start).
- Practical guidance on penalty parameter and runtime comparisons could be strengthened.

Overall
A well-motivated and technically solid paper that advances time-sensitive recommendation by marrying Hawkes processes with low-rank transfer and an efficient solver. Despite some caveats (stability, broader evaluation), the contribution is significant and of interest to the NIPS community. I lean toward acceptance, provided the authors address the stability and evaluation concerns in the rebuttal and final version.
