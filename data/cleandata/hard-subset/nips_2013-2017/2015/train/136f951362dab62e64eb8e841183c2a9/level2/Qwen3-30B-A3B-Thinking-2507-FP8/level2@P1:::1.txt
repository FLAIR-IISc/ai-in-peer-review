

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption data. The authors address two important but underexplored questions in recommendation systems: (1) how to recommend the most relevant item at the right moment, and (2) how to predict the next returning time of users to services. The main technical contributions include a convex optimization formulation that combines Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix constraints, an efficient algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, and theoretical convergence analysis.

The paper is well-organized and provides a clear background on temporal point processes, which helps readers unfamiliar with the topic. The connection between self-exciting point processes and low-rank models is novel and well-motivated, with the authors appropriately addressing limitations of existing approaches (particularly Kapoor et al.'s work). The convex formulation (Equation 4) is theoretically sound, and the authors provide a thorough convergence analysis (Theorem 2) showing the algorithm achieves optimal convergence rates. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with both synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II medical dataset), and the authors compare against multiple relevant baselines including Poisson processes, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses an important gap in recommendation systems by explicitly modeling temporal dynamics rather than focusing solely on static preferences.
2. The convex formulation and efficient optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) are theoretically grounded and achieve O(1/t) convergence, which is significant for scalability to large datasets.
3. The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over baselines on both item recommendation and returning-time prediction tasks, with the Hawkes model better capturing temporal dynamics than competing approaches.
4. The framework is flexible and can incorporate additional contextual information (user profiles, spatial features, etc.), making it broadly applicable.

Weaknesses:
1. While the paper mentions that the model can incorporate additional contextual information, there is limited discussion on how exactly this would be implemented or the specific benefits of doing so for different contexts. A more concrete example would strengthen this claim.
2. The paper does not adequately address how the model handles cold-start problems (new users or new items with few or no historical events), which is a common challenge in recommendation systems and particularly relevant for "next returning time" prediction.
3. The evaluation metrics could be more thoroughly explained. For item recommendation, the paper uses "predicted rank" but doesn't specify whether this is the average rank or some other aggregation method across items and users.
4. The comparison with related work could be more detailed. While the authors mention Kapoor et al., they don't discuss other relevant papers in temporal recommendation, such as Xiong et al.'s work on temporal collaborative filtering.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by bridging temporal point processes with low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendations. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling. The work is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical insights with practical applications in recommendation systems.

One minor suggestion: The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm compared to existing methods, especially for the largest datasets (hundreds of millions of events mentioned in the abstract). While the paper mentions scalability, a more explicit comparison of time complexity would be helpful.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of time-sensitive recommendation. The theoretical framework is novel, the algorithm is efficient and well-analyzed, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. I recommend acceptance.
