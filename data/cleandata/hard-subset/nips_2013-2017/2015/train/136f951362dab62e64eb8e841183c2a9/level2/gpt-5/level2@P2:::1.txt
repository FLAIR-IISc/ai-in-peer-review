Summary
The paper addresses time-sensitive recommendation by modeling recurrent user-item interactions with Hawkes point processes and sharing statistical strength across pairs via low-rank structure. Each user–item pair (u, i) follows a self-exciting Hawkes process with exponential kernel: λu,i(t) = Λ0(u,i) + A(u,i) ∑j exp(−(t − tu,ij)/σ). To generalize to unobserved pairs, the base intensities Λ0 and self-excitation coefficients A are constrained to be low rank via nuclear-norm regularization. Estimation is posed as convex minimization of the negative log-likelihood plus nuclear norms under elementwise nonnegativity. To avoid full SVDs, the authors introduce auxiliary variables Z1,Z2 and a quadratic penalty tying (Λ0,A) to (Z1,Z2). They derive a hybrid algorithm that applies projected (accelerated) proximal gradient on (Λ0,A) and conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe with rank-1 LMOs) on (Z1,Z2), with a global O(1/t) rate and per-iteration costs proportional to the number of events plus two top singular-vector computations. Experiments on large synthetic data (~100M events) and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) show improved item ranking and return-time prediction relative to Poisson (homogeneous), SVD, tensor factorization baselines, and a survival-analysis/HsMM method (STiC). The authors also include time-rescaling (QQ) diagnostics suggesting Hawkes fits better than survival models.

Relation to prior work
The modeling leverages classic Hawkes processes [Hawkes ’71] and recent learning of Hawkes kernels [Zhou et al. ’13; Du et al. ’15], but applies them to recommendation with low-rank parameter sharing. Compared to survival-based return-time prediction [Kapoor et al. ’14], the Hawkes model captures self-excitation and supports recommendations for unseen user–item pairs through low-rank structure. Optimization-wise, the paper builds on proximal/conditional gradient methods [Lan ’13; Harchaoui et al.], adapting them to a block-structured composite objective with nonnegativity and nuclear norms.

Strengths
- Clear, unified formulation linking continuous-time point processes with collaborative low-rank modeling; the likelihood is convex in the parameters with nonnegativity, making the overall estimator convex.
- Scalable algorithmic design: projecting onto the nonnegative orthant is cheap; nuclear-norm handling via FW with rank-1 LMOs avoids full SVDs; empirical scaling to millions of pairs and 100M events is compelling.
- Solid empirical evaluation: both item-at-time ranking and return-time prediction are considered; challenging held-out-item setting is included; QQ diagnostics for model fit are a nice touch.
- Theoretical convergence analysis for the hybrid scheme with explicit rates; practical per-iteration complexity is reasonable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Equivalence of the penalty reformulation: Theorem 1 claims exact equivalence between the original problem and the penalized formulation for finite ρ>ρ*. For quadratic penalties on equality constraints, exactness at finite ρ is generally not guaranteed without additional assumptions; moreover, ρ* depends on unknown optimal quantities. In practice you tune ρ, but the theoretical claim seems strong and needs clarifications and precise conditions.
- Modeling assumptions: only self-excitation within a user–item pair is modeled; there is no cross-excitation across items (e.g., artists or related products) or global exogenous periodicity (daily/weekly cycles). The baseline Λ0 is constant over time; thus “right moment” recommendations rely mainly on recent same-pair events. This may limit expressiveness in domains where exogenous rhythms dominate.
- Kernel flexibility: σ appears to be set globally. Allowing per-user/item (or learned) decay parameters would be valuable, though it would break convexity; at minimum, an ablation showing sensitivity to σ would help.
- Baseline fairness and breadth: tensor methods are sensitive to discretization; details on the number of intervals and tuning are missing. Additional baselines such as Poisson factorization with temporal components, renewal-process models, or Hawkes with grouped/shared parameters (e.g., per-user/item factors) would strengthen claims.
- Stability/branching ratio: the standard Hawkes stability condition (self-excitation mass < 1) is not discussed or enforced. Although finite-horizon likelihoods are well-defined, this omission could affect predictions and interpretability.
- Practicalities: return-time prediction is done via Monte Carlo (Ogata). The number of samples, runtimes, and variance are not reported. Reproducibility details (code, hyperparameters, stopping criteria) are limited in the main text.

Quality
- Technical soundness is mostly high: the convex estimator and gradients for exponential Hawkes are standard; the hybrid algorithm is plausible, and the rate (dominated by O(1/t)) matches expectations for FW. The exact-penalty claim (Theorem 1) is the weakest point and should be carefully revised or reframed as an approximation with asymptotic equivalence as ρ→∞.
- Empirical claims align with results shown, but more quantitative detail (tables with MAE/rank, CIs, wall-clock times, memory) would improve rigor.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. Some notation is inconsistent (λu,i0 vs Λ0(u,i)), and there are minor typos (“O(1/ )” in abstract; “feature ψu,i” instead of “vector ψu,i”). A brief complexity analysis per iteration would be helpful. Please specify the number of Monte Carlo samples used for return-time prediction.

Originality
- Combining Hawkes-based continuous-time modeling with low-rank nuclear-norm sharing for recommendation is novel and timely. The hybrid optimization scheme is a reasonable synthesis of known components; the claim of being “first of its kind” should be tempered given related conditional-gradient/proximal hybrids in the literature.

Significance
- The problem is important (time-sensitive personalization and return-time prediction), and the approach is likely to be useful to practitioners. Demonstrated scalability is a plus. Broader impact would increase with modeling of exogenous periodicities and/or cross-excitation.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Clarify Theorem 1: provide precise conditions under which finite-ρ exactness holds, or otherwise position (5) as a penalized approximation whose solutions approach those of (4) as ρ increases. Discuss how ρ is selected in practice and sensitivity.
- Discuss/ensure Hawkes stability constraints (e.g., bounds on A) or justify why they are unnecessary in your estimation/prediction regime.
- Add ablations on σ and on the rank (strength of nuclear norms). Consider a simple extension with a time-of-day covariate in Λ0 to capture periodicity.
- Report quantitative metrics with variances and runtimes; specify the number of Ogata samples and the computational overhead.
- Expand baseline details (number of tensor intervals, tuning ranges); consider adding a renewal-process or Cox process with time-varying baseline as a stronger temporal baseline.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated continuous-time, low-rank framework for recommendation and return-time prediction.
- Convex formulation with a practical, scalable algorithm; theoretical rate.
- Strong empirical performance and good-scale experiments; diagnostic checks.

Cons for acceptance
- Questionable exact-penalty equivalence; some overstatement in optimization novelty.
- Limited modeling of exogenous temporal structure and cross-item effects.
- Baseline/tuning details and runtime/reproducibility information are sparse.
