- Paper tackles two time-sensitive recommendation tasks: item ranking at a given time and next return-time prediction for user–item pairs.
- Models each user–item event sequence as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t)=λu,i0+αu,i∑γ(t,tu,ij).
- Shares strength across pairs via low-rank structure on base intensities Λ0 and excitation coefficients A enforced by nuclear-norm regularization.
- Parameter estimation is formulated as a convex program (negative log-likelihood + nuclear norms) with nonnegativity constraints.
- Introduces an auxiliary-variable formulation and a hybrid optimizer using proximal gradient for nonnegativity and conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe) for nuclear-norm variables.
- Provides convergence guarantees (overall O(1/t) rate with O(1/k^2) and O(1/k) components).
- Experiments on synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) show improved ranking and return-time prediction against several baselines.
- Framework can incorporate additional context and is positioned as broadly applicable beyond recommendation.
- Connects temporal point processes with low-rank modeling via nuclear norms, differing from prior Hawkes and influence-network work by focusing on user–item self-excitation and low-rank sharing.
- Compared to survival and semi-Markov approaches, Hawkes model captures history-dependent excitation and can recommend unseen items.
- Compared to tensor Poisson factorization, the model operates in continuous time and directly yields return-time distributions without discretization.
- With exponential kernels, the negative log-likelihood is convex in nonnegative parameters, making the overall objective convex.
- Mapping from intensity to item ranking and return-time density is consistent with point process theory.
- Auxiliary-variable penalty separates nonnegativity from nuclear-norm constraints, enabling scalable optimization.
- Hybrid algorithm is well-motivated; convergence bound explanations align with Frank–Wolfe behavior.
- Theorem 1 claims quadratic penalty exactness but depends on a problem-specific threshold ρ* involving optimal solutions, requiring more intuition or conditions for finiteness.
- Stability of Hawkes processes (branching ratio < 1) is not explicitly enforced or discussed in the formulation.
- Evaluation protocol (temporal holdout per user and fully held-out sequences) is appropriate and baselines are relevant.
- Requested additional evaluation details: standard ranking metrics (Recall@k, NDCG, Hit@k), calibration plots or CIs for return-time prediction, and practical inference details (Ogata thinning samples, computational overhead).
- Paper is well-organized with helpful derivations and readable pseudocode but contains minor typos and notational inconsistencies.
- Theorem assumptions (exactness) and optimizer settings (step sizes, Lipschitz constants) should be more explicitly stated.
- Main novelty: coupling Hawkes processes with low-rank nuclear-norm sharing and a scalable hybrid optimization scheme for time-sensitive recommendation.
- Significance: direct continuous-time ranking and return-time prediction, scalable to millions of pairs and ~10^8 events, with likely impact on temporal recommendation research.
- Strengths: principled convex continuous-time model, scalable optimization avoiding full SVDs, strong empirical results, and flexible framework.
- Weaknesses and suggestions: clarify/justify finite ρ* and provide sensitivity analysis; add or discuss stability constraints and report empirical branching ratios.
- Further suggestions: extend evaluation with Recall@k/NDCG/CIs, detail thinning/inference latency, include TimeSVD++ and temporal Poisson baselines, and perform ablations on kernel bandwidth, regularization weights, and ρ.
- Consider evaluating limited cross-item excitation and comparing to non-convex factorized variants to assess modeling choices.
- Pros for acceptance: solid modeling, convex formulation, scalable algorithm, unified treatment of two tasks, and demonstrated empirical benefits.
- Cons for acceptance: theoretical exactness of penalty needs clarification, missing stability discussion, and evaluation could be strengthened.
- Overall recommendation: promising and timely contribution that would be stronger with clarifications on penalty exactness and stability plus enhanced empirical evaluation.
