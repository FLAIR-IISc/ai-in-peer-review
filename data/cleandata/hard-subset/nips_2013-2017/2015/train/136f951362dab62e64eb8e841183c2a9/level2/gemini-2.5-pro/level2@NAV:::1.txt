This paper proposes a novel and elegant framework for time-sensitive recommendations, addressing two key problems: recommending the right item at the right time and predicting when a user will return to a service. The core contribution is to model the sequence of a user’s interactions with an item as a self-exciting Hawkes process. To generalize across the sparse user-item matrix, the paper assumes that the matrices representing the baseline intensity and the self-excitation parameters are low-rank. This formulation connects the literature on point processes with collaborative filtering. The work significantly extends prior art in temporal recommendations, such as methods based on survival analysis, by creating a model that can recommend new items rather than just predicting future events for previously consumed ones.

The paper has several significant strengths. The primary strength is its originality in combining Hawkes processes with low-rank matrix factorization to create a principled model for recurrent user activity. The technical quality is high; the authors present a convex formulation and develop a novel, efficient optimization algorithm with a formal convergence analysis, which is a valuable contribution in itself. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The proposed model consistently outperforms well-chosen baselines, including tensor factorization and classic matrix factorization, on three diverse real-world datasets. The use of the time-change theorem to validate the Hawkes process assumption on real data is a particularly nice touch that adds to the paper's rigor.

Despite the strengths, there are a few areas for improvement. The most notable weakness is the lack of discussion regarding the computational complexity at inference time. The proposed recommendation procedure involves calculating the intensity function for every potential item for a given user, which may not be scalable for systems with very large item catalogs. A discussion of potential approximations or candidate generation strategies would strengthen the practical applicability of the work. Secondly, the experiments rely on a parametric exponential triggering kernel for simplicity. While this is a reasonable choice, a brief discussion on the potential benefits and challenges of using more flexible, non-parametric kernels would be welcome.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant and novel contribution to the field of recommender systems. The proposed model is theoretically sound, empirically effective, and addresses important practical problems. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
