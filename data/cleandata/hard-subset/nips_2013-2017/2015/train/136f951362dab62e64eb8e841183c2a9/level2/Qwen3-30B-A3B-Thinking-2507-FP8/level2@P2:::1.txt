

This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes (Hawkes processes) with low-rank models to capture recurrent temporal patterns in user-item consumption data. The authors address two key problems: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and predicting the next returning time of users. The paper introduces a convex optimization formulation that can be efficiently solved using a new algorithm blending proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence rate. The authors demonstrate scalability to large datasets (millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of temporal events) and superior predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art baselines on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Strengths:**
The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical contribution: the novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank matrix models for time-sensitive recommendation. The convex formulation (Equation 4) is well-motivated, and the optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) provides an efficient solution with proven convergence guarantees (Theorem 2). The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their approach. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with both synthetic data experiments demonstrating the algorithm's convergence and scalability, and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) validating the practical utility of the approach. The paper also provides a clear explanation of how the model can incorporate additional contextual information, which enhances its potential applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness is the insufficient statistical evidence supporting the claim of "superb predictive performance." While Figure 2(e-f) and Figure 3 show comparative results, the paper does not provide statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests) to demonstrate that the observed performance differences between the proposed method and baselines are meaningful. This is particularly important given the competitive nature of recommendation systems research. Additionally, the paper's discussion of related work could be strengthened. While it cites Kapoor et al. [12, 11], it doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from other recent work on Hawkes processes for recommendation, such as Du et al. [8, 9, 10] who also applied Hawkes processes to modeling user activities. The paper's claim that their model can "generalize to recommend any new item" lacks concrete evidence beyond the synthetic experiments.

Another issue is the limited exploration of the low-rank assumption. While the paper justifies it by stating "users' behaviors and items' attributes can be categorized into a limited number of prototypical types," it doesn't provide empirical evidence that the true Λ0 and A matrices are indeed low-rank in real-world datasets. The paper also claims to incorporate "other extra context information" but provides no experiments or discussion on how this affects performance, which weakens the significance of this claim.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Include statistical significance tests for the comparative results to strengthen the claims about predictive performance.
2. Expand the related work section to better distinguish the proposed approach from other Hawkes process-based recommendation methods, particularly those by Du et al. [8, 9, 10].
3. Conduct additional experiments to validate the low-rank assumption on real datasets (e.g., by examining the singular values of Λ0 and A).
4. Provide a concrete example or experiment showing how incorporating contextual information (e.g., user profiles, spatial features) improves performance.

Overall, this paper presents a theoretically sound and practically relevant approach to time-sensitive recommendation. The novel connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a significant contribution, and the optimization algorithm is well-designed. While the empirical claims could be strengthened with more rigorous statistical analysis and better differentiation from related work, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of temporal recommendation systems. The scalability to large datasets and the clear theoretical guarantees make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
