Summary
The paper studies two time-sensitive recommendation problems: (1) ranking items for a user at a given time and (2) predicting the next return time for a user–item pair. The work models each user–item event sequence as a self-exciting Hawkes process with intensity λu,i(t) = λu,i0 + αu,i∑γ(t, tu,ij), and shares statistical strength across all user–item pairs via low-rank structure on the base intensities Λ0 and the self-excitation coefficients A, enforced through nuclear norms. Parameter estimation is posed as a convex program (negative log-likelihood plus nuclear norm penalties with nonnegativity constraints). To scale optimization, the paper introduces an auxiliary-variable formulation and a hybrid algorithm that uses proximal gradient on the nonnegativity-constrained variables and conditional gradient (Frank–Wolfe) for the nuclear-norm-regularized variables, with an O(1/t) convergence guarantee. Experiments on large synthetic data and three real datasets (last.fm, tmall, MIMIC II) show improved item ranking and return-time prediction versus Poisson baselines, tensor Poisson factorization, SVD, and a survival/HMM approach [11, 12]. The framework can incorporate additional context and suggests broader applicability.

Relation to prior work
The formulation connects temporal point processes [4–6, 10, 14] with low-rank modeling via nuclear norms [24], and is related to learning Hawkes processes and their kernels in networks [8, 29, 30], but differs by focusing on user–item self-excitation with low-rank parameter sharing and on time-sensitive recommendation tasks. Compared to survival analysis and semi-Markov approaches for return-time prediction [11, 12], the Hawkes-based model captures history-dependent excitation and generalizes to recommending items not yet consumed by a user. Relative to time-aware recommendation via tensor factorization with Poisson losses [3, 21, 25], this work models continuous time without discretization and directly produces a return-time distribution.

Quality
- Modeling and convexity: With exponential kernels, the negative log-likelihood has the form −∑log(w⊤φ)+w⊤ψ, which is convex in nonnegative parameters w, so the overall objective with nuclear norms is convex. The mapping from intensity to item ranking and to return-time density is consistent with point process theory [1].
- Optimization: The auxiliary-variable penalty formulation enables separating nonnegativity from nuclear norms. The hybrid algorithm is well-motivated; the convergence bound includes O(1/k^2) and O(1/k) terms, yielding the standard O(1/k) rate typical for Frank–Wolfe components.
- Theorem 1 (exactness of the quadratic penalty) is important for connecting the penalized problem to the constrained one. However, quadratic penalties are typically not exact in general convex programs; the stated result depends on a problem-dependent threshold ρ* that involves optimal solutions {Λ*,A*,Z*}. Additional detail or intuition on when this threshold is finite and how it can be approximated in practice would strengthen the theory.
- Process stability: For Hawkes processes with exponential kernels, stability requires αu,i ∫γ dt < 1 (branching ratio < 1). The formulation does not state an explicit constraint enforcing stability. Clarifying whether this is enforced (e.g., by projecting α or by adding soft constraints) is advisable.
- Evaluation: The experimental protocol (temporal holdout per user, plus fully held-out sequences) is appropriate. The baselines are relevant, including Poisson, tensor Poisson regression [3], and survival/HMM [11, 12]. Reported gains are consistent with modeling history dependence. Additional standard ranking metrics (Recall@k, NDCG, Hit@k) would complement the “rank of the true item” metric. For return-time prediction, reporting calibration plots or confidence intervals would help assess uncertainty. Practical details (number of samples for Ogata thinning, computational overhead per prediction) would aid reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized with a clear problem statement, model, and algorithmic description. The derivation of the likelihood and the sufficient statistics (φ, ψ) is helpful. Pseudocode is readable, though a few typos remain (e.g., “O(1/ )”, “returningtime”); fixing notational inconsistencies and minor grammatical errors would improve readability. The assumptions behind Theorem 1 and the exact setting of step sizes and Lipschitz constants in Theorem 2 could be explained more explicitly.

Originality
The main novelty lies in coupling Hawkes processes with low-rank parameter sharing across user–item pairs via convex nuclear-norm regularization, and in developing a scalable hybrid optimization scheme tailored to this structure. This appears distinct from prior Hawkes learning in influence networks [8, 29, 30] and from time-aware tensor factorization [3, 21, 25]. Casting time-sensitive recommendation and return-time prediction in this unified continuous-time framework is original and practically relevant.

Significance
The ability to rank items at specific times and to predict return times directly from a continuous-time model is valuable for recommender systems and beyond (e.g., healthcare). The scalability to millions of pairs and 10^8 events is significant. The approach is likely to influence subsequent work on temporal recommenders and continuous-time modeling with shared structure.

Main strengths
- Clear, principled continuous-time model with convex estimation and nonnegativity.
- Scalable hybrid optimization with convergence guarantees; avoids full SVDs.
- Strong empirical results on both ranking and return-time prediction across synthetic and real datasets.
- Flexible framework that can incorporate context.

Main weaknesses and constructive suggestions
- Exact penalty claim (Theorem 1): Provide conditions ensuring finite ρ*, practical guidance to select ρ, and empirical sensitivity analysis. If exactness cannot be guaranteed, position the result as an approximation with bounds on constraint violation.
- Stability constraints: Add a constraint or regularizer to ensure αu,i∫γ < 1, or discuss why estimates remain in the stable regime; report the empirical branching ratios.
- Evaluation metrics: Report Recall@k/Hit@k/NDCG alongside mean rank; include standard errors or confidence intervals; detail m for thinning and inference latencies.
- Baselines: Consider adding time-aware MF (e.g., TimeSVD++ [16]) and Poisson factorization variants with temporal dynamics for the ranking task to broaden comparisons.
- Ablations: Show the effect of kernel bandwidth σ, nuclear-norm weights (λ, β), and ρ on performance and rank of Λ0, A. A comparison to a factorized (non-convex) variant would illuminate the benefit of nuclear norms.
- Cross-excitation: The current model uses only self-excitation within a user–item pair. Discuss or experiment with limited cross-item excitation (e.g., artist–genre coupling) to assess potential gains.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid modeling contribution with convex formulation and scalable algorithm.
- Addresses two important, practical tasks in a unified manner.
- Demonstrated scalability and consistent empirical gains.
- Clear connection to and extension of point process literature.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical exactness of the penalty method requires clarification; current presentation leaves open questions.
- Missing stability discussion for Hawkes parameters.
- Evaluation could be strengthened with additional metrics and baselines.

Overall recommendation
Promising and timely contribution with solid methodology and impactful empirical results. With clarifications on the penalty exactness and stability, and minor enhancements to the empirical evaluation, the paper would make a strong addition to the program.
