

This paper proposes a novel framework that connects self-exciting point processes (Hawkes processes) with low-rank matrix models to address two time-sensitive recommendation problems: recommending the most relevant item at the right moment and predicting user returning times. The authors establish a convex optimization formulation for the problem and develop an efficient algorithm combining proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods, achieving O(1/t) convergence rate.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through comprehensive experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II). The authors show their model outperforms several strong baselines including Poisson process, STiC, SVD, and tensor factorization methods on both recommendation ranking and returning time prediction tasks. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 2) provides solid convergence guarantees for their optimization algorithm.

The work shows significant originality by bridging two distinct areas (temporal point processes and low-rank matrix factorization) for recommendation systems. The connection to existing literature is well-established, particularly in the related work section where they clearly distinguish their approach from Kapoor et al.'s survival analysis-based methods.

The significance of the work is evident from the practical applications described (music streaming, e-commerce, healthcare), where time-sensitive recommendations can substantially improve user engagement. The model's ability to incorporate additional context information (user profiles, spatial features) further enhances its practical utility.

Strengths: Strong theoretical foundation, comprehensive empirical validation, clear problem formulation, well-motivated novelty, and practical relevance.

Weaknesses: The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of limitations, particularly regarding the model's sensitivity to the choice of triggering kernel parameters. While they acknowledge that the Poisson process serves as a baseline, they could better explain why their Hawkes-based approach is fundamentally different from traditional temporal recommendation methods. The computational complexity analysis could be more detailed, especially regarding the trade-offs between model complexity and scalability.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient technical details to allow reproducibility. The algorithm description is precise and the convergence analysis is rigorous.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of recommendation systems, addressing a timely problem with significant practical implications. The technical quality, novelty, and significance meet NIPS standards, making it a compelling candidate for acceptance. The authors should consider addressing the limitations mentioned above in a revised version.
