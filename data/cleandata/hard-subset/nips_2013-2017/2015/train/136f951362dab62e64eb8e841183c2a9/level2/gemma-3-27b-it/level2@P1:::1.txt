This paper proposes a novel framework for time-sensitive recommendation by connecting self-exciting point processes and low-rank models. The authors aim to address two key questions: recommending the most desirable item at the right moment and predicting the next returning time of a user. The core idea is to model user activities as a Hawkes process with low-rank parameters, enabling knowledge transfer across user-item pairs. A convex optimization formulation is presented, accompanied by an efficient algorithm with provable convergence guarantees. Extensive experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The paper builds upon a growing body of work recognizing the limitations of traditional recommendation systems that ignore temporal dynamics. It directly addresses the recent work of Kapoor et al. [12, 11] by offering a method that *can* generalize to recommend new items, a critical distinction. The connection between Hawkes processes and low-rank models is a novel contribution, allowing for scalable parameter estimation and knowledge sharing. The theoretical analysis regarding the convergence rate of the optimization algorithm (O(1/t)) is also a strong point.  The inclusion of real-world datasets (last.fm, tmall.com, MIMIC II) strengthens the practical relevance of the proposed method. Previous NIPS papers have also explored temporal dynamics in recommendation, such as [16, 23, 15], but this work more directly models the event sequences using point processes, a different and potentially more expressive approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Hawkes processes and low-rank models for time-sensitive recommendation is a genuinely novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The convex formulation and convergence analysis provide a strong theoretical grounding for the method.
* **Scalability:** The algorithm is designed to scale to large datasets with millions of user-item pairs and hundreds of millions of events.
* **Empirical Performance:**  The experimental results demonstrate superior performance compared to several baselines on both synthetic and real-world data.  The ablation studies (e.g., comparing Tensor-#-Last and Tensor-#-Avg) are helpful in understanding the impact of different components.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.
* **Connections to Related Work:** The paper adequately references and differentiates itself from existing literature. The discussion of the limitations of Kapoor et al.’s work is particularly well done.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Kernel Choice Justification:** While the exponential kernel is a common choice, the paper doesn't fully justify why it's preferred over other potential kernels (e.g., Weibull, Log-logistic). A brief discussion of the implications of the kernel choice would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The experiments tune ρ, λ, and β.  A discussion on the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be useful. Are there specific guidelines for selecting these parameters in practice?  
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While several baselines are included, the comparison could be broadened to include more recent and sophisticated temporal recommendation algorithms (beyond Tensor Factorization and STiC).  Specifically, methods incorporating attention mechanisms for temporal modeling are increasingly popular.
* **Real-World Data Detail:** More details about the datasets (e.g., data sparsity, average number of events per user) would provide better context for interpreting the results.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses primarily on the algorithmic aspects. A discussion of practical challenges in deploying this system in a real-world scenario (e.g., cold-start problem, computational cost for online recommendation) would strengthen the work.
* **Minor Typo:** The sentence “The unit time for both dataset is hour” in section 5.2 should be “The unit time for both datasets is hour.”



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to time-sensitive recommendation.
* The theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees are a significant contribution.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate superior performance.
* The work addresses an important and increasingly relevant problem in recommendation systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is somewhat limited.
* The paper lacks a thorough discussion of parameter sensitivity and practical considerations.
* The justification for the kernel choice could be strengthened.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential to impact the field of recommendation systems. The novelty of the approach, the strong theoretical foundation, and the compelling experimental results outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the feedback regarding parameter sensitivity, comparisons to more recent SOTA methods, and practical considerations. A more detailed justification for the kernel choice would also be beneficial.
