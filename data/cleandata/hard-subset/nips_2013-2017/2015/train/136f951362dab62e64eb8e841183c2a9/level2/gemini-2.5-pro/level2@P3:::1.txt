This paper addresses the problem of time-sensitive recommendation, focusing on two key questions: what to recommend at a given moment, and when a user is likely to return to a service. The work proposes a novel framework that models recurrent user activities by connecting low-rank matrix models with self-exciting Hawkes point processes. For each user-item pair, the sequence of consumption events is modeled as a Hawkes process, whose intensity is determined by a baseline preference and a self-exciting term capturing influence from past events. To generalize across the sparse user-item matrix and enable recommendations for unobserved pairs, the matrices of baseline intensities and self-excitation parameters are assumed to be low-rank.

The parameters of this "Low Rank Hawkes Process" model are learned via a convex optimization problem, which minimizes the negative log-likelihood subject to nuclear norm regularization and non-negativity constraints. The paper introduces an efficient optimization algorithm that blends proximal gradient and conditional gradient methods to solve this problem, providing a theoretical analysis that shows an O(1/t) convergence rate. The proposed model is evaluated on one synthetic and three real-world datasets on the tasks of time-sensitive item ranking and returning-time prediction, demonstrating superior performance against several relevant baselines, including static matrix factorization, tensor factorization, and other temporal models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly significant and practical problem in recommender systems that is often overlooked by traditional models: the temporal dynamics of user engagement. The proposed unification of collaborative filtering (via low-rank models) and temporal point processes (via Hawkes processes) is a novel and powerful idea. It provides an elegant, unified framework for addressing both the "what" and "when" of recommendation in continuous time. This work moves beyond static ratings or discrete time slots, which is a valuable contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and presents a complete contribution.
    *   The formulation of the problem as a regularized maximum likelihood estimation for a collection of low-rank Hawkes processes is well-motivated and mathematically rigorous.
    *   The development of a custom optimization algorithm tailored to the specific problem structure (non-negativity plus nuclear norm regularization) is a notable strength. The hybrid proximal-conditional gradient approach is efficient, scalable, and comes with a formal convergence analysis (Theorem 2). This contribution may be of independent interest for related matrix/tensor learning problems.
    *   The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The use of both synthetic data (to verify model recovery and scalability) and diverse, large-scale real-world datasets demonstrates the method's practical applicability. The comparison against a well-chosen set of baselines effectively highlights the benefits of explicitly modeling self-excitation and using a continuous-time framework. The use of the time-change theorem to validate the model's goodness-of-fit (Figure 3) adds significant credibility to the modeling assumptions.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The motivation is compelling, the background on point processes is helpful, and the proposed model is developed in a logical progression. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, allowing the reader to understand the outcomes and their implications.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, there are areas where it could be improved:

1.  **Reproducibility Details:** The performance of the model likely depends on several hyperparameters (regularization parameters `Œª`, `Œ≤`, penalty `œÅ`, and the kernel bandwidth `œÉ`). The paper states these were tuned for best performance. For enhanced reproducibility, it would be beneficial to provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process, such as the range of values explored and the validation strategy used.

2.  **Optimization Details:** The equivalence between the two optimization formulations (Eq. 4 and 5) depends on `œÅ` being sufficiently large (Theorem 1). However, the threshold `œÅ*` depends on the unknown optimal solution. The paper handles this by tuning `œÅ` empirically, which is a pragmatic choice. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of `œÅ` would be insightful. Furthermore, while the algorithm is presented, a bit more intuition behind the accelerated update steps (e.g., the role of the `Uk-1` sequence) in the main text could improve accessibility for a broader audience.

3.  **Experimental Comparison:** The evaluation is strong, but a comparison of the computational cost (e.g., training time) of the proposed method versus the baselines would be a valuable addition. While scalability is shown to be linear in the number of events, a direct comparison would help practitioners assess the trade-offs between predictive accuracy and computational expense.

4.  **Model Extensions:** The paper mentions the possibility of extending the model to incorporate additional context by making the intensity function dependent on other features. This is a promising direction. While a full implementation is beyond the scope of a revision, even a short discussion on how the formulation (e.g., Eq. 3) would change could make the claimed flexibility more concrete for the reader.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel and elegant synthesis of low-rank models and Hawkes processes to address the important problem of time-sensitive recommendation. The technical contributions, including the scalable optimization algorithm with theoretical guarantees, are substantial. The empirical results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The work is likely to be influential and inspire further research in temporal recommendation and dynamic user modeling.
*   **Con:** Minor weaknesses exist regarding the clarity of hyperparameter tuning and a lack of explicit comparison of computational times.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of recommender systems and machine learning.
