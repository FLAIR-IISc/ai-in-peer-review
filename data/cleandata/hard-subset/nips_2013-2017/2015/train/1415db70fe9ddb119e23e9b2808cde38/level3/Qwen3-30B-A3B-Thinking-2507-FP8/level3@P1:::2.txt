

This paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure, addressing a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of EM for latent variable models in high dimensions. The authors make two main contributions: (1) a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step that enforces sparsity, establishing geometric convergence to an estimator with near-optimal statistical rate of O(sqrt(s*log(d)/n)), and (2) a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional components, establishing asymptotic normality with optimal semiparametric efficiency.

The work builds upon the recent low-dimensional guarantees of [2], extending them to high-dimensional settings where the dimension d exceeds the sample size n. The authors properly contextualize their work against related literature, noting that while [2] established geometric convergence rates in low dimensions, their work is the first to provide statistical guarantees for high-dimensional EM. They also distinguish their approach from tensor-based methods [1,9] that achieve suboptimal rates in high dimensions, and from post-selection inference methods for generalized linear models [13,16,26,32] that don't apply to latent variable models.

The paper demonstrates the framework's effectiveness on Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, providing concrete verification of the theoretical conditions for these specific cases. The theoretical results are comprehensive, establishing both computational and statistical guarantees simultaneously. The authors prove that with appropriate initialization, the high-dimensional EM algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator achieving the near-minimax optimal rate, and that their decorrelated score statistic achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound.

The paper's strengths include its technical depth, comprehensive theoretical framework, and clear presentation of the main contributions. The related work section is well-organized and adequately contextualizes the paper within the NIPS literature. The authors effectively demonstrate how their framework generalizes and improves upon previous work, particularly [2] for the low-dimensional case.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. The theoretical developments in Sections 3 and 4 are extremely dense and challenging to follow, with many technical conditions and assumptions that require significant effort to parse. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of the key technical innovations, particularly regarding the role of the truncation step (Algorithm 1, line 6). The reviewer questions what happens if this truncation step is omitted, as it appears crucial to achieving the sparsity structure but isn't sufficiently motivated.

The paper claims that the statistical rate O(sqrt(s*log(d)/n)) is minimax-optimal but provides no proof of this claim in the main manuscript, only citing a reference that may not directly establish this result. This is a significant omission for a paper making such a strong claim about optimality.

The practical implementation concerns are substantial. The sparsity parameter in Equation (3.8) depends on the unknown true sparsity level s*, making it unclear how to select this parameter in practice. Similarly, the initialization requirement ||β^init - β*||_2 < R/2 is theoretically justified but leaves open the question of how to obtain such an initialization in real applications. The paper provides no empirical evidence or practical guidance for these critical implementation details.

While the paper makes a theoretically important contribution to statistical theory, its practical relevance to machine learning researchers is questionable. The high-dimensional EM algorithm requires knowledge of the true sparsity level and a good initialization, which are often unavailable in practice. The paper would benefit from more discussion of practical implementation challenges and potential workarounds.

The paper excels in quality, providing rigorous theoretical guarantees that advance the statistical understanding of EM in high dimensions. It demonstrates strong originality by extending the EM framework to high dimensions with sparsity constraints, a significant gap in the literature. The significance is moderate - the theoretical results are important for statistical theory but may have limited immediate impact on machine learning practice due to the implementation challenges.

In summary, this is a technically strong paper that makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding EM in high dimensions. However, the dense presentation, lack of proof for the minimax optimality claim, and substantial practical implementation concerns (particularly regarding sparsity parameter selection and initialization) prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The paper would benefit from significant simplification of the theoretical presentation, explicit proof of the minimax optimality claim, and more practical discussion of implementation details. As a theoretical contribution to statistics, it's valuable, but as a machine learning contribution, its practical impact appears limited.
