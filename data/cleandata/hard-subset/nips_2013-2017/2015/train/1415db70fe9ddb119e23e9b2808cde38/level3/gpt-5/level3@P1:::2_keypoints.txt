- Paper develops a high-dimensional EM framework with a truncation (T-) step for sparsity and a decorrelated-score inferential procedure for low-dimensional targets amid high-dimensional nuisances.
- Algorithm achieves geometric convergence from suitable initialization and near-minimax ℓ2 rates O(√(s* log d / n)) up to log n factors; inference yields asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency.
- Theory covers both exact and gradient M-steps under unified conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity/smoothness, ℓ1 sample-to-population error) and is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.
- Strength: timely and ambitious—bridges computation-statistics gap for EM when p ≫ n with both estimation and inference guarantees.
- Strength: clear conceptual innovation—T-step plus entrywise (ℓ∞/ℓ1) controls produce sharp rates and support control along the EM trajectory.
- Strength: generality—applies to exact and gradient M-steps, verifies conditions for canonical models, and attains semiparametric information bound for inference.
- Strength: careful technical execution—clean decomposition of optimization vs. statistical error, geometric contraction proofs, and Dantzig-based decorrelated-score construction.
- Weakness: manuscript is extremely dense; Sections 3–4 need streamlining and better structuring of assumptions and intuition.
- Suggestion: move key assumptions into a roadmap subsection with intuitive explanations and a table mapping to model verifications.
- Suggestion: separate exact vs. gradient M-step results with a concise comparison of requirements (e.g., ρ1 vs. ρ2, step size).
- Suggestion: include short proof sketches for Theorems 3.4 and 4.6 in the main text, relegating constants to the appendix.
- Suggestion: add an ablation figure demonstrating necessity of truncation and the effect of bs choice.
- Practical gap: initialization guidance is thin—request concrete initializers meeting the basin condition and empirical sensitivity analysis.
- Practical gap: bs selection guidance lacking—request data-driven recipes (stability selection, information criteria, validation, monotone path with early stopping).
- Practical gap: tuning for Dantzig step (λ) needs a finite-sample rule and discussion of computational cost and numerical stability for Tn(·) estimation.
- Practical gap: clarify whether resampling EM analysis transfers to standard single-sample EM and document empirical differences.
- Specific question: standardize the minimax-rate claim, provide clear minimax lower bounds, scope, and proofs/citations for which models and SNR ranges it holds.
- Specific question: clarify role of truncation—what fails without T-step, and provide theorem or empirical ablation quantifying the performance gap.
- Specific question: sensitivity to gradient step size η = 2/(ν+μ) and whether adaptive line-search alternatives are viable.
- Specific question: quantify finite-sample constants (basin radius R and contraction factor ρ) for the worked models in terms of SNR and dimensions for practitioner guidance.
- Quality: technically sound with standard, carefully verified assumptions; inference result achieving semiparametric efficiency is a major contribution.
- Clarity: writing is precise but heavy; implementing suggested structural edits and practical guidelines would improve accessibility.
- Originality: high—novel combination of truncation, high-dimensional EM analysis, and semiparametric inference beyond GLMs.
- Significance: high for theory and potentially impactful for practitioners, contingent on clearer guidance for initialization, tuning, and computational overhead.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contributions, unified framework, semiparametric efficiency, and advances over prior high-dimensional EM/tensor guarantees.
- Arguments against acceptance: dense exposition, limited practical guidance and empirical ablations, and some claims (minimax optimality) need clearer centralized statements and proofs.
- Post-rebuttal verdict: reviewer raised score from 6 to 7 pending clarifications on initialization and bs selection; overall a solid statistics contribution with practicality caveats.
