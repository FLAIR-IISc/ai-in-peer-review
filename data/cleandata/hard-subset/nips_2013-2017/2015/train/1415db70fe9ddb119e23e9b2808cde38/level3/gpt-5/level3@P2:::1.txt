Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a unified theory for high-dimensional latent-variable models fitted by EM. The main ideas are: (i) a truncated EM algorithm (E/M/T) that enforces sparsity by retaining the largest bs coordinates after each M-step, with guarantees of geometric optimization error decay and near-optimal statistical error O(√(s*)·√(log d/n)) under suitable initialization and assumptions; and (ii) an inferential procedure for a low-dimensional component of the parameter based on a “decorrelated score” constructed from Qn(·; ·), shown to be asymptotically normal with variance attaining the semiparametric information bound. The analysis covers both exact maximization and a gradient-ascent M-step.

This extends the recent population-to-sample analyses of EM in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) by handling p ≫ n with sparsity, and connects to the literature on de-biasing/desparsification for inference (Zhang & Zhang 2014; van de Geer et al. 2014; Ning & Liu 2014/2017). Relative to tensor methods for latent-variable estimation, the statistical rates here are sharper in high dimensions and the approach applies beyond low-order moment methods.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: computation-statistics guarantees for EM in high dimensions with latent structure, including inference.
- Clear decomposition of optimization vs statistical error; geometric convergence and near-minimax rates under explicit conditions.
- Inference achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound and is computationally feasible.
- Framework treats both exact and gradient M-steps; models illustrated include GMM and mixture of regressions.

Weaknesses / areas for improvement
Quality
- Heavy technical assumptions: the basin, strong concavity/smoothness at the population level, Lipschitz-gradient conditions tied to the M-map, sparsity of the nuisance “orthogonalization” vector w*, and a resampling-based analysis. Please clarify which are essential and which are proof artifacts; discuss their plausibility in the presented models and beyond. In particular:
  - For GMM and mixture of regressions, give interpretable conditions in the main text (signal-to-noise/separation, design assumptions, sub-Gaussianity), and explicit constants or scalings where possible.
  - The sparsity of w* (kw*|0 = s*w) is nontrivial; discuss when it holds and how to diagnose it in practice.
  - The analysis focuses on a resampling/split-sample EM; comment on whether single-sample iterates enjoy the same bounds (e.g., via martingale or leave-one-out arguments).
- Initialization is assumed within a constant-factor ℓ2 radius but practical construction is deferred. This is critical for both estimation and inference.

Clarity
- The main paper defers many key arguments/conditions to the supplement. Including concise proof sketches and a table summarizing assumptions and required sample sizes for each model would greatly aid readability.
- Provide guidance on hyperparameters (bs and the Dantzig tuning λ), step size in gradient EM, and when to use each M-step variant.

Originality
- The truncated EM for high-dimensional latent models and the support-evolution analysis appear novel and technically nontrivial.
- The decorrelated score construction for inference matches the Ning & Liu decorrelated/orthogonal score approach (Ning & Liu, 2014; 2017), specialized via Theorem 2.1 that links derivatives of Q to the likelihood score and Fisher information in latent-variable settings. Please explicitly cite this connection, state that the test coincides with Ning & Liu’s method under your mapping, and delineate what is new (e.g., adapting the orthogonalization to a nonconvex EM estimator and to the latent-variable Q-function, verifying concentration/Lipschitz conditions for Tn, handling non-global optima).

Significance
- The results are potentially impactful for theory and practice on high-dimensional latent-variable modeling. Practical adoption would benefit from clearer guidelines on initialization and tuning, and more extensive experiments comparing against penalized MLEs and tensor methods.

Specific requests and suggestions
- Initialization: Provide practical recipes per model that satisfy the required relative error bound:
  - GMM: spectral method or k-means++ on a random subset; a few steps of EM from multiple random starts with hard truncation; method-of-moments with thresholding.
  - Mixture of regressions: tensor method (Spectral Experts) followed by sparsity thresholding; lasso on pseudo-complete data via responsibilities from one EM E-step; sample-splitting to form a pilot consistent estimator then refine with truncated EM.
  - State required SNR/separation or design conditions under which these pilots meet the basin assumption with high probability, with citations and quantitative constants if available.
- When to use exact vs gradient M-step: Give guidance based on model complexity and computational budget. Exact M-step when closed-form or cheap convex inner problems exist; gradient M-step when M-step is expensive or ill-conditioned. Recommend step-size choices: η = 2/(ν+μ) if (ν, μ) can be estimated; otherwise backtracking or Barzilai–Borwein; discuss robustness to misspecification.
- Choosing bs and λ in practice:
  - bs: set via a fraction of an initial sparsity estimate (e.g., support size from a cross-validated lasso on a surrogate objective), or select bs along a path by monitoring a validation Qn and stability selection; advise slight overspecification (bs ≈ c·ŝ with c in [1.5, 3]) given Theorem 3.4 requires bs ≥ Cs*.
  - λ for the Dantzig problem: theory suggests λ ≍ √(log d / n) times an estimate of scale; recommend a multiplier bootstrap or cross-validation on the resulting test size in synthetic null splits; provide a default λ = cσ̂√(log d/n) with c chosen by a small grid and size diagnostics.
  - Sensitivity: include an empirical sensitivity study showing estimation error vs bs and test size/power vs λ.
- Assumptions: In the main text, explicitly state for GMM and mixture regressions: SNR/separation, sub-Gaussian noise/design, bounds on s*, s*w, and the resulting sample-size scaling so readers can judge applicability.
- Novelty vs Ning & Liu: Add an explicit paragraph clarifying that your decorrelated score coincides with Ning & Liu’s orthogonal score after identifying rQn with the normalized score and Tn with the observed information; emphasize the new elements needed in latent-variable/EM settings (Theorem 2.1, concentration/Lipschitz for Tn under nonconvexity, inference with non-global estimators).
- Experiments: Please expand empirical evaluation (beyond timing/accuracy curves) to include:
  - Comparisons to ℓ1-penalized EM (if solvable), gradient-EM with ℓ1 penalty, tensor methods.
  - Calibration of the score/Wald tests (size and power) under varying s*, s*w, and SNR.
  - Robustness to mis-specified bs and λ, and to weaker initialization.

Pros for acceptance
- Advances theory of EM in the high-dimensional regime with both estimation and inference.
- Near-optimal statistical rates and semiparametric efficiency.
- Generally applicable framework (two M-step variants) with clear optimization-statistics decomposition.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy technical assumptions; some are not fully interpretable in the main text.
- Practical guidance on initialization and tuning is currently insufficient.
- Dependence on resampling in the analysis raises questions about single-sample practice.
- Relation to existing inference methods (Ning & Liu) should be made explicit.

Questions for the authors
- Can your proofs be adapted to single-sample iterates without resampling? If not, what failure modes do you observe?
- How realistic is the sparsity of w* in common latent-variable models? Any diagnostics?
- Can bs be adapted online (e.g., support growth/shrinkage rules) with guarantees?
- For low SNR GMMs, how does performance degrade when strong concavity fails locally?

Minor comments
- Please correct incomplete/malformed references and explicitly add Ning & Liu (2014/2017), Zhang & Zhang (2014), van de Geer et al. (2014).
- Move Theorem 2.1’s proof sketch and implications into the main text.

Overall: A strong and timely theoretical contribution. With clearer practical guidance, explicit credit and positioning relative to Ning & Liu, and more self-contained assumptions, this would be a solid accept.
