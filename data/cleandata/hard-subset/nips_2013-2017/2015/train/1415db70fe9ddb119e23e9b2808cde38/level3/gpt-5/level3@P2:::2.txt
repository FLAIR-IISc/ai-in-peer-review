Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops a high-dimensional theory for EM in latent-variable models. The algorithm augments classical EM with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step that enforces sparsity at each iteration, and analyzes both exact M-step and gradient-ascent M-step variants. Under suitable initialization and local regularity conditions, the algorithm converges geometrically within a basin and achieves a (near-)minimax statistical rate O(sqrt(s* log d / n), up to log n factors). Beyond point estimation, the paper proposes a decorrelated score (and Wald) inference procedure for a low-dimensional component of the parameter in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance, proving asymptotic normality with variance attaining the semiparametric information bound.

The work builds on the population-to-sample analysis of EM in the low-dimensional setting (Balakrishnan et al., 2017), extending it to d >> n by introducing and controlling the truncation step and establishing entrywise concentration necessary for high-dimensional rates. For inference, it connects derivatives of the Q-function to the likelihood score and Fisher information (a standard EM identity) and leverages Dantzig-style regression to estimate the decorrelating direction, in the spirit of de-biased/orthogonalized inference methods, but adapted to latent-variable models where the estimator is not a convex-program optimum. Comparisons with tensor methods and ℓ1-penalized likelihood for mixtures are discussed; the former typically yield suboptimal rates, and the latter lack inference guarantees.

Strengths:
- Clear algorithmic template (E/M/T-steps) with analysis for exact and gradient M-steps.
- Geometric optimization error plus sharp statistical error decomposition; near-optimal rates for sparse high-dimensional latent-variable models.
- Inference contribution is strong: decorrelated score with asymptotic normality at the semiparametric efficiency bound.
- Unified framework with instantiations for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions; conditions are made explicit and checkable.
- Good coverage of related work and positioning relative to EM analyses and high-dimensional inference literature.

Weaknesses and suggestions for clarity:
- Sections 3–4 are dense. Consider: (a) an informal “roadmap” of assumptions (why each is needed, how they are verified), (b) moving technical constants (e.g., bs sizing, radii, and basin parameters) to an appendix and keeping only interpretable forms in the main text, (c) a figure outlining the proof structure (population contraction → sample perturbation → support control via T-step → entrywise error → inference).
- Please provide a concise ablation/remark: what breaks if the T-step is omitted? A formal statement that the algorithm without truncation achieves only O(sqrt(d/n)) rates (or fails to stay in the basin) would help readers appreciate the necessity of truncation.
- The analysis focuses on a resampling variant (fresh data per iteration). Clarify whether the bounds extend to the single-sample-pass EM and, if not, what additional dependence terms appear.

Technical questions for authors:
- Minimax optimality: The paper claims sqrt(s* log d / n) is (near-)minimax optimal. Please either provide a precise minimax lower bound for the considered latent models or cite an appropriate reference establishing the lower bound in these settings (beyond linear models). If the claim is model-specific (e.g., GMM, mixture of regressions), state the conditions explicitly.
- Choosing bs in practice: Eq. (3.8) requires bs ≳ s*, but s* is unknown. Please add practical guidance: e.g., (i) run a path over bs and select via held-out likelihood or BIC-type criteria; (ii) stability selection; (iii) start with a large bs and shrink via thresholding based on estimated entrywise noise level; (iv) seed bs using an initial ℓ1-penalized step (Lasso or penalized M-step) and set bs = c · |supp(β̂lasso)|.
- Initialization: The guarantees require βinit within a Euclidean ball. Please include constructive initializers per model (e.g., spectral/tensor moments for mixtures, k-means or method-of-moments for GMM under separation, or a few steps of gradient EM with ℓ1-penalization followed by hard-thresholding). State sufficient separation/noise conditions under which these initializations land in the basin with high probability.
- Role of T-step: Beyond intuition, can you quantify the support evolution and show that without T-step the iterates cannot maintain sparsity (and hence the entrywise error bound and basin preservation fail)?
- Tuning of Dantzig selector (λ): Provide a data-driven choice (e.g., via concentration of Tn and Lipschitz terms) and discuss sensitivity.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The technical development appears sound, with careful control of optimization and statistical errors and a nontrivial support-evolution analysis. The inference theory is compelling and achieves the semiparametric variance lower bound. Please tighten the minimax optimality claim with a formal lower bound or precise citation.
- Clarity: Overall well-organized, but the main theoretical sections are challenging to parse. Improving exposition as suggested above would broaden accessibility. Some references in the draft appear garbled; please fix bibliographic details.
- Originality: Combining EM with iterative truncation and delivering both high-dimensional estimation and asymptotically exact inference for latent-variable models is novel and differentiates from prior EM and de-biasing literature.
- Significance: The results are important for bridging computation and statistics in high-dimensional latent models. Practical adoption may hinge on clear initialization and tuning guidance; adding experiments demonstrating robustness and comparative performance would strengthen impact for the ML community.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong and general theoretical contributions (estimation + inference) for an important algorithmic paradigm (EM) in high dimensions.
- Semiparametric efficiency for inference under latent-variable structure.
- Applicable to multiple models; analysis covers both exact and gradient M-steps.

Cons for acceptance:
- Dense presentation; accessibility to a broad NeurIPS audience is limited.
- Practical guidance (initialization, choosing bs, tuning) is not fully specified; empirical validation is limited/not included here.
- Minimax optimality claim needs clearer support.
