- Proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm with truncation enforcing sparsity and proves geometric convergence with near-optimal statistical rates.  
- Develops a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional components in high-dimensional latent variable models.  
- Theoretical contributions are substantial and technically rigorous, decomposing estimation error into optimization and statistical terms (Theorem 3.4).  
- Claim of minimax-optimal rate √(s* log d / n) lacks an explicit minimax lower bound proof or citation in the manuscript.  
- Practical concerns: tuning parameter ^s depends on unknown true sparsity s* and requires stronger discussion or guidance.  
- Initialization requirement (‖β^init - β*‖_2 < R/2) is stringent and needs more clarification on feasibility in practice.  
- The necessity and role of the truncation step are not demonstrated—paper does not analyze what happens without truncation.  
- Sections 3 and 4 are dense and technically challenging; abstract conditions (3.1–3.3, 4.1–4.4) lack concrete examples or intuition.  
- Paper is well-organized and well-written with comprehensive related work coverage.  
- Originality: meaningful extension of EM analysis to high-dimensional sparse settings and a novel decorrelated inference approach.  
- Significance: addresses an important gap marrying computation and statistics in high-dimensional latent variable models, with potential domain impact (e.g., genomics).  
- Practical utility is limited by complex initialization and tuning requirements and a focus on theory over implementation.  
- Strengths: unified theoretical framework across multiple latent variable models and clearly articulated main contributions.  
- Weaknesses: missing minimax proof, insufficient practical implementation discussion, dense presentation of technical conditions, and unclear necessity of truncation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: first computationally feasible theoretical guarantees for high-dimensional EM with sparsity, unified estimation and inference framework, rigorous convergence analysis, and novel inference statistic.  
- Arguments against acceptance: absent minimax optimality proof, practical tuning and initialization concerns, and overly dense technical exposition limiting accessibility.  
- Recommendation: accept (7/10) as a strong theoretical contribution, but require addressing minimax claim and improving practical guidance and exposition.
