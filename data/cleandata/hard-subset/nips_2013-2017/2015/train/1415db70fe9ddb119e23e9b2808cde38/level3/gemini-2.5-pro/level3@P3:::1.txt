### **Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a general theoretical framework for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates a hard-thresholding (truncation) step after each M-step to enforce sparsity. They prove that with a suitable initialization, this algorithm converges geometrically to a solution that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical rate of convergence (`sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`). Second, building on this estimator, they develop a procedure for statistical inference (hypothesis testing and confidence intervals) for low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. This is achieved via a decorrelated score test, for which they establish asymptotic normality. The framework is general and its application is demonstrated for high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixtures of linear regression. This work extends the low-dimensional analysis of the EM algorithm by Balakrishnan et al. (2014) to the high-dimensional setting and provides the first computationally feasible method with guarantees for both optimal estimation and valid inference in this challenging class of models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a crucial and difficult problem at the intersection of high-dimensional statistics and latent variable modeling. Providing a computationally tractable algorithm (EM) with rigorous guarantees for both estimation and inference is a major contribution that significantly advances the state of the art. Latent variable models are ubiquitous, and this work provides a foundational toolkit for their application in modern high-dimensional settings.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and technically sound. The authors successfully navigate the challenges posed by the non-convexity of the likelihood and the introduction of the truncation step within the EM iterations. Decomposing the error into optimization and statistical components and proving geometric convergence for the former and near-optimal rates for the latter is a strong theoretical result.

3.  **Novelty:** While building upon existing lines of work (EM theory, high-dimensional inference), the synthesis is highly novel. The key innovation lies in analyzing the properties of an estimator that is not a global optimum of a convex program (unlike Lasso) and showing it is sufficiently accurate to enable valid post-estimation inference. Applying tools like the decorrelated score test in this context is non-trivial and requires a distinct and challenging analysis.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

The paper is theoretically impressive, but its practical applicability and clarity could be substantially improved by addressing the following points:

1.  **Practicality of Initialization:** A critical assumption for the entire theoretical framework is the availability of an initial estimator `╬▓^{init}` that is already within a constant relative error of the true parameter `╬▓*` (line 60). The paper provides no practical guidance on how to obtain such an estimator. For latent variable models, finding a good initialization is itself a hard problem. The authors should discuss potential methods (e.g., spectral methods, tensor methods, or even a simple method of moments on a subset of data) and the conditions under which these methods might satisfy the required initialization accuracy. Without this, the applicability of the proposed algorithm remains theoretical.

2.  **Guidance on Algorithm and Parameter Choices:**
    *   **Algorithm Choice:** The paper presents two M-step implementations: exact maximization (Algorithm 2) and gradient ascent (Algorithm 3). There is no discussion of the trade-offs. Guidance should be provided on when one might be preferred. For example, for models where the M-step is a simple closed-form update (like the Gaussian mixture model), exact maximization is feasible. For more complex models (e.g., mixture of GLMs), the M-step may be an intractable subproblem, making the gradient ascent version the only practical choice.
    *   **Tuning Parameters:** The choices for the sparsity level `bs` and the Dantzig selector parameter `╬╗` are defined by theoretical quantities (e.g., `s*`, `ÔçúT`, `ÔçúL`, `ÔçúEM`) that are unknown in practice. This is a major gap between theory and practice. The paper would be much stronger if it proposed and analyzed data-dependent selection methods, such as cross-validation, or discussed potential information criteria (e.g., BIC-type penalties) for choosing `bs` and `╬╗`. An assessment of the method's sensitivity to these parameters would also be valuable.

3.  **Positioning Relative to Existing Work:** The decorrelated score test is a general construction for high-dimensional inference. The formulation here closely follows the general framework developed in Ning & Liu ("A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for High Dimensional Models," JASA 2017). This should be explicitly cited. While the fundamental idea of the test is not new, the novelty and main contribution here is demonstrating that this inferential machinery can be successfully applied to the local solution found by the proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm. This is non-trivial because, unlike in GLMs where the estimator is the global optimum of a convex program, here the estimator's properties must be established through a careful analysis of the iterative algorithm, which is a key challenge the authors have overcome. Clarifying this distinction would better highlight the paper's specific contribution.

4.  **Clarity on Technical Assumptions:** The main theorems rely on a number of technical assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness, properties of the Fisher information matrix). While the authors state these are verified for the examples in the appendix, the main text would benefit from a more intuitive discussion of what these assumptions mean and under what practical conditions they are likely to hold or fail. For instance, the local strong concavity of the population Q-function is a crucial condition that depends on factors like the separation between mixture components. A brief discussion of these aspects would make the theory more accessible to a broader audience.

5.  **Paper Organization:** The paper relegates a substantial amount of critical material, including proofs and verification of assumptions, to a very long supplement. While page limits are a constraint, the main paper would be more self-contained and convincing if it included high-level proof sketches for the main theorems. For example, a brief sketch illustrating how the analysis handles the perturbation introduced by the truncation step and how the statistical error is controlled throughout the iterations would significantly improve readability and appreciation of the technical challenges involved.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to a challenging and important area. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarity, practical guidance, and positioning, and can be addressed in a revised version. I recommend acceptance.
