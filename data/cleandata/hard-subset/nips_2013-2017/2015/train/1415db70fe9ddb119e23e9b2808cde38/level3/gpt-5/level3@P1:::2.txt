This paper develops a high-dimensional EM framework for latent-variable models that (i) augments EM with a truncation step to enforce sparsity and (ii) builds a decorrelated-score–based inferential procedure for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. Under suitable initialization and regularity conditions, the algorithm achieves geometric convergence within a basin of attraction and delivers (near-)minimax ℓ2 estimation rates of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to log n factors). On inference, the proposed decorrelated score and the associated Wald-type constructions yield asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency for the target component, even though the estimator itself is not a global optimum of any convex objective. The theory covers both exact and gradient M-steps and provides unified conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity/smoothness, and an ℓ1-type sample-to-population error bound). The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, improving on earlier low-dimensional EM analyses (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) and extending debiasing/desparsification ideas (van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang) beyond GLMs to nonconvex latent-variable likelihoods.

Strengths
- Timely and ambitious: bridges the computation-statistics gap for EM in the p ≫ n regime with both estimation and inference guarantees.
- Clear conceptual innovation: the T-step (hard truncation) coupled with entrywise (ℓ∞/ℓ1) error controls allows sharp rates and support control along the EM trajectory.
- Generality: covers exact and gradient M-steps; conditions are verified for two canonical latent-variable models; inference attains the semiparametric information bound.
- Careful technical execution: decomposition into optimization vs. statistical error, geometric contraction, and Dantzig-based construction for decorrelated scores are well argued; population–sample connections (Theorem 2.1) are clean.

Weaknesses and suggestions for clarity
- The manuscript is extremely dense. Sections 3–4 could be streamlined. Suggestions:
  - Move key assumptions (Conditions 3.1–3.3, 4.1–4.4) into a “roadmap” subsection with brief intuition and a table mapping them to model-specific verifications.
  - Separate results for exact vs. gradient M-steps with a concise comparison of requirements (e.g., ρ1 vs. ρ2, step size).
  - Provide a short proof sketch for Theorems 3.4 and 4.6 in the main text, relegating technical constants to the appendix.
  - Add an ablation figure showing the necessity of truncation and the effect of bs choice.
- Practical guidance is thin:
  - Initialization: Please discuss concrete initializers that meet the basin condition (||βinit − β*||2 ≤ R/2), e.g., method-of-moments/spectral initializers for mixtures, short runs of untruncated/ℓ1-penalized EM, or sample-splitting + debiased steps. Empirical sensitivity to initialization would help.
  - Choosing bs in (3.8): In practice s* is unknown. Provide data-driven recipes (e.g., stability selection, information criteria, validation of support size, monotone path in bs with early stopping) and report robustness.
  - Tuning for the Dantzig step (2.6): Give a practical rule for setting λ in finite samples; discuss computational cost and numerical stability for Tn(·) estimation.
  - Resampling variant: The analysis uses a resampling EM; clarify whether the same guarantees (or close analogues) hold for the standard single-sample EM and document empirical differences.

Specific questions
- Minimax optimality claim: The abstract/intro claims (near-)optimal rate sqrt(s* log d / n) (sometimes written as sqrt(s* log(d/n))). Please (a) standardize the rate statement, (b) provide a clear minimax lower bound and scope (which models, parameter classes, and signal-to-noise ranges), with a self-contained proof or precise citations.
- Role of truncation: What breaks if the T-step is omitted? Without truncation, does the algorithm still geometrically contract but plateau at a suboptimal rate (≈ sqrt(d/n)) due to ℓ2 error accumulation? Please include a theorem or at least an empirical ablation showing the gap.
- Choice of gradient step size: The theory fixes η = 2/(ν+μ). How sensitive is performance to misspecification of μ, ν? Any adaptive line-search alternative?
- Finite-sample constants: For the two worked models, can you quantify the basin radius R and contraction factor ρ in terms of SNR and dimensions, to guide practitioners?

Quality
- Technical soundness appears high. Assumptions are standard for nonconvex EM analyses and are carefully mapped to model-specific verifications. The inference result (Theorem 4.6) achieving the semiparametric information bound is a strong contribution. However, clearer presentation of minimax lower bounds and practical selection of bs/initialization would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
- The writing is generally precise but heavy. Incorporating the proposed structural edits and practical guidelines would materially improve readability for the NIPS audience.

Originality
- High. Extends recent EM population-to-sample analyses to the high-dimensional, nonconvex, latent-variable setting with a novel truncation mechanism and delivers asymptotic inference with semiparametric efficiency. This combination is new relative to prior work on GLMs or tensor methods.

Significance
- High for the statistics/learning theory community; potentially impactful for practitioners working with mixtures/latent models in high dimensions. Practical adoption will depend on the guidance about initialization, tuning, and computational overhead.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong theoretical contributions for both estimation and inference; semiparametric efficiency.
- General framework covering popular latent models; unifies exact and gradient M-steps.
- Advances over prior EM/tensor-method guarantees in high dimensions.

Arguments against acceptance
- Accessibility: dense exposition; limited practical guidance on initialization and tuning.
- Empirical evaluation (as presented) appears limited; ablations on truncation, initialization, and tuning would help.
- Some claims (minimax optimality) need clearer, centralized statements and proofs/citations.

Post-rebuttal verdict
- After clarifications and added guidance on initialization/bs selection, I raised my score from 6 to 7. This is a solid statistics contribution; while challenging for some ML readers and with practicality caveats, it advances the state of the art on high-dimensional EM estimation and inference.
