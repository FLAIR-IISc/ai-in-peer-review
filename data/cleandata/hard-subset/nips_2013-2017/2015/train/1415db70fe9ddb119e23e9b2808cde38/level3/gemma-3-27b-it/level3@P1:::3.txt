This paper tackles a fundamental challenge in latent variable modeling: extending the theoretical guarantees of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to the high-dimensional setting where the number of parameters exceeds the sample size. The authors propose a novel “high-dimensional EM” algorithm, incorporating a truncation step during both the E-step and M-step to promote sparsity and achieve statistical optimality.  Alongside this algorithmic development, they present a new inferential procedure based on a decorrelated score statistic, enabling hypothesis testing for low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. The work builds upon previous NIPS contributions such as [2, 18, 25, 30] which established convergence and rates for the EM algorithm in low dimensions, and attempts to bridge the gap between computational feasibility and statistical optimality in high dimensions – a gap previously unaddressed.

The core strength of this paper lies in providing a comprehensive theoretical framework for analyzing the high-dimensional EM algorithm. The authors demonstrate geometric convergence of the estimator to a near-optimal rate, specifically  √(sparsity * log(d/n)), under appropriate initialization. This is a significant achievement, as existing theoretical guarantees are often lacking in high dimensions, and attaining such rates is challenging due to the non-convexity of the likelihood function.  Furthermore, the development of an asymptotically normal decorrelated score statistic provides a crucial tool for statistical inference, allowing practitioners to assess the uncertainty associated with parameter estimates.  The careful consideration of both computational and statistical guarantees, and the attempt to provide a unified analytic framework, demonstrates a thorough and rigorous approach. The application of the framework to Gaussian Mixture Models and Mixture of Regression models illustrates its broad applicability.

However, several weaknesses and areas for improvement warrant consideration. The necessity of the proposed “sparse EM” algorithm, involving truncation at each step, is not fully convincing. The authors acknowledge that for some models (specifically mentioning estimating the M-step at sparse vectors being as easy as at all vectors), the truncation may not be essential.  A more detailed discussion comparing the performance and theoretical properties of the proposed algorithm with a standard EM algorithm followed by a single final truncation step would be valuable.  The authors briefly mention linear regression as a counterexample, but further elaboration is needed.  A related point is the assumption of a suitable initialization within a defined basin of attraction. While common in EM analysis [30], the practical implications of this assumption for real-world applications and strategies for achieving such initialization deserve more attention.  The presented technical conditions, while necessary for the theoretical results, are quite involved.  Simplifying or providing more intuitive interpretations of these conditions could enhance the accessibility of the paper. 

The paper would also benefit from mentioning related work in sparse optimization, such as the work by Langford et al. [2009] on truncated gradient methods, which shares conceptual similarities with the proposed approach.  Furthermore, the experimental section is absent, and details on the initialization strategy used in simulations (or proofs) should be explicitly reported to facilitate reproducibility. A discussion of the computational cost of the truncation step would also be beneficial, particularly as it is performed iteratively.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper addresses a long-standing open problem – extending EM convergence guarantees to the high-dimensional regime.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The mathematical analysis is thorough and provides strong theoretical foundations for the proposed algorithm and inferential procedure.
*   **Significance:** The results have the potential to significantly impact the field of latent variable modeling, enabling reliable parameter estimation and inference in high-dimensional settings.
*   **Generality:** The framework is shown to apply to a range of models, demonstrating its broad applicability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Unclear Advantage of Sparse EM:** The necessity and benefits of the iterative truncation step, as opposed to a single final truncation, remain somewhat unclear.
*   **Technical Complexity:** The numerous technical conditions may hinder accessibility and understanding for readers outside the immediate area.
*   **Lack of Experiments:** The absence of experimental validation limits the ability to assess the practical performance of the proposed algorithm.



**Overall assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this paper represents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in high dimensions. The rigorous analysis and novel inferential procedure make it a valuable addition to the NIPS literature. While the necessity of the iterative truncation remains open to debate, the theoretical results are compelling and the potential impact is substantial. I recommend **acceptance** with a request for the authors to address the concerns regarding the sparse EM algorithm, clarify the technical conditions, and include experimental results.  Providing more detail on the initialization strategy and computational cost would also strengthen the paper.
