This paper develops a high-dimensional EM framework for latent-variable models with two main results: (i) a truncated EM algorithm that enforces sparsity and achieves geometric convergence to a statistically near-optimal estimator; and (ii) an inferential procedure for a low-dimensional target (a coordinate or small subvector) via a decorrelated score (and Wald) test with asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency. The analysis covers both exact-M and gradient-M variants of EM, and provides sample- and population-level conditions ensuring contraction and sharp entrywise statistical control. Applications to Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions illustrate the framework.

Relation to prior work: The computational analysis extends the “from population to sample” EM theory of Balakrishnan et al. to p ≫ n by introducing a truncation step that preserves sparsity. The inferential component follows the decorrelated score/debiased M-estimation literature (e.g., Ning & Liu), but adapts it to EM estimators that are not global minimizers of convex losses, using the Q-function to connect gradients and Fisher information in latent-variable settings. Compared to tensor methods for mixtures, the proposed rates are substantially sharper in high dimensions.

Strengths:
- Clear algorithmic idea (E/M + truncation) with a unified analysis for both exact and gradient M-steps.
- Sharp estimation rates O(√(s* log d/n)) up to logs, with explicit optimization vs statistical error decomposition and geometric contraction.
- Inference achieves asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency, filling a gap for latent-variable models with high-dimensional nuisance parameters.
- Conditions are explicitly stated and verified for representative models (Gaussian mixture, mixture of regressions).

Weaknesses:
- Strong conditions (notably, SNR for mixtures, sparsity of w* in the orthogonalization step, and an initialization with constant relative error) are central, yet practical guidance to meet them is limited in the main text.
- Tuning and implementation choices (sparsity level ŝ and Dantzig λ) lack concrete, data-driven prescriptions and sensitivity analysis.
- Several key technical ideas (support evolution under truncation, entrywise statistical error control) are deferred to the supplement, making it hard to assess without cross-referencing.
- The relationship to Ning & Liu’s decorrelated score is not made explicit enough; readers may be unclear about what is reused versus what is novel here.

Quality: The technical development appears sound, with appropriate conditions (concavity/smoothness in a basin, Lipschitz gradient conditions tailored to exact vs gradient M-step, and entrywise concentration for the M-step’s sample-population gap). The decomposition into optimization and statistical errors is convincing and standard in modern high-dimensional optimization. It would help to provide proof sketches and intuitions for the truncation-step analysis in the main paper.

Clarity: The high-level narrative is clear; however, practical guidance (initialization, algorithm choice, tuning) should be elevated into the main text. Some notation (Tn, w, basin B) could be summarized in a self-contained figure/table for readability.

Originality: The truncated EM analysis for high-dimensional latent-variable models, with entrywise control enabling optimal rates, is novel. The inference procedure’s statistical core matches Ning & Liu’s decorrelated score test; the new contribution is making it work for EM-based estimators and latent-variable likelihoods (via the Q-to-score/Fisher bridge and tailored concentration), which is nontrivial.

Significance: Provides the first comprehensive estimation-and-inference theory for high-dimensional EM across a class of latent-variable models with practical algorithmic variants. Likely to influence both methodology and theory.

Detailed suggestions addressing key points:
- Initialization βinit: Please give concrete, model-specific initializers and finite-sample heuristics. For two-component spherical GMM with means ±β*, a practical initializer is the leading eigenvector of the centered covariance (Σ̂ − σ̂2I), scaled by a rough SNR estimate; thresholding yields sparsity. For mixtures of regressions, discuss spectral experts/tensor initializers (Chaganty & Liang) with subsequent thresholding, or a few iterations of gradient-EM with a large support cap and early stopping. Provide sufficient conditions (SNR, sample size) ensuring the stated constant-relative-error property, and report empirical robustness when the initializer is weaker.
- When to use exact-M vs gradient-M: Recommend exact-M when a closed-form M-step exists or when the per-iteration cost is light; it typically yields a stronger contraction (smaller ρ1) per iteration. Use gradient-M for large-scale problems or complex M-steps; give guidance on step-size (η = 2/(ν+μ)) estimation (e.g., via line-search or curvature estimates from the E-step). Discuss wall-clock tradeoffs and convergence in practice.
- Choosing ŝ and λ: Provide defaults and data-driven rules. For ŝ (bs), suggest overspecifying by a modest factor relative to s* to control truncation bias (e.g., ŝ ≈ c·s*, c∈[2,4]), and select via a validation set optimizing held-out log-likelihood or a BIC-type criterion combining fit and support size; stability selection across runs is also helpful. For λ in the Dantzig selector, recommend λ = c·σ̂·√(log d/n) times a curvature factor derived from Tn, with c chosen by a small validation grid; report sensitivity of inference (type I error, power) to λ.
- Decorrelated score and prior art: Please explicitly cite Ning & Liu (Ann. Stat. 2017; arXiv 2013) and related desparsification work, and state that your test statistic coincides with their decorrelated score when Q is the usual likelihood and w is estimated via a Dantzig/ℓ1 program. Clarify that your new development is (i) identifying rQ and Tn as normalized score and Fisher information in latent-variable models, and (ii) verifying the requisite concentration/Lipschitz properties for EM-based estimators under truncation, nonconvexity, and high dimensions.
- Assumptions: Summarize, in the main text, the regimes under which Conditions 3.1–3.3 and 4.1–4.4 hold for each model. For GMM, highlight the SNR threshold ensuring local strong concavity and Lipschitz gradient (your constants 1, μ, ν), and state the sample size scaling n ≳ s* log d. For mixtures of regressions, specify distributional assumptions on covariates (sub-Gaussian, bounded covariance), noise, and the sparsity of w* required for inference. Discuss the practicality of the sparsity of w* (sparsity of the orthogonalization vector) and when it may fail.
- Exposition: Move proof sketches of the truncation-step effect on support evolution, and the entrywise M-step concentration, into the main paper; add a short “practical guide” subsection consolidating initialization, algorithm choice, and tuning.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid and timely theory bridging computation and inference for high-dimensional latent-variable models.
- Clear algorithm with strong guarantees and practical variants.
- Semiparametric efficiency for inference.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on strong SNR and sparsity-of-w* assumptions; limited discussion of their realism.
- Insufficient practical guidance and empirical validation in the main text.
- Some novelty in inference rests on adapting known decorrelated score ideas; clearer attribution needed.

Overall: A strong theoretical contribution with meaningful methodological impact. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved clarity, explicit attribution to Ning & Liu for the score test, and more practical guidance and empirical illustration.
