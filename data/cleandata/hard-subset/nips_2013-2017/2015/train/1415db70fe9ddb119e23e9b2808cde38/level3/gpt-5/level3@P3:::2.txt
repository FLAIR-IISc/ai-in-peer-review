Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm that explicitly enforces sparsity via a truncation step after each E- and M-step. Under model-specific regularity conditions and a suitable initialization, the authors prove geometric (optimization) convergence and a final estimation error of order sqrt(s* log d / n) up to logarithmic factors. They also introduce an inferential framework: a decorrelated score (and Wald) test for low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters, achieving asymptotic normality with variance equal to the semiparametric efficiency bound. The theory covers both exact maximization and gradient-ascent implementations of the M-step and is illustrated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Relative to prior EM analyses that focus on low-dimensional settings or local convergence to a stationary point (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015), the main novelty is a computationally feasible EM scheme with explicit sparsity control and rates in the high-dimensional regime. Compared to high-dimensional inference methods largely developed for GLMs via convex programs (debiased/desparsified Lasso, post-selection inference), the paper extends semiparametric-style inference to latent variable models where the likelihood is non-convex and the estimator is not a global optimum. The work also contrasts with tensor methods for latent variable models, arguing for better statistical rates and broader applicability.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A carefully tailored, truncated EM procedure with conditions guaranteeing convergence to a global neighborhood and near-minimax statistical rates. The decomposition into optimization vs. statistical error is clean and insightful.
- Inference contribution: A decorrelated score test achieving the semiparametric information bound in latent variable models with high-dimensional nuisance parameters.
- Organization and related work: The paper is well structured, with a thorough and accurate positioning relative to EM analysis, tensor methods, and high-dimensional inference.

Weaknesses and clarity suggestions
- Density of presentation: Sections 3 and 4 are very dense. The layered conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity/smoothness, statistical error, and inference-specific concentration/Lipschitz bounds) obscure the main ideas.
  - Suggestions: (i) Provide a running, intuitive example (e.g., two-component spherical GMM) alongside each condition to explain its meaning and how it is verified; (ii) summarize constants and scaling in a single “at-a-glance” table; (iii) move some technical lemmas and resampling proofs to the appendix and keep the main text focused on high-level proof flow; (iv) include a short “how to use” recipe: choose bs, initialize, step-size, stopping, and inference steps.
- Practical guidance: The paper needs clearer, practitioner-level instructions for choosing tuning parameters, initializing, and diagnosing when conditions are likely to hold.

Specific questions and concerns
- Minimax optimality claim: The manuscript states that the estimator attains a (near-)optimal statistical rate, often written as sqrt(s* log d / n) (the text also uses sqrt(s* log(d/n))). I could not find a self-contained minimax lower bound in the main text for the latent variable settings considered. Please:
  - Either provide a precise theorem (with assumptions) proving the lower bound for your model classes, or
  - Cite an existing lower bound that matches your setting and rate (including dependence on separation/SNR and mixture parameters). If the rate is only known for special cases (e.g., sparse mean estimation under mixtures), clarify the scope. Also resolve the log(d) vs. log(d/n) discrepancy and ensure consistent notation throughout.

- Choice of bs in Eq. (3.8): bs must be on the order of s*, which is unknown.
  - Please provide practical guidance: e.g., a path over bs with validation on held-out likelihood; stability selection; information criteria (BIC/AIC variants on the completed-data log-likelihood); or a conservative over-specification bs = c·sqrt(n / log d) capped at d, with post-hoc thresholding. A short sensitivity analysis would help.

- Initialization: The theory requires an initial βinit with ||βinit – β*||2 < R/2. How to obtain this in practice?
  - For GMMs: suggest and document a simple, verifiable initializer (e.g., spectral/method-of-moments followed by hard thresholding; or k-means on a random subset with screening). For mixtures of regressions: a tensor/spectral initializer or method-of-moments followed by sparsity projection. Please quantify the required separation/SNR and sample size to guarantee the basin-of-attraction condition and provide empirical sensitivity plots to initialization quality.

- Role of truncation step: What happens if truncation is omitted?
  - My understanding is that without truncation the iterates need not remain sparse, Condition 3.3 fails to yield entrywise control, and the statistical error scales like sqrt(d/n), breaking the high-dimensional rate and potentially violating the basin-of-attraction. Please confirm and, if possible, add a theorem/remark and an ablation demonstrating the necessity of truncation in practice.

- Resampling vs. single-sample analysis: The main theorem is proved for a resampling version of the algorithm. Please clarify whether analogous guarantees hold without sample splitting and, if so, under what mixing/stability conditions; otherwise, discuss the practical gap.

- Computational aspects of inference: The Dantzig-style step to estimate w can be heavy. Please discuss viable alternatives (nodewise Lasso, CLIME-like approximations, or ridge-based approximations) and their impact on theory and runtime.

Quality
The technical development is strong and carefully argued. Assumptions (e.g., sparsity of w*, separation/SNR, basin-of-attraction) are standard for this line of work but should be made more transparent where possible. Missing or unclear pieces are the explicit minimax lower bound, concrete initialization strategies, and pragmatic tuning guidance.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well organized but hard to follow in the core theory sections. The suggestions above (running example, summary table, practitioner recipe) would materially improve readability for the NIPS audience.

Originality
High. To my knowledge, this is the first general high-dimensional EM framework with both optimal (or near-optimal) estimation rates and asymptotically efficient inference for latent variable models.

Significance
High for the statistics/ML theory community. For practitioners, usefulness depends on clearer guidance and lighter-weight inference routines; as written, practical uptake may be limited.

Pros
- Novel high-dimensional EM with truncation and rigorous guarantees.
- Asymptotically efficient inference for non-convex latent variable models.
- Solid comparison to prior EM/tensor/inference literature.

Cons
- Dense presentation; practical guidance is limited.
- Requires nontrivial initialization and knowledge of sparsity level.
- Inference step may be computationally heavy.
- Minimax optimality claim needs a clear proof or citation.

Post-rebuttal verdict
After the rebuttal, I raise my score from 6 to 7. I view this as a solid statistics contribution with strong theoretical value. However, it remains challenging for the broader NIPS audience and its practical usefulness is uncertain without additional guidance and experiments addressing tuning, initialization, and the necessity of truncation.

Recommendation: 7 (accept). I encourage the authors to address the clarity and practicality points above in the camera-ready.
