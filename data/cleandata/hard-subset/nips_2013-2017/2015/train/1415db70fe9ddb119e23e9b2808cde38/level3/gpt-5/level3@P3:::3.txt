Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a high-dimensional theory for EM with two main contributions. First, it proposes a sparse EM procedure that appends a hard-thresholding (truncation) step to each E/M iteration to enforce sparsity. Under smoothness/strong concavity and local Lipschitz gradient assumptions on the population Q-function, and with an initialization inside a basin of attraction, the estimator converges geometrically to the truth up to a statistical error of order sqrt(s*) times the l_infty M-step estimation error (which is typically O(sqrt(log d / n))). Second, the paper introduces a decorrelated score test for a low-dimensional parameter component that is asymptotically normal and attains the semiparametric information bound, enabling valid inference with high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The framework covers both exact and gradient-based M-step implementations and is illustrated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

The work builds on the population-to-sample EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) by adapting it to the high-dimensional regime through iterative truncation and entrywise concentration. For inference, it connects to desparsification/debiasing approaches (e.g., Zhang & Zhang 2014; van de Geer et al. 2014) via a Dantzig-selector-based decorrelation step, but extends them beyond convex GLMs to nonconvex latent variable models where global optima are infeasible. The truncation step resembles ideas from sparse optimization and hard-thresholded algorithms (e.g., Langford et al. 2009 on truncated gradient), and could be discussed in this context.

Strengths
- Quality: Clear decomposition of error into optimization (geometric decay) and statistical (near-minimax) terms. The asymptotic normality result attains the semiparametric information bound. Conditions are stated cleanly and verified for two canonical latent-variable models. The analysis treats both exact and gradient M-steps.
- Originality: First general high-dimensional convergence and inference guarantees for EM in latent-variable models, with novel technical control in l_infty for the sample-population M-step discrepancy restricted to sparse vectors.
- Significance: Provides a computationally feasible route to optimal rates and valid inference for widely used models (GMM, mixture of regressions), which could influence practice and spur further work on nonconvex high-dimensional inference.
- Clarity: The framework is well-structured; assumptions (Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness, Statistical-Error) are explicit; the role of the basin of attraction and initialization is clearly acknowledged.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Necessity of per-iteration truncation: For several models, estimating the M operator at arbitrary vectors can be no harder (statistically or computationally) than at sparse vectors, which raises the question whether truncation is needed beyond analysis convenience. Could ordinary EM followed by a single truncation/projection achieve the same rate and inference? The paper should either provide a counterexample (the authors later argue linear regression is one) or an empirical comparison showing the advantage of iterative truncation.
- New technical condition: Condition 3.3 requires l_infty control of Mn(·) − M(·) when the input is sparse. Please clarify when this is strictly weaker/easier than controlling Mn − M uniformly over all vectors, and whether it truly necessitates truncation for the covered models.
- Resampling variant: The main finite-sample analysis focuses on a resampling version of EM. Please discuss whether the same guarantees hold (perhaps with minor changes) for a single-sample path and what additional dependence issues arise.
- Initialization: Guarantees require starting in a basin where Q is nearly quadratic. The experimental section should report the exact initialization scheme, its success probability, and sensitivity analyses. Practical guidance on how to find such initializations for the covered models would increase utility.
- Inference assumptions: The decorrelated score requires the auxiliary vector w* to be sparse. Please quantify when this holds for the presented models and how violations affect power and validity. Also discuss computational cost and tuning for the Dantzig step in high d.
- Comparisons and related work: It would be helpful to position the truncation step relative to prior sparse optimization with truncation/hard-thresholding (e.g., Langford et al. 2009; iterative hard-thresholding) and to penalized EM variants (e.g., l1-penalized EM). Including baselines such as (i) EM + final truncation, (ii) l1-penalized EM/prox-EM, and (iii) thresholded gradient EM would strengthen the empirical story.

Quality
- Theoretical claims are well-supported and carefully proved under explicit conditions, with model-specific verifications.
- Experiments (as presented) should disclose: initialization, choice of bs, step sizes, stopping criteria, and whether exact vs gradient M-step was used. Provide comparisons to strong sparse baselines and ablations on truncation frequency.

Clarity
- The paper is well-written. Suggestions: consolidate notation for population vs sample M-steps; clearly separate analysis for the resampling and single-sample variants; gather all tuning parameter choices (bs, η, Dantzig λ) in one place with practical defaults.

Originality
- The integration of iterative truncation within EM for high-dimensional nonconvex models with inference guarantees is novel relative to prior EM analyses and debiasing literature.

Significance
- Important step towards principled estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent-variable models. The practical necessity of iterative truncation needs clearer empirical and theoretical justification to maximize impact.

Specific suggestions
- Add and discuss related sparse/truncated optimization work (e.g., Langford, Li, Zhang, JMLR 2009, “Sparse Online Learning via Truncated Gradient”), and connections to iterative hard-thresholding.
- Provide an explicit example (or reference) where EM + one-shot truncation fails to match your guarantees; if linear regression is a counterexample, summarize why.
- Report and analyze initialization strategies and sensitivity; include ablations on bs mis-specification.
- Compare against l1-penalized EM/prox-EM and post hoc truncation.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, general theoretical framework with both estimation and inference.
- Near-optimal statistical rates and semiparametric efficiency.
- Covers multiple important latent-variable models and both M-step implementations.

Cons for acceptance
- Necessity and empirical advantage of per-iteration truncation remain uncertain.
- Reliance on resampling analysis; limited discussion of single-sample trajectories.
- Practical guidance on initialization and inference tuning could be expanded.

Overall recommendation
Leaning positive. The paper provides important high-dimensional convergence and inference guarantees for EM. Clarifying the necessity and empirical benefit of the sparse EM variant, and strengthening experimental comparisons and initialization reporting, would make the contribution even more compelling.
