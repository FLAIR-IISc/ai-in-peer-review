This paper presents a novel high-dimensional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm designed for latent variable models with sparse parameters, accompanied by a statistical framework for inference. The authors address a significant gap in the literature, as traditional EM convergence guarantees do not extend to the high-dimensional setting (d > n). The core contribution lies in a modified EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, coupled with theoretical results establishing geometric convergence and achieving a near-optimal statistical rate of convergence of  √(s log(d/n)), where 's' represents sparsity.  A second notable contribution is the development of a decorrelated score test for hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components within the high-dimensional parameter space.

The work builds upon previous research attempting to bridge the gap between computational feasibility (local optima attained by EM) and statistical guarantees (typically requiring global optima).  References [30, 25, 2] are correctly positioned as prior work, with the authors clearly highlighting that existing results break down in high dimensions. The paper correctly identifies the challenges posed by high dimensionality, particularly the ill-defined nature of the maximum likelihood estimator without regularization and the difficulty of establishing asymptotic normality due to high-dimensional nuisance parameters.  The proposed approach aims to overcome these hurdles, offering a computationally feasible and statistically sound framework.

The strengths of the paper lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and its focused problem statement. The organization is logical, and the related work is comprehensively summarized, demonstrating an understanding of the existing literature. The authors convincingly articulate the limitations of prior approaches and position their work as a significant advance. However, the density of the mathematical presentation in Sections 3 and 4 significantly hinders readability and accessibility. These sections are laden with notation and require substantial effort to follow, even for readers familiar with the field.  Simplification and increased intuitive explanation would greatly benefit these sections. 

A critical claim made is that the achieved convergence rate of √(s log(d/n)) is minimax-optimal.  However, the manuscript lacks a clear demonstration or citation to support this assertion. Providing a proof or a more detailed justification for this optimality claim is crucial.  

Practical concerns also arise. Equation (3.8) defines a sparsity parameter, ^s, which inherently depends on the unknown true sparsity level, 's'.  The paper does not address how to practically estimate 's' in a real-world scenario, raising questions about the applicability of the algorithm without prior knowledge of the underlying sparsity structure.  Similarly, obtaining an initial parameter estimate, beta^init, satisfying the condition ||beta^init - beta*||_2 < R/2 (as required for the convergence guarantee) presents a practical challenge that the authors do not fully address. What strategies can be employed to achieve such an initialization?

Furthermore, the role of the truncation step within Algorithm 1 warrants further investigation. The authors should discuss what implications arise if the truncation step is omitted. Would the algorithm still converge, albeit at a different rate, or would it fail to converge altogether? This investigation would provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed modification.

In summary, this paper presents a technically strong contribution to the statistical theory of the EM algorithm in high dimensions. The theoretical guarantees are impressive, and the proposed decorrelated score test represents a valuable addition to the statistical toolkit. However, the density of the presentation and the lack of guidance on practical implementation details (sparsity parameter estimation, initialization) limit its immediate impact. Addressing these concerns, particularly providing justification for the optimality claim and clarifying the practical aspects of the algorithm, would significantly enhance the paper's value.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees.
*   Addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding high-dimensional EM.
*   The proposed decorrelated score test is a novel and potentially useful contribution.
*   Comprehensive related work demonstrates a strong understanding of the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   High density of mathematical notation in Sections 3 and 4 hinders readability.
*   Lack of justification for the minimax-optimality claim.
*   Practical concerns regarding the estimation of the sparsity parameter and initialization.
*   Limited discussion on the impact of omitting the truncation step.
