This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure into parameter estimation. The algorithm is tailored for high-dimensional latent variable models, where the dimension d is much larger than the sample size n. The authors establish a general theory for the EM algorithm, providing guarantees for its convergence to the global solution and deriving its convergence rate.

The paper makes two main contributions. First, it proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm that converges at a geometric rate and achieves an estimator with the (near-)optimal statistical rate of convergence. Second, it constructs a decorrelated score statistic for testing hypotheses related to low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear summary of related work. The writing is clear, and the notation is well-defined. The authors also provide a detailed proof of their results in the appendix.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to establish a general theory for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The authors provide a unified framework for simultaneous parameter estimation and uncertainty assessment, which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, the contents of the paper are very dense, and Sections 3 and 4 are hard to follow. The authors could simplify the presentation of their results and provide more intuition behind their proofs.

The paper also raises several questions. For example, the authors claim that the rate of convergence sqrt(s * log(d/n)) is minimax-optimal, but they do not provide a clear proof of this statement. Additionally, the authors use the notation ^s, which depends on the unknown s, in Equation (3.8). It is not clear how to choose ^s in practice.

Furthermore, the authors assume that the initial estimate beta^init satisfies ||beta^init - beta*||_2 < R/2, but it is not clear how to obtain such an initial estimate in practice.

The role of the truncation step in Algorithm 1 is also not clearly explained. The authors should provide more intuition behind this step and explain what happens if the truncation is omitted.

Overall, this paper is a solid contribution to the field of statistics, but it may be hard for machine learning researchers to follow. The practical usefulness of the proposed algorithm is also questionable, as it requires a good initial estimate and a careful choice of the sparsity parameter.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear proof of their results. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed algorithm and its potential applications.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but the notation is sometimes confusing. The authors could provide more intuition behind their proofs and simplify the presentation of their results.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of statistics by establishing a general theory for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The authors also provide a novel decorrelated score statistic for testing hypotheses related to low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in statistics and provides a new approach to parameter estimation and uncertainty assessment in high-dimensional settings. The results of the paper have the potential to impact the field of statistics and machine learning, but more work is needed to demonstrate the practical usefulness of the proposed algorithm.
