

# Review: High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality

This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity through a truncation step at each iteration, along with a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional components. The authors establish geometric convergence guarantees for the sparse-EM estimator to the true parameter (up to statistical error) and prove asymptotic normality for the proposed inferential procedure.

The paper builds on recent work by Balakrishnan et al. (2017) and others who established EM convergence guarantees in low dimensions. The key innovation is the incorporation of sparsity constraints through a truncation step, which enables the authors to establish statistical rates of convergence in high dimensions (s* log d/n) rather than the standard low-dimensional rate (sqrt(d/n)). The authors relate their work to sparse optimization literature (e.g., Langford et al. 2009 on truncated gradient methods), though they could have cited this more explicitly.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper provides the first theoretical guarantees for EM in high-dimensional latent variable models, establishing both computational (geometric convergence) and statistical (near-optimal rate) properties.
2. The decorrelated score statistic achieves the semiparametric information bound, which is a significant theoretical contribution to high-dimensional inference.
3. The analysis for Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models demonstrates the framework's applicability to a broad class of models.
4. The paper carefully distinguishes between statistical and optimization errors in the convergence rate, providing a clear decomposition of the estimation error.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The necessity of the sparse-EM procedure is questionable. The authors note that for many models (Gaussian mixtures, non-gradient mixture of regressions), estimating M at all vectors is as easy as at sparse vectors, which undermines the justification for the truncation step.
2. The paper raises but does not adequately resolve the question of whether ordinary EM followed by a single truncation could achieve similar guarantees. The counterexample in linear regression (mentioned but not elaborated) needs stronger justification.
3. The technical condition requiring infinity-norm control on M-step error for sparse vectors is novel but lacks clear justification for why it cannot be replaced by standard L2-norm control.
4. The experimental section fails to specify initialization methods, which is critical given the paper's reliance on initialization within a basin of attraction.
5. The paper does not sufficiently address why the truncation step is necessary when the M-step itself could potentially incorporate sparsity constraints.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs of geometric convergence and asymptotic normality are rigorous, though the reliance on a basin of attraction assumption (requiring initialization within a specific region) limits practical applicability. The authors acknowledge limitations, but the necessity of the sparse-EM variant remains unconvincingly established.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with careful notation. However, the experimental section lacks details on initialization, which is crucial for reproducibility. The connection between the theoretical conditions and practical implementation could be strengthened.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution in the high-dimensional EM framework, particularly in establishing inference guarantees. While the truncation idea has precedents in sparse optimization (e.g., Langford et al. 2009), the application to EM and the establishment of statistical guarantees in high dimensions is novel. The decorrelated score statistic for latent variable models is a significant advance over existing approaches.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important gap in the theory of EM for high-dimensional settings, which has significant implications for applications in genomics, image analysis, and other fields where high-dimensional latent variable models are prevalent. However, the practical advantage of the proposed sparse-EM over simpler alternatives (like standard EM followed by truncation) remains uncertain, which limits the paper's immediate impact.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* First theoretical framework for high-dimensional EM with both estimation and inference guarantees; establishes near-optimal statistical rates; provides a general method applicable to many latent variable models; decorrelated score statistic achieves semiparametric efficiency.

*Con:* Necessity of the sparse-EM procedure is not convincingly established; counterexample to simpler approaches (ordinary EM + truncation) is not fully developed; experimental section lacks critical initialization details; technical condition for M-step error requires further justification.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to high-dimensional statistics, establishing important convergence guarantees for EM in high dimensions. However, the specific advantage of the proposed sparse-EM variant over simpler alternatives remains uncertain, and the paper would be strengthened by addressing these concerns. The theoretical results are significant, but the practical justification for the methodological innovation requires further development. Given the importance of the problem addressed, I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should clarify the necessity of their approach in revisions.
