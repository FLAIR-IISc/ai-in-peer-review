

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure for parameter estimation in latent variable models, along with a decorrelated score test for asymptotic inference of low-dimensional components. The authors establish a general theoretical framework that provides both computational and statistical guarantees, filling a significant gap in the literature where existing EM theory only applies to low-dimensional settings.

The paper builds upon the foundational work of [2] which established geometric convergence rates and statistical rates for the low-dimensional EM algorithm. The key innovation is the introduction of a truncation step in the E-step and M-step to enforce sparsity, enabling the analysis of high-dimensional settings (d >> n). The authors further develop a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing, establishing its asymptotic normality and optimality in the semiparametric sense.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a significant contribution by extending EM algorithm theory to high-dimensional settings with sparsity assumptions, a problem that has not been adequately addressed before.
2. The theoretical results are well-motivated and provide a unified framework applicable to a broad family of latent variable models (Gaussian mixtures, mixture of regressions).
3. The decorrelated score test is a novel application to latent variable models, and the authors successfully establish its asymptotic normality and optimality.
4. The paper provides a clear decomposition of estimation error into optimization and statistical components, which is insightful for understanding the algorithm's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper lacks sufficient practical guidance. The requirement for an initial estimator with relative error ||β^init - β*||_2 / ||β*||_2 ≤ ε (line 60, p.2) is not addressed with practical methods for obtaining such an initialization. Without this, the theoretical guarantees remain largely impractical.

2. The paper does not provide guidance on when to use the exact maximization (Algorithm 2) versus gradient ascent (Algorithm 3) implementation of the M-step. This is a significant omission for practitioners who need to choose between these two approaches.

3. The paper mentions tuning parameters (bs and λ for the Dantzig selector) but fails to explain how to choose them in practice, assess sensitivity, or offer data-dependent selection methods. This severely limits the paper's practical utility.

4. The authors should explicitly cite Ning & Liu (2017) and clarify whether applying their decorrelated score method to latent variable models required new developments beyond the existing framework. The paper currently presents the decorrelated score test as novel without sufficient contextualization.

5. The numerous technical assumptions required for the main theorems (Conditions 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) are only claimed to be verified in the appendix, not in the main paper. For a NIPS submission, these verifications should be presented in the main text for key models like Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions.

6. The paper relies heavily on a lengthy supplement for key technical details and verifications. The main paper should include more details on the technical challenges and proofs to stand on its own.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Significant theoretical contribution extending EM algorithm theory to high-dimensional settings
- Provides the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models
- Establishes a unified framework applicable to multiple models (Gaussian mixtures, mixture of regressions)
- The decorrelated score test is novel for latent variable models and achieves semiparametric efficiency

*Con:*
- Lacks practical implementation details (initialization, tuning parameters, algorithm selection)
- Verifications of technical assumptions for specific models are relegated to the appendix
- Insufficient contextualization of the decorrelated score test relative to existing work
- The paper is not sufficiently self-contained; key details are buried in the supplement

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes an important theoretical contribution that fills a significant gap in the literature. The core ideas are novel and well-motivated, and the theoretical results are sound. However, the paper's practical utility is severely limited by the lack of implementation guidance, and the main paper does not sufficiently verify the technical assumptions for the models of interest. The theoretical framework is strong but would be significantly enhanced by including more practical details and making the verification of technical conditions accessible in the main text.

I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the practical implementation concerns and to provide more detailed verification of technical assumptions for the key models. The paper's theoretical contribution is valuable, but it would be more impactful if it were more accessible to practitioners.
