This paper presents a novel high-dimensional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with theoretical guarantees for parameter estimation and hypothesis testing in latent variable models. The authors address a significant gap in the literature by providing convergence rates and asymptotic normality results for the EM algorithm in the challenging high-dimensional setting (d > n), leveraging sparsity assumptions. The core idea is to incorporate a truncation step into the EM iterations to promote sparsity and achieve optimal statistical rates. The paper also proposes a decorrelated score test for low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters.

The work builds upon existing literature concerning the convergence of the EM algorithm, notably [30, 25, 2], which establish guarantees in the low-dimensional regime.  It distinguishes itself by tackling the high-dimensional case, where standard maximum likelihood estimation is ill-posed without regularization. The authors correctly identify the lack of theoretical foundations for the EM algorithm in high dimensions and aim to bridge this gap. Related work in high-dimensional inference, such as Lasso-based methods [8, 26, 32] and tensor methods [1, 9], are appropriately discussed, highlighting the novelty of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, establishing convergence rates and asymptotic normality under reasonable assumptions.
* **Novelty:** Addressing the EM algorithm in high dimensions with guarantees is a significant contribution. The truncation step is a clever addition to enforce sparsity.
* **Generality:** The framework is presented as applicable to a broad family of latent variable models, demonstrating its potential impact.
* **Clear Organization & Related Work:** The paper is generally well-structured and provides a good overview of related research. The positioning of the work within the existing literature is well done.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density and Accessibility:** Sections 3 and 4 are extremely dense and mathematically involved. The numerous conditions and technical details make it difficult for readers to follow the main ideas without significant effort. Simplification and more intuitive explanations would greatly improve accessibility.
* **Minimax Optimality:** The claim of achieving a minimax-optimal rate of convergence (sqrt(s * log(d/n))) is made, but the justification or proof of this optimality isn't readily apparent in the main manuscript. A clear statement of the corresponding minimax lower bound or a reference providing it would strengthen this claim.
* **Practical Implementation Concerns:** Equation (3.8) involves estimating ‘s’ (sparsity level), which is often unknown in practice.  The paper doesn't offer guidance on how to choose an appropriate estimate (^s) for practical applications.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The requirement for an initial parameter estimate (beta^init) with a specific distance constraint (||beta^init - beta*||_2 < R/2) poses a challenge. The paper lacks discussion on how to obtain such an initialization in real-world scenarios.
* **Role of Truncation:** A brief ablation study or discussion on the impact of omitting the truncation step in Algorithm 1 would be valuable. Understanding the consequences of removing this key component would provide further insight into its importance.



**Specific Questions/Suggestions:**

* **Minimax Optimality Proof:** Please provide a justification for the claim that the achieved convergence rate is minimax-optimal.
* **Sparsity Estimation:** How can the sparsity level ‘s’ be estimated in practice for implementation of the algorithm?
* **Initialization Strategy:**  What practical strategies can be used to obtain a suitable initial parameter estimate beta^init that satisfies the required constraint?
* **Truncation Step Analysis:** What happens if the truncation step is removed from Algorithm 1?  Does the algorithm still converge, and if so, at what rate?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and novelty in addressing a long-standing problem.
* Potential for significant impact on the field of high-dimensional statistical inference.
* Solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* High density and mathematical complexity may limit accessibility for a broad audience.
* Practical implementation concerns regarding sparsity estimation and initialization.
* Lack of clarity regarding minimax optimality and the role of the truncation step.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a solid contribution to the statistical theory of the EM algorithm. The theoretical results are rigorous and the proposed approach is novel. However, the density of the presentation and the practical implementation concerns raise some reservations.  While the paper is highly valuable to researchers specializing in statistical optimization, it may be challenging for machine learning practitioners without a strong statistical background.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding accessibility by simplifying the presentation in Sections 3 and 4 and providing more intuitive explanations.  Addressing the questions regarding minimax optimality, sparsity estimation, initialization, and the truncation step would significantly strengthen the paper.
