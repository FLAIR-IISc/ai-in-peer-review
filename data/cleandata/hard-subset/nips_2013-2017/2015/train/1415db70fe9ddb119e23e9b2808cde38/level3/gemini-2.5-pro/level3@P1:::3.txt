This paper presents a general theoretical framework for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models using a novel variant of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The two primary contributions are: (1) a high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation step (T-step) after each M-step to enforce sparsity on the parameter iterates, and (2) a decorrelated score statistic for performing hypothesis tests on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector.

The authors provide a unified analysis for this framework. For estimation, they prove that under certain regularity conditions and with a suitable initialization, their algorithm converges at a geometric rate. The final estimator is shown to achieve a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of approximately sqrt(s*log(d)/n), where s is the true sparsity, d is the dimension, and n is the sample size. This extends previous analyses of the EM algorithm (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.), which were limited to the low-dimensional (d << n) regime. For inference, the authors show that their proposed decorrelated score statistic is asymptotically normal, enabling valid hypothesis testing. The limiting variance of the test statistic is shown to achieve the semiparametric information bound, making it statistically optimal. The framework is demonstrated on two key models: the high-dimensional Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental and challenging problem: establishing theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional setting where d >> n. This has been a long-standing open problem, and providing a computationally feasible algorithm with provable guarantees for both optimal estimation and asymptotic inference is a major contribution to the field.
2.  **Novelty and Technical Quality:** The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm, which combines the classic EM steps with an iterative hard-thresholding procedure, is a novel approach for this problem domain. The theoretical analysis is substantial and appears technically sound. It successfully navigates the challenges of non-convexity inherent in the EM objective and the high-dimensionality by carefully controlling the evolution of the iterates' support and establishing fine-grained, entrywise statistical error bounds.
3.  **Comprehensive Framework:** A particularly strong aspect of the work is that it goes beyond point estimation to provide a rigorous framework for statistical inference. Developing methods for valid uncertainty quantification (e.g., p-values and confidence intervals) for estimators derived from non-convex optimization procedures in high dimensions is a critical and active area of research. The proposed decorrelated score test is a significant advance for latent variable models.

**Weaknesses and Questions (Arguments against Acceptance / Points for Clarification):**

1.  **Motivation for Iterative Truncation:** A central question is the necessity and advantage of performing a truncation step at *every* iteration. The analysis introduces a new technical condition (Condition 3.3) that requires controlling the infinity-norm of the statistical error of the M-step operator, but only for sparse input vectors. However, for the motivating examples like Gaussian mixture models and mixture of linear regressions, the M-step update can be computed just as easily for a dense input vector as for a sparse one. It is not clear that restricting the analysis to sparse inputs provides any significant simplification or computational benefit in these cases. This raises the question of whether a simpler procedure, such as running a standard (perhaps L1-regularized) EM algorithm and performing a single hard-thresholding step at the end, could achieve similar theoretical guarantees. A discussion comparing the proposed iterative approach to such alternatives would significantly strengthen the paper's motivation.
2.  **Relation to Existing Sparse Optimization Algorithms:** The proposed algorithm is closely related to the family of iterative hard thresholding (IHT) or truncated gradient algorithms. The paper would benefit from explicitly acknowledging and citing this line of work (e.g., Langford et al., JMLR 2009, "Sparse Online Learning via Truncated Gradient"; Blumensath & Davies on IHT) to better contextualize the algorithmic contribution within the broader sparse optimization literature.
3.  **Practicality of Initialization:** The theory relies on an initial parameter estimate that is already within a specific "basin of attraction" around the true parameter. While this is a standard assumption in the analysis of non-convex optimization, it is a critical practical detail. For the method to be useful, such an initialization must be obtainable. The paper should specify what initialization procedures were used in any experiments and briefly discuss how this condition might be met in practice (e.g., via spectral methods).

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a strong, technically deep, and significant theoretical contribution. It provides the first computationally tractable framework with guarantees for both optimal estimation and valid inference for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models. The results are important and likely to be influential.

The primary weakness lies in the motivation for the specific algorithmic choice of iterative truncation, as simpler alternatives may exist and the computational justification is not fully clear for the models considered. However, the analytical framework developed to prove the result is valuable in its own right, and these concerns are primarily points for clarification that can be addressed in a revision. The strengths of the paper, particularly the novel and unified theory of estimation and inference, substantially outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
