This paper presents a novel high-dimensional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and an accompanying inferential framework for latent variable models in the `d >> n` regime. The main idea is to adapt the classical EM algorithm for sparse, high-dimensional parameter estimation by introducing a truncation step (T-step) after the standard E-step and M-step. This T-step enforces sparsity on the parameter estimate at each iteration. The authors provide a unified theoretical analysis for this algorithm, demonstrating that with a suitable initialization, it converges at a geometric rate. The resulting estimator is shown to achieve a (near-)minimax optimal statistical rate of convergence of approximately `sqrt(s* log(d)/n)`.

A second major contribution is the development of a procedure for statistical inference based on the estimator produced by the high-dimensional EM algorithm. The authors construct a "decorrelated score test" to perform hypothesis tests on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. They prove that this test statistic is asymptotically normal and that its limiting variance achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound, making it statistically efficient. The framework is general and its application is illustrated for Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models. This work builds upon prior analysis of the EM algorithm in low dimensions (e.g., [2]) and high-dimensional inference techniques (e.g., [26, 32]), but extends them to the challenging setting of non-convex optimization for latent variable models in high dimensions.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a very important and difficult problem: performing both optimal estimation and valid statistical inference for latent variable models in high-dimensional settings using the popular EM algorithm. Providing a computationally feasible algorithm with guarantees for both geometric convergence and near-optimal statistical rates is a significant theoretical advance. The development of an asymptotically normal inferential framework for the output of a non-convex procedure like EM is particularly novel and valuable.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and appears to be technically sound. The authors establish a clear framework based on a set of verifiable conditions and provide rigorous proofs for their main claims regarding both the estimation error (Theorem 3.4) and the asymptotic normality of the score statistic (Theorem 4.6).

3.  **Clarity of Organization:** The paper is well-organized. The introduction effectively situates the work within the context of existing literature on EM algorithms, high-dimensional estimation, and inference, clearly articulating the paper's novel contributions. The methodology is presented logically before the theoretical guarantees are detailed.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is extremely dense, particularly in the theory sections (Sections 3 and 4). The analysis relies on a large number of abstract technical conditions (e.g., Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness, Statistical-Error) and assumptions. While this is necessary for rigor, it makes the core results very difficult to parse for a general machine learning audience. The intuition behind why these conditions hold and what they mean for practitioners could be explained more clearly. A simplification of the presentation would significantly improve the paper's readability and impact.

2.  **Practical Applicability Concerns:** The practical usefulness of the proposed algorithm is limited by several strong assumptions that are not addressed in the paper:
    *   **Initialization:** The theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.4) hinge on the availability of an initial estimator `init` that is already within a certain `l2`-ball of the true parameter `*` (i.e., `||init - *||_2 < R/2`). This is a common requirement for analyzing non-convex optimization, but the paper provides no discussion on how to obtain such an initial estimate in practice. Without a practical initialization method, the algorithm cannot be readily applied.
    *   **Sparsity Parameter Selection:** The algorithm and theory require a sparsity parameter `bs` that, according to Eq. (3.8), must be on the order of the true, unknown sparsity `s*`. The paper does not discuss how a practitioner would choose `bs` without knowing `s*`. This is a critical tuning parameter, and the lack of guidance on its selection is a major practical limitation.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper claims that the statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d)/n)` is (near-)minimax-optimal. While this is a well-known result in high-dimensional statistics for models like sparse linear regression, could the authors provide a specific citation or a brief argument in the appendix to substantiate this claim in the context of the latent variable models considered here?

2.  Could the authors elaborate on the role of the truncation step? Specifically, what would happen if this step were omitted from Algorithm 1? It seems this step is crucial for maintaining sparsity and enabling the high-dimensional analysis, but a more explicit discussion of its necessity would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid and significant theoretical contribution to the intersection of latent variable models, high-dimensional statistics, and the EM algorithm. The dual guarantees on estimation and inference are impressive. However, its impact is hindered by the dense and highly technical presentation, making it inaccessible to many NIPS researchers. More importantly, the strong and unaddressed assumptions regarding initialization and tuning parameter selection cast doubt on the algorithm's immediate practical utility. While the theoretical novelty is high, the paper would be stronger if it provided more intuition and addressed the practical implementation challenges. It is a strong statistics paper that may be of limited interest to the broader machine learning community in its current form.
