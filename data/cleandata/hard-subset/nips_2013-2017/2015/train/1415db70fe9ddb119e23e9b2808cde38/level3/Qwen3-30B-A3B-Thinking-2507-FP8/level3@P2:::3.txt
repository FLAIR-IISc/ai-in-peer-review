

# Review for "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

This paper addresses an important gap in the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity via a truncation step at each iteration, and develop a unified inferential framework for hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters. The work extends the recent advances on low-dimensional EM algorithms by [2, 18, 25, 30] to the high-dimensional regime, where the dimension d exceeds the sample size n.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

The paper makes two main contributions: (1) a high-dimensional EM algorithm with truncation that achieves geometric convergence to an estimator with near-optimal statistical rate of convergence, and (2) a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference on low-dimensional components that attains the semiparametric information bound. The authors establish their results for a broad family of latent variable models, including Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models. This work builds on the recent low-dimensional guarantees of [2] but extends them to high dimensions by introducing the truncation step to enforce sparsity structure. The paper is well-positioned in the literature, as it addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of EM algorithms in high dimensions, where existing methods fail to provide statistical guarantees.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for both estimation and inference in high dimensions. The authors establish geometric convergence rates for the high-dimensional EM algorithm (Theorem 3.4) and asymptotic normality of the decorrelated score statistic (Theorem 4.6), with careful analysis of both optimization and statistical errors. The assumptions are well-motivated and the proofs are technically sound, with detailed appendix proofs.

**Originality:** The work is highly original in its approach to high-dimensional EM. The truncation step (line 6 of Algorithm 1) is a novel technical contribution that enables the analysis in high dimensions. The decorrelated score statistic for inference is also a significant contribution, as previous work on high-dimensional inference (e.g., [13, 15, 26, 32]) primarily focused on generalized linear models rather than latent variable models.

**Significance:** This paper establishes the first computationally feasible approach for optimal parameter estimation and asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The results have important implications for practitioners working with high-dimensional data in fields like genomics, finance, and image analysis where latent variable models are commonly used.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, with clear definitions of the algorithm and theorems. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The appendix provides sufficient detail for the proofs.

## Weaknesses

**Necessity of Truncation:** The authors' claim that the truncation step is essential for high-dimensional guarantees is questionable. For many models (e.g., Gaussian mixtures, non-gradient mixture of regressions), estimating the M-step at all vectors is as easy as at sparse vectors, suggesting that ordinary EM followed by a single truncation might achieve similar guarantees. The authors later argue that linear regression is a counterexample, but this argument is not fully convincing. A more thorough comparison of the proposed method with a simpler "EM+truncation" approach would strengthen the paper.

**Experimental Section:** The experimental section (which appears to be missing in the provided excerpt) should report the specific initialization used, which is crucial for the theoretical guarantees. Without knowing the initialization method, it's difficult to assess the practical feasibility of the proposed algorithm.

**Citation of Related Work:** The paper should cite related work on sparse optimization methods, such as Langford et al. (2009) on truncated gradient methods, which has conceptual similarities to the proposed truncation step.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:** This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding EM algorithms in high dimensions, providing the first rigorous guarantees for both estimation and inference. The results are technically sound, and the paper is well-written with clear theoretical contributions that advance the field.

**Against Acceptance:** The necessity of the proposed sparse EM variant over simpler alternatives (like EM followed by a single truncation) is not convincingly established. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with simpler approaches, and the experimental section should provide more practical details.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison with a simpler "EM followed by single truncation" approach, with a theoretical analysis of when the proposed iterative truncation is necessary.
2. Include details about the initialization method in the experimental section.
3. Cite Langford et al. (2009) on truncated gradient methods in the related work section.
4. Clarify the intuition for why the proposed method is necessary for linear regression (the counterexample mentioned in the key points), as this is a crucial point for the paper's contribution.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes an important theoretical contribution to high-dimensional statistics. While the necessity of the iterative truncation step could be more convincingly established, the paper provides valuable theoretical guarantees that advance our understanding of EM algorithms in high dimensions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points above.
