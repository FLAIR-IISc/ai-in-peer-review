

This paper proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm for latent variable models that incorporates sparsity through a truncation step, establishing both computational convergence guarantees and statistical optimality. The authors extend previous low-dimensional EM analysis [2, 30] to high-dimensional settings, where they establish geometric convergence rates for the proposed algorithm and develop a decorrelated score test for asymptotic inference. The work builds upon and significantly extends the framework of Ning & Liu [27] for high-dimensional inference, while addressing the unique challenges of latent variable models where global optima are computationally infeasible.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm with sparsity enforcement, (2) theoretical guarantees for both parameter estimation and asymptotic inference in high dimensions, and (3) the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The theoretical results are impressive, establishing near-minimax optimal statistical rates and semiparametrically efficient inference.

However, several critical issues need clarification before the paper can be accepted. First, the paper fails to specify practical conditions for obtaining the initial estimator β^init that satisfies the required initialization condition (line 60, p.2). The authors should provide concrete guidance on how to construct such an initial estimator for specific models. Second, the paper does not offer practical guidance on when to use the exact maximization versus gradient ascent implementations of the M-step. Third, the selection of tuning parameters ĥs and λ lacks justification; the authors should provide sensitivity analysis and data-dependent selection methods.

The decorrelated score test is essentially a direct application of Ning & Liu's method [27], but the paper does not explicitly acknowledge this or clarify whether any new development was needed to apply it to latent variable models. The technical assumptions (Conditions 3.1-3.3) require more careful justification for the specific latent variable models considered, particularly regarding whether they hold in practice.

While the theoretical framework is compelling, the paper's main contribution appears to be the extension of existing high-dimensional inference tools to latent variable models, with the key technical novelty being the incorporation of the truncation step in the EM algorithm. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of why applying Ning & Liu's framework to latent variable models is nontrivial, and from expanding the main text to include more technical details rather than relegating them to the supplement.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with significant potential impact, but it requires substantial clarification of practical implementation details and more precise attribution of prior work before it can be accepted. The significance is high for practitioners working with high-dimensional latent variable models, but the current presentation does not fully demonstrate the novelty relative to existing high-dimensional inference methods.
