- Proposes a sparse EM algorithm that adds a hard-thresholding step per E/M iteration and proves geometric convergence up to a statistical error scaling with sqrt(s*)·O(sqrt(log d / n)).  
- Introduces a decorrelated score test for a low-dimensional component that is asymptotically normal and attains the semiparametric information bound despite high-dimensional nuisances.  
- Framework covers both exact and gradient-based M-steps and is illustrated for Gaussian mixture models and mixtures of regressions.  
- Builds on Balakrishnan et al. (2017) by adapting population-to-sample EM analysis to the high-dimensional regime via iterative truncation and entrywise concentration.  
- Connects to desparsification/debiasing methods using a Dantzig-selector-based decorrelation step, extending them to nonconvex latent-variable models.  
- Novel technical contribution: l_infty control of the sample-population M-step discrepancy restricted to sparse vectors.  
- Clear decomposition of error into optimization (geometric decay) and statistical (near-minimax) components; assumptions are explicit and verified for two models.  
- First general high-dimensional convergence and inference guarantees for EM in latent-variable models—original and potentially influential.  
- The asymptotic normality result achieves semiparametric efficiency.  
- Raises question about necessity of per-iteration truncation: could one-shot post-EM truncation suffice in many models?  
- Requests clarification/justification for Condition 3.3: when is sparse-input l_infty control strictly easier than uniform control and does it force truncation?  
- Main finite-sample analysis uses a resampling EM variant; need discussion of whether single-sample-path guarantees hold and what dependence issues arise.  
- Initialization requirement: need experimental details, success probability, sensitivity analyses, and practical strategies to find basin-of-attraction starts.  
- Inference relies on sparsity of auxiliary vector w*; quantify when this holds, effects of violations on power/validity, and computational/tuning costs for the Dantzig step.  
- Experiments should disclose initialization, batch sizes, step sizes, stopping criteria, and whether exact vs gradient M-step was used.  
- Recommend empirical comparisons to baselines: (i) EM + final truncation, (ii) l1-penalized EM/prox-EM, and (iii) thresholded gradient EM, plus ablations on truncation frequency.  
- Suggest positioning truncation relative to prior sparse/truncated optimization (e.g., Langford et al. 2009) and iterative hard-thresholding literature.  
- Ask for an explicit example (or sketch) where one-shot truncation fails (authors claim linear regression is such a case).  
- Pros for acceptance: strong general theory, near-optimal rates, semiparametric efficiency, and coverage of important models and M-step types.  
- Cons for acceptance: unclear necessity/advantage of iterative truncation, reliance on resampling analysis, and insufficient practical guidance on initialization and inference tuning.  
- Overall recommendation: leaning positive contingent on clarifying truncation necessity, strengthening empirical comparisons, and expanding initialization/inference practicalities.
