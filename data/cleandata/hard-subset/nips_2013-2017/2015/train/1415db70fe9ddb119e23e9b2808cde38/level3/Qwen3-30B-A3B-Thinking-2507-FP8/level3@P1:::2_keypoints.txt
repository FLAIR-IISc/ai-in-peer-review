- Proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step to enforce sparsity.  
- Two main contributions: (1) algorithm with geometric convergence to an estimator with rate O(sqrt(s* log d / n)), and (2) a decorrelated score statistic for low-dimensional hypothesis testing with asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency.  
- Extends recent low-dimensional EM guarantees (e.g., [2]) to high-dimensional settings (d > n).  
- Claims to be the first to provide statistical guarantees for high-dimensional EM.  
- Distinguishes itself from tensor-based methods (which have suboptimal rates) and from post-selection inference approaches that don’t apply to latent variable models.  
- Provides concrete verification of theoretical conditions for Gaussian mixture and mixture-of-regression models.  
- Theoretical results are comprehensive, giving simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees and proving geometric convergence under suitable initialization.  
- Strengths: strong technical depth, comprehensive theoretical framework, clear presentation of main contributions, and well-organized related work.  
- Presentation weakness: Sections 3–4 are extremely dense and difficult to follow due to many technical conditions and assumptions.  
- Lacks intuitive explanation and motivation for the truncation step (Algorithm 1, line 6) and unclear what happens if it is omitted.  
- Claims minimax-optimality of the O(sqrt(s* log d / n)) rate but provides no proof in the manuscript and cites a reference that may not directly establish it.  
- Practical implementation concern: sparsity parameter depends on unknown true sparsity s*, with no guidance for selection in practice.  
- Practical implementation concern: the required initialization (||β^init − β*||2 < R/2) is theoretically assumed but no practical method is given to obtain it.  
- No empirical evidence or practical guidance for critical implementation details like parameter selection and initialization.  
- Practical relevance to machine learning may be limited because the method requires knowledge of true sparsity and good initialization, often unavailable in practice.  
- Overall assessment: technically strong and original theoretical contribution to statistics, but dense presentation, missing minimax proof, and substantial practical gaps reduce its suitability for NIPS without significant revisions.
