

This paper proposes a sparse EM algorithm for high-dimensional latent variable models, introducing a truncation step at each iteration to enforce sparsity and establishing convergence guarantees and a decorrelated score statistic for inference. The work builds on prior EM convergence results [2, 30] but extends them to high-dimensional settings where dimension d exceeds sample size n, which has been largely unaddressed in the literature.

The main strengths are significant: the authors establish geometric convergence of the sparse-EM estimator with a near-optimal statistical rate of O(√(s* log d/n)), where s* is the true sparsity level. The decorrelated score statistic achieves asymptotic normality with optimal semiparametric variance, which is particularly valuable for inference in high-dimensional settings. The framework applies broadly to models like Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions, providing the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and inference in these high-dimensional contexts.

However, several concerns weaken the contribution. The necessity of iterative truncation is questionable, as the authors acknowledge that for many models (e.g., Gaussian mixtures), estimating M at all vectors is as easy as at sparse vectors. The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why ordinary EM followed by a single truncation wouldn't suffice (though the authors later argue linear regression is a counterexample). The assumption of smoothness and near-quadratic Q-function in a basin of attraction is restrictive, and the infinity-norm control on M-step error for sparse vectors appears to be a technical artifact rather than a fundamental requirement. The experimental section omits initialization details, making reproducibility difficult. The paper should also cite related sparse optimization work (e.g., Langford et al. 2009 on truncated gradient).

Overall, while the theoretical contributions are valuable for high-dimensional EM theory, the specific advantages of the proposed sparse EM variant remain uncertain. The paper makes an important step toward high-dimensional EM analysis but doesn't fully establish the necessity of its proposed methodology. The work is technically sound (Quality: Strong) but could be clearer about the limitations and necessity of its approach (Clarity: Moderate). The problem is novel (Originality: Strong) and addresses an important gap, though the significance is somewhat diminished by the unresolved necessity question (Significance: Moderate).
