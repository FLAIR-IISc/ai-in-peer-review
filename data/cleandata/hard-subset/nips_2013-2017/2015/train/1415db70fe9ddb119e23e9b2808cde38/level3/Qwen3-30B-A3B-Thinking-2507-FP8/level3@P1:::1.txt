

This paper makes a significant contribution by developing a general theoretical framework for high-dimensional latent variable models using the EM algorithm, addressing a critical gap in the literature. The authors propose a high-dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step to enforce sparsity, establishing geometric convergence to an estimator achieving near-optimal statistical rates. They further develop a decorrelated score test for inference on low-dimensional components, establishing asymptotic normality with optimal limiting variance.

The work builds upon recent advances in low-dimensional EM analysis by [2, 18, 25, 30], extending them to high-dimensional settings where traditional approaches fail. Unlike [2] which establishes p(d/n) rates for low-dimensional regimes, the authors achieve the sharper p(s* log d/n) rate by incorporating sparsity via truncation. This is a substantial theoretical advance, as the paper provides the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and inference in high dimensions for latent variable models.

Strengths include the elegant theoretical framework that unifies parameter estimation and inference, the clear decomposition of estimation error into optimization and statistical components (equation 1.1), and the rigorous proof of asymptotic normality for the decorrelated score statistic. The application to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models demonstrates the framework's generality and practical relevance.

However, several concerns require clarification. First, the paper lacks practical guidance for obtaining the required initialization β^{init} satisfying ||β^{init} - β^*||_2 / ||β^*||_2 ≤ ε. This is crucial for implementation, as poor initialization can prevent convergence. The authors should provide concrete initialization strategies (e.g., thresholded MLE, spectral methods) that satisfy this condition for their models.

Second, the paper does not adequately address when to use the exact maximization (Algorithm 2) versus gradient ascent (Algorithm 3) implementations of the M-step. The authors should provide computational complexity comparisons and practical guidelines based on problem size, sparsity, and available computational resources.

Third, the tuning parameters bs (sparsity level) and λ (Dantzig selector parameter) lack practical selection methods. The paper should discuss sensitivity to these parameters and propose data-dependent selection procedures (e.g., cross-validation, BIC-like criteria) to make the approach more implementable.

Fourth, while the decorrelated score test resembles Ning & Liu's method, the paper does not explicitly acknowledge this connection or clarify what novel developments were needed to apply it to latent variable models. The authors should explicitly cite Ning & Liu (2014) and explain the non-trivial adaptations required for their framework, particularly regarding the handling of latent structures and non-convex optimization.

Fifth, the numerous technical assumptions (Conditions 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) require more justification. While the paper verifies them for specific models in the supplement, the main text should provide clearer evidence that these hold for Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models in realistic scenarios. For instance, the condition that w* = I(β^*)^{-1}_{·,·} I(β^*)_{·,α} is sparse (implied by Assumption 4.5) needs more justification for the considered models.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is non-trivial: applying decorrelated inference to EM-based estimators for latent variable models is significantly more challenging than for convex problems due to the non-convexity of the likelihood and the lack of global optimality guarantees. The authors' key innovation is the truncation step that enforces sparsity along the solution path, which requires novel statistical and computational analysis to handle the evolving support sets.

The paper would be substantially strengthened by moving critical technical details from the supplement to the main text. The current reliance on a lengthy supplement makes it difficult to assess the completeness of the theoretical framework. For example, the proof sketch for Theorem 3.4 and the verification of assumptions for specific models should be included in the main paper.

In conclusion, this is an important theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of high-dimensional EM algorithms and inference for latent variable models. The paper makes a compelling case for the theoretical novelty and significance of the work. However, to maximize its practical impact, the authors should address the implementation concerns regarding initialization, algorithm selection, tuning parameter choice, and technical assumptions. With these clarifications, this paper would represent a major advance in the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the implementation concerns.
