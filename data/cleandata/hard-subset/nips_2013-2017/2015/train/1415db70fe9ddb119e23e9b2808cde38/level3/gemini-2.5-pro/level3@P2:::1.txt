### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a general theoretical framework for parameter estimation and statistical inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm which incorporates a hard-truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. They prove that, given a suitable initialization within a basin of attraction, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to a solution that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`. Second, based on the estimator produced by their algorithm, they construct a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing on individual components of the high-dimensional parameter. They establish the asymptotic normality of this statistic, showing that it achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound. The proposed framework is general and its application is illustrated for high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models. This work extends prior analyses of the EM algorithm, such as Balakrishnan et al. (2017), from the low-dimensional to the challenging high-dimensional setting.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a highly significant and challenging problem. The EM algorithm is a workhorse for latent variable models, but its theoretical properties in the high-dimensional (`d >> n`) regime are poorly understood. This work provides a major step towards bridging the gap between this popular computational tool and rigorous statistical theory for high-dimensional estimation and inference. The development of a computationally feasible method with guarantees for both optimal estimation and valid inference is a substantial contribution.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm, which interleaves standard EM steps with a truncation operation, is a natural and novel approach for incorporating sparsity. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and technically deep, establishing both computational (geometric convergence) and statistical (near-optimal rates) guarantees for a non-convex, iterative procedure. Analyzing an estimator that is not necessarily a global optimum of a convex program and developing a valid inferential framework for it is a non-trivial technical achievement.

3.  **Broad Applicability:** The framework is presented in a general form, based on high-level conditions on the population and sample Q-functions. This suggests that the approach could be applicable to a wide range of high-dimensional latent variable models beyond the two examples provided, making the results broadly impactful.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Practical Guidance:** The paper, while technically strong, falls short on providing practical guidance for implementation, which limits its immediate utility.
    *   **Initialization:** The theoretical guarantees hinge on an initial estimator `θ^{init}` that is already within a certain radius of the true parameter `θ*`. This is a strong assumption. The paper provides no practical method for obtaining such an initializer. For the results to be compelling, some discussion of how this can be achieved (e.g., via a spectral method or another tractable procedure) is necessary.
    *   **Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm requires setting a sparsity level `bs`, and the inference procedure requires a tuning parameter `λ` for the Dantzig selector. The paper offers no guidance on how to choose these crucial parameters in practice (e.g., through cross-validation) or analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to their misspecification. A data-dependent selection procedure would make the method much more practical.
    *   **Algorithm Choice:** The authors present two versions of the M-step (exact maximization and gradient ascent) but do not discuss the practical trade-offs or provide recommendations on when to use one over the other.

2.  **Positioning with Respect to Prior Work:**
    *   The proposed decorrelated score test for inference is methodologically very similar to the debiasing/desparsifying techniques developed for high-dimensional linear and generalized linear models (e.g., Zhang & Zhang, 2014; van de Geer et al., 2014; Ning & Liu, 2017). The authors should explicitly acknowledge this connection, cite the relevant literature (e.g., Ning & Liu, "A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models," 2017), and more clearly articulate the novelty of their contribution. The main technical challenge here, which should be highlighted, is adapting these methods to an estimator that does not arise from a convex KKT condition, but rather from a complex iterative procedure. Clarifying why this adaptation is non-trivial would strengthen the paper's claims of originality.

3.  **Verification of Assumptions and Paper Organization:**
    *   The main theorems rely on a series of abstract technical conditions (e.g., Conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.4). The authors state that these conditions are verified for the specific models in the appendix. However, given their centrality to the paper's claims, the main text would be significantly strengthened by including more intuition or summary of the proofs for why these (potentially restrictive) assumptions hold for the latent variable models considered.
    *   The main paper is very dense and outsources a substantial amount of critical material—including the verification of assumptions—to the supplement. While page limits are a constraint, this makes the paper difficult to evaluate as a self-contained unit. Moving some of the key technical arguments or insights from the appendix into the main body would improve readability and clarity.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **Arguments for:** The paper provides a novel, computationally feasible, and theoretically rigorous framework for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models, a problem of central importance to the NIPS community. The results are strong and represent a significant advance over existing literature.
*   **Arguments against:** The paper has significant weaknesses in terms of practical guidance (initialization, tuning parameter selection), which may limit its impact. Furthermore, the clarity regarding its novel contributions, particularly in the inference part, could be improved, and the reliance on a long appendix for core technical verifications makes the main paper less convincing on its own.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to an important problem. The weaknesses, while notable, are largely related to clarity, practical considerations, and positioning relative to prior art. These seem addressable in a revision. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the points raised above to improve the paper's clarity, practical relevance, and impact.
