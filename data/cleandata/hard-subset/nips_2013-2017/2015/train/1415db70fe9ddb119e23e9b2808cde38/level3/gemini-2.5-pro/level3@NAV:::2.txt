This paper proposes a high-dimensional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for latent variable models where the parameter dimension `d` is much larger than the sample size `n`. The core technical innovation is the introduction of a truncation step within the EM iterations to enforce parameter sparsity. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing that their algorithm, with a suitable initialization, converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-optimal statistical rate of convergence. As a second major contribution, the paper develops a statistical inference framework based on a decorrelated score test to perform hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. This work significantly extends recent analyses of the EM algorithm in low-dimensional settings to the challenging high-dimensional, regularized regime, where most existing inference techniques are tailored for convex optimization problems.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its theoretical significance and originality. It provides a comprehensive and unified framework for both estimation and inference for a difficult class of non-convex problems, bridging an important gap between computational algorithms and statistical theory. The manuscript is well-organized and effectively contextualizes its contributions with a sufficient summary of related work.

The main weakness is the paper’s clarity. The theoretical exposition, particularly in Sections 3 and 4, is extremely dense and challenging to follow. This lack of accessibility could hinder its impact on a broader machine learning audience. Furthermore, several practical aspects of the proposed method are not fully addressed. This leads to the following questions for the authors:

1.  The paper claims the statistical error rate of sqrt(s * log(d/n)) is near minimax-optimal. A proof or clear reference for this claim is missing from the manuscript and should be included.
2.  The choice of the sparsity parameter `^s` in Eq. (3.8) depends on the true (and unknown) sparsity `s`. How should this crucial hyperparameter be selected in practice?
3.  The convergence guarantees depend on an initial estimator `beta^init` being within a certain radius of the true parameter. Could the authors discuss practical methods for obtaining such an initialization?
4.  What is the precise role of the truncation step in Algorithm 1? A discussion of the algorithm's behavior if this step were omitted would better justify its necessity.

In conclusion, this paper presents a solid and valuable contribution to high-dimensional statistics. However, its dense presentation and unaddressed questions regarding practical implementation currently limit its direct applicability. Clarification on these points would substantially improve the paper’s quality and potential impact.
