## Review of “High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality”

This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors propose a modified EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, and establish statistical and computational guarantees, including a near-optimal convergence rate and asymptotic normality for hypothesis testing. This work builds upon existing literature on EM convergence (e.g., [2, 25, 30]) which primarily focuses on low-dimensional settings, and attempts to bridge the gap between computation and statistical optimality in high dimensions.

The strengths of the paper lie in its ambitious goal and the attempt to provide a comprehensive theoretical framework. The proposed truncation step is a reasonable approach to address the challenges of high dimensionality, and the authors demonstrate a strong technical capability in deriving convergence rates and establishing asymptotic normality. The consideration of both maximization and gradient ascent implementations of the M-step adds to the flexibility of the proposed framework. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the practical guidance for implementing the algorithm is limited. While the paper mentions initialization requirements (line 60, p.2), it lacks a detailed discussion of how to obtain a suitable initial estimator β<sup>init</sup> in practice. Similarly, the choice of tuning parameters ĥs and λ is not adequately addressed, and a sensitivity analysis is missing. Data-dependent selection methods would be highly valuable.

Second, the connection to existing work, particularly Ning & Liu [*], is not clearly established. The authors mention the decorrelated score test matches their method but fail to explicitly cite it and detail any novel developments required for its application in this context. 

Third, the numerous technical assumptions required for the main theorems raise concerns about the applicability of the framework to realistic latent variable models. A thorough discussion of whether these assumptions hold for the considered models (Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression) is needed.

Finally, the paper relies heavily on a long supplement for technical details and proofs. Key insights and derivations should be included in the main paper to improve readability and allow for a more complete understanding of the contributions. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper addresses an important open problem in statistical inference for latent variable models and presents a novel algorithmic approach with theoretical guarantees.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of practical guidance, insufficient discussion of existing work, potentially restrictive assumptions, and excessive reliance on supplementary material.

**Overall Assessment:** While the paper presents a significant theoretical contribution, the lack of practical guidance and clarity regarding existing work limit its immediate impact. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to address the aforementioned weaknesses in a revised version. The authors should prioritize providing practical implementation details, clarifying the novelty relative to Ning & Liu, and discussing the assumptions in the context of realistic models. Expanding the main paper with more technical details would also significantly improve its accessibility.
