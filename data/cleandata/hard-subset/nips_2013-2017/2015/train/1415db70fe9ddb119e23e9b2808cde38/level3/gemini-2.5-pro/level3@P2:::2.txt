### **Summary**

This paper presents a general theoretical framework for applying the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm to high-dimensional latent variable models where the number of parameters `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a novel "high-dimensional EM algorithm" that incorporates a truncation step after the standard E- and M-steps to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. They prove that under certain conditions, including a sufficiently good initialization, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-optimal statistical rate of convergence of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`, where `s` is the true sparsity of the parameter vector. Second, building on this estimator, the authors develop a decorrelated score test for performing hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. This procedure allows for statistical inference and uncertainty quantification. The paper's framework is contrasted with prior work on EM, such as that of Balakrishnan et al., which focused on the low-dimensional setting (`d << n`), and with high-dimensional inference techniques for convex models like the Lasso, noting that this work addresses the challenging non-convex optimization landscape of latent variable models.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a critical and challenging gap between the widespread use of the EM algorithm and the lack of theoretical guarantees in the high-dimensional setting. Providing a unified framework that establishes both computational guarantees (geometric convergence) and statistical guarantees (near-optimal estimation rates and asymptotic normality for inference) is a significant theoretical achievement. The modification of the EM algorithm with a truncation step is a simple yet novel idea to handle sparsity, and the development of an inferential framework for the resulting estimator is a valuable contribution.

2.  **Clarity of Organization and Literature Review:** The paper is well-organized, with a clear introduction that effectively situates the work within the existing literature. The authors do an excellent job of summarizing related work and precisely articulating how their contributions differ from and advance upon prior research in both low-dimensional EM analysis and high-dimensional inference.

3.  **Generality:** The proposed framework is presented in a general form and then applied to specific models like the Gaussian mixture model and mixture of regression model. This demonstrates the potential breadth of its applicability to a wide range of high-dimensional latent variable problems.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Density and Readability:** The main theoretical sections (Sections 3 and 4) are exceptionally dense and may be difficult to follow for readers not already deeply expert in this specific subfield. The analysis relies on a series of abstract technical conditions (e.g., Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness, Statistical-Error) that, while necessary for the proofs, make the main theorems challenging to parse and the underlying intuition hard to grasp. The paper would be significantly improved by providing more high-level intuition behind these conditions and the proof strategies.

2.  **Practical Concerns and Assumptions:** The practical applicability of the proposed method seems limited due to several strong assumptions that may be difficult to verify or satisfy in practice.
    *   **Initialization:** The theoretical guarantees hinge on obtaining an initial estimate `init` that is already close to the true parameter `*`, specifically `||init - *||_2 < R/2`. This is a common requirement in non-convex optimization theory, but the paper offers no practical guidance on how to find such an initialization for general latent variable models. The performance of the entire algorithm depends critically on this unresolved step.
    *   **Tuning Parameter Selection:** The choice of the sparsity parameter `bs` is crucial. According to Equation (3.8), the theoretical requirement for `bs` depends on the true (and thus unknown) sparsity `s*`. The paper does not discuss how `bs` should be selected in practice (e.g., via cross-validation), which is a major omission for practitioners wishing to implement the algorithm.

3.  **Unsupported Claim:** The abstract and introduction repeatedly claim that the `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)` statistical rate of convergence is "(near-)minimax-optimal." While this is likely true for this class of problems, no proof or specific citation is provided in the main text to substantiate this important claim.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a formal proof or a precise citation for the claim that the `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)` statistical rate is minimax-optimal for the high-dimensional latent variable models considered?
2.  How would you recommend a practitioner choose the sparsity parameter `bs`? The theoretical value in Eq. (3.8) depends on the unknown `s*`, making it unusable in practice.
3.  Could you elaborate on potential strategies for obtaining an initial estimate that satisfies the required initialization condition `||init - *||_2 < R/2`? Does this condition pose a significant practical barrier?
4.  To better understand the contribution of the T-step, what would happen if it were omitted from Algorithm 1? Would the algorithm still converge, perhaps to a dense solution with a suboptimal statistical rate (e.g., proportional to `sqrt(d/n)`)? Clarifying this would highlight the necessity of your proposed modification.

### **Overall Assessment**

This is a strong and technically deep paper that makes a solid contribution to the statistical machine learning literature. It provides the first, to my knowledge, comprehensive theoretical analysis of the EM algorithm for both estimation and inference in the high-dimensional regime. However, the presentation is very dense, which may limit its accessibility to the broader machine learning audience at NIPS. Furthermore, significant questions remain about the practical utility of the proposed algorithm due to its strong reliance on a good initialization and the lack of guidance on tuning parameter selection. While it is an impressive piece of theoretical work, its practical impact is less clear. It feels more like a statistics paper that would be a very strong fit for a top-tier statistics journal, but may be of limited interest to a large fraction of the NIPS community due to its theoretical density and questionable practical usefulness.

Despite these concerns, the theoretical novelty and significance of the results warrant its acceptance.
