This paper proposes a modified Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for high-dimensional latent variable models, aiming to address the lack of theoretical guarantees in the high-dimensional regime. The core contribution lies in a truncation step within the EM iteration, promoting sparsity and allowing for convergence rate analysis and asymptotic normality results. A novel statistical test framework for assessing estimator sparsity is also presented.

The work builds upon prior research addressing the limitations of the EM algorithm, particularly its tendency to converge to local optima [30, 25, 2]. It extends the guarantees established in the low-dimensional setting [2] to high dimensions by incorporating a sparsity-inducing truncation step. The paper also relates to the broader literature on high-dimensional inference, acknowledging connections to Lasso-type estimators and debiasing techniques [13, 26, 32], though emphasizing the differences arising from the latent variable setting. The approach differentiates itself from tensor methods [1, 9] by achieving sharper statistical rates.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-organized and presents a clear outline of the proposed method and theoretical results. The related work section is comprehensive, demonstrating awareness of relevant literature and positioning the contribution effectively. The authors attempt to provide a unified framework, applicable to various latent variable models, with detailed analysis for Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models. The derivation of the decorrelated score test is a valuable addition.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's density is a significant drawback. Sections 3 and 4, crucial for understanding the theoretical guarantees and inferential framework, are exceedingly technical and challenging to follow. Simplifying the notation and providing more intuitive explanations would greatly improve readability. The numerous technical conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) feel overwhelming without clear guidance on their practical implications.

**Questions/Concerns:**

*   **Minimax Optimality:** The claim of achieving a minimax-optimal rate of convergence (1.1) is made without a formal proof in the main manuscript. A clear proof or explicit citation demonstrating this optimality is essential. The reviewer’s request for this is valid.
*   **Practicality of ^s:** Equation (3.8) relies on an estimate of the true sparsity level, ^s, which is unknown in practice. The paper lacks discussion on how to choose ^s effectively in real-world applications. Heuristic strategies or adaptive methods for selecting ^s are needed.
*   **Initialization:** Obtaining an initial beta^init satisfying ||beta^init - beta*||_2 < R/2, as required for the convergence guarantee, seems challenging in practice. The paper should offer practical guidance on obtaining such an initialization or discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to the initial parameter setting.
*   **Truncation Step:** The role of the truncation step in Algorithm 1 remains somewhat unclear. What happens if this step is omitted? How does it impact convergence and the statistical properties of the estimator? A more detailed explanation and perhaps empirical results comparing the algorithm with and without truncation would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the statistics of high-dimensional latent variable models. However, its accessibility is hampered by its technical density and lack of practical guidance. While the results are mathematically rigorous, their relevance to machine learning practitioners is questionable. The requirement for a good initialization and the difficulty in choosing the sparsity parameter ^s limit its immediate applicability.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high dimensions.
*   Development of a new statistical test framework.
*   Potential for advancements in statistical modeling of complex data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Low readability due to technical density.
*   Lack of practical guidance for implementation.
*   Questionable immediate usefulness for machine learning applications.

**Verdict:** The reviewer's initial score of 6, raised to 7 after rebuttal, reflects this assessment. The paper is a valuable contribution to the statistical literature, but its limited accessibility and practical concerns prevent a stronger recommendation. A major revision addressing the clarity issues, providing practical guidance on parameter tuning, and offering a more detailed explanation of the truncation step would significantly improve the paper’s impact.
