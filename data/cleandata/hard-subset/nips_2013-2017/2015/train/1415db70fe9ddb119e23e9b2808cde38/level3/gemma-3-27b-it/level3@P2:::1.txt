This paper presents a theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, addressing a significant gap in existing literature which largely focuses on low-dimensional settings. The authors provide convergence guarantees for a modified EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, and develop a novel decorrelated score test for hypothesis testing regarding low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters. The work builds on prior results concerning EM convergence (e.g., Balakrishnan & Tseng, 2013; Tsybakov, 2018) and statistical inference after model selection (e.g., Bello et al., 2018), extending these to the high-dimensional regime.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The authors tackle a challenging problem – statistical and computational guarantees for EM in high dimensions – and make a concrete contribution with the proposed truncated EM algorithm. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, establishing convergence rates and asymptotic normality under clearly stated conditions. The connection to existing work is acknowledged, and the paper positions itself appropriately within the literature. The potential impact is significant, as a reliable and theoretically grounded EM algorithm for high-dimensional models could be broadly applicable.

However, the paper also suffers from several weaknesses. Primarily, a significant barrier to practical application is the lack of concrete guidance on parameter tuning and initialization. While the paper states an initialization requirement (β^{init} within a certain error bound of the true parameter), it doesn’t offer practical methods for *obtaining* such an initialization. This is a critical omission. Similarly, the choice of the sparsity parameter ĥs and the stepsize λ (in the gradient ascent implementation) are left unspecified, and the sensitivity analysis is missing. Data-dependent selection methods for these parameters would greatly enhance the paper’s practical relevance.

Regarding the decorrelated score test, the authors acknowledge its similarity to the method presented in Ning & Liu (2017) but don’t adequately detail *what* new development was needed to apply it within their framework. A clearer explanation of the non-triviality of extending that work here is required. A simple citation isn't sufficient; detailing the specific challenges and how they were addressed is crucial.

The extensive list of technical conditions is another concern. The paper doesn’t thoroughly discuss whether these conditions are likely to hold for the considered latent variable models (Gaussian Mixture and Mixture of Regression). A more detailed justification for the applicability of these conditions to these models is needed. The complexity of these conditions also makes the results less accessible.

Finally, the heavy reliance on a lengthy supplement is a drawback. Key details regarding proofs and specific model applications are relegated to the supplement, making it difficult to fully assess the contribution without significant effort. Expanding the main paper to include more of this critical information would substantially improve its clarity and impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding high-dimensional EM.
*   Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithm.
*   Offers a novel decorrelated score test for hypothesis testing.
*   Potential for broad applicability across latent variable models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of practical guidance on parameter tuning and initialization.
*   Insufficient explanation of the novelty relative to existing work (Ning & Liu, 2017).
*   Limited discussion of the validity of technical assumptions for specific models.
*   Over-reliance on supplementary material.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of the EM algorithm in high dimensions. However, its practical relevance is limited by the lack of guidance on parameter selection and initialization. Addressing these weaknesses, particularly by providing concrete recommendations for parameter tuning and a more detailed explanation of the connection to existing work, would significantly strengthen the paper. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the paper currently falls short of being truly impactful due to these practical limitations.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. I encourage the authors to address the points raised above and resubmit.
