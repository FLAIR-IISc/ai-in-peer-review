This paper presents a theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in the high-dimensional setting where the number of parameters `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The primary contributions are twofold. First, the authors propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the standard EM procedure by adding a hard-thresholding (truncation) step after each M-step to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. For this algorithm, they prove that with a suitable initialization, the iterates converge geometrically to a solution that achieves the near-minimax optimal statistical rate of convergence. Second, the paper develops a method for statistical inference by introducing a decorrelated score statistic. This statistic is shown to be asymptotically normal, enabling hypothesis testing and uncertainty quantification for individual components of the high-dimensional parameter vector.

The work builds upon recent advances in understanding the non-asymptotic behavior of the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional setting (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017), extending the analysis to the challenging sparse, high-dimensional regime. The inference component connects to the broader literature on debiasing and desparsification for high-dimensional models, adapting these ideas to the non-convex, latent variable context where the estimator is not the solution to a simple convex program.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance:** This paper addresses a critical and long-standing gap in the literature. The EM algorithm is a workhorse for latent variable models, yet its theoretical guarantees in the modern `d >> n` setting have been lacking. Providing a computationally feasible algorithm with rigorous guarantees for both optimal estimation and asymptotic inference is a major contribution.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and appears sound. The paper establishes a geometric rate of convergence for the optimization error while controlling the statistical error, ultimately showing that the final estimator achieves a near-optimal `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)` statistical rate. The derivation of an asymptotically normal score statistic is a non-trivial extension that significantly increases the paper's impact beyond point estimation.
3.  **Generality:** The framework is presented in a general form with a set of verifiable conditions, which are then shown to hold for important models like Gaussian mixture models and mixtures of regressions. This suggests the approach could be applied to a broad class of high-dimensional latent variable models.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While the paper's contributions are significant, the motivation for the specific algorithmic design and some aspects of the analysis could be clarified and strengthened.

1.  **Necessity of Iterative Truncation:** The central modification proposed is the introduction of a truncation step *within every iteration* of the EM algorithm. The justification for this specific design is not fully convincing. The key technical assumption enabling the analysis (Condition 3.3) requires control over the infinity-norm error of the M-step operator, `||M(θ) - Mn(θ)||∞`, but only for *sparse* input vectors `θ`. However, for the canonical examples provided (Gaussian mixtures, mixture of regressions), the sample M-step operator `Mn(θ)` is no harder to compute or analyze for a dense `θ` than for a sparse one. This raises the question of whether this assumption is a proof convenience or a fundamental requirement. If the analysis could be extended to handle dense inputs to the M-operator, it might be possible to analyze a much simpler algorithm.

2.  **Comparison to a Simpler Baseline:** A natural and simpler alternative to the proposed algorithm would be to run the standard EM algorithm to convergence (or for a fixed number of steps) and apply a single hard-thresholding step to the final parameter estimate. It is not clear from the paper why the proposed iterative truncation is necessary or superior to this "EM-then-truncate" baseline. A discussion of this alternative, potentially with a theoretical argument or a counterexample (e.g., showing how standard EM iterates could diverge or fail to maintain a good support set), would greatly strengthen the motivation for the proposed T-step.

3.  **Relation to Existing Sparse Optimization Methods:** The proposed algorithm, which interleaves a mapping (the EM update) with a hard-thresholding projection, is conceptually very similar to the family of Iterative Hard Thresholding (IHT) and Truncated Gradient methods. It would be beneficial to place the work in this broader context by citing and briefly discussing related literature, such as Langford et al. (2009) on truncated gradients.

4.  **Details on Initialization:** The theoretical guarantees rely on an initialization `θ^(0)` that lies within a "basin of attraction" of the true parameter. This is a standard assumption for non-convex problems, but its practical implication is crucial. For the results to be fully appreciated, the experimental section should explicitly state the initialization method used and provide some justification for why it is expected to satisfy the theoretical requirements. This detail is essential for reproducibility and for assessing the practical applicability of the algorithm.

### Overall Evaluation

This is a strong and important paper that provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for using the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The joint establishment of estimation- and inference-optimality for a computationally tractable algorithm is a significant achievement. The main weakness is in the justification for the specific algorithmic choice of iterative truncation over simpler alternatives. Addressing the points above, particularly by clarifying the necessity of the per-iteration T-step, would substantially improve the paper. Despite these questions, the paper's contributions are novel and significant, and it represents a clear advance in the field. I recommend acceptance.
