Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a general theory for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models. It makes two main contributions. First, it proposes a “sparse EM” procedure that augments the classical E- and M-steps with a truncation step (T-step), which keeps only the top-b_s coordinates in magnitude at every iteration. Under smoothness/strong-concavity and Lipschitz conditions for the population Q-function within a basin of attraction, and with an initialization inside that basin, the iterates contract geometrically toward the truth up to an irreducible statistical error. The final estimator achieves a near-minimax rate: roughly sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (up to model-dependent factors). The theory covers both exact maximization and gradient ascent implementations of the M-step.

Second, building on the sparse-EM estimate, the paper proposes a decorrelated (debiased) score statistic for testing a low-dimensional component (e.g., a single coordinate) of a high-dimensional parameter in latent variable models. Using a Dantzig-type estimator to construct a “decorrelating” vector, the resulting score is asymptotically normal with variance attaining the semiparametric information bound, enabling optimal tests and confidence intervals. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Relation to prior work: The analysis extends recent nonasymptotic EM theory in the low-dimensional regime (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) by incorporating sparsity and delivering high-dimensional rates. On the inference side, it connects to de-biasing/desparsification ideas for GLMs and linear models (e.g., Zhang & Zhang, 2014; van de Geer et al., 2014; Javanmard & Montanari, 2014), but adapts them to nonconvex latent-variable likelihoods via population Q-function identities. The truncation step is conceptually related to hard-thresholding methods (e.g., iterative hard thresholding) and truncated gradient for inducing sparsity in iterative procedures (Langford, Li & Zhang, ICML 2009). Compared to tensor methods for latent variable models (e.g., Anandkumar et al., 2014), the proposed approach achieves sharper rates and covers genuinely high-dimensional settings.

Strengths
- General and technically careful framework: simultaneous computational (geometric contraction) and statistical (near-optimal rates) guarantees under clearly stated conditions for both exact- and gradient-EM variants.
- New entrywise/statistical error control: the analysis hinges on componentwise concentration of the sample M-operator, tailored to sparse inputs, which is more refined than prior L2-based analyses.
- Asymptotically optimal inference: the decorrelated score achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound for low-dimensional targets in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, a nontrivial extension beyond convex models.
- Concrete model instantiations: Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions illustrate how the abstract conditions are verified, including SNR regimes and resulting rates.
- Closes a gap between computation and high-dimensional statistics for EM-type nonconvex problems.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Necessity and advantage of iterative truncation: It remains unclear when truncating at every step is essential versus running ordinary EM and truncating once at the end. The theory requires the T-step to keep iterates within the basin and to control support evolution, but practitioners would benefit from empirical and/or theoretical comparisons to “EM + one-shot hard thresholding.” The paper briefly suggests a counterexample; please provide a concrete one (ideally within the latent-variable scope), and include an empirical ablation (iterative vs. one-shot truncation).
- Statistical error condition and sparsity restriction: Condition 3.3 requires entrywise control of the sample M-operator but only for sparse inputs. For many models the cost of evaluating Mn does not depend on sparsity of its input, raising the question whether the truncation is analytically convenient but not strictly necessary. A discussion of whether similar rates can be obtained without enforcing sparsity along the path (and what breaks) would strengthen the contribution.
- Initialization: The guarantees hinge on being in a basin of attraction. Please (i) report exactly what initialization is used in experiments; (ii) discuss practically viable initializers (e.g., spectral/tensor, method-of-moments, random restarts) and their basin guarantees; and (iii) provide sensitivity analyses to initialization quality.
- Tuning parameters and robustness: Guidance for choosing b_s (when s* is unknown), step sizes, and the Dantzig tuning parameter is limited. Please add practical selection strategies (e.g., cross-validation, information criteria) and robustness experiments to mis-specified sparsity levels.
- Resampling in theory vs. practice: Some analyses use a resampling version of the algorithm. Clarify which results transfer to the standard single-sample-pass algorithm, and provide empirical evidence that the practical procedure behaves as predicted.
- Decorrelating vector sparsity: The inference relies on sparsity of w* (the solution of a Fisher-information linear system). Please discuss how plausible this is for the considered models, provide empirical estimates of s_w*, and consider alternative estimators (e.g., nodewise regressions) and their computational cost compared to the Dantzig selector.
- Assumption regimes: Guarantees for Gaussian mixtures require sufficiently large SNR. It would help to report empirical performance as SNR decreases and to delineate the boundary where the conditions likely fail.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with careful nonasymptotic reasoning and clear connections to population quantities via the Q-function identities. The assumptions are standard in spirit (local strong concavity/smoothness, Lipschitzness, initialization requirements), but the paper should better articulate when they hold and the practical cost of verifying them. Proofs hinge on fine-grained entrywise control, which is appropriate for high-dimensional rates.

Clarity
Generally clear but notation heavy. Suggestions:
- Summarize Conditions 3.1–3.3 and 4.1–4.4 in a compact table listing what concentrates at which norm and their typical orders for the two example models.
- State explicitly which results require resampling and which hold for the standard algorithm.
- In experiments, report the initialization, b_s choice, and all tuning parameters, and include ablation studies (with/without T-step, mis-specified b_s).

Originality
Original in combining EM analysis with sparsity-enforcing truncation and delivering semiparametrically efficient inference for latent variable models in high dimensions. The combination and the technical path (entrywise control of the M-operator and support evolution) are novel beyond prior low-dimensional EM theory and de-biasing work for convex models.

Significance
High: Provides a broadly applicable template for bringing both optimal estimation and valid inference to nonconvex latent-variable problems in the high-dimensional regime. Likely to influence follow-up work on nonconvex estimation with structure and high-dimensional inference beyond GLMs.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theoretical contributions with near-minimax rates and optimal inference.
- General framework covering exact and gradient EM.
- Clear advances over prior EM analyses confined to low dimensions.
- Relevance to many latent-variable models of interest to the community.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical necessity/benefit of iterative truncation insufficiently justified; missing ablations and counterexamples.
- Heavy reliance on initialization and local conditions with limited practical guidance.
- Some theory uses resampling; practical parity needs clarification.
- Limited experimental details on tuning and robustness.

Suggestions and requested revisions
- Add a baseline: ordinary EM followed by one-shot truncation, and iterative hard-thresholding without the EM structure; compare accuracy, support recovery, and runtime.
- Provide a concrete counterexample where one-shot truncation fails but iterative truncation succeeds, preferably within a latent-variable setting.
- Cite and discuss connections to truncated gradient (Langford, Li & Zhang, ICML 2009) and iterative hard-thresholding lines of work.
- Report initialization strategies and sensitivity; justify basin-of-attraction sizes for the instantiated models.
- Expand on practical guidelines for b_s and the Dantzig tuning parameter; consider nodewise alternatives.
- Clarify which results require resampling; if possible, include statements and/or experiments for the standard algorithm.

Overall
An important and timely contribution with solid analysis and impactful implications for high-dimensional latent-variable estimation and inference. With clarifications and stronger empirical support regarding the necessity and benefits of iterative truncation and practical choices, the paper would be a strong addition to NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
