This paper develops a high-dimensional theory for EM in latent-variable models, comprising (i) a sparse EM algorithm that appends a truncation step (T-step) to each E/M iteration to enforce sparsity, and (ii) an inferential procedure based on a decorrelated score for low-dimensional components. The authors show that, with an initialization inside a basin of attraction and under smoothness/concavity conditions on the population Q-function, the iterates converge geometrically to a neighborhood of the truth; the residual error is statistical and scales as sqrt(s*) times an entrywise (ℓ∞/ℓ1-type) error in the empirical M-operator, which concentrates at roughly sqrt(log d / n). Hence the final estimator achieves (near-)minimax rate sqrt(s* log d / n) up to log factors. For inference, they construct a Dantzig-type decorrelating vector from the empirical counterpart of the Fisher information derived from Q, leading to a score statistic that is asymptotically normal with variance attaining the semiparametric efficiency bound, despite high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The framework covers exact and gradient M-steps and is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Relation to prior work: The analysis builds on population-to-sample EM contraction ideas (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.), but extends to the high-dimensional regime via a truncation step and new entrywise concentration for the M-operator. For inference, it adapts/develops decorrelated/debiased techniques (van de Geer et al.; Javanmard & Montanari; Zhang & Zhang) to latent-variable settings where global convex optimization is unavailable. Compared to tensor methods for mixtures, the proposed approach achieves sharper rates and broader applicability. The truncation mechanism is related in spirit to truncated gradient and hard-thresholding methods in sparse optimization (e.g., Langford et al., IHT/HTP), though here it is coupled to EM’s Q-function.

Strengths:
- Addresses a central gap between computational local optima (EM) and statistical guarantees in high-dimensional latent-variable models.
- Clear separation of optimization error (geometric decay) and statistical error (entrywise concentration), yielding near-optimal estimation rates.
- General framework that handles exact and approximate M-steps; model-specific verifications provided.
- Inferential component is strong: decorrelated score achieves semiparametric efficiency for low-dimensional targets with high-dimensional nuisance.
- Technical novelty: ℓ∞/ℓ1 control of the empirical M-operator restricted to sparse inputs and careful analysis of support evolution under truncation.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Necessity of truncation at every iteration is not fully clarified. For many models, evaluating Mn(·) at dense vs. sparse inputs may have similar cost; could ordinary EM followed by a single truncation (or early stopping + truncation) achieve similar rates? The authors mention linear regression as a counterexample; it would help to formalize this (theoretically or empirically) in latent-variable settings.
- Initialization within the basin of attraction is required but practical strategies are not discussed in depth; experiments should report and justify initialization schemes.
- The theory uses a resampling version of the algorithm to simplify dependence across iterations; discuss how guarantees translate to the single-sample setting and any price paid.
- The sparsity assumption on the decorrelating vector w* (support size s*w) is critical for inference. Provide intuition and empirical evidence that it holds in the considered models; discuss diagnostics when it fails.
- Tuning: guidance for choosing bs, the Dantzig tolerance, and step sizes is limited; sensitivity analyses would be useful.
- Some rates include extra log factors (e.g., log n), and constants in the basin/contraction conditions may be conservative; comment on empirical tightness.

Quality: The analysis appears technically careful and sound, with well-motivated conditions (population strong concavity/smoothness in a basin; Lipschitz-type gradient conditions; entrywise concentration). The decorrelation argument is clean and connects Q-derived quantities to likelihood score/Fisher information. Model-specific verifications strengthen credibility.

Clarity: Generally well-written, though notation is heavy. Suggestions:
- Add more intuition about how the T-step affects support evolution and why entrywise error control is essential.
- Provide a concise “recipe” for practical deployment (initialization, bs selection, stopping, and tuning for the Dantzig step).
- Clarify the computational differences and pros/cons between exact and gradient M-steps.

Originality: High. Combining EM with iterative truncation and developing valid inference for latent-variable models in high dimensions is novel. The ℓ∞-focused statistical error control tailored to EM is a notable contribution.

Significance: High. The framework offers the first computationally feasible route to both optimal estimation and valid inference for a broad class of latent-variable models in high dimensions, likely to influence subsequent methodological and theoretical work.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, general theory with both estimation and inference.
- Near-optimal rates and semiparametric efficiency.
- Addresses an important and timely problem (high-dimensional latent models).
- Applicability to multiple models and M-step implementations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical aspects (initialization, tuning, necessity of per-iteration truncation) need clearer justification and empirical validation.
- Reliance on resampling for analysis; translation to single-sample setting could be elaborated.
- Assumptions on w* sparsity may be restrictive in some models.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include experiments comparing: (a) sparse-EM with per-iteration truncation, (b) standard EM + final truncation, and (c) ℓ1-penalized M-steps, under matched computational budgets.
- Report initialization methods used and sensitivity to initialization.
- Add citations to related sparse optimization with truncation (e.g., Langford et al. 2009) and hard-thresholding algorithms; discuss connections/differences.
- Provide practical heuristics for choosing bs and Dantzig tolerance; include ablation on these choices.
- Discuss conditions under which w* is sparse in the showcased models and provide empirical checks.

Overall: An important and well-executed contribution that advances both theory and practice of high-dimensional latent-variable estimation and inference. With added empirical clarification on the truncation design and initialization, it would be a strong NIPS paper.
