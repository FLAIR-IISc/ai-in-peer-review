This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in the high-dimensional setting where the number of parameters `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The primary contribution is a modified EM algorithm that incorporates a hard truncation step to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. The authors provide a rigorous analysis showing that, under certain conditions and with a suitable initialization, their algorithm converges at a geometric rate to a solution that achieves the near-minimax optimal statistical error rate. A second key contribution is the development of a statistical inference framework based on a "decorrelated score test," which allows for hypothesis testing and uncertainty quantification for individual parameters, a challenging task in high-dimensional non-convex models. This work builds upon recent advances in understanding the low-dimensional EM algorithm (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) and extends them to the high-dimensional sparse regime, a setting of great practical importance. The proposed inferential procedure also connects to the broader literature on high-dimensional inference for convex models (e.g., debiased Lasso) but uniquely adapts these ideas to the local optima found by the non-convex EM procedure.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a long-standing and important problem: the lack of theoretical guarantees for the widely used EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. By providing a unified framework for both optimal parameter estimation and asymptotic inference, the work makes a significant and original contribution. It establishes the first computationally feasible approach with strong theoretical backing for a broad class of latent variable models in this regime.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of theoretical work. The authors provide a detailed analysis with conditions guaranteeing geometric convergence of the algorithm and derive the statistical rate of convergence for the resulting estimator. The construction of the decorrelated score test for hypothesis testing is also a strong technical result, proving asymptotic normality and showing that its limiting variance achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively situates the work within the existing literature, highlighting the gap it aims to fill. The methodology is presented logically, and the separation of the theory into computation/estimation (Section 3) and inference (Section 4) makes the structure easy to follow. The summary of related work is sufficient and accurately positions the paper's contributions.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The main weakness of the paper is its density. While well-organized, the theoretical content in Sections 3 and 4 is very challenging to follow, particularly for readers who are not experts in high-dimensional statistics. The numerous conditions, assumptions, and theorems are presented in a very compact, notation-heavy format that obscures the underlying intuition. The paper would be significantly improved by adding more high-level explanations to simplify the core ideas for a broader machine learning audience. For example, a more intuitive discussion of why the truncation step is essential and how the `l_infinity`-norm error analysis (Condition 3.3) is key to handling it would be very beneficial.

2.  **Unsupported Claim of Optimality:** The authors repeatedly claim that the statistical rate `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)` is (near-)minimax-optimal. While this is a well-known result in high-dimensional statistics, the paper does not provide a proof or a specific citation to a theorem that establishes this lower bound for the class of latent variable models considered. For the sake of rigor, this claim should be formally substantiated, at least with a reference in the appendix.

3.  **Practicality Concerns:** The paper's focus is theoretical, but some practical aspects that are crucial for implementation are not fully addressed:
    *   **Choice of Sparsity Parameter `^s`:** Equation (3.8) specifies the choice of the sparsity parameter `^s`, but it depends on the unknown true sparsity `s*`. How should a practitioner choose `^s` in a real-world application? The paper offers no guidance on this critical tuning parameter.
    *   **Initialization:** The theoretical guarantees rely on an initial parameter estimate `beta^init` that is already close to the true parameter `beta*` (i.e., `||beta^init - beta*||_2 < R/2`). This is a strong assumption. How can one obtain such an initialization in practice? Without a clear and feasible initialization procedure, the practical applicability of the proposed algorithm is questionable.

4.  **Algorithmic Justification:** The truncation step is the central innovation of the proposed algorithm. It would be very helpful if the authors could clarify its precise role. What would happen if this step were omitted? Would the standard EM algorithm with `l1`-regularization in the M-step work? A discussion of why the proposed hard-truncation approach is necessary or preferable would strengthen the paper's message.

In summary, this paper is a very solid and important contribution to statistical machine learning theory. It provides a rigorous and novel framework for estimation and inference using a modified EM algorithm in high dimensions. The technical depth and originality are major strengths. However, the dense presentation makes it inaccessible to a large part of the NIPS audience, and significant practical concerns regarding initialization and hyperparameter selection limit its immediate usefulness. Despite these weaknesses, the paper's theoretical significance is substantial, as it provides foundational guarantees for a fundamental algorithm in a challenging and highly relevant setting.
