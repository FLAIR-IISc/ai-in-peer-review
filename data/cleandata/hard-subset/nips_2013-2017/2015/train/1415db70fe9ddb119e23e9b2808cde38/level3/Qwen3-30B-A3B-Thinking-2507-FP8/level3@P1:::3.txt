

This paper presents a general theory of the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for high-dimensional latent variable models, making two significant contributions. First, it proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity via a truncation step in both E-step and M-step, achieving geometric convergence to an estimator with near-optimal statistical rate of convergence. Second, it develops a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference on low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters, establishing the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and inference in high dimensions. The authors relate their work to previous NIPS contributions: [2] established convergence rates for low-dimensional EM, while [30] provided general sufficient conditions for EM convergence. This work extends these by addressing high-dimensional settings where sparsity is critical.

The paper's main strength lies in its comprehensive theoretical framework that simultaneously establishes statistical and computational guarantees. The authors prove that with appropriate initialization, the proposed sparse EM algorithm converges geometrically to an estimator with error bounded by optimization error (decreasing geometrically) plus statistical error (scaling as √(s* log d/n), where s* is the true sparsity level). This statistical rate is near-minimax-optimal for high-dimensional settings. The decorrelated score statistic they propose is shown to be asymptotically normal with optimal limiting variance, achieving the semiparametric information bound. The theoretical development is thorough, with detailed conditions that are verified for specific models (Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression).

The paper's technical novelty is substantial. The authors introduce a new condition requiring ∞-norm control on the error in estimating the M operator, which is necessary only for sparse vectors. This addresses a key challenge in high-dimensional EM analysis. The truncation step, while adding computational complexity, is theoretically justified as necessary for handling the high-dimensional nuisance parameters that complicate inference. The authors effectively argue that standard EM followed by a single truncation would not suffice, as demonstrated by their counterexample with linear regression.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that warrant consideration. The necessity of the iterative truncation step is somewhat questionable, as the authors themselves note that for many models (e.g., Gaussian mixtures), estimating M at all vectors is as easy as at sparse vectors. This raises the question of whether ordinary EM followed by a single truncation could achieve similar guarantees. The authors later argue linear regression is a counterexample, but this claim needs stronger justification. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm versus simpler alternatives.

The experimental section is underdeveloped, with no clear description of the initialization method used. Given the critical dependence on initialization within a basin of attraction, this omission is problematic. The authors should report what initialization was used and how it satisfied the required conditions.

The paper could also strengthen its connection to related work in sparse optimization. While the authors cite [2] and [30], they miss opportunities to reference key sparse optimization literature (e.g., Langford et al. 2009 on truncated gradient methods), which would better position their contribution within the broader sparse optimization literature.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important gap in high-dimensional latent variable modeling, providing the first unified framework for optimal estimation and inference. The results have clear potential applications in high-dimensional mixture models, which are increasingly important in modern data analysis. The theoretical guarantees are compelling, though the practical advantages of the specific sparse EM variant require further clarification.

The paper excels in technical quality, with rigorous proofs and comprehensive conditions that hold for a broad family of models. The writing is clear, though the theoretical sections could be more accessible to a wider audience. The main contributions are original, with clear differentiation from previous work on EM algorithms in low-dimensional settings.

In summary, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to high-dimensional latent variable modeling. The main strengths are its comprehensive convergence guarantees and the development of the decorrelated score statistic for inference. The main weaknesses are the questionable necessity of iterative truncation, the underdeveloped experimental section, and insufficient connection to related sparse optimization work. The paper is technically sound and makes an important contribution, but the specific advantages of the proposed sparse EM variant need stronger justification.

Pro: Comprehensive theoretical framework with simultaneous statistical and computational guarantees, near-optimal convergence rates, novel decorrelated score statistic for inference.
Con: Unclear necessity of iterative truncation, insufficient comparison with simpler alternatives, missing initialization details in experiments, underdeveloped connection to sparse optimization literature.

This paper advances the state of the art in high-dimensional latent variable modeling and should be accepted, with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses in the final version.
