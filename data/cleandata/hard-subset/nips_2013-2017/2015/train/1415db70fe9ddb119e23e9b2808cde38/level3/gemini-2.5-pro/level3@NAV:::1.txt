This paper presents a theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings, where the number of parameters `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors make two main contributions. First, they propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation step to enforce sparsity, proving that it converges at a geometric rate to an estimator with a near-optimal statistical rate of convergence. Second, they develop a decorrelated score test for this estimator, enabling valid hypothesis testing and uncertainty quantification for individual parameters. This work extends the theoretical analysis of the EM algorithm, such as that by Balakrishnan et al. (2017), to the challenging high-dimensional regime and adapts concepts from high-dimensional inference to the setting of a non-convex, iterative optimization procedure.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a crucial and long-standing problem at the intersection of computational methods and statistical theory. Providing rigorous guarantees for estimation and, importantly, inference for the widely-used EM algorithm in high dimensions is a significant contribution that could impact both researchers and practitioners.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical results are comprehensive and strong. The paper establishes both computational guarantees (geometric convergence) and statistical guarantees (near-minimax optimal rates) for estimation, as well as asymptotic normality for the proposed inferential procedure.
*   **Originality:** The proposed framework, which combines the analysis of the EM algorithm with iterative truncation and a subsequent decorrelation step for inference, is novel. Analyzing the properties of an estimator that is not a global optimum of a convex program is technically challenging and represents a valuable advance.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is theoretically strong, but its clarity and practical utility could be significantly improved by addressing the following points:

1.  **Practical Implementation:** The theory relies on several conditions and tuning parameters that lack practical guidance.
    *   The analysis assumes a suitable initial estimator `β^{init}`. Please clarify the conditions it must satisfy and suggest practical methods for obtaining it in the context of the models discussed.
    *   The paper proposes both maximization and gradient ascent M-steps. It would be very helpful to provide guidance on the trade-offs and when each algorithm is preferred in practice.
    *   Guidance on selecting the tuning parameters `bs` (sparsity level) and `λ` (Dantzig selector penalty) is needed. Discussing data-dependent selection methods and the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Context and Novelty:** The paper's contribution relative to existing work could be stated more clearly.
    *   The decorrelated score test is closely related to methods for M-estimators, such as in Ning & Liu (2017, JASA). The authors should explicitly cite this and other relevant work, and more clearly articulate the novel technical developments required to apply this inferential framework to the local solution of a non-convex algorithm like the proposed high-dimensional EM.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:**
    *   The main results depend on a number of high-level technical assumptions. While these are verified in the appendix, the main paper would benefit from a more intuitive discussion of why these assumptions are expected to hold for the latent variable models considered.
    *   Many key technical details and proof arguments are in the supplement. Moving some of this material, particularly proof sketches that highlight the main technical challenges (e.g., analyzing the iterative truncation), into the main paper would make it more self-contained and better showcase the technical depth of the contributions.
