This paper presents a theoretical analysis of a high-dimensional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors make two primary contributions: first, they propose a sparse EM algorithm that incorporates a hard-thresholding (truncation) step at each iteration to enforce sparsity, and prove that it converges geometrically to an estimator with a near-optimal statistical rate. Second, they introduce a decorrelated score statistic based on this estimator, enabling asymptotically normal hypothesis testing for low-dimensional components of the parameter vector. This work extends the theoretical understanding of EM, notably the low-dimensional analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017), to the challenging high-dimensional setting where the number of parameters far exceeds the sample size. The inference framework is also a novel contribution, adapting ideas from high-dimensional debiasing to the local optima of non-convex latent variable models.

The primary strength of this paper is its significant theoretical contribution. Establishing computational and statistical guarantees for an EM-type algorithm in the high-dimensional regime is an important and difficult problem. The authors provide a comprehensive framework that not only guarantees geometric convergence to a statistically near-optimal solution but also provides a computationally feasible method for valid inference. The analysis appears technically sound and addresses a critical gap between the widespread use of EM and its theoretical foundations in modern, high-dimensional settings.

However, the paper has weaknesses that temper its overall impact. The central motivation for the proposed algorithm—performing truncation at *every* iteration—is not sufficiently justified. It is unclear why this is superior to a simpler baseline, such as running a standard EM (perhaps with regularization) and performing a single truncation at the end. The authors should provide a stronger argument or a clear counterexample demonstrating the necessity of their iterative approach. Furthermore, the analysis relies on a new technical condition requiring infinity-norm control on the error of the M-step operator for sparse inputs. For many of the models discussed, such as Gaussian mixtures, computing this operator is no easier for sparse vectors than for dense ones, which questions the practical computational advantage of the proposed method. Finally, the connection to prior work could be strengthened; the truncation step is highly related to truncated gradient methods (e.g., Langford et al., 2009), which should be cited and discussed. On a minor note, the experiments should explicitly state the initialization procedure used, as the theoretical guarantees depend on it.

In summary, this paper provides valuable and novel theoretical guarantees for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The results are significant and advance the state of the art. However, the justification for the specific algorithmic choice of iterative truncation over simpler alternatives needs to be considerably strengthened to make a more compelling case for the proposed method's practical utility.
