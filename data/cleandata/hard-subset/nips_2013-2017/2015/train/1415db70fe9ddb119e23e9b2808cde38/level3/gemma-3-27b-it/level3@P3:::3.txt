This paper tackles the challenging problem of establishing theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors propose a sparse EM procedure incorporating truncation steps to enforce sparsity, and crucially, derive asymptotic normality results for parameter estimation, enabling hypothesis testing. This is a significant contribution, as existing theory largely breaks down when the number of parameters exceeds the sample size.

The paper builds upon prior work establishing convergence guarantees for the EM algorithm in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2013; Tsiatis et al., 2018). It correctly identifies the gap between computational feasibility (local optima found by EM) and statistical guarantees (typically requiring global optima). The work acknowledges and extends the view of EM as a perturbed gradient method, as in Tsiatis et al. (2013), but importantly, introduces a truncation step to address high dimensionality. The connection to sparse optimization, specifically truncated gradient methods (Langford et al., 2009), is appropriately noted.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a strong theoretical framework, establishing geometric convergence and statistical rates of convergence. The p√s log(d/n) rate is near-optimal, given the sparsity assumption.
* **Asymptotic Normality:** The derivation of an asymptotically normal decorrelated score statistic is a major achievement, opening the door to valid inference in high-dimensional settings. This is a significant advance over methods focusing solely on point estimation.
* **Generality:** The framework is presented as applicable to a broad family of latent variable models (illustrated with Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions).
* **Clear Identification of Challenges:** The authors clearly articulate the technical challenges arising from high dimensionality and sparsity, particularly the need for a novel statistical error condition for sparse vectors.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Necessity of Truncation:** While the paper demonstrates that the sparse EM *can* achieve good results, it doesn't definitively establish that truncation is *necessary*.  The authors acknowledge this and pose a valid question regarding whether a single truncation *after* ordinary EM would suffice, but the counterexample provided (linear regression) feels somewhat specific and doesn't entirely resolve the concern. A more extensive investigation into the conditions where truncation offers a clear advantage would strengthen the contribution.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The requirement for "appropriate initialization" within a basin of attraction is a common issue with EM, but it’s crucial to detail how this initialization is obtained in practice. The paper lacks sufficient detail about the initialization procedure used in the experiments.
* **Technical Conditions:**  While the conditions are clearly stated, they can feel somewhat opaque. More intuitive explanations of what these conditions entail for specific models would enhance understanding.
* **Constant Factors:** The constants hidden within the "O()" and "OP()" notation can significantly impact the practical implications of the results.  A more careful discussion of these constants, even if qualitative, would be helpful.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The work is complete in the sense that it provides a full theoretical treatment, but a more thorough empirical evaluation with a clearer discussion of initialization would be beneficial. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, which is commendable.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the mathematical notation is dense and may be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with high-dimensional statistics. Expanding on the intuition behind the technical conditions and providing more concrete examples would improve clarity.

**Originality:** The combination of establishing convergence guarantees *and* asymptotic normality for a sparse EM algorithm in high dimensions is novel.  The specific technical contributions, such as the refined statistical error condition, represent genuine advances.

**Significance:** The results are important for the field of high-dimensional statistics and latent variable modeling. The ability to perform valid inference in these settings is crucial for many applications. The work has the potential to influence future research in both theoretical and applied areas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Provides important theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in a challenging high-dimensional setting.
* Establishes asymptotic normality, enabling hypothesis testing.
* Presents a general framework applicable to a broad class of models.
* Rigorous mathematical treatment.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The necessity of the truncation step remains somewhat unclear.
* Limited discussion of initialization procedures.
* Technical conditions could be more intuitive.




**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. While the necessity of the truncation step is an open question, the demonstrated convergence and, crucially, the derivation of asymptotic normality results are significant achievements. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the comments regarding initialization and further clarify the advantages of their sparse EM approach over simpler truncation strategies.
