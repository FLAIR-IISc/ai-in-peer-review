## Review of High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality

This paper presents a theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models. The core contribution lies in a modified EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, coupled with a novel decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional parameters. The authors establish statistical rate of convergence and asymptotic normality, addressing a significant gap in the existing literature which largely focuses on low-dimensional scenarios.

The paper builds upon previous work establishing convergence guarantees for the EM algorithm, referencing [2, 18, 25, 30]. It correctly identifies the challenges posed by high dimensionality, where the maximum likelihood estimator is often ill-defined and existing statistical guarantees break down. The proposed truncation step addresses this by promoting sparsity, enabling the derivation of convergence rates and inferential procedures. The work also acknowledges and contrasts itself with alternative approaches like tensor methods [1, 9] and Lasso-based methods [15, 26, 32], highlighting the novelty of applying these tools within the EM framework for latent variable models.  The comparison to [2] is well-articulated, emphasizing the limitations of their low-dimensional results and the additional complexity introduced by sparsity enforcement.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a mathematically sound and comprehensive theoretical framework, with clearly stated conditions and theorems. The proofs appear rigorous, though deferred to the appendix.
* **Novel Contribution:** The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm with the truncation step is a valuable addition to the toolkit for handling latent variable models in high dimensions. The decorrelated score statistic provides a theoretically grounded approach to inference.
* **Addressing a Gap:** The work directly tackles a critical gap in the literature concerning the statistical properties of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings.
* **Generality:** The framework is presented as being applicable to a broad family of statistical models, demonstrated through applications to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Guidance:** The paper lacks sufficient practical guidance for implementation.  The choice of initialization, crucial for convergence (see line 60, p.2, requiring `init ⇤ /k ⇤k 2  `), is only mentioned in passing.  More detailed advice on obtaining such an initial estimator would be beneficial.  Similarly, the conditions for selecting the sparsity parameter `bs` and the stepsize `⌘` are complex and require further clarification for practical application.  A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these tuning parameters, and potentially data-dependent selection methods, is absent.
* **Comparison to Related Work:** While the paper acknowledges related work such as [2] and [15], a more detailed comparison, specifically addressing the non-trivial adaptations needed to apply those techniques within the EM framework, would strengthen the presentation.  Notably, the paper mentions the decorrelated score test is related to work by Ning & Liu [*], but doesn’t clearly state *what* development was needed to adapt that test to this specific context. An explicit citation and discussion of differences is crucial.
* **Technical Assumptions:** The numerous technical conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) are essential for the theoretical results, but their practical validity for the considered models, or other potential applications, isn’t thoroughly discussed. The authors mention verifying these conditions in the appendix, but a more intuitive explanation of when these conditions are likely to hold would be valuable.
* **Appendix Dependency:** Key details and proofs are relegated to a lengthy appendix. While this is common, it limits the accessibility of the paper. Expanding the main paper with more detailed explanations of the core ideas and potentially summarizing key proof steps would improve clarity.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in statistical modeling.
* The results fill a significant gap in the existing literature on the EM algorithm.
* The framework is general and applicable to a wide range of latent variable models.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of practical guidance and discussion of implementation details limits the immediate applicability of the work.
* The extensive list of technical assumptions and their verification require careful scrutiny.
* The reliance on a lengthy appendix hinders accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of latent variable modeling. However, addressing the weaknesses regarding practical guidance, clarifying the connection to existing work (specifically Ning & Liu), and improving accessibility would substantially strengthen the manuscript. I believe with revisions addressing these points, this paper is worthy of acceptance.  I recommend a **weak accept** contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised above, particularly providing more guidance on practical implementation and clarifying the novelty relative to existing methods.



**Confidence:** 7/10
