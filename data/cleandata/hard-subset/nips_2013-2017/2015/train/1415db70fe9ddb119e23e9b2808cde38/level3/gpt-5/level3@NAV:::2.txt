Summary: The paper proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm that augments the E/M steps with a truncation step to enforce sparsity, together with conditions under which the iterates contract geometrically and achieve a near-minimax estimation rate. A second contribution is an inferential framework based on a decorrelated score that yields asymptotically normal tests for low-dimensional components, enabling assessment of sparsity patterns. The work builds on the “from population to sample” EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al., extending it to the p >> n regime by controlling entrywise errors and support evolution, and connects to high-dimensional inference via Dantzig-type projections (van de Geer et al.; Zhang & Zhang). Compared to tensor and convex-penalized approaches, this yields sharper rates and inference without requiring a global optimum of a convex surrogate.

Quality: The theory appears careful and technically strong; the Lipschitz/concavity and statistical-error conditions are stated clearly and verified for GMM and mixture regression. However, two claims need clarification: (1) the manuscript occasionally suggests convergence to a “global solution,” but the main theorems establish geometric contraction within a basin toward a sparse fixed point with optimal statistical error; please clarify the scope and avoid implying global optimality. (2) The statement that the p(s log(d/n)) rate is minimax-optimal needs a precise lower bound or citation; I could not find a proof in the main text.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and related work is thoroughly summarized. That said, Sections 3–4 are dense. Please simplify by adding a high-level roadmap, isolating the role of each assumption, and providing an ablation/intuition for the truncation step (why hard-thresholding versus ℓ1-penalized M-step; what breaks without truncation?).

Originality: Combining EM with iterative truncation and providing semiparametric-efficient inference for latent-variable models in high dimensions is novel and distinct from prior EM analyses and from GLM-focused debiasing.

Significance: Statistically important; practical usefulness is less clear due to tuning and initialization requirements.

Specific questions/concerns:
- Minimax claim: Provide a clear proof or a precise citation for the lower bound matching √(s log d / n).
- Practical tuning: Eq. (3.8) uses ŝ that depends on unknown s*. How should ŝ be chosen in practice (e.g., path + BIC/stability selection, or adaptive thresholding with theoretically justified rules)?
- Initialization: How to obtain βinit with ||βinit − β*||2 < R/2? Please give concrete, model-specific procedures and sensitivity analyses.
- Truncation: What is the empirical/theoretical impact if the T-step is omitted? An ablation would help.

Pros:
- Strong theory with near-minimax rates and asymptotically optimal tests
- Clear relation to prior EM and high-dim inference
- General framework (exact and gradient M-steps)

Cons:
- Dense presentation in key sections
- Practical tuning/initialization unclear
- Accessibility to a general ML audience is limited

After rebuttal: Authors addressed several points; I raise my score from 6 to 7. Solid statistical contribution, though practicality and accessibility remain concerns.
