This paper develops a sparse variant of EM that appends a hard-thresholding step each iteration to enforce a target sparsity, and a decorrelated-score–based inferential procedure for testing whether a given coordinate is nonzero. Relative to prior population-to-sample EM analyses in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.), the theory is explicitly high-dimensional: with initialization inside a basin where the Q-function is smooth and nearly quadratic, the sparse-EM iterates contract geometrically to the truth up to a statistical floor that scales as sqrt(s*) times the M-step’s ℓ∞ estimation error (typically O(sqrt(log d/n))), yielding near-minimax rates. For inference, the authors introduce a Dantzig-based decorrelated score and prove asymptotic normality with variance attaining the semiparametric information bound, extending debiasing ideas beyond convex M-estimators to latent-variable settings. The framework covers both exact and gradient M-steps and is illustrated on Gaussian mixtures and mixture-of-regressions.

Quality: The analysis is technically careful and advances high-dimensional guarantees for EM, including an ℓ∞ control on the M-operator error specialized to sparse inputs—a new and clearly stated condition. The geometric convergence plus optimal statistical rate and the semiparametric efficiency result are strong. That said, the necessity and advantage of per-iteration truncation remain unclear: for models where estimating M at arbitrary vectors is as easy as at sparse ones, ordinary EM followed by a single truncation could plausibly achieve similar rates; a dedicated comparison (theory or experiments) would strengthen the case. The dependence on an initialization within the basin and resampling-based proof setup should be discussed vis-à-vis practical single-sample runs.

Clarity: Generally well organized but dense. Please specify precisely the initialization used in experiments, sensitivity to the sparsity level bs, and stopping rules. A brief schematic of assumptions (Concavity-Smoothness, Lipschitz-Gradient variants, Statistical-Error) and their verification pathways would aid readability.

Originality: High. The sparse-EM contraction analysis and decorrelated score for latent-variable EM are novel. Please cite related sparse optimization work on truncated gradients (e.g., Langford, Li, Zhang, ICML 2009).

Significance: High. The results provide important high-dimensional convergence and inference guarantees for a widely used algorithmic paradigm.

Pros for acceptance:
- Geometric convergence with near-minimax statistical rates in high dimensions
- Asymptotically normal, semiparametrically efficient decorrelated score
- Covers exact and gradient M-steps; broadly applicable

Cons against acceptance:
- Uncertain necessity/benefit of iterative truncation vs EM + one-shot truncation
- Strong reliance on basin initialization; limited guidance for practice
- Need clearer experiments and baselines reflecting the above questions

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the role of truncation and strengthening experimental comparisons.
