### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a general theoretical framework for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models using a novel variant of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors' main contributions are twofold. First, they propose a "high-dimensional EM algorithm" which integrates a hard truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. For this algorithm, they establish theoretical guarantees, showing that with a suitable initialization, it converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of approximately sqrt(s*log(d)/n). This analysis covers both exact maximization and gradient ascent implementations of the M-step. Second, the paper develops an inferential procedure based on a "decorrelated score test" that allows for hypothesis testing and uncertainty quantification for low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. The authors prove that this test statistic is asymptotically normal and that its limiting variance achieves the semiparametric information bound, rendering it statistically optimal. The framework is applied to two important high-dimensional latent variable models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model. This work builds upon prior analysis of the EM algorithm in low-dimensional settings (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) by extending it to the high-dimensional regime, where sparsity is crucial and new analytical tools are required.

### Strengths (Arguments Pro Acceptance)

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a fundamental and challenging problem: providing a unified, computationally feasible framework with strong theoretical guarantees for both estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The gap between the practical utility of EM and its theoretical understanding in the `d >> n` setting is significant, and this paper makes a major step toward closing it.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** The main results are impressive. The simultaneous establishment of geometric computational convergence and near-optimal statistical rates for the proposed algorithm is a strong contribution. Furthermore, providing a rigorous inferential framework for the local optima found by an EM-type algorithm, rather than a global optimum of a convex program, is a novel and important extension of recent work on high-dimensional inference.
3.  **Generality of the Framework:** The authors present their analysis under a general set of conditions (Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness, Statistical-Error) and then verify them for specific models. This approach makes the framework potentially applicable to a broader class of latent variable models beyond the two examples provided.

### Weaknesses (Arguments Con Acceptance / Major Points for Revision)

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper has several weaknesses, primarily related to practical applicability, clarity of its novel contributions, and the verifiability of its assumptions.

1.  **Lack of Practical Guidance:** The paper is heavy on theory but offers little guidance on how its proposed methods would be used in practice.
    *   **Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm and the inference procedure depend on crucial tuning parameters, namely the sparsity level `bs` for truncation and the parameter `λ` for the Dantzig selector. There is no discussion on how to select these parameters in a data-dependent manner (e.g., via cross-validation). An assessment of the methods' sensitivity to these choices is also missing. This is a critical omission for a method intended for practical use.
    *   **Choice of Algorithm:** The paper analyzes two M-step implementations (exact maximization and gradient ascent) but provides no discussion on the trade-offs or practical guidance on when to prefer one over the other.

2.  **The Initialization Requirement:** The theoretical guarantees hinge on an initial estimator `θ^(init)` that is already within a certain radius of the true parameter `θ*`. This is a common requirement in analyses of non-convex optimization, but the paper fails to provide any practical method for obtaining such an initializer. Without a concrete and computationally feasible initialization strategy, the applicability of the entire theoretical framework is called into question.

3.  **Clarity on Originality of the Inference Procedure:** The originality of the decorrelated score test as presented is not clearly delineated from existing work. The method described in Section 2.2 and the associated theory appear to be a direct application of the general framework for debiased/desparsified inference developed for M-estimators, particularly the work of Ning & Liu (JASA, 2017, "A general theory of statistical inference for regularized M-estimators"). The paper should explicitly cite this or related foundational works (e.g., van de Geer et al., 2014; Zhang & Zhang, 2014) and clarify what specific technical innovations were required to adapt this framework to the setting of an estimator derived from a truncated EM algorithm, which is not a standard M-estimator. Without this clarification, the second major contribution of the paper seems less novel than claimed.

4.  **Assumptions and Technical Density:** The main theorems rely on a large number of high-level technical assumptions (Conditions 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.4, and Assumption 4.5). While the paper states these are verified for the examples in the appendix, the main text would be significantly improved by providing more intuition about what these conditions mean for the specific models considered and how restrictive they are. This would help the reader gauge the practical scope of the results.

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper relegates a substantial amount of critical detail to the appendix. While page limits are a constraint, the main paper could do a better job of summarizing the key technical challenges and proof ideas. For example, a more detailed discussion of why the truncation step poses significant challenges for the analysis, and how the `l1`-norm statistical error (Condition 3.3) is crucial for handling it, would strengthen the main text and better highlight the paper's core technical contributions.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a theoretically deep and significant contribution to the understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The dual guarantees for estimation and inference are a major step forward. However, the work is hampered by a lack of practical guidance, an unaddressed initialization requirement, and insufficient clarity regarding the novelty of its inferential procedure relative to existing literature. I would be inclined to recommend acceptance if the authors can address these points, particularly by providing a clearer discussion of the relationship to prior work on high-dimensional inference and offering plausible, practical strategies for initialization and tuning parameter selection.
