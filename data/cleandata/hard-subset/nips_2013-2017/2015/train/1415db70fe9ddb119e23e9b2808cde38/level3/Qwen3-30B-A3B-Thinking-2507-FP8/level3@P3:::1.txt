

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm with sparsity-inducing truncation steps for latent variable models, establishing both computational and statistical guarantees. The authors make two key contributions: (1) a high-dimensional EM algorithm with geometric convergence to a near-optimal estimator, and (2) a decorrelated score test for asymptotic inference of low-dimensional components. The work builds on recent low-dimensional EM convergence results by [2] but extends them to high-dimensional settings where sparsity is required.

**Strengths:**
The paper provides a theoretically rigorous framework for high-dimensional latent variable models, which is a significant gap in the literature. The two main theorems (3.4 and 4.6) establish both computational convergence rates and statistical optimality, which is rare in high-dimensional inference. The decorrelated score test achieves semiparametric efficiency, matching the information bound. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with verifications for Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models. The paper makes a clear case for the novelty of adapting the decorrelated score approach (from Ning & Liu [26]) to latent variable models, where the estimator is not a global optimum of a convex program.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper lacks sufficient practical guidance on implementation details. Specifically:

1. **Initialization:** The paper requires an initial estimator β^init satisfying ||β^init - β*||₂/||β*||₂ ≤ ε, but provides no practical methods to obtain such an estimator in high dimensions. For example, in Gaussian mixture models, spectral methods could be used, but this is not discussed. The authors should elaborate on practical initialization strategies for different latent variable models.

2. **Algorithm selection:** The paper proposes two M-step implementations but offers no clear guidance on when to use exact maximization versus gradient ascent. For instance, exact maximization would be preferable when the M-step has a closed-form solution, while gradient ascent would be better for large-scale problems.

3. **Tuning parameters:** The paper uses "bs" as a sparsity parameter (not "ĥs" as mentioned in the query) and "λ" (denoted as " " in the paper) for the Dantzig selector. The authors should provide data-dependent methods for choosing these parameters (e.g., cross-validation) and discuss sensitivity to these choices. The current paper only states theoretical bounds without practical implementation guidance.

4. **Ning & Liu connection:** The decorrelated score test matches Ning & Liu's method [26], but the paper should explicitly cite this and clarify that while the score test framework is similar, the adaptation to latent variable models requires new theoretical developments to handle the non-convexity and local optimum nature of the EM algorithm.

5. **Assumptions:** While the paper verifies assumptions for specific models, it doesn't discuss whether these assumptions hold for broader classes of latent variable models. The technical conditions (Conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.5) are quite involved, and the paper would benefit from a discussion of their practical plausibility.

6. **Technical depth:** Key technical challenges (particularly the analysis of the truncation step and its effects on the iterative solution path) are relegated to the supplement. The main paper should include more details on these challenges to improve accessibility.

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to high-dimensional latent variable modeling, establishing the first computationally feasible framework for optimal estimation and inference. While the theoretical results are strong, the paper would be substantially improved with more practical guidance on implementation details, particularly regarding initialization, algorithm selection, and tuning parameter choices. The connection to Ning & Liu should be explicitly acknowledged, and the paper should clarify why adapting their method to latent variable models required non-trivial theoretical developments.

The paper meets the NIPS quality standards in terms of technical soundness (with thorough theoretical analysis), originality (novel application to latent variable models with sparsity), and significance (addressing a fundamental gap in high-dimensional latent variable inference). With minor revisions to address the practical implementation concerns, this would be an excellent contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
