This paper presents a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings where the parameter dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the standard EM procedure by adding a truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity on the parameter iterates. Under suitable conditions, including a good initialization, this algorithm is shown to converge at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a (near-)minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`. Second, building on this estimator, the paper develops a decorrelated score statistic for performing hypothesis tests on individual components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. This statistic is proven to be asymptotically normal, providing a valid method for statistical inference.

This work builds upon recent advances in the non-asymptotic analysis of the EM algorithm in low-dimensional settings, particularly the work of Balakrishnan et al. (2014), which also views EM as a perturbed gradient method. However, the present paper tackles the significant additional challenges of high dimensionality by integrating sparsity-enforcing steps and providing a much more fine-grained statistical analysis in the `l_infinity`-norm. The inferential part of the paper connects to the growing literature on high-dimensional inference for regularized estimators (e.g., debiased Lasso), but is novel in its application to the non-convex setting of latent variable models where the estimator is not the global optimum of a convex program.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing problem: how to perform statistically sound estimation and inference for latent variable models in the high-dimensional regime. Providing a computationally tractable algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees for this class of problems is a major contribution and will be of great interest to both the machine learning theory and practitioner communities.

2.  **Quality:** The theoretical results are strong and comprehensive. The authors establish not just an error bound for the final estimator, but also characterize the algorithm's computational convergence rate. The statistical rate achieved is near-optimal. The development of a decorrelated score statistic with proven asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency is a particularly impressive and valuable result, as valid inference is often much harder than point estimation in high dimensions.

3.  **Originality:** The unified framework that simultaneously guarantees geometric computational convergence, optimal statistical rates, and valid asymptotic inference for a high-dimensional, non-convex procedure is highly novel. While components of the analysis draw from existing literature, their synthesis to handle the EM algorithm is a significant technical achievement.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Justification of the Proposed Algorithm:** The main algorithmic novelty is the introduction of a truncation step at *every* iteration. While this enables the theoretical analysis, the paper does not sufficiently motivate why this iterative truncation is necessary compared to simpler alternatives. For example, could one run a standard `l1`-regularized EM algorithm and perform a single truncation on the final estimate to achieve similar guarantees? A more thorough discussion motivating this specific algorithmic choice, perhaps with a counterexample illustrating the failure of simpler approaches, would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution. (I note the authors mentioned to me in discussion that standard linear regression is such a counterexample; including this insight in the paper would be very helpful).

2.  **Technical Assumptions:** The analysis relies on a new technical condition (Condition 3.3) that requires `l_infinity`-norm control of the statistical error `M(theta) - Mn(theta)` only for sparse vectors `theta`. This condition feels tailored to the proposed algorithm. For many models, such as the Gaussian Mixture Model example, the M-step operator is simple (e.g., a weighted average) and its statistical error can likely be controlled for *any* `theta` in the basin of attraction, not just sparse ones. If a more general error bound is available, it calls into question whether the restriction to sparse inputs (and by extension, the iterative truncation) is a fundamental necessity or a convenience for the proof. Clarifying this point would help readers understand the true source of difficulty in the high-dimensional setting.

3.  **Related Work:** The idea of using truncation within an iterative algorithm to maintain sparsity is related to the literature on projected or truncated gradient methods. For instance, the work of Langford et al. (2009, "Sparse Online Learning via Truncated Gradient") is relevant and should be discussed.

4.  **Experimental Clarity:** The theory relies critically on an initialization that is already within a certain radius of the true parameter. For the results to be of practical relevance, it is important to know how such an initialization can be obtained. The paper should specify the initialization procedure used in any experiments to allow for reproducibility and to give readers a sense of the practical feasibility of this requirement.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides the first computationally feasible and theoretically-backed framework for both optimal estimation and valid inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.
*   The technical contributions are substantial, successfully analyzing a non-convex iterative procedure in a high-dimensional setting.
*   The results are significant and address a major open problem at the intersection of statistics and machine learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The central algorithmic proposal (iterative truncation) is not fully motivated over simpler, more standard alternatives.
*   Some of the technical assumptions appear to be artifacts of the specific analysis rather than fundamental properties of the problem, potentially limiting the perceived necessity of the proposed algorithm.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a very strong paper that makes significant theoretical contributions to a difficult and important problem. The weaknesses identified primarily relate to the motivation and justification of the specific algorithmic choices, rather than the quality of the theoretical analysis itself. These are points that can be addressed in a revised version. I believe the strengths, particularly the establishment of a unified framework for estimation and inference, far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
