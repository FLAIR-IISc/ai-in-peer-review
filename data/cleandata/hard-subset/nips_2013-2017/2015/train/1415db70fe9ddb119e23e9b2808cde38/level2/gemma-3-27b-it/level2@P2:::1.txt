This paper proposes a high-dimensional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with theoretical guarantees for parameter estimation and hypothesis testing in latent variable models. The core idea is to incorporate a truncation step into the standard EM algorithm to enforce sparsity, allowing for statistical rate optimality even when the dimensionality exceeds the sample size. The work builds upon previous efforts to understand the convergence properties of EM, particularly [2, 25, 30], and extends them to the high-dimensional setting. The proposed approach addresses a significant gap in the literature, providing a computationally feasible method with established statistical properties for high-dimensional latent variable models.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Establishing theoretical guarantees for EM in high dimensions is a crucial and challenging problem. The paper successfully tackles this, offering a practical algorithm with provable performance.
* **Novel approach:** The introduction of the truncation step is a clever way to enforce sparsity and achieve statistical rate optimality. This distinguishes the work from prior literature that primarily focuses on low-dimensional settings.
* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides rigorous analysis, establishing both computational convergence rates (geometric rate) and statistical properties (near-optimal rate of convergence and asymptotic normality). The decomposition of estimation error into optimization and statistical components is well-motivated and informative.
* **Generality:** The framework is designed to be applicable to a broad family of latent variable models, illustrated with examples of Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.
* **Inference procedure:** The development of a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing is a valuable contribution, addressing a key challenge in high-dimensional inference. Establishing asymptotic normality is a significant achievement.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical complexity:** The paper is densely written and requires a strong background in statistical learning theory to fully understand. The numerous conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) and their interplay can be difficult to follow. A more intuitive explanation of these conditions would be helpful.
* **Constant factors:** The statistical rates often involve unspecified constant factors (e.g., C, C0). While common in theoretical analyses, clarifying the dependence of these constants on problem parameters (sparsity level, dimensionality, sample size) would enhance the practical relevance.
* **Assumptions:** The assumptions required for the theoretical guarantees are quite strong, particularly Assumption 4.5 regarding the Fisher information matrix and the scaling of various quantities with dimensionality and sample size.  The discussion of the implications and limitations of these assumptions could be more thorough. The requirement that s⇤w be small (mentioned towards the end of section 4.1) is non-trivial and could limit applicability.
* **Limited empirical evaluation:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical results. While the authors mention applications to Gaussian mixture and regression models, a more extensive empirical evaluation demonstrating the practical performance of the algorithm and the accuracy of the hypothesis tests would strengthen the work.
* **Notation:** The paper uses a lot of notation, making it harder to parse, and some of the notation is not entirely consistent (e.g., different uses of subscript notation for variables).

**Specific Comments:**

* In Condition 3.3, the use of `1 norm is motivated as providing a finer-grained error analysis. However, the practical implications of this choice could be discussed further.  Does it lead to computational advantages or disadvantages?
* The discussion around the relationship to existing tensor methods could be expanded. While the paper mentions the slower statistical rates of tensor methods, a more detailed comparison would be informative.
* Equation (1.1) uses a somewhat unusual notation (· indicating multiplication), which might be confusing for some readers.
* The discussion of the limitations of the proposed algorithm (e.g., the strong assumptions) is relatively brief. A more comprehensive discussion of potential scenarios where the algorithm might fail or perform poorly would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in statistical learning.
* The proposed algorithm and theoretical results are significant contributions to the field.
* The work is technically sound and provides rigorous guarantees.
* The generality of the framework and the inclusion of inference procedures add to its value.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical complexity and strong assumptions might limit the accessibility and applicability of the work.
* The lack of extensive empirical evaluation weakens the practical impact.
* The dense writing style could make it difficult for some readers to fully appreciate the contributions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of the EM algorithm in high dimensions. While the technical complexity and strong assumptions are potential drawbacks, the novelty of the approach, the rigor of the analysis, and the importance of the problem addressed outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity, provide more context for the assumptions, and potentially include some illustrative empirical results. I suggest the authors focus on making the paper more accessible to a broader audience while retaining the technical depth.
