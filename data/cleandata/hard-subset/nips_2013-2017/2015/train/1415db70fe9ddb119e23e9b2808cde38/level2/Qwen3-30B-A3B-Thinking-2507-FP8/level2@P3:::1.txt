

The paper introduces a high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity through a truncation step, establishing geometric convergence and near-optimal statistical rates for parameter estimation in high-dimensional latent variable models. It further proposes a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference on low-dimensional components, achieving semiparametric efficiency. This work addresses a significant gap in the literature, as previous theoretical guarantees for EM algorithms only applied to low-dimensional settings where d << n, while high-dimensional regimes (d >> n) were largely unexplored due to the lack of computational and statistical guarantees.

The paper makes strong theoretical contributions with well-structured theorems establishing both computational and statistical guarantees. The high-dimensional EM algorithm (Algorithm 1) extends classical EM by adding a truncation step (T-step) that enforces sparsity, enabling analysis in high-dimensional settings. Theorem 3.4 provides a clear decomposition of estimation error into optimization error (geometric rate) and statistical error (near-optimal rate of √(s* log d/n)), which is significantly sharper than previous low-dimensional results (√(d/n)). The decorrelated score statistic (Theorem 4.6) achieves asymptotic normality with optimal limiting variance, establishing the first computationally feasible approach for inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.

The work demonstrates significant originality by extending EM algorithm theory to high dimensions while addressing the dual challenges of optimization and inference. The truncation step represents a novel technical contribution that enables the analysis of sparsity structure along the solution path. The paper properly contextualizes its contributions against related work, particularly noting how it builds upon [2] while addressing high-dimensional limitations, and distinguishes itself from tensor methods [1] and debiasing approaches for generalized linear models [13, 26, 32]. The theoretical framework is sufficiently general to apply to multiple latent variable models, with verification for Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models.

The paper excels in technical quality, with rigorous proofs provided in the appendix and careful attention to the interplay between optimization and statistical error. The conditions (Conditions 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) are well-motivated and verified for specific models, though the technical nature of these conditions may pose challenges for readers seeking immediate implementation. The presentation is clear and well-organized, with the main results concisely stated before detailed technical development.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it provides a unified framework for both estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models, which are widely used in applications from genomics to social sciences. The near-optimal statistical rates and semiparametric efficiency of the inference procedure represent meaningful advances over existing methods. The results are likely to be valuable to researchers working with high-dimensional data and latent structures.

Some minor weaknesses could be addressed: The paper could provide more intuition about why the truncation step is necessary for high-dimensional analysis rather than simply stating it as a technical device. The relationship between the sparsity parameter bs and the true sparsity level s* could be more clearly explained, particularly regarding how to choose bs in practice. While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the paper might benefit from a brief discussion of computational complexity compared to alternative high-dimensional methods.

The paper's technical contributions are substantial, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the results represent a meaningful advance in the field. The framework is general enough to apply to multiple latent variable models while providing tight statistical guarantees. The proposed methods address a significant gap in the literature, making this work highly relevant for the NIPS community.

The paper meets all four NIPS evaluation criteria: It is technically sound with well-supported claims (Quality), clearly written with appropriate organization (Clarity), presents novel contributions that significantly extend previous work (Originality), and addresses an important problem with impactful results (Significance). The work represents a substantial contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and latent variable modeling, advancing both theory and practical methodology.
