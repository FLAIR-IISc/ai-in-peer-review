### Summary of the Paper

This paper develops a comprehensive theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings where the parameter dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The work makes two primary contributions. First, it proposes a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" that incorporates a truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. For this algorithm, the paper establishes that with a proper initialization, the iterates converge at a geometric rate to a solution that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of sqrt(s*log(d)/n). Second, the paper develops a procedure for statistical inference on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. It introduces a "decorrelated score statistic" based on the estimator from the proposed EM algorithm and proves it is asymptotically normal. This enables hypothesis testing and the construction of confidence intervals. The theoretical framework is presented generally and its applicability is demonstrated for two important latent variable models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model.

The work is well-situated within the literature. It builds upon recent analyses of the EM algorithm in low-dimensional settings, particularly the work of Balakrishnan et al. (2014) [Ref 2], and extends them to the more challenging high-dimensional regime. The key novelty in the estimation analysis is the careful handling of the sparsity-inducing truncation step. For inference, the work adapts ideas from the high-dimensional inference literature (e.g., desparsification/debiasing for the Lasso) to the significantly more complex setting of estimators produced by a non-convex iterative procedure, which is a notable advance.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper presents a substantial and technically sophisticated piece of work. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis, with detailed proofs provided in the appendix. The decomposition of the estimation error into an optimization component (which decays geometrically) and a statistical component (which determines the final accuracy) is an elegant and effective analytical strategy. The conditions required for the main theorems are stated formally (e.g., Conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.4), and their validity is then verified for the specific model examples, making the general framework concrete. The result establishing the asymptotic normality of the decorrelated score statistic, and showing its limiting variance achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound, is a state-of-the-art contribution for this class of problems. The work appears to be a complete and technically sound theoretical contribution.

### Clarity and Organization

The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem, summarizes the main contributions, and precisely situates the work with respect to existing literature. The methodology is presented logically, first introducing the algorithm and then the inference procedure. The theoretical results are stated in clear theorems, with the necessary assumptions laid out beforehand. This structure makes the paper's dense technical content accessible. Reproducing the theoretical results would require a deep dive into the appendix, but the paper provides a clear roadmap for doing so.

### Originality and Significance

The originality and significance of this work are high. While the EM algorithm is a classical method, its theoretical properties in the high-dimensional sparse setting have remained largely unexplored. This paper provides the first general framework that establishes both computational guarantees (geometric convergence) and optimal statistical guarantees (near-minimax estimation rates) for a tractable EM-type algorithm in this setting.

The contribution to high-dimensional inference is particularly noteworthy. Most existing work on inference focuses on estimators that are global solutions to convex optimization problems (e.g., the Lasso). This paper breaks new ground by developing a valid inferential procedure for a local solution obtained via a non-convex optimization path. Given the prevalence of latent variable models and the EM algorithm in practice, these theoretical results are of great importance and are likely to be influential. The framework has the potential to be applied to many other high-dimensional models with latent structure, advancing the state of the art in a significant way.

### Suggestions for Improvement

The paper is strong, but could be improved in a few areas to maximize its clarity and impact. The following suggestions are offered to help refine the manuscript.

1.  **The Initialization Assumption:** The analysis hinges on the availability of an initial estimator `init` that is already within a constant-factor radius of the true parameter `theta*`. This is a strong assumption and a common one in the analysis of non-convex problems. However, the practical utility of the algorithm depends on the ability to find such an initialization. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion of how one might obtain such an initializer in practice. For the specific examples (GMM, mixture of regressions), are there known computationally efficient methods (e.g., based on spectral techniques or moment methods) that could provide an initialization satisfying the required conditions? Acknowledging this prerequisite more explicitly in the main text and providing potential strategies would be very helpful.

2.  **Practical Guidance on Tuning Parameters:** The proposed algorithm and inference procedure depend on tuning parameters, such as the sparsity level `bs` and the Dantzig selector parameter `lambda`. The theory provides guidance on their scaling with respect to `n`, `d`, and `s*`. However, for a practitioner wishing to apply these methods, advice on how to select these parameters from data would be invaluable. A discussion of potential data-driven choices (e.g., using cross-validation for `bs`) and the robustness of the procedures to these choices would be a useful addition.

3.  **Analysis of the Resampling Version:** The theoretical analysis is carried out for a "resampling version" of the algorithm, as detailed in the appendix. This is a standard proof technique used to ensure independence between iterates and simplify the analysis of statistical error propagation. It would be beneficial to briefly mention this in the main text (e.g., at the start of Section 3.1) and to add a sentence explaining why the results are expected to transfer to the standard, non-resampling algorithm that would be used in practice.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While its theoretical contributions are significant enough to stand on their own, the claims of computational feasibility and statistical optimality would be powerfully demonstrated with a simulation study. Even a small set of simulations for one of the example models could show the estimation error scaling as predicted by the theory and demonstrate that the proposed hypothesis test achieves the correct nominal size. This would provide a valuable complement to the theoretical analysis. While this may be a substantial revision, it is worth considering for a future version of the work.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a fundamental, important, and open problem: the behavior of the EM algorithm in high dimensions.
    *   Provides strong, novel theoretical results, including geometric convergence rates, near-optimal statistical rates, and the first valid inference procedure for estimators from an EM-type algorithm in this setting.
    *   The framework is general and its application is demonstrated on important statistical models.
    *   The work is of high technical quality and is very clearly presented.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The theory relies on a strong initialization assumption, without providing a constructive method for obtaining such an initial point.
    *   The paper lacks any empirical validation, making it difficult to assess the finite-sample performance of the proposed methods and their robustness to the choice of tuning parameters.

On balance, the strengths of the paper, particularly the novelty and significance of its theoretical contributions, are substantial and outweigh the weaknesses. The paper represents a major step forward in our understanding of a cornerstone algorithm of statistical machine learning.
