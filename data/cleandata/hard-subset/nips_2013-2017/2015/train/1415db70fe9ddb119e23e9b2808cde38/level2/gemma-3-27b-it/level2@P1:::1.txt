This paper proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm with theoretical guarantees for parameter estimation and hypothesis testing in latent variable models. The core idea is to incorporate a truncation step into the EM iterations to enforce sparsity, leading to improved statistical rates and enabling asymptotic inference. The authors establish convergence rates and asymptotic normality results, demonstrating the optimality of their approach.

The paper builds upon existing work on the EM algorithm, particularly [2, 18, 25, 30], which focus on low-dimensional settings. It addresses the critical gap in theoretical understanding for high-dimensional scenarios where the MLE is often ill-defined without regularization. Related work on high-dimensional estimation, such as Lasso and tensor methods, are discussed, and the authors highlight the novelty of their approach, particularly in providing inferential tools for latent variable models.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm with truncation is a valuable contribution, addressing a significant gap in the literature. It provides a computationally feasible approach to optimal estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the algorithm, establishing geometric convergence rates and asymptotic normality under reasonable conditions. The statistical rates achieved are near-optimal.
* **Generality:** The framework is presented in a general way, allowing application to various latent variable models (Gaussian mixture, mixture of regression).
* **Inference Procedure:** Developing a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing in high dimensions is a significant addition and addresses a crucial need in practical applications.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly presents the methodology and theoretical results. The algorithm is clearly laid out.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Condition Complexity:** The conditions for the theoretical guarantees (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) are numerous and can be difficult to verify in practice. While the authors demonstrate verification for specific models, the general applicability could be limited if these conditions are hard to satisfy for other models.  More discussion on the practical implications of these conditions would be helpful.
* **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The algorithm's performance relies on a suitable initialization (init ⇤ /k ⇤k 2 <  ). The paper does not provide concrete guidance on how to obtain such an initialization in practice. This is a common issue with EM algorithms, but addressing it in more detail would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.
* **Constant Factors:** The theoretical rates often involve unspecified constant factors (e.g., C, C0).  The dependence of these constants on various parameters (d, n, s) is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the results.
* **Computational Cost:** The truncation step (line 6 of Algorithm 1) might add significant computational overhead, especially for very high-dimensional problems.  A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the theory is strong, the paper lacks empirical results demonstrating the performance of the algorithm on real or simulated datasets.  Including such experiments would significantly strengthen the claims and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical machine learning – high-dimensional inference in latent variable models.
* The theoretical results are rigorous and contribute significantly to our understanding of the EM algorithm in challenging settings.
* The proposed algorithm and inferential procedure offer a novel and potentially valuable tool for practitioners.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the methodology and theoretical results.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The conditions for the theoretical guarantees are complex and might limit the practical applicability of the algorithm.
* The lack of empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of the proposed method.
* The sensitivity to initialization and potential computational cost could be significant drawbacks.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field. While the theoretical focus and complex conditions are limitations, the novelty and rigor of the work are compelling. The potential for application to important problems in latent variable modeling is significant. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with the following suggestions:

1.  **Address Initialization:** Provide more discussion and potentially guidance on obtaining appropriate initializations for the algorithm.
2.  **Clarify Constant Factors:** Discuss the dependence of the constant factors in the theoretical rates on the relevant parameters.
3.  **Computational Complexity:** Add a discussion on the computational complexity of the algorithm, including the impact of the truncation step.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** Strongly encourage the authors to include empirical results demonstrating the performance of the algorithm on real or simulated datasets. Even a limited set of experiments would significantly strengthen the paper.
5. **Condition Discussion**: Provide a more thorough discussion regarding the difficulty in verifying conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.5 for different models. A simple checklist of conditions that are frequently violated, and a discussion on strategies for addressing these violations, would greatly improve the practical relevance of the work.
