This paper provides a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings, where the number of parameters `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which incorporates a hard-thresholding (truncation) step after the standard M-step to enforce sparsity. They prove that with a good initialization, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`. Second, building on this estimator, they develop a procedure for asymptotic inference (hypothesis testing) on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. They propose a "decorrelated score statistic" and prove it is asymptotically normal with a variance that achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. The framework is general and is demonstrated on two important latent variable models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model.

This work sits at the intersection of several important research lines. It directly extends the recent analysis of the EM algorithm in low-dimensional settings (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) to the more challenging high-dimensional regime. The key novelty here is the integration and analysis of a truncation step to handle sparsity, which requires a more refined, entrywise (`l_infinity`) analysis of the statistical error, rather than the `l_2` analysis sufficient for low-dimensional problems. For the inference part, the paper masterfully adapts ideas from the high-dimensional inference literature for convex models (e.g., debiased Lasso by Zhang & Zhang, 2014; van de Geer et al., 2014) to the non-convex setting of latent variable models, where the estimator is not a global optimum but rather the output of an iterative algorithm. This is a non-trivial extension and addresses a significant open problem.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The EM algorithm is one of the most widely used algorithms in statistics and machine learning, yet its theoretical properties in the modern high-dimensional setting were largely unknown. This paper makes a landmark contribution by providing the first general framework with rigorous guarantees for both statistically optimal estimation and valid asymptotic inference for EM in this setting. The results are of high importance to both theorists and practitioners.

2.  **Originality and Technical Depth:** The paper is highly original. The combination of the iterative EM analysis with sparsity-inducing truncation steps and the subsequent development of a valid inferential procedure for the resulting local optimum is a novel and powerful idea. The technical analysis appears solid and is quite challenging. In particular, controlling the support of the iterates and analyzing the propagation of entrywise error through the non-linear EM updates is a significant technical achievement.

3.  **Generality:** The authors present their results in a general framework, based on a set of clearly stated (though abstract) conditions on the population-level Q-function and the statistical error of the M-step. This makes the framework potentially applicable to a wide range of high-dimensional latent variable models beyond the two examples provided.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Initialization Requirement:** The theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithm hinge on the existence of an initial estimator that is already reasonably close to the true parameter vector. This is a common requirement in the analysis of non-convex optimization problems. However, the paper does not discuss how to obtain such an initial estimator. This is a critical practical issue, as a poor initialization could lead the algorithm to an arbitrarily bad local optimum. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a discussion of potential initialization strategies (e.g., via spectral or tensor methods) and citing relevant work, even if a full analysis is beyond the scope of this work.

2.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** The main theorems rely on a series of high-level technical conditions (e.g., Conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.4). While these are necessary for the general proof, their abstraction can make it difficult for the reader to grasp the intuition of why they should hold for a given model. For example, Condition 3.3 essentially assumes a well-behaved `l_infinity` statistical error for the M-step. A more intuitive explanation in the main text of what properties of a model lead to these conditions being satisfied would improve the paper's accessibility.

3.  **Resampling-based Analysis:** The theoretical analysis is performed for a resampling version of the algorithm (Algorithm 4 in the appendix), where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a standard analytical tool to ensure independence, but it does not match the standard implementation where the full dataset is used at each step. The authors should briefly comment on this discrepancy in the main paper and argue why the results are expected to hold for the standard algorithm, perhaps by citing literature where this gap is discussed.

4.  **Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm and the inference procedure depend on tuning parameters, namely the sparsity level `bs` and the Dantzig selector parameter `lambda`. The theory provides their required scaling, but offers no guidance on how to choose them in practice from data. A short discussion on potential data-driven choices (e.g., cross-validation) would enhance the paper's practical relevance.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent, technically deep, and highly significant paper that addresses a major open problem in machine learning and statistics. It provides the first comprehensive theoretical treatment of the EM algorithm for estimation and inference in high dimensions. The weaknesses identified, particularly regarding the initialization assumption, are common limitations in this research area and do not detract significantly from the paper's core contributions. The paper is well-written, clearly positions itself with respect to prior work, and the results are substantial. It represents a major advance and is a strong candidate for acceptance.
