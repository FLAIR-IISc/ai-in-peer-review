- Proposes a High-Dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step enforcing sparsity and proves geometric convergence to near-minimax estimation error sqrt(s*log d / n).
- Develops a decorrelated score statistic for inference on low-dimensional components and proves its asymptotic normality for hypothesis testing and CIs.
- Shows the decorrelated score achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound for the limiting variance.
- Presents a general theoretical framework and verifies applicability for Gaussian mixture and mixture-of-regressions models.
- Builds on low-dimensional EM analyses (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2014) and adapts high-dimensional inference ideas to non-convex EM estimators.
- Provides rigorous, detailed proofs with an elegant error decomposition into an optimization term (geometric decay) and a statistical term (final accuracy).
- Conditions for the theorems are stated formally and checked for the example models, making the framework concrete.
- The paper is very well written and organized, with clear problem setup, methodology, and theorem statements.
- Original and significant: first general framework giving computational (geometric) and near-optimal statistical guarantees for tractable EM in high-dimensional sparse settings.
- Notable advance in high-dimensional inference by providing valid procedures for local non-convex solutions rather than convex global estimators.
- Major weakness: strong initialization assumption requiring an initializer within constant radius of the truth, with no constructive method provided.
- Practical guidance lacking on selecting tuning parameters (sparsity level bs, Dantzig lambda) and robustness to those choices.
- Theoretical analysis uses a resampling-version of the algorithm; this should be mentioned in the main text with justification for transferability to the standard algorithm.
- Entirely theoretical with no empirical validation; simulations would help demonstrate finite-sample performance and test calibration.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important open problem, provides novel theoretical results, general framework, and high technical quality.
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on a strong, unexplained initialization assumption and lack of empirical results to assess practical performance.
