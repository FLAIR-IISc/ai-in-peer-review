Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a unified computational-statistical theory for EM in high-dimensional latent variable models. The main algorithmic idea augments classical EM with a truncation step (T-step) that keeps the bs largest-magnitude coordinates after each M-step (either exact maximization or one-step gradient ascent). With an initialization inside a basin of attraction, the iterates converge geometrically to an s∗-sparse estimator whose error decouples into an optimization term and a statistical term. The latter attains a near-minimax rate O(√(s∗ log d / n)) up to log n factors via entrywise control of the “population–sample” M-step discrepancy.

On inference, the paper constructs a decorrelated score based on a Dantzig-type estimator of the nuisance projection vector w, proving asymptotic normality with variance equal to the semiparametric efficient information for the low-dimensional component of interest. The theory is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, with explicit conditions (e.g., SNR bounds for GMM).

This work extends low-dimensional EM analyses (e.g., Balakrishnan, Wainwright, Yu, 2014/2017) to the high-dimensional regime with sparsity, and bridges to the high-dimensional inference literature (e.g., Zhang & Zhang, 2014; van de Geer et al., 2014; Javanmard & Montanari, 2014) by handling latent-variable nonconvexity where global convex optima are unavailable. Compared to tensor methods for latent models (e.g., Anandkumar et al., 2014; Chaganty & Liang, 2013), the proposed approach achieves sharper statistical rates and is computationally closer to EM workflows used in practice.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: A simple, practically motivated modification of EM (post-M-step truncation) with a clear computational-statistical tradeoff, yielding geometric contraction plus near-optimal high-dimensional rates.
- New inferential framework for latent-variable models: Decorrelated score built from EM objects, achieving semiparametric efficiency despite high-dimensional nuisance parameters and nonconvexity.
- Generality: Covers both exact and gradient M-steps under unified conditions (local strong concavity/smoothness and Lipschitz-type properties of Q), with model-specific verifications for GMM and mixture-of-regressions.
- Technical novelty: Entrywise control of population–sample M-step error and careful tracking of support evolution induced by truncation; these ingredients are distinct from prior EM analyses and essential in high dimension.
- Clear separation of optimization vs. statistical errors; explicit dependence on s∗, d, n.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Resampling EM in the analysis: The main convergence theorem is proved for a resampling variant (fresh data per iteration). This simplifies concentration but is not the standard practice. While common as a technical device, explicit theory for the single-sample iterate would strengthen the claims.
- Initialization and basin assumptions: Guarantees require initialization in a local basin and SNR conditions (e.g., for GMM). Practical guidance on initializers (e.g., spectral/tensor, method-of-moments, or K-means-based) and their sample complexity is only briefly mentioned; a consolidated recipe would improve applicability.
- Choice of sparsity level bs: The theory assumes bs of the same order as s∗; procedures for selecting bs and robustness to misspecification are not discussed in the main text.
- Scope of model verification: Conditions are verified for two canonical models. Additional latent models (e.g., mixtures with unknown covariances, HMMs, topic models) are noted as possible, but concrete verifications would bolster generality claims.
- Computational aspects of inference: The Dantzig step uses Tn (an empirical analog of Fisher blocks). Practical computation, tuning of λ, and numerical stability in high d, as well as sensitivity to model misspecification, are not empirically assessed.
- Empirical validation: The paper appears theory-only. Finite-sample experiments would help corroborate rates, robustness to initialization/bs, and power/type-I error of the score/Wald tests, compared against penalized EM without truncation, tensor methods, and de-biased GLM baselines where applicable.

Quality
The theoretical development is careful and technically substantial. Assumptions are standard for local EM analyses and are plausibly verified for the worked examples. The inference theory connects EM quantities to the score/Fisher information and attains the semiparametric information bound. The main caveat is reliance on resampling EM; a discussion of how the arguments extend (e.g., via uniform concentration or empirical process tools) to the standard setting would improve rigor-to-practice alignment.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized with helpful algorithms and a clear roadmap. However, notation is dense and many essential definitions (e.g., Tn, w, condition constants) are deferred to the appendix. Bringing a compact “Assumptions at a glance” table into the main text, summarizing initialization requirements, bs selection guidance, and typical magnitudes of the concentration terms for the showcased models, would improve readability.

Originality
High. The integration of truncation-EM with sharp entrywise control and decorrelated inference for latent-variable models appears new. Prior high-dimensional inference methods largely rely on convexity and do not cover EM-estimated latent models.

Significance
High. EM remains a workhorse for latent-variable estimation. Providing near-optimal rates and valid inference under high-dimensional sparsity, with explicit computational considerations, is likely to influence both theory and practice. The approach could stimulate further development for broader latent structures.

Suggestions for improvement (prioritized and feasible)
- Provide theory or at least a clear sketch for single-sample EM, outlining additional uniform concentration steps and any changes in rates.
- Add a practical initialization section with recommended methods per model, with conditions under which they land in the basin.
- Discuss data-driven selection of bs (e.g., cross-validation, information criteria) and present sensitivity analysis.
- Include a small empirical section: synthetic GMM and mixture-of-regressions demonstrating convergence, rate scaling with (n, d, s∗), and coverage/power of the proposed tests; compare to tensor methods and penalized EM without truncation.
- Clarify computational cost of the Dantzig step and Tn estimation; suggest scalable approximations when d is large (e.g., block-diagonal structure, Hessian sketching).
- Improve exposition by consolidating key conditions and their instantiated constants for the two example models in the main text.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, widely used algorithm (EM) in a challenging and practically relevant high-dimensional setting.
- Delivers both optimal-rate estimation and valid inference with semiparametric efficiency.
- Technical contributions are nontrivial and broadly applicable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of empirical validation.
- Main convergence theorem uses a resampling variant; standard single-sample EM guarantees are not provided.
- Practical guidance on initialization and tuning is limited in the main text.

Overall evaluation
A strong theoretical contribution that advances understanding of EM in high dimensions and introduces a compelling inference framework for latent-variable models. Despite the noted limitations, the significance and novelty merit acceptance, especially with minor revisions to address practicality and clarity.
