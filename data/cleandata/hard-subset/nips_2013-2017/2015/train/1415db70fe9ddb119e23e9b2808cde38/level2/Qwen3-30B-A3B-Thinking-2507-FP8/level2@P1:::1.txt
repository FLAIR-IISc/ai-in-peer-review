

This paper presents a significant extension of the EM algorithm to high-dimensional latent variable models, addressing the critical gap between computational feasibility and statistical guarantees in high dimensions. The authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, establishing both geometric convergence rates for parameter estimation and asymptotic normality for a new decorrelated score test. The work is theoretically rigorous and fills a notable void in the literature, as prior EM analyses were confined to low-dimensional settings.

**Strengths**  
1. **Theoretical Novelty and Completeness**: The paper successfully bridges computation and statistics in high dimensions. The truncation step (line 6, Algorithm 1) is a clever innovation that enables sparsity-aware convergence analysis, resolving a key challenge in high-dimensional EM. The decomposition of estimation error into optimization (geometric rate) and statistical (near-optimal $O(\sqrt{s^* \log d / n})$) terms (Theorem 3.4) is elegant and clearly articulated.  
2. **Unified Inference Framework**: The decorrelated score statistic (Section 2.2) is a major contribution, providing the first computationally feasible approach for asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The proof that it achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound (Theorem 4.6) is compelling and addresses a long-standing gap.  
3. **Rigorous Model Coverage**: The authors validate their framework on Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, verifying all technical conditions (Appendix E/F) and demonstrating concrete statistical rates. This avoids the common pitfall of abstract theory without empirical grounding.  
4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-structured. The summary of related work (Section 1) effectively situates the contribution against [2, 18, 30] and distinguishes it from tensor methods [1, 9] and Lasso-based inference [13, 26]. Notation is consistent, and key lemmas (e.g., Theorem 2.1) are well-motivated.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Computational Cost of Truncation**: While the truncation step enables sparsity, the paper does not discuss its computational overhead. For large $d$, computing $\text{supp}(\cdot, b_s)$ at each iteration (line 6) may dominate runtime. A brief analysis of how $b_s$ scales with $d$ or a comparison to alternative sparsity enforcement (e.g., soft thresholding) would strengthen the practical appeal.  
2. **Sparsity Assumption Sensitivity**: The analysis assumes knowledge of $s^*$ (true sparsity) via $b_s = \Theta(s^*)$ (Condition 3.3, Eq. 3.8). In practice, $s^*$ is unknown. The authors should discuss how $b_s$ could be selected data-adaptively (e.g., via cross-validation) and whether this affects the theoretical guarantees.  
3. **Limited Empirical Validation**: The paper relies on theoretical implications (e.g., "for Gaussian mixtures, $\|\hat{\theta} - \theta^*\| \leq C \sqrt{s^* \log d \log n / n}$"). Adding a small simulation study comparing the proposed EM to tensor methods [9] or gradient EM [23] in high dimensions would bolster the claims. For instance, how does the decorrelated score test perform in finite samples?  

**Significance and Originality**  
This work is highly original: it is the first to provide *simultaneous* statistical and computational guarantees for EM in high dimensions, extending [2] (low-dimensional) to $d \gg n$ regimes. The inference framework (decorrelated score) is particularly impactful, as existing methods for latent variables [15, 16, 24] rely on convex optimization assumptions that do not hold for nonconcave EM objectives. The significance is amplified by the broad applicability to latent variable models (Gaussian mixtures, regression mixtures), which are ubiquitous in practice.

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a strong, technically sound contribution to the NIPS community. The theoretical advances are substantial, the writing is clear, and the framework is broadly applicable. The weaknesses noted are minor and could be addressed in revision (e.g., adding a brief discussion of $b_s$ selection or a small simulation). The paper meets all NIPS criteria:  
- *Quality*: Rigorous proofs, well-justified conditions, and thorough model-specific verification.  
- *Clarity*: Excellent organization and notation.  
- *Originality*: Novel truncation-based EM and inference framework for high dimensions.  
- *Significance*: Solves a fundamental problem in scalable statistical inference for latent models.  

**Minor Suggestions for Revision**  
1. In Section 3.1, clarify whether $b_s$ must be *exactly* $\Theta(s^*)$ or if a mild overestimation (e.g., $b_s = O(s^* \log d)$) suffices.  
2. In the abstract, emphasize that the decorrelated score test is *the first* for high-dimensional latent variable models (currently phrased as "for low-dimensional components," which is less precise).  
3. Discuss the impact of the truncation step on the basin of attraction $B$ (Condition 3.2) in the context of initialization.  

Overall, this is a top-tier contribution that advances both theory and practice. I strongly recommend acceptance.
