Summary
The paper develops a general theoretical framework for EM in high-dimensional latent variable models. It proposes a truncated EM algorithm (E-step/M-step followed by a hard-thresholding “T-step”) that enforces sparsity along the iterate path. With suitable initialization and mild population smoothness/concavity and stability assumptions, the algorithm converges geometrically to an estimator achieving (near-)minimax-optimal ℓ2 error on the order of sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to log n), for both an exact M-step and a gradient-ascent M-step. Beyond point estimation, the paper introduces a “decorrelated score” test (via a Dantzig-selector-based orthogonalization) and proves asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, where the technical conditions are verified and the claimed rates derived.

Relation to prior work
The work extends the population-to-sample EM theory of Balakrishnan et al. (2014/2017) from low-dimensional settings to high-dimensional ones by incorporating sparsity via a truncation step and deriving entrywise statistical error bounds. For inference, it adapts debiasing/desparsification ideas (Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.; Javanmard & Montanari) to latent-variable likelihoods using an EM-based Fisher/Louis identity to construct an approximately orthogonal score. Compared to tensor methods for latent models, the proposed approach yields sharper (near-optimal) rates under sparsity and handles inference.

Strengths
- Clear, unified analysis yielding simultaneous computational (geometric contraction) and statistical guarantees with near-optimal rates for high-dimensional latent models.
- Handles both exact and gradient M-steps with explicit contraction constants and sample error terms; careful decomposition into optimization vs. statistical error.
- Inference contribution is strong: decorrelated score/Wald tests with provable asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency under high-dimensional nuisance.
- Verification of conditions for two canonical latent models (Gaussian mixtures, mixtures of regressions), including explicit rates.
- Technically nontrivial handling of the truncation step via entrywise (ℓ∞/ℓ1) statistical control rather than coarser ℓ2 bounds.

Weaknesses
- Theory is developed for a resampling version of EM (fresh data per iteration) to simplify analysis; the practical single-sample iteration is not analyzed in the main results. A discussion of how to transfer rates to the standard setting (beyond appendix pointers) would strengthen the work.
- Requires a good initialization within a basin of attraction; practical, provably good initializers are not fully specified (e.g., spectral/tensor or method-of-moments initializations), and the sensitivity to initialization is not empirically assessed.
- The sparsity parameter bs must be set of order s*. Guidance for choosing bs in practice (when s* is unknown) is not fully developed; hard-thresholding can be brittle to mis-specification.
- The inference step assumes sparsity of w* (a nodewise-style assumption on the Fisher information) and requires solving a Dantzig program based on Tn(β) that involves conditional expectations/second derivatives under latent structure; computational feasibility and numerical stability are not empirically demonstrated.
- Empirical results are absent in the provided version. Even a small-scale study demonstrating finite-sample behavior (rates, robustness to tuning, coverage of CIs) would improve the paper’s practical relevance.

Quality
- Technical development is careful, with clear separation of population contraction, sample perturbation, and truncation effects. Conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity-smoothness, statistical error) are standard in spirit and checked for two models.
- The key EM identities (score and Fisher via Q) are correctly invoked (Fisher/Louis identities). The resulting decorrelated score is sound and achieves semiparametric efficiency.
- One concern is the reliance on entrywise control of Mn vs. M uniform over s-sparse inputs (Condition 3.3), which can be nontrivial to verify beyond the showcased models; the generality claim should be tempered.

Clarity
- Overall well organized and readable despite heavy notation. The algorithmic steps, assumptions, and theorem statements are clear.
- Some notation and constants could be streamlined; practical guidance for tuning (bs, stepsize, Dantzig tolerance) should be consolidated.
- The references list appears garbled in places and should be cleaned.

Originality
- Novel synthesis: a truncated high-dimensional EM with rigorous computational-statistical guarantees, and a new EM-based decorrelated score for inference in latent models. Goes beyond prior EM theory and beyond existing high-dimensional inference confined to convex GLMs.

Significance
- Addresses an important gap: statistically optimal estimation and valid inference for high-dimensional latent variable models using a computationally feasible algorithm. Likely to influence future work on nonconvex high-dimensional estimation and post-selection inference under latent structure.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical experiments for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions: (i) estimation error vs. n, d, s*, (ii) effect of initialization, (iii) robustness to bs mis-specification, (iv) coverage and power of the decorrelated tests vs. de-biased Lasso baselines (where applicable).
- Discuss practical initialization strategies with citations and, if possible, theoretical conditions linking those initializers to the required basin.
- Offer data-driven rules for choosing bs (e.g., BIC-style, stability selection, cross-validation proxies) and the Dantzig tuning parameter; comment on computational cost.
- Extend the theory (even partially) to the standard (non-resampled) EM under mixing/time-uniform concentration, or include a clear theorem if available in the appendix.
- Clarify the feasibility of computing Tn(β) in complex latent models and provide implementation details.
- Improve the related work section: include more precise citations to debiasing/desparsification literature and recent EM analyses; fix reference formatting.

Minor comments
- Several typographical/formatting issues in references and symbols (e.g., subscripts/superscripts, corrupted author names) should be corrected.
- Define all norms and constants upon first use and ensure consistent notation for vectors/matrices.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical contributions on both estimation and inference with near-optimal guarantees.
- Broad potential impact on high-dimensional latent variable modeling.
- Careful and general analysis applicable to multiple EM variants.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of empirical validation.
- Resampling analysis gap vs. practical EM.
- Sensitivity to initialization and tuning not fully addressed.

Overall recommendation: Leaning accept for strong theory; adding experiments and clarifying practical aspects would make this a clear accept. Reviewer confidence: high on theory, moderate on practical relevance without experiments.
