This paper presents a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings (d >> n), providing guarantees for both parameter estimation and statistical inference. The primary contributions are twofold. First, the authors propose a high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation step to enforce sparsity, proving that with proper initialization, it converges geometrically to an estimator with a (near-)minimax optimal statistical rate. Second, they develop a decorrelated score test based on this estimator, establishing its asymptotic normality and enabling hypothesis testing for low-dimensional components of the parameter vector. This work significantly extends previous analyses of the EM algorithm, such as Balakrishnan et al. (2017), from the low-dimensional to the high-dimensional regime.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and important problem. While the EM algorithm is widely used, its theoretical properties in high dimensions have been poorly understood. This work provides the first, to my knowledge, unified framework for both optimal point estimation and valid asymptotic inference for the EM algorithm in this challenging setting. The results are highly significant, filling a major gap between theory and practice for a broad class of latent variable models.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and technically impressive. The authors carefully lay out the necessary assumptions and provide a detailed roadmap for proving their main claims regarding computational convergence, statistical error rates, and asymptotic normality. The framework is general and its application to specific models like Gaussian mixtures is clearly demonstrated.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the complete absence of experiments. While the theoretical contributions are strong, a simulation study would be invaluable to validate the results. For example, demonstrating the empirical convergence rate, the finite-sample performance of the score test, and the sensitivity to initialization would greatly strengthen the paper’s practical impact.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, but the theoretical conditions are abstract. While verified in the appendix, providing more intuition in the main text about when these conditions (e.g., Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness) hold would improve readability.
*   **Practicality:** The reliance on a good initialization, while standard in non-convex optimization theory, remains a practical challenge that is not fully addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel and highly significant theoretical foundation for the EM algorithm in high dimensions.
2.  Offers a unified framework for both estimation and inference, which is a major advance.
3.  The technical analysis is sound and of high quality.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The lack of any experimental results makes it difficult to assess the practical implications and robustness of the proposed method.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper with significant contributions. Despite the lack of experiments, the importance and novelty of the theoretical results merit its acceptance. The authors are strongly encouraged to include a simulation study in the final version to complement their excellent theoretical work.
