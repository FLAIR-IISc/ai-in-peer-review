This paper studies EM for high-dimensional latent variable models and makes two main claims: (i) a truncated high-dimensional EM algorithm that, with suitable initialization, converges geometrically and achieves (near-)minimax optimal estimation error O(√(s* log d / n)); (ii) a computationally feasible inferential procedure based on a decorrelated score that yields asymptotically normal tests for low-dimensional components and achieves the semiparametric information bound. The work builds on and extends recent population-to-sample EM analyses in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) by adding a truncation step to enforce sparsity throughout the iterations, and connects to high-dimensional inference via debiasing/desparsification ideas (e.g., van de Geer et al., related Dantzig-type constructions). The theory covers both exact and gradient M-steps and is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Quality: The technical development is careful, with clear conditions separating optimization and statistical errors. Theorem 3.4 provides finite-sample, iteration-wise bounds under Lipschitz/strong concavity and an ℓ1-based statistical error control tailored to the truncation step. The inference result (Theorem 4.6) is well-motivated via a link to the likelihood score and Fisher information, and achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound. However, empirical validation is absent; the practicality of initialization, tuning bs, and solving Dantzig programs is not assessed.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized, though notation is heavy. Some references appear incomplete or malformed. The role and practicality of the resampling version used for analysis could be explained more prominently, along with guidance for choosing bs and the step size.

Originality: The truncation-augmented EM with rigorous high-dimensional rates and the first inferential guarantees for latent variable models via EM are novel. The combination of optimization analysis with semiparametric-efficient inference is a notable advance over prior EM or desparsified-GLM work.

Significance: If the assumptions are reasonably met in practice, the results could influence both theory and methodology for high-dimensional latent variable modeling.

Limitations and suggestions:
- Provide initialization strategies with guarantees or empirical evidence (e.g., spectral/tensor warm starts).
- Add synthetic and real-data experiments comparing to ℓ1-penalized EM/gradient-EM and tensor methods; include sensitivity to bs, initialization quality, and sample splitting.
- Offer data-driven tuning (cross-validation, information criteria) and discuss computational cost of the Dantzig step.
- Improve reference completeness and clarify conditions (e.g., sparsity of w*, basin size, required SNR).

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, general theoretical contributions bridging estimation and inference.
- Near-optimal rates and semiparametric efficiency.
- Applicable to canonical latent-variable models; supports both exact and gradient M-steps.

Cons:
- No experiments; practical impact unclear.
- Potentially strong assumptions (initialization, w* sparsity, tuning near s*).
- Some clarity and citation issues.
