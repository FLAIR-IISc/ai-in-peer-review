This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for parameter estimation and statistical inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm," which modifies the classic EM algorithm by adding a truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity. They prove that under certain conditions, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate. Second, they develop a "decorrelated score test" for performing hypothesis tests on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. They establish the asymptotic normality of their proposed test statistic, showing that it achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. The proposed framework is general and its effectiveness is illustrated on high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixtures of regression.

This work builds upon the recent line of research that provides statistical guarantees for the EM algorithm in low-dimensional settings, particularly the work of Balakrishnan et al. (2014). It significantly extends this prior work to the more challenging high-dimensional regime (d >> n). The introduction of the truncation step is the key algorithmic modification that enables this extension. Furthermore, the paper tackles the difficult problem of statistical inference, drawing inspiration from methods like the debiased Lasso developed for convex models, but adapting them to the non-trivial setting where the estimator is a local optimum from a non-convex procedure.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of great importance. The EM algorithm is one of the most widely used algorithms in statistics and machine learning, yet its theoretical properties in the high-dimensional setting were largely unknown. This paper provides the first comprehensive framework for both computationally efficient, statistically optimal estimation and valid asymptotic inference for a broad class of high-dimensional latent variable models. This is a substantial contribution that could have a high impact on both theory and practice.
*   **Originality:** The contributions are highly original. The high-dimensional EM algorithm with its iterative truncation step is a novel and intuitive adaptation. More impressively, the development of a valid inferential procedure for the output of an EM algorithm in a non-convex, high-dimensional setting is a major conceptual advance. It successfully bridges the gap between the theory of high-dimensional inference (often for convex M-estimators) and the practice of using iterative algorithms for latent variable models.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper appears to be technically very strong. The authors provide a rigorous and unified analysis that simultaneously characterizes the computational and statistical properties of their proposed methods. The main theorems are powerful, establishing geometric convergence for the algorithm and asymptotic normality for the inference procedure. The decomposition of the estimation error into optimization and statistical components is clear and insightful. The technical conditions are laid out formally and then verified for the specific model examples.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Initialization:** The theoretical guarantees rely on a suitable initialization that is already within a certain radius of the true parameter. This is a common requirement for non-convex optimization analysis, but it remains a strong assumption. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of practical methods for obtaining such an initialization. While a full theoretical analysis of an initialization procedure might be beyond the scope of this work, acknowledging this challenge and pointing to potential heuristics or methods (e.g., spectral methods) would be very helpful for the reader and for practitioners wishing to apply this method.
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are significant enough to stand on their own, the inclusion of even a small simulation study would have made the results more concrete and compelling. For example, simulations could verify the geometric convergence rate, show that the estimator's error scales as predicted (with s, d, n), and demonstrate that the confidence intervals constructed from the decorrelated score test achieve the nominal coverage level.
*   **Clarity on Assumptions:** The paper relies on a number of complex technical conditions. While this is unavoidable for a result of this nature, the intuition behind some of them could be slightly better explained in the main text. For instance, Assumption 4.5 involves several conditions on the Fisher information matrix and scaling requirements for the sample size. A brief, more intuitive explanation of what these conditions mean for a practitioner (e.g., the condition `s_w* = o(sqrt(n/log d))` implies a sparsity assumption on the inverse Fisher information) would improve readability.
*   **Tuning Parameters:** The proposed algorithm and inference procedure rely on tuning parameters, such as the sparsity level `bs` and the Dantzig selector parameter `lambda`. The theory provides guidance on their scaling, but a discussion of how these might be selected in practice (e.g., using cross-validation or other data-driven methods) would enhance the practical relevance of the work.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes fundamental contributions to the theory of the EM algorithm and high-dimensional statistics. The development of a unified, computationally feasible framework for both optimal estimation and valid inference in high-dimensional latent variable models is a significant achievement. The weaknesses are minor in comparison to the strengths and mostly relate to providing more practical guidance and empirical context for the powerful theoretical results. The paper is well-written, the results are important, and it clearly advances the state of the art. I strongly recommend acceptance.
