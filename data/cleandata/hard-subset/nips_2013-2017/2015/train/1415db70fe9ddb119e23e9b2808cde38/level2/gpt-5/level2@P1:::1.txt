Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops a general theory for EM in high-dimensional latent-variable models. It introduces a high-dimensional EM algorithm that augments the standard E/M-steps with a truncation step (T-step) that enforces sparsity by keeping the bs largest coordinates. Under suitable initialization and population regularity conditions, the algorithm converges geometrically to a sparse estimator that achieves a near-minimax statistical error rate of order sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (up to log n factors). The analysis covers both exact maximization and gradient-ascent implementations of the M-step, providing simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees.

Beyond estimation, the paper proposes a decorrelated score-based inferential procedure for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, proving asymptotic normality with variance achieving the semiparametric information bound. The methodology is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, with model-specific verification of the conditions and explicit rate statements.

This work extends the low-dimensional EM theory of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) ([2] in the paper) to the high-dimensional regime, borrowing the “population-to-sample” perspective and geometric convergence analysis, but adding sparsity-enforcing truncation and a finer entrywise statistical control needed to track supports. The inferential component builds on ideas from de-biasing/desparsification in high-dimensional GLMs (e.g., Javanmard & Montanari; van de Geer et al.), but adapts them to latent-variable models by leveraging EM-specific score/Fisher identities.

Strengths:
- Conceptual contribution: A clean and general augmentation of EM with T-steps that is both computationally simple and theoretically tractable; connects sparse estimation with EM iterates.
- Theory: Clear decomposition of optimization vs. statistical error; geometric convergence within a basin; near-optimal rates for a broad class of models.
- Inference: Decorrelated score statistic with asymptotic normality achieving semiparametric efficiency for low-dimensional components despite high-dimensional nuisance parameters; to my knowledge, this is new for EM-based estimators in latent-variable models.
- Generality: Covers exact and gradient M-step implementations; unified conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, smoothness/strong concavity, and entrywise sample-to-population M-step error) that are verified for Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions.
- Careful attention to high-dimensional specifics: entrywise concentration, support evolution under truncation, and conditions ensuring the iterates remain in a basin.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Initialization: Guarantees require an initializer within a constant fraction of the true parameter in ℓ2. While common in EM analyses, practical strategies for obtaining such initializers in high dimension (beyond citing existing spectral/tensor or method-of-moments initializers) should be made more explicit and tailored to the models considered. For Gaussian mixtures, the required SNR condition (||θ*||/σ ≥ r) appears strong; a discussion of the dependence of r on d and n would help.
- Resampling vs. practical algorithm: Main estimation theory is proved for a resampling (fresh-sample) version to ease dependence issues across iterations. Please clarify whether analogous bounds (perhaps with extra log factors) hold for the single-sample iterative algorithm used in practice, and summarize the key technical obstacles and any partial results.
- Choice of bs: Theory assumes bs is of the same order as s*. In practice s* is unknown. How sensitive are rates and convergence to over/under-specification? The bounds suggest a √bs dependence; a simple data-driven rule (e.g., path over bs with validation or information criteria) and guidance on safe over-specification would be useful. Can the method adapt to s*?
- Assumption on w* sparsity: The inference requires sparsity of w* (the solution to a Fisher information regression) with s*w = o(√(n/log d)). This can be restrictive and model-dependent. Please provide more intuition/examples for when this holds in Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, and whether alternative constructions (e.g., nodewise regressions, ridge-regularized debiasing) could relax it.
- Computational aspects of inference: The Dantzig selector for w can be computationally heavy in very high d. Discuss practical solvers, complexity per iteration, and whether a lasso-type approximation with theoretical guarantees is feasible.
- Extra log n factor: The estimation rate includes an extra log n. Is this an artifact of resampling/union bounds across iterations? Could one remove it via stopping-time selection or refined concentration?
- Empirical evaluation: The paper would be strengthened by simulations validating (i) the geometric optimization behavior, (ii) the estimation rates vs. d, n, s*, and (iii) coverage and power of the decorrelated score test, compared to alternatives (penalized likelihood + debiasing, tensor methods, or EM without truncation).

Quality:
The arguments are technically careful and consistent with the literature’s standards (population-to-sample EM analyses, high-dimensional concentration). The identities relating Qn and Tn to the score/Fisher are standard but used effectively to enable inference with EM-based estimators. Conditions are numerous but interpretable; model-specific instantiations are appreciated.

Clarity:
Overall well organized, though notation is dense. The algorithm boxes help. It would help to:
- Summarize at a glance all key constants and their roles; isolate where each assumption is used.
- Provide a brief “practical recipe” for tuning bs, stepsize, and Dantzig parameter, with defaults.

Originality:
High. Extends EM theory to high-dimensional sparse settings with a simple truncation mechanism and provides the first inference procedure with semiparametric efficiency guarantees for EM in latent-variable models.

Significance:
High. The approach bridges a real gap between computationally tractable local EM iterates and statistically optimal estimation/inference in high dimensions, with potential impact across many latent-variable models.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Novel and general high-dimensional EM framework with strong computational/statistical guarantees.
- First asymptotic inference (decorrelated score) for EM-based estimators in latent variable models, achieving semiparametric efficiency.
- Near-optimal estimation rates; geometric convergence; covers exact and gradient M-steps.
- Solid comparison to and extension of recent EM theory and high-dimensional inference literature.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Reliance on nontrivial initialization and strong SNR/basin conditions without fully practical guidance.
- Analysis primarily for a resampling version; practical single-sample guarantees not front-and-center.
- Sparsity assumption on w* may be restrictive; inference requires solving a high-dimensional Dantzig problem.
- Lack of empirical validation.

Suggestions for the authors:
- Provide concrete initialization strategies and SNR/separation requirements per model with citations, and discuss robustness if initialization is outside the basin.
- State a theorem (even with looser constants) for the standard non-resampling algorithm, or clearly delineate when the resampling assumption can be dropped.
- Add simulations to confirm rates and inference, and to explore sensitivity to bs mis-specification.
- Discuss computational choices for the Dantzig step and possible lasso-based alternatives.
- Clarify whether support recovery properties (in addition to estimation error) can be obtained under standard beta-min conditions.

Overall evaluation:
A strong theoretical contribution with clear novelty and potential impact. With added empirical validation and clarified practical guidance, this would make a compelling NIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance.
