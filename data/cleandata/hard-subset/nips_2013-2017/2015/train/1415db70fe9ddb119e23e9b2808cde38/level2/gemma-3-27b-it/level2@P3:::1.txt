This paper proposes a high-dimensional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with theoretical guarantees for parameter estimation and hypothesis testing in latent variable models. The core contribution lies in a truncation step incorporated into the EM algorithm to enforce sparsity, enabling statistical rate of convergence and asymptotic normality in regimes where the dimension exceeds the sample size. The work builds upon previous results establishing convergence of EM in low dimensions ([2, 18, 25, 30]) and addresses the gap in theoretical understanding when d > n.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important problem. The proposed algorithm and the accompanying theoretical analysis represent a significant contribution to the field. The derivation of statistical rates and the establishment of asymptotic normality for hypothesis testing are particularly noteworthy. The generality of the framework, demonstrated through applications to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, strengthens the impact of the work.  The presentation is generally clear, and the mathematical derivations seem rigorous (though a detailed check requires significant effort).

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. First, while the authors acknowledge related work, the connection to recent developments in high-dimensional statistics (e.g., post-selection inference, debiasing techniques for Lasso) could be strengthened. More explicit discussion of how the proposed framework differs from and complements these approaches would be valuable. The references to these related techniques feel somewhat cursory. Second, the conditions required for the theoretical guarantees (Conditions 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) are numerous and complex. A more intuitive explanation of these conditions and their implications would improve readability. The sensitivity analysis—how the results change with violations of these conditions—is not discussed.  Third, the constants hidden within the asymptotic notation (e.g., C, C0) are not sufficiently discussed. While standard in theoretical statistics, acknowledging their potential impact on practical performance would be beneficial.  Fourth, while the paper mentions the computational cost of the truncation step is “reasonable,” a more detailed discussion of the algorithm's complexity and scalability would be helpful.  Fifth, the paper lacks any empirical evaluation. Including simulations to demonstrate the performance of the algorithm and validate the theoretical results would significantly strengthen the contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel approach to high-dimensional EM with strong theoretical guarantees.
*   Addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding the statistical properties of EM in high dimensions.
*   Clear presentation of the algorithm and mathematical derivations.
*   Generality of the framework, demonstrated through applications to multiple models.
*   Establishment of asymptotic normality for hypothesis testing.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited discussion of connections to related work in high-dimensional statistics.
*   Complex and numerous conditions for theoretical guarantees.
*   Lack of sensitivity analysis regarding the conditions.
*   Opaque constants in asymptotic notation.
*   Absence of empirical evaluation.
*   Limited discussion of computational cost beyond stating it’s “reasonable.”

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound solution to a challenging problem.
*   The results significantly advance the understanding of EM algorithms in high-dimensional settings.
*   The work has the potential to impact researchers and practitioners working with latent variable models.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of empirical validation raises concerns about the practical applicability of the algorithm.
*   The complex conditions and opaque constants may limit the usefulness of the theoretical results.
*   The limited discussion of related work could lead to overlooking potential connections and improvements.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong theoretical contribution with the potential for significant impact. However, the lack of empirical evaluation and the complexity of the theoretical framework raise some concerns.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion of related work, focusing on connections to recent developments in high-dimensional statistics.
2.  Provide a more intuitive explanation of the key conditions, possibly with examples.
3.  Discuss the sensitivity of the results to violations of the conditions.
4.  Acknowledge and discuss the potential impact of the hidden constants on practical performance.
5.  Include simulations to validate the theoretical results and demonstrate the algorithm's performance.
6.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the algorithm's computational complexity.
