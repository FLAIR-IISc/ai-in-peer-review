- Paper develops a unified computational-statistical theory for truncated-EM (T-step after M-step) in high-dimensional latent-variable models, proving geometric convergence to an s*-sparse estimator with optimization and statistical error decomposition.
- Proposes a decorrelated score using a Dantzig-type estimator for nuisance projection w, proving asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency for low-dimensional components.
- Instantiates theory for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions with explicit conditions (e.g., SNR bounds).
- Extends low-dimensional EM analyses to high-dimensional sparse settings and connects to high-dimensional inference literature, handling nonconvex latent-variable challenges.
- Conceptual advance: simple, practical EM modification (truncation) yielding geometric contraction and near-optimal high-dimensional rates.
- New inferential framework: achieves semiparametric efficiency despite high-dimensional nuisance and nonconvexity.
- Generality: unified treatment of exact and gradient M-steps under common local concavity/smoothness and Lipschitz conditions.
- Technical novelty: entrywise control of population–sample M-step error and tracking of support evolution due to truncation.
- Clear separation of optimization vs. statistical errors with explicit s*, d, n dependence.
- Main convergence theorem is proved for a resampling EM variant (fresh data per iteration), which is nonstandard and weakens practical claims.
- Guarantees require initialization in a local basin and SNR conditions; practical initialization methods and sample complexity are only briefly discussed.
- Theory assumes knowledge of truncation level bs ~ s*; no procedures or robustness analysis for selecting bs are provided.
- Model verification is limited to two canonical models; extensions to other latent models are suggested but not verified.
- Computational aspects of inference (computing Tn, tuning λ, numerical stability) are not empirically addressed.
- Paper appears theory-only; lacks finite-sample experiments to validate rates, robustness, and testing performance versus alternatives.
- Theoretical development is careful and substantial, with plausible assumptions and connection of EM quantities to Fisher information.
- Reliance on resampling EM is the main technical caveat; extension arguments to single-sample EM would strengthen rigor.
- Presentation is generally clear but notation is dense and many definitions are deferred to the appendix.
- Recommends adding an "Assumptions at a glance" table and consolidated guidance on initialization and bs selection to improve readability.
- Originality is high: novel integration of truncation-EM, entrywise control, and decorrelated inference for latent models.
- Significance is high: provides near-optimal estimation and valid inference for EM in high-dimensional sparse settings, likely to influence theory and practice.
- Suggested improvements: theory/sketch for single-sample EM, practical initialization guidance with conditions, data-driven bs selection and sensitivity analysis.
- Suggested improvements: add synthetic experiments (GMM and regressions) comparing to tensor methods and penalized EM; demonstrate convergence and test performance.
- Suggested improvements: clarify computational cost of Dantzig step and Tn estimation and propose scalable approximations (e.g., block-diagonal, sketching).
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important algorithm (EM) in high-dimensional settings, delivers optimal-rate estimation and semiparametric-efficient inference, and contributes nontrivial technical advances.
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of empirical validation, reliance on resampling EM for main theorem, and limited practical guidance on initialization and tuning.
- Overall: strong and novel theoretical contribution that merits acceptance subject to minor revisions addressing practicality and clarity.
