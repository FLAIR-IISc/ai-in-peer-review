- Paper claims a truncated high-dimensional EM that, with suitable initialization, converges geometrically and attains near-minimax error O(√(s* log d / n)).  
- Paper claims a computationally feasible inferential procedure using a decorrelated score that yields asymptotically normal tests for low-dimensional components and reaches the semiparametric information bound.  
- Builds on population-to-sample EM analyses by adding truncation to enforce sparsity and connects to debiasing/desparsification literature for high-dimensional inference.  
- Theory covers both exact and gradient M-steps and is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.  
- Technical development is careful, separating optimization and statistical errors, with finite-sample, iteration-wise bounds under Lipschitz/strong concavity.  
- Theorem 3.4 gives ℓ1-based statistical error control tailored to the truncation step; Theorem 4.6 links the inference to likelihood score and Fisher information achieving semiparametric efficiency.  
- Empirical validation is absent, so practicality of initialization, tuning of bs, and solving Dantzig programs is unassessed.  
- Paper is generally well organized but uses heavy notation and has some incomplete/malformed references.  
- The role and practicality of the resampling version, and guidance for choosing bs and step size, need clearer explanation.  
- The truncation-augmented EM and first EM-based inferential guarantees for latent variable models are novel contributions.  
- Combining optimization analysis with semiparametric-efficient inference is a notable advance over prior EM or desparsified-GLM work.  
- If assumptions hold in practice, the results could significantly impact theory and methodology for high-dimensional latent variable modeling.  
- Suggested additions: initialization strategies with guarantees or empirical evidence (e.g., spectral/tensor warm starts).  
- Suggested additions: synthetic and real-data experiments comparing to ℓ1-penalized EM/gradient-EM and tensor methods, including sensitivity analyses.  
- Suggested additions: data-driven tuning procedures (cross-validation, information criteria) and discussion of Dantzig-step computational cost.  
- Suggested clarifications: complete references and clearer statement of conditions (sparsity of w*, basin size, required SNR).  
- Pros: strong, general theoretical contributions, near-optimal rates, semiparametric efficiency, applicability to canonical latent-variable models, supports exact and gradient M-steps.  
- Cons: no experiments so practical impact is unclear, potentially strong assumptions (initialization, w* sparsity, tuning near s*), and some clarity/citation issues.
