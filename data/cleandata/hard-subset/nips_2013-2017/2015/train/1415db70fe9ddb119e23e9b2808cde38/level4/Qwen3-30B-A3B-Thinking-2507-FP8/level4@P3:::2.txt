

This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure for latent variable models. The authors make two main contributions: (i) a high-dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step that achieves near-optimal statistical rates of convergence (p(s* log d/n)), and (ii) a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters. The paper establishes theoretical guarantees for both estimation and inference under a unified framework.

The work builds upon recent advances in EM algorithm analysis (e.g., [2, 18, 25, 30]) but addresses a significant gap: existing guarantees apply only to low-dimensional settings where d ≪ n, while high-dimensional regimes (d ≫ n) lack theoretical foundations. The authors extend the EM framework to handle high dimensions by introducing a sparsity-enforcing truncation step in both E and M steps. This approach differs fundamentally from prior work in two key aspects: (1) it establishes a (near-)minimax optimal rate of convergence (p(s* log d/n) vs. p(d/n) in low dimensions), and (2) it provides the first computationally feasible approach for asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The paper also properly contextualizes its contributions against related work on debiasing methods for Lasso [13, 26, 32] and tensor methods [1, 9], which are limited to low dimensions or suboptimal rates.

The paper's strengths are significant. **Quality** is excellent: the theoretical framework is rigorous, with well-structured conditions (3.1-3.3) and thorough proofs in the appendix. The convergence rate (3.10) cleanly decomposes optimization and statistical errors, and the asymptotic normality result (Theorem 4.6) achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound, which is a strong theoretical contribution. **Originality** is high: the paper addresses a clear gap in the literature and introduces a novel algorithmic framework (with truncation step) that enables high-dimensional inference where none existed before. The connection to existing EM literature is well-maintained, and the comparison to [2] is particularly insightful regarding the statistical rate improvement. **Significance** is strong: the proposed framework applies to a broad family of latent variable models (Gaussian mixtures, mixture of regressions), and the results advance both computation (geometric convergence) and statistical theory (asymptotic inference) in high dimensions. The practical implications for statistical inference in high-dimensional settings are compelling.

**Clarity** is the paper's main weakness. While the mathematical exposition is technically sound, the presentation is dense and challenging to follow, particularly in Sections 3 and 4. The truncation step (line 6 of Algorithm 1) is critical but not sufficiently motivated; the authors should clarify why this step is necessary (e.g., without it, the estimator would not be sparse, and the statistical rate would degrade to p(d/n)). The connection between the theoretical conditions (3.1-3.3) and the specific latent variable models (Gaussian mixtures, mixture of regressions) is somewhat opaque in the main text, with key verifications relegated to the appendix. The paper could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the decorrelated score test and its advantages over existing debiasing methods.

Two specific technical concerns require clarification: (1) The paper claims the rate p(s* log d/n) is "near-minimax-optimal" (line 066), but the minimax lower bound is not explicitly derived in the main text (it's referenced in [2] but not proven here). The authors should either provide a brief justification or clarify that this rate matches the known lower bound for similar problems. (2) The choice of the sparsity parameter ŝ (in Eq. 3.8) depends on the true sparsity level s*, which is unknown in practice. The paper should discuss practical strategies for selecting ŝ (e.g., cross-validation, data splitting) and how to obtain a suitable initialization satisfying ||β̂^init - β*||_2 ≤ R/2.

The paper's main weakness is its accessibility. While the theoretical contributions are substantial, the dense presentation and lack of intuitive explanations make it difficult for many NIPS readers (who may not be statistical theorists) to grasp the core ideas. The authors should consider adding a more intuitive explanation of the truncation step's necessity and its effect on the convergence behavior, as well as a brief example illustrating the decorrelated score test in action.

In summary, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature on high-dimensional latent variable models. The paper provides the first computationally feasible framework for optimal estimation and inference in high dimensions, with strong theoretical guarantees. While the presentation could be improved for broader accessibility, the technical contributions are significant and well-justified.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity. The authors should address the two technical concerns mentioned above and provide more intuitive explanations of the truncation step and the decorrelated score test. The paper is well-positioned to advance the field of high-dimensional statistical inference for latent variable models, and its theoretical contributions are substantial enough to warrant publication at NIPS.
