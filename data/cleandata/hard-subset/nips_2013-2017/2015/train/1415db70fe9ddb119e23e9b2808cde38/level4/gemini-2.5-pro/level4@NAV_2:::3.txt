This paper presents a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings where the number of parameters `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The primary contributions are twofold: (i) a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates an iterative truncation step to enforce sparsity, and (ii) a decorrelated score statistic for performing asymptotic inference on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. The work builds upon recent theoretical analyses of the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional regime [2], extending the guarantees to the challenging `d >> n` setting. The key innovation is to introduce and analyze a truncation step within the EM iterations to handle the high dimensionality, a departure from standard regularization techniques.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing gap between the widespread practical use of the EM algorithm and the lack of theoretical guarantees for it in high-dimensional latent variable models. Providing the first computationally feasible approach with guarantees for both optimal estimation and asymptotic inference in this setting is a significant contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results are strong. The authors prove that, under appropriate conditions and with a suitable initialization, their proposed algorithm converges at a geometric rate. The final estimator achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`. Furthermore, the proposed decorrelated score statistic is shown to be asymptotically normal and achieves the semiparametric information bound, making it statistically optimal.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly articulates its contributions, assumptions, and main theoretical results. The framework is presented in a general form before being applied to specific models like the Gaussian mixture model, which aids in understanding the scope of the methodology.

**Weaknesses and Points for Improvement:**

*   **Necessity of the Iterative Truncation:** A core element of the proposed method is the truncation (T-step) performed at each iteration. The analysis hinges on controlling the `l_infinity`-norm of the statistical error of the M-step for sparse inputs. However, for models like the Gaussian mixture example, computing the M-step for a sparse vector is not fundamentally different or easier than for a dense one. This prompts the question of whether this iterative hard-thresholding is essential. It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion on why a simpler approach—for instance, running a standard (perhaps regularized) EM algorithm and performing a single truncation only on the final estimate—would not suffice. While a single-shot truncation is known to fail for problems like sparse linear regression, its specific implications within the non-convex EM framework deserve discussion to better motivate the proposed iterative design.
*   **Relation to Broader Literature:** The use of an iterative truncation/hard-thresholding step is a common technique in the broader sparse optimization and online learning literature. It would be beneficial to connect the proposed "T-step" to this existing body of work (e.g., iterative hard thresholding algorithms, or methods like truncated gradients as in Langford et al., 2009) to better contextualize the algorithmic contribution.
*   **Initialization:** The theoretical guarantees rely on an initialization within a certain basin of attraction. For practical utility and reproducibility, the authors should specify how such an initialization can be obtained for the models considered, especially in the experimental section (if applicable in the full version of the paper).

In summary, this paper provides an important and rigorous theoretical analysis for parameter estimation and inference using the EM algorithm in high dimensions. The results are significant and address a challenging open problem. While the motivation for the specific iterative truncation design could be further clarified in relation to simpler alternatives and the broader literature, the overall contribution is substantial.
