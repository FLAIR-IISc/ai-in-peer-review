A sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure is proposed, modifying the classical EM algorithm by truncating the parameter vector at each iteration to a desired sparsity level. Accompanying this is an asymptotically normal statistic, built upon a decorrelated score, for testing the non-zero status of specific parameter vector entries.

The convergence guarantee for the sparse EM states that the estimate converges geometrically to the true value (in the number of iterations), up to a statistical error term. This error term scales as the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step, which is typically logarithmic in the ambient dimension. 

This work builds upon recent analogous results for EM in low-dimensional settings. As in those prior studies, the analysis relies on assumptions regarding the smoothness and near-quadraticity of the Q-function within a specific basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized within this basin. The novel condition here is control over the infinity norm of the error in the M-step estimation. This condition is specifically required for sparse vectors; however, for many models, estimating M over all possible vectors presents no greater computational challenge than estimation over sparse vectors – this holds true for the Gaussian mixture example considered, and for the non-gradient version of the mixture of regression model. This observation raises a pertinent question: is the truncated EM algorithm truly necessary? Could an equivalent guarantee be achieved, for instance, by performing ordinary EM under the same conditions, followed by a single truncation step? EDIT: The authors’ observation regarding linear regression as a special case appears to provide a compelling counterargument to the efficacy of a single truncation step.

The paper would benefit from a discussion of related work in sparse optimization (e.g., “Sparse online learning via truncated gradient” Langford et al., 2009). 

The experimental section should explicitly state the initialization procedure used. In summary, the authors propose a sparse EM variant for parameter estimation in high-dimensional settings. The resulting convergence guarantees represent an important contribution to high-dimensional learning, although the necessity of the sparse variant of EM to achieve these results remains somewhat unclear.
