**Summary**

This paper presents a comprehensive theoretical framework for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a novel "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the standard EM procedure by incorporating a truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity in the parameter iterates. Under certain regularity conditions and with a suitable initialization, this algorithm is proven to converge at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate. Second, building on this estimator, the paper develops a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing on individual components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. This statistic is shown to be asymptotically normal, enabling valid statistical inference. This work significantly extends recent theoretical analyses of the EM algorithm, such as that of Balakrishnan et al. (2014), from the low-dimensional (d << n) to the high-dimensional (d >> n) regime.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental and long-standing problem: providing theoretical guarantees for the widely-used EM algorithm in the high-dimensional setting. Establishing a computationally tractable procedure with simultaneous guarantees on computational efficiency (geometric convergence), statistical optimality (near-minimax rates), and valid inference (asymptotic normality) is a major contribution to the field. These results are likely to be highly influential for both researchers and practitioners working with high-dimensional latent variable models.

2.  **Technical Quality and Originality:** The theoretical analysis is novel and rigorous. Extending the analysis of EM to the high-dimensional regime introduces significant challenges, which the authors address effectively. The key innovation is the iterative truncation step and the associated analysis that carefully tracks the iterate's support and controls entrywise statistical errors (via the L-infinity norm in Condition 3.3). The development of a valid inferential procedure for the local optima produced by an EM-type algorithm is also a substantial and original contribution.

3.  **Generality and Completeness:** The framework is presented in a general form under a clear set of verifiable conditions, and its applicability is demonstrated on important models like Gaussian Mixture Models and Mixture of Regressions. The paper provides a very complete story, moving from algorithmic design to computational and statistical guarantees for point estimation, and finally to uncertainty quantification via hypothesis testing.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Motivation for Iterative Truncation:** A central question is whether the proposed iterative truncation is strictly necessary. Could a similar theoretical guarantee be obtained by running a more standard EM algorithm and applying a single truncation step only to the final estimate? The authors' insight that this fails even in the special case of linear regression is a powerful argument, but this point could be elaborated on more explicitly in the main text. Providing stronger intuition for why iterative hard thresholding is crucial for keeping the iterates within the desired basin of attraction would significantly strengthen the paper's motivation for the novel algorithm.

2.  **Connections to Broader Sparse Optimization Literature:** The proposed algorithm, which interleaves a maximization/gradient step with a truncation (hard thresholding) step, bears a strong resemblance to Iterative Hard Thresholding (IHT) and related projection-based methods in sparse optimization. While the objective function here is more complex due to the latent variables, discussing these connections (e.g., to work like Blumensath & Davies on IHT, or Langford et al., 2009 on truncated gradients) would help place the algorithmic contribution in a broader context and clarify its relationship to existing sparse recovery techniques.

3.  **Clarity of Assumptions and Experimental Details:**
    *   Condition 3.3, which bounds the statistical error of the M-step, is assumed to hold for any sparse input vector. However, for some of the examples considered (e.g., the maximization M-step for the Gaussian mixture model), the M-step operator Mn(θ) is computed without exploiting the sparsity of θ. It would be helpful to discuss if this assumption is a technical convenience for the proof or if it reflects a genuine property that can be easily verified.
    *   The experimental section should specify the method of initialization used. Since the theoretical guarantees are local and depend on starting within a basin of attraction, this detail is essential for reproducibility and for assessing the practical utility of the proposed method.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to an important area of machine learning. It provides the first computationally feasible approach with strong performance guarantees for both estimation and inference using the EM algorithm in high dimensions. While the motivation for the iterative truncation could be further clarified and connections to related work could be strengthened, these points do not detract from the importance of the results. The technical depth and the significance of the contributions make this a clear candidate for acceptance.
