The paper proposes a novel high dimensional EM algorithm that naturally incorporates sparsity structure into parameter estimation for inferring high dimensional latent variable models. The authors establish a general theory of the EM algorithm, including simultaneous statistical and computational guarantees for the proposed high dimensional EM algorithm and the respective uncertainty assessment procedure. The paper makes two main contributions: (i) a novel high dimensional EM algorithm that converges at a geometric rate and attains an estimator with the (near-)optimal statistical rate of convergence, and (ii) a new inferential procedure for testing hypotheses related to low dimensional components of high dimensional parameters.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly [2], which considers the low dimensional regime. The authors extend the results of [2] to the high dimensional setting, where the dimension d is much larger than the sample size n. The paper also applies the work of Ning and Liu [A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models] to the problem of inference.

The strengths of the paper include its ability to establish a general inferential framework for simultaneous parameter estimation and uncertainty assessment based on a novel high dimensional EM algorithm. The paper provides a unified analytic framework for high dimensional EM algorithm and the respective uncertainty assessment procedure. The authors also illustrate the effectiveness of their framework by applying it to two high dimensional latent variable models, namely Gaussian mixture model and mixture of regression model.

However, there are several weaknesses and areas that require further discussion. Firstly, the authors should discuss under what conditions the initial solution, \beta^{\rm init}, can be found to satisfy the requirement in line 60 on page 2. This is crucial because the convergence of the algorithm relies heavily on the choice of the initial solution.

Secondly, the authors propose two algorithms, but there is no practical guidance on which one to use. The authors should provide more discussion on the choice of algorithm and the trade-offs between them.

Thirdly, the choice of tuning parameters, \hat s and \lambda, is crucial in high-dimensional inference. The authors should discuss how to choose these parameters in practice and how sensitive the results are to their choice. Is there a way to choose them in a data-dependent way?

Fourthly, the decorrelated score test is the same as in [A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models]. The authors should cite this fact and discuss what is new in their application of the existing inference procedure in the current context.

Lastly, the main theoretical results hold under a large number of technical conditions. The authors should discuss whether these conditions/assumptions are satisfied for the considered latent variable models.

In terms of the quality of the paper, it is technically sound, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field. However, the paper could benefit from more discussion on the practical implications of their results and the choice of tuning parameters. Additionally, the authors should make it clearer what is exactly novel in this paper and why the application of existing tools is nontrivial.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by establishing a general inferential framework for simultaneous parameter estimation and uncertainty assessment based on a novel high dimensional EM algorithm.
* The authors provide a unified analytic framework for high dimensional EM algorithm and the respective uncertainty assessment procedure.
* The paper illustrates the effectiveness of their framework by applying it to two high dimensional latent variable models.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper relies heavily on the choice of the initial solution, and the authors do not provide sufficient discussion on how to find a suitable initial solution.
* The authors do not provide practical guidance on the choice of algorithm and the trade-offs between them.
* The choice of tuning parameters is crucial, and the authors do not provide sufficient discussion on how to choose them in practice.
* The decorrelated score test is not new, and the authors do not provide sufficient discussion on what is new in their application of the existing inference procedure in the current context.
