

This paper introduces a high-dimensional extension of the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm that incorporates sparsity constraints through a truncation step at each iteration, along with a novel inferential procedure based on decorrelated score statistics for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional parameter components in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors establish both computational and statistical guarantees for their approach, demonstrating geometric convergence of the algorithm and asymptotic normality of the proposed test statistic.

The main contributions align with recent advances in EM theory for latent variable models, particularly extending the work of [2] which established convergence guarantees in low-dimensional settings. The authors appropriately build upon [2]'s framework by incorporating a truncation step to handle high-dimensional challenges, which enables them to establish a near-minimax optimal statistical rate of convergence (p(s* log d/n)) as opposed to the p(d/n) rate in low-dimensional settings. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, covering both parameter estimation and asymptotic inference within a unified framework.

Strengths:
- The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of EM in high-dimensional settings, where existing guarantees do not apply.
- The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm provides the first computationally feasible approach for optimal parameter estimation in high-dimensional latent variable models.
- The theoretical guarantees are rigorous, with careful attention to both optimization error (geometric convergence) and statistical error (near-optimal rate).
- The decorrelated score statistic achieves the semiparametric information bound, demonstrating theoretical optimality for inference in high dimensions.
- The framework is general and successfully applied to two important high-dimensional latent variable models (Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression).

Weaknesses:
- The necessity of the truncation step at each iteration is not fully justified; while the authors provide a counterargument to using a single post-EM truncation step, this point could be strengthened with additional theoretical or empirical evidence.
- The connection to existing sparse optimization literature (e.g., Langford et al. 2009 on "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient") is not adequately discussed.
- The experimental section lacks details about the initialization procedure, which is crucial for the algorithm's performance and theoretical guarantees.
- The paper does not sufficiently address whether the truncation step is truly necessary or whether similar guarantees could be achieved through alternative approaches.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs are detailed and address both computational and statistical aspects. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach and carefully characterize the conditions under which their guarantees hold.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout the paper.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by extending EM theory to high-dimensional settings with sparsity constraints, which has not been previously established. The truncation step is a novel technical innovation that enables the authors to handle high-dimensional challenges, differentiating this work from previous low-dimensional EM analyses.

Significance: The results are highly significant as they provide the first computationally feasible approach for optimal parameter estimation and asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. This work will likely influence both theoretical and applied research in high-dimensional statistics and machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper provides a theoretically rigorous solution to an important problem in high-dimensional statistics with clear practical implications. The technical contributions are substantial and well-motivated.

Arguments against acceptance: The necessity of the iterative truncation step is not fully justified, and the connection to related sparse optimization literature is insufficiently addressed. The experimental section lacks critical details about initialization.
