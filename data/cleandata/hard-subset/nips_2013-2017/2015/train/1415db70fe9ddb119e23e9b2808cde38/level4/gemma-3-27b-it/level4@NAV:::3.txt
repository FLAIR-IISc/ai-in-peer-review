A novel sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure is presented, which enhances the classical EM algorithm by imposing a sparsity constraint on the parameter vector at each iteration. Accompanying this is an asymptotically normal statistic, constructed using a decorrelated score, designed to test the significance of individual parameter vector elements.

The theoretical guarantee for this sparse EM method establishes geometric convergence to the true parameter value (with respect to iteration number), subject to a statistical error term. This error term scales as the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the error in the M-step – a quantity typically logarithmic in the data dimension. 

This work builds upon recent advancements in EM convergence guarantees for low-dimensional scenarios.  Consistent with prior work, the analysis relies on assumptions regarding the smoothness and near-quadraticity of the Q-function within a defined region of attraction, and initialization within that region. A key new condition centers on controlling the infinity norm of the error in the M-step estimation. While this condition is specifically needed for sparse parameter vectors, the authors note that for many models, estimating M across all possible vectors does not present added complexity compared to sparse vectors (as demonstrated in the Gaussian mixture model and the non-gradient mixture of regression example). This prompts a consideration: is the truncated EM approach genuinely necessary? Could equivalent guarantees be achieved by applying standard EM under similar conditions, followed by a single, final truncation? EDIT: The authors’ demonstration of linear regression as a specific case effectively argues against the sufficiency of a single truncation step.

The manuscript would benefit from a discussion of related work in the broader field of sparse optimization, such as, for instance, Langford et al. (2009) on “Sparse online learning via truncated gradient”.

The experimental results section should explicitly detail the initialization strategy employed. In summary, this paper introduces a sparse EM algorithm for high-dimensional parameter estimation. The established convergence guarantees represent a valuable contribution to the theory of high-dimensional learning, although the unique benefit of the sparse EM variant – as opposed to standard EM with post-processing – remains somewhat open to question.
