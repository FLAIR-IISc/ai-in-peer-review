### Summary

This paper presents a theoretical framework for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models where the true parameter vector is sparse. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a novel "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the standard EM algorithm by incorporating a truncation step after the M-step to enforce sparsity. Under certain conditions, including a sufficiently accurate initialization, this algorithm is shown to converge at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves the near-minimax optimal statistical rate of convergence. Second, the paper develops a procedure for statistical inference based on this estimator. They construct a "decorrelated score statistic" and prove its asymptotic normality, which enables hypothesis testing and the construction of confidence intervals for low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. The authors demonstrate their general framework on two examples: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model.

This work extends the recent line of research that provides theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional setting (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017, cited as [2] in the paper) to the more challenging high-dimensional (d >> n) regime. It also contributes to the high-dimensional inference literature (e.g., for Lasso) by developing a framework suitable for non-convex, EM-based estimators, which is a significant and novel step.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The paper tackles a fundamentally important and difficult problem: providing a computationally tractable algorithm with strong statistical guarantees for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The theoretical contributions are significant and appear to be sound. The establishment of a unified framework that guarantees both near-optimal estimation and valid asymptotic inference for a class of models where the likelihood is non-concave is a major strength. The paper is also well-organized and provides a thorough overview of related work.

Despite these strengths, there are several points that could be clarified to improve the paper's clarity and impact, particularly concerning its practical implications and the motivation for certain design choices.

**Major Points:**

1.  **Practicality of Assumptions and Tuning Parameters:** The theoretical guarantees rely on two critical conditions that may be difficult to satisfy in practice.
    *   **Initialization:** The analysis requires an initial estimate `θ_init` that is already within a certain Euclidean distance of the true parameter `θ*` (line 275: `||θ_init - θ*||_2 < R/2`). This is a common requirement in the analysis of non-convex problems. However, the paper does not discuss how to obtain such an initial estimate. Could the authors comment on potential methods for initialization (e.g., using tensor methods, even if suboptimal, or a different tractable procedure) and whether these methods are likely to satisfy the required condition? Without a clear and practical initialization strategy, the applicability of the theoretical results is limited.
    *   **Sparsity Parameter `bs`:** The choice of the sparsity parameter `bs` in the truncation step, as given in Eq. (3.8), depends on the true sparsity level `s*`, which is unknown in any real application. How should `bs` be chosen in practice? Would a data-driven procedure like cross-validation be feasible, and would the theoretical guarantees still hold?

2.  **Necessity of the Truncation Step:** A central contribution is the introduction of the truncation step (T-step) in Algorithm 1. While the authors state that it "effectively enforces the sparsity," the motivation for this specific mechanism over more standard approaches is not fully clear. For instance, one could enforce sparsity by adding an L1 penalty to the M-step objective function. Could the authors elaborate on what happens if the T-step is omitted? Furthermore, what are the advantages of this hard-thresholding-based truncation step compared to a penalized M-step? A more detailed discussion would help readers understand the importance and novelty of this algorithmic choice.

3.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is very dense, and the theoretical arguments in Sections 3 and 4 are challenging to follow for readers who are not experts in high-dimensional statistics. While the rigor is appreciated, the paper would be more accessible to the broader machine learning audience at NIPS if the authors provided more high-level intuition behind the key conditions (e.g., Conditions 3.1-3.3) and the main steps of the proofs. For example, a brief paragraph explaining intuitively why the `l_inf`-norm is used for the statistical error in Condition 3.3 and how it facilitates the analysis of the truncation step would be very helpful.

**Minor Point:**

4.  **Minimax Optimality:** The introduction claims that the derived statistical rate of `sqrt(s* log(d)/n)` is "(near-)minimax-optimal" (line 66). While this is a well-known result in high-dimensional statistics for related problems (like sparse linear regression), it would strengthen the paper to include a specific citation that establishes this lower bound for the class of latent variable models considered here.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper with impressive results. Addressing the questions above, particularly regarding the practical aspects of initialization and parameter tuning, would significantly enhance its contribution and impact.
