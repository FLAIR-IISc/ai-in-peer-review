A novel sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure is presented, which enhances the classical EM algorithm by enforcing a specified level of sparsity on the parameter vector at each iteration. Accompanying this is an asymptotically normal statistic, constructed using a decorrelated score, designed for hypothesis testing regarding the non-zero status of individual parameter entries.

The theoretical guarantee for the sparse EM algorithm establishes geometric convergence to the true parameter value (with respect to iteration number), subject to a statistical error term. This error term scales with the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the estimation error in the M-step – a quantity often logarithmic in the data dimension. 

This work builds upon recent advances in EM convergence analysis within low-dimensional settings. Consistent with prior research, the analysis relies on assumptions of sufficient smoothness and near-quadraticity of the Q-function within a defined basin of attraction, alongside initialization within that basin. A key new condition is control over the infinity norm of the error in the M-step estimation. While this condition is specifically required for sparse parameter vectors, the authors note that for many models, estimating M across all possible vectors presents comparable computational cost to estimating it only for sparse vectors (as demonstrated in the Gaussian mixture and non-gradient mixture of regression examples). This observation prompts consideration of whether the truncated EM algorithm is strictly necessary; could equivalent guarantees be achieved through standard EM, followed by a single truncation step? *Note:* The authors’ demonstration of linear regression as a counterexample provides a strong argument against the sufficiency of a single truncation.

The manuscript would benefit from a discussion of potentially relevant work in sparse optimization, such as Langford et al. (2009) on “Sparse online learning via truncated gradient,” among others. 

Furthermore, the experimental section should explicitly detail the initialization strategy employed. In summary, this paper introduces a sparse EM variant for high-dimensional parameter estimation, and the derived convergence guarantees represent a valuable contribution to the field of high-dimensional learning. However, the necessity of the sparse modification to achieve these guarantees remains an open question.
