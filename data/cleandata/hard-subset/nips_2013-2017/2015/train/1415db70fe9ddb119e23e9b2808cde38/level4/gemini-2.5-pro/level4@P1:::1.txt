### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings, where the number of parameters `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The work makes two primary contributions. First, for parameter estimation, the authors propose a novel "high-dimensional EM algorithm" that incorporates a hard-thresholding step after the standard E-step and M-step to enforce sparsity. Under the assumption of an appropriate initialization, this algorithm is shown to converge at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical rate of convergence. Second, for statistical inference, the paper develops a procedure based on a decorrelated score statistic to test hypotheses and construct confidence intervals for low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. This inferential procedure is proven to be asymptotically normal and to achieve the semiparametric information bound, rendering it statistically optimal. The framework is general, and its utility is demonstrated on high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models. This work effectively extends the low-dimensional analysis of the EM algorithm, such as that in Balakrishnan et al. (2017) [2], to the more challenging high-dimensional regime.

### **Strengths**

*   **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing gap between the widespread practical use of the EM algorithm and the lack of theoretical guarantees for it in modern high-dimensional settings. Providing a unified framework that establishes guarantees for both optimal estimation and valid inference is a significant contribution to the fields of statistical machine learning and high-dimensional statistics.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results presented are strong and comprehensive. The authors establish a geometric rate of convergence for their proposed algorithm and a near-optimal statistical rate (`sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`) for the final estimator, which is a substantial achievement for a non-convex procedure like EM. Furthermore, the development of an asymptotically normal and efficient inferential procedure is a key result that advances the state of the art.
*   **Generality:** The framework is presented in a general form, suggesting it is applicable to a broad class of latent variable models. The analysis is supported with detailed applications to important models, including Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regressions.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

The paper is a strong contribution, but its clarity and practical relevance could be improved by addressing the following points.

1.  **Clarity on Novelty and Connection to Prior Work:** The paper skillfully builds upon several important lines of recent research. The estimation framework extends the analysis of [2] to high dimensions, while the inference procedure employs a decorrelated score test that is very similar to the one developed by Ning and Liu (2017) in "A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models." While combining these ideas is a valuable contribution, the paper's unique technical novelty could be highlighted more clearly. Specifically, the framework by Ning and Liu was developed for estimators that are global optima of convex, penalized loss functions. In contrast, the estimator here is the output of the iterative, non-convex EM procedure, which converges to a local optimum. What were the specific technical challenges in adapting the decorrelation framework to this setting, and how were they overcome? A more detailed discussion of this point would strengthen the paper's originality.

2.  **Practical Guidance and Tuning:** The theoretical guarantees rely on several conditions and tuning parameters whose practical selection is not discussed, which may limit the method's adoption by practitioners.
    *   **Initialization:** The theory requires an initial estimator that is already within a certain radius of the true parameter. This is a common requirement in non-convex optimization, but it is a critical one. Could the authors comment on how such an initial estimator can be obtained in practice for the models they consider? Are there specific procedures (e.g., spectral methods) that can be proven to satisfy the initialization condition?
    *   **Choice of M-step:** The paper analyzes two implementations of the M-step (exact maximization and gradient ascent). Is there any practical guidance or trade-off to consider when choosing between them? For instance, does one offer better computational efficiency for a small cost in statistical performance in typical scenarios?
    *   **Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm requires a sparsity parameter `bs`, and the inference procedure requires a tuning parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector. The choice of these parameters is known to be critical for the performance of high-dimensional methods. How should these be chosen from data in a principled way? The paper would be more impactful if it included a discussion on data-driven selection methods (e.g., cross-validation) and the sensitivity of the results to these choices.

3.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** The main theorems are presented under a set of high-level technical conditions (e.g., Conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.4). While the authors state that these conditions are verified for specific models in the appendix, the main paper would benefit from more intuition about them. Are they mild and expected to hold for a wide range of latent variable models, or are they restrictive? Briefly explaining the role of each assumption and why it is necessary would improve the paper's clarity and help the reader assess the scope and applicability of the results.

4.  **Paper Organization:** The main paper reads somewhat like a high-level summary for the much more detailed technical analysis in the supplementary material. Given the page limits, this is understandable, but it makes it difficult to fully appreciate the technical depth of the work from the main text alone. The paper could be improved by moving some key proof ideas or technical insights from the appendix into the main body, perhaps in a condensed form, to better illustrate the challenges encountered and the authors' novel solutions.
