This paper proposes a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm tailored to sparse latent-variable models. The method augments the classical E- and M-steps with a truncation step that enforces sparsity along the iterate path. Under suitable initialization, the authors establish geometric convergence to a local solution and derive a near-optimal statistical error rate. Building on the estimator, they further develop a decorrelated score (and related Wald) test to conduct asymptotically valid inference on low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The framework accommodates both exact maximization and gradient-ascent implementations of the M-step, and is illustrated on Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Relation to prior work: The paper extends recent analyses of EM in low dimensions (e.g., geometric convergence to a local optimum and population-to-sample arguments) to the high-dimensional regime. The key novelty is to control both computation and statistics when d ≫ n by enforcing sparsity throughout the iterations and by analyzing entrywise statistical errors to achieve rates of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to log factors). On the inferential side, it adapts decorrelated/debiased score ideas beyond convex GLMs to latent-variable models where the likelihood is nonconcave and global optima are typically inaccessible.

Quality: The theoretical development appears careful and technically sophisticated, with explicit assumptions for stability of the EM map, statistical error control, and concentration of the quantities used in the score test. The results cover both estimation error (optimization versus statistical terms) and asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency for the target components.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and the related work is adequately covered. However, Sections 3 and 4 are very dense and difficult to follow. Additional exposition, intuitive explanations of the key conditions, and guidance for practical choices (e.g., tuning, initialization) would significantly improve readability for the NIPS audience.

Originality: The truncation-augmented EM algorithm and its unified analysis for both estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent-variable models are novel. Extending decorrelated-score inference beyond convex settings is also original and valuable.

Significance: This is a solid statistical contribution with clear theoretical interest. That said, the practical usefulness for real-world applications remains somewhat uncertain, given the sensitivity to initialization, tuning, and sparsity specifications, and the complexity of the assumptions.
