This paper proposes a sparse variant of the Expectation–Maximization (EM) algorithm that enforces sparsity by truncating the parameter vector at each iteration to a prescribed sparsity level. In addition, it develops an inference procedure based on a decorrelated score to test whether an individual coordinate of the parameter vector is nonzero, with an asymptotic normality guarantee.

On the algorithmic side, the main theoretical result shows that, under standard smoothness and local strong concavity assumptions on the Q-function and with initialization in a suitable basin of attraction, the sparse EM iterates converge geometrically to the true parameter, up to a statistical error term. The residual error scales with the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the M-step statistical error, which is typically only logarithmic in the ambient dimension. This extends recent low-dimensional EM convergence analyses to a high-dimensional, sparse regime.

A notable new condition is the requirement of uniform control (in infinity norm) on the M-step estimation error, restricted to sparse vectors. In many models, however, establishing such control for all parameter vectors is no more difficult than restricting to sparse vectors—this appears to be the case for the Gaussian mixture example and for the non-gradient mixture-of-regressions example discussed by the authors. This observation raises the question of whether the iterative truncation is strictly necessary for achieving the stated guarantees. Could one run the standard EM algorithm under the same conditions and apply a single truncation step at the end? The authors’ point that linear regression is encompassed as a special case is a convincing counterargument: in that setting, iterative sparsification is known to be crucial for both optimization and statistical control, which suggests that per-iteration truncation is not merely a cosmetic modification.

The inference component—an asymptotically normal decorrelated score for a single coordinate—addresses an important gap in high-dimensional latent-variable models, where post-selection inference is notoriously subtle. The approach is conceptually aligned with recent developments in high-dimensional inference that construct orthogonalized (or debiased) scores to attain valid asymptotics. Clarity on how the nuisance components are estimated within the EM framework, and on the conditions ensuring valid variance estimation and coverage, would further strengthen this contribution.

Strengths:
- Clear algorithmic idea (iterative truncation) with theory establishing geometric convergence up to a near-optimal statistical floor that depends only logarithmically on dimension.
- Extension of EM guarantees to high-dimensional sparse settings, complementing prior low-dimensional analyses.
- A principled inference procedure via decorrelated scores, enabling coordinate-wise hypothesis tests.

Concerns and questions:
- Necessity of iterative truncation: Although the linear regression special case supports the authors’ design choice, it would be helpful to articulate more precisely when iterative truncation changes the optimization trajectory in a way that single-shot truncation cannot emulate (e.g., through stability or contraction properties that fail without truncation).
- Assumptions and verification: The analysis relies on smoothness and near-quadratic behavior of the Q-function within a basin of attraction. Please clarify the size of this basin for the presented models and how the required initialization is obtained in practice. The empirical section should state exactly how initialization is performed.
- M-step error control: The theory invokes infinity-norm control of the M-step error. Provide concrete bounds and their dependence on sample size, dimension, and model parameters for each example (e.g., Gaussian mixtures, mixtures of regressions), and explain whether these bounds hold uniformly or only on high-probability events.
- Choice of sparsity level: How is the truncation level selected in practice? Is it assumed known, or tuned via a data-driven criterion? Sensitivity analyses would be valuable.
- Inference details: For the decorrelated score, specify how the nuisance projection is computed, the estimator of the asymptotic variance, and whether the conclusions hold uniformly over neighborhoods of the true parameter. Confidence intervals in addition to p-values would be useful.

Related work:
- It would be helpful to discuss connections to sparse optimization methods that enforce sparsity iteratively, such as truncated gradient approaches (e.g., Langford et al., 2009), and to contrast your per-iteration truncation with proximal or hard-thresholding variants used in high-dimensional M-estimation.
- Please clarify how your inference method relates to general frameworks for debiased or orthogonalized estimation in high dimensions, highlighting what is specific to EM and latent-variable settings.

Empirical evaluation:
- Report the initialization strategy and any heuristics used to ensure entry into the basin of attraction.
- Include comparisons among: (i) standard EM; (ii) standard EM followed by a single truncation; and (iii) the proposed sparse EM, to empirically demonstrate the necessity and benefits of iterative truncation.
- Provide sensitivity to the chosen sparsity level and to sample size, and report computational overhead relative to standard EM.

Overall assessment:
The paper tackles an important problem—scalable estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent-variable models—with a simple algorithmic modification and solid theoretical guarantees. The convergence analysis appears technically sound and the inference component is timely. The main open issue is the practical and theoretical necessity of iterative truncation versus post-hoc truncation, along with clearer guidance on initialization, M-step error control, and parameter tuning. Addressing these points, expanding the empirical comparisons, and enriching the discussion of related sparse optimization work would significantly strengthen the paper. Even with these caveats, the results are promising and relevant to the NIPS community.
