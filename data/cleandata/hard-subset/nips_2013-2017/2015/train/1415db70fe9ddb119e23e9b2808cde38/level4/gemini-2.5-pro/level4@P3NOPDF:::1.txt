This paper addresses the problem of estimation and inference for latent variable models in a high-dimensional setting. The authors analyze the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with regularization, establishing convergence guarantees for the proposed estimators. Furthermore, they develop a procedure for statistical inference, including hypothesis testing and the construction of confidence intervals, by adapting the decorrelated score test framework.

The primary strength of the paper is its synthesis of several important lines of research in high-dimensional statistics. It provides a unified framework for analyzing the regularized EM algorithm for a class of latent variable models and connects this estimation procedure to established methods for statistical inference. The problem is timely and significant.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several major weaknesses that need to be addressed concerning the clarity of its novel contributions, the justification of its theoretical assumptions, and practical guidance for implementation.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Clarity of Novel Contributions:** The paper combines existing results in a clever way, but it is not sufficiently clear what the core novel contributions are. The work appears to extend the analysis in [2] to a high-dimensional context and apply the inference framework of Ning and Liu [*] to the resulting estimators. The connection to the decorrelated score test from Ning and Liu [*] is fundamental to the inference part of the paper and should be made more explicit and properly cited early on. The current presentation could be interpreted as downplaying this reliance. The authors should clearly articulate the novel technical challenges that arise when applying these existing tools to the regularized EM setting. For instance, are there specific difficulties in adapting the decorrelated score methodology that required new theoretical developments? Without this clarification, the work appears to be a straightforward, albeit non-trivial, application of existing machinery.

2.  **Justification and Verification of Assumptions:** The main theoretical results are predicated on a large number of technical conditions. The paper would be significantly improved by a more thorough discussion of whether these assumptions are realistic and verifiable for the specific latent variable models considered. In particular:
    *   **Initialization:** A critical assumption is the existence of an initial solution, $\beta^{\rm init}$, that lies within a certain neighborhood of the true parameter (as required in line 60 on page 2). How can such an initialization be obtained in practice? The authors should discuss under what conditions this is possible and provide a concrete procedure if one exists. Without a reliable method for finding a suitable starting point, the practical applicability of the theoretical guarantees is limited.
    *   **General Conditions:** The authors should provide more intuition and justification for the other technical assumptions. Are they standard in the literature, or are some of them particularly strong? A discussion that connects these abstract conditions back to the concrete properties of the models of interest (e.g., mixture models, probit models) would be very valuable.

3.  **Practical Guidance and Tuning Parameters:** From a practical standpoint, the paper lacks sufficient guidance on implementation.
    *   **Choice of Algorithm:** Two algorithms are proposed. Is there practical guidance on when to use one over the other? What are the trade-offs in terms of computational cost, stability, or theoretical guarantees?
    *   **Tuning Parameters:** The proposed methods depend on crucial tuning parameters, namely the sparsity level $\hat s$ and the regularization parameter $\lambda$. The choice of these parameters is of utmost importance for achieving good statistical properties in high-dimensional inference. How should a practitioner choose them in a data-dependent way (e.g., via cross-validation)? How sensitive are the final estimates and inference results to these choices? The paper needs to provide a discussion on this topic, as the validity of the inference often relies heavily on the correct selection of such parameters.

4.  **Presentation and Technical Depth:** The main paper currently reads like a high-level summary, or a "trailer," for the technical details contained in the long supplementary material. While page limits are a constraint, the paper would be significantly strengthened by including more intuition about the key technical challenges and how they were overcome in the main text. This would help the reader better appreciate the paper's contributions without having to dive into the appendix immediately.

In summary, while the paper tackles an important problem and presents a technically sound approach, its contribution needs to be framed more clearly in the context of existing literature. The authors must provide more compelling arguments regarding the novelty of their technical work, better justify the assumptions underlying their theory, and offer crucial practical guidance on implementation and parameter tuning.
