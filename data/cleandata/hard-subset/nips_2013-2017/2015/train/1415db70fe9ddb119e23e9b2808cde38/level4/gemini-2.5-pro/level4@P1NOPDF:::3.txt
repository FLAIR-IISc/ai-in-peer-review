This paper proposes a sparse EM procedure, a modification of the classical EM algorithm that truncates the parameter vector to a desired sparsity level at each M-step. The authors also introduce an asymptotically normal statistic, based on a decorrelated score, for testing whether a specific entry of the parameter vector is non-zero.

The primary theoretical contribution is a guarantee that the sparse EM estimate converges geometrically to the true parameter value. This convergence holds up to a statistical error term that depends on the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the M-step's statistical error. Crucially, this latter error term is typically only logarithmic in the ambient dimension.

This work extends recent theoretical guarantees for EM from the low-dimensional to the high-dimensional sparse setting. Similar to previous work, the analysis assumes that the Q-function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic within a basin of attraction and that the algorithm is initialized within this basin. A key new assumption is the requirement of infinity norm control on the M-step estimation error. While this condition is formally required to hold only for sparse vectors, for many models—including the Gaussian mixture example considered here and the non-gradient version of the mixture of regression model—the difficulty of optimizing the M-step objective is the same for both sparse and dense vectors.

This naturally raises the question of whether the iterative truncation is truly necessary. Could a similar guarantee be obtained by running standard EM and then performing a single truncation at the end? The authors' observation that their framework includes sparse linear regression as a special case—a setting where a single final truncation step is known to be insufficient—provides a compelling argument for the necessity of the proposed iterative approach.

A few minor points for improvement:

*   The discussion of related work could be strengthened by including connections to other iterative hard-thresholding or truncation methods in the sparse optimization literature (e.g., "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" by Langford et al., 2009).
*   The experimental section should state what initialization procedure was used to ensure clarity and reproducibility.

In summary, this paper presents an important result for parameter estimation in high-dimensional settings. The convergence guarantees for the proposed sparse EM algorithm are a significant contribution, although the practical necessity of the iterative truncation over a simpler procedure was not immediately obvious. However, the connection to sparse linear regression provides a solid justification for the proposed method.
