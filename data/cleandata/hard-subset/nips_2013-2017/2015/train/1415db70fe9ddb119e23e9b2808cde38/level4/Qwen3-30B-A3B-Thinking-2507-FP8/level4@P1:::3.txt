

This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure through a truncation step at each iteration, along with a new inferential procedure for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional components in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors establish simultaneous statistical and computational guarantees for both parameter estimation and uncertainty assessment.

The paper builds upon recent advances in low-dimensional EM analysis [2, 18, 25, 30] by addressing the critical gap in high-dimensional settings where standard EM lacks theoretical guarantees. While prior work establishes geometric convergence rates and statistical rates of √(d/n) in low dimensions, this paper extends these results to high dimensions by incorporating sparsity assumptions, achieving the near-optimal rate of √(s* log d/n) where s* is the true sparsity level. The authors make two key contributions: (i) a high-dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step that enforces sparsity during iterations, and (ii) a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotically normal inference of low-dimensional parameter components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters.

The paper's theoretical framework is well-structured and general, applying to a broad family of latent variable models including Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions. The main results (Theorems 3.4 and 4.6) establish that with appropriate initialization, the proposed algorithm converges geometrically to a point within the statistical error bound of the minimax optimal rate, and the decorrelated score statistic achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound for inference. The authors carefully address the unique challenges of high-dimensional settings, particularly the need for entrywise statistical error analysis rather than √(d/n) rates, and demonstrate how their truncation step enables both computational and statistical guarantees.

The paper's strengths are substantial. First, it provides the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models, which is a significant contribution to the field. Second, the theoretical framework is remarkably general and applicable to multiple latent variable models, with careful verification of technical conditions for specific examples. Third, the authors demonstrate the necessity of their truncation step through a convincing argument that ordinary EM followed by a single truncation would not yield similar guarantees, particularly for models like mixture of regression where the M-step involves non-convex optimization.

The paper is well-written with clear technical exposition, and the proofs appear rigorous. The experimental section (though brief in the current draft) would benefit from more detailed implementation specifics, particularly regarding initialization strategies and parameter selection for the truncation step.

A few minor concerns warrant attention: The connection to sparse optimization literature (e.g., "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" by Langford et al., 2009) could be strengthened in the related work section, as the truncation technique bears resemblance to methods in that area. Additionally, the paper could clarify whether the truncation step is necessary for all high-dimensional latent variable models or only specific ones like Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions.

The paper's significance is high, as it bridges the gap between computation and statistics in high-dimensional latent variable modeling—a persistent challenge in the field. The results are not merely incremental but provide a fundamentally new framework for high-dimensional EM that could impact a broad range of applications in unsupervised learning, signal processing, and statistical inference.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: It is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims (Quality), clearly written with sufficient detail for reproducibility (Clarity), presents a novel combination of familiar techniques for high-dimensional inference (Originality), and addresses an important problem with demonstrable advances over prior work (Significance).

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of EM algorithms in high-dimensional settings. The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm with its associated inferential framework represents a substantial advance that will likely influence future research in high-dimensional latent variable modeling. I strongly recommend acceptance.
