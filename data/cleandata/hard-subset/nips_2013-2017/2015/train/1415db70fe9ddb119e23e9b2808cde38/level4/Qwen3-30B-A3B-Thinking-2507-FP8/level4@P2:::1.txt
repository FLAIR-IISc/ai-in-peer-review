

This paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm for latent variable models, establishing both computational and statistical guarantees for parameter estimation and asymptotic inference in high-dimensional settings. The authors make two key contributions: (i) a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity through truncation steps in the E-step and M-step, achieving geometric convergence to a near-optimal estimator, and (ii) a decorrelated score test for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters. The paper establishes theoretical guarantees for both estimation and inference under a unified framework, applying it to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical contributions. It provides the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors successfully extend the low-dimensional analysis of [2] to high dimensions by introducing the truncation step, which enables them to establish sharper statistical rates (p(s* log d/n) rather than p(d/n)). The decorrelated score test is theoretically justified, achieving the semiparametric information bound for the low-dimensional components. The paper demonstrates strong technical rigor in establishing both optimization and statistical guarantees, with the results being generalizable across multiple latent variable models.

However, several concerns need to be addressed for the paper to meet NIPS quality standards. First, the paper lacks sufficient discussion on the practical implementation of the algorithm. The authors propose two implementations of the M-step (exact maximization and gradient ascent) but do not provide guidance on which to use in practice. The paper should include empirical comparisons of these implementations to help practitioners make informed choices.

Second, the paper does not adequately address the choice of tuning parameters, particularly the sparsity parameter $\hat{s}$ and the threshold $\lambda$ used in the truncation step. In high-dimensional inference literature, the choice of these parameters critically affects the statistical properties of the estimators. The authors should discuss practical data-dependent methods for selecting these parameters and analyze the sensitivity of the results to their choice. Without this, the practical applicability of the proposed method remains unclear.

Third, the decorrelated score test appears to be a direct application of Ning and Liu's work [26] rather than a novel development. The authors should explicitly cite this prior work and clarify what new theoretical developments were required to apply it to the high-dimensional EM context. The current presentation risks understating the contribution of the paper by not clearly distinguishing between existing methodology and new theoretical insights.

Fourth, the paper relies on several technical conditions (Conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.5) that need to be verified for the specific models under consideration. The authors should provide more detailed discussion on whether these conditions are satisfied for the Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models they consider. The appendix contains verification for specific models, but this crucial information should be summarized in the main text to help readers assess the applicability of the theoretical results.

Finally, the paper functions primarily as a "trailer" for a longer supplementary material, which is acceptable, but it should contain more information about the technical challenges involved in extending the low-dimensional analysis to high dimensions. The current version does not sufficiently highlight the non-trivial aspects of the theoretical development, particularly regarding how the truncation step affects the iterative solution path and why the entrywise statistical error analysis is necessary rather than just the l2-norm analysis.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important gap in the literature on high-dimensional latent variable models. The proposed framework could potentially impact practitioners working with high-dimensional mixture models, though the practical implementation concerns need to be resolved. The paper is original in its approach to combining sparsity with the EM algorithm for high-dimensional inference, though the decorrelated score test component requires clearer differentiation from existing work.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, meeting the clarity criterion. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to understand the technical contributions, though the practical guidance could be strengthened.

In summary, the paper makes a theoretically significant contribution to extending the EM algorithm to high dimensions, establishing important statistical and computational guarantees. However, to fully satisfy NIPS quality standards, the authors need to address the practical implementation concerns, clarify the novelty of the decorrelated score test, provide more detailed discussion of the technical conditions for specific models, and strengthen the practical guidance for tuning parameter selection. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong contribution to the field.
