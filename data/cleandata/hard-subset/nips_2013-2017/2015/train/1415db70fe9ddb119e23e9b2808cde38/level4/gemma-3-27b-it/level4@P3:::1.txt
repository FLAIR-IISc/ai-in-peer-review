This paper presents a theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, addressing a significant gap in the existing literature. The authors establish convergence guarantees for a modified EM algorithm incorporating sparsity and develop an inferential procedure for low-dimensional components. The work builds upon previous results for the EM algorithm in low dimensions [2, 18, 25, 30] and draws connections to recent advances in high-dimensional inference [4, 13, 15, 16, 17, 19, 20, 22, 23, 24, 26, 29, 31, 32]. The core contribution lies in extending these ideas to a challenging high-dimensional setting with latent variables, and providing both estimation and inference guarantees. 

The paper is generally well-structured and clearly presents the mathematical framework. The motivation is well-explained, and the authors accurately position their work relative to existing literature. The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm, featuring a truncation step to enforce sparsity, is a reasonable approach. The theoretical results, particularly the convergence rate and asymptotic normality of the decorrelated score statistic, are significant contributions. However, several points need addressing to strengthen the paper.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Theoretical understanding of EM in high dimensions is limited, and this paper directly tackles that challenge.
* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper presents rigorous analysis, building on established results and providing new guarantees.
* **Practical relevance:** The developed inferential procedure allows for hypothesis testing, enhancing the practical utility of the method.
* **Clear presentation:** The mathematical notation and derivations are generally well-presented, and the algorithm descriptions are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The paper relies on a specific initialization condition (line 60, page 2). The authors should discuss practical strategies for finding an initial solution that satisfies this requirement. Providing guidance on initialization is crucial for real-world applicability.
* **Algorithm Selection:** The paper presents two M-step implementations (Algorithm 2 & 3). A discussion regarding practical guidance on selecting between the exact maximization and gradient ascent approaches would be beneficial. What are the trade-offs in terms of computational cost and statistical accuracy?
* **Tuning Parameter Selection:** The choice of sparsity parameter *bs* and other tuning parameters is critical. The paper lacks a discussion on how to select these parameters in practice.  How sensitive are the results to these choices? Could data-driven methods (e.g., cross-validation) be employed, and if so, how would that impact the theoretical guarantees? This is particularly important given the established literature emphasizing tuning parameter sensitivity in high-dimensional inference.
* **Relationship to Existing Inference Procedures:** The authors mention that the decorrelated score test is related to the work of Ning and Liu [Yang Ning, Han Liu, A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models]. This connection should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed in more detail.  What novel contributions are made when applying this existing framework to the current latent variable model setting?  Simply stating it is “not swept under the rug” is insufficient.
* **Technical Condition Verification:** The theoretical results rely on a large number of technical conditions (Conditions 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5). The authors should provide a more detailed discussion of whether these conditions hold for the example latent variable models considered (Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression). A more thorough verification of the assumptions would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **"Trailer" Effect:** The paper feels somewhat incomplete, with many details relegated to the appendix. While extensive supplementary material is common, the core paper should provide a more self-contained and complete presentation of the main ideas and results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding the theoretical properties of EM in high dimensions.
* The technical results are rigorous and contribute to our understanding of the algorithm's behavior.
* The proposed inferential procedure offers a valuable tool for practitioners.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of discussion regarding initialization, tuning parameter selection, and the practical implications of the technical conditions weakens the paper's practical relevance.
* The connection to existing inference procedures requires further elaboration.
* The paper relies heavily on the appendix, making it difficult to fully grasp the details without extensive reading.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical contribution with the potential to be highly impactful. However, the concerns raised above regarding practical considerations and the completeness of the presentation need to be addressed. I recommend **acceptance with major revisions**. The authors should focus on providing more practical guidance, clarifying the relationship to existing work, and strengthening the discussion of the technical conditions. Addressing these points will significantly enhance the paper's value to the NIPS community.
