Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm for latent-variable models. The key algorithmic idea is to append a truncation (T-) step to the classical E- and M-steps, enforcing sparsity along the iteration path. With suitable initialization, the authors prove geometric convergence to a sparse estimator that achieves a near-minimax statistical rate, roughly O(s*1/2·(log d/n)1/2), where s* is the true sparsity. Beyond estimation, the paper proposes inference for low-dimensional components via a decorrelated score (and corresponding Wald) statistic, claiming asymptotic normality with variance attaining the semiparametric efficiency bound. The framework covers both exact maximization and gradient-ascent implementations of the M-step and is illustrated on Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

This work builds on the population-to-sample EM analysis in the low-dimensional regime (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) by adapting it to high dimensions through a new T-step and a finer entrywise statistical analysis. For inference, it follows the decorrelated/debiased-score approach pioneered for high-dimensional GLMs and related models (e.g., Ning and Liu, van de Geer et al.), but transplanted here to nonconvex latent-variable likelihoods and EM iterates.

Strengths:
- A clear and practical modification of EM (the T-step) that explicitly enforces sparsity and aligns computation with statistical guarantees in high dimensions.
- Convergence guarantees that cleanly separate optimization error (geometric) from statistical error (near-optimal in s* and d).
- Coverage of two M-step implementations (exact and gradient), with rates and contraction factors explicitly identified.
- A general inferential framework whose limiting variance matches the semiparametric information bound under high-dimensional nuisance, with verification for representative latent-variable models.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Initialization: The theory assumes an initializer βinit satisfying the relative-error condition (line 60, page 2). Under what concrete modeling conditions and algorithms can such an initializer be obtained in practice (e.g., spectral/tensor moments, k-means for GMM, method-of-moments for mixtures of regressions)? Please discuss sufficient conditions and offer references or practical recipes, including sample-size requirements for these initializers.
- Choice of algorithm: There are two M-step implementations (exact vs. gradient). Is there practical guidance on when to prefer one over the other (e.g., per-iteration cost, curvature/smoothness regimes, robustness to step-size tuning)? Any empirical comparison would be helpful.
- Tuning parameters: How should ŝ (the truncation level) and λ (e.g., the Dantzig-selector constraint in the inference step) be selected in practice? How sensitive are estimation and inference to misspecification of ŝ and λ? Please discuss data-driven procedures (cross-validation, information criteria, Lepski-type selection, stability selection) and provide guidance calibrated to the rates in your theory (e.g., λ scaling with √(log d/n) and model-specific constants).
- Inference novelty and attribution: The decorrelated score test used here coincides in form with the approach of Ning and Liu (“A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models”) and related debiasing/desparsification work. This should be clearly cited where the method is introduced, and the paper should delineate what is new in this context (e.g., establishing the key identities linking Qn and Tn to log-likelihood scores and Fisher information for latent-variable models; verifying the concentration/Lipschitz conditions along EM iterates; handling nonconvexity and non-global estimators).
- Assumptions: The main results rely on a number of technical conditions (Lipschitz-gradient properties, concavity/smoothness in a basin, entrywise statistical error bounds, sparsity of the efficient-weight vector w*). While some are verified in the appendix for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, the main text should summarize which conditions hold for each model, under what signal-to-noise, separation, or design assumptions, and how restrictive they are. In particular, please discuss the sparsity assumption on w* (support size s*w) and its practical plausibility.
- Exposition: The main paper reads as a trailer for the long supplement. It would help to include more intuition about the technical challenges unique to the T-step (e.g., support evolution under statistical error; why entrywise control is essential), highlight the key lemmas that differ from low-dimensional EM analyses, and summarize the verification of Conditions 4.1–4.4 for the showcased models.

Quality:
The theoretical development appears sound and carefully connects optimization and statistics. However, the paper would benefit from concrete guidance on initialization and tuning, and a clearer mapping from assumptions to practice for the included models. Empirical demonstrations (even brief) comparing exact vs. gradient M-steps and illustrating tuning sensitivity would strengthen the practical message.

Clarity:
The writing is generally clear but notation-heavy. Bringing critical assumptions, intuition, and model-specific conditions into the main text would improve readability. Please clearly attribute the inference methodology and specify what is adapted or extended.

Originality:
The algorithmic addition (T-step) and the high-dimensional EM analysis are novel and interesting. The inference component largely adapts established decorrelated-score ideas; the novelty lies in verifying their applicability to latent-variable EM estimators under nonconvexity.

Significance:
If the assumptions are reasonably met in practice and tuning can be handled robustly, the results are significant: they provide a computationally tractable path to near-optimal estimation and valid inference in high-dimensional latent-variable models.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel high-dimensional EM framework with truncation and provable near-minimax rates.
- Unified computational and inferential guarantees, including semiparametric efficiency for low-dimensional targets.
- Verification on two important latent-variable models.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical aspects (initialization, tuning, algorithm choice) are under-specified.
- Inference methodology is largely borrowed; the paper should more clearly articulate the new technical contributions in making it work here.
- Heavy reliance on supplemental material; the main text should contain more of the key ideas and assumptions.

Overall, I see a solid and potentially impactful contribution, but I encourage the authors to strengthen the discussion of initialization and tuning, clarify novelty relative to Ning and Liu and to low-dimensional EM analyses, and summarize the verification of assumptions for the presented models in the main paper.
