The authors propose a sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure that modifies the classical EM algorithm by truncating the parameter vector at each step to a desired sparsity level. Additionally, they introduce an asymptotically normal statistic based on a decorrelated score for testing whether a specific entry of the parameter vector is non-zero. The sparse EM algorithm comes with a convergence guarantee, stating that the estimate converges geometrically to the true value in the number of iterations, up to a statistical error term that depends on the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step. Notably, this error term is typically only logarithmic in the ambient dimension.

This result builds upon recent similar findings for EM in low-dimensional settings. The authors assume that the Q-function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic within a certain basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized within this basin. A new condition is introduced, requiring control over the infinity norm of the error in estimating the M-step. This condition is specific to sparse vectors, but the authors note that for many models, estimating the M-step at all possible vectors is just as easy as at sparse vectors. This raises a question about the necessity of the truncated EM algorithm. Could a similar guarantee be obtained by performing ordinary EM under the same conditions, followed by a single truncation step? The authors' observation that linear regression is a special case where this approach would not work seems to be a convincing argument against a single truncation step.

The authors may benefit from discussing related work in sparse optimization, such as "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" by Langford et al. (2009). This could provide additional context and help situate their contribution within the broader literature.

In the experimental section, the authors should specify the initialization method used for the sparse EM algorithm. The proposed sparse EM variant is a valuable contribution to parameter estimation in high-dimensional settings, and the convergence guarantees provided are an important result for high-dimensional learning. However, the importance of the sparse variant of EM to the obtained results is not entirely clear, and further discussion on this point would be beneficial.
