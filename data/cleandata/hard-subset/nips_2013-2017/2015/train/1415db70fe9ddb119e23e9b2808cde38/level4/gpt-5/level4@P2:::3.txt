Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm that enforces sparsity by adding a truncation step after each E/M iteration. Two implementations of the M-step are considered: exact maximization and a single gradient-ascent step. With an appropriate initialization inside a basin of attraction and under smoothness/strong-concavity conditions on the population Q function, the authors prove geometric convergence of the optimization error and a (near-)minimax statistical rate for estimation: roughly √s* · √(log d / n) (up to logarithmic factors), where s* is the true sparsity. Beyond point estimation, the paper proposes a decorrelated score test for low-dimensional components of the parameter that is asymptotically normal and achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound, enabling valid hypothesis testing and confidence intervals in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters.

The work builds on and extends recent theory for EM in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.), which established geometric convergence to a local optimum with statistical rate √(d/n) given a suitable initialization. The key novelty here is handling d ≫ n via a truncation step, entrywise control of the sample-to-population M-step discrepancy, and a high-dimensional inference procedure akin in spirit to de-biasing/desparsifying methods for linear/GLM models, but tailored to latent variable models where convexity and global optima are unavailable. Applications to Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions illustrate the assumptions and rates.

Strengths:
- Technically solid and timely: provides simultaneous statistical and computational guarantees for EM in high dimensions, including explicit optimization and statistical error decompositions and geometric convergence.
- Near-optimal estimation rates under sparsity, with model-specific concentration results that deliver entrywise M-step error of order √(log d / n).
- A general and computationally feasible inferential framework (decorrelated score and Wald tests) that achieves the semiparametric information bound—novel for latent variable models in high dimensions.
- Covers both exact M-step and gradient EM, broadening applicability.
- Clear connection to and advancement over the population-to-sample EM analyses in the low-dimensional literature.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Necessity and practical impact of the truncation step: while theoretically crucial for controlling support and obtaining ℓ∞-type errors, it remains somewhat unclear when truncated EM materially improves over running standard EM with a single post-hoc truncation/thresholding step. The authors’ argument using linear regression as a special case is helpful, but a direct empirical comparison would strengthen the case.
- Tuning and knowledge of sparsity: guarantees require the truncation level to be of the same order as s*, and sample sizes large enough so that √s* · ε (entrywise error) is small. Guidance for choosing the truncation level, step sizes, and stopping criteria in practice is limited.
- Initialization within a basin of attraction is assumed; the paper does not provide a general, data-driven initializer with guarantees. This is critical in nonconvex latent variable models.
- Inference relies on a Dantzig-type estimator of a decorrelating vector with its own sparsity assumption; the paper could better discuss when this assumption holds for the considered models and how sensitive inference is to violations.
- The analysis uses a resampling variant to simplify proofs; discussion of how results translate to a single-sample implementation would be useful.
- Experimental section: currently light on details (e.g., initialization, tuning) and ablation studies; more evaluation of coverage/type-I error and power of the proposed tests would increase confidence.

Quality:
- High technical quality: assumptions are clearly stated and verified for representative models; rates are transparent; the inference procedure is grounded in score/Fisher information identities adapted to latent variable models.

Clarity:
- Generally well organized, but notation is heavy. A concise roadmap, clearer separation of population vs. sample arguments, and a brief intuitive explanation of the truncation step’s role in tracking support would improve readability. Please specify initializations, truncation levels, step sizes, and stopping rules used in experiments.

Originality:
- Original in bringing together truncated EM with entrywise statistical control to obtain near-optimal rates in high dimension, and in developing valid inference (decorrelated score) for latent variable models without relying on convexity or global optima. Related sparse optimization ideas (e.g., truncated gradient methods such as Langford et al., 2009) could be discussed to situate the truncation mechanism in a broader context.

Significance:
- Significant contribution to high-dimensional learning with latent variables: offers the first computationally feasible pathway to both optimal estimation and valid inference for a broad class of models. Likely to influence subsequent work on nonconvex estimation and high-dimensional inference.

Suggestions and questions for the authors:
- Empirically compare: (a) truncated EM, (b) standard EM with post-hoc truncation, (c) EM with ℓ1-penalized or proximal M-steps. Report estimation error, support recovery, and inference coverage.
- Provide practical guidance and sensitivity analyses for initialization, truncation level, and stepsize; consider simple initializers with partial guarantees.
- Expand experiments to include calibration of the decorrelated score/Wald tests (type I error, power, CI coverage), and robustness to weaker signal-to-noise or model misspecification.
- Discuss the sparsity assumption on the decorrelating vector and when it holds in Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions.
- Add a short related-work paragraph connecting your truncation to truncated gradient and other sparsity-enforcing online/proximal schemes.

Pros:
- Near-optimal high-dimensional rates with geometric optimization convergence.
- General, implementable inference achieving semiparametric efficiency.
- Applicable to multiple latent variable models and both EM variants.

Cons:
- Requires careful initialization and tuning; theory assumes knowledge of sparsity order.
- Justification of truncation vs. simpler post-hoc thresholding could be strengthened empirically.
- Experimental details and inference evaluation are limited.

Overall, this is a strong and impactful paper. With clearer experimental reporting and an empirical comparison clarifying the necessity and benefits of the truncation step, it would make an excellent addition to the program.
