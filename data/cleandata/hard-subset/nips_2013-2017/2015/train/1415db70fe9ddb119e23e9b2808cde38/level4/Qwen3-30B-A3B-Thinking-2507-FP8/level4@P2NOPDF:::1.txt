

The paper addresses EM in high-dimensional settings, establishing convergence guarantees and developing an inference procedure. While the combination of existing techniques is clever, the novelty and technical contributions require clearer articulation.  

A critical gap concerns the initial solution \(\beta^{\text{init}}\). The paper requires \(\beta^{\text{init}}\) to satisfy a condition (line 60, p.2), but provides no discussion of how such an initial solution can be practically obtained. The authors should clarify under what conditions \(\beta^{\text{init}}\) exists and how it can be constructed for the models under consideration.  

The paper proposes two algorithms but offers no guidance on when to prefer one over the other. Practical recommendations for algorithm selection based on problem characteristics (e.g., sparsity level, sample size) would significantly strengthen the contribution.  

Similarly, the choice of tuning parameters \(\hat{s}\) and \(\lambda\) lacks justification. The authors should address: (i) how to select these in practice, (ii) the sensitivity of results to their choice, and (iii) whether data-dependent selection methods (e.g., cross-validation, stability-based approaches) are feasible. In high-dimensional inference literature, tuning parameter selection is critical for valid statistical properties, and this omission weakens the practical applicability of the work.  

The decorrelated score test used is identical to that in Ning & Liu (2014). This should be explicitly acknowledged and cited, rather than presented as novel. The paper must clarify what new technical developments are required to apply Ning & Liu’s framework to the latent variable models considered here. Merely adapting existing tools without addressing context-specific challenges does not constitute a significant contribution.  

The main theoretical results rely on numerous technical assumptions. The authors should discuss whether these assumptions are verifiable for the specific latent variable models analyzed (e.g., Gaussian mixtures, factor models). A brief assessment of the plausibility of these conditions in practice would greatly enhance the paper’s credibility.  

The paper effectively extends Ning & Liu (2014) to high-dimensional settings and applies their framework to inference. However, the core novelty is obscured. The work reads as a "trailer" for a longer supplementary document, rather than a self-contained contribution. The main paper should include sufficient technical detail to justify the theoretical claims, particularly regarding the nontrivial challenges in adapting Ning & Liu’s methods to the current context. Specific examples of these challenges (e.g., handling latent variables in the decorrelation step, dependency structures) would clarify why the application is nontrivial.  

In summary, the paper has potential but requires substantial revisions to clarify its contributions and address practical implementation concerns. The theoretical framework is well-motivated, but the presentation obscures what is genuinely new. The authors must: (1) specify conditions for obtaining \(\beta^{\text{init}}\), (2) provide tuning parameter guidance, (3) explicitly cite and contextualize the decorrelated score test, and (4) detail the technical hurdles in adapting existing methods. Without these, the paper risks being seen as a routine extension rather than a meaningful advance.  

The supplementary material should not replace the main text; key insights and technical arguments must be present in the paper itself. Addressing these points would transform the submission from a promising draft into a compelling contribution to high-dimensional inference.
