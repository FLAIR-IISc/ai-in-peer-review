A modified EM procedure is introduced, extending classical EM by incorporating a truncation step to achieve a specified sparsity level in the parameter vector at each iteration. Additionally, an asymptotically normal test statistic is proposed, based on a decorrelated score, to assess whether a particular entry in the parameter vector is nonzero.

The sparse EM algorithm offers a convergence guarantee, ensuring that the estimate converges geometrically to the true value with respect to the number of iterations, subject to a statistical error term. This error term is proportional to the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step, which typically grows logarithmically with the ambient dimension.

This work builds upon recent results for EM in low-dimensional settings, where similar guarantees have been established. The analysis relies on the assumptions that the Q function exhibits sufficient smoothness and near-quadratic behavior within a certain basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized within this basin. A novel condition is imposed, requiring control over the infinity norm of the error in estimating M, which is necessary for sparse vectors but may be readily satisfied in various models, including the Gaussian mixture example and the non-gradient version of the mixture of regression model.

This raises the question of whether the truncated EM algorithm is essential, or if similar guarantees could be achieved through ordinary EM, followed by a single truncation step. However, the authors' observation that linear regression represents a special case may argue against the sufficiency of a single truncation step.

It may be beneficial to discuss related work in sparse optimization, such as "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" by Langford et al (2009), to provide a more comprehensive context.

The experimental section should specify the initialization method employed. The proposed sparse EM variant offers a valuable contribution to parameter estimation in high-dimensional settings, with the convergence guarantees being a significant result for high-dimensional learning. Nevertheless, the importance of the sparse EM variant in achieving these results remains uncertain.
