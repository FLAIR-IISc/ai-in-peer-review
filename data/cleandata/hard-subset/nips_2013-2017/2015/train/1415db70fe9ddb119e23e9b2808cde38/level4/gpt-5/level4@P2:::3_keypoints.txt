- Paper proposes a high-dimensional truncated EM algorithm (with exact or gradient M-steps) that enforces sparsity via a post-iteration truncation step.  
- Proves geometric optimization convergence and near-minimax estimation rates ≈ √s*·√(log d / n) under smoothness/strong-concavity and suitable initialization.  
- Develops a decorrelated score/Wald inference procedure for low-dimensional components that is asymptotically normal and attains the semiparametric efficiency bound.  
- Extends low-dimensional EM theory to d ≫ n by controlling entrywise sample-to-population M-step discrepancy and tracking support.  
- Applications to Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions verify assumptions and illustrate rates.  
- Strength: technically solid with clear optimization/statistical error decomposition and geometric convergence proofs.  
- Strength: near-optimal estimation rates with model-specific concentration giving ℓ∞-type M-step errors of order √(log d / n).  
- Strength: general, computationally feasible inference framework novel for latent variable models in high dimensions.  
- Strength: covers both exact M-step and gradient EM implementations.  
- Weakness: practical necessity/benefit of truncation vs. standard EM plus post-hoc thresholding is not empirically demonstrated.  
- Weakness: theory requires knowing sparsity order for truncation and large samples so √s*·ε is small; limited practical guidance for choosing truncation level, stepsize, and stopping.  
- Weakness: relies on initialization within a basin of attraction but provides no general data-driven initializer with guarantees.  
- Weakness: inference depends on a Dantzig-type decorrelating vector with its own sparsity assumption; unclear when this holds and how sensitive results are to violations.  
- Weakness: analysis uses a resampling variant to simplify proofs; translation to single-sample implementation needs discussion.  
- Weakness: experiments are light on details (initialization, tuning) and lack ablation, coverage/type-I error, and power evaluations for the tests.  
- Clarity: high-quality writing but heavy notation; would benefit from a concise roadmap, clearer population vs. sample separation, and intuitive explanation of truncation’s role.  
- Originality: novel combination of truncated EM and entrywise control for near-optimal high-dimensional rates and first valid inference for latent variable models without convexity.  
- Suggested experiments: compare (a) truncated EM, (b) EM + post-hoc truncation, and (c) ℓ1-penalized/proximal EM on estimation, support recovery, and inference calibration.  
- Suggested additions: provide practical tuning/sensitivity guidance, simple initializers with partial guarantees, discussion of decorrelator sparsity in considered models, and relate truncation to truncated-gradient literature.  
- Overall: strong, impactful paper with significant contributions; would be improved by clearer experiments and empirical justification of truncation.
