This paper introduces a sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure that modifies the classical algorithm by truncating the parameter vector to a specified sparsity level at each iteration. The authors also propose an asymptotically normal statistic, based on a decorrelated score, for hypothesis testing on individual parameter entries.

The theoretical analysis establishes that the sparse EM estimate converges geometrically to the true parameter value, up to a statistical error. This error term is shown to depend on the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step, which is typically only logarithmic in the ambient dimension. These results build upon recent work providing similar guarantees for EM in low-dimensional settings. The analysis relies on standard assumptions, such as the Q-function being sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic within a basin of attraction and the algorithm being initialized within this basin. A key new assumption is the requirement of infinity norm control on the M-step estimation error.

This new condition raises a central question about the necessity of the proposed algorithm. While the analysis requires this control only for sparse vectors, for many models—including the Gaussian mixture example presented—evaluating the M-step is of comparable difficulty for both sparse and dense vectors. This suggests the possibility of an alternative approach: could a similar theoretical guarantee be achieved by running the standard EM algorithm and applying a single truncation step to the final estimate? However, the authors' observation that linear regression is a special case of their framework appears to provide a convincing counterargument to this simpler "post-processing" approach.

The paper could be strengthened by discussing related work from the sparse optimization literature, for example, "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" (Langford et al., 2009). Additionally, the experimental section should be improved by explicitly stating the initialization procedure used.

In summary, this work provides important convergence guarantees for a sparse EM variant in high-dimensional settings, which is a valuable contribution. However, the fundamental importance of the iterative sparsity-inducing step in EM to achieving these results, compared to simpler alternatives, is not yet fully established.
