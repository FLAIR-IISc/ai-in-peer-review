This paper addresses the challenging problem of applying the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to high-dimensional latent variable models with sparse parameters. The authors develop a modified EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity and provide theoretical guarantees for both estimation accuracy and asymptotic inference. They demonstrate that, given appropriate initialization, the algorithm converges at a geometric rate and achieves a near-optimal statistical rate of convergence of O(sqrt(s*log(d/n))), where s is the sparsity level, d is the dimension, and n is the sample size. They further propose a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing regarding low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter.

The paper is well-structured and clearly identifies a significant gap in existing literature: the lack of theoretical guarantees for EM in high-dimensional settings. The related work is adequately summarized, placing the contribution within the broader context of statistical estimation and inference. The authors demonstrate a strong theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm and its properties. However, the paper is exceptionally dense and the technical details, particularly in Sections 3 and 4, can be challenging to follow. Increased clarity and simplification would greatly benefit the readership.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** The paper tackles a critical issue in the application of EM algorithms to modern high-dimensional data.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The analysis is rigorous, with clear conditions and convergence guarantees.
*   **Novel Contribution:** The introduction of a truncation step and the associated theoretical analysis represent a significant advance in the field.
*   **Comprehensive Framework:** The paper provides a complete framework encompassing both estimation and inference.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density and Clarity:** The technical presentation is very dense and requires significant effort to follow, potentially limiting its accessibility.
*   **Justification of Optimality:** The claim of minimax optimality (line 66) lacks explicit proof within the main manuscript. While likely established in related work, a brief citation or explanation would strengthen the argument.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper does not adequately address practical challenges of implementing the algorithm. Specifically:
    *   The sparsity parameter ^s in Eq. (3.8) depends on the unknown true sparsity level, s. Guidance on selecting ^s in practice is needed.
    *   The requirement for a specific initialization (||beta^init - beta*||_2 < R/2, line 275) seems challenging to meet in real-world applications.
*   **Role of Truncation Step:** The importance of the truncation step is not fully explained intuitively. A clear illustration of what happens without it would significantly enhance understanding.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of EM algorithms in high dimensions.
*   The results are rigorously proven and offer valuable insights into the convergence properties and statistical performance of the proposed algorithm.
*   The work addresses a crucial gap in the literature and has the potential to influence future research in this area.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The high level of technical detail and density may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
*   The lack of discussion regarding practical implementation challenges raises concerns about the real-world applicability of the proposed method.
*   The absence of a clear justification for the minimax optimality claim weakens the overall argument.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to a challenging problem. While the density and lack of practical guidance are concerns, the strength of the theoretical results and the significance of the contribution warrant serious consideration. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and address the practical considerations outlined above. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a brief justification or citation supporting the claim of minimax optimality.
2.  Discuss strategies for selecting the sparsity parameter ^s in practice.
3.  Provide guidance on obtaining an appropriate initialization.
4.  Explain the consequences of omitting the truncation step.
5.  Consider adding more intuitive explanations and examples to enhance clarity.
