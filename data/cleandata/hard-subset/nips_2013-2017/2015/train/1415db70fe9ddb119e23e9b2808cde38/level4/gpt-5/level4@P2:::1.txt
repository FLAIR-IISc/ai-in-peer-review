Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm for latent-variable models by adding a truncation (hard-thresholding) step that enforces sparsity after each M-step. With suitable initialization, the algorithm is shown to converge geometrically to a statistically accurate estimator achieving near-optimal rates on the order of sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n), up to logarithmic factors, for both exact M-steps and a gradient-ascent approximation. Building on this estimator, the authors construct a decorrelated score (and corresponding Wald) test for low-dimensional components of the parameter, and establish asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency.

This work extends recent EM theory in the low-dimensional regime (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) to the high-dimensional case by incorporating sparsity and carefully controlling optimization versus statistical errors. For inference, the proposed decorrelated score framework closely follows the high-dimensional inference literature (e.g., Ning and Liu, “A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models,” and related de-biasing/desparsification works). The claimed novelty is to make these guarantees work for latent-variable models where the objective is nonconcave and the estimator is a local optimum obtained by EM with truncation.

Strengths:
- Tackles an important gap between computational procedures (EM) and statistical guarantees in high dimensions.
- Clear decomposition between optimization and statistical errors, with geometric contraction and near-minimax statistical rates.
- Two practical M-step implementations (exact and gradient) under a unified analysis.
- Asymptotically normal, semiparametrically efficient tests for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters.
- Applications to Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions illustrate the framework’s scope.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Initialization: The main guarantees require an initializer with bounded relative error. Please discuss, with citations and concrete procedures, how such an initializer can be obtained in practice for the considered models (e.g., method-of-moments/tensor/spectral initializations, k-means for mixtures, convex relaxations, or sample splitting). What sample sizes and signal-to-noise regimes ensure the stated condition holds?
- Algorithm choice: Provide practical guidance on when to prefer the exact versus gradient M-step (computational complexity per iteration, robustness to stepsize, sensitivity to smoothness constants, and empirical convergence behavior).
- Tuning parameters: How should the sparsity level s_hat and regularization λ be chosen in practice? How sensitive are estimates and tests to these choices? Please discuss data-driven strategies (e.g., cross-validation on Qn, information criteria, stability selection, Lepski-type rules, or theory-informed defaults), and clarify how the choice of s_hat interacts with Theorem 3.4’s requirements.
- Attribution and novelty for inference: The decorrelated score/Wald construction appears to follow Ning and Liu (and related de-biasing work). Please explicitly cite this line of work in the main text, carefully delineate what is reused versus what is new, and explain what technical innovations are required to make these procedures work with (i) nonconvex latent-variable likelihoods, (ii) EM iterates that are not global optima, and (iii) the additional truncation step.
- Assumptions and verifiability: The main theorems rely on several technical conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity/smoothness, concentration for Tn, and statistical error bounds). For each model, please summarize in the main paper (not only the appendix) the key constants, sample-size regimes, and SNR conditions under which these hold, and provide intuition for why they are satisfied.
- Practicalities: Discuss stepsize selection for the gradient M-step without knowledge of µ and ν; robustness if these constants are misspecified; computational complexity of E/M/T steps per iteration; and how mis-specifying s_hat (too small or too large) affects support evolution and error bounds.

Quality:
- Technical development appears solid and carefully structured, subject to the above clarifications. The optimization–statistical error decomposition and control of truncation effects are well motivated.
- The paper would benefit from moving high-level statements of key lemmas and model-specific conditions to the main text, along with a brief proof sketch to convey the main ideas behind handling the truncation step and the concentration of Tn.

Clarity:
- Overall well organized, but the paper currently functions as a “trailer” for a long appendix. Please add more intuition about the basin-of-attraction arguments, the role of the T-step, and how entrywise statistical control feeds into support stability. Provide explicit, model-specific statements of sample size/SNR requirements and default tuning recommendations. Fix minor typos and ensure consistent notation for norms and tuning parameters.

Originality:
- The algorithmic combination of EM with iterative truncation for latent-variable models and its unified analysis appears novel. The inference component should clearly credit prior decorrelation/de-biasing methods and highlight what is new in adapting them to nonconvex EM-based estimators.

Significance:
- If the stated conditions can be met with practical initializers and tuning, the framework provides an important and broadly applicable tool for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent-variable models, with compelling statistical guarantees.

Pros:
- Addresses a central problem; near-optimal rates; geometric convergence; semiparametric efficiency for inference; general framework with two M-step variants.

Cons:
- Heavy reliance on technical conditions with limited main-text guidance on when they hold; unclear practical pathways for initialization and tuning; attribution and novelty of the inference piece need to be clarified.

Requests for revision:
- Add explicit guidance and references for obtaining valid initializers.
- Provide practical rules for choosing s_hat, λ, and stepsizes; include sensitivity analyses or simulations.
- Clearly cite Ning and Liu (and related works) for the decorrelated score/Wald procedures, and articulate the novel technical contributions needed in this setting.
- Summarize, in the main text, verifiable conditions and sample size/SNR regimes for the models studied, with intuition and key constants.
