A sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure is proposed, modifying the classical EM algorithm by truncating the parameter vector at each iteration to a desired sparsity level. The authors also present an asymptotically normal statistic, based on a decorrelated score, for testing the non-zero status of specific entries within the parameter vector.

The convergence guarantee for the sparse EM states that the estimate converges geometrically to the true value (in terms of iteration number), up to a statistical error term. This error term scales with the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step, which is typically logarithmic in the ambient dimension. 

This work builds upon recent related results for EM in low-dimensional settings. As in previous work, the analysis relies on assumptions regarding the sufficient smoothness and near-quadraticity of the Q-function within a specific basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized within that basin. The novel condition introduced here concerns control of the infinity norm of the error in estimating the M-step. This condition is specifically required for sparse vectors, but the authors acknowledge that for many models, estimating M across all possible vectors is computationally equivalent to estimating it only for sparse vectors – a point illustrated by the Gaussian mixture example and the non-gradient version of the mixture of regression model. This raises a natural question: is the truncated EM algorithm genuinely necessary? Could a comparable guarantee be obtained by performing standard EM under the same conditions, followed by a single truncation step? *EDIT:* The authors’ observation that linear regression represents a special case here provides a compelling argument against the sufficiency of a single truncation step. 

There is potentially relevant work in the broader field of sparse optimization that the paper could benefit from discussing, for example, Langford et al.’s “Sparse online learning via truncated gradient” (2009).

The experimental section should clarify the initialization strategy used. In summary, the paper proposes a sparse EM variant for parameter estimation in high-dimensional settings. The provided convergence guarantees represent an important contribution to high-dimensional learning, although the necessity of the sparse variant of EM to achieving these results remains somewhat unclear.
