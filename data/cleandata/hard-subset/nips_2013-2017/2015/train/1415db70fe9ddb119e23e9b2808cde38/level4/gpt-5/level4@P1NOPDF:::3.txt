The paper proposes a sparse EM procedure that modifies classical EM by truncating the parameter vector at each iteration to a target sparsity level. It also introduces an asymptotically normal test statistic based on a decorrelated score for testing whether a specific coordinate of the parameter vector is nonzero.

The main theoretical guarantee shows that the sparse EM iterate converges geometrically (in the number of iterations) to the true parameter, up to a statistical error term that scales with the square root of the sparsity level times the l-infinity norm of the M-step’s statistical error. The latter typically depends only logarithmically on the ambient dimension. This extends recent results for EM in low-dimensional settings.

As in prior work, the analysis assumes that the Q-function is sufficiently smooth and approximately quadratic within a basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized within that basin. The new requirement is l-infinity control on the M-step estimation error. While this condition is needed only on sparse vectors, for many models estimating the M-step at arbitrary vectors is no harder than at sparse ones (as in the Gaussian mixture example and in the non-gradient version of the mixture of regression model). This raises the question of whether the truncated EM is strictly necessary. Could one run standard EM under the same conditions and then apply a single truncation at the end, while retaining similar guarantees? EDIT: The authors’ observation that linear regression is a special case here is a convincing argument against relying on a single truncation step.

There appears to be related work in sparse optimization that could be discussed, for example “Sparse Online Learning via Truncated Gradient” (Langford et al., 2009), among others. While the settings differ, the conceptual similarity—iterative truncation to enforce sparsity—seems relevant for positioning the contribution.

The empirical section should clearly state the initialization strategy, given the dependence of the theory on being within the basin of attraction. Reporting sensitivity to different initializations would also be informative.

Overall, the sparse EM variant targets high-dimensional parameter estimation and the convergence guarantees are valuable for high-dimensional learning. However, the necessity of truncating at every iteration for achieving these guarantees remains somewhat unclear under the stated assumptions, given that the critical M-step l-infinity control may already hold more broadly in several models.
