

The paper proposes a sparse EM algorithm that enforces sparsity by truncating the parameter vector at each iteration to a target sparsity level. It also introduces an asymptotically normal test statistic based on a decorrelated score for testing individual parameter entries.  

The convergence guarantee states that the sparse EM estimate converges geometrically to the true parameter (in the number of iterations), up to a statistical error term that scales with the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the estimation error in the M-step. This error is typically logarithmic in the ambient dimension. The result extends recent low-dimensional EM analyses, relying on similar assumptions: smoothness and near-quadratic behavior of the Q-function within a local basin of attraction, alongside initialization within this basin. The key novelty is the requirement for an infinity norm bound on the M-step estimation error—this condition applies only to sparse vectors. However, for many models (e.g., Gaussian mixtures and non-gradient mixture regression), estimating the M-step over all vectors is no harder than over sparse vectors. This raises the question: is the truncation step truly necessary? Could the same guarantee be achieved by running standard EM under identical conditions and then applying a single truncation? The authors’ argument that linear regression serves as a special case against a single truncation step is compelling.  

The paper should reference related work in sparse optimization, such as Langford et al.’s "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" (2009), to contextualize the truncation approach. Additionally, the experimental section must specify the initialization procedure used.  

While the convergence guarantees represent a significant contribution to high-dimensional learning, the necessity of the sparse EM variant for achieving these results remains unclear. The authors do not convincingly demonstrate that standard EM followed by truncation would fail under the stated conditions, despite the linear regression counterexample. The sparse variant’s role appears more procedural than essential, warranting deeper justification. Overall, the technical contribution is promising but hinges on unresolved questions about the algorithm’s fundamental design.
