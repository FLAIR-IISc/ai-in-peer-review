

The paper investigates expectation-maximization (EM) in high-dimensional settings, establishing convergence results and developing an inference procedure. However, several critical points require clarification and strengthening.

First, the authors should address under what conditions an initial solution $\beta^{\rm init}$ satisfying the requirement in line 60 (page 2) can be practically obtained. Second, given the two proposed algorithms, the paper lacks practical guidance on selecting between them. Third, the choice of tuning parameters $\hat{s}$ and $\lambda$ remains unclear: how should they be selected in practice? How sensitive are the results to their choice? Is there a data-driven method for their selection? Given the critical role of tuning parameters in high-dimensional inference literature for achieving good sampling properties, this gap must be addressed.

The decorrelated score test used is identical to that in Ning and Liu (2017) [*]. This prior work must be properly cited, and the paper should explicitly clarify what novel adaptations are required to apply this existing inference procedure to the current latent variable context. The claim of novelty is currently insufficient.

The main theoretical results rely on numerous technical conditions. The authors must discuss whether these conditions are satisfied for the specific latent variable models considered, rather than merely assuming them. 

Furthermore, while the paper cleverly combines existing results (e.g., extending Ning and Liu [2] to high-dimensional EM), the exact novelty and nontrivial technical challenges underlying the main results are not sufficiently articulated. The submitted manuscript reads as a preliminary "trailer" for the supplementary material, yet it should contain more substantive detail on the technical hurdles required to establish the core theoretical contributions. The supplementary material alone cannot substitute for clear exposition of these challenges in the main paper. [*] Ning, Y., & Liu, H. (2017). A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models.
