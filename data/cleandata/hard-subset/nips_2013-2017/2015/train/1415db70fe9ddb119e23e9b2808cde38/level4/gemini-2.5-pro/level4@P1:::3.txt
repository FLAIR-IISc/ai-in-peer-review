This paper presents a framework for parameter estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The primary contributions are twofold. First, the authors propose a high-dimensional EM algorithm that modifies the classical procedure by adding a truncation step after each M-step, which enforces a desired sparsity level on the parameter vector at each iteration. Second, they introduce an inferential procedure based on a decorrelated score statistic, which allows for hypothesis testing on individual components of the high-dimensional parameter and is shown to be asymptotically normal.

The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees for the proposed sparse EM algorithm. Building on recent results for EM in low-dimensional settings, the authors assume a good initialization within a local basin of attraction where the population Q-function satisfies certain smoothness and strong concavity conditions. Under these assumptions, they demonstrate that the sequence of estimates converges at a geometric rate to a solution that lies within a statistical error bound of the true parameter. This statistical error achieves the near-optimal rate for sparse problems, scaling with the square root of the sparsity level (s*) and a logarithmic factor of the ambient dimension (log d).

This work is a significant extension of recent theoretical analyses of the EM algorithm to the more challenging high-dimensional regime. The main strengths of the paper are its originality and potential impact. To my knowledge, this is the first work to establish a computationally feasible approach with rigorous guarantees for both optimal estimation and asymptotic inference for EM in a high-dimensional context. The development of an end-to-end framework that provides both a fast-converging algorithm and a valid method for uncertainty quantification is a major theoretical contribution that addresses a critical gap for high-dimensional latent variable models.

While the results are impressive, a central question arises regarding the necessity of the iterative truncation. The analysis hinges on a key new condition: control of the statistical error of the M-step in the infinity norm. This condition is assumed to hold for sparse input vectors. However, for several models, including the Gaussian mixture example provided, computing the M-step for a dense vector is no more difficult than for a sparse one. This raises the question of whether a simpler approach—for instance, running a standard EM algorithm and applying a single truncation step at the very end—could achieve similar theoretical guarantees under the same conditions. That said, the authors' observation that linear regression is a special case of their framework provides a compelling argument against a single truncation step. Iterative truncation is likely necessary to ensure the solution path remains within a region where the statistical error can be controlled, a property that a single post-hoc truncation may not guarantee.

A few minor points could further improve the paper:

*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of related work in the sparse optimization literature, such as methods based on truncated gradients (e.g., "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" by Langford et al., 2009), to better contextualize the algorithmic contribution.
*   The experimental section should specify the initialization procedure used for the simulations. Since the theory relies on a good initialization, understanding how this was achieved in practice is crucial for assessing the method's practical applicability and for reproducibility.

In conclusion, this paper presents an important and high-quality contribution to high-dimensional learning. The convergence guarantees and the inferential theory for the sparse EM variant represent a significant advance for latent variable models. The work is technically sound, original, and addresses a problem of high significance to the NIPS community.
