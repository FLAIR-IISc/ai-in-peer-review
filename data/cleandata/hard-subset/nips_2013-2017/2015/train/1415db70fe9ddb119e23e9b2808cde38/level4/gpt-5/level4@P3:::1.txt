Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm for latent variable models. The algorithm augments the classical E/M steps with a truncation step that keeps the bs largest coordinates, thereby enforcing sparsity along the optimization path. Two implementations of the M-step are covered: exact maximization and a single gradient-ascent update. Under suitable initialization within a basin of attraction, the iterates contract at a geometric rate up to a statistical error, and the final estimator achieves a (near-)minimax rate of order sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d/n) (up to log factors). Building on this estimator, the paper proposes a decorrelated score for inference on low-dimensional components, proving asymptotic normality with semiparametric-efficient variance in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters.

The work is closely related to Balakrishnan et al. (2014/2016), who analyze EM in low dimensions and obtain geometric convergence to a neighborhood of a local optimum with rate O(sqrt(d/n)). The present paper extends the analysis to high-dimensional regimes by enforcing sparsity and controlling entrywise errors. For inference, the paper employs decorrelation techniques in the spirit of Ning & Liu (2014/2015), van de Geer et al. (2014), and Zhang & Zhang (2014), adapting them to nonconvex latent-variable likelihoods via identities connecting Q-function derivatives to the score and Fisher information. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: integrates sparsity-inducing truncation into EM and analyzes its optimization-statistics tradeoff in high dimensions.
- Theory: establishes geometric contraction plus a sharp statistical term of order sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d/n) (up to logs), under both exact and gradient M-step implementations.
- Inference: provides asymptotically normal, semiparametrically efficient tests for low-dimensional components even when the estimator is not a global optimum of a convex program.
- Generality: conditions are stated abstractly and verified for two canonical latent-variable models; the approach appears portable to other latent structures.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Initialization: The guarantees require an initializer within a relative-error neighborhood of the truth. Please provide concrete, model-specific procedures and conditions that achieve this (e.g., spectral/tensor initializers, method-of-moments, sample splitting), with explicit scaling in n, s*, d, and SNR. In particular, under what separation/SNR and sample-size regimes can βinit satisfying the stated bound be constructed for GMMs and mixtures of regressions?
- Choice of algorithm (exact vs gradient M-step): Offer practical guidance on when to prefer Algorithm 2 vs Algorithm 3. How sensitive are results to the stepsize choice when µ, ν are unknown? Recommend a data-driven stepsize (e.g., backtracking/Armijo) and discuss computational complexity per iteration and overall.
- Tuning parameters: The theory involves bs (sparsity level) and λ for the Dantzig-type estimator w(·). Please provide:
  - Data-driven strategies to set bs (e.g., cross-validation on held-out likelihood/Q, information criteria, stability selection), and discuss robustness to mis-specification (over- vs under-truncation).
  - Calibrations for λ in terms of √(log d/n) with model-specific constants, and practical recipes for selecting it (e.g., multiplier bootstrap, plug-in variance, MAD).
  - Empirical sensitivity analyses, if available.
- Verification of assumptions: Many technical conditions (Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness, Statistical-Error, Fisher information regularity, sparsity of w*) are relegated to the appendix. It would help the reader if the main text summarized, for each model:
  - The explicit basin radius, SNR/separation requirements, and sample-size scaling ensuring the conditions.
  - The order of the entrywise statistical error ε and any extra factors.
  - The sparsity requirements on w* (kw*k0), why they are reasonable, and consequences if violated.
- Relation to existing inference methods: The proposed decorrelated score follows the same construction as prior work (e.g., Ning & Liu; van de Geer et al.; Zhang & Zhang), with the novelty here being its adaptation to latent-variable models whose estimators come from a nonconvex EM procedure. Please cite these works prominently and clarify precisely what is new: (i) the identities (Theorem 2.1) linking Q-derivatives to score/Fisher to enable decorrelation in latent models; (ii) controlling the impact of truncation and approximate M-steps on the score; and (iii) achieving semiparametric efficiency under nonconvex estimation.
- Proof/algorithm mismatch: The main contraction result is stated for a resampled EM variant. Please discuss to what extent the guarantees carry over to the practical, single-sample EM, and whether sample-splitting is recommended in practice.

Evaluation by review criteria
- Quality: The results appear technically sound and are supported by clear high-level conditions that are verified for two models. However, the reliance on many abstract constants and a resampling proof structure makes practical implications harder to assess. Strengthening the discussion on initialization, tuning, and assumption regimes would improve credibility and usability.
- Clarity: The paper is dense and depends heavily on a long appendix. The main text would benefit from: (i) a succinct roadmap of the proof ideas for handling truncation and support evolution; (ii) a table summarizing model-specific conditions and rates; and (iii) more intuition for Theorem 2.1 and the decorrelation step.
- Originality: The truncation-in-EM analysis in high dimensions and the inference guarantees for latent-variable models are novel and nontrivial. The inference component builds on established decorrelation methods; the contribution is their careful adaptation to nonconvex EM estimators.
- Significance: Addressing estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models is important. Achieving near-optimal rates and semiparametric efficiency under an implementable algorithm is impactful, and the framework should be of interest to both theory and practice.

Specific questions and suggestions to authors
- Initialization: Provide explicit initializers and guarantees (e.g., tensor/spectral methods for mixtures, thresholded method-of-moments, or EM warm starts with heavy regularization).
- Tuning: Include concrete defaults and guidance for (bs, λ, stepsize), and sensitivity analysis. Can bs be adapted on the fly (e.g., via a schedule or stopping rule using changes in Q or support stability)?
- Algorithm choice: Compare Algorithms 2 and 3 empirically and discuss convergence-time trade-offs and robustness.
- Assumptions: Move a concise summary of model-specific parameter regimes (SNR, n vs s*, d) to the main text.
- Inference: Clearly cite Ning & Liu (and related works) and articulate the new technical ingredients needed here.
- Practicality: Comment on whether the Dantzig step for w can be replaced by a Lasso-type regression and whether that affects theory.

Pros
- Solid theoretical development with near-optimal rates.
- First comprehensive inference framework for high-dimensional latent-variable models via EM.
- Covers both exact and approximate M-steps; general and potentially extensible.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on technical conditions and resampling proofs, with limited main-text guidance.
- Lack of practical prescriptions for initialization and tuning.
- Inference method largely builds on prior decorrelation literature; novelty should be better delineated.

Overall assessment
A strong and timely theoretical contribution that extends EM analysis to high-dimensional settings and brings valid inference to latent-variable models. With clearer guidance on initialization/tuning, explicit discussion of assumption regimes, and proper positioning relative to existing inference work, this paper would merit acceptance. I currently view it as borderline accept leaning positive.
