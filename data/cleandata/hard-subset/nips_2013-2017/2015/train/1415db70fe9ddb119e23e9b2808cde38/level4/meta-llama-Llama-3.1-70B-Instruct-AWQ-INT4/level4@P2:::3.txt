This paper proposes a sparse EM algorithm for parameter estimation in high-dimensional settings and develops a novel asymptotically normal statistic for testing hypotheses about low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. The sparse EM algorithm modifies the classical EM algorithm by truncating the parameter vector at each step to a desired sparsity level, effectively incorporating sparsity structure into parameter estimation. The authors establish a theoretical guarantee for the sparse EM algorithm, demonstrating that the estimate converges geometrically to the true value up to a statistical error term. This result is significant for high-dimensional learning, as it provides a computationally feasible approach for optimal parameter estimation.

The proposed asymptotically normal statistic, based on a decorrelated score, allows for testing whether a specific entry of the parameter vector is non-zero. This is an important contribution, as it enables the construction of confidence intervals for low-dimensional parameters in high-dimensional linear models. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by applying it to Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

The paper builds upon recent results for EM in the low-dimensional setting, assuming that the Q function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic in a certain basin of attraction. The algorithm is initialized in the same basin, and the authors introduce a new condition, the infinity norm control on the error in estimating M, which is required only for sparse vectors. This raises the question of whether the truncated EM algorithm is necessary, as a similar guarantee might be obtained by performing ordinary EM under the same conditions, followed by a single truncation step.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach, including a detailed discussion of the conditions required for the sparse EM algorithm to work. They also provide a comparison with related work, including tensor methods and sparse optimization techniques. However, the experimental section is limited, and the authors should provide more details about the initialization used in their experiments.

In terms of related work, the authors could further discuss the connection between their approach and other sparse optimization techniques, such as the "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" method proposed by Langford et al. (2009). This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the sparse EM algorithm and its relationship to other methods in the field.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of high-dimensional learning, providing a novel approach for parameter estimation and hypothesis testing. While some questions remain about the necessity of the truncated EM algorithm, the authors' analysis and results demonstrate the importance of their approach.
