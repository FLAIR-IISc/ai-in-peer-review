This paper investigates the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for sparse latent variable models in a high-dimensional setting. The authors establish theoretical guarantees for the convergence of their proposed EM procedure and develop a method for statistical inference, including hypothesis testing and the construction of confidence intervals.

The work combines existing high-dimensional techniques in a clever way, primarily by extending prior analyses of EM to a high-dimensional regime and employing the decorrelated score framework of Ning and Liu [*] for inference. While the direction is promising, the paper's primary weakness is its lack of clarity regarding the precise novelty of its contributions. The authors should explicitly delineate which components are direct applications of existing results and what new technical challenges arose that required non-trivial extensions.

Major points for revision are detailed below:

**1. Clarification of Novelty and Relation to Prior Work**
A significant concern is the treatment of the decorrelated score test. The proposed inference procedure appears to be a direct application of the general theory developed by Ning and Liu [*]. This prior work must be appropriately cited and discussed in detail. More importantly, the authors should clarify if any new theoretical developments were necessary to adapt this existing inference framework to the EM setting for latent variable models. For example, were there specific challenges in constructing the inverse covariance matrix estimator or in analyzing the score function in this context? Without this clarification, the novelty of the inference portion of the paper is difficult to assess.

**2. Practical Implementation and Tuning Parameters**
The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing more guidance on practical implementation.
*   **Algorithm Choice:** Two distinct algorithms are proposed. The authors should offer practical advice or empirical evidence on when one might be preferred over the other.
*   **Tuning Parameter Selection:** The choice of tuning parameters, such as $\hat{s}$ and $\lambda$, is critical for the performance of high-dimensional methods. The paper currently lacks a discussion on how to select these in a data-driven manner (e.g., via cross-validation). Furthermore, an analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the choice of these parameters would be valuable. The literature on high-dimensional inference has repeatedly shown that theoretical guarantees can be fragile without robust, data-dependent methods for parameter tuning.

**3. Justification of Theoretical Assumptions**
The theoretical results rely on a number of technical conditions, and their plausibility should be discussed.
*   **Initialization:** A key assumption is the existence of an initial solution, $\beta^{\text{init}}$, that lies within a certain neighborhood of the true parameter (as specified on page 2, line 60). This is a strong requirement. The authors should discuss under what conditions such an initializer can be found and, ideally, provide a practical algorithm for obtaining it.
*   **Verification of Assumptions:** The main theorems are contingent on a large set of technical assumptions. The paper would be more convincing if the authors discussed whether these assumptions are realistic and verifiable for the specific latent variable models under consideration (e.g., mixture models, mixed-effects models).

**4. Presentation and Technical Depth**
The main body of the paper defers most technical details to the extensive supplementary material. While this is common due to space constraints, the current submission reads more like a summary of results than a self-contained paper. To better convince the reader of the work's technical depth, the authors should consider moving key technical lemmas, proof sketches, or more detailed intuition from the supplement into the main text. This would help illuminate the specific challenges encountered and the novel techniques developed to overcome them.

In summary, the paper tackles an important problem and presents an interesting synthesis of existing ideas. However, it requires substantial revision to clarify its novel contributions, justify its theoretical assumptions, and provide crucial practical guidance for implementation. Addressing these points is essential to make a compelling case for the paper's contribution to the field.
