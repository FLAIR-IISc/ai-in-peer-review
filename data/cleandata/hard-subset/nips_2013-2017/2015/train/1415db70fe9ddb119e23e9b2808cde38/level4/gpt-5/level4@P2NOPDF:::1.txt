This paper analyzes the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings, establishing convergence guarantees and developing an associated inference procedure. The theoretical framework is ambitious and potentially impactful, as it aims to merge nonconvex optimization (EM) with modern high-dimensional inference.

Strengths:
- Provides convergence results for EM under high dimensionality.
- Proposes two algorithmic variants and an inference procedure.
- Seeks to leverage decorrelated-score methodology for valid inference after high-dimensional estimation.

Major concerns and requests for clarification:
1) Initialization. The results assume an initial estimator beta_init that satisfies the condition stated on p.2, line 60. Please discuss concrete conditions or algorithms under which such an initializer can be obtained in the considered models. For example, can spectral methods, method-of-moments, sample splitting, or a few steps of gradient EM guarantee the required proximity? What sample size and signal-to-noise conditions are needed? Without a practical path to such an initializer, the guarantees are difficult to interpret.

2) Choice between the two proposed algorithms. Please provide practical guidance on when to use each algorithm. For instance, how do they compare in computation, robustness to model misspecification, statistical efficiency, and stability to initialization? Are there regimes (sample size, sparsity level, or signal strength) where one dominates the other? Empirical comparisons illustrating these trade-offs would be valuable.

3) Tuning parameters. The procedure depends on s_hat and lambda. How should these be set in practice, and how sensitive are the results to their values? Is there a data-driven selection strategy (e.g., cross-validation, information criteria, theory-guided scaling) that retains valid inference? In high-dimensional inference, tuning plays a critical role in both power and coverage; please discuss defaults, robustness, and, if possible, theoretical guarantees for data-dependent choices.

4) Relation to existing inference methods. The decorrelated score test used here appears to coincide with that of Ning and Liu, “A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High-Dimensional Models.” This connection should be explicitly cited and discussed. What, if anything, is new in adapting their framework to the EM context? For example, do you need new concentration bounds, nuisance-gradient approximations, or verification of orthogonality in latent-variable models? Please delineate which parts are direct applications of existing results and which require novel technical work.

5) Assumptions and their verifiability. The main results rest on many technical conditions. Please explain which of these are satisfied for the latent-variable models you study and under what regimes (e.g., restricted strong concavity/smoothness, gradient stability, Fisher information invertibility, and sparsity structure). A table or succinct checklist mapping assumptions to specific models and proofs would greatly improve clarity. If some conditions are only verifiable in idealized settings (e.g., Gaussian covariates or well-separated components), please state this explicitly.

Novelty and contribution:
- The paper extends existing EM analyses to high dimensions and applies the Ning–Liu decorrelated-score framework for inference. While the integration is clever, the novelty relative to prior work needs to be articulated more clearly. What aspects of the EM analysis go beyond known results? Why is applying decorrelated scores here nontrivial? Please identify the key technical bottlenecks and summarize the new proof ideas that overcome them (e.g., controlling nonconvexity-induced bias in high dimension, bounding the error of the E-step’s surrogate, or establishing uniform stability across iterations).

Presentation:
- The submission reads as a trailer for the much longer supplement. The main paper should better highlight the technical challenges and provide intuition for the main lemmas and how they fit together. Even a compact proof sketch for a central theorem would help readers assess the rigor and novelty without relying entirely on the supplement.

Overall assessment:
This is a timely topic that aims to bridge nonconvex estimation via EM with valid high-dimensional inference. The theoretical results could be impactful, but the paper should (i) justify and operationalize the initialization requirement, (ii) provide concrete guidance on algorithm and tuning choices, (iii) clearly credit and position the inference procedure relative to Ning–Liu, and (iv) demonstrate that the technical assumptions hold for the intended latent-variable models. Clarifying these points would significantly strengthen the contribution and its practical relevance.
