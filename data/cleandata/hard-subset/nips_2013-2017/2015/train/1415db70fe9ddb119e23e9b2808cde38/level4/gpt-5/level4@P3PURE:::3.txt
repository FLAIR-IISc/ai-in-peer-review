This paper proposes a sparse EM procedure that modifies the classical EM algorithm by truncating the parameter vector at each iteration to a prescribed sparsity level. It also introduces an asymptotically normal statistic, based on a decorrelated score, for testing whether a specific entry of the parameter vector is nonzero.

The main guarantee for the sparse EM shows geometric convergence (in the number of iterations) toward the true parameter, up to a statistical error term of order sqrt(s) times the ℓ∞ norm of the M-step statistical error, which is typically only logarithmic in the ambient dimension. This result follows recent analogous guarantees for EM in low-dimensional settings. As in that line of work, the analysis assumes the Q-function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic within a basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized in this basin. The new condition here is an ℓ∞ control on the M-step estimation error. Although this condition is required only for sparse vectors, in many models establishing it uniformly over all parameter vectors is no harder than doing so for sparse ones (as in the Gaussian mixture example considered and the non-gradient version for the mixture-of-regressions model). This raises a question: is the truncated EM algorithm necessary? Could one obtain a similar guarantee by running ordinary EM under the same conditions and then applying a single truncation step? Edit: The authors’ observation that linear regression is a special case here is a convincing argument against relying on a single post-EM truncation step.

There appears to be related work in sparse optimization that would be useful to discuss, such as “Sparse online learning via truncated gradient” (Langford et al., 2009), among others.

The experimental section should specify the initialization used.

Overall, the paper proposes a sparse EM variant for high-dimensional parameter estimation. The convergence guarantees are an important contribution to high-dimensional learning, although the importance of the sparse EM variant itself to obtaining these results remains somewhat unclear.
