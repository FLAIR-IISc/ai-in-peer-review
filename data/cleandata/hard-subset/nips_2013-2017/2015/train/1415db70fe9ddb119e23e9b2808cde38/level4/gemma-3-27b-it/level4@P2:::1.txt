This paper investigates the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, providing convergence guarantees and developing an inferential procedure. The core contribution lies in a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm incorporating sparsity, achieving near-optimal statistical rates, and enabling asymptotic inference via a decorrelated score test. The work builds upon existing literature, notably extending results from [2] to the high-dimensional regime and leveraging techniques from Ning and Liu [1]. 

The paper begins by correctly identifying a gap between the computational limitations of standard EM (converging to local optima) and the statistical guarantees typically requiring global optima. It proposes addressing this gap with a truncated EM algorithm designed to promote sparsity. The theoretical analysis focuses on establishing both optimization and statistical rates, demonstrating geometric convergence under appropriate initialization and achieving a p√s*log(d/n) rate, where s* is the sparsity level and d is the dimensionality. Furthermore, the authors develop a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing concerning low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters, achieving asymptotic normality.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a critical issue – the lack of theoretical guarantees for EM in high dimensions – a common scenario in modern applications.
*   **Technical Depth:** The analysis is mathematically rigorous, building upon recent advances in high-dimensional statistics and optimization. The proposed algorithm and associated theory appear well-developed.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The development of an inferential procedure is valuable, enabling hypothesis testing and confidence interval construction, crucial for real-world applications.
*   **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper explicitly acknowledges and builds upon related work, particularly [2] and [1], demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** The requirement for a specific initialization (line 60, page 2) raises a practical concern. The paper would benefit from a discussion on how to obtain such an initialization, or providing guidance on strategies for finding a suitable starting point.
*   **Algorithm Selection:** The paper presents two M-step implementations (Algorithm 2 & 3). It lacks practical guidance on which implementation to choose and under what conditions each performs best.
*   **Tuning Parameter Selection:** The choice of the sparsity parameter *bs* and other tuning parameters (e.g., lambda) is critical. The paper needs to address how these parameters can be selected in practice, potentially through data-dependent methods, and explore the sensitivity of the results to their choice. Given the importance of tuning parameter selection in high-dimensional inference, a more in-depth discussion is warranted.
*   **Novelty Clarification:** While the authors acknowledge building upon existing work, a clearer articulation of the *novel* contributions of this paper beyond the combination of existing tools would be beneficial. Specifically, explaining what makes the application of these tools non-trivial in the current context would strengthen the presentation.
*   **Technical Condition Verification:** The results rely on numerous technical conditions. The paper should explicitly discuss whether these conditions are satisfied for the specific latent variable models considered (Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression) or provide a roadmap for verifying them for other models. The details seem relegated to supplementary material; a more concise overview in the main text would improve accessibility.
*   **Citation of Related Inference Work:** The decorrelated score test shares similarities with work by Ning and Liu [1]. This connection should be explicitly acknowledged with a clear citation and a discussion of how the current work extends or differs from their approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental theoretical gap in the analysis of EM algorithms.
*   The proposed algorithm and theoretical results are technically sound and potentially impactful.
*   The work demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature and provides valuable insights into high-dimensional inference.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The practical considerations regarding initialization and tuning parameter selection are not adequately addressed.
*   The level of detail is somewhat unbalanced, with significant reliance on supplementary material, hindering the accessibility of the core contributions.
*   The articulation of novelty could be improved.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding practical implementation and a clearer presentation of novelty, would substantially strengthen the submission. I would lean towards **weak accept** contingent on the authors addressing the points above in a revision. A more thorough discussion of practical considerations would greatly enhance the paper’s value to the broader NIPS community.
