This paper addresses the challenging problem of applying the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to high-dimensional latent variable models, where the parameter of interest is sparse. The authors develop a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, and provide theoretical guarantees for its convergence rate and the asymptotic normality of the resulting estimator. They establish that, with appropriate initialization, the algorithm achieves a (near-)optimal statistical rate of convergence of O(sqrt(s*log(d/n))), where s is the sparsity level and d is the dimension. Furthermore, they propose a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing regarding low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter, demonstrating optimality in terms of the semiparametric information bound.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated, placing the work within the context of existing literature on the EM algorithm and high-dimensional statistics. The authors thoroughly review related work, highlighting the gaps their research addresses. The technical contributions—establishing both statistical and computational guarantees for the proposed algorithm—are significant. The proposed truncation step is a key innovation for handling high dimensionality and inducing sparsity. The development of a decorrelated score statistic for inference is also a valuable contribution.

However, the paper is very dense and technically demanding. While the organization is logical, the mathematical notation and derivations are complex, potentially hindering accessibility for readers not deeply familiar with the field.  The theoretical results, while rigorous, could benefit from more intuitive explanations. 

Several points warrant further clarification. The claim of a minimax-optimal rate of convergence (sqrt(s*log(d/n))) is stated without explicit justification within the main manuscript. Providing a more direct link to relevant minimax lower bounds would strengthen this claim.  Practical considerations regarding parameter tuning are also underexplored. Specifically, the paper does not provide clear guidance on how to choose the sparsity parameter bs (defined in Eq. 3.8) in practice, given that the true sparsity level ‘s’ is unknown. Similarly, the requirement for a specific initialization (||beta^init - beta*||_2 < R/2) poses a practical challenge, and the authors should discuss strategies for obtaining such an initial estimate. Finally, a more intuitive explanation of the necessity of the truncation step (Algorithm 1, line 6) and a discussion of the algorithm’s behavior *without* truncation would be beneficial for understanding its role. What issues arise without truncation, and how does it address them?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The work presents a novel algorithm and theoretical framework for addressing a challenging problem in high-dimensional statistics.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous statistical guarantees for both estimation and inference, establishing convergence rates and asymptotic normality.
*   **Significance:** The results advance the state of the art in applying the EM algorithm to high-dimensional settings, potentially impacting various applications involving latent variable models.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithm and theoretical analysis appear technically sound and well-motivated.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Accessibility:** The paper is dense and technically demanding, potentially limiting its accessibility to a broad audience.
*   **Practical Guidance:** The paper lacks practical guidance on parameter tuning and initialization, which could hinder its real-world application.
*   **Intuitive Explanation:**  Some key aspects of the algorithm (e.g., the truncation step) and theoretical results could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
