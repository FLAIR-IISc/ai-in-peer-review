This paper introduces a modification of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm tailored for high-dimensional settings where the true parameter is assumed to be sparse. The authors provide theoretical guarantees, establishing conditions for the algorithm's convergence to the global optimum and deriving its rate of convergence. Furthermore, the paper presents a statistical framework for testing the sparsity of the estimated parameter.

The paper is well-organized and includes a sufficient summary of related work. The theoretical contributions appear sound. However, the presentation is very dense, which makes the core technical sections (particularly 3 and 4) difficult to follow. The paper would be significantly more accessible to a broader audience if the authors provided more intuition behind their theoretical results and simplified the exposition.

**Major Comments and Questions:**

1.  **Minimax Optimality:** On line 66, the authors state that the rate of sqrt(s*log(d/n)) is minimax-optimal. A clear proof or a specific citation for this claim should be provided in the main manuscript to make the paper self-contained and substantiate this important claim.

2.  **Practical Considerations:** There are concerns regarding the practical implementation of the proposed method, as some theoretical requirements seem to rely on unknown quantities:
    *   How should the parameter `^s` (Equation 3.8) be chosen in practice, given its dependence on the true sparsity level `s`, which is typically unknown?
    *   The convergence proof requires an initial value `beta^init` such that ||beta^init - beta*||_2 < R/2 (line 275). How can such an initialization be found in a real-world setting where `beta*` is unknown? The authors should discuss practical strategies for initialization and the algorithm's sensitivity to this choice.

3.  **Role of the Truncation Step:** The motivation for the truncation step in Algorithm 1 is unclear. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed explanation of its importance. For instance, what would be the theoretical or empirical consequences of removing this step from the algorithm? A discussion of its role in the convergence proof or its effect on performance would be very helpful for understanding the mechanics of the proposed method.

**Concluding Summary:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with rigorous analysis, making it a valuable contribution to the statistics literature. However, its dense mathematical presentation may limit its accessibility for a broader machine learning audience. The paper's impact could also be enhanced by bridging the gap between theory and practice, particularly concerning hyperparameter selection and initialization. While the work is technically solid, its practical utility for real-world applications remains somewhat unclear without further guidance.
