The paper studies the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in a high-dimensional setting, providing convergence results and developing an inference procedure. The authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity and establishes both statistical and computational guarantees, including asymptotic normality for parameter estimation. The work extends existing results on the EM algorithm, particularly [2], to the high-dimensional regime, and leverages tools from recent advances in high-dimensional inference, such as those in [Ning and Liu, 2017]. 

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant gap between the theoretical guarantees for low-dimensional EM and its practical application in high-dimensional scenarios. The core idea of incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity is a sensible approach. The authors clearly state their contributions, which include both an algorithm and theoretical results establishing its convergence and statistical properties. The presentation is generally clear, though somewhat dense, and the paper follows a logical structure.

However, several points could be strengthened to improve clarity and impact. First, the practical implications of the initialization requirement (line 60, page 2) – specifically, how to find an initial solution satisfying the given condition – should be discussed. Providing guidance on this point would enhance the paper's practicality.

Second, while two implementations of the M-step are presented (Algorithms 2 and 3), the paper lacks practical guidance on which one to prefer. Discussing the trade-offs between computational cost and accuracy for each implementation would be valuable.

Third, the sensitivity of the results to the choice of tuning parameters, $\hat{s}$ and $\lambda$, should be addressed. How can these parameters be selected in a data-dependent manner? Given the importance of tuning parameter selection in high-dimensional inference, a discussion of this aspect is crucial.

Fourth, the authors state that the decorrelated score test is similar to that in [Ning and Liu, 2017].  This connection should be explicitly cited and discussed in more detail. What specific aspects of the application to latent variable models require novel development beyond simply applying the existing procedure?  Is there any adaptation or refinement needed?

Finally, the main theoretical results rely on a substantial number of technical conditions. The authors should explicitly discuss whether these conditions are satisfied for the specific latent variable models considered (Gaussian Mixture Model and Mixture of Regression Model). A more detailed discussion of the conditions’ plausibility and implications would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to high-dimensional EM with theoretical guarantees.
*   Combines existing techniques in a clever and principled manner.
*   Addresses a significant problem with practical relevance.
*   Clear presentation of contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of practical guidance on initialization and tuning parameter selection.
*   Insufficient discussion of the novelty compared to existing work, particularly [Ning and Liu, 2017].
*   Limited discussion of the validity of technical conditions for specific models.
*   The paper feels somewhat incomplete, relying heavily on supplementary material for detailed explanations.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the theory of high-dimensional EM. However, addressing the concerns outlined above – particularly regarding practical implementation and clarity of novelty – would significantly strengthen the manuscript. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing these points.

**Reference:**

Ning, Y., & Liu, H. (2017). A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models. *Journal of the Royal Statistical Society: Series B (Statistical Methodology)*, *79*(4), 847–874.
