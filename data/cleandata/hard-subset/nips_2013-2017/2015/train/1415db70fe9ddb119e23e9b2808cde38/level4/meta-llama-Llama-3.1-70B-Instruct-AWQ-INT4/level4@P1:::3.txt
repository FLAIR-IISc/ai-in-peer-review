This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure into parameter estimation for high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors also develop an asymptotically normal decorrelated score statistic for testing hypotheses related to low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter.

The high-dimensional EM algorithm modifies the classical EM algorithm by introducing a truncation step that enforces the sparsity of the attained estimator. The authors establish simultaneous statistical and computational guarantees for the proposed algorithm and the respective uncertainty assessment procedure. Specifically, they prove that the algorithm converges geometrically to the true value up to a statistical error term that is proportional to the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step.

The proposed decorrelated score statistic is shown to be asymptotically normal, and its limiting variance is optimal in the sense that it attains the semiparametric information bound for the low-dimensional components of interest in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework by applying it to two high-dimensional latent variable models, namely Gaussian mixture model and mixture of regression model.

Overall, the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of high-dimensional learning by establishing a general inferential theory for simultaneous parameter estimation and uncertainty assessment based on a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure into parameter estimation for high-dimensional latent variable models.
* The authors establish simultaneous statistical and computational guarantees for the proposed algorithm and the respective uncertainty assessment procedure.
* The proposed decorrelated score statistic is shown to be asymptotically normal, and its limiting variance is optimal.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes that the Q function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic in a certain basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized in the same basin. This assumption may not hold in all cases.
* The new condition of infinity norm control on the error in estimating M is required only for sparse vectors. However, for many models, estimating M at all possible vectors is just as easy as at sparse vectors.
* The paper does not provide a clear justification for the necessity of the truncated EM algorithm. Could a similar guarantee be obtained by performing ordinary EM under the same conditions, followed by a single truncation step?

Suggestions:

* The authors should provide more discussion on the related work in sparse optimization and its relevance to the proposed algorithm.
* The experimental section should state what initialization was used for the high-dimensional EM algorithm.
* The authors should provide more justification for the necessity of the truncated EM algorithm and discuss the implications of their results in the context of high-dimensional learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a significant contribution to the field of high-dimensional learning by establishing a general inferential theory for simultaneous parameter estimation and uncertainty assessment based on a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm.
* The proposed algorithm and decorrelated score statistic have the potential to be widely applicable in high-dimensional latent variable models.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper assumes that the Q function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic in a certain basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized in the same basin. This assumption may not hold in all cases.
* The new condition of infinity norm control on the error in estimating M is required only for sparse vectors. However, for many models, estimating M at all possible vectors is just as easy as at sparse vectors.
* The paper does not provide a clear justification for the necessity of the truncated EM algorithm. Could a similar guarantee be obtained by performing ordinary EM under the same conditions, followed by a single truncation step?
