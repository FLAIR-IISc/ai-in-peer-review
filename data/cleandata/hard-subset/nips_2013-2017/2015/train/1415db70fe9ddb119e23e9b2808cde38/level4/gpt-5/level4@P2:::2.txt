Summary
This paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm for latent variable models that leverages sparsity. The method augments the classical E- and M-steps with a truncation step (T-step) that keeps the largest bs coordinates, enforcing sparsity along the iterates. The authors analyze both exact maximization and gradient-ascent implementations of the M-step. With a suitable initialization, they establish geometric optimization convergence to a neighborhood of the truth and a final estimation error that matches the near-minimax rate, roughly sqrt(s* log d / n) up to logarithmic factors. Beyond estimation, they construct a decorrelated score (and corresponding Wald) test for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, showing asymptotic normality with variance achieving the semiparametric information bound. They illustrate the framework on Gaussian mixture models and mixtures of regressions, contrasting with prior EM analyses in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) and with debiasing/desparsification methods that focus on convex GLMs, as well as tensor approaches that can be suboptimal or low-dimensional.

Strengths
- A unified computational-statistical analysis for high-dimensional EM with explicit optimization and statistical error decomposition.
- Near-optimal estimation rates under sparsity and asymptotically valid inference achieving semiparametric efficiency.
- Covers both exact and gradient M-step implementations, with clear conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity-smoothness, and statistical error) and model instantiations.
- To my knowledge, provides one of the first general high-dimensional inferential frameworks for latent-variable EM estimators that are not global optima of convex programs.

Weaknesses and questions
- Readability: Sections 3 and 4 are quite dense and difficult to follow for many ML readers. More intuition, streamlined proofs in the main text, and a schematic of the proof flow would help.
- Practical guidance: bs in Eq. (3.8) depends on s*, which is unknown. Please provide a principled data-driven choice (e.g., cross-validation, information criteria, or adaptive thresholding) and discuss sensitivity.
- Initialization: The guarantees require an initializer β_init with ||β_init − β*||_2 ≤ R/2 (line 275). In practice, how can one obtain such an initializer reliably? For the two examples, could you describe and empirically validate concrete initializers (e.g., method-of-moments, spectral, or warm-start L1-penalized estimators)?
- Role of the T-step: Please clarify why the truncation step is essential. What fails if we omit it (e.g., loss of entrywise control leading to inflated error and drift outside the basin)? A small ablation showing EM without truncation (or with only L1-regularized M-steps) would be informative.
- Minimax optimality: On line 066, the manuscript claims the sqrt(s* log(d)/n) (or written as sqrt(s* log(d/n))) rate is (near-)minimax-optimal. Could you either include a self-contained argument or cite a precise minimax lower bound for the considered model classes in the main text (not only the supplement), and reconcile the log(d) vs. log(d/n) notation?
- Assumptions: The sparsity of w* (from the Dantzig selector in inference) is crucial. Please discuss when this holds in the presented models and practical diagnostics for violation. Also comment on robustness to model misspecification and finite-sample performance when the basin-of-attraction conditions are only approximately met.

Quality
- Technical soundness appears strong, with careful separation of optimization and statistical errors and explicit verification for two models. The asymptotic inference result and efficiency claim are convincing under the stated assumptions. Additional empirical validation and practical tuning guidance would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized with thorough related work, but Sections 3–4 are hard to parse. Suggestions: move some technical conditions to an appendix while adding intuition/examples in the main text; include a figure summarizing the algorithm and proof roadmap; add a practical “how to tune and initialize” subsection.

Originality
- The truncation-augmented EM with a unified high-dimensional analysis and decorrelated score for latent-variable models is novel relative to prior low-dimensional EM analyses and debiased-Lasso inference for convex GLMs.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is high. Practical significance depends on the availability of good initializers and tuning rules; clearer guidance and experiments would broaden impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, general theoretical contributions bridging computation and inference in high-dimensional latent-variable settings.
- Near-optimal rates and semiparametric efficiency.
- Applicability to multiple models and both M-step implementations.

Cons for acceptance
- Steep readability curve; limited practical guidance on tuning and initialization.
- Empirical evaluation and real-data utility remain limited, making practical usefulness less clear.

Requests for revision
- Provide a clear, main-text justification or citation for the minimax optimality claim.
- Add concrete procedures for choosing bs and initializing β, with empirical sensitivity analyses.
- Explain and, if possible, empirically demonstrate the necessity of the T-step.
- Improve exposition in Sections 3–4 with added intuition and a proof roadmap.

Overall recommendation
- I lean positive based on the strength and generality of the theory, with reservations about readability and practical guidance.
