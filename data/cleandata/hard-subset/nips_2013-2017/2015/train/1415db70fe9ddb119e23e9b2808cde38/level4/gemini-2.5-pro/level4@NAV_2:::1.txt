This paper presents a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings, where the number of parameters `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation step within each iteration to enforce sparsity on the parameter estimates. They establish that, with a proper initialization, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`. Second, building on this estimator, they develop a decorrelated score test for conducting statistical inference on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. They prove this test statistic is asymptotically normal and that its limiting variance attains the semiparametric information bound, rendering it optimal. This work extends the low-dimensional analysis of the EM algorithm, such as that by Balakrishnan et al. (2017), to the challenging high-dimensional sparse regime. The key innovation for the estimation portion is the integration of the truncation step and the corresponding theoretical analysis, which requires a more fine-grained, entrywise characterization of the statistical error. For inference, the work adapts the debiasing/decorrelating framework, which has been successful for high-dimensional convex models, to the context of EM, where the estimator is a local optimum of a non-convex likelihood.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a critical and long-standing problem. The EM algorithm is a workhorse for latent variable models, yet its theoretical guarantees in the prevalent high-dimensional setting are lacking. This work provides a much-needed bridge between computational practice and statistical theory, offering what appears to be the first computationally feasible method with guarantees for both optimal estimation and valid inference in this context.
*   **Quality and Completeness:** The theoretical treatment is thorough, addressing both the computational aspects (geometric convergence) and the statistical properties (estimation rates and asymptotic normality for inference). The results are strong, establishing near-optimal statistical rates and semiparametric efficiency, which are the gold standard for high-dimensional analysis.
*   **Generality:** The framework is developed under a set of general conditions and its applicability is demonstrated on important and non-trivial examples, including Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models. This suggests the approach could have broad impact across various applications that rely on latent variable models.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

1.  **Practicality of Initialization:** The main theoretical results hinge on the availability of an initial estimator `init` that lies within a certain radius of the true parameter `*`. While this is a standard assumption in the analysis of non-convex optimization problems, the paper would be significantly more impactful if it provided concrete guidance on how to obtain such an initial estimator for the models considered. A discussion of practical, polynomial-time procedures (e.g., based on spectral methods or other moment-based approaches) that can provably satisfy the initialization condition would be a valuable addition.
2.  **Choice of Tuning Parameters:** The proposed algorithm and inference procedure depend on crucial tuning parameters, namely the sparsity level `bs` in Algorithm 1 and the regularization parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector in the decorrelated score test. The practical performance and theoretical validity of high-dimensional methods are often highly sensitive to the choice of these parameters. The paper does not discuss how to select these in a data-driven way (e.g., via cross-validation). Providing guidance on this practical issue is essential for the method's usability.
3.  **Originality in the Context of High-Dimensional Inference:** The construction of the decorrelated score test is highly reminiscent of the general framework for hypothesis testing in high-dimensional models proposed by Ning and Liu (JASA, 2017), among others. The paper should explicitly acknowledge and cite this line of work and clarify its novel contributions. The main challenge here is adapting such an inference procedure, typically developed for global optima of convex programs, to the local solution returned by the non-convex EM algorithm. A more detailed discussion of the specific technical innovations required to overcome this challenge would help delineate the paper's novelty.
4.  **Verification of Technical Conditions:** The analysis relies on a number of high-level technical conditions (e.g., Conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.4). While the authors state that these are verified in the appendix, the main text would be more convincing and self-contained with some additional intuition or proof sketches for why these assumptions are reasonable and hold for the specific latent variable models under study.
5.  **Guidance on M-step Implementation:** The paper presents two implementations for the M-step: exact maximization (Algorithm 2) and gradient ascent (Algorithm 3). It would be helpful to include a discussion on the trade-offs between these two approaches. Are there classes of models where one is computationally superior or leads to better theoretical guarantees than the other?

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper addresses a highly important problem at the intersection of optimization and high-dimensional statistics. The proposed framework is comprehensive, providing strong theoretical guarantees for both estimation and inference for the widely used EM algorithm in a sparse, high-dimensional setting. The work appears technically solid and its potential impact is substantial.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The paper's novelty concerning the inference procedure is not clearly situated within the existing literature on high-dimensional inference. Crucial practical details regarding initialization and tuning parameter selection are omitted, which currently limits the method's immediate applicability. The presentation could be improved by providing more insight into the verification of the key technical assumptions in the main text.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions. Addressing the points above, particularly regarding the practical implementation and a clearer positioning of the inference methodology, would further strengthen the manuscript.
