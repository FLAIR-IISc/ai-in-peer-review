

This paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm for latent variable models, establishing convergence guarantees and developing a novel inferential framework for high-dimensional settings. The authors address the significant gap between computational and statistical guarantees in high-dimensional latent variable models by introducing a truncation step that enforces sparsity structure. The work builds upon [2], which established low-dimensional convergence rates for EM, but extends it to high dimensions by incorporating sparsity constraints. The main contributions are: (1) a high-dimensional EM algorithm with geometric convergence to a (near-)optimal statistical rate of O(√(s* log d/n)), and (2) a decorrelated score test for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional components that achieves semiparametric efficiency.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees for high-dimensional EM, which is the first such framework for latent variable models in high dimensions. The truncation step (T-step) is a clever technical innovation that enables the analysis of sparsity structure along the solution path. The authors demonstrate the framework's applicability to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, providing concrete verification of their conditions for these specific cases. The decorrelated score test achieves the semiparametric information bound, which is a significant theoretical achievement for inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured, with clear theoretical results supported by rigorous analysis. The main theorems establish the decomposition of estimation error into optimization and statistical components, with the statistical error term achieving the near-minimax optimal rate. The presentation of the algorithms (Algorithms 1-3) and the decorrelated score test (Equations 2.5-2.7) is clear and well-motivated.

However, several aspects require clarification to strengthen the paper's contribution and practical relevance:

1. The paper lacks discussion of practical initialization strategies. The requirement for an initialization satisfying ||β^init - β*||/||β*|| ≤ ε (line 60) is critical but not addressed in terms of how to obtain such an initialization in practice. For high-dimensional problems, finding a suitable initialization is often nontrivial, and the authors should discuss methods like spectral initialization or simple thresholding that could satisfy this condition.

2. The paper proposes two implementations of the M-step (exact maximization vs. gradient ascent) but provides no practical guidance on when to use each. The authors should add a discussion of computational complexity, empirical performance, and practical considerations for selecting between these implementations.

3. The choice of tuning parameters (ŝ and λ) is critical for high-dimensional inference, yet the paper lacks discussion of how to select them in practice. The authors should address sensitivity to these parameters and discuss data-dependent selection methods (e.g., cross-validation or stability selection). The existing literature on high-dimensional inference (e.g., Ning & Liu [27]) emphasizes that tuning parameter choice is crucial for good sampling properties.

4. The decorrelated score test appears to be an adaptation of the method in Ning & Liu [27], but the paper does not clearly distinguish how the application to latent variable models requires new theoretical developments. The authors should explicitly state what novel technical challenges arise in adapting this method to their framework and how their analysis differs from the existing work.

5. While the paper mentions that conditions will be verified for specific models in the appendix, the main paper should include a brief discussion of whether these conditions hold for the Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models they apply the framework to. The reader should be convinced that the conditions are not vacuous for the considered models.

The paper has strong theoretical contributions that advance the understanding of EM in high-dimensional settings. However, the practical relevance would be significantly enhanced by addressing the initialization issue, providing guidance on algorithm selection, and clarifying the tuning parameter choices. The connection to existing work on high-dimensional inference should be more explicitly articulated, particularly regarding the novelty of their decorrelated score test for latent variable models.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of EM in high dimensions. The framework is general and applicable to a broad family of models, and the results represent a significant step forward in establishing computational and statistical guarantees for high-dimensional latent variable models. With the suggested clarifications and additions, the paper would be even stronger.

I recommend acceptance, as the paper makes a clear and significant theoretical contribution to the field. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The minor concerns about practical implementation and novelty clarification do not detract from the paper's scientific merit.
