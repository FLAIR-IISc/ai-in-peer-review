- Introduces a high-dimensional EM algorithm with an iterative truncation step and a decorrelated score for inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.  
- Establishes geometric (computational) convergence of the algorithm and asymptotic normality of the proposed test statistic.  
- Proves near-minimax optimal statistical rates (p(s* log d / n)) for parameter estimation in the high-dimensional setting.  
- Shows the decorrelated score statistic attains the semiparametric information bound for low-dimensional components.  
- Provides a unified theoretical framework covering both estimation and inference, with detailed proofs.  
- Applies the framework to Gaussian mixture and mixture-of-regression models, demonstrating generality.  
- Novelty: extends low-dimensional EM theory to high dimensions via a truncation step, a technical innovation not previously established.  
- Strength: first computationally feasible approach with optimal estimation and rigorous inferential guarantees for high-dimensional latent variable models.  
- Strength: thorough analysis separating optimization error (geometric) and statistical error (near-optimal rate).  
- Weakness: necessity of performing truncation at every iteration is not fully justified; more theoretical or empirical evidence is needed.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of connections to sparse optimization literature (e.g., truncated gradient methods).  
- Weakness: experimental details, especially initialization procedures crucial for performance and theory, are lacking.  
- Quality: technically sound with detailed proofs and clearly stated conditions for guarantees.  
- Clarity: well-organized and clearly written, with consistent notation enabling reproducibility.  
- Significance: results are important and likely to influence theoretical and applied work in high-dimensional statistics and ML.  
- Arguments for acceptance: substantial, well-motivated theoretical contributions addressing an important open problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: iterative truncation's necessity is unclear, literature connections are weak, and experiments omit critical initialization details.
