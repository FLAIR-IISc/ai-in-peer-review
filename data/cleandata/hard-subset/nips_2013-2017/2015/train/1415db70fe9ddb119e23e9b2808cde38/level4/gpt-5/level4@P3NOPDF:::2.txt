Summary
The paper proposes a sparsity-aware modification of the EM algorithm for high-dimensional settings where the true parameter vector is sparse. The authors establish conditions under which the algorithm converges to the global optimum and derive explicit convergence rates. They also develop a statistical testing framework to assess the sparsity of the estimator. The paper is well structured and the related work is adequately covered, but parts of the technical development (especially Sections 3 and 4) are dense and difficult to follow.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and timely problem: scalable, theoretically grounded EM in high-dimensional, sparse regimes.
- Provides nontrivial theory, including global convergence under identifiable conditions and explicit rates.
- Proposes a statistical testing framework to interrogate sparsity, which is valuable for practice.
- Organization and positioning relative to prior work are generally solid.

Weaknesses and suggestions for improvement
- Accessibility: Sections 3 and 4 are heavy with technical details and assumptions. Provide high-level intuition before statements, isolate key special cases (e.g., canonical GLMs or Gaussian mixtures with sparse means), and move some proofs and technical lemmas to the supplement while preserving self-contained proof sketches in the main text.
- Practical guidance: The method requires hyperparameters and initialization that are nontrivial to set in practice (see questions below). Add concrete heuristics, sensitivity analyses, and ablations.
- Empirical scope: The empirical evaluation could better demonstrate practical usefulness. Include comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., proximal-gradient or coordinate descent with sparsity-inducing penalties; EM with soft or hard thresholding variants; penalized likelihood approaches) and report runtime, stability under mis-specified sparsity, and robustness to noise/model misspecification.

Main questions for the authors
1) Minimax-optimality claim (Line 66): You state that the rate sqrt(s log(d/n)) is minimax-optimal. Please clarify:
- Is this the estimation error in ℓ2-norm, and under which model class and identifiability/regularity conditions?
- Provide a formal statement and proof sketch in the main text, or explicitly cite a theorem from the literature that applies directly to your setting (e.g., classical sparse estimation lower bounds adapted to your latent-variable model). If the optimality relies on additional assumptions (restricted strong concavity/smoothness, incoherence, signal strength), please list them explicitly.

2) Choice of s-hat in Eq. (3.8): Since s is unknown in practice, how should s-hat be chosen?
- Please provide principled strategies (e.g., cross-validation on the observed-data likelihood, information criteria such as BIC/EBIC calibrated for latent-variable models, stability selection, or path-wise selection with held-out likelihood).
- Quantify sensitivity of performance and theory to mis-specification of s-hat. If the guarantees require s-hat ≥ s, discuss practical surrogates and the impact on error bounds.

3) Initialization (Line 275, ||β_init − β*||2 < R/2): How can practitioners obtain such an initialization?
- Suggest concrete methods (e.g., penalized method-of-moments or spectral initializers; Lasso/Group-Lasso on pseudo-complete data; a few proximal-gradient steps on a convex surrogate; multi-start with screening).
- Provide theoretical or empirical evidence that these initializers fall within the basin of attraction with high probability under your assumptions. If only heuristic, present empirical success rates and sensitivity plots versus initialization quality.

4) Truncation step in Algorithm 1: Why is it essential?
- Please give intuitive and formal justification for truncation (e.g., it enforces sparsity, controls variance of the M-step, and keeps the iterate within a region where local strong concavity holds).
- What happens if truncation is omitted or replaced with softer shrinkage? Include an ablation comparing: (i) no truncation, (ii) soft-thresholding, (iii) your truncation. Report effects on convergence, support recovery, and estimation error. If the theory breaks without truncation, explain precisely which lemmas fail.

Clarity and presentation
- Provide a “roadmap” figure or boxed summaries of key assumptions (e.g., local strong concavity/smoothness, signal-to-noise conditions, identifiability) and their roles in the proofs.
- Before each major theorem, add a brief paragraph explaining the core idea, how EM updates interact with sparsity, and why truncation/regularization is needed.
- Move some technical derivations to the appendix but keep proof sketches and interpretation in the main text to make the paper self-contained.
- Unify notation and explicitly define all constants (including R) where they first appear.

Empirical evaluation
- Include experiments across varying n, d, s to illustrate the predicted rate. Plot ℓ2-error versus sqrt(s log d / n) to verify linear scaling.
- Add real-data case studies where latent-variable models and sparsity are plausible. Highlight practical gains over off-the-shelf penalized estimators and standard EM.
- Report computational cost and wall-clock times; discuss scalability and any parallelization or screening tricks.
- Provide code and scripts to facilitate reproducibility.

Reproducibility
- Clearly document all hyperparameter choices, selection criteria, and initialization procedures.
- State random seeds, stopping criteria, and tolerance thresholds; report sensitivity to these choices.

Overall assessment
This is a solid theoretical contribution that advances understanding of EM in high-dimensional sparse settings and proposes a statistically principled testing framework. The main limitations are practical guidance (choice of s-hat, initialization, truncation) and readability for a broader ML audience. With clearer exposition, explicit proofs or citations for the minimax claim, and stronger empirical demonstrations and ablations, the paper’s impact would be significantly enhanced.
