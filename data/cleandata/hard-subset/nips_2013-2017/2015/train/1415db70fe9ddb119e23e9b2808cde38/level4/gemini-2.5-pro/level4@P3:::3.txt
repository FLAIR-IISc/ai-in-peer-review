This paper presents a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in the high-dimensional setting where the number of parameters `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a novel "high-dimensional EM algorithm" which modifies the classical EM procedure by introducing a truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity on the parameter vector. They provide theoretical guarantees showing that, with a proper initialization, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`, where `s` is the true sparsity level. Second, the paper develops a procedure for asymptotic inference on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. This is achieved via a decorrelated score statistic, which is shown to be asymptotically normal, enabling hypothesis testing and the construction of confidence intervals.

The work builds upon recent advances in the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional setting (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017), extending these analyses to the more challenging high-dimensional regime. The key technical innovations include the introduction of the iterative truncation step and an analysis that relies on controlling the entrywise (`l-infinity`) statistical error of the M-step, which is necessary for handling the `d >> n` case. The proposed inference method is also significant as it provides a computationally feasible approach for models with non-convex likelihoods, a setting where many existing high-dimensional inference techniques for convex models like the Lasso are not applicable.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** This paper tackles a highly important and challenging problem. The EM algorithm is a workhorse in statistics and machine learning, but its theoretical properties in high dimensions have been largely unexplored. The paper provides the first comprehensive framework that establishes both computational guarantees (geometric convergence) and statistical guarantees (near-optimal estimation and valid asymptotic inference) for EM in this setting. This is a substantial theoretical contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears to be sound and rigorous. The decomposition of the estimation error into an optimization component that decays geometrically and a statistical component that matches the minimax lower bounds is elegant and insightful. The development of a valid inferential procedure for a non-convex, algorithmically-defined estimator is a major strength and a significant advance for latent variable models.

3.  **Generality:** The framework is presented in a general form and then instantiated for two important models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regressions model. This demonstrates the potential for broad applicability of the proposed methods and theory.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Motivation for Iterative Truncation:** The central modification proposed is the iterative truncation step. While the analysis is built around this specific algorithm, the paper could benefit from a more thorough justification of why this iterative approach is necessary or superior to simpler alternatives. For instance, could one achieve similar guarantees by running a standard (perhaps regularized) EM algorithm and applying a single truncation step only to the final estimate? The authors' observation that their framework can be seen as a generalization of methods like Iterative Hard Thresholding (IHT) for sparse linear regression—where iterative thresholding is known to be effective—provides a strong intuition. However, a more explicit discussion contrasting the proposed algorithm with a "single-shot truncation" baseline for general latent variable models would significantly strengthen the paper's motivation.

2.  **Connection to Existing Sparse Optimization Literature:** The idea of enforcing sparsity via iterative truncation (or thresholding) is a cornerstone of the sparse optimization literature (e.g., IHT). While the application within the EM framework is novel, the paper would be more complete if it situated itself within this broader context. A brief discussion of related work, such as "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" (Langford et al., 2009) or the literature on projected gradient descent, could provide valuable context.

3.  **Initialization Assumption:** The theoretical results rely on the algorithm being initialized within a specific "basin of attraction" around the true parameter. This is a standard assumption in the analysis of non-convex optimization. However, the practical implications of this assumption warrant more discussion. How can one obtain such an initialization? Are there data-driven methods (e.g., using tensor methods, even if suboptimal for the final estimate) that can provably produce a suitable starting point? Without this, the practical applicability of the theoretical guarantees remains in question. This point is particularly relevant for the experimental evaluation, which should explicitly state the initialization method used to allow for reproducibility and to assess the practicality of the assumption.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides the first rigorous theoretical guarantees for estimation and inference using the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional setting.
*   The results are technically deep and address a problem of fundamental importance to the NIPS community.
*   The development of a computationally feasible inference procedure for high-dimensional latent variable models is a significant and novel contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The motivation for the central algorithmic modification—the iterative truncation step—could be strengthened with a more explicit comparison to simpler alternatives.
*   The strong reliance on a good initialization, without sufficient guidance on how to obtain one in practice, slightly limits the immediate practical impact of the theoretical results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a very strong theoretical paper that makes significant contributions to our understanding of a classical algorithm in the modern high-dimensional setting. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to motivation and discussion of practical considerations, rather than fatal flaws in the technical results. These points can be addressed in a revision. The paper's strengths, particularly its novelty and the importance of the problem it solves, are substantial. I recommend acceptance.
