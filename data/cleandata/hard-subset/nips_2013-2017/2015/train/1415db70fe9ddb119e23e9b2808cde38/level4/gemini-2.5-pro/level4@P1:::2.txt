This paper proposes a modification of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for high-dimensional settings where the true parameter is sparse. The authors introduce a novel "high-dimensional EM algorithm" which adds a truncation step after the standard E- and M-steps to enforce sparsity. Under certain conditions, they prove that this algorithm converges geometrically to a solution that achieves a (near-)minimax optimal statistical rate. Furthermore, they construct a statistical inference framework based on a decorrelated score test, which allows for hypothesis testing on individual parameters.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing gap between the widespread use of the EM algorithm and the lack of theoretical guarantees in the high-dimensional regime (d >> n). Providing a unified framework for both optimal estimation and asymptotic inference for this class of problems is a significant and valuable contribution.
*   **Originality:** The proposed algorithm, which integrates a truncation step to enforce sparsity along the optimization path, appears novel. The theoretical analysis, which establishes simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees for a non-convex procedure in high dimensions, is sophisticated and extends previous work from the low-dimensional setting in a non-trivial way.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results are comprehensive, covering rates of convergence for the estimator and the asymptotic normality of the proposed test statistic. The analysis appears to be sound, connecting the EM algorithm to broader concepts in high-dimensional statistics.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The primary weaknesses of the paper relate to its clarity and the practical implications of its theoretical assumptions.

1.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is well-organized, and the introduction provides a good overview of related work. However, the main theoretical sections (3 and 4) are extremely dense and difficult to follow. The heavy reliance on a large number of technical conditions, assumptions, and specialized notation makes it challenging for a non-specialist to grasp the core insights of the proofs. While the technical depth is necessary, the paper would benefit significantly from more intuitive explanations to accompany the formal statements. This would make the work more accessible to the broader machine learning community.

2.  **Practical Applicability:** The analysis relies on several strong assumptions that may be difficult to verify or satisfy in practice.
    *   The choice of the sparsity parameter `bs` in Eq. (3.8) depends on the true sparsity level `s*`, which is unknown in real applications. How should `bs` be chosen in practice?
    *   The convergence guarantees require an initial parameter `init` that is already close to the true parameter `*` (i.e., `||init - *||_2 < R/2`). How can such a good initialization be obtained in practice for complex latent variable models? A discussion of practical strategies for these issues (e.g., using cross-validation for `bs`, or specific initialization procedures) would greatly strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

3.  **Justification of the Truncation Step:** The importance of the new truncation step in Algorithm 1 is not fully elaborated. Could the authors provide more intuition on what would happen if this step were omitted from their algorithm? For example, would the standard regularized EM algorithm (without explicit truncation) fail to converge, or would it simply achieve a suboptimal statistical rate? Clarifying the necessity of this step is crucial, as it is a core component of the proposed method.

4.  **Justification of Optimality:** The authors state that the derived statistical rate of `sqrt(s* log d/n)` is (near-)minimax-optimal. While this claim is well-established for other high-dimensional models like the Lasso, a citation or a brief justification would be helpful for the class of latent variable models considered here to make the paper more self-contained.
