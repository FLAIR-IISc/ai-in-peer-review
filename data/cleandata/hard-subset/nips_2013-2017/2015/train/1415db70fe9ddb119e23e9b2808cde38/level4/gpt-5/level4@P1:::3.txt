Summary
This paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm that enforces sparsity by truncating the parameter vector after each M-step. With suitable initialization and under local smoothness/concavity conditions on the population Q-function, the algorithm achieves geometric convergence (in iterations) up to a statistical error of order sqrt(s*) times the entrywise (infinity-norm) statistical error of the M-step. For many models this entrywise error scales like sqrt(log d / n), yielding (near-)optimal rates. The framework covers both exact maximization and gradient-ascent implementations of the M-step.

Beyond estimation, the paper proposes a decorrelated score for testing low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. The resulting statistic is asymptotically normal and, under standard Fisher information assumptions and a sparsity condition on a nuisance-weight vector, attains the semiparametric information bound. The theory is illustrated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of linear regressions.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on recent analyses of EM in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.), which establish geometric convergence from a good initialization under local strong concavity and smoothness. The present contribution extends these ideas to high dimensions via a truncation step that maintains sparsity along the optimization path, and analyzes entrywise statistical errors to obtain sharp rates. For inference, the paper adapts decorrelation/debiasing ideas previously developed for high-dimensional generalized linear models to the latent-variable setting, where global convex optima are not available. This contrasts with tensor-based methods, which often yield suboptimal rates and primarily target low-dimensional regimes.

Strengths
- A clear and general algorithmic template: E-step, M-step (exact or gradient), followed by truncation to enforce sparsity.
- A clean decomposition of estimation error into optimization error (geometric in t) and statistical error of order sqrt(s*)·ε∞ with ε∞ ≈ sqrt(log d / n).
- Theoretical coverage of both exact and gradient M-step variants under explicit Lipschitz-gradient and concavity/smoothness conditions.
- An inferential procedure (decorrelated score and Wald variants) with asymptotic normality and optimal variance (semiparametric efficiency) for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters.
- Concrete instantiations for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, with near-minimax rates.

Weaknesses and questions
- Necessity of truncation vs. post-hoc truncation: The guarantees rely on maintaining sparsity along the optimization path via the T-step. A natural question is whether similar guarantees could be achieved by running ordinary EM under the same assumptions and applying a single truncation at the end. The authors’ observation that linear regression is a special case provides a convincing argument against a single truncation step, since maintaining sparsity throughout is critical for controlling the pathwise statistical error and the basin-of-attraction conditions. It would still be helpful to state this implication explicitly in the main text.
- Statistical-Error condition: The key new requirement is an infinity-norm control on Mn(θ) − M(θ) for s-sparse θ. In several models (e.g., the Gaussian mixture and the non-gradient mixture-of-regressions examples), computing Mn at arbitrary θ is no harder than at s-sparse θ. In such cases, is there an accompanying statement showing that truncation is essential for theory (not just analysis convenience), or could one obtain comparable rates for untruncated EM under the same ε∞ control, followed by a final truncation? Clarifying this would sharpen the conceptual message.
- Proof technique vs. practical algorithm: The main theorem is stated for a resampling (sample-splitting) version to simplify analysis. Please clarify whether the same rates hold for the standard algorithm run on the full sample, and, if so, what additional dependence (if any) appears in constants or log factors.
- Empirical details: The experiments should report the initialization strategy, sensitivity to the sparsity level bs and (for gradient EM) the stepsize η, and comparisons to alternatives such as ℓ1-penalized EM without truncation and tensor-based methods. This would help assess robustness and practical gains due to truncation.
- Related work: It may be useful to discuss connections to sparse optimization with truncation/projection, e.g., “Sparse online learning via truncated gradient” (Langford et al., 2009), to better situate the T-step among related projection/truncation strategies.

Quality
The technical development appears sound, with assumptions clearly stated: local strong concavity/smoothness of Q(·; θ*), Lipschitz-gradient properties tailored to exact vs. gradient M-steps, and entrywise concentration for Mn. The inferential theory is careful about nuisance parameter handling and makes standard Fisher information assumptions; the sparsity assumption on the decorrelating weight vector is reasonable but should be discussed in terms of verifiability in the presented models.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized but dense. Suggestions:
- Gather the main assumptions (basin-of-attraction size, bs choice, and sample size requirements) in the main text with brief guidance on tuning (bs, η, Dantzig selector parameter).
- Clearly indicate where resampling is used only for analysis.
- In experiments, specify initialization and tuning procedures.

Originality
Introducing a truncation step into EM to obtain high-dimensional rates, together with an inferential procedure tailored to nonconvex latent variable models, is a novel and timely contribution relative to prior EM analyses and debiasing methods focused on convex GLMs.

Significance
The results are potentially impactful: they provide the first unified, computationally feasible framework for both optimal-rate estimation and valid inference in high-dimensional latent variable models under widely used algorithms.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong and general theoretical guarantees with near-optimal rates and asymptotically valid inference.
- Coverage of both exact and gradient M-steps, broadening applicability.
- Relevance to widely used latent variable models (Gaussian mixtures, mixture of regressions).

Arguments against acceptance
- The necessity and practical benefit of the truncation step, vs. alternatives, could be clarified more explicitly.
- Empirical evaluation lacks key details (initialization, sensitivity analyses, baselines).

Overall
An important and well-motivated contribution to high-dimensional learning with EM. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the role of truncation versus post-hoc sparsification, explaining the resampling assumption, and strengthening the experimental section.
