- Paper proposes a high-dimensional EM with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step that, with suitable initialization, contracts geometrically and achieves near-minimax rate ≈ sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n).
- Paper also develops a decorrelated score test for low-dimensional components, proving asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters.
- Framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.
- Extends prior local EM convergence analyses to high dimensions and complements de-biasing literature by handling nonconvex EM-based estimators rather than convex global optima.
- Claims sharper statistical rates and broader regimes compared with tensor methods for latent variable models.
- Technical contributions: careful decomposition of error into optimization (geometric decay) and statistical (entrywise deviation) terms with appropriate high-dimensional scaling.
- Inference procedure uses a Dantzig-type estimator for the influence vector and attains the semiparametric information bound.
- Originality: novel integration and analysis of hard-thresholding within EM and new high-dimensional inference results for EM estimators.
- Significance: provides a general, computationally feasible recipe for high-dimensional estimation and inference beyond GLMs with near-optimal guarantees.
- Clarity: high-level structure and assumptions are clearly presented, though notation is heavy.
- Weakness: limited practical guidance on initialization, basin-of-attraction size, and empirical sensitivity to initialization (need to specify initializers used in experiments).
- Weakness: unclear necessity of iterative truncation versus post-hoc truncation or ℓ1-penalized EM; suggests discussion and empirical comparison to penalized-EM baselines.
- Weakness: scope and applicability of Condition 3.3 (entrywise M-step error) need clarification across distributions, design covariances, noise types, and for no-resampling implementations.
- Weakness: insufficient tuning and implementation guidance (choice of sparsity level bs, stepsize, Dantzig tuning), robustness to bs mis-specification, and computational cost reporting (Dantzig program runtime).
- Weakness: empirical validation needs more details (exact initialization, truncation level, stepsizes, stopping criteria) and comparisons to ℓ1-penalized likelihood and spectral/tensor methods.
- Weakness: related work should include connections to truncated-gradient and iterative hard-thresholding literature and to penalized EM/post-selection inference methods.
- Evaluation (Quality): strong theoretical contributions with careful control of errors, but relies on local regularity and initialization assumptions.
- Evaluation (Clarity): generally good but notation heavy; recommend a glossary, checklist for verifying assumptions, and schematic algorithm/inference pipeline.
- Evaluation (Originality): high for combining truncated EM with sharp statistical and inferential guarantees.
- Evaluation (Significance): high due to gap in inference for nonconvex latent variable models in high dimensions.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel general framework, near-optimal rates with computational guarantees, semiparametrically efficient inference, clear model instantiations.
- Arguments against acceptance: practical aspects (initialization, tuning, no-resampling theory) need work; limited empirical baselines; strong assumptions (sparsity of w*, high SNR) may limit applicability.
- Overall: strong theoretical paper advancing high-dimensional EM and inference, but needs more practical guidance, empirical comparisons, and discussion of key assumptions.
