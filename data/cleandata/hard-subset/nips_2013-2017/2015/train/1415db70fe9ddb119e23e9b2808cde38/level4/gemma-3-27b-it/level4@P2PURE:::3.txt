A sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure is presented, modifying the classical EM algorithm by truncating the parameter vector to a desired sparsity level at each iteration. Alongside this, the authors propose an asymptotically normal statistic, based on a decorrelated score, for testing the non-zero status of specific parameter vector entries. 

The convergence guarantee for the sparse EM algorithm establishes geometric convergence to the true parameter value (with respect to the number of iterations), up to a statistical error term. This error term scales with the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the error in the M-step – typically logarithmic in the ambient dimension. 

This work builds upon recent analogous results for EM in low-dimensional settings. As in prior work, the analysis relies on the Q function being sufficiently smooth and approximately quadratic within a defined basin of attraction, and requires initialization within that basin. The novel condition here concerns control of the infinity norm of the error during M-step estimation. This condition is specifically required for sparse vectors, but the authors acknowledge that for many models – including the Gaussian mixture example and the non-gradient version of the mixture of regression model – estimating M across all possible vectors is computationally equivalent to estimating it for sparse vectors. This raises a question regarding the necessity of the truncated EM algorithm; could a comparable guarantee be achieved, for example, by applying standard EM under similar conditions, followed by a single truncation step? However, the authors’ observation that linear regression represents a special case provides a compelling argument against the sufficiency of a single truncation.

The related work section could benefit from a discussion of relevant literature in sparse optimization, such as Langford et al.’s “Sparse online learning via truncated gradient” (2009), as an illustrative example. 

The experimental section should specify the initialization method used. In summary, the paper proposes a sparse EM variant for high-dimensional parameter estimation, and the presented convergence guarantees represent a valuable contribution to high-dimensional learning theory. However, the added benefit of the sparse variant, beyond its impact on the convergence guarantee, remains somewhat unclear.
