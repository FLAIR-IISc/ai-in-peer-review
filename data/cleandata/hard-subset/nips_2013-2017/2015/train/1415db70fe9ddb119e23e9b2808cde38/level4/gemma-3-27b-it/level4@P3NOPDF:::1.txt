## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission: [Paper Title & Authors - *Please fill these in*]

This paper investigates Expectation-Maximization (EM) in high-dimensional settings, providing convergence results and a corresponding inference procedure. The work builds upon existing literature, notably extending results from Ning and Liu (2013) to this more complex regime. While the paper demonstrates a clever combination of established techniques, its novelty and the specific challenges overcome in applying those techniques to this context require further clarification.

The authors establish theoretical guarantees for the proposed EM algorithm. However, a crucial practical consideration remains unaddressed: the initialization of the algorithm. Specifically, the paper requires an initial solution, $\beta^{\rm init}$, satisfying the condition outlined in line 60 on page 2. The authors should discuss under what circumstances such an initialization can be reliably found. Providing guidance on this initialization step would significantly enhance the practical utility of the method.

Two algorithms are proposed. The paper would benefit from practical guidance regarding which algorithm is preferable in different scenarios. A comparative analysis, or at least a discussion outlining the trade-offs between the two approaches, is necessary.

A significant practical concern revolves around the selection of tuning parameters, namely $\hat{s}$ and $\lambda$. The paper currently lacks a discussion of how these parameters should be chosen in practice. Given the critical impact of tuning parameter selection on sampling properties in high-dimensional inference – as acknowledged in the related literature – a detailed examination of their sensitivity and potential data-dependent selection methods is crucial.  Exploring techniques for automated parameter selection, or providing concrete recommendations based on data characteristics, would greatly strengthen the paper.

The resulting decorrelated score test is noted to be equivalent to that presented in Ning and Liu (2013). This equivalence should be explicitly cited and acknowledged, rather than presented implicitly.  Furthermore, the authors should articulate whether any new theoretical developments were necessary to adapt the existing inference procedure to the current high-dimensional setting. Specifically, what adjustments or refinements were required to ensure the validity of the test within this new context?

The main theoretical results rely on a substantial number of technical conditions. While comprehensive, the authors should dedicate a section to discussing the plausibility of these conditions, specifically in relation to the latent variable models they consider. Providing arguments, or even examples, to demonstrate that these assumptions are not overly restrictive for relevant applications would substantially bolster the paper’s theoretical foundation.

The current presentation feels somewhat terse, akin to a “trailer” for the more detailed supplementary material. While extensive supplementary material can be valuable, the main paper should provide a more self-contained and comprehensive overview of the key contributions and technical challenges. More detail within the core manuscript, particularly regarding the adaptations of existing techniques, is needed to fully convey the paper’s intellectual contribution.  

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically sound extension of existing work to a high-dimensional setting. However, it falls short in providing practical guidance for implementation and lacks a clear articulation of its novel contributions. Addressing the points raised above—particularly regarding initialization, parameter selection, the connection to Ning and Liu (2013), and the justification of technical assumptions—would significantly improve the paper's clarity, impact, and overall quality.
