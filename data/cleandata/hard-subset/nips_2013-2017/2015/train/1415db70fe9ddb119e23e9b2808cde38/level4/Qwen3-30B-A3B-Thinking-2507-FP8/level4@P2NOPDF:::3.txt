

This paper introduces a sparse EM algorithm that enforces sparsity by truncating the parameter vector at each iteration to a specified level. It also proposes an asymptotically normal test statistic based on a decorrelated score for hypothesis testing on individual parameter entries. The main theoretical contribution establishes geometric convergence of the sparse EM estimate to the true parameter (up to statistical error) as a function of iteration count. The statistical error term scales with the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the estimation error in the M-step, which is typically logarithmic in the ambient dimension.  

The convergence guarantee builds on recent low-dimensional EM results, requiring similar conditions: smoothness and near-quadratic behavior of the Q-function within a basin of attraction, plus initialization within this basin. The novel condition imposed is the infinity norm control on the M-step estimation error, which is only necessary for sparse vectors. However, for many models (including the Gaussian mixture example and the non-gradient mixture-of-regression variant), estimating the M-step across all vectors is as feasible as for sparse vectors. This raises a critical question: is the truncation step genuinely necessary, or could similar guarantees be achieved via standard EM followed by a single post-hoc truncation? The authors' demonstration that linear regression is a special case of their framework provides compelling evidence against the single-truncation alternative, as standard EM fails in this setting.  

The paper would benefit from discussing related work in sparse optimization, such as Langford et al.'s "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" (2009), which addresses similar truncation strategies. This omission weakens the contextualization of the proposed method.  

The experimental section lacks clarity on initialization procedures, which are crucial for EM convergence. Without details on how initial points were selected (e.g., random, heuristic, or based on prior knowledge), the empirical results are difficult to interpret or reproduce. The paper claims the sparse EM variant enables high-dimensional parameter estimation, and the convergence guarantees are indeed significant for high-dimensional learning. However, the necessity of the *sparse* EM procedure—rather than standard EM with post-hoc sparsity—remains somewhat ambiguous without stronger experimental validation. The theoretical arguments (particularly the linear regression special case) support the necessity of truncation during iterations, but the experiments should directly compare sparse EM against standard EM followed by truncation to reinforce this point.  

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound extension of EM for high-dimensional settings with a novel convergence analysis. The core contribution is well-motivated and the theoretical results are rigorous. Key improvements needed are: (1) clarifying the necessity of iterative truncation versus post-hoc truncation via additional experiments; (2) incorporating relevant sparse optimization literature; and (3) specifying initialization methods in experiments. The theoretical advances are valuable, but the practical justification for the sparse EM variant would be strengthened by addressing these points. The work makes a meaningful contribution to high-dimensional statistical learning, though its immediate impact hinges on resolving the role of the truncation mechanism.
