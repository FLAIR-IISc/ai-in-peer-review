This paper introduces a sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm designed for high-dimensional settings. The proposed method modifies the classical EM framework by incorporating a hard-thresholding step after each M-step, enforcing a desired sparsity level on the parameter vector throughout the optimization process. Additionally, the authors present an asymptotically normal statistic, based on a decorrelated score function, for conducting statistical inference on individual parameters (i.e., testing whether a specific parameter is non-zero).

The primary theoretical contribution is a non-asymptotic convergence guarantee for the sparse EM algorithm. The authors prove that, under certain conditions, the parameter estimates converge at a geometric rate to a neighborhood of the true sparse parameter vector. The size of this neighborhood, or the statistical error, is shown to be proportional to the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step. This error term scales favorably (e.g., logarithmically) with the ambient dimension for many models.

These results extend recent theoretical analyses of EM from the low-dimensional to the high-dimensional sparse setting. The analysis relies on standard assumptions, such as the smoothness and local strong concavity of the Q-function, as well as proper initialization within a basin of attraction. A key new assumption is the requirement of infinity norm control over the statistical error in the M-step.

While this condition is formally required only for sparse parameter vectors, I note that for several models—including the Gaussian mixture model discussed in the paper and the non-gradient version of the mixture of regressions model—the M-step is no more difficult to compute for dense vectors than for sparse ones. This observation raises a critical question regarding the necessity of the proposed iterative truncation scheme. Specifically, could a similar theoretical guarantee be achieved by a simpler procedure, such as running the standard EM algorithm for several iterations and then applying a single hard-thresholding step to the final estimate? The authors' argument that linear regression, a setting where iterative thresholding is known to be beneficial over a single-step approach, is a special case of this framework provides a compelling reason to favor the proposed iterative sparse EM algorithm. However, a more explicit discussion of this point in the paper would strengthen the motivation for the algorithm.

A few minor points for consideration:

*   The discussion of related work could be broadened. The core idea of iterative truncation or thresholding has been explored extensively in the sparse optimization literature. For instance, methods like truncated gradient descent (e.g., Langford et al., 2009, "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient") share a similar spirit, and it would be valuable to position the proposed work within this broader context.
*   In the experimental evaluation, the initialization procedure for the algorithm is not specified. Given the theoretical requirement for initialization within a basin of attraction, detailing this strategy is crucial for reproducibility and for understanding the practical performance of the method.

In summary, this paper presents a sparse variant of the EM algorithm for high-dimensional parameter estimation, supported by rigorous theoretical guarantees on its convergence. The analysis is a significant contribution to the literature on high-dimensional learning with latent variables. While the results are important, the paper could be strengthened by more explicitly justifying the necessity of the iterative truncation scheme over simpler alternatives.
