### Summary

This paper presents a theoretical framework for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors make two main contributions. First, they propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity. Under certain conditions, and with a suitable initialization, they prove that this algorithm converges geometrically to an estimator that achieves a (near-)optimal statistical rate of convergence (specifically, sqrt(s*log(d)/n)). Second, building on this estimator, they develop an inferential procedure based on a decorrelated score test to perform hypothesis testing and construct confidence intervals for low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. The authors demonstrate the application of their general framework to two specific models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model. This work extends the theoretical analysis of the EM algorithm, previously established in low-dimensional settings (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017), to the more challenging high-dimensional regime where the number of parameters `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`.

### Strengths

*   **Significant Problem:** The paper addresses a fundamental and important problem. The EM algorithm is a workhorse for latent variable models, but its theoretical properties in the high-dimensional setting are not well understood. Providing a computationally feasible algorithm with guarantees for both optimal estimation and valid inference is a significant contribution to the field.
*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, establishing geometric convergence for the optimization error and a near-minimax optimal rate for the statistical error. The subsequent development of an asymptotically normal test statistic for inference is also a powerful result, as inference is often much harder than estimation in high-dimensional settings.
*   **General Framework:** The authors present their results within a general framework and lay out a clear set of technical conditions. Applying this framework to specific, widely-used models like Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models effectively demonstrates its breadth and utility.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Practicality of Initialization:** A critical assumption for the theoretical guarantees is the availability of an initial estimator that is already within a certain radius of the true parameter (as stated in Theorem 3.4). The paper does not discuss how such an estimator can be obtained in practice for the models considered. The feasibility of this initialization step is crucial for the overall applicability of the proposed algorithm. Could the authors comment on whether standard methods (e.g., spectral or tensor-based methods) can be proven to provide an initial estimate that satisfies the required conditions?

2.  **Clarity on Novelty in the Inferential Procedure:** The decorrelated score test is a powerful technique for high-dimensional inference. However, the proposed test appears to follow the general framework developed in previous work, such as Ning and Liu (2017) ("A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models"). The paper should explicitly cite this and related works and clarify the specific technical innovations required to apply this framework to the estimator produced by the proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm. The main challenge, which should be highlighted, is that the estimator here is a local optimum of a non-convex procedure, unlike in many previous works where the estimator (e.g., Lasso) is the global solution to a convex problem. Detailing these unique challenges would better position the paper's contribution.

3.  **Guidance on Implementation and Tuning Parameters:** The paper leaves several practical questions unanswered.
    *   It proposes two implementations for the M-step (exact maximization vs. a gradient ascent step) but provides no practical guidance on their relative merits or when one should be preferred.
    *   The algorithm and the inferential procedure depend on key tuning parameters, namely the sparsity level `bs` and the Dantzig selector's regularization parameter `λ`. The theory assumes these are chosen appropriately, but a practical, data-driven method for their selection (e.g., via cross-validation) is not discussed. A discussion on the sensitivity of the results to these parameters and potential selection methods would greatly enhance the paper's practical value.

4.  **Clarity and Reliance on the Appendix:** The paper's main body presents the general theory, but the verification of the numerous and complex technical conditions for the specific latent variable models is deferred entirely to the appendix. While this is common due to space constraints, the paper would be more convincing and self-contained if the main text provided more intuition or brief proof sketches for why these key assumptions (e.g., Condition 3.2 Concavity-Smoothness, Condition 3.3 Statistical-Error) are satisfied. As written, the main paper reads somewhat like a "trailer" for the technical results contained in the supplement, forcing the reader to take on faith that the general theory applies to the examples as claimed.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that addresses an important and challenging problem. It commendably combines ideas from the analysis of EM algorithms and high-dimensional inference to build a unified framework. The primary weaknesses lie in the lack of discussion on the practical aspects of the algorithm (initialization, tuning parameter selection) and in the clarity of its novel contributions relative to existing high-dimensional inference literature. The heavy reliance on the appendix to substantiate key claims also detracts from the completeness of the main manuscript. Despite these points, the work represents a significant step forward in understanding EM in high dimensions. If the authors can address the questions regarding initialization and clarify the novelty of their inferential approach, the paper would be a very strong contribution to the conference.
