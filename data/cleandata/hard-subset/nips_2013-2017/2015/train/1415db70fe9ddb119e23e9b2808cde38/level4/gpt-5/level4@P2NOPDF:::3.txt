The paper proposes a sparse EM algorithm that, at each iteration, truncates the parameter vector to a prescribed sparsity level. It also introduces a decorrelated score statistic with asymptotic normality for testing whether a particular parameter coordinate is nonzero.

The main theoretical guarantee establishes that, under suitable conditions, the sparse EM iterates converge geometrically (in iteration count) to the true parameter, up to a statistical error term. This error scales with the square root of the sparsity and the infinity norm of the M-step’s statistical error, which is typically only logarithmic in the ambient dimension.

These results extend recent convergence analyses of EM in low-dimensional settings to the high-dimensional sparse regime. As in prior work, the analysis assumes that the Q-function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic within a basin of attraction, and that initialization falls within this basin. The new requirement is an infinity-norm bound on the M-step estimation error, needed only for sparse vectors. However, for many models, controlling this error uniformly (not just on sparse vectors) is no harder—for example, in the Gaussian mixture case and in the non-gradient variant of the mixture of regressions. This naturally prompts a question: Is iterative truncation essential? Could one run standard EM under the same conditions and then apply a single truncation at the end? The authors’ observation that linear regression appears as a special case is a persuasive counterpoint, suggesting that a single post hoc truncation may not suffice and that iterative sparsity enforcement can be critical.

There seems to be related literature in sparse optimization and truncation-based methods that merits discussion, such as “Sparse online learning via truncated gradient” (Langford et al., 2009).

The experimental section should clearly specify the initialization strategy used, since the theory relies on starting within a basin of attraction.

Overall, the paper offers a sparse EM variant tailored to high-dimensional estimation and provides meaningful convergence guarantees that advance the theory of high-dimensional learning. While the analysis highlights conditions under which iterative truncation is beneficial, clarifying the precise necessity of the sparse variant relative to alternatives (e.g., standard EM with a final truncation) would strengthen the contribution, though the linear regression special case already alleviates this concern to a significant extent.
