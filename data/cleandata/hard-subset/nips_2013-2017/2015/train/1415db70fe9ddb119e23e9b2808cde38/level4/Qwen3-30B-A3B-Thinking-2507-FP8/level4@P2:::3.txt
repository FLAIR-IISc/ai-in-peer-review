

This paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm that incorporates sparsity constraints through a truncation step at each iteration. The authors establish both computational and statistical guarantees for their method, demonstrating that the algorithm converges geometrically to an estimator with near-optimal statistical rate of convergence. Additionally, they develop a decorrelated score test for hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter, establishing its asymptotic normality with optimal limiting variance.

The work builds upon recent NIPS papers that have established theoretical guarantees for EM in low-dimensional settings (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2017, Wang et al. 2015). The authors extend this line of work to high-dimensional regimes, where the standard EM algorithm fails to provide statistical guarantees due to the non-identifiability of the maximum likelihood estimator without sparsity assumptions. They show that their sparse EM procedure achieves the minimax-optimal rate of convergence for high-dimensional latent variable models, which is significantly better than the low-dimensional rates previously established.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation in high-dimensional latent variable models, addressing a significant gap in the literature. Second, the authors establish simultaneous statistical and computational guarantees for their high-dimensional EM algorithm, showing that the estimation error decomposes into an optimization error (decreasing geometrically with iterations) and a statistical error (achieving the near-optimal rate of √(s* log d/n)). Third, they develop a decorrelated score statistic that achieves the semiparametric information bound for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, establishing the first computationally feasible approach for optimal inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.

The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear technical results and thorough comparison with related work. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from existing methods, including tensor-based approaches (which suffer from suboptimal statistical rates) and Lasso-based inference methods (which primarily apply to generalized linear models rather than latent variable models). The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, covering both exact and gradient ascent implementations of the M-step, and the results are illustrated through applications to Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

The main weaknesses concern the necessity of the truncation step at each iteration. While the authors argue that a single truncation after EM would not suffice (using linear regression as a special case), this argument could be strengthened. The paper should more thoroughly address whether a post-EM truncation step could achieve similar guarantees, particularly since for many models (like Gaussian mixtures), estimating the M-step for sparse vectors appears no more difficult than for general vectors. The experimental section also lacks details about the initialization procedure, which is critical for EM-based methods and should be specified.

The paper meets all four NIPS quality criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical results are technically sound and well-supported by rigorous analysis, with careful attention to both computational and statistical guarantees.
- Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, though the necessity of iterative truncation could be better justified.
- Originality: The work extends EM to high-dimensional settings with sparsity constraints, establishing the first theoretical guarantees for parameter estimation and inference in this regime for latent variable models.
- Significance: The results address a fundamental problem in high-dimensional statistics with clear implications for both theoretical and applied research in machine learning and statistics.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a significant theoretical contribution to a central problem in statistical learning. The authors have effectively demonstrated the importance of their approach through careful theoretical analysis and comparison with prior work. The decorrelated score test represents a particularly valuable contribution, as it enables hypothesis testing and confidence interval construction in high-dimensional latent variable models for the first time.

The main question for the authors is whether the truncation must occur at each iteration rather than as a post-processing step. While their argument using linear regression as a special case is somewhat convincing, additional analysis comparing the two approaches would strengthen the paper. The experimental section should also specify the initialization procedure used.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of EM in high-dimensional settings. With minor improvements to the discussion of the truncation step and additional experimental details, it would be an excellent paper for NIPS. The work provides a valuable framework for high-dimensional latent variable modeling with both optimal estimation and inference guarantees, making it a significant advance in the field.
