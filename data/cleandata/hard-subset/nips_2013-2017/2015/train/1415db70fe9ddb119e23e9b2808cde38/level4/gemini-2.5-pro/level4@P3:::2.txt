### Summary

This paper presents a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings, where the parameter dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The core contributions are twofold. First, the authors propose a novel "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the standard EM procedure by incorporating a hard-thresholding (truncation) step after the M-step to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates at each iteration. Under certain conditions, including a sufficiently accurate initialization, this algorithm is shown to converge at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical rate of convergence. Second, the paper develops an inferential procedure based on a "decorrelated score test" that allows for hypothesis testing and uncertainty quantification for low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. This is a significant step forward, as inference is notoriously difficult in this setting. The work builds upon recent analyses of the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional regime (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) but introduces new techniques to handle the challenges posed by high dimensionality and sparsity.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing gap in the literature by providing a rigorous analysis of the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional sparse setting. It provides what appears to be the first comprehensive framework that establishes both computational guarantees (geometric convergence) and strong statistical guarantees (near-optimal estimation and asymptotic normality for inference) for a tractable EM-based procedure.

2.  **Novel Methodology:** The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm, with its explicit truncation step to maintain sparsity along the optimization path, is a simple yet powerful modification. Furthermore, the development of a decorrelated score test for the output of a non-convex, iterative procedure like EM is a sophisticated and important contribution to the field of high-dimensional inference.

3.  **Generality and Scope:** The theoretical framework is developed in a general form and then effectively demonstrated on important and widely used latent variable models, including Gaussian Mixture Models and Mixture of Regression Models. This showcases the potential for broad applicability of the proposed methods and analysis techniques.

4.  **Clarity of Positioning and Organization:** The paper is well-organized and does an excellent job of summarizing related work and clearly positioning its contributions within the extensive literature on the EM algorithm, high-dimensional estimation, and high-dimensional inference.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors (Arguments Against Acceptance)

1.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** While the paper is well-structured, the theoretical sections (Sections 3 and 4) are extremely dense and technically demanding. The reliance on a series of abstract conditions makes the core arguments challenging to follow for a broader machine learning audience not deeply specialized in high-dimensional statistics. The manuscript could be significantly improved by adding more high-level intuition explaining why these specific conditions are necessary and what they mean for the algorithm's behavior.

2.  **Practicality of Theoretical Assumptions:** The main results hinge on two strong assumptions that may limit the method's immediate practical application. Addressing these points would greatly strengthen the paper:
    *   **Initialization:** The theory requires an initial estimate `θ_init` that is already within a certain `l2`-ball of the true parameter `θ*`. The paper does not discuss how to obtain such an initial estimate in practice. Since the performance of the EM algorithm is highly sensitive to initialization, especially for non-convex problems, this is a crucial point for practitioners. Could the authors suggest a practical and theoretically-grounded method for finding a suitable initialization?
    *   **Sparsity Parameter:** The choice of the sparsity parameter `bs` in Eq. (3.8) depends on the true sparsity level `s*`, which is unknown in real-world scenarios. How should this hyperparameter be chosen in a data-driven way (e.g., via cross-validation)? Does the theory provide any guidance on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of `bs`?

3.  **Justification of the Truncation Step:** The introduction of the truncation step (T-step) is the key algorithmic modification. It would be very helpful if the authors could provide more explicit intuition on its role. Specifically, what would be the theoretical and practical consequences of omitting this step and instead relying solely on a regularized M-step (e.g., with an `l1` penalty)? Does the explicit T-step offer advantages beyond simply enforcing sparsity, perhaps by correcting the optimization path in a way that standard regularization cannot?

4.  **Minimax Optimality Claim:** The introduction states that the `sqrt(s* log d/n)` rate is (near-)minimax-optimal. While this is a well-known result for models like sparse linear regression, it would strengthen the paper to include a specific citation that establishes this lower bound for the general class of high-dimensional latent variable models considered here.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a very strong theoretical paper that makes significant contributions to the statistical understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The unified treatment of estimation and inference is particularly impressive. However, its dense presentation and strong assumptions on initialization and hyperparameter knowledge currently limit its accessibility and immediate practical utility. While it is an excellent statistics paper, its impact on the broader machine learning community could be enhanced by addressing the practical questions raised above and providing more intuition behind the technical conditions.
