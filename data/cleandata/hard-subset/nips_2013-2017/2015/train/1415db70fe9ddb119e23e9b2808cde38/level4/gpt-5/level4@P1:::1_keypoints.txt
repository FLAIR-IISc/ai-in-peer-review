- Proposes a high-dimensional EM with a truncation (T-) step enforcing sparsity and proves geometric convergence to a near-minimax sparse estimator (rate ~ s*^(1/2)(log d/n)^(1/2)).  
- Develops decorrelated score and Wald inference for low-dimensional components, claiming asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency.  
- Framework covers both exact and gradient-ascent M-steps and is demonstrated on Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.  
- Builds on low-dimensional population-to-sample EM analyses by adding a T-step and finer entrywise analysis; adapts decorrelated-score ideas to nonconvex latent-variable settings.  

Strengths:
- T-step is a clear, practical modification that aligns computation with high-dimensional sparsity guarantees.  
- Convergence results cleanly separate geometric optimization error from near-optimal statistical error.  
- Analysis addresses both exact and gradient M-step implementations with explicit rates and contraction factors.  
- Inference framework attains the semiparametric information bound and is verified for representative latent-variable models.  

Weaknesses / Questions:
- Initialization: theory requires a relative-error initializer; unclear which practical algorithms/conditions provide this and what sample sizes are needed.  
- Algorithm choice: no practical guidance or empirical comparison on when to use exact vs. gradient M-steps (cost, curvature, step-size robustness).  
- Tuning: unclear how to choose truncation level ŝ and inference tuning λ in practice; need data-driven selection methods and sensitivity analysis.  
- Inference attribution: decorrelated score closely matches Ning & Liu and related work; paper should cite these and clearly state what is novel here.  
- Assumptions: many technical conditions—main text should state which hold per model, required SNR/separation/design assumptions, and plausibility of sparsity for the efficient weight w*.  
- Exposition: main text depends heavily on the supplement; needs more intuition on T-step challenges (support evolution, entrywise control) and key lemmas that differ from low-dim EM.  

Quality:
- Theoretical development appears sound but would benefit from concrete guidance on initialization/tuning and empirical illustrations.  

Clarity:
- Writing is generally clear but notation-heavy; critical assumptions, intuition, and attribution for inference should be moved into the main text.  

Originality:
- The T-step and high-dimensional EM analysis are novel; the inference component mainly adapts existing decorrelated-score methodology but extends it to nonconvex EM estimators.  

Significance:
- Potentially significant if assumptions and tuning are practical, offering tractable near-optimal estimation and valid inference in high-dimensional latent-variable models.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel high-dimensional EM with truncation and provable near-minimax rates, plus unified computational and inferential guarantees verified on key models.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical aspects (initialization, tuning, algorithm selection) under-specified, inference largely borrowed without clear delineation of new technical contributions, and excessive reliance on supplement.  

Overall recommendation:
- Solid and impactful contribution contingent on stronger discussion of initialization/tuning, clearer attribution of inference novelty, and bringing model-specific assumption verifications into the main paper.
