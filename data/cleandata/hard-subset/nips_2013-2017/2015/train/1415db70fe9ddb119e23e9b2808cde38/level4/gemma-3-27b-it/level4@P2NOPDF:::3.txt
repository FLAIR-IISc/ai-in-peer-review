This paper introduces a sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure that promotes parameter sparsity by truncating the parameter vector at each iteration to a predefined sparsity level. Accompanying this algorithm is an asymptotically normal statistic, constructed using a decorrelated score, designed to test the significance of individual parameter entries – specifically, whether they are non-zero.

The authors provide a convergence guarantee for the sparse EM algorithm, demonstrating geometric convergence to the true parameter value (as measured by the number of iterations). This guarantee includes a statistical error term proportional to the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the error in the M-step. Importantly, this M-step error is often only logarithmic in the ambient dimension.

This work builds upon recent advances in EM convergence guarantees for low-dimensional settings.  As with prior results, the analysis relies on assumptions of sufficient smoothness and near-quadraticity of the Q-function within a specific basin of attraction, and requires initialization within that basin. The novelty here lies in an additional condition controlling the infinity norm of the error introduced during the M-step estimation.  

While this condition is only strictly necessary for sparse parameter vectors, the authors acknowledge that estimating M for all parameters may not be substantially more complex than estimating it for sparse parameters in many models – they demonstrate this for the Gaussian mixture model and the non-gradient version of the mixture of regression model. This observation naturally raises the question of whether the truncated EM algorithm is essential, or if a comparable guarantee could be achieved by performing standard EM under the same conditions, followed by a single, final truncation step. However, the authors convincingly argue against this simplification, noting the connection to linear regression where such a single truncation would be insufficient.

The paper would benefit from a discussion of related work in sparse optimization. For example, methods like “Sparse online learning via truncated gradient” (Langford et al., 2009) explore similar sparsity-inducing techniques and their connection to the presented work is worth exploring. 

A crucial detail missing from the experimental section is a description of the parameter initialization procedure. Clarity on this point is essential for reproducibility and proper evaluation of the results.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable theoretical result – a convergence guarantee for sparse EM in high-dimensional settings.  While the necessity of the sparse variant of EM, as opposed to standard EM with a final truncation, is initially debatable, the authors provide a compelling justification. The importance of the convergence guarantees for high-dimensional learning is significant, making this work a worthwhile contribution.
