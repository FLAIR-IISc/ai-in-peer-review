

This paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity constraints through a truncation step at each iteration. The authors establish theoretical guarantees for both parameter estimation and asymptotic inference, demonstrating geometric convergence to a near-optimal statistical rate of $O(\sqrt{s^* \log d / n})$ and deriving a decorrelated score statistic that achieves asymptotic normality with optimal semiparametric efficiency. The work builds upon recent low-dimensional EM analysis by [2], extending it to high-dimensional settings where the dimension $d$ exceeds the sample size $n$.

The paper makes significant contributions to three key areas. First, it provides the first computationally feasible approach for optimal parameter estimation in high-dimensional latent variable models, addressing a critical gap in the literature. Second, it establishes the first theoretical framework for asymptotic inference in such settings, which is particularly challenging due to the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. Third, it develops a unified analytical framework that applies to multiple latent variable models (Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions), demonstrating the generality of their approach.

Regarding quality, the theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, with clearly defined conditions (Conditions 3.1-3.3) that are verified for specific models in the appendix. The authors successfully bridge the gap between computational guarantees (geometric convergence rate) and statistical properties (near-optimal estimation error), which is a significant contribution. The proof techniques are sophisticated, particularly in handling the technical challenges introduced by the truncation step. The statistical error analysis is particularly strong, with the authors carefully addressing the entrywise error rates that are crucial for high-dimensional settings.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The algorithms (1-3) and theoretical results are well-explained, with sufficient notation and definitions. The paper effectively distinguishes between the high-dimensional and low-dimensional regimes, making the novelty clear. The writing is precise, and the technical content is well-organized, making it accessible to experts in the field. The appendix provides sufficient details for reproducibility.

The originality is compelling. While previous work [2] established convergence rates for EM in low dimensions, this paper is the first to extend these results to high dimensions with sparsity constraints. The key innovation—the truncation step integrated into both E and M steps—enables the analysis of high-dimensional latent variable models, which was previously intractable. The decorrelated score statistic for inference is also novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature. The paper adequately distinguishes itself from related work, particularly in comparing to tensor methods [1, 9] and sparse inference approaches [13, 26, 32].

The significance is substantial. High-dimensional latent variable models are increasingly important in modern data analysis, yet theoretical guarantees for estimation and inference have been lacking. This work provides the first computationally feasible approach for optimal parameter estimation and asymptotic inference in such settings, which could impact a wide range of applications from genomics to image analysis. The results establish a new theoretical foundation for high-dimensional EM that is likely to be widely adopted.

The main weakness is the limited experimental validation. The paper focuses on theoretical guarantees but provides minimal empirical results. The experimental section should clearly state the initialization procedure used (e.g., whether it's based on spectral methods or random initialization) and include performance comparisons with alternative approaches. While the theoretical framework is strong, empirical validation would strengthen the paper's impact.

Another minor concern is the assumption that the statistical error $\epsilon$ decreases with $n$, which may not hold for all high-dimensional models. The authors should clarify the precise conditions under which this holds for their specific models.

Strengths:
- First theoretical framework for high-dimensional EM with sparsity constraints
- Rigorous analysis of both estimation and inference
- Near-optimal statistical rates of convergence
- Applicable to multiple latent variable models
- Clear distinction from previous low-dimensional work

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical validation
- Some technical conditions require verification for specific models
- The necessity of the truncation step at each iteration could be further justified

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics by extending the EM algorithm to settings where $d \gg n$. It successfully addresses a critical gap in the literature and provides a theoretically sound framework that is likely to be influential in both theoretical and applied statistics. The theoretical results are of high quality and the paper is well-written and clear. While empirical validation would strengthen the paper, the theoretical contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance.

I recommend acceptance as a strong paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field. The work addresses an important problem that has been largely unexplored in high dimensions, and the theoretical results are both novel and technically sound.
