- Paper develops a general theoretical framework for EM in high-dimensional (d >> n) sparse latent-variable models.  
- Main algorithmic contribution: a high-dimensional EM that adds a truncation (hard-threshold) step to enforce sparsity.  
- Theoretically, with a good initialization the algorithm converges geometrically to an estimator achieving a (near-)minimax statistical rate.  
- Inferential contribution: a decorrelated score test for hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components derived from the nonconvex estimator.  
- Extends recent low-dimensional EM analyses to the high-dimensional regime and provides novel inference for nonconvex estimators (distinct from Lasso debiasing).  
- Strength: addresses a significant, challenging problem and provides the first computationally feasible approach with guarantees for both estimation and inference.  
- Strength: rigorous, unified theoretical analysis giving computational (geometric) and statistical (near-optimal) rates plus asymptotic normality of the test statistic.  
- Strength: paper is well-structured and situates contributions clearly in the literature.  
- Weakness: Sections 3–4 are very dense and rely on abstract conditions; more intuition and explanations of why conditions are needed would improve accessibility.  
- Practical concern: results require an initialization within a specific radius of the truth — no concrete guidance on how to obtain such an init in practice.  
- Practical concern: truncation sparsity parameter bs depends on unknown true sparsity s* (Eq. 3.8); no data-driven tuning guidance is provided.  
- Methodological question: need justification for the hard-threshold T-step — would an l1-penalized M-step work, or is hard thresholding technically essential?  
- Presentation issue: claim that the sqrt(s* log d / n) rate is (near-)minimax-optimal should cite the lower-bound result for the specific model class in the main text.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution; addressing practical implementation, clarifying technical conditions, and adding supporting citations would enhance impact.
