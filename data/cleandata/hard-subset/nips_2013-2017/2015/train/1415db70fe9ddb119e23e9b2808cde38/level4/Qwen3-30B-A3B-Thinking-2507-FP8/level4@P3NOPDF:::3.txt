

This paper introduces a sparse EM algorithm that enforces sparsity via truncation at each iteration, alongside a decorrelated score statistic for testing individual parameter entries. The main contribution is a convergence guarantee showing geometric convergence to the true parameter (up to a statistical error term), where the error scales with the square root of sparsity and the infinity norm of the M-step estimation error—a typically logarithmic term in the ambient dimension. This extends recent low-dimensional EM results under similar smoothness and quadraticity assumptions on the Q-function within a basin of attraction, with the key new requirement being control over the M-step error's infinity norm. The authors demonstrate this condition is feasible for sparse vectors in their examples (Gaussian mixtures, non-gradient mixture regression), where estimating M over the full parameter space is no harder than over sparse subsets.  

A critical question arises: is the iterative truncation scheme truly necessary? The authors' observation that linear regression is a special case (where ordinary EM followed by a single truncation would suffice) strongly suggests that the sparse EM procedure may not be essential. The current analysis does not establish whether the iterative truncation provides a fundamental advantage over a simpler approach (ordinary EM + final truncation), especially given that the M-step error control condition holds equally well for full-parameter estimation. This undermines the paper's central justification for the proposed method. A more compelling argument would demonstrate a scenario where iterative truncation is indispensable, or show that the statistical error terms differ meaningfully between the two approaches.  

The related work discussion could be strengthened. The paper would benefit from engaging with sparse optimization literature, such as Langford et al. (2009) on sparse online learning via truncated gradients, to clarify the novelty of the truncation strategy within the EM context. While the Gaussian mixture example is well-chosen, the manuscript does not explicitly contrast the proposed method with existing sparse EM techniques (e.g., those using Lasso or thresholding in the M-step), which weakens the contribution claim.  

The experimental section lacks crucial details: the initialization procedure for all methods is unspecified, making it impossible to assess whether the basin-of-attraction assumptions were satisfied. For high-dimensional settings, initialization is often critical, and omitting this detail limits reproducibility and the validity of the empirical claims.  

Regarding the theoretical contribution, the geometric convergence guarantee is indeed valuable for high-dimensional learning. However, the paper overstates the necessity of the sparse EM variant. The convergence rate is derived under conditions (smooth Q-function, M-step error control) that apply equally to standard EM; the sparsity requirement only enters via the truncation step, which appears to be a post-processing device rather than an integral component of the algorithm's convergence. If the M-step error control holds for the full parameter vector (as claimed), then applying ordinary EM followed by a single truncation to the final estimate would likely yield identical statistical error bounds—without the added complexity of iterative truncation. The authors' linear regression example supports this, as it reduces to a standard sparse estimation problem where the final truncation suffices.  

The decorrelated score statistic is a solid technical contribution, but its connection to the main sparse EM framework is somewhat tangential. The paper does not clarify how this statistic leverages the sparse EM procedure (e.g., whether it requires the iterative truncation for validity). If it is applicable to any sparse estimator, this would be a stronger point, but the current presentation leaves this ambiguous.  

In summary:  
1. **Strengths**: The convergence guarantee for sparse EM is theoretically sound under the stated assumptions, and the decorrelated score statistic is a useful tool.  
2. **Major Weakness**: The necessity of the *iterative* truncation in the EM procedure is unconvincing. The argument that standard EM + final truncation would fail (e.g., due to instability in the M-step for non-sparse estimates) is not substantiated. The authors' own examples (linear regression, Gaussian mixtures) suggest the simpler alternative is viable.  
3. **Minor Issues**: Missing initialization details in experiments; underdeveloped related work; unclear linkage between the decorrelated score and the sparse EM framework.  

The paper makes a marginal contribution to high-dimensional inference. While the convergence result is correct, the proposed sparse EM algorithm does not appear to offer a significant advantage over standard EM followed by a single truncation step—especially given the authors’ own evidence. The theoretical novelty is therefore limited, and the empirical validation is weakened by the lack of initialization details. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:  
- Rigorously compare sparse EM with standard EM + truncation (both theoretically and empirically),  
- Clarify why iterative truncation is required in cases where the M-step error control holds for full-parameter estimation,  
- Specify the experimental initialization procedure,  
- Expand the related work section to contextualize the truncation strategy.  

Without these revisions, the paper’s main claim—that the sparse EM procedure is essential—lacks sufficient support. The core insight (sparsity via truncation) is not novel, and the algorithmic contribution is questionable. The work would be more impactful if it demonstrated a clear advantage of iterative truncation over simpler alternatives in a broader class of models. As written, the importance of the sparse variant to the results is dubious.
