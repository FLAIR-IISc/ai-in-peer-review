This paper investigates Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms within a high-dimensional framework, establishing convergence results and proposing an inference procedure.

The paper would benefit from a discussion of the conditions under which an initial solution, β<sup>init</sup>, satisfying the requirement specified in line 60 of page 2 can be reliably found. Determining practical methods for initialization is crucial for the algorithm’s usability.

Two algorithms are presented; however, the paper lacks guidance on selecting between them. Clarifying the strengths and weaknesses of each, and providing recommendations based on problem characteristics, would significantly enhance its practical value.

A key concern relates to the choice of tuning parameters, specifically  ̂s and λ. The authors should address how these parameters are selected in practice and analyze the sensitivity of the results to their values. Given the importance of tuning parameter selection in achieving desirable sampling properties in high-dimensional inference – a point well-established in the literature – a data-dependent method for their selection would be particularly valuable. 

The decorrelated score test employed is identical to that described in Ning and Liu [1]. This connection should be explicitly acknowledged through citation rather than being presented as a novel contribution. Furthermore, the authors should clarify if any new development was necessary to adapt the existing inference procedure to the specifics of their current context.

The theoretical results are predicated on a substantial number of technical conditions. The authors should provide a detailed discussion of whether these conditions are reasonably satisfied within the scope of the latent variable models they consider. Justification of these assumptions is vital for establishing the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.

[1] Yang Ning and Han Liu, “A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models.”

This work extends the results of Ning and Liu [1] to a high-dimensional setting and applies their framework to the problem of inference. The paper demonstrates a clever combination of existing results. However, the novelty of the contributions needs to be more clearly articulated. It is not immediately apparent what specific new challenges are overcome, or why applying established tools is not straightforward in this context.

Currently, the paper feels like a condensed preview of the detailed technical arguments presented in the supplementary material. A stronger submission would include a more comprehensive overview of the technical complexities involved in establishing the main results *within* the paper itself. The core challenges and key insights should be highlighted to provide a clearer understanding of the contribution, independent of the supplementary material.
