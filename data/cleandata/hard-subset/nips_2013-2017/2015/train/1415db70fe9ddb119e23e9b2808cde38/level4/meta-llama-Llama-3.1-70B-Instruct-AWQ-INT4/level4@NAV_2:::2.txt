The authors propose a novel high dimensional EM algorithm that naturally incorporates sparsity structure into parameter estimation. They establish a general theory of the EM algorithm for inferring high dimensional latent variable models, making two key contributions: (i) a high dimensional EM algorithm that converges at a geometric rate and attains an estimator with the (near-)optimal statistical rate of convergence, and (ii) a new inferential procedure for testing hypotheses for low dimensional components of high dimensional parameters.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a thorough review of related work. However, the contents are dense, and some sections (mainly 3 and 4) are challenging to follow. The authors could simplify the presentation to make it more accessible to readers.

The authors demonstrate that their framework allows for two implementations of the M-step: exact maximization and approximate maximization via gradient ascent. They illustrate the effectiveness of their framework by applying it to two high dimensional latent variable models: Gaussian mixture model and mixture of regression model.

The authors establish a unified analytic framework that provides simultaneous statistical and computational guarantees for the proposed high dimensional EM algorithm and the respective uncertainty assessment procedure. They prove that the iterative solution sequence satisfies a specific error bound with high probability, and the proposed decorrelated score statistic is asymptotically normal.

The authors also discuss the implications of their work for specific models, including Gaussian mixture model and mixture of regression model. They show that their framework establishes the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and asymptotic inference in high dimensions.

Some questions and concerns arise from the paper:

1. The authors claim that the rate of convergence is (near-)minimax-optimal, but the proof is not provided in the main manuscript. It would be helpful to include a clear proof or a reference to the supplementary material.
2. The choice of the sparsity parameter bs depends on the true sparsity level s, which is unknown in practice. The authors should provide guidance on how to choose bs in practice.
3. The authors require an appropriate initialization init with a relative error upper bounded by a constant. It is unclear how to find such an initial value in practice.
4. The truncation step in Algorithm 1 is crucial, but its importance is not clearly explained. The authors should provide more insight into what happens if the truncation step is skipped.

Overall, the paper presents a solid contribution to the field of statistics, but its readability and practicality could be improved.
