This paper presents a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional statistical settings. The authors introduce a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that integrates a truncation step within its iterations to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. The primary contributions are twofold: first, for parameter estimation, the paper establishes that with a suitable initialization, the proposed algorithm achieves a geometric convergence rate and yields an estimator with a near-optimal statistical rate of convergence. Second, for statistical inference, the authors propose a decorrelated score test to conduct hypothesis tests for low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector, establishing its asymptotic normality. The utility of this framework is demonstrated on two important latent variable models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model. This work represents a significant extension of the low-dimensional analysis of the EM algorithm by Balakrishnan et al. [2] to the challenging high-dimensional (d >> n) regime.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a fundamental and important problem. The EM algorithm is a workhorse for parameter estimation in latent variable models, yet its theoretical properties, particularly in the high-dimensional settings now common in modern data analysis, are not well understood. Providing a computationally feasible algorithm with rigorous guarantees on both its computational (geometric convergence) and statistical (near-minimax optimality) performance is a major contribution to the field.

2.  **Comprehensive Theoretical Framework:** The scope of the work is notably ambitious and complete. It moves beyond point estimation to provide a method for statistical inference, including the construction of hypothesis tests. This provides a much-needed, unified toolkit for researchers and practitioners who use latent variable models with high-dimensional data.

3.  **Novelty in High-Dimensional Inference:** A key contribution is the successful development of a valid inferential procedure for the estimator produced by the proposed EM algorithm. Most existing high-dimensional inference techniques (e.g., debiasing/decorrelation methods) are designed for estimators that are global solutions to convex optimization problems (e.g., the Lasso). This work breaks new ground by showing how such an inferential framework can be adapted for an estimator derived from a non-convex, iterative procedure, which is a crucial and non-trivial step forward.

**Areas for Improvement and Questions for the Authors**

While the theoretical contributions are substantial, the paper could be significantly improved by addressing several points related to practical implementation and clarity of the technical novelty.

1.  **Practical Guidance on Implementation:**
    *   **Initialization:** The theoretical results hinge on the availability of an initial estimator `θ^(init)` that lies within a certain radius of the true parameter `θ*`. This is a standard assumption in non-convex optimization theory, but its practical implication is critical. The paper would be more impactful if it included a discussion on how to obtain such an initial point in practice. Are there specific, computationally efficient procedures (e.g., based on spectral methods or other moment-based approaches) that can be shown to satisfy the initialization requirements for the models considered?
    *   **Tuning Parameter Selection:** The algorithm and the inference procedure depend on key tuning parameters, such as the sparsity level `bs` in Algorithm 1 and the regularization parameter `λ` for the Dantzig selector in the decorrelated score test. The performance of high-dimensional methods is often highly sensitive to the choice of such parameters. The authors should provide practical guidance on how these can be selected in a data-driven way (e.g., via cross-validation or other model selection criteria) and ideally discuss the sensitivity of the results to these choices.
    *   **Choice of M-step:** The paper proposes two implementations for the M-step (exact maximization and gradient ascent). A brief discussion on the practical trade-offs (e.g., computational cost vs. convergence speed) and recommendations on when to prefer one over the other would be helpful for practitioners.

2.  **Clarity of Contribution and Technical Novelty:**
    *   **Relation to Prior Work on Inference:** The decorrelated score test is a powerful and increasingly standard tool. Its formulation in Section 2.2 is very similar to that presented in prior work on high-dimensional inference, such as in Ning and Liu (2017, "A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High Dimensional Models"). This connection should be explicitly cited and discussed. The primary novelty in this paper appears to be the successful application and analysis of this test for an estimator that is not a standard M-estimator. The authors should elaborate on the unique technical challenges that arise from this adaptation. For example, how does the analysis handle the fact that the final estimator `θ_hat` is the product of an iterative algorithm and does not necessarily satisfy the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions of a penalized likelihood problem?
    *   **Elucidation of Technical Challenges:** The main paper serves as a summary of extensive technical work detailed in the appendix. To improve its self-containedness and impact, the authors could provide more intuition in the main text about the key technical hurdles they overcame. What are the main analytical difficulties introduced by adding the truncation step to the EM iterations, and how does this complicate the analysis of the solution path compared to the low-dimensional case in [2]? Clarifying these points would help the reader better appreciate the paper's technical depth.

3.  **Accessibility of Technical Assumptions:** The main theorems rely on a series of high-level technical conditions (e.g., Conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.4). While it is noted that these are verified for specific models in the appendix, the main paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of what these conditions mean in the context of latent variable models. Are these assumptions mild and expected to hold broadly, or are they strong conditions that might limit the applicability of the theory?

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes significant theoretical advances on the analysis of the EM algorithm in a high-dimensional context. The work is comprehensive, addressing both estimation and inference. The main weaknesses relate to a lack of practical guidance and a need for greater clarity regarding the precise nature of the technical innovations, particularly in relation to existing literature. Addressing the points above would substantially strengthen the paper and broaden its impact.
