This paper proposes a sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure that modifies the classical EM algorithm by truncating the parameter vector at each iteration to enforce a desired level of sparsity. Alongside this algorithm, the authors introduce an asymptotically normal statistic, built upon a decorrelated score, for hypothesis testing regarding the non-zero status of individual parameter entries.

The core theoretical contribution is a convergence guarantee for the sparse EM algorithm. Specifically, the estimate is shown to converge geometrically to the true parameter value (with respect to the number of iterations), accompanied by a statistical error term. This error term scales with the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the error incurred in the M-step – a quantity that is often, and thankfully, only logarithmic in the ambient dimension. 

This work builds upon recent advancements in convergence analysis for EM algorithms in low-dimensional settings. As in these prior works, the analysis relies on assumptions regarding the smoothness and approximate quadratic nature of the Q-function within a specified basin of attraction, and crucially, that the algorithm is initialized within this basin. The novelty here lies in an additional condition requiring control over the infinity norm of the error introduced when estimating the M-step.  While this condition is only strictly necessary for sparse parameter vectors, the authors acknowledge that for many models – including the Gaussian mixture model used in their examples and the non-gradient version of a mixture of regression models – estimating M across all parameter vectors presents computational complexity comparable to that of sparse vectors. This observation naturally prompts the question of whether the truncated EM procedure is truly essential.  Could an equivalent guarantee be achieved through standard EM, initialized under similar conditions, followed by a single, final truncation step? The authors address this point, noting that linear regression, as a special case, provides a compelling argument against the sufficiency of a single truncation.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work in the broader field of sparse optimization. For example, the work of Langford et al. (“Sparse online learning via truncated gradient,” 2009) presents relevant techniques that warrant consideration.  Expanding this section would situate the proposed method within the existing landscape of sparsity-inducing algorithms.

The experimental evaluation lacks a crucial detail: the initialization scheme employed.  Clearly stating the initialization method is essential for reproducibility and for understanding the algorithm's practical behavior. 

In summary, this paper presents a sparse EM variant tailored for high-dimensional parameter estimation. The derived convergence guarantees represent a significant theoretical result for high-dimensional learning. However, the necessity of the sparse EM algorithm itself – as opposed to standard EM with post-processing – remains somewhat open to question, despite the authors’ argument relating to linear regression. Addressing the related work and clarifying the initialization strategy would strengthen the paper considerably.
