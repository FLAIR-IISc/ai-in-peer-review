

This paper introduces a modified EM algorithm designed for sparse, high-dimensional parameter estimation. The authors establish convergence conditions to the global solution, derive the rate of convergence, and propose a statistical test framework to assess the sparsity pattern of the estimator. The paper is well-structured, with a thorough related work section. However, the technical exposition in Sections 3 and 4 is dense and difficult to follow; simplification would significantly improve accessibility.

Three key concerns require clarification:

1.  **Minimax Optimality Claim**: The authors assert in Line 66 that the rate √(s log(d/n)) is minimax-optimal. The main manuscript does not provide a proof for this claim. Could the authors explicitly state the minimax lower bound and its matching upper bound within the main text?

2.  **Practical Implementation of ^s**: Equation (3.8) defines ^s as a function of the unknown sparsity level s. How should ^s be selected in practice when s is unknown? Additionally, the convergence analysis (Line 275) requires an initial estimate β^init satisfying ||β^init - β*||₂ < R/2. How can such an initial value be obtained reliably in real applications?

3.  **Role of Truncation**: The truncation step in Algorithm 1 is critical to the method's design. What specific issues arise if this step is omitted? Please elaborate on the theoretical or practical consequences of skipping truncation.

Following the authors' rebuttal, which satisfactorily addressed these points, I have increased my score from 6 to 7. The paper presents a strong statistical contribution with rigorous theoretical foundations. However, the technical depth and notation may pose challenges for ML researchers. While the method is theoretically sound, the practical utility for real-world applications remains somewhat unclear, particularly regarding the feasibility of implementing the required initial conditions and sparsity estimation in complex settings. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of implementation strategies and concrete examples demonstrating the method's advantages over existing approaches in relevant domains. Overall, it is a solid statistical contribution, but its accessibility and immediate applicability for the broader ML community require further refinement.
