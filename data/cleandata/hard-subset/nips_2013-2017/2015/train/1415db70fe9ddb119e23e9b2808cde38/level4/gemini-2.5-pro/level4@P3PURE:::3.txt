This paper proposes a sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure that modifies the classical algorithm by truncating the parameter vector at each M-step to a specified sparsity level. The authors also introduce an asymptotically normal statistic, based on a decorrelated score, for testing the significance of individual parameters.

The primary theoretical contribution is a guarantee that the sparse EM estimate converges geometrically to the true parameter, up to a statistical error term. This error term depends on the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error from the M-step, which is shown to be logarithmic in the ambient dimension for typical models. This work extends recent convergence results for EM in the low-dimensional setting. Similar to previous work, the analysis assumes that the Q-function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic within a basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized within this basin. A key new condition is the requirement of infinity norm control on the M-step estimation error.

A critical point of discussion revolves around this new infinity norm condition. While the theory requires this control only for sparse vectors, for many models—including the Gaussian mixture and non-gradient mixture of regression examples presented—estimating the M-step for a dense vector is computationally equivalent to estimating it for a sparse one. This observation raises a fundamental question: is the iterative truncation inherent to the sparse EM algorithm truly necessary? Could a similar guarantee be obtained by running the standard EM algorithm under the same conditions and performing a single truncation only at the final step? The paper's argument that linear regression is a special case of this framework appears to provide a convincing counterargument to the single-truncation approach, but this point could be elaborated upon to clarify the necessity of the proposed method.

The discussion could be strengthened by connecting the proposed method to related work in the sparse optimization literature, such as truncated gradient methods (e.g., Langford et al., "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient," 2009).

In summary, this paper introduces a sparse EM algorithm for high-dimensional parameter estimation, supported by important theoretical convergence guarantees. However, the main weakness is that the necessity of the iterative sparse EM procedure, compared to a simpler "EM then truncate" baseline, is not fully established. On a minor note, the experimental section should explicitly state the initialization procedure used.
