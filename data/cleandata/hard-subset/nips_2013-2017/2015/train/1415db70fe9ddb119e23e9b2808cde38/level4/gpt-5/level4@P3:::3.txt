Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies EM for high-dimensional latent variable models and makes two main contributions. First, it proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm that inserts a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after each E/M update to enforce sparsity. With an appropriate initialization, the iterates contract geometrically to a neighborhood of the truth, and the final estimator achieves a (near-)minimax statistical rate of order sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (up to extra log factors), where s* is the sparsity level. The analysis covers both exact maximization and a gradient-ascent implementation of the M-step. Second, building on the resulting estimator, the paper develops a decorrelated score test for low-dimensional components of the parameter vector (e.g., a single coordinate), establishing asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency even in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

This work extends a recent line of results on EM that characterize local geometric convergence under suitable initialization in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., population-to-sample EM analysis), and classical EM convergence (Wu; Tseng’s proximal-point perspective). It complements high-dimensional inference via de-biasing/desparsification (e.g., van de Geer et al.; Zhang & Zhang) by handling nonconvex likelihoods and estimators produced by EM rather than global optima of convex programs. Compared with tensor methods for latent variable models, the proposed approach targets sharper statistical rates and broader high-dimensional regimes.

Strengths:
- Quality/technical soundness: The theory is detailed and carefully structured. The contraction result decomposes error into an optimization term (geometric decay) and a statistical term driven by an entrywise deviation ε between population and sample M-steps; this is the right scale for high-dimensional control. The inference procedure leverages a Dantzig-type estimator of the influence vector to construct a decorrelated score, and the asymptotic variance attains the semiparametric information bound.
- Originality: Integrating hard-thresholding within EM and analyzing its effect on both support evolution and estimation error appears novel for latent variable models. Establishing asymptotic inference for EM-based estimators in high dimensions is also new and impactful.
- Significance: Provides a general, computationally feasible recipe for high-dimensional estimation and inference beyond GLMs, with concrete treatments of Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions. The guarantees are near-optimal and connect computation and statistics.
- Clarity of main ideas: The high-level structure (E/M/T steps; two M-step implementations; conditions on Lipschitz-gradient and local concavity; resampling-based analysis) is clear.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Initialization and basin-of-attraction: Practical guidance is limited. Please specify the initialization used in experiments and discuss data-driven initializers (e.g., spectral, method-of-moments, k-means for GMMs), and the empirical sensitivity to initialization. Clarifying how large the SNR (for GMMs) must be and how the basin radius compares with typical initialization error would aid reproducibility.
- Necessity of iterative truncation: The paper argues for the T-step at every iteration. A natural question is whether (i) ordinary EM followed by a single truncation or (ii) EM with an ℓ1 penalty in the M-step (cf. penalized EM for mixtures) would deliver comparable guarantees. The linear regression special case supports the iterative truncation choice; consider adding a short discussion contrasting your bound with what one might get from post-hoc truncation or ℓ1-penalized EM, even if only at a heuristic level. Empirical comparison to a penalized-EM baseline would be informative.
- Scope of Condition 3.3 (entrywise M-step error): The theory assumes high-probability ℓ∞ control on Mn(·)−M(·) for sparse inputs. Please clarify when this is available (e.g., distributional assumptions on covariates in mixture of regressions, dependence on design covariance, non-Gaussian noise), and whether similar bounds hold without sample splitting/resampling. An extension of the analysis to a single-sample (no resampling) implementation would increase practical relevance.
- Tuning and implementation details: Provide concrete guidance on choosing bs (sparsity level), stepsize for gradient EM, and the Dantzig tuning parameter for the decorrelated score. Discuss robustness to mis-specifying bs and to violations of the sparsity of the influence vector w*. Report computational cost (especially for solving the Dantzig program) and wall-clock comparisons.
- Empirical validation: Please state the exact initialization used, the truncation level, stepsizes, and stopping criteria. Consider adding comparisons to (i) ℓ1-penalized likelihood via coordinate descent or proximal methods, and (ii) spectral/tensor methods where applicable, to illustrate advantages in both accuracy and runtime.
- Related work: It would be helpful to discuss connections to truncated gradient and hard-thresholding strategies in optimization (e.g., “Sparse Online Learning via Truncated Gradient,” Langford et al., 2009; iterative hard thresholding) to position your T-step within that literature. Also contrast with penalized EM approaches for mixtures and with post-selection inference methods in high dimensions.

Evaluation by NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Strong, with clear statements of contraction, careful control of statistical errors, and asymptotic normality with optimal variance. The reliance on local regularity and initialization is standard in EM analyses but should be discussed more practically.
- Clarity: Generally good, but notation is heavy. Suggestions: collect all main assumptions early with a concise glossary; provide a brief “how to verify” checklist for new models; move some technical constants to an appendix table; add a schematic of the algorithm and inference pipeline.
- Originality: High for combining truncated EM with sharp statistical and inferential guarantees for latent variable models.
- Significance: High, given the gap in inference for nonconvex latent variable models in high dimensions and the generality of the framework.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and general framework enabling both estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models.
- Near-optimal statistical rates with explicit computational guarantees.
- Asymptotically valid, semiparametrically efficient inference procedure beyond GLMs.
- Clear instantiations for two canonical models.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical aspects (initialization, tuning, and no-resampling theory) need further development.
- Limited empirical comparisons to strong high-dimensional baselines (e.g., ℓ1-penalized EM).
- Assumptions such as sparsity of the influence vector w* and strong SNR may restrict applicability; more discussion/examples would help.

Overall: This is a strong theoretical paper that advances the state of the art on high-dimensional EM and inference for latent variable models. With additional clarity on initialization and practical tuning, and stronger empirical baselines, it would make a valuable contribution.
