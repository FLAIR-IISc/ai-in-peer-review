This paper proposes a sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure, which modifies the classical EM algorithm by introducing a truncation step to enforce a desired sparsity level on the parameter vector after each iteration. Additionally, the paper introduces an asymptotically normal statistic, based on a decorrelated score, for conducting hypothesis tests on whether a specific entry of the parameter vector is non-zero.

The primary contribution of this work is a theoretical guarantee for the proposed sparse EM algorithm. The authors prove that their estimate converges geometrically to the true parameter value, up to a statistical error. This error term scales with the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step, which itself is typically logarithmic in the ambient dimension. This result extends recent theoretical analyses of EM in low-dimensional settings to the high-dimensional sparse regime, which is a significant and timely contribution.

The analysis builds upon previous work by assuming that the Q-function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic within a basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized within this basin. A key new condition is the requirement of infinity norm control on the error in the M-step. While the theory only requires this condition to hold for sparse vectors, the authors acknowledge that for many models—such as the Gaussian mixture model presented—estimating the M-step for any vector is computationally equivalent to estimating it for a sparse one.

This observation raises a fundamental question about the necessity of the proposed iterative truncation scheme. Specifically, could a similar theoretical guarantee be obtained by running the standard EM algorithm (under the same assumptions) and applying a single hard-thresholding step only at the final iteration? I acknowledge the authors' point that for certain sub-problems, such as linear regression, a single truncation step would be insufficient. This is a compelling argument. However, it would strengthen the paper to elaborate on this point and more clearly delineate the class of models for which iterative truncation is essential, versus those where a "run-then-truncate" approach might suffice. Clarifying this would help solidify the motivation for the sparse EM variant itself, beyond its role as an analysis tool.

The paper could also be strengthened by a discussion of related work in the broader sparse optimization literature. For instance, methods based on truncated gradients (e.g., "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient," Langford et al., 2009) share a similar spirit of iteratively enforcing sparsity and might provide valuable context for the proposed algorithm.

Finally, in the experimental section, the initialization strategy for the algorithm is not specified. Given the theoretical reliance on initialization within a basin of attraction, detailing the initialization method is crucial for reproducibility and for assessing the practical applicability of the theoretical results.

In summary, this paper presents an important theoretical result for parameter estimation in high-dimensional latent variable models. The convergence guarantees for the sparse EM algorithm are a valuable contribution. The central uncertainty lies in clarifying the precise role and necessity of the iterative sparsity projection compared to simpler alternatives. Addressing this point more directly would significantly enhance the paper's impact.
