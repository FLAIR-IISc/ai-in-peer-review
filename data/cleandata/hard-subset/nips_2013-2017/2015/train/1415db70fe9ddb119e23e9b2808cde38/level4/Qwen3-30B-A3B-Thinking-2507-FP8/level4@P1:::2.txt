

This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure for parameter estimation in latent variable models, along with a new inferential procedure for testing hypotheses about low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters. The authors make two key contributions: (i) a high-dimensional EM algorithm with geometric convergence rates and near-optimal statistical rates of convergence, and (ii) a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference that achieves the semiparametric information bound. This work extends the theoretical guarantees of the EM algorithm from low-dimensional to high-dimensional settings, where traditional approaches are not applicable due to the nonconcavity of the likelihood function and the challenges of high-dimensional nuisance parameters.

The paper is well-structured and provides a thorough comparison with related work. The authors appropriately reference seminal papers on the EM algorithm [12, 30], recent advances in low-dimensional EM analysis [2, 18, 25], and related work on high-dimensional inference [13, 26, 32]. They clearly articulate how their approach differs from existing methods, particularly by addressing the high-dimensional regime where d ≫ n, which has been largely unexplored in the context of EM algorithms.

Strengths:
- The theoretical framework is comprehensive, providing both computational and statistical guarantees for the proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm.
- The authors establish a near-optimal statistical rate of convergence (s* log d/n) for the estimator, which is a significant improvement over the d/n rate in low-dimensional settings.
- The decorrelated score statistic achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound, which is a notable theoretical contribution.
- The framework is general enough to apply to multiple latent variable models (Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression), with specific verifications provided in the appendix.

Weaknesses:
- The paper is technically dense, particularly in Sections 3 and 4, which makes it challenging to follow for readers unfamiliar with high-dimensional statistics. More intuitive explanations or visualizations would help improve readability.
- The manuscript does not provide clear guidance on how to choose the sparsity parameter ŝ in practice (Eq. 3.8), which depends on the unknown true sparsity level s*. Similarly, it's unclear how to obtain an appropriate initial estimate β̂^init satisfying ||β̂^init - β*||_2 ≤ R/2 in real applications.
- The importance of the truncation step (Algorithm 1, line 6) is not sufficiently justified. The authors should explain what would happen if this step were omitted, particularly in terms of the statistical properties of the estimator.
- While the authors claim that sqrt(s*log(d/n)) is minimax-optimal (line 066), they do not provide a proof of this claim in the main manuscript. This is a critical gap in the theoretical argument.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the literature on high-dimensional inference for latent variable models. It addresses an important gap in the existing literature by establishing the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and asymptotic inference in high dimensions. The results have the potential to be impactful for practitioners working with high-dimensional latent variable models.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS in terms of technical quality, originality, and significance. It advances the state of the art by providing the first theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The results are significant for the field as they establish a framework for optimal estimation and inference in high dimensions for a broad class of latent variable models.

Pro:
- Novel theoretical framework for high-dimensional EM algorithm
- Near-optimal statistical rates of convergence
- Semiparametrically efficient inference procedure
- Applicable to multiple latent variable models
- Comprehensive theoretical guarantees

Con:
- Poorly explained practical implementation details (sparsity parameter selection, initialization)
- Dense technical presentation that hinders readability
- Missing proof of minimax optimality claim

The authors have made a solid contribution to the field, but the paper would benefit from improved clarity and more practical guidance. Given the theoretical strength of the work, I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the concerns about practical implementation in the camera-ready version. After the authors' rebuttal (which increased the score from 6 to 7), I acknowledge that they satisfactorily addressed the concerns about the minimax optimality claim. However, the paper remains challenging to read for the typical NIPS audience, and the practical utility of the method for real-world applications is still somewhat questionable.
