This paper proposes a novel high dimensional EM algorithm that naturally incorporates sparsity structure into parameter estimation. The algorithm modifies the classical EM algorithm by truncating the parameter vector at each step to a desired sparsity level. Additionally, the authors propose a decorrelated score statistic for testing hypotheses related to low dimensional components of high dimensional parameters. The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, including its convergence guarantees and the asymptotic normality of the decorrelated score statistic.

The paper makes two significant contributions. Firstly, it proposes a high dimensional EM algorithm that achieves an estimator with the (near-)optimal statistical rate of convergence. Secondly, it develops a decorrelated score statistic that allows for asymptotic inference for low dimensional components of high dimensional parameters. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework by applying it to two high dimensional latent variable models, namely Gaussian mixture model and mixture of regression model.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their methodology and theoretical results. The use of technical conditions and assumptions is well-justified, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of their implications. The paper also provides a comprehensive review of related work, including recent results on EM in the low-dimensional setting.

One potential criticism of the paper is that the importance of the sparse variant of EM to the obtained results is not entirely clear. The authors assume that the Q function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic in a certain basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized in the same basin. These assumptions are similar to those made in previous work on EM in the low-dimensional setting. However, the authors' observation that linear regression is a special case of their framework, and that a single truncation step is not sufficient to achieve the desired guarantees, provides a convincing argument for the necessity of the truncated EM algorithm.

Another potential area for improvement is the experimental section, which is currently missing. The authors should provide a detailed description of the initialization used in their experiments, as well as a comparison of their results with those obtained using alternative methods.

In terms of related work, the authors may want to consider discussing recent results in sparse optimization, such as "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" by Langford et al (2009). This could provide additional context and insights into the importance of sparsity in high dimensional learning.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of high dimensional learning, and the authors' theoretical results are an important step forward in our understanding of the EM algorithm in high dimensional settings. With some minor revisions, including the addition of an experimental section and a discussion of related work in sparse optimization, this paper has the potential to make a major impact in the field.
