A sparse Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure is proposed, modifying the classical EM algorithm by truncating the parameter vector at each step to achieve a desired sparsity level. Additionally, an asymptotically normal statistic based on a decorrelated score is introduced for testing whether a specific entry of the parameter vector is non-zero.

The sparse EM algorithm provides a guarantee of geometric convergence to the true value, up to a statistical error term. This error term is proportional to the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step, which is typically logarithmic in the ambient dimension. This result builds upon recent similar findings for EM in low-dimensional settings.

Similar to previous work, the analysis assumes that the Q function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic within a certain basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized within this basin. A new condition is introduced, requiring control over the infinity norm of the error in estimating M. Notably, this condition is only necessary for sparse vectors. However, for many models, estimating M at all possible vectors is as straightforward as estimating it at sparse vectors. This raises the question of whether the truncated EM algorithm is necessary. Could a similar guarantee be obtained by performing ordinary EM under the same conditions, followed by a single truncation step? The authors' observation that linear regression is a special case in this context appears to provide a convincing argument against a single truncation step.

Some related work in sparse optimization, such as "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" by Langford et al. (2009), may be worth discussing in this context. The experimental section should specify the initialization method used. The proposed sparse EM variant for parameter estimation in high-dimensional settings yields important convergence guarantees for high-dimensional learning. However, the significance of the sparse variant of EM in achieving these results is not entirely clear.

Overall, the paper presents a novel sparse EM algorithm with convergence guarantees, which is an important contribution to high-dimensional learning. Nevertheless, further discussion on the necessity of the sparse EM algorithm and its relationship to existing work in sparse optimization would strengthen the paper.
