This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in latent variable modeling: applying the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm effectively in high-dimensional settings where the number of parameters far exceeds the sample size. The authors provide a theoretical framework for a modified EM algorithm incorporating sparsity, establishing convergence rates and developing an inferential procedure for hypothesis testing. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, with appropriate initialization and a truncation step to enforce sparsity, the algorithm can achieve near-optimal statistical rates and enable asymptotically valid inference.

The paper is well-structured and positions itself clearly within the existing literature on EM algorithm convergence and high-dimensional statistics. The related work section is comprehensive, accurately summarizing prior efforts and highlighting the gaps this work aims to fill. However, as noted in the initial draft, the density of the presentation, particularly in sections 3 and 4, presents a challenge to clarity. While mathematically rigorous, the technical details could benefit from further explanation and simplification to broaden accessibility.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** The paper tackles a significant limitation of the EM algorithm – its lack of theoretical guarantees in high-dimensional settings – opening up possibilities for applying it to more complex, realistic models.
*   **Novel Technical Contributions:** The introduction of the truncation step within the EM framework is a key innovation. The authors rigorously analyze its impact on both the computational convergence and statistical properties of the estimator.
*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides formal convergence rates and establishes asymptotic normality for the estimator and associated hypothesis tests, offering a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Generality:** The framework is presented as applicable to a broad family of latent variable models, increasing its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density and Technicality:** The mathematical notation and derivations are dense, potentially hindering understanding for readers not deeply familiar with the specific technical tools employed.
*   **Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the practical implications – specifically, determining appropriate values for parameters like the sparsity level (bs) and initialization conditions (beta^init) – are not fully addressed. The dependence on unknown quantities like the true sparsity level `s*` poses a challenge for real-world implementation.
*   **Justification of Optimality:** The claim of minimax optimality (line 66) requires more explicit justification within the main manuscript. Referencing the appropriate theoretical results supporting this claim would strengthen the argument.
*   **Impact of Truncation:** The authors do not clearly explain the negative consequences of skipping the truncation step, making it hard to fully understand its importance.

**Specific Questions & Concerns Addressed (from initial draft):**

The authors provided a helpful rebuttal addressing the initial concerns.  Specifically:

*   **Minimax Optimality:** The rebuttal clarifies that the minimax optimality claim refers to the convergence rate for sparse estimators and directs the reader to relevant theoretical results in the literature.
*   **Sparsity Level Selection:** The authors discuss techniques for selecting the sparsity level, including cross-validation and information criteria.
*   **Initialization:**  The rebuttal suggests practical initialization strategies, such as using a small random vector or employing a preliminary screening procedure.
*   **Importance of Truncation:** The authors explain that without truncation, the EM algorithm is likely to converge to a suboptimal solution, especially in high dimensions.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the behavior of the EM algorithm in high dimensions.
*   The proposed algorithm and inferential procedure offer a potentially valuable tool for analyzing complex latent variable models.
*   The paper is rigorously written and demonstrates a deep understanding of the relevant statistical concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The high level of technicality and lack of detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges may limit the paper’s immediate impact.
*   The dependence on unknown parameters in the algorithm (e.g., the true sparsity level) could hinder its widespread adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid statistical contribution that addresses an important problem. While the paper requires a significant investment from the reader due to its technical density, the theoretical results are compelling and offer a valuable addition to the literature on high-dimensional statistics and the EM algorithm. The rebuttal adequately addressed the initial concerns regarding practical implementation. While the practical usefulness remains somewhat questionable, the theoretical contribution justifies acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept (Score: 7/10) – A strong paper with significant theoretical merit, albeit with some limitations in terms of accessibility and practical guidance.
