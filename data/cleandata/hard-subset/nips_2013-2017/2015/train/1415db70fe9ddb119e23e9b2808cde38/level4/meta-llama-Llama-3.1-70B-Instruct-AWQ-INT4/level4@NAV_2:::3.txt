The paper proposes a novel high dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure into parameter estimation. The algorithm is shown to converge at a geometric rate to the true parameter, up to a statistical error term that depends on the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step. The authors also propose a decorrelated score statistic for testing hypotheses about low dimensional components of high dimensional parameters, which is shown to be asymptotically normal.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a general inferential theory for the EM algorithm in high dimensional settings. The authors provide a unified framework for simultaneous parameter estimation and uncertainty assessment, which is a significant advancement in the field. The paper also includes a thorough analysis of the theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithm, including the convergence rate and the statistical error term.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The use of technical conditions and assumptions is well-motivated and clearly explained. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with related work, which helps to situate their contributions in the context of existing research.

One potential limitation of the paper is that the importance of the sparse variant of EM to the obtained results is not entirely clear. The authors' observation that linear regression is a special case of their framework seems to be a convincing argument against a single truncation step, but it is not entirely clear why the truncated EM algorithm is necessary. It would be helpful to see a more detailed discussion of this point, as well as a comparison with related work in sparse optimization.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of high dimensional learning. The development of a general inferential theory for the EM algorithm in high dimensional settings is a major advancement, and the proposal of a decorrelated score statistic for testing hypotheses about low dimensional components of high dimensional parameters is a novel and important result.

The paper is well-suited for publication in a top-tier journal, and the results are likely to be of interest to a broad range of researchers in machine learning and statistics. The authors' writing style is clear and concise, making the paper accessible to a wide range of readers.
