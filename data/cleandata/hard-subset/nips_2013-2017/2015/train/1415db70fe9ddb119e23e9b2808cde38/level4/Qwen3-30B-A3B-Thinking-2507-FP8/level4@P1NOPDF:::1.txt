

The paper investigates expectation-maximization (EM) for high-dimensional inference, establishing convergence guarantees and developing a novel inference procedure. However, several critical points require clarification.  

First, the authors must address under what conditions one can obtain an initial solution $\beta^{\rm init}$ satisfying the requirement in line 60 (p. 2). Without this, the proposed framework lacks a concrete starting point.  

The paper introduces two algorithms but provides no practical guidance on selecting between them. The authors should clarify when one algorithm is preferable to the other, particularly in terms of computational efficiency or statistical performance.  

Regarding tuning parameters, the paper does not specify how to choose $\hat s$ and $\lambda$ in practice, nor does it address their sensitivity. Given that tuning parameter selection is critical for achieving valid sampling properties in high-dimensional inference (as established in the literature), the authors must discuss: (1) data-dependent methods for choosing these parameters, and (2) empirical evidence on how results vary with their values.  

The decorrelated score test employed is identical to that in Ning and Liu (2017). This should be explicitly acknowledged and cited, rather than treated as novel. The authors must clarify what novel developments are required to adapt this existing inference procedure to their latent variable framework. If no new theory is needed, the contribution’s novelty is significantly diminished.  

The main theoretical results rely on multiple technical conditions. The authors should explicitly discuss whether these conditions hold for the specific latent variable models they consider. A brief analysis of model assumptions (e.g., sparsity, eigenvalue constraints) in the context of their applications would strengthen the paper.  

Finally, while the paper cleverly combines existing results (notably extending Ning and Liu, 2017, to high-dimensional EM), it reads like a "trailer" for the supplementary material. The main text should more clearly articulate the paper’s *novel contributions*—not just the combination of prior work—and detail the specific technical challenges overcome to establish the main results. The supplementary material should not serve as the primary vehicle for explaining the core innovations. The current presentation risks understating the paper’s true novelty and obscuring the significance of its technical advances.
