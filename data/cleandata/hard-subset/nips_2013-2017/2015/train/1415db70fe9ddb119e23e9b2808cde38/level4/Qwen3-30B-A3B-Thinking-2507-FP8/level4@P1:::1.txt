

This paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm for latent variable models with sparse structure, establishing both computational and statistical guarantees for parameter estimation and inference. The authors introduce a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, proving geometric convergence to an estimator with (near-)optimal statistical rate of $O(\sqrt{s^* \log d / n})$. They further develop a decorrelated score test for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional parameter components, establishing asymptotic normality with optimal limiting variance. This work extends the theoretical framework of [2] to high-dimensional settings, addressing a significant gap in the literature where no theoretical guarantees existed for EM in high dimensions.

The paper makes two primary contributions: (i) a high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity via truncation steps, achieving geometric convergence rates and optimal statistical rates, and (ii) a decorrelated score test for asymptotic inference of low-dimensional parameter components. These contributions are significant because they establish the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors demonstrate their framework's applicability to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, showing how their approach differs from prior work that focused on low-dimensional settings.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical framework is comprehensive, addressing both computational and statistical aspects simultaneously. The extension of the EM algorithm to high dimensions is technically sophisticated, with careful attention to the challenges posed by the truncation step. The authors provide a unified analytic framework that applies to a broad family of latent variable models, which is a significant contribution to the field. The proof techniques for establishing the geometric convergence rate and the asymptotic normality of the decorrelated score statistic are particularly impressive, demonstrating deep theoretical understanding.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the authors should clarify the conditions under which a suitable initial solution $\beta^{\text{init}}$ satisfying $\|\beta^{\text{init}} - \beta^*\|_2 / \|\beta^*\|_2 \leq \epsilon$ can be found in practice. The paper assumes such initialization is available but does not discuss practical methods for obtaining it in high-dimensional settings. This is particularly important given that finding good initializations for EM algorithms is notoriously difficult.

Second, the paper proposes two algorithmic implementations (exact maximization and gradient ascent), but provides no practical guidance on which to use in different scenarios. The authors should discuss computational trade-offs between these approaches and provide empirical evidence to support their recommendations.

Third, the paper lacks detailed discussion of tuning parameter selection. The authors use $\hat{s}$ and $\lambda$ but do not address how these should be chosen in practice, how sensitive the results are to their choice, or whether data-dependent methods (such as cross-validation) can be applied. This is a significant omission given the critical importance of tuning parameter selection in high-dimensional inference.

Fourth, the decorrelated score test closely resembles the method in Ning and Liu [27], but this connection is not sufficiently acknowledged. The authors should explicitly cite Ning and Liu's work and clarify what novel theoretical developments were required to apply their framework to high-dimensional latent variable models. This is particularly important as the paper's main claim to novelty hinges on extending existing inference methods to this new context.

Fifth, while the paper states that conditions are verified for specific models in the appendix, it does not provide sufficient discussion of whether these technical conditions are reasonable for the considered models. The authors should more clearly explain why these conditions are plausible for Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, rather than simply stating they are satisfied.

The paper's presentation as a "trailer" for supplementary material is problematic. The main body should contain sufficient technical detail to understand the core contributions, not merely outline them. The authors should expand the main text to include more details about the technical challenges in establishing the main results, particularly regarding the analysis of the truncation step's effects on the iterative solution sequence.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The statistical analysis is rigorous and the computational guarantees are clearly established. The paper is well-organized and generally clear, though some technical passages could be tightened for readability.

The originality is strong in the high-dimensional setting, though the inference approach builds on existing techniques. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from previous low-dimensional results [2, 25, 30] and the more recent work on high-dimensional inference [27, 26, 32].

The significance is high. This work addresses a critical gap in the literature by providing the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The results have clear potential for impact in applications involving high-dimensional data with latent structures.

In conclusion, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by extending EM to high-dimensional settings with rigorous theoretical guarantees. However, the paper would be significantly stronger with more detailed discussion of initialization strategies, practical algorithm selection guidance, tuning parameter selection methods, and clearer articulation of how their inference procedure extends existing work. The authors should also provide more concrete details about the technical conditions in the main text rather than deferring them to the appendix.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution extending EM to high dimensions with rigorous computational and statistical guarantees; clear demonstration of the framework's applicability to important models; comprehensive treatment of both estimation and inference.

Con: Insufficient discussion of practical implementation details (initialization, algorithm selection, tuning parameters); insufficient acknowledgment of the relationship to existing inference methods; technical conditions not sufficiently explained in the main text; presentation as a "trailer" for supplementary material rather than a complete paper.
