Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm for latent variable models when the true parameter is sparse. The key idea is to append a truncation step (T-step) to each EM iteration, retaining only the bs largest coordinates to enforce sparsity. Under appropriate initialization and regularity conditions, the algorithm achieves geometric optimization convergence and (near-)minimax statistical rates of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to log factors). The paper also proposes a decorrelated score for inference on low-dimensional components of the parameter in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, and proves asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency.

The work builds on and extends recent analyses of EM in low-dimensional settings (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) by handling d >> n with sparsity. For inference, it connects to the desparsification/debiasing literature (e.g., van de Geer et al., Javanmard & Montanari, Zhang & Zhang), but adapts the construction to latent variable models where likelihoods are non-convex and global optima are intractable. The paper demonstrates the framework on high-dimensional Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, and discusses both exact and gradient-ascent M-steps.

Strengths
- Novelty: A conceptually clean and broadly applicable modification of EM that enforces sparsity along the trajectory, coupled with finite-sample, non-asymptotic guarantees.
- Theory: Clear decomposition of estimation error into optimization and statistical components; geometric convergence and near-optimal rates; asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency for a decorrelated score test in latent-variable settings.
- Generality: Covers exact and approximate (gradient) M-steps under unified conditions; the analysis framework highlights what is needed to verify conditions across models.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity and density: Sections 3 and 4 are dense and hard to parse for many NIPS readers. Consider moving some technical conditions and proofs to the appendix, adding intuition boxes (e.g., why each condition is needed), and including a running toy example that translates the conditions into model-specific quantities.
- Practical guidance:
  • Choice of bs (sparsity level): Eq. (3.8) requires bs to scale with s*, which is unknown. Please provide a practical selection strategy (e.g., cross-validation on predictive likelihood, information criteria, stability selection, or an adaptive rule based on thresholding gradients or iterates). A short sensitivity study would help.
  • Initialization: The guarantees require an initializer within R/2 of β*. Please discuss model-specific initializers (e.g., spectral or method-of-moments/tensor-based, k-means/EM warm starts for mixtures, Lasso-based estimates for mixture of regressions) and multiple restarts plus selection by held-out likelihood. Give concrete defaults.
  • Truncation step: The manuscript should more explicitly justify why the T-step is essential. Skipping truncation would not enforce sparsity; the iterates would generally be dense, leading to statistical errors that scale like sqrt(d/n) rather than sqrt(s* log d / n), and the support of the iterates could drift, making it harder to remain in the basin of attraction. The entrywise control (ℓ1 statistical error) used in the proofs leverages sparsity at each step; without truncation, these key lemmas fail. A short subsection contrasting “with vs. without truncation” would strengthen the narrative.
- Minimax claim: The claim that the estimator achieves the (near-)minimax rate sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to log n) is central. Please (i) state explicitly the precise lower bound and its assumptions, (ii) cite where it is proved (main text if possible; otherwise provide a clear pointer to the appendix or external references), and (iii) clarify the “near-” qualifier (e.g., extra log n).
- Theory vs. practice: The main theorems analyze a resampling version of the algorithm for technical simplicity. Please clarify what guarantees carry over to the practical, single-sample implementation, and whether empirical results suggest similar behavior.
- Applicability: Empirical evidence on real datasets is limited, and the strong conditions and tuning requirements may deter practitioners. Additional experiments comparing against ℓ1-penalized EM baselines, tensor methods, and desparsified procedures where applicable (even if approximate) would help establish usefulness.

Quality
- Technically solid and careful, with non-asymptotic bounds and a clean separation of optimization and statistics. Assumptions are standard for this line of work, though checking them can be involved for new models. The inference results are particularly compelling, achieving the semiparametric efficiency bound.

Clarity
- Well organized overall, but technical sections are very dense. Adding intuitive explanations, diagrams of the algorithmic pipeline, and explicit pointers to proofs and conditions would significantly improve readability.

Originality
- High. The sparse EM with iterative truncation and the decorrelated score for latent variable models constitute a novel synthesis that goes beyond prior EM analyses and debiasing methods tailored to convex M-estimators.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is strong: first general framework for optimal estimation and asymptotic inference for high-dimensional latent variable models via EM-type iterations. Practical significance depends on availability of good initializers and robust tuning; additional empirical validation would enhance impact.

Specific questions for the authors
- Minimax optimality: Please provide a self-contained statement and proof outline (or precise pointer) supporting the sqrt(s* log d / n) rate claim.
- Choice of bs and initialization: Include practical selection guidelines and a default recipe per model class.
- Necessity of the T-step: Add an ablation (without T-step) to illustrate degradation in rates and/or convergence.
- Conditions and constants: Offer guidance on checking key conditions (e.g., SNR regimes for mixtures) and discuss sensitivity to misspecification.

Pros for acceptance
- Advances state-of-the-art theory for high-dimensional EM with both estimation and inference.
- General, modular framework with clear conditions.
- Semiparametric efficiency for inference in latent-variable settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Dense exposition; accessibility to the NIPS audience could be improved.
- Limited practical guidance on tuning and initialization; few real-data experiments.
- Some claims (minimax rate) need clearer in-text support.

After rebuttal
The authors addressed my main concerns satisfactorily, especially regarding the role of truncation and practical guidance. I am raising my score from 6 to 7. This is a solid statistical contribution, though still challenging for many ML practitioners, and the practical usefulness would benefit from more empirical validation and guidance.
