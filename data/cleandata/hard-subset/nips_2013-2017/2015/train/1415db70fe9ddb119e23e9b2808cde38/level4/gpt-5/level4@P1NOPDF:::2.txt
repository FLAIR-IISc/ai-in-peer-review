In this paper, the authors propose a modification of the EM algorithm tailored to sparse, high-dimensional settings. They establish conditions under which the method converges to the global solution and derive its convergence rate. They also develop a statistical testing framework that allows one to assess the sparsity of the estimator.

The paper is well organized, and the related work is adequately summarized. However, the presentation is very dense—especially Sections 3 and 4—and some arguments are hard to follow. Additional simplification and intuition would help readers.

- In line 066, the authors claim that sqrt(s*log(d/n)) is minimax-optimal. I could not find a proof of this in the main manuscript (I did not read the supplementary material). Could you provide a clear, self-contained proof or at least a complete sketch within the paper?

- In Eq. (3.8), ^s depends on the unknown true sparsity level s. How should ^s be chosen in practice? Please provide concrete guidance (e.g., data-driven selection, cross-validation, stability selection, or information criteria), and discuss the impact of mis-specifying ^s. In addition, how can one obtain an initial value beta^init satisfying ||beta^init - beta*||_2 < R/2 (line 275) in practice? What practical initialization strategies meet this condition, and how sensitive are the guarantees to violations of this requirement?

- I do not see why the truncation step in Algorithm 1 is essential. What happens if we omit truncation from the EM updates? Do the theoretical guarantees fail (e.g., loss of contractivity or identifiability), or is the effect primarily empirical (e.g., slower convergence, support instability, or accumulation of small spurious coefficients)? A comparison—both theoretical intuition and empirical evidence—would clarify its necessity.
