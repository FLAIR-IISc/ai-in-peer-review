This paper introduces a sparse variant of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which modifies the classical procedure by applying a truncation operator to the parameter vector at each iteration to enforce a specified level of sparsity. Additionally, the authors propose an asymptotically normal test statistic, derived from a decorrelated score, for conducting inference on whether individual parameters are non-zero.

The theoretical analysis establishes that the sparse EM estimate achieves a geometric rate of convergence to the true parameter, within a statistical error bound. This error term is characterized by the square root of the sparsity level and the L-infinity norm of the M-step's statistical error, a quantity that is generally only logarithmic in the ambient dimension. This contribution builds upon recent theoretical work on EM in low-dimensional regimes. Consistent with prior work, the analysis relies on assumptions of sufficient smoothness and near-quadrature of the Q-function within a basin of attraction, where the algorithm must also be initialized. A key new assumption is the requirement of L-infinity norm control over the M-step estimation error.

While this condition is formally required only for sparse vectors, for many models—including the Gaussian mixture example presented and the non-gradient version of the mixture of regression model—the computational cost of the M-step is equivalent for both sparse and dense vectors. This observation raises a fundamental question regarding the necessity of the proposed iterative truncation. Specifically, could a comparable theoretical guarantee be achieved by running the standard EM algorithm under identical conditions and applying a single truncation step only to the final estimate? EDIT: The authors’ point that linear regression represents a special case of their framework provides a compelling argument against the viability of such a single-step truncation approach.

The discussion could be strengthened by contextualizing the proposed method with related work in the sparse optimization literature. For instance, methods like truncated gradient (e.g., Langford et al., 2009) appear relevant and warrant discussion.

For reproducibility, the experimental section should explicitly detail the initialization strategy used for the algorithm. In summary, this work proposes a sparse EM algorithm for high-dimensional parameter estimation. The accompanying convergence guarantees are a significant contribution to the theory of high-dimensional learning, although the critical role of the iterative sparse EM procedure in achieving these results is not entirely clear.
