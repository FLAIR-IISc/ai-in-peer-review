The paper introduces a sparsity-aware EM variant that augments the classical EM procedure with a truncation step enforcing a prescribed sparsity level at each iteration. It also develops an inferential method based on a decorrelated score, yielding an asymptotically normal statistic for testing whether a particular coordinate of the parameter vector is nonzero.

The main guarantee for the sparse EM shows geometric convergence (in iteration count) toward the truth, up to a statistical error that scales with the square root of the sparsity and the sup-norm of the M-step’s estimation error, which typically grows only logarithmically with the ambient dimension.

This work builds on recent results establishing analogous properties of EM in low-dimensional regimes. As in that line of work, the analysis assumes the Q-function is sufficiently smooth and approximately quadratic within a suitable basin of attraction, and that the initialization lies in that basin. The additional ingredient here is a sup-norm control of the error in approximating the M-step, required only for sparse vectors; for many models, however, computing the M-step at arbitrary points is no harder than at sparse points (as in the Gaussian mixture example and the non-gradient variant of mixture of regressions). This raises a concern: is the truncation within EM essential? Could one run standard EM under the same assumptions and then apply a single truncation afterward? EDIT: the authors’ observation that linear regression falls under this framework makes a single post hoc truncation unconvincing.

There appears to be related literature in sparse optimization worth discussing, e.g., “Sparse online learning via truncated gradient” (Langford et al., 2009).

The experiments should explicitly describe the initialization strategy.

In sum, the paper proposes a sparse EM approach for high-dimensional estimation. The convergence results are valuable for high-dimensional learning, though the necessity of the truncated variant for the stated guarantees remains somewhat unclear.
