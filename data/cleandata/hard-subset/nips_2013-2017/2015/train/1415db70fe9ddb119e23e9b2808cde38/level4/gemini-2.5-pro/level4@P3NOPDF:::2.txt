This paper proposes a modification of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm tailored for high-dimensional settings where the true underlying parameter is sparse. The authors provide a theoretical analysis, establishing conditions for the algorithm's convergence to the global optimum and deriving its rate of convergence. Additionally, they introduce a statistical testing framework to assess the sparsity of the estimated parameter.

The paper is well-organized and presents a rigorous theoretical treatment of a challenging and important problem. The authors provide a sufficient summary of related work, effectively situating their contribution within the existing literature. The theoretical analysis, including the convergence guarantees and the derivation of the convergence rate, appears to be a solid contribution.

However, the main weakness of the paper is its density. The presentation, particularly in Sections 3 and 4, is highly technical and difficult to follow. While the results are valuable, the exposition could be significantly improved with more intuition and simplification to make the work more accessible to the broader machine learning community.

Below are several specific points that require clarification:

1.  **Justification for Minimax Optimality:** The authors state (line 066) that the derived convergence rate of sqrt(s*log(d/n)) is minimax-optimal. This is a crucial claim that motivates the work, but a proof or clear citation for this result is not provided in the main manuscript. To strengthen the paper, the authors should either include a brief sketch of the proof or cite the specific theorem from the literature that establishes this lower bound.

2.  **Practical Parameter Selection and Initialization:** The theoretical guarantees rely on conditions that may be difficult to meet in practice.
    *   In Equation (3.8), the parameter `^s` is defined in terms of the true sparsity level `s`. In most real-world applications, `s` is unknown. The paper would be more impactful if it included guidance on how to choose `^s` in a data-driven way (e.g., via cross-validation or another model selection criterion).
    *   The convergence analysis requires an initial parameter `beta^init` that is already reasonably close to the true parameter `beta*`, specifically satisfying ||beta^init - beta*||_2 < R/2 (line 275). It is not obvious how to find such an initialization in practice. The authors should suggest a practical procedure for obtaining a suitable initial value.

3.  **Importance of the Truncation Step:** The motivation for the truncation step in Algorithm 1 is unclear. A detailed explanation of why this step is necessary would be highly beneficial. For example, what are the theoretical or empirical consequences of omitting it? Is it essential for ensuring convergence, achieving the desired statistical rate, or managing numerical stability?

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment:**

The authors provided a satisfactory response that addressed many of the concerns raised above. Their clarifications have strengthened the paper and improved my confidence in its contributions.

This is a solid statistical paper with strong theoretical results. However, it remains a difficult read for researchers without a deep background in high-dimensional statistics. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the practical utility of the proposed method is somewhat underexplored. For the final version, I strongly encourage the authors to incorporate the clarifications from their rebuttal into the main text to improve its accessibility. A discussion on the method's limitations and practical implementation challenges would also be a welcome addition.
