

This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure through a truncation step, providing the first theoretically grounded framework for optimal parameter estimation and asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors extend prior work on EM algorithm convergence (e.g., [2, 30]) to high-dimensional settings where traditional guarantees fail due to non-identifiability without sparsity assumptions. Their key contributions are: (1) a high-dimensional EM algorithm with geometric convergence to a near-optimal estimator achieving the statistical rate of O(√(s* log d/n)), and (2) a decorrelated score test for hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components that attains semiparametric efficiency. The framework is validated on Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models.

**Strengths:**
- **Quality:** The theoretical guarantees are comprehensive and rigorous. The paper establishes both computational (geometric convergence rate) and statistical (near-minimax optimal rate) guarantees under well-defined conditions. The decorrelated score test achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound, representing a significant theoretical advance.
- **Originality:** This is the first framework to simultaneously address optimal estimation and asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models using the EM algorithm. The truncation step (Algorithm 1, line 6) is a novel technical contribution that enables sparsity handling, distinguishing this work from prior EM convergence analyses in low dimensions [2, 30].
- **Significance:** The framework provides a computationally feasible approach to high-dimensional inference for latent variable models, which has been a longstanding challenge. This addresses an important gap in the literature, as existing tensor methods [1, 9] achieve suboptimal rates in high dimensions.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally dense, particularly in Sections 3 and 4. The technical arguments would benefit from more intuitive explanations and simplified presentation. The proof of minimax-optimality for the statistical rate (O(√(s* log d/n))) is not clearly articulated in the main text (the supplementary material appears necessary for verification).
- **Practical Considerations:** The paper lacks concrete guidance for practitioners. The sparsity parameter ^s (Equation 3.8) depends on the unknown true sparsity level s*, making its practical selection unclear. Similarly, the requirement for an initialization satisfying ||β^init - β*||_2 ≤ R/2 (line 275) is theoretically justified but lacks practical implementation details.
- **Empirical Validation:** While theoretically sound, the paper provides minimal empirical evidence to demonstrate the method's practical utility. The significance of the approach for real-world applications remains questionable without concrete case studies or comparisons to existing high-dimensional inference methods.

**Key Questions:**
1. The minimax-optimality of the statistical rate O(√(s* log d/n)) is claimed but not clearly proven in the main text. The proof appears to be relegated to supplementary materials, which is problematic for a high-impact venue.
2. The choice of ^s in practice is unclear since it depends on the unknown sparsity s*. The authors should provide guidance on selecting ^s in real applications.
3. The truncation step (Algorithm 1, line 6) is critical for enforcing sparsity but its necessity is not sufficiently explained. What happens to the convergence guarantees if this step is omitted?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Theoretical contribution is strong, novel, and addresses a significant gap in high-dimensional inference for latent variable models.
- The framework is general and applicable to multiple statistical models (Gaussian mixtures, mixture of regressions).
- The decorrelated score test achieves semiparametric efficiency, which is a major theoretical advance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The manuscript is excessively dense and difficult to follow, particularly for readers without deep statistical background.
- Practical implementation details are lacking, making it challenging to assess real-world applicability.
- Limited empirical validation reduces confidence in the method's practical utility.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a solid statistical contribution that makes important theoretical advances in high-dimensional inference for latent variable models. The paper is well-motivated and the theoretical framework is sound. However, the extreme density of the presentation significantly hinders readability, and the lack of practical guidance and empirical validation reduces its impact for the NIPS audience. While the theoretical results are compelling, the paper would benefit from substantial clarification and simplification of the technical arguments. The significance for real-world applications remains somewhat questionable without empirical demonstrations.

Given the strong theoretical contribution but significant clarity and practicality concerns, I recommend acceptance with a score of 7 (strong accept), acknowledging it as a valuable statistical contribution that would benefit from clearer presentation. The authors have addressed the key questions in their rebuttal, particularly regarding the minimax-optimality proof and practical implementation considerations, which strengthened my assessment. However, the paper's density remains a barrier to broader accessibility for the NIPS community.
