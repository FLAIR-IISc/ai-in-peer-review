Summary
The paper studies expectation–maximization (EM) in high-dimensional settings, establishes nonasymptotic convergence guarantees, and develops an inference procedure. The work aims to extend existing EM analyses to p ≫ n regimes and to adapt decorrelated-score-based inference to latent-variable models estimated via EM.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: theoretical guarantees and valid inference for high-dimensional EM.
- Provides general conditions under which EM achieves statistical and algorithmic guarantees, with applications to latent-variable models.
- Attempts to combine convergence analysis with inference procedures in a unified framework.

Main weaknesses and questions
1) Initialization: The results rely critically on an initial estimator β_init that lies within a basin of attraction (see line 60, p.2). Please specify:
- Concrete conditions ensuring existence of such β_init (e.g., signal strength, sample size, restricted curvature).
- Practical initialization strategies that provably meet these conditions in your settings, such as spectral/method-of-moments initializers, convex relaxations, or Lasso-type estimators based on imputed or partial data.
- Whether multiple random starts plus validation likelihood can reliably find an admissible initializer, and how many starts are needed at your target dimensions.

2) Two algorithms: You propose two algorithms but offer limited guidance on which to use. Please add:
- A comparison of per-iteration computational complexity, memory footprint, and typical number of iterations.
- Conditions favoring each method (e.g., when conditional expectations are expensive/cheap, when missingness is heavy, or when sparsity is extreme).
- An empirical comparison on representative models highlighting accuracy, runtime, and stability across initializations.

3) Tuning parameters ŝ and λ:
- Provide principled, data-driven choices. For λ, theory often suggests c·σ√(log p/n) with an estimator of σ; discuss feasible σ estimators in latent-variable settings. For ŝ (sparsity/truncation level), discuss cross-validation, information criteria, stability selection, or path-based heuristics with stopping rules.
- Quantify sensitivity of estimation, Type I error, and power to mis-specification of ŝ and λ. If the inference relies on orthogonality, explain to what extent incorrect tuning degrades coverage and FWER.
- If possible, provide default settings with theoretical justification and practical calibration guidance.

4) Inference and relation to prior work:
- The decorrelated (orthogonal) score test appears identical to Ning and Liu, “A General Theory of Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Regions for Sparse High-Dimensional Models.” Please cite this work clearly and delineate what is new here.
- Specify the nontrivial steps required to apply their framework in your context. For example: verifying Neyman orthogonality for the latent-variable likelihood, establishing the l1-rate for the high-dimensional nuisance estimator produced by EM under nonconvexity, controlling error in the plug-in precision/score approximation generated by the E-step, or handling dependence introduced by pseudo-complete data.
- Clarify whether any novel variance estimation, sample-splitting, or cross-fitting strategies are required (or avoided) and why.

5) Assumptions and scope:
- The main theorems rely on many technical conditions (e.g., local strong concavity/restricted curvature, smoothness/Lipschitz continuity of the population Q-function, gradient stability, contraction of the EM operator, sub-Gaussian tails, and eigenvalue bounds). Please discuss, for each targeted model class, whether these assumptions hold and under what parameter ranges. Concrete checks for canonical examples (mixtures of Gaussians, mixtures of regressions, missing covariates) would be valuable.
- Where possible, summarize the implied sample complexity, signal-to-noise thresholds, and scaling in (n, p, s) for these models, and whether the conditions are near-optimal compared to known lower/upper bounds.

6) Exposition and the role of the supplement:
- The submission reads as a brief “trailer” for an extensive supplement. The main paper should contain more intuition and technical highlights:
  - Clear statements of key assumptions and their interpretations.
  - High-level proof sketches explaining how EM’s contraction, statistical error, and inference steps interface.
  - A concise roadmap of what is genuinely new versus imported from prior work.

Additional suggestions
- Provide a theorem or proposition giving sufficient conditions for successful initialization and a concrete initializer achieving them, with probability bounds.
- Add ablations illustrating the effect of tuning parameters on estimation error and on test size/power, and a sensitivity analysis to model misspecification.
- Report empirical results comparing the two algorithms across initializations and tuning choices, with wall-clock time and iteration counts.
- Discuss failure modes: what happens outside the basin of attraction, under weak signals, or with heavy-tailed noise? Are there safeguards (e.g., gradient clipping, robust E-steps)?
- Clarify notational conventions early (define ŝ and λ upon first use) and fix minor typos (e.g., “lambda,” not “lamdba”).
- Ensure complete and consistent citations to Ning and Liu and any other closely related EM and de-biasing literature.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important and challenging problem and appears to assemble convergence and inference guarantees for high-dimensional EM under a general framework. However, the current version needs clearer articulation of novelty relative to established de-biasing methods, concrete guidance on initialization and tuning, and a more transparent verification of assumptions for the intended model classes. Strengthening these aspects in the main text (not only the supplement) would significantly improve clarity, practical relevance, and perceived contribution.
