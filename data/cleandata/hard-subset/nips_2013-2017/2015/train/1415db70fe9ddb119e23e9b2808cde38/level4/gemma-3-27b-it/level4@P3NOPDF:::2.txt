## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission – [Paper ID]

This paper proposes a modified Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm tailored for estimating sparse, high-dimensional parameters. The authors establish convergence conditions to a global solution and provide a rate of convergence analysis. A novel statistical testing framework is also presented to assess the sparsity of the resulting estimator.

The paper is well-structured and includes a reasonably comprehensive overview of related work. However, the presentation is quite dense, and sections 3 and 4, in particular, require significant effort to follow. Increased simplification and further explanation of key concepts would substantially improve readability.

I have several specific questions and concerns. First, the authors claim in line 66 that $\sqrt{s \log(d/n)}$ is minimax-optimal. While this is a strong claim, the manuscript does not contain a proof or citation supporting this assertion. Providing this justification, either within the main text or in supplementary material, is crucial. 

Second, Equation (3.8) introduces $\hat{s}$, an estimate of the sparsity level *s*, which itself depends on *s*. This presents a practical challenge, as *s* is typically unknown in real-world applications. The paper needs to clearly articulate how $\hat{s}$ should be selected in practice. Furthermore, initializing the algorithm requires finding a value $\beta^{init}$ satisfying $||\beta^{init} - \beta^*||_2 < R/2$ (line 275). A discussion of practical methods for obtaining such an initial value would be valuable.

Finally, the purpose of the truncation step within Algorithm 1 remains unclear. The authors should explain the consequences of omitting this step and how it impacts the performance or convergence of the EM algorithm. What issues arise without truncation?

**After Rebuttal:**

The authors’ response to my concerns was satisfactory and addressed my key questions. As a result, I have increased my score from 6 to 7. This is a solid statistical contribution. However, it remains challenging to read for a machine learning researcher less familiar with the underlying statistical theory. Furthermore, the practical utility of the proposed method for real-world machine learning applications remains somewhat questionable, potentially due to the sensitivity to initialization and the difficulty in accurately estimating the sparsity level.
