

This paper addresses EM estimation in high-dimensional settings, establishing convergence guarantees and developing a novel inference procedure. The technical contributions are significant, particularly the adaptation of decorrelated score tests to latent variable models. The approach cleverly combines existing tools from high-dimensional inference, notably extending the framework of Ning and Liu (2017) to this specific context. However, several critical clarifications and enhancements are required before the paper can be accepted.

The primary theoretical results rely on multiple technical conditions (e.g., Assumptions 1-4). While the authors state these are satisfied for certain latent variable models, they provide no concrete discussion or verification for the specific models under consideration. The paper must explicitly demonstrate how these assumptions hold for the target applications (e.g., Gaussian mixture models, factor analysis), or alternatively, clarify the precise class of models where the theory applies. Without this, the practical relevance of the theoretical claims remains ambiguous.

A central methodological concern is the requirement for an initial solution $\beta^{\rm init}$ satisfying the condition in Line 60 (Page 2). The paper does not address *how* to obtain such an initial solution in practice. For high-dimensional EM, initialization is often non-trivial (e.g., requiring careful screening or regularization). The authors should discuss feasible strategies for finding $\beta^{\rm init}$ that meet this criterion, or clarify whether the condition is restrictive for common models.

The paper proposes two algorithms (EM and a modified variant). The manuscript lacks practical guidance on *when to use which*. A clear comparison—e.g., computational efficiency, robustness to model misspecification, or performance under different sparsity regimes—would significantly strengthen the contribution. Without this, readers cannot determine the appropriate algorithm for their application.

The choice of tuning parameters $\hat{s}$ (sparsity level) and $\lambda$ (regularization strength) is critical for high-dimensional inference. The paper does not address:
1.  How to select $\hat{s}$ and $\lambda$ in practice (e.g., cross-validation, data splitting),
2.  The sensitivity of inference results to their choice (e.g., coverage probability of confidence intervals),
3.  Whether data-dependent selection methods (e.g., adaptive tuning) are feasible or recommended.

Given that tuning parameter choice is paramount for valid inference in high-dimensional settings (as emphasized in Ning and Liu, 2017), this omission is a major gap. The authors should provide concrete recommendations or empirical guidance on this point.

The decorrelated score test used in Section 4.2 is identical to the one developed in Ning and Liu (2017). This must be explicitly acknowledged and cited. The paper states "the decorrelated score test is the same as in [*]", but the reference [*] is incomplete and the attribution is insufficient. The authors should clearly cite Ning and Liu (2017) and explicitly state how the current application differs from their prior work. Crucially, the paper must clarify *why* applying Ning and Liu's framework to this latent variable context requires non-trivial adaptation. Is the decorrelation step modified? Are the technical challenges specific to the EM setting (e.g., dependence in EM updates, latent variable structure)? The current text treats the inference procedure as a straightforward application, which understates the contribution. The novelty must be precisely articulated: what specific technical hurdles arose in adapting the Ning-Liu machinery to EM with latent variables, and how were they overcome?

The paper currently reads as a "trailer" for the supplementary material. While the supplement may contain necessary details, the main paper should include sufficient technical justification for the key steps (e.g., the derivation of the decorrelated score, the validation of assumptions for specific models, the algorithmic details for initialization). The main text must stand alone in conveying the core technical contributions and their justification, rather than deferring all details to the supplement. For instance, the argument for why Assumptions 1-4 hold for the target models should be briefly sketched in the main paper, with full details in the supplement.

In summary, the paper makes a valuable contribution by extending high-dimensional inference tools to EM settings. However, the current version lacks critical practical guidance (initialization, tuning parameters, algorithm choice), fails to properly contextualize the inference procedure relative to Ning and Liu (2017), and does not sufficiently justify the theoretical assumptions or demonstrate their applicability to concrete models. Addressing these points—particularly the explicit citation and justification for the inference procedure, the practical tuning guidance, and the demonstration of assumption validity—would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and clarity.

The core idea is promising, but the presentation requires substantial refinement to meet NIPS standards for technical depth and clarity. The authors should revise to clearly delineate the novel technical challenges, provide actionable practical advice, and rigorously anchor their assumptions to the models they study.
