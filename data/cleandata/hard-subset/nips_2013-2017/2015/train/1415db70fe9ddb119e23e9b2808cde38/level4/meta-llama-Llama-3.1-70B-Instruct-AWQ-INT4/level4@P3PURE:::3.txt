This paper proposes a sparse EM procedure that modifies the classical EM algorithm by truncating the parameter vector at each step to a desired sparsity level. Additionally, an asymptotically normal statistic is introduced, based on a decorrelated score, to test whether a specific entry of the parameter vector is non-zero.

The authors provide a guarantee for the sparse EM, stating that the estimate converges geometrically to the true value in the number of iterations, up to a statistical error term. This error term is proportional to the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step, which is typically only logarithmic in the ambient dimension. This result builds upon recent similar guarantees for EM in low-dimensional settings.

The analysis relies on the assumption that the Q function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic within a certain basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized within this basin. A new condition is introduced, requiring control over the infinity norm of the error in estimating M. Notably, this condition is only necessary for sparse vectors. However, for many models, estimating M at all possible vectors is as easy as estimating it at sparse vectors (as seen in the Gaussian mixture example and the non-gradient version of the mixture of regression model). This raises the question of whether the truncated EM algorithm is necessary. Could a similar guarantee be obtained by performing ordinary EM under the same conditions, followed by a single truncation step? The authors' observation that linear regression is a special case here provides a convincing argument against a single truncation step.

Some related work in sparse optimization, such as "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" by Langford et al (2009), may be relevant to this discussion and could be explored further.

The experimental section would benefit from a clear statement on the initialization method used. The proposed sparse EM variant for parameter estimation in high-dimensional settings is an important contribution, and the convergence guarantees provided are a significant result for high-dimensional learning. However, the importance of the sparse variant of EM to the obtained results is not entirely clear.
