A sparse EM procedure is proposed, modifying the classical EM algorithm by truncating the parameter vector at each step to a desired sparsity level. Additionally, an asymptotically normal statistic based on a decorrelated score is proposed for testing whether a specific entry of the parameter vector is non-zero.

The sparse EM algorithm comes with a convergence guarantee, stating that the estimate converges geometrically to the true value (in the number of iterations), up to a statistical error term. This error term is proportional to the square root of the sparsity level and the infinity norm of the statistical error in the M-step, which is typically only logarithmic in the ambient dimension. This result builds upon recent similar findings for EM in the low-dimensional setting.

Similar to previous work, the analysis assumes that the Q function is sufficiently smooth and nearly quadratic in a certain basin of attraction, and that the algorithm is initialized within the same basin. A new condition is introduced, requiring control over the infinity norm of the error in estimating M. Notably, this condition is only necessary for sparse vectors. However, for many models, estimating M at all possible vectors is just as easy as at sparse vectors (as seen in the Gaussian mixture example and the non-gradient version of the mixture of regression model). This raises the question of whether the truncated EM algorithm is truly necessary. Could a similar guarantee be obtained, for instance, by performing ordinary EM under the same conditions, followed by a single truncation step? The authors' observation that linear regression is a special case here seems to be a convincing argument against a single truncation step.

Some related work in sparse optimization, such as "Sparse online learning via truncated gradient" by Langford et al (2009), may be relevant and worth discussing.

The experimental section should specify the initialization method used. A sparse EM variant is proposed for parameter estimation in high-dimensional settings. The convergence guarantees provided are an important result for high-dimensional learning, although the importance of the sparse variant of EM to the obtained results is not entirely clear.
