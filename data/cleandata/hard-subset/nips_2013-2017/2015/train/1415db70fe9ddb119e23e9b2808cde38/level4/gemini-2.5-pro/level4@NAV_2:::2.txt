This paper presents a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings where the parameter dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The primary focus is on sparse latent variable models. The authors make two main contributions: First, they propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation step in each iteration to enforce sparsity. Under certain conditions, including a sufficiently accurate initialization, they prove that this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a (near-)minimax optimal statistical rate of convergence. Second, building on this estimator, they develop a decorrelated score test for performing hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. This work extends the recent analysis of the EM algorithm in low-dimensional settings (e.g., [2]) to the more challenging high-dimensional regime, and it provides a novel inferential framework for estimators derived from non-convex optimization, distinct from existing work on debiasing estimators from convex programs like the Lasso.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a critical and challenging problem at the intersection of latent variable models, high-dimensional statistics, and non-convex optimization. Providing the first computationally feasible approach with theoretical guarantees for both optimal parameter estimation and valid asymptotic inference for the EM algorithm in high dimensions is a significant contribution to the field.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and rigorous. The authors establish a unified framework that simultaneously provides guarantees on the computational rate of convergence (geometric) and the statistical rate of the final estimator (near-optimal), along with the asymptotic normality of their proposed test statistic.
*   **Clarity of Organization:** The paper is well-structured. The introduction effectively situates the work within the existing literature, clearly highlighting the limitations of prior work and the novelty of the paper's contributions.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Clarity and Accessibility:** While well-organized, the technical exposition in Sections 3 and 4 is extremely dense and relies on a series of abstract conditions. This makes the core arguments difficult to follow for a broad audience. While the general framework is powerful, providing more intuition in the main text for why these conditions are necessary and how they are met in the key examples would significantly improve the paper's readability and impact.

*   **Practical Considerations:** The theoretical guarantees hinge on several assumptions whose practical feasibility is not fully discussed.
    1.  **Initialization:** The analysis requires an initial estimator `init` to be within a specified radius of the true parameter `*`. How can such an initial point be found in practice? This is a well-known and often difficult problem for non-convex methods, and some discussion of practical strategies or references to relevant techniques would strengthen the paper.
    2.  **Choice of Tuning Parameters:** The theoretical results for the truncation step require setting a sparsity parameter `bs` that depends on the true, unknown sparsity level `s*` (Equation 3.8). How should this parameter be chosen in a real-world application where `s*` is unknown? Guidance on data-driven selection of `bs` would be valuable.

*   **Justification of the Truncation Step:** The introduction of the T-step is the key algorithmic modification. It would be helpful to explicitly discuss what would happen if this step were omitted. For instance, if one were to use a standard `l1`-penalized M-step, would the analysis still hold, or does the hard-thresholding nature of the T-step provide a specific technical advantage that is crucial for the proofs? Clarifying this would better motivate the specific design of Algorithm 1.

*   **Unsupported Claims in Main Text:** The abstract and introduction state that the `sqrt(s* log d / n)` statistical rate is (near-)minimax-optimal. While this is a standard result in high-dimensional statistics, for the sake of a self-contained argument, a citation to the corresponding lower-bound proof for the specific class of models considered should be included in the main text.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper with significant contributions to the understanding of the EM algorithm in modern, high-dimensional settings. Addressing the questions regarding practical implementation and improving the clarity of the technical sections would further enhance its quality and impact.
