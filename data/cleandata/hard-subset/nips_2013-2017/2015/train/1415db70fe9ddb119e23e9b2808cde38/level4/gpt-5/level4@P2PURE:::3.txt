The paper proposes a sparse EM algorithm that modifies classical EM by truncating the parameter vector at each iteration to a target sparsity level. It also introduces an asymptotically normal test statistic based on a decorrelated score to test whether a particular coordinate of the parameter vector is nonzero.

The theoretical guarantee for the sparse EM shows geometric convergence (in the number of iterations) to the true parameter, up to a statistical error of order sqrt(s) times the infinity norm of the M-step statistical error, which typically depends only logarithmically on the ambient dimension.

These results build on recent analogous guarantees for EM in low-dimensional settings. As in prior work, the analysis assumes that the Q-function is sufficiently smooth and approximately quadratic within a certain basin of attraction, and that initialization lies within this basin. The new ingredient is an infinity-norm control on the error in estimating the M-step operator. Although this condition is only required for sparse vectors, in many models estimating the M-step for arbitrary vectors is no harder than for sparse ones (as in the Gaussian mixture example provided, and in the non-gradient version of the mixture of regressions). This raises a natural question: is the truncated EM algorithm necessary? Could one obtain a similar guarantee by running standard EM under the same conditions and then applying a single truncation step? EDIT: The authors’ observation that linear regression is a special case here provides a convincing argument against relying on a single truncation step.

There appears to be related work in sparse optimization that merits discussion, e.g., “Sparse online learning via truncated gradient” (Langford et al., 2009).

The experimental section should clearly specify the initialization used.

Overall, the paper presents a sparse EM variant for high-dimensional parameter estimation. The convergence guarantees are an important contribution to high-dimensional learning, though it remains uncertain how essential the sparse EM variant is to achieving these results.
