Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a high-dimensional counterpart of the EM algorithm for latent-variable models. The core idea is to augment each EM iteration with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step that retains the bs largest-magnitude coordinates, enforcing sparsity along the path. The authors analyze both exact M-step and gradient (approximate) M-step variants. Under local curvature and Lipschitz conditions for the population Q-function and an initialization within a basin of attraction, they show geometric contraction of the optimization error and a final estimation error that matches the near-minimax rate O(√(s* log d / n), up to log factors). Beyond estimation, they construct a decorrelated score (and Wald) test for a low-dimensional component of the parameter, using a Dantzig-selector estimate of the orthogonalizing vector, and show asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency. The framework is illustrated on Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, with explicit verifications of the conditions (e.g., SNR conditions for GMM).

This builds on Balakrishnan et al. (2014) who analyzed EM in the low-dimensional regime, and on high-dimensional inference via debiasing/desparsification (e.g., van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang, Javanmard & Montanari), extending such ideas to nonconvex latent-variable likelihoods and EM-based estimators. It is also related to penalized EM for mixture models (e.g., Städler et al., Khalili & Chen) and to hard-thresholding / IHT methods for sparsity. Compared to tensor methods for mixtures (Anandkumar et al.), the statistical rates claimed here are sharper in both low- and high-dimensional regimes.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A simple, implementable modification (T-step) that enforces sparsity within EM and yields optimal estimation rates, with clear decomposition into optimization and statistical errors.
- Theoretical innovation: Entrywise (ℓ1) control of the sample/population M-step discrepancy tailored to analyze the effect of truncation. The analysis covers both exact and gradient M-steps and provides geometric contraction.
- Inference: The decorrelated score for nonconvex latent-variable models is novel and important; asymptotic normality with efficient variance is established under explicit conditions, bridging the gap between computation and inference.
- Generality: The framework is stated abstractly and then specialized to GMM and mixtures of regressions, with verifications of Lipschitz/curvature/statistical error conditions.
- Clarity of the optimization–statistics tradeoff: The proofs delineate the basin-of-attraction role, the tuning of bs, and the eventual domination by the statistical term.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Initialization: The guarantees require an initial point within a relatively small basin (in ℓ2 relative error). Practical procedures to obtain such initializations in high dimensions are not detailed beyond referencing prior low-dimensional work. Please provide explicit, computationally feasible initializers (e.g., spectral/tensor, method-of-moments, or sample splitting + thresholding) and theory or empirical evidence of their success under your high-dimensional conditions.
- Knowledge of sparsity and tuning: The algorithm critically relies on bs ≍ s*. In practice s* is unknown. How sensitive are guarantees and empirical performance to mis-specification? Can you propose a data-driven rule (cross-validation, BIC-type criteria, stability selection) or a gradually decreasing thresholding schedule? A theorem quantifying the impact of over/under-selecting bs would strengthen the contribution.
- Resampling analysis vs. practical algorithm: The main estimation theorem analyzes a resampling/split-sample EM, which is rarely used in practice due to sample inefficiency. While common as a proof device, it would be valuable to either (i) provide theory for the standard single-sample iterate (e.g., via empirical process coupling across iterations), or (ii) include experiments showing that the single-sample algorithm behaves as predicted.
- Assumptions’ scope: For GMM, the curvature/Lipschitz constants depend on a strong SNR (kθ*||/σ ≥ r). Please discuss how tight this is, whether your conditions allow overlap of components, and how performance degrades as SNR decreases. Similarly, the inference theory assumes sparsity of w* (the orthogonalization vector). This is standard, but its interpretability for mixtures deserves discussion; give examples where s_w* is small vs. large.
- Computational aspects of inference: Solving the Dantzig program with Tn(·) involves d×d matrices per test and depends on the current estimate. For large d this can be heavy. Please comment on scalable solvers, approximate variants (nodewise regression), and the effect on coverage/power.
- Comparison to penalized EM/soft-thresholding: The truncation-based “hard” thresholding is natural but related to projected gradient/IHT literature. A clearer comparison—both theoretically and empirically—to ℓ1-penalized EM or proximal-gradient EM (Städler et al., 2010) would help assess benefits/drawbacks (bias, support recovery, stability).
- Empirical evaluation: The paper would benefit from simulations on GMM and mixtures of regressions comparing (i) classical EM + post-thresholding, (ii) ℓ1-penalized EM/gradient EM, (iii) the proposed truncated EM; include sensitivity to initialization, misspecified bs, and assess Type I error and power of the decorrelated tests.

Quality
The technical development appears sound and carefully structured, with conditions stated cleanly and verified for example models. The decomposition of error and the inference theory are compelling. However, some proofs are deferred and rely on resampling; a brief proof sketch in the main text would improve accessibility. Typos and incomplete references should be corrected.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well-organized. Notation is heavy; a table of symbols and a running example in the main text would aid readability. Provide more intuition for Conditions 3.1–3.3 and for the construction of Tn(·). The reference list has many formatting errors and missing details.

Originality
High. The combination of truncated EM with sharp high-dimensional rates and semiparametric-efficient inference for latent-variable models is, to my knowledge, new.

Significance
High. Many latent-variable models are ubiquitous in ML; providing both estimation and valid inference in high dimensions is impactful and likely to influence subsequent work.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, general framework bridging computation and inference in high-dimensional latent-variable models.
- Near-optimal estimation rates with geometric convergence.
- First asymptotically valid, efficient tests for low-dimensional components in this setting.
- Clear specialization to important models (GMM, mixed regression).

Arguments against acceptance
- Dependence on strong initialization and knowledge of sparsity; limited guidance for practice.
- Theory for resampling rather than standard EM; limited empirical validation in the current version.
- Strong SNR and sparsity-of-w* assumptions may limit applicability.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a theoretically justified initializer under your high-dimensional assumptions?
- How robust is the method to over/under-specifying bs? Any adaptive selection strategy?
- Do your results extend to multi-component mixtures (K>2) and to missing-data models beyond mixtures?
- Can you comment on extending inference to multiple coordinates with FDR control?

Overall: I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying initialization and adding empirical validation.
