**Summary**

This paper presents a unified theoretical framework for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models using a novel variant of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors identify a critical gap in the literature: while the EM algorithm is a workhorse for fitting latent variable models, its theoretical guarantees are largely confined to the low-dimensional setting (d << n). To address this, the paper makes two primary contributions. First, it proposes a "High Dimensional EM algorithm" that augments the standard E- and M-steps with a truncation (T-step) to enforce sparsity at each iteration. The authors prove that, when started with a sufficiently good initialization, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to a solution that achieves a (near-)minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d) / n)`. Second, the paper develops a procedure for statistical inference on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. This is achieved via a "decorrelated score test," which adapts recent ideas from the high-dimensional inference literature (e.g., debiased Lasso) to the challenging non-convex setting of EM. The authors establish the asymptotic normality of their proposed test statistic, demonstrating that it achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. This work significantly extends the analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2014) to the high-dimensional sparse regime and provides the first, to my knowledge, computationally feasible approach with guarantees for both optimal estimation and valid inference for a broad class of high-dimensional latent variable models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a problem of fundamental importance. The EM algorithm is ubiquitous, yet its behavior in the modern high-dimensional setting has remained poorly understood. By providing a framework that guarantees both optimal estimation and valid uncertainty quantification (hypothesis testing), this work represents a major theoretical advance. These results are likely to be highly influential for both theorists and practitioners working with complex, high-dimensional latent variable models.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The contributions are highly original. The proposed algorithm, combining EM with iterative hard thresholding, is a natural but non-trivial extension to the high-dimensional setting. The theoretical analysis required to handle this modification, particularly the shift from `l_2`-norm to `l_infinity`-norm error analysis to control the effect of truncation, is a key technical innovation. Furthermore, the development of a valid inferential procedure for the local optimum found by an iterative algorithm (which is not the solution to a convex program) is a novel and powerful idea that extends the scope of high-dimensional inference methods.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The analysis is rigorous and is built upon a clear set of regularity conditions. The decomposition of the estimation error into an optimization component (which decays geometrically) and a statistical component (which determines the final accuracy) is elegant and insightful (Theorem 3.4). The connection established in Theorem 2.1 between the gradient of the Q-function and the true score function is a crucial link that allows the authors to build their inference theory on solid ground.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, presents the methodology and main results concisely, and relegates the more technical proofs to the appendix. The use of general, abstract conditions (e.g., Condition 3.1-3.3) to first present the main theorem, followed by verification for specific models, makes the framework easy to understand and appreciate its generality.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The Initialization Requirement:** The entire theory hinges on the existence of an initial estimator that is already within a constant factor of the true parameter's `l_2`-norm. This is a very strong assumption and a common limitation in analyses of non-convex problems. The paper does not provide a practical, data-driven procedure for obtaining such an initial point. Without a method to find this starting point, the operational value of the theoretical guarantees is diminished. A more thorough discussion of this limitation, including potential strategies (even if they have suboptimal rates, like the tensor methods mentioned) or the conditions under which a simple starting point might suffice, would significantly strengthen the paper.

2.  **Choice of Tuning Parameters:** The practical implementation of the proposed algorithm and inference procedure depends on tuning parameters, namely the sparsity level `bs` and the Dantzig selector parameter `lambda`. The theoretical results provide guidance on their scaling (e.g., Eq. 3.8 and 4.5), but these depend on unknown quantities like the true sparsity `s*`. The paper lacks any discussion on how to select these parameters in practice (e.g., via cross-validation or other data-driven methods), which is a crucial step for real-world application.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is substantial, the claims of providing a "computationally feasible approach" would be much more compelling if supported by even a small set of simulation experiments. Such experiments could have been used to (a) verify that the predicted convergence rates and statistical error are observed in practice, and (b) assess the finite-sample performance of the decorrelated score test, such as its nominal size and power.

4.  **Assumptions for Inference:** The inference theory relies on the sparsity of the "projection" vector `w*` (Assumption 4.5 and the surrounding discussion). This is a strong, non-obvious assumption on the structure of the Fisher Information matrix. While standard in the high-dimensional inference literature, its implications in the context of latent variable models are not discussed. A brief, intuitive explanation of what this assumption means for, say, a Gaussian Mixture Model would be valuable for the reader.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a long-standing and important open problem: the theoretical behavior of EM in high dimensions.
*   Provides the first unified framework for both near-optimal estimation and valid asymptotic inference for a broad class of high-dimensional latent variable models.
*   The technical contributions, particularly the analysis of the truncated EM and the adaptation of debiasing methods to a non-convex algorithm's output, are novel and significant.
*   The paper is clear, well-structured, and presents a complete and rigorous theoretical story.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The strong initialization requirement is a major caveat that is not sufficiently addressed.
*   The complete lack of guidance on tuning parameter selection or any form of empirical validation limits the paper's immediate practical relevance.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a foundational contribution to the understanding of latent variable models in high dimensions. The strengths—in particular, the significance of the problem and the novelty and quality of the theoretical results—are substantial. While the weaknesses concerning initialization and practical implementation are not minor, they are common to this area of research and do not undermine the importance of the theoretical breakthrough. The paper is a clear candidate for acceptance at NIPS. I would strongly encourage the authors to incorporate a discussion of the practical limitations (initialization, tuning) in the final version to provide a more balanced perspective.
