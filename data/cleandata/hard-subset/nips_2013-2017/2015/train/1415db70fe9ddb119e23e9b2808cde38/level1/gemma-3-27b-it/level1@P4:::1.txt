This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models. The core idea is to introduce a truncation step into the EM algorithm to enforce sparsity, allowing for statistical rate convergence and asymptotic normality. The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant gap in the existing literature.

**Summary of Ideas and Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing EM theory, which primarily focuses on low-dimensional settings. They build upon recent work by [2], extending their geometric convergence results to the high-dimensional regime. The key novelty lies in the truncation step, which is crucial for achieving sparsity and enabling statistical analysis.  The paper acknowledges related work on high-dimensional estimation, including Lasso-type methods ([13, 26, 32]) and tensor methods ([1, 9]), and positions its contribution as providing a computationally feasible and statistically sound approach specifically tailored to latent variable models where global optimization is intractable. The connection to existing inference methods for sparse models is also appropriately noted.

**Strengths:**

* **Addressing a Significant Gap:** The paper tackles a crucial problem – providing theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high dimensions – where such guarantees are largely absent.
* **Novel Approach:** The truncation step is a clever idea to induce sparsity and facilitate analysis.
* **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, including convergence rates for parameter estimation and asymptotic normality for hypothesis testing.
* **General Framework:** The framework appears to be applicable to a broad family of latent variable models, as demonstrated with Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models.
* **Clear Presentation of Assumptions:** The assumptions are clearly laid out, and the connection to related work is well established.
* **Optimality Claim:** The authors correctly position their inference results in terms of semiparametric efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The mathematical presentation is dense and requires significant effort to follow. While thorough, the notation is heavy and could be streamlined for better readability.
* **Conditionality:** The results are conditional on several assumptions (Conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.5), which, while standard, might be difficult to verify in practice for complex models. The sensitivity of the results to these conditions is not fully discussed.
* **Constant Factors:** The constants involved in the convergence rates (e.g., in Theorem 3.4) are not fully characterized. This makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the theoretical results. The dependence on factors like log n and log d could be substantial.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical aspects. Practical aspects of the algorithm, such as tuning parameter selection (especially *bs*), and computational cost are not discussed in detail.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks empirical validation. While the focus is on theory, some simulations would strengthen the credibility of the results.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in statistical learning.
* The proposed method is novel and theoretically well-supported.
* The results are significant, potentially enabling the use of EM in high-dimensional settings with confidence.
* The paper is rigorous and provides a solid foundation for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The mathematical complexity might limit its accessibility and impact.
* The strong assumptions and lack of empirical validation raise concerns about practical applicability.
* The lack of detailed discussion regarding constant factors hinders assessment of practical relevance.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of the EM algorithm in high dimensions. While the complexity and lack of empirical validation are weaknesses, the novelty, rigor, and importance of the problem addressed outweigh these concerns.  I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors consider adding a discussion of practical implications and potentially including some preliminary simulations in a revised version. I would also suggest carefully considering simplifying the notation and presentation to enhance clarity.
