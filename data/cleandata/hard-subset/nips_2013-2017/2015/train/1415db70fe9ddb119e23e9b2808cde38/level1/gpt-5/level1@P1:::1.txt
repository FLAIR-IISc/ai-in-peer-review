Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops a general theory for EM in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors propose a “high-dimensional EM” that augments the classical E- and M-steps with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step to enforce sparsity along the iterates. They analyze two M-step implementations: exact maximization and a gradient ascent “generalized EM.” With an initialization in a basin of attraction, they establish geometric contraction of the optimization error and a near-minimax statistical error of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to a log n factor). Beyond estimation, they construct a decorrelated score test (via a Dantzig-type estimator of a nuisance-orthogonalization vector) yielding asymptotic normality with variance equal to the semiparametric efficiency bound for the low-dimensional target in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. They provide a unified set of conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity/smoothness, and sample-to-population M-step deviation in ℓ1) and verify them for Gaussian mixture and mixture-of-regressions models under suitable SNR and sparsity assumptions.

This work builds on and extends the “from population to sample” analysis of EM in the low-dimensional regime (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2014), bringing both computational and statistical guarantees to the high-dimensional setting. For inference, it adapts decorrelated/debiased techniques (e.g., Zhang & Zhang, van de Geer et al.) to nonconvex latent variable models where global optima are computationally out of reach. Compared to tensor/spectral methods for latent variable models, it attains sharper rates in high dimensions and provides inference.

Strengths:
- Clear algorithmic idea (EM + truncation) with a unified analysis that yields both geometric optimization convergence and near-optimal statistical rates in high dimensions.
- Handles both exact and gradient M-steps with explicit contraction factors tied to smoothness/strong concavity.
- Entrywise (ℓ1) control of sample-to-population M-step deviation enables sharp high-dimensional rates; careful treatment of how truncation affects support evolution.
- Asymptotic inference for low-dimensional components with nuisance-orthogonalization achieving the semiparametric efficiency bound; to my knowledge, first such result for EM-based estimators in high-dimensional latent models.
- Applicability demonstrated (at least theoretically) for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions with explicit conditions (e.g., SNR requirements).
- Conceptually bridges the computational-statistical gap for nonconvex high-dimensional latent variable models.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Initialization: Guarantees require starting within a basin of attraction. The paper does not provide concrete, general-purpose initialization procedures with provable proximity for the high-dimensional setting (beyond model-specific high SNR assumptions). Discussion of practical initializers (spectral/tensor starts, sample splitting, or method-of-moments) and their guarantees would strengthen the contribution.
- Resampling for analysis: The main rate theorem is stated for a resampling (sample-splitting across iterations) variant to control dependence. While standard in theory, a clear argument or empirical evidence that the same guarantees (or essentially the same) hold without resampling would be valuable.
- Tuning and practicality: The truncation level b_s must be of the same order as s*. How to choose it in practice (cross-validation, information criteria, stability) and sensitivity analysis are not discussed. Similarly, the Dantzig tuning λ for the decorrelation vector and the step size for the gradient M-step require guidance.
- Assumptions: The sparsity of the orthogonalization vector w* (s_w* = o(√(n/log d))) may be restrictive in some models; the paper should discuss when this is natural. The SNR/separation assumptions for mixtures (e.g., large r) may be strong; explicit tradeoffs between SNR and sample size would help.
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is notation-heavy; many key derivations and model-specific verifications are deferred to the appendix. The reference list in the provided version contains several incomplete or malformed entries; related literature on iterative hard thresholding (e.g., Blumensath & Davies) and recent debiasing (e.g., Javanmard & Montanari) should be cited and discussed. A more explicit comparison with ℓ1-penalized EM variants (penalized M-step without hard truncation) would help contextualize the truncation choice.
- Empirical validation: The submission would benefit from synthetic and real-data experiments showing estimation error rates, sensitivity to initialization and b_s, and coverage of confidence intervals, compared to tensor/spectral methods and penalized EM baselines.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The technical development is solid and well-motivated, with careful decomposition into optimization and statistical errors, and a principled inference construction attaining the semiparametric bound. Assumptions are reasonable for the intended models but could be discussed more thoroughly for generality and verifiability. The reliance on resampling for theory is a limitation.
- Clarity: Algorithm boxes help, but the paper is dense. Improving exposition around assumptions, initialization, and tuning would enhance accessibility. Fix the bibliography and notation typos.
- Originality: High. Integrating truncation into EM with sharp high-dimensional rates and providing decorrelated inference for latent variable models appears novel and impactful.
- Significance: High. Many practitioners use EM for latent models; providing the first computationally feasible path to optimal estimation and valid inference in high dimensions is important and likely to influence future work.

Pros for acceptance:
- First unified estimation + inference theory for high-dimensional latent variable EM.
- Near-optimal rates and semiparametric efficiency; geometric convergence guarantees.
- General framework covering exact and gradient EM, with verification for canonical models.

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical aspects (initialization, tuning) underdeveloped; lack of experiments.
- Analysis relies on resampling; extension to full-sample EM not fully explicit.
- Some assumptions (w* sparsity, SNR) may be restrictive; discussion could be richer.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide concrete, implementable initialization strategies with guidance/guarantees.
- Add empirical results comparing to tensor/spectral and penalized EM, including inference coverage.
- Discuss automatic selection of b_s and λ (e.g., cross-validation, stability, theory-driven heuristics).
- Clarify how the analysis extends to the no-resampling algorithm and whether the same rates hold.
- Broaden related work: iterative hard thresholding/hard-thresholded EM, debiasing (Javanmard & Montanari), and penalized EM approaches; fix and complete the references.
- Expand discussion of the sparsity of w* and when it holds in common latent models.

Overall: This is a strong theoretical paper addressing an important gap with novel, broadly applicable results. With improved clarity and empirical validation, it would make a valuable NIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
