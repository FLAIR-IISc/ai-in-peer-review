- Presents a general theoretical framework for EM in high-dimensional settings (d >> n) with two main contributions: a truncated (hard-thresholding) EM for sparse estimation and a decorrelated-score inference procedure.

- Proposes a novel high-dimensional EM that inserts a truncation step after E/M steps and proves geometric convergence to a near-minimax (sqrt(s*log d / n)) estimator under suitable initialization.

- Develops a decorrelated score statistic for inference on low-dimensional components, proving asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency.

- Extends prior low-dimensional EM analysis (Balakrishnan et al., 2014) to the high-dimensional, non-convex setting and adapts debiasing techniques from high-dimensional convex models.

- Demonstrates the framework on Gaussian mixture and mixture-of-regression models.

- Strength — Quality & Significance: First unified theoretical framework guaranteeing computational efficiency, statistical optimality, and valid inference for high-dimensional EM.

- Strength — Originality: The truncation-augmented EM and inference for outputs of a non-convex iterative algorithm are novel and non-trivial.

- Strength — Clarity: Well-written, modular presentation with clear conditions (Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness, Statistical-Error) and verification for examples.

- Weakness — Initialization requirement: Guarantees are local and require an initial estimator within constant-factor error, with no general practical method provided.

- Weakness — Resampling analysis: Main proofs assume a resampling variant (fresh data per iteration), not the single-dataset algorithm used in practice, with no quantification of the gap.

- Weakness — Complex inference assumptions: Assumption 4.5 is technical and requires sparsity of w* and intricate sample-size interplay, which may be hard to verify for new models.

- Weakness — Lack of empirical validation: No simulations or real-data experiments to demonstrate practical performance, tuning choices, or finite-sample accuracy.

- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses an important open problem, provides novel and substantial technical contributions, delivers strong theoretical guarantees likely to have major impact.

- Arguments against acceptance: Relies on strong initialization, analyzes a resampling algorithm rather than the practical one, and omits empirical validation.
