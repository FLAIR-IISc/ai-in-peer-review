Proposes a truncated EM algorithm that inserts a sparsity-enforcing T-step after the E- and M-steps, analyzed for both exact and gradient-ascent M-steps.

Develops a decorrelated score and Wald inferential procedure for low-dimensional components with high-dimensional nuisance parameters using a Dantzig-type construction.

Establishes geometric convergence under suitable initialization and model-specific regularity, achieving near-minimax ℓ2 error O(√(s* log d / n)).

Shows the score statistic is asymptotically normal and semiparametrically efficient.

Instantiates the framework for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Extends population-to-sample EM analyses and debiased GLM inference to nonconvex latent-variable settings, addressing gaps left by tensor methods and convex-penalized approaches.

Technical development is careful, with a clear separation of optimization and statistical errors and verification of conditions for exemplar models.

Assumptions are strong: requires a basin-of-attraction initialization, high SNR/separation for mixtures, sparsity of the efficient influence direction w*, and resampling per iteration.

Proofs rely on a resampling analysis (fresh data per iteration); practical single-sample EM guarantees are suggested but not fully proved.

Practical translation needs more guidance: tuning of truncation level b_s near s* and choice of the Dantzig parameter are under-specified, as are initialization and stopping rules.

Clarity is generally good but notation is heavy; the paper could streamline conditions and improve reference formatting.

Originality is high: the sparsity-truncated EM with rigorous rates plus decorrelated score inference for latent models is novel.

Significance is high: provides the first computationally feasible route to optimal estimation and asymptotically valid inference in high-dimensional latent-variable models.

Pros for acceptance: new algorithmic framework with geometric convergence and near-minimax rates; first semiparametrically efficient inference for high-dimensional latent EM; covers exact and gradient EM and two canonical models.

Cons for acceptance: reliance on resampling, strong assumptions (initialization, SNR, w* sparsity), and limited empirical validation and practical tuning guidance.
