

This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure through a truncation step, addressing a significant gap in the literature where existing theoretical guarantees for EM only hold in low-dimensional settings (d << n). The authors build upon foundational work by [2, 30] that established convergence properties for EM in low dimensions, extending it to high dimensions with rigorous statistical and computational guarantees. Their key contributions include a high-dimensional EM algorithm with geometric convergence rate to an estimator achieving near-optimal statistical rate (sqrt(s* log d / n)), and a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference of low-dimensional parameter components.

The paper demonstrates exceptional quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors carefully decompose the estimation error into optimization and statistical components, establishing both computational and statistical guarantees. The proof techniques are sound and address the significant challenges of high dimensionality, particularly in characterizing entrywise statistical error. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized presentation, clear algorithmic descriptions, and consistent notation. The originality is strong, as this represents the first framework for optimal estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models via EM, distinguishing it from previous work on low-dimensional EM [2] and tensor methods [1].

The significance is substantial, addressing a critical need in modern statistical applications where high-dimensional latent variable models are increasingly common. The framework applies broadly to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, establishing the first computationally feasible approach for optimal inference in high dimensions. The decorrelated score statistic achieves semiparametric efficiency, which is a notable theoretical advance.

Weaknesses include the restrictive sparsity assumption on w* (necessary for the decorrelation approach), and limited empirical validation beyond theoretical guarantees. The computational complexity of the truncation step could be a practical concern for very large d.

Pro: First unified framework for high-dimensional EM with both estimation and inference guarantees; rigorous theoretical contributions that advance the state of the art; well-motivated and technically sound; significant applicability to important statistical models.

Con: Some assumptions may limit practical applicability; limited empirical validation; computational complexity not thoroughly addressed.

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field with high technical quality, originality, and significance, warranting acceptance at NIPS.
