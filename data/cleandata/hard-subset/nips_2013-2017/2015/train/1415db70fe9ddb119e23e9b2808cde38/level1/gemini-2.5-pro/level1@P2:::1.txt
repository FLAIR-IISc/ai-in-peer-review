**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the significant gap in the theoretical understanding of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings where the number of parameters `d` exceeds the sample size `n`. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the standard EM procedure by adding a truncation (hard thresholding) step after the M-step. This enforces sparsity on the parameter vector at each iteration. Under the assumption of a good initialization, they prove this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of sqrt(s*log(d)/n). This is a substantial improvement over existing low-dimensional theory.

Second, building on this estimator, the paper develops a procedure for statistical inference. It introduces a "decorrelated score test" for testing hypotheses on individual components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. This is a non-trivial extension of recent ideas on "debiasing" or "desparsifying" estimators from convex regularized models (like the Lasso) to the non-convex setting of latent variable models, where the estimator is the output of an iterative algorithm. The authors prove that their proposed test statistic is asymptotically normal and that its variance achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. The general framework is demonstrated on high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixtures of regressions.

This work builds upon the recent analysis of the EM algorithm in low dimensions by Balakrishnan et al. (2014), who analyzed EM as a form of perturbed gradient method. However, the introduction of the truncation step and the high-dimensional setting necessitate a significantly different and more refined analysis based on entrywise error bounds. The inference framework is novel for EM and connects to the broader high-dimensional inference literature (e.g., Zhang & Zhang, 2014; van de Geer et al., 2014).

**Evaluation**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are strong and appear to be rigorously supported by the proofs provided in the appendix. The analysis is comprehensive, covering both computational convergence rates and statistical properties (estimation error and asymptotic normality for inference). The authors are careful to state their assumptions clearly (e.g., Conditions 3.1, 3.2, 3.3 for estimation, and 4.1-4.5 for inference). These assumptions, while technical, are standard for this line of work and are verified for the specific models considered. The decomposition of the estimation error into an optimization term that decays geometrically and a final statistical error term is elegant and insightful. The work represents a complete and substantial theoretical contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly situates the work within the literature, and precisely states the main contributions. The methodology is presented logically, first introducing the algorithm and then the inferential procedure. The main theorems are stated cleanly in the body of the paper, with the more model-specific and technical details deferred to the appendix, which is appropriate for a conference paper. The notation is dense, as is necessary for the topic, but it is consistent and well-defined. The authors have done an admirable job of making a very technical topic accessible to readers with a strong background in high-dimensional statistics and optimization.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it leverages recent advances in both the analysis of EM and high-dimensional inference, the synthesis is novel and significant.
1.  It is the first work to provide a general theoretical framework establishing geometric convergence and near-optimal statistical rates for an EM-type algorithm in the `d >> n` regime. The key innovation is the analysis of the truncation step within the EM iterations.
2.  More strikingly, it presents the first general, computationally feasible procedure for asymptotic inference (hypothesis testing and, by extension, confidence intervals) for parameters of high-dimensional latent variable models estimated via EM. Applying the "decorrelation" or "debiasing" idea to the output of a non-convex iterative algorithm, rather than the global optimum of a convex problem, is a major conceptual and technical leap.
The paper clearly distinguishes its contributions from prior art, providing a thorough comparison to related work.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is very high. The EM algorithm is one of the most widely used algorithms in statistics and machine learning, and its application to high-dimensional problems has largely outpaced its theoretical understanding. This paper provides a foundational theoretical basis for using EM in modern, high-dimensional settings.
*   **For researchers:** The analytical framework is likely to be highly influential and can serve as a blueprint for analyzing other high-dimensional latent variable models.
*   **For practitioners:** It provides a concrete, computationally tractable algorithm (Algorithm 1) that comes with performance guarantees. Crucially, the inference procedure allows practitioners to move beyond point estimation to quantify uncertainty (e.g., calculating p-values for feature significance), which is a critical need in many scientific applications.
This work fundamentally advances the state of the art by providing the first unified and computationally feasible approach for both optimal estimation and valid inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
*   **Fundamental Contribution:** Provides the first comprehensive theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm (both estimation and inference) in the high-dimensional setting.
*   **Technical Strength:** The results are strong (geometric convergence, near-optimal rates, asymptotic normality) and the analysis appears rigorous.
*   **High Impact:** Addresses a long-standing open problem for a workhorse algorithm. The results are of great importance to both theorists and practitioners in machine learning and statistics.
*   **Clarity and Originality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and presents a novel and powerful synthesis of ideas from optimization and high-dimensional statistics.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Cons / Points for Improvement):**
*   **Initialization:** The theory relies on an initialization that is already within a certain radius of the true parameter. While this is a common requirement for non-convex problems, the paper does not offer a general, provably effective method for finding such an initialization (e.g., via spectral methods), which remains a critical practical step.
*   **Technical Assumptions:** The results for inference rely on a sparsity assumption on `w*` (the representation of one part of the Fisher information in terms of another), which may not be easy to verify and may not hold for all models of interest.
*   **Tuning Parameters:** The practical choice of tuning parameters, such as the sparsity level `bs` in the algorithm and the regularization parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector, is not discussed in detail.

Despite these minor limitations, which are common in theoretical papers of this nature and open avenues for future work, the strengths of this paper are overwhelming. It is a landmark paper that solves a major theoretical problem with significant practical implications.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept.
