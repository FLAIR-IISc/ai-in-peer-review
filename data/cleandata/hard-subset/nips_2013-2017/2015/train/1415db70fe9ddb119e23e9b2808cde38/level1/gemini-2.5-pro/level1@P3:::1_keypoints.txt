- Proposes a High-Dimensional EM algorithm that adds a truncation step to enforce sparsity and extends EM to the d >> n regime.  
- Proves geometric convergence from a good initialization and achieves near-minimax estimation error rate sqrt(s* log d / n).  
- Develops a decorrelated score test for inference on low-dimensional components and establishes asymptotic normality achieving the semiparametric information bound.  
- Extends prior low-dimensional EM theory to the high-dimensional sparse setting and offers the first computationally feasible method with guarantees for both estimation and inference.  
- Significance: tackles an important, widely used method (EM) in a practically relevant high-dimensional setting with potentially high impact.  
- Originality: novel combination of EM with iterative hard thresholding and shift from l2- to l_infty-norm analysis to handle truncation.  
- Technical quality: rigorous analysis decomposing optimization and statistical errors, with key links between Q-function gradients and true score.  
- Clarity: well-written, organized, and uses abstract conditions plus model-specific verifications to communicate generality.  
- Weakness: theory requires a strong initial estimator within a constant l2-distance of the truth, with no practical procedure provided.  
- Weakness: lacks guidance on selecting tuning parameters (sparsity level bs and Dantzig lambda) in practice.  
- Weakness: purely theoretical with no simulations or experiments to validate convergence rates, error behavior, or test size/power.  
- Weakness: inference relies on a strong sparsity assumption on the projection vector w*, with limited discussion of its implications.  
- For acceptance: addresses a long-standing open problem and provides the first unified framework for near-optimal estimation and valid inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.  
- For acceptance: presents novel, significant technical contributions adapting debiasing ideas to a non-convex EM output and is rigorous and clear.  
- Against acceptance: strong initialization requirement and absence of empirical/tuning guidance limit practical relevance despite theoretical strength.
