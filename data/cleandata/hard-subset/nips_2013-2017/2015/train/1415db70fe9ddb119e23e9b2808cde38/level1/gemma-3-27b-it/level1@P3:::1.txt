This paper tackles a significant and challenging problem: providing theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors make a substantial contribution by developing a high-dimensional EM algorithm with sparsity-inducing truncation steps and establishing both statistical and computational guarantees, including asymptotic normality for low-dimensional parameters of interest. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and technically rigorous, addressing a critical gap in the existing literature.

**Summary of the Paper & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the recent progress in understanding the convergence of the EM algorithm, particularly [2, 18, 25, 30]. However, these works primarily focus on the low-dimensional regime. This paper extends this work to high dimensions (d > n) by introducing a truncation step in both the E and M steps to enforce sparsity. This is a clever and crucial modification. The authors correctly identify the main obstacles in high dimensions: the ill-defined maximum likelihood estimator without regularization, and the difficulty of establishing asymptotic normality due to high-dimensional nuisance parameters.  The proposed method provides a computationally feasible approach to optimal estimation and inference, addressing these challenges. The paper distinguishes itself from other high-dimensional estimation techniques like tensor methods [1, 9] by achieving a sharper statistical rate (p s* log d/n) compared to slower rates obtained by tensor methods, and by focusing on the *EM algorithm* itself, rather than alternative estimation procedures.  Connections to related work in sparse estimation (Lasso, Dantzig selector [8]) are also well-discussed.  The authors appropriately position their work within the broader landscape of statistical inference in high dimensions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The development of a high-dimensional EM algorithm with guaranteed convergence and statistical properties is a significant advancement.  The truncation step is a key innovation.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis, including geometric convergence rates and asymptotic normality results. The technical conditions are clearly stated and, as claimed, are verifiable for specific models.
* **Generality:** The framework is general enough to be applied to a range of latent variable models, demonstrated with examples of Gaussian Mixture Models and Mixture of Regression Models.
* **Practical Relevance:** The algorithm has the potential to be useful in a wide range of applications where high-dimensional latent variable models are employed.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, making the complex technical details relatively easy to follow. The use of algorithms and notation is consistent.
* **Thorough Comparison to Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of related work and clearly articulate the contributions of their paper.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The theoretical analysis is quite involved, and the numerous conditions may make it challenging for practitioners to apply the algorithm without a strong statistical background.  The assumptions, while clearly stated, are numerous and require careful verification.
* **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The algorithm's performance relies heavily on a "good" initialization, as indicated by the condition init ⇤ /k ⇤k 2  . The paper doesn’t offer much guidance on how to obtain such an initialization in practice. While acknowledging this, a discussion of practical initialization strategies would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:**  The truncation step, while crucial for theoretical guarantees, might add to the computational cost of the algorithm, especially for very high-dimensional problems. A discussion of the computational complexity and potential optimization strategies would be valuable.
* **Assumptions on Sparsity:** The requirement for sparsity (s*) and the related assumptions about the w* vector (in Section 4) are strong.  While justified in the context of asymptotic optimality, the practical implications of these assumptions deserve further discussion. Are there scenarios where the method performs well even when these assumptions are not fully met?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in statistical machine learning.
* The technical contributions are substantial and demonstrate a deep understanding of the EM algorithm and high-dimensional statistics.
* The theoretical results are rigorously established and provide valuable insights into the behavior of the algorithm.
* The generality of the framework and the examples provided suggest broad applicability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the theory and the sensitivity to initialization might limit the practical adoption of the algorithm.
* The computational cost of the truncation step could be a concern for very high-dimensional problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. While the theoretical complexity and practical considerations related to initialization and computational cost are legitimate concerns, they do not outweigh the importance of the theoretical advances.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors could consider:

1.  Adding a brief discussion of practical strategies for obtaining a good initialization.
2.  Providing a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm and potential optimization techniques.
3.  Discussing the sensitivity of the algorithm to violations of the sparsity assumptions.
4. Minor clarification in the notation throughout the paper to improve readability.



The paper represents a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and will likely stimulate further research in this area.
