**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a general theoretical framework for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a novel "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the standard EM procedure by adding a truncation (T-step) after each maximization (M-step) to enforce sparsity on the parameter estimates. For this algorithm, they prove that with a suitable initialization, the iterates converge at a geometric rate to a solution that achieves the near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of sqrt(s* log(d)/n). Second, building on this estimator, they develop a procedure for statistical inference on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. They construct a "decorrelated score statistic" that is shown to be asymptotically normal, enabling hypothesis testing and the construction of confidence intervals. The limiting variance of their statistic achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. The proposed framework is general and is demonstrated on high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

The work is well-situated within the existing literature. It directly extends the recent analysis of the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional setting by Balakrishnan et al. (2014, [2]) to the challenging high-dimensional (d >> n) regime. The key novelty distinguishing it from [2] is the introduction of the truncation step to handle high dimensionality and the corresponding need for a more refined, entrywise statistical analysis. For the inference part, the paper draws inspiration from the rapidly developing field of high-dimensional inference for convex models (e.g., debiased Lasso by Zhang & Zhang, 2014 [32]; van de Geer et al., 2014 [26]), but makes a significant original contribution by adapting these ideas to estimators that are local optima of a non-convex optimization problem, rather than global optima of a convex one.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a problem of fundamental importance. While the EM algorithm is a workhorse in practice for latent variable models, its theoretical properties in the high-dimensional setting were largely unknown. This work provides the first computationally tractable EM-based procedure with rigorous guarantees for both optimal parameter estimation and valid asymptotic inference in this challenging regime. This is a major step forward in closing the gap between theory and practice for a wide class of important statistical models.

2.  **Originality:** The contributions are highly original. The high-dimensional EM algorithm, incorporating an explicit sparsity-inducing truncation step within the iterative process, is a simple yet novel idea. The theoretical analysis required to show convergence and statistical optimality in the presence of this non-standard step is non-trivial. The development of a valid inferential procedure based on a local optimum of a non-convex problem is a particularly impressive and novel achievement, extending the scope of high-dimensional inference beyond the well-trodden ground of convex M-estimation.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The authors present a unified and rigorous analytical framework based on a clear set of verifiable conditions (e.g., Concavity-Smoothness, Statistical-Error). The decomposition of the total error into an optimization component that decays geometrically and a statistical component that dictates the final error rate is clean and insightful. The proofs appear to be sound and handle the complex interplay between the optimization dynamics and statistical noise with care.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the proposed algorithms are presented precisely, and the main theoretical results are highlighted upfront. The authors do a commendable job of explaining the key ideas and relating their work to various lines of prior research.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The Initialization Problem:** The entire theoretical analysis hinges on the existence of an initial estimator that lies within a local basin of attraction of the true parameter. This is a strong assumption, and the paper provides no discussion on how to obtain such an initialization in practice. While this is a common challenge in the analysis of non-convex problems (and is also present in the low-dimensional analysis of [2]), its practical importance cannot be overstated. Without a reliable and data-driven initialization procedure, the applicability of the proposed algorithm is limited. A discussion of potential strategies, such as using spectral methods or other moment-based estimators, would have significantly strengthened the paper.

2.  **Absence of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. There are no simulations or experiments on real data. For a conference like NIPS, some form of empirical validation is highly desirable. Simulations could have been used to: (a) verify that the theoretical convergence rates hold in practice for finite samples; (b) demonstrate the empirical coverage of the proposed confidence intervals; and (c) study the algorithm's sensitivity to the choice of tuning parameters and the quality of the initialization.

3.  **Tuning Parameter Selection:** The algorithm requires a sparsity parameter `bs`, and the inference procedure requires a tuning parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector. The theory provides guidance on the order of magnitude for these parameters, but these depend on unknown quantities like the true sparsity `s*`. The paper offers no practical guidance on how a user should set these parameters, for instance via cross-validation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first comprehensive theoretical framework for estimation and inference using an EM-type algorithm for high-dimensional latent variable models.
*   The theoretical contributions are significant, novel, and technically deep, pushing the boundaries of high-dimensional statistics.
*   The work addresses a major open problem with high potential impact for both researchers and practitioners working with latent variable models.
*   The paper is written with outstanding clarity.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The strong reliance on a "good" initialization without a method to find one is a major practical limitation.
*   The complete lack of experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical performance and robustness of the proposed methods.
*   Practical issues such as tuning parameter selection are not addressed.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a substantial and novel contribution to an important and difficult problem. The authors provide a rigorous and comprehensive analysis of a new EM algorithm for high-dimensional estimation and inference. While the practical aspects concerning initialization and empirical performance are not addressed, the significance and quality of the theoretical results are undeniable. This work provides a strong foundation for future research and is likely to be highly influential. I strongly recommend acceptance. I would urge the authors to add a discussion on the initialization problem and potential solutions in the final version, as this would make the paper even more impactful.
