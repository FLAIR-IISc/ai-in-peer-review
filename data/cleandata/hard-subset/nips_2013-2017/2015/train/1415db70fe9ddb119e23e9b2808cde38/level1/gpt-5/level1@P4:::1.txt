Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops a general theory and algorithms for high-dimensional EM in latent variable models. The first contribution is a high-dimensional EM algorithm that appends a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after each E/M-step to enforce sparsity. Under local initialization and regularity conditions on a population Q-function (Lipschitz-gradient and strong concavity/smoothness) plus an entrywise statistical error control between population and sample M-steps, the iterates converge geometrically to an estimator that achieves a near-optimal rate O(sqrt(s* log d / n), up to log n). The analysis covers both exact M-step and a gradient-ascent M-step with stepsize 2/(μ+ν). The second contribution is a decorrelated score (and Wald) test for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, built via a Dantzig-type estimator of a projection vector w and using derivatives of Qn(·;·). The test statistic is asymptotically normal with variance attaining the semiparametric efficiency bound.

Relative to prior EM analyses (e.g., Wu 1983; Tseng 2004) and recent finite-sample population-to-sample analyses in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., “From population to sample”), this work extends to the p ≫ n regime by introducing a truncation step and by controlling entrywise statistical errors. Compared to high-dimensional inference for GLMs via debiasing/desparsification (e.g., van de Geer et al.; Zhang & Zhang), this work targets nonconvex latent-variable likelihoods and an estimator not necessarily a global optimum of a convex program. The paper contrasts with tensor methods for mixtures (e.g., Anandkumar et al.), highlighting their suboptimal rates in some settings. Applications are shown to Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, with SNR and sparsity assumptions under which conditions are verified.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The optimization analysis is carefully stated under explicit conditions: (i) population Lipschitz-gradient (two variants depending on M-step implementation), (ii) population strong concavity/smoothness around the truth, (iii) entrywise concentration controlling Mn(·)−M(·) in ℓ1 when the input is sparse, and (iv) initialization inside a basin. The resulting geometric contraction plus a uniform statistical error floor is standard and appears internally consistent. The resampling (sample-splitting) device for analysis is standard to control dependence across iterates, though it may be conservative in practice.
- The truncation step is analyzed via support evolution and requires bs to be of the same order as s*, which is reasonable for theory. Bounds track the interaction between truncation error and estimation error via ℓ1 entrywise controls; this is a nontrivial advance over ℓ2-only analyses.
- The inference procedure is grounded by identities linking gradients of Qn to the score and Fisher information (Theorem 2.1). Under concentration and Lipschitz conditions on Tn(·), along with sparsity of w* and suitable tuning for the Dantzig selector, asymptotic normality is established with semiparametric efficiency.
- Assumptions are strong but clearly stated: suitable initialization; strong concavity in a neighborhood; SNR conditions (e.g., for GMM); sparsity of both θ* and w*; knowledge (or accurate upper bound) of s* to set bs; and sufficiently large n relative to s*, s*w, log d. These are typical for high-dimensional nonconvex problems but limit generality.
- The analysis for two models demonstrates verification of conditions and yields near-minimax rates. Some constants and extra log factors are not optimized, but this does not detract from the main claims.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized, with algorithmic descriptions, clearly labeled conditions (3.1–3.3, 4.1–4.4), and main results (Theorems 3.4 and 4.6). The role of each condition is explained.
- However, the presentation is dense and relies heavily on appendices. Practical guidance on selecting bs, step sizes, λ in the Dantzig program, and stopping criteria is limited. The necessity and implications of sample splitting (Algorithm 4) are mentioned but not discussed empirically.
- A concise discussion of initialization strategies (e.g., spectral/tensor warm starts for mixtures) would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
- The truncation-EM framework for high-dimensional latent-variable models appears novel and distinct from penalized EM variants. The combination of computational analysis (geometric convergence with truncation) and optimal-rate statistical guarantees in p ≫ n is original.
- The decorrelated score construction tailored to the EM/Q-function for latent-variable models, achieving semiparametric efficiency without a convex global optimum, is a notable and novel contribution.

Significance:
- Establishing both estimation and valid inference for high-dimensional latent-variable models fills an acknowledged gap. The results are likely to influence subsequent work on nonconvex high-dimensional MLEs beyond mixtures.
- Practical impact will depend on availability of good initializations and robustness to model misspecification; nonetheless, the theoretical framework is strong and broadly relevant.

Strengths:
- Clear, general framework covering exact and gradient M-steps with truncation.
- Near-optimal estimation rates under explicit and verifiable conditions.
- Inference method with asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency.
- Application to canonical latent-variable models with concrete guarantees.
- Careful treatment of entrywise statistical errors and support evolution.

Weaknesses:
- Requires strong initialization within a basin; practical strategies are not detailed.
- Assumes knowledge of or tight bounds on sparsity s* to set bs; tuning guidance is limited.
- Sample-splitting analysis may be conservative and could degrade finite-sample performance; practical alternatives are not discussed.
- Assumptions such as w*-sparsity and high SNR for GMM may be restrictive.
- Limited empirical guidance and discussion of computational cost and robustness.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide practical initialization procedures (e.g., tensor/spectral or method-of-moments warm starts) and discuss their basin guarantees.
- Offer data-driven tuning strategies for bs and Dantzig λ, and stopping rules; discuss sensitivity.
- Comment on removing or mitigating sample splitting in practice (e.g., via martingale analysis).
- Expand empirical evaluation (if not already in the full version), including misspecified models and comparisons to penalized EM and tensor methods.
- Clarify computational complexity per iteration (both M-step variants and Dantzig program) and scalability.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important gap: high-dimensional EM with both estimation and inference guarantees.
- Strong, general, and carefully proved results with optimal rates and efficiency.
- Applicable to a broad family of latent-variable models; techniques likely reusable.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical applicability limited by strong initialization and sparsity assumptions; tuning guidance is sparse.
- Heavy reliance on sample splitting and technical conditions may reduce real-world utility without further empirical validation.
- Scope of demonstrated models is limited to two examples; broader empirical evidence would strengthen impact.
