This paper presents a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings, where the parameter dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The primary contributions are twofold. First, the authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation (hard thresholding) step after the standard E- and M-steps to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. For this algorithm, they establish theoretical guarantees on its computational and statistical performance, showing that with a suitable initialization, it converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a (near-)minimax optimal statistical rate of convergence. Second, the paper develops a procedure for statistical inference based on the estimator produced by their algorithm. A decorrelated score statistic is constructed to test hypotheses on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. The authors prove that this statistic is asymptotically normal and that its limiting variance achieves the semiparametric information bound, rendering it optimal.

The work is situated at the intersection of non-convex optimization for latent variable models and high-dimensional statistics. It significantly extends the analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2014) [2], which provided convergence guarantees for the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional regime (`d << n`), to the more challenging high-dimensional setting. The key innovation for estimation is the introduction of the truncation step, which necessitates a more fine-grained analysis than previous work. For inference, the paper adapts ideas from the debiasing/desparsifying literature for high-dimensional convex models (e.g., van de Geer et al., 2014 [26]; Zhang & Zhang, 2014 [32]) to the non-convex EM setting, where the estimator is a local rather than global optimum. The general framework is demonstrated on two important latent variable models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model.

### Strengths

*   **Quality and Significance:** The paper makes a fundamental contribution by providing the first unified theoretical framework for both optimal parameter estimation and valid asymptotic inference for the EM algorithm in a high-dimensional context. This addresses a long-standing gap between the widespread practical use of EM for latent variable models and the lack of theoretical guarantees in modern statistical settings (d >> n). The results are strong, establishing geometric convergence computationally and near-optimal rates (sqrt(s*log(d)/n)) statistically. The inferential theory, which proves asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency, is a particularly significant advance for this class of non-convex problems.

*   **Originality:** The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm, which augments the standard procedure with an iterative truncation step, is a novel and natural approach to incorporate sparsity. The development of an inference procedure for the output of an iterative, non-convex algorithm is a key novelty. While the decorrelated score method draws inspiration from prior work on convex models, its adaptation and analysis in the context of EM, where the estimator is not the solution to a convex program, is highly non-trivial and original.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly motivate the problem, state their contributions, and carefully position their work relative to existing literature. The theoretical framework is presented in a general form using a clear set of conditions (e.g., Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness, Statistical-Error), which are then verified for specific model instances. This modular presentation makes the core ideas of the analysis transparent and potentially easier to adapt to other latent variable models.

### Weaknesses

*   **Initialization Requirement:** The theoretical guarantees for the algorithm are local in nature and depend critically on the availability of an initial estimator that is already within a constant-factor relative error of the true parameter. The paper does not provide a general, computationally tractable procedure for obtaining such an initialization. While this is a common challenge in the analysis of non-convex algorithms, it remains a practical limitation on the applicability of the stated guarantees.

*   **Analysis of a Resampling Algorithm:** The main convergence theorems are proven for a "resampling version" of the algorithm, which assumes that a fresh, independent batch of data is drawn at each iteration. This is a standard analytical technique that simplifies the proof by removing statistical dependencies across iterations. However, in practice, the algorithm is run on a single, fixed dataset. The paper does not discuss or quantify the potential gap between the theory for the resampling version and the performance of the practical, single-sample algorithm.

*   **Complexity of Assumptions for Inference:** The assumptions required for the asymptotic normality result (Assumption 4.5) are quite technical and involve several conditions on the sample size and the interplay between multiple problem-dependent quantities. In particular, the theory requires sparsity on `w*`, a vector related to the projection of one part of the Fisher information matrix onto another. While this is analogous to assumptions in other high-dimensional inference literature, it may be a strong requirement that is difficult to verify a priori for new models.

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical and presents no numerical simulations or experiments on real data. While the theoretical contributions are substantial on their own, empirical results would have strengthened the paper by: (1) demonstrating the practical performance of the algorithm and inference procedure, (2) providing insight into the choice of tuning parameters (e.g., the sparsity level `bs`), and (3) assessing the finite-sample accuracy of the asymptotic theory.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper addresses a highly important and challenging problem: establishing a rigorous theoretical foundation for the EM algorithm in high dimensions.
2.  It provides the first comprehensive framework that simultaneously guarantees computational efficiency (geometric convergence), statistical optimality (near-minimax rates), and valid asymptotic inference for a broad class of latent variable models.
3.  The technical contributions are novel and substantial, successfully extending sophisticated analysis from both non-convex optimization and high-dimensional inference to a new and difficult setting.
4.  The work is of high significance and is likely to have a major impact on future research in high-dimensional statistics and machine learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The strong assumption of a good initial estimator is a significant caveat, as a method for obtaining one is not provided.
2.  The theoretical analysis is for a resampling-based algorithm, which does not match the practical implementation, leaving a potential gap in the guarantees.
3.  The complete absence of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical implications of the theory, such as the size of constants or the required sample sizes for the asymptotics to hold.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its theoretical novelty and significance in establishing a foundational framework for high-dimensional EM, are compelling. The weaknesses, while notable, are common in initial theoretical works on complex non-convex problems and do not undermine the importance of the core contributions.
