

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

This paper addresses a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings, where the dimension d exceeds the sample size n. The authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, establishing geometric convergence rates with (near-)optimal statistical error. Additionally, they develop a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference on low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters, which is the first such framework for latent variable models in high dimensions.

The work builds on the foundational contributions of [2, 25, 30] that established convergence guarantees for EM in low dimensions. The key innovation lies in the truncation step (line 6 of Algorithm 1), which enforces sparsity along the solution path. This distinguishes the paper from [2] (which established p(d/n) rates in low dimensions) and from tensor methods [1, 9] (which typically achieve suboptimal rates like p(d⁶/n) for mixture models). The authors also connect their work to high-dimensional inference literature [13, 26, 32] for GLMs, but crucially extend it to latent variable models where the nonconcavity of the likelihood makes global optimization intractable.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical guarantees. The decomposition of estimation error into optimization error (geometric decay) and statistical error (p(s*·log d/n)) is well-motivated and clearly presented. Theorems 3.4 and 4.6 establish the first simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees for high-dimensional EM, including the optimal semiparametric efficiency of the decorrelated score statistic. The analysis of the truncation step's impact on the iterative sequence is particularly sophisticated, addressing a non-trivial technical challenge. The appendix provides detailed verifications of conditions for Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, demonstrating the framework's applicability.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear definitions, consistent notation, and logical flow. The high-level intuition for the truncation step and decorrelated score statistic is well-explained in the introduction and appendix. The main theorems are precisely stated, and the relationship between the error terms and theoretical rates is transparent. The connection between the proposed framework and existing literature is thoroughly documented, avoiding overclaiming.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by extending EM to high-dimensional settings with theoretical guarantees. The truncation step is a novel technical innovation that enables the analysis of sparsity-constrained EM in high dimensions. The decorrelated score statistic for latent variable models represents a major advance over existing high-dimensional inference methods, which typically require convex optimization frameworks. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work through its focus on non-convex latent variable models and the computational feasibility of the proposed approach.

**Significance:** This work addresses a fundamental gap in statistical learning theory for latent variable models. The ability to perform both estimation and inference in high-dimensional settings is crucial for modern applications (e.g., genomics, recommendation systems) where latent structures are common but high dimensionality is the norm. The proposed framework is general and applicable to a broad family of models, and the established rates match minimax lower bounds, indicating theoretical optimality.

**Strengths:** The paper's most compelling strengths are its theoretical completeness (covering both estimation and inference), the elegant integration of sparsity into EM through the truncation step, and the demonstration of semiparametric efficiency for the inference procedure. The careful distinction between optimization error and statistical error provides valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior.

**Weaknesses:** The paper would benefit from more discussion of practical implementation challenges, particularly regarding the choice of the sparsity parameter bs (Eq. 3.8) and the difficulty of obtaining the required initialization. While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the absence of empirical validation (beyond the theoretical examples) weakens the practical impact. The computational complexity of the exact M-step (Algorithm 2) is not thoroughly analyzed, which could be a concern for large-scale applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** This is a theoretically rigorous contribution that solves a fundamental problem in high-dimensional statistical learning. The paper establishes the first computationally feasible approach for optimal estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models, with the theoretical guarantees matching minimax rates. The framework is general and applicable to a broad class of models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The lack of empirical validation is a notable limitation, as the practical utility of the theoretical framework remains untested. The assumption of a good initialization (requiring init - θ* / ||θ*||₂ ≤ ε) may be restrictive in practice.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the EM algorithm in high dimensions. While the absence of empirical results is a minor drawback, the theoretical depth and significance of the work justify its acceptance. The paper represents a significant step toward closing the gap between computation and statistics in high-dimensional latent variable models, with potential applications across multiple fields.
