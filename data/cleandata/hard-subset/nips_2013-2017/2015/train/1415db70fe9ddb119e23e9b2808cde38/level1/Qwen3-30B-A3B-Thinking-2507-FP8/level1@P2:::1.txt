

This paper introduces a high-dimensional extension of the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm that incorporates sparsity structure, providing statistical guarantees for both parameter estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors address a significant gap in the literature, as previous theoretical work on EM algorithms has focused on low-dimensional settings where the dimension d is much smaller than the sample size n. Their key contributions are: (i) a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step to enforce sparsity, which converges at a geometric rate to an estimator with near-optimal statistical rate of convergence (p(s* log d/n)), and (ii) a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters, which achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound.

The paper relates well to previous work, particularly the foundational work by [2] which established convergence rates for EM in low dimensions. The authors clearly differentiate their high-dimensional approach from this prior work, noting that their truncation step (T-step) is a key innovation that enables the analysis in high dimensions. They also contrast their method with tensor-based approaches [1, 9] that are suboptimal in high dimensions and with Lasso-based inference methods [13, 26, 32] that are not applicable to latent variable models due to the nonconvexity of the likelihood function.

The paper excels in quality. The theoretical results are rigorous, with detailed conditions (Conditions 3.1-3.3) and comprehensive proofs in the appendix. The authors establish both computational (geometric convergence rate) and statistical (optimal rate of convergence) guarantees, which is a significant achievement. They provide a unified framework that handles both exact maximization and gradient ascent implementations of the M-step, adding to the generality of their approach. The decorrelated score statistic for inference achieves the semiparametric information bound, demonstrating the optimality of their approach.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-structured and clearly written. The main contributions are stated upfront, and the algorithms are presented with precise pseudocode (Algorithms 1-3). The notation is consistent, and the appendix provides sufficient detail for experts to verify the proofs. However, some technical conditions (e.g., Condition 3.3) might be challenging for readers without deep expertise in high-dimensional statistics.

The originality of the work is strong. The truncation step in the high-dimensional EM algorithm is a novel technical contribution that enables the authors to establish the first theoretical guarantees for EM in high dimensions. The decorrelated score statistic for inference in latent variable models is also new, as previous work on inference in high dimensions has focused on models with convex loss functions rather than latent variable models with nonconvex likelihoods.

The significance of the paper is high. High-dimensional latent variable models are widely used in applications, but there has been a lack of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in these settings. This paper fills that gap by providing a general framework that establishes both optimal estimation and asymptotic inference for a broad family of models. The results are applicable to important models like Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models, and they represent a substantial advance over previous work on EM algorithms.

Strengths:
- Novel high-dimensional EM algorithm with truncation step
- First theoretical guarantees for EM in high-dimensional latent variable models
- Optimal statistical rate of convergence (p(s* log d/n))
- Semiparametrically efficient inference via decorrelated score statistic
- Unified framework applicable to multiple latent variable models
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with detailed proofs

Weaknesses:
- The technical conditions (e.g., Condition 3.3) are somewhat complex and might be difficult to verify for new models
- The paper focuses on Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, leaving open the question of applicability to other models
- The assumptions about sparsity of w* (in Assumption 4.5) might be restrictive for some applications

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper addresses a fundamental problem in high-dimensional statistics with a novel solution
- The theoretical results are strong and significant for the field
- The paper provides a general framework that can be applied to many latent variable models
- The results represent a substantial advance over previous work on EM algorithms

Arguments against acceptance:
- The technical complexity might limit the accessibility of the results to some readers
- The paper could benefit from more empirical validation to complement the theoretical results

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and latent variable models. The authors successfully bridge the gap between computation (EM algorithm) and statistics (optimal estimation and inference) in high-dimensional settings. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses an important open problem. I believe this paper would be of interest to the NIPS community and would make a valuable contribution to the field. The paper meets all the review criteria at a high level, with exceptional quality, clarity, originality, and significance. I recommend acceptance.
