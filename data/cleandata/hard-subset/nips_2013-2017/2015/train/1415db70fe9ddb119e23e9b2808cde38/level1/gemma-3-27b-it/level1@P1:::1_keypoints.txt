- Tackles theoretical guarantees for EM in high-dimensional latent variable models via a modified EM with a truncation step enforcing sparsity.  
- Establishes statistical and computational guarantees, including asymptotic normality for low-dimensional components.  
- Builds on low-dimensional EM convergence literature and differs by adding truncation to handle p > n challenges.  
- Relates to proximal methods and gradient ascent but highlights novelty and analysis challenges introduced by truncation.  
- Positions as an improvement over tensor methods and Lasso-like approaches for nonconvex latent-variable likelihoods.  
- Novel contribution: high-dimensional EM with truncation is practically motivated and theoretically significant.  
- Theoretical rigor: comprehensive analysis with convergence rates and asymptotic normality under clear conditions.  
- General framework: applicable to a broad family of latent variable models (e.g., Gaussian mixtures, mixture of regressions).  
- Optimality: decorrelated score test achieves the semiparametric information bound.  
- Presentation: paper is well-written, organized, and algorithms are clearly defined.  
- Complexity: theory requires many technical conditions, making the paper hard to digest for non-experts.  
- Computational cost: truncation step may add overhead; practical scaling implications are not thoroughly discussed.  
- Sensitivity to initialization: algorithm needs a good initial estimate but provides limited guidance on obtaining one.  
- Strong inference assumptions: asymptotic results require strong sparsity/Fisher information conditions that may not hold in practice.  
- Limited empirical evaluation: no simulations or real-data experiments to demonstrate practical performance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a significant open problem, is novel, rigorous, and broadly applicable.  
- Arguments for rejection: theoretical complexity and strong assumptions may limit practicality; lack of empirical validation and possible computational concerns.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical paper with significant impact; recommendation to accept.  
- Suggested improvements: add intuitive explanations of conditions, discuss computational costs of truncation, provide initialization strategies, include empirical results, and relax or better justify inference assumptions.
