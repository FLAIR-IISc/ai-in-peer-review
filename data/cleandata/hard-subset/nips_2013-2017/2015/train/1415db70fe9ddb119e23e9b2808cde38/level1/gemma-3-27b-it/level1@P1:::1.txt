This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors make a significant contribution by proposing a modified EM algorithm with a truncation step to enforce sparsity and establishing statistical and computational guarantees, including asymptotic normality for low-dimensional components. The work is well-motivated, addressing a clear gap in the existing literature.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Previous Work:**

The core idea is to modify the standard EM algorithm by incorporating a truncation step after each E-step and M-step. This truncation forces sparsity in the parameter estimates, which is crucial for obtaining theoretical guarantees when the dimensionality exceeds the sample size. The paper builds on previous work establishing convergence and statistical rates for the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional regime (e.g., [2, 18, 25, 30]). However, these results do not extend to high dimensions. This paper specifically addresses this limitation. The authors relate their approach to proximal point methods [25] and gradient ascent, but highlight the novelty of the truncation step and the resulting challenges in analysis.  They position their work as offering improvements over tensor methods [1, 9] and other high-dimensional estimation techniques like Lasso [13, 15, 16, 17, 19, 20, 22, 23, 24, 26, 29, 31, 32], particularly in the context of latent variable models where the likelihood is non-convex.  The asymptotic inference results are novel for high-dimensional latent variable models, differing from existing approaches focusing primarily on convex programs.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm with the truncation step is a significant contribution. It provides a practical approach to address a fundamental limitation of the standard EM algorithm in high dimensions.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, establishing both convergence rates for parameter estimation and asymptotic normality for hypothesis testing. The conditions are clearly stated, and the proofs (referenced to the appendix) seem carefully constructed.
* **Generality:** The framework is general enough to be applied to a broad family of latent variable models, as demonstrated by the application to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models.
* **Optimality:** The authors show that the proposed decorrelated score test achieves the semiparametric information bound, indicating its optimality.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear exposition of the methodology and theoretical results.  Algorithms are well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The theory is complex and requires a significant number of conditions. While necessary for rigor, this can make the paper difficult to grasp for readers not deeply familiar with the field. More intuitive explanations of the conditions would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost:**  The truncation step, while crucial for theoretical guarantees, potentially adds computational overhead. The paper doesn’t thoroughly discuss the practical implications of this overhead, particularly in terms of scaling to very high dimensions.
* **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The algorithm's performance is sensitive to the initialization. The paper states a requirement on the relative error of the initial parameter, but provides limited guidance on how to obtain such an initialization in practice.
* **Assumptions for Inference:** The assumptions required for the asymptotic inference results (Assumption 4.5 and associated conditions) are quite strong, particularly regarding the sparsity of the Fisher information matrix. These assumptions might not hold for all practical applications.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any empirical evaluation to demonstrate the performance of the proposed algorithm and inferential procedure on real or simulated datasets.  While theoretical guarantees are important, empirical validation is essential to assess the practical utility of the method.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in statistical inference for high-dimensional latent variable models.
* The proposed algorithm and theoretical framework are novel and make a substantial contribution to the field.
* The results are mathematically rigorous and provide strong guarantees for the performance of the algorithm.
* The generality of the framework allows it to be applied to a wide range of models.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The complexity of the theory and the strong assumptions required for asymptotic inference might limit the practical applicability of the method.
* The lack of empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the real-world performance of the algorithm.
* The computational cost of the truncation step could be a concern for large-scale applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with significant potential impact.  While the complexity and lack of empirical evaluation are concerns, the novelty and rigor of the work outweigh these drawbacks. I recommend **acceptance** with the following suggestions for improvement:

1.  **Provide more intuitive explanations** of the key conditions and assumptions.
2.  **Discuss the computational implications** of the truncation step in more detail.
3.  **Include a discussion of initialization strategies** for obtaining a suitable initial parameter estimate.
4.  **Consider adding some preliminary empirical results** to demonstrate the performance of the algorithm on simulated or real datasets.  Even a small-scale empirical study would significantly strengthen the paper.
5.  **Address the potential limitations of the assumptions** needed for the inferential framework and explore alternative approaches for weaker assumptions.
