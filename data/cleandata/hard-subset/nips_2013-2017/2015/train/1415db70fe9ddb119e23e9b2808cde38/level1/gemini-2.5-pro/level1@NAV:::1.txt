This paper presents a general theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in the high-dimensional setting where the number of parameters `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The core contributions are twofold. First, the authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm which integrates a truncation step after the standard E- and M-steps to enforce sparsity. They prove this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate. Second, and crucially, they move beyond point estimation to develop a decorrelated score statistic for conducting hypothesis tests on low-dimensional components of the parameter vector. They establish the asymptotic normality of this statistic, enabling valid statistical inference. This work significantly extends recent analyses of the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional setting [2] and connects to the broader literature on high-dimensional inference for convex models like the Lasso [26, 32], distinguishing itself by tackling the challenging non-concave likelihoods inherent to latent variable models.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing problem. The EM algorithm is a cornerstone of statistical machine learning, yet its theoretical properties in high dimensions have remained largely uncharacterized. This work provides the first rigorous, unified framework for both optimal estimation and valid inference, a major contribution that will likely be influential for both theoreticians and practitioners working with high-dimensional latent variable models.
*   **Originality:** The introduction of the truncation step within the EM framework is an elegant and novel method for incorporating sparsity. Furthermore, developing a provably valid inferential procedure for the output of a non-convex iterative algorithm is highly original and technically demanding, representing a substantial advance over existing work that focuses on global optima of convex problems.
*   **Quality:** The technical quality of the paper is very high. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis, and the authors carefully state the conditions under which their results hold. The paper provides a complete piece of work, covering both computational convergence and statistical properties (estimation error and asymptotic normality for inference).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, outlines the contributions, and situates the work within the relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The theoretical guarantees, particularly the geometric convergence, depend on a suitable initialization within a "basin of attraction." This is a common requirement in the analysis of non-convex optimization, but the paper does not provide guidance on practical strategies for finding such an initial point. While this does not diminish the theoretical contribution, it is a practical consideration for implementation.
*   The technical conditions for the main theorems are stated in a general and abstract form. While these are verified for the Gaussian Mixture and Mixture of Regression models, it may be challenging for a reader to readily check these conditions for a new latent variable model.

In summary, this is a landmark paper that provides fundamental theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high dimensions. The strengths, particularly its significance and originality in enabling both estimation and inference, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It represents a substantial advance for the field.
