Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops a general theory for EM in high-dimensional latent variable models with two main contributions: (i) a high-dimensional EM algorithm (EM + truncation) that enforces sparsity via a hard-thresholding “T-step” after each E/M step, and (ii) an inferential framework based on a decorrelated score that yields asymptotically normal tests for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. Under suitable initialization and regularity conditions, the estimator enjoys geometric optimization convergence and (near-)minimax statistical rates O(√(s* log d / n)) up to logs, and the score test is asymptotically normal with variance achieving the semiparametric efficiency bound.

This work builds on population-to-sample EM analyses in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan, Wainwright, Yu), extending to high dimensions by introducing truncation and by controlling entrywise statistical error in the M-step. For inference, it adapts “debiased/desparsified” ideas (van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang) to latent variable models where global convexity and exact penalized MLEs are unavailable. The paper contrasts with tensor/moment methods that often have worse rates and focuses on computationally feasible EM iterates rather than global optima. Applications to Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions are outlined.

Strengths:
- Conceptual advance: a unified framework giving simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees for sparse EM in high dimensions, covering both exact and gradient M-step implementations.
- Technical novelty: careful handling of the truncation step’s effect on support evolution and entrywise error to attain optimal rates; analysis via a basin-of-attraction and Lipschitz-gradient/concavity-smoothness conditions.
- Inference contribution: a decorrelated score constructed from EM quantities, with asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency, filling a gap for high-dimensional latent variable models.
- Clear statements of conditions and main theorems, with specialization to concrete models (GMM, mixture of regressions) and explicit rates.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Initialization: The theory assumes an initializer within a constant relative error ball. How to obtain such an initializer in high dimensions with sparsity is only briefly discussed. Concrete, computationally efficient initialization procedures (e.g., spectral/tensor-based warm starts restricted to sparse supports, or method-of-moments followed by thresholding) should be specified and their guarantees aligned with the required basin.
- Choice of sparsity parameter bs: The analysis requires bs of the same order as s*. In practice s* is unknown. Guidance on adaptive selection (e.g., using information criteria, stability selection, or a continuation schedule) and robustness to mis-specification (over- vs under-shooting) would strengthen applicability.
- Resampling analysis vs single-batch: The main estimation theorem is proved for a resampling (sample-splitting) variant to simplify dependence. Practitioners typically reuse all data across iterations. Either a proof sketch or empirical evidence that similar rates hold without resampling would help, or at least a discussion of the technical obstacles.
- Assumptions for inference: The sparsity of w* (a column of the precision of the Fisher block) can be restrictive and model-dependent. The paper could better justify when this holds for the showcased models (e.g., diagonal covariance or weak cross-feature dependence) and discuss diagnostics if the assumption fails.
- Computational aspects of the Dantzig step: Solving the Dantzig problem with Tn may be demanding in d. Discussion of scalable solvers or alternatives (e.g., nodewise Lasso approximations, proximal variants) and empirical runtime would be valuable.
- Empirical validation: The excerpt does not include experiments; if the full paper has limited experiments, adding synthetic and real-data studies demonstrating estimation accuracy, support recovery, and calibration of the score/Wald tests (coverage, Type I error, power) would substantially improve the submission.
- Related work breadth: The truncation step is related to iterative hard-thresholding and projected gradient methods for sparse M-estimation. A more explicit comparison to this literature (e.g., IHT/IHT-M for nonconvex losses; projected gradient for high-dimensional M-estimation) would help position the algorithmic contribution.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically solid, with clear separation of optimization vs statistical error. Conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity/strong concavity near truth, entrywise concentration) are reasonable for sub-Gaussian designs and separated mixtures; SNR assumptions are explicitly stated for GMM. The inference result is rigorous and attains the efficient variance. The main caveats are strong initialization and sparsity-of-w* assumptions, and reliance on resampling for clean proofs.
- Clarity: Well-organized main results, clear algorithm description, and conditions labeled systematically. However, notation density is high; simplifying derivative notation and bringing essential parts of the inference construction and assumptions from the appendix into the main text would improve readability. A brief “assumptions at a glance” table for each model would help practitioners.
- Originality: High. Extends EM theory into high-dimensional sparse settings with a novel truncation-based EM and delivers the first computationally feasible asymptotic inference for latent variable models in high dimensions, to my knowledge. The use of EM-based quantities in the decorrelated score is also original.
- Significance: High. Many practical latent variable models are high-dimensional. Providing optimal rates with computational guarantees and valid inference is impactful. The framework should spur follow-up work on other latent models and on practical initialization/selection schemes.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, general theoretical framework bridging computation and statistics in high-dimensional EM.
- Near-optimal estimation rates and semiparametric-efficient inference.
- Applicability to common latent models (GMM, mixture of regressions) with concrete specializations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical gaps: initialization, selection of bs, and computational burden of the Dantzig step not fully resolved.
- Reliance on resampling for theory; clarity on single-batch behavior is limited.
- Potentially strong assumptions for inference (sparse w*) with limited justification across models.
- If experiments are minimal, empirical support is insufficient.

Questions for authors:
- What practical initialization do you recommend, and under what conditions does it satisfy the required basin for your models?
- How sensitive is performance to misspecified bs? Can you propose a data-driven rule?
- Do your guarantees (or experiments) extend to the non-resampling algorithm using the full dataset each iteration?
- Can the decorrelated score be computed with a nodewise regression surrogate to reduce computation? Does theory accommodate this?

Recommendation:
Leaning accept, contingent on clarifying initialization/selection and adding empirical validation. The contributions are substantive and timely, with strong potential impact despite some practical assumptions.
