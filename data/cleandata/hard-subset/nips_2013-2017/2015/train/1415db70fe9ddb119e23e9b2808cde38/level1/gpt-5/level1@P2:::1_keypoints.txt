- Paper develops a high-dimensional EM with truncation (hard-threshold T-step) and a decorrelated score for asymptotically normal inference of low-dimensional components amid high-dimensional nuisance parameters.  
- Builds on low-dimensional population-to-sample EM analyses and debiased/desparsified inference, extending them to sparse latent variable models and contrasting with tensor/moment methods.

- Strength: unified framework giving simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees for sparse EM, covering exact and gradient M-steps.  
- Strength: technical novelty in controlling truncation’s effect on support evolution and entrywise M-step error to attain near-optimal rates.  
- Strength: inference contribution via an EM-based decorrelated score that is asymptotically normal and semiparametric-efficient.  
- Strength: clear theorem statements and specialization to concrete models (GMM, mixtures of regressions) with explicit rates.

- Weakness: theory assumes an initializer within a constant relative error ball but provides only brief guidance on how to obtain such sparse high-dimensional warm starts.  
- Weakness: analysis requires choosing bs ≈ s*, but s* is unknown; lacks adaptive selection strategies and robustness analysis to mis-specification.  
- Weakness: main estimation theorem uses resampling (sample-splitting); behavior and guarantees for practical single-batch reuse of data are not shown.  
- Weakness: inference assumes sparsity of w* (a precision-column), which can be restrictive and is not fully justified across showcased models.  
- Weakness: solving the Dantzig step may be computationally heavy in high d; paper lacks discussion of scalable solvers or nodewise-Lasso alternatives and runtime.  
- Weakness: empirical validation appears limited or absent; experiments demonstrating estimation accuracy, support recovery, and test calibration are needed.  
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to iterative hard-thresholding/projected gradient literature for sparse nonconvex M-estimation.

- Quality: theoretically careful and technically solid, but caveats include strong initialization and resampling reliance.  
- Clarity: well-organized with systematic conditions, but notation is dense and some inference assumptions belong in main text; an “assumptions-at-a-glance” would help.  
- Originality: high — extends EM theory to high-dimensional sparse settings and offers novel EM-based decorrelated inference.  
- Significance: high — provides impactful framework for many practical high-dimensional latent models with computational and inferential guarantees.

- Pros for acceptance: strong general theoretical framework, near-optimal estimation rates, semiparametric-efficient inference, and applicability to common latent models.  
- Cons for acceptance: practical gaps (initialization, bs selection, Dantzig computation), reliance on resampling for proofs, possibly strong inference assumptions, and limited experiments.

- Question: What practical initialization do you recommend and under what conditions does it land in the required basin?  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to misspecified bs, and can you propose a data-driven selection rule?  
- Question: Do guarantees or experiments extend to the non-resampling algorithm that reuses the full dataset each iteration?  
- Question: Can the decorrelated score be computed via nodewise regression surrogates to reduce computation, and does theory support this?

- Recommendation: Leaning accept, contingent on clarifying initialization/bs selection, addressing resampling vs single-batch behavior, and adding empirical validation.
