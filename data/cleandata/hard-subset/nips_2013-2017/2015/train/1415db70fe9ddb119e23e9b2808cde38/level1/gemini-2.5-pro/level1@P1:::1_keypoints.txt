- Proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm that adds a truncation (T-step) after each M-step to enforce sparsity and proves geometric convergence to near-minimax error sqrt(s* log d / n).
- Develops a decorrelated score statistic for inference on low-dimensional components that is asymptotically normal and achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound.
- Framework is general and demonstrated on high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regressions.
- Extends Balakrishnan et al. (2014) low-dimensional EM analysis to the high-dimensional setting via truncation and refined entrywise analysis.
- Adapts high-dimensional inference techniques (e.g., debiased Lasso ideas) to estimators that are local optima of a non-convex problem—a novel contribution.
- Significance: provides the first computationally tractable EM-based procedure with rigorous guarantees for both estimation and inference in the d >> n regime.
- Originality: the sparsity-inducing truncation within EM and the inferential development from non-convex local optima are both novel and non-trivial.
- Technical quality: presents a unified, rigorous framework with verifiable conditions and a clear decomposition of optimization vs statistical error; proofs appear sound.
- Clarity: paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and clearly motivated, with precise algorithm descriptions and highlighted theoretical results.
- Weakness—initialization: theoretical guarantees require a good initializer in the basin of attraction, but the paper provides no method or discussion for obtaining such initialization.
- Weakness—empirics: entirely theoretical with no simulations or real-data experiments to validate finite-sample convergence, CI coverage, or sensitivity to tuning/initialization.
- Weakness—tuning: practical selection of sparsity parameter bs and Dantzig selector tuning lambda is not addressed, and theory depends on unknown quantities like s*.
- Arguments for acceptance: major theoretical advance, significant and deep contributions, high potential impact, and excellent exposition.
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy reliance on an unavailable initialization, absence of experiments, and lack of practical tuning guidance.
- Conclusion: an excellent and likely influential theoretical paper recommended for acceptance, with a suggested addition of discussion and potential solutions for initialization and practical implementation.
