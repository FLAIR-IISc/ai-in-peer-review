Summary
The paper proposes a high-dimensional variant of EM that augments the classical E/M steps with a truncation step (hard thresholding to the top bs entries) to enforce sparsity along the iterates. The authors develop a unified analytic framework covering both exact M-step and gradient-ascent M-step, and prove simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees: (i) geometric contraction of the optimization error within a local basin and (ii) near-optimal statistical error of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to log factors), once the optimization error decays below the stochastic floor. Beyond estimation, the paper develops a decorrelated score/Wald inference procedure adapted to latent-variable likelihoods by exploiting identities linking the Q-function derivatives to the score and Fisher information; the resulting test is asymptotically normal with variance achieving the semiparametric efficiency bound in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Relation to prior work
This work extends the “from population to sample” EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) from low-dimensional to high-dimensional regimes. The truncation step parallels ideas in iterative hard-thresholding and sparse first-order methods, but is, to my knowledge, new in the context of EM with rigorous nonasymptotic analysis of the iterates. On inference, the decorrelated score builds on desparsification/debiasing ideas (van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang, Ning & Liu) but adapts them to latent-variable likelihoods via Theorem 2.1, producing semiparametrically optimal tests. Compared to l1-penalized EM variants (e.g., Städler et al.; other penalized mixture literature) and tensor/moment methods (Anandkumar et al.), the paper provides sharper rates in high dimension and adds valid inference.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a simple, implementable modification (T-step) that enforces sparsity along EM iterates, analyzed under both exact and gradient M-steps.
- Sharp theory: clean decomposition into optimization versus statistical error; near-minimax estimation rates up to logs; explicit geometric convergence rates within a basin.
- Inference: a unified decorrelated score tailored to latent-variable models, with rigorous normality and semiparametric efficiency; accommodates high-dimensional nuisance without requiring a global optimum of a convex problem.
- General framework: conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, local strong concavity/smoothness, l1 statistical error for M vs Mn) are transparent and verified for two canonical models.
- Bridges computation and statistics in a regime where MLE is ill-defined and penalized likelihoods are nonconvex.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Initialization and basin: Results require an initializer within a local basin (relative l2 error ≤ constant) and, for mixtures, a sufficiently large SNR. The paper does not provide a concrete, computationally efficient initializer with guarantees in the same high-dimensional regime (e.g., method-of-moments/tensor + thresholding, spectral initializers). This is critical for practicality.
- Resampling EM: The main estimation theorem analyzes a “resampling”/sample-splitting variant for technical convenience. While common in theory, it is not the algorithm practitioners would run; extensions of the proofs to the single-batch setting (without fresh data each iteration) are not provided.
- Tuning and adaptivity: The sparsity parameter bs must be of the same order as s*, and several other tuning parameters (step size, constraint radius for the Dantzig selector, etc.) depend on unknown quantities. There is little guidance on data-driven selection and its impact on guarantees.
- Dantzig step scalability: The inference procedure requires solving a high-dimensional Dantzig problem to estimate w (per target). Computational and numerical stability concerns are not discussed; nodewise Lasso-style alternatives and their theoretical equivalence could be considered.
- Assumptions for inference: Asymptotic normality requires sparsity of w* (the inverse-information regression vector), eigenvalue conditions, and multiple small-o conditions (e.g., s_w* sqrt(log d / n) → 0). The paper asserts plausibility but provides limited discussion of when these hold for mixtures beyond the isotropic, well-separated setting.
- Empirical evaluation: No experiments are presented. For NeurIPS, empirical validation (finite-sample estimation error and CI coverage across d, n, s*, and SNR; comparisons with l1-EM, gradient EM, and tensor initializers) would significantly strengthen the paper.
- Clarity and presentation: The exposition is notation-heavy with several typographical issues and incomplete references. The Q-function identities (Theorem 2.1) require standard but nontrivial regularity assumptions (dominated convergence/interchange of differentiation and integration) that should be stated explicitly in the main text.

Technical questions and suggestions
- Provide a concrete, analyzable initialization scheme in high dimension (e.g., method-of-moments or spectral initialization followed by thresholding), and state explicit conditions under which it lands in the basin.
- Replace the resampling analysis with a single-batch argument (e.g., uniform empirical process control over sparse sets) or at least include a theorem sketch indicating what changes.
- Discuss how to choose bs in practice (cross-validation, information criteria, or adaptive thresholding) and whether theoretical guarantees can be made adaptive to s*.
- For the Dantzig step, consider and compare nodewise Lasso or CLIME-style alternatives; characterize the computational complexity.
- Clarify whether σ is known in the Gaussian mixture application and whether it needs to be estimated; if estimated, how does it affect rates and inference?
- Comment on robustness to mild model misspecification and on extensions to non-spherical covariances.

Quality: Strong theoretical development with careful decomposition and nonasymptotic rates; inference results are well-motivated and technically interesting, though dependent on nontrivial conditions and resampling.

Clarity: Generally clear for experts, but the paper would benefit from streamlined notation, explicit regularity assumptions in the main text, corrected typos, and more intuition.

Originality: High for combining truncated EM analysis with valid high-dimensional inference in latent-variable models; extends and integrates multiple strands of recent literature.

Significance: Potentially high; if supported by practical initialization and experiments, this could become a reference for high-dimensional EM with inference.

Pros
- Novel and general framework with near-optimal rates
- Semiparametrically efficient inference in latent-variable settings
- Covers exact and gradient M-steps; unifies computation and inference

Cons
- Depends on strong initialization/SNR assumptions; no initializer provided
- Resampling-based analysis; no single-batch guarantees
- No experiments; several tuning parameters and computational concerns left open

Recommendation
Borderline accept if the venue tolerates theory-centric contributions; otherwise, encourage a revision with practical initialization, single-batch analysis or justification, and empirical validation.
