

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm with theoretical guarantees for both estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors introduce a truncation step to enforce sparsity during the E-step and M-step of the classical EM algorithm, allowing them to establish geometric convergence rates and optimal statistical rates of convergence in high-dimensional settings where d >> n. The paper further develops a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter, proving it achieves semiparametric efficiency.

The work addresses a significant gap in the literature. While the low-dimensional EM algorithm has been well-studied (e.g., [2, 18, 25, 30]), no prior work has established theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional regimes. The authors correctly identify that standard maximum likelihood estimators are not well-defined without regularization in high dimensions, and existing approaches to high-dimensional inference (e.g., debiasing methods for Lasso) do not apply to latent variable models due to their non-convex likelihood structures. The paper's main novelty lies in the truncation step that enforces sparsity along the solution path, enabling the first unified framework for both estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm with truncation, (2) theoretical guarantees for geometric convergence to a near-optimal estimator, and (3) a decorrelated score statistic that achieves semiparametric efficiency for inference. The authors demonstrate the framework's applicability to Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models, establishing the first theoretical guarantees for these models in high dimensions.

The theoretical results are impressive. Theorem 3.4 establishes a decomposition of the estimation error into optimization and statistical components, with the latter achieving the near-minimax optimal rate of √(s* log d/n). Theorem 4.6 proves the decorrelated score statistic is asymptotically normal with optimal limiting variance, achieving the semiparametric information bound. This is particularly significant as the paper correctly notes that existing high-dimensional inference methods (e.g., [13, 26, 32]) typically require convex optimization problems, which do not apply to the non-convex likelihoods of latent variable models.

The paper's main strength is its conceptual novelty and the breadth of its contribution to a significant open problem. The truncation step is a bold and simple modification to the classical EM algorithm that enables the theoretical results. The unified framework for both estimation and inference is particularly valuable, as most high-dimensional work focuses on estimation alone.

The paper has a few limitations that are acceptable given the novelty of the contribution. The lack of empirical validation is notable but perhaps justified by the focus on theoretical guarantees. The truncation step is somewhat ad hoc, though the authors provide reasonable justification through their theoretical analysis. The assumptions required (Conditions 3.1-3.3) are standard for high-dimensional analysis but may be restrictive in practice.

The related work comparison is thorough and well-motivated, correctly identifying the gap this work fills. The paper properly distinguishes itself from low-dimensional EM work ([2]) and from high-dimensional inference methods for generalized linear models (e.g., [13, 26, 32]). The connection to tensor methods ([1, 9]) is also appropriately addressed.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field. It introduces a new direction for high-dimensional statistical inference in latent variable models, addressing a fundamental gap in the literature. The theoretical results are rigorous and establish a new standard for high-dimensional EM algorithms. While the paper would benefit from empirical validation, the theoretical novelty and completeness of the framework outweigh this limitation.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound with thorough theoretical analysis, clearly written with a well-structured argument, and makes a significant contribution to the field. The problem is important, the approach is novel, and the results advance the state of the art in high-dimensional statistical inference.

Pro: Novel approach to high-dimensional EM with sparsity; first unified framework for estimation and inference; semiparametric efficiency; thorough theoretical analysis; clear comparison with related work.

Con: Limited empirical validation; some assumptions may be restrictive; truncation step could be better motivated.

This paper represents a major advance in the understanding of EM algorithms in high-dimensional settings. The proposed framework opens up new possibilities for statistical inference in high-dimensional latent variable models, which have numerous applications in genomics, image analysis, and other fields. I believe this work is highly significant and should be accepted at NIPS.
