This paper tackles a challenging and important problem: extending the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm to the high-dimensional setting. The authors correctly identify the gap between the practical ubiquity of EM and the lack of theoretical guarantees when *d* > *n*. Their approach, incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, is novel and potentially impactful. The paper is ambitious and presents a strong theoretical framework, establishing convergence rates and asymptotic normality under reasonable conditions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of the truncation step within the EM algorithm is a genuinely new idea, addressing a core limitation of existing theory. This is not simply an application of existing high-dimensional statistical tools to EM; it fundamentally alters the algorithm.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, deriving statistical rates of convergence and establishing asymptotic normality for the estimator and the resulting score statistic. The connection to optimal estimation rates is clearly articulated.
*   **Generality:** The framework is presented in a general way, applicable to a range of latent variable models (Gaussian Mixture and Mixture of Regression are used as examples).
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The authors clearly articulate the problem and its significance, highlighting the gap between computation and statistical guarantees.
*   **Optimality Results:** Showing the asymptotic normality and optimality of the score statistic is a strong contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technicality & Accessibility:** The paper is *very* technically dense. While rigor is appreciated, the sheer number of conditions and the complexity of the derivations may make it inaccessible to a broader audience. More intuitive explanations or guiding examples within the theoretical sections could improve clarity.
*   **Empirical Validation Limited:** The paper focuses almost exclusively on theoretical results. While demonstrating the theory on more than just the two examples (GMM and MoR) isn't strictly *required*, some limited empirical validation (even simulations) showing the practical benefits of the truncation step – demonstrating improved performance compared to standard EM, or illustrating the sparsity induced – would substantially strengthen the work. It's hard to gauge the practical impact without seeing it in action.
*   **Conditions are Numerous:** While the authors attempt to verify their conditions for specific models, the sheer number of conditions (3.1, 3.2, 3.3, 4.1-4.5) feels burdensome. A more streamlined set of assumptions, or a clear discussion of their necessity, would be helpful.
*   **Sparsity Assumption:** The requirement for the w vector in the inference section to be sparse could be seen as a limiting factor. While justified by the authors, it’s worth considering the implications and potential sensitivity to this assumption.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a truly novel approach to a long-standing problem.
*   The theoretical guarantees are strong and provide a solid foundation for further research.
*   The potential impact on the field of latent variable modeling is significant.
*   The work is ambitious and tackles a difficult challenge.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The extreme technicality and lack of empirical validation may limit its immediate impact.
*   The numerous conditions raise questions about the practical applicability of the theory.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant theoretical contribution. While the density of the technical details and the limited empirical validation are weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the proposed approach outweigh these concerns. The paper introduces a fundamentally new way to think about EM in high dimensions, and the resulting theoretical guarantees are impressive. I am inclined to recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors should consider adding some limited empirical results and, if possible, streamlining the presentation of their theoretical results for broader accessibility in a future version.  This paper pushes boundaries and, despite imperfections, feels like a valuable addition to the NIPS program.
