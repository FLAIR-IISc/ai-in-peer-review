Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models via a modified EM algorithm. The core algorithm augments the classical EM with a truncation step (hard-thresholding) after each M-step to enforce sparsity. Two M-step implementations are covered: exact maximization and a single gradient-ascent step. The main computational-statistical theorem decomposes the estimation error into an optimization term that contracts geometrically and a statistical term of order √(s*)·√(log d/n) (up to logs), yielding near-minimax rates once optimization error vanishes. Beyond point estimation, the paper proposes a decorrelated score (and Wald) procedure for inference on low-dimensional components while treating the rest as high-dimensional nuisance. The score uses a Dantzig-type estimator of a “decorrelating” vector w built from derivatives of the Q-function; the resulting test is asymptotically normal with variance achieving the semiparametric information bound. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

This work builds on the population-to-sample EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017), extending from low to high dimension. It also connects to desparsified/debiased inference in high-dimensional M-estimation (e.g., van de Geer et al., Javanmard & Montanari) by crafting a decorrelated score adapted to latent variable models, where global convexity and global optima are unavailable. Compared to tensor methods for latent-variable models, which often have suboptimal rates and focus on low-dimensional settings, this work targets high-dimensional regimes and achieves sharper rates under sparsity. It also relates to penalized EM for mixtures (e.g., Städler et al., Khalili & Chen), but goes further by (i) analyzing an explicitly sparse EM iterate sequence and (ii) providing asymptotic inference.

Quality
- Theory: The main convergence result (Theorem 3.4) is technically careful: it requires smoothness/strong concavity of the population Q in a basin, Lipschitz-type conditions tailored to the chosen M-step, and a fine-grained ℓ1 statistical error between sample and population M-steps. The use of a truncation step necessitates entrywise control; this is a nontrivial refinement over prior ℓ2 analyses. The inference result (Theorem 4.6) leverages an identity relating Q-derivatives to the likelihood score and Fisher information (Theorem 2.1), and shows asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency under concentration and Lipschitz conditions on Tn and an ℓ∞-rate for the estimator.
- Assumptions: The results rely on (a) suitable initialization in a basin of attraction, (b) sparsity of the parameter and also sparsity of the decorrelation vector w*, (c) tuning bs at the right order s*, and (d) a resampling version of the algorithm to simplify analysis. For concrete models, additional conditions appear (e.g., SNR for GMM). These assumptions are fairly standard in high-dimensional nonconvex estimation, but some are strong in practice (see weaknesses).
- Correctness: The claims are appropriately qualified. The results for GMMs demonstrate the expected √(s* log d/n) rate (up to logs). The asymptotic normality result clearly states the needed scaling conditions on s*, s*w, and n.

Clarity
The high-level ideas are clear, and the decomposition into optimization vs statistical error is well presented. The paper is notation-heavy; several definitions are deferred to appendices, and the manuscript contains numerous typos/formatting glitches (e.g., missing spaces, broken references, indexing). The resampling assumption is only mentioned briefly; its role and the path to a single-sample analysis could be clarified. The discussion of tuning (bs, step size, Dantzig parameter) is mostly asymptotic; more guidance would help practitioners.

Originality
- Novelty lies in (i) introducing a sparsity-enforcing truncation step within EM and analyzing its support evolution and entrywise errors, and (ii) developing decorrelated-score inference for latent variable models using EM-based estimators, achieving the semiparametric bound. To my knowledge, inference for EM estimators in high dimensions under latent structures has not been established previously.
- Relation to existing methods like ℓ1-penalized EM and iterative hard-thresholding (IHT) is mentioned only tangentially; drawing explicit connections would strengthen the originality narrative.

Significance
If broadly correct, the framework is impactful: many applications use mixtures or other latent structures in high dimensions, and both estimation guarantees and valid inference are valuable. The combination of computational and inferential theory for nonconvex likelihoods is timely. The lack of empirical evaluation weakens immediate practical impact, but the theoretical advances are significant.

Strengths
- Clear computational-statistical tradeoff with geometric contraction and near-optimal statistical rate.
- Handles both exact and gradient M-steps under unified conditions.
- Decorrelated score tailored to EM/latent variable settings; achieves semiparametric efficiency.
- Verification for two canonical models, including explicit rates and SNR conditions.
- Careful entrywise statistical error analysis to control truncation effects.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practicality of assumptions:
  - Initialization: The theory assumes starting within a basin. Please provide concrete, data-driven initialization schemes (e.g., spectral/tensor, k-means++, method-of-moments) with guarantees that enter the basin under your conditions.
  - Choice of bs: The theory requires bs ≈ s*. Discuss data-driven selection (e.g., BIC/CV, stability selection) and robustness when bs is mis-specified.
  - Sparsity of w*: The inference requires s*w = o(√(n/log d)). Provide intuition and concrete verification for the models considered, and discuss when this fails.
- Resampling for analysis: Theorems are proved for a resampling version to decouple iterates. Please clarify whether analogous bounds hold for the practical single-sample algorithm, or outline how to extend the proofs (e.g., via empirical process/martingale techniques).
- Computational burden: Each iteration requires solving a Dantzig program for w; discuss complexity, alternatives (nodewise Lasso), and frequency (once vs per-iteration).
- Comparisons and experiments:
  - No empirical results are provided. Simulations on GMM/MoR comparing (a) truncated-EM (both M-step variants), (b) ℓ1-penalized EM, (c) gradient EM with soft-thresholding, and (d) tensor/Spectral methods would substantiate claims, including inference coverage.
  - Ablations on initialization quality, bs, and SNR would be informative.
- Relation to IHT/projection methods: The T-step is akin to IHT/projection onto sparse sets. Please relate to classical IHT theory and clarify differences due to EM’s nonconvex surrogate.
- Conditions: Some Lipschitz/concavity conditions on population Q may be strong; provide intuition on their validity and scaling for the covered models. The GMM requires high SNR; can the method tolerate moderate overlap?
- Tuning guidance: Provide finite-sample choices for step sizes, number of iterations T, and Dantzig tuning in terms of n,d, and data-driven estimators of variance.
- Minor: Clean up references (several are incomplete or malformed) and typos; ensure all notation (e.g., norms, subscript conventions) is defined in the main text.

Overall recommendation
This is a strong theoretical contribution on high-dimensional EM with both estimation and inference guarantees. With added clarity and at least a modest empirical section, it would be a solid fit for NeurIPS. As it stands, I lean toward acceptance on theoretical merit, but I urge the authors to address the practicality and exposition issues above.
