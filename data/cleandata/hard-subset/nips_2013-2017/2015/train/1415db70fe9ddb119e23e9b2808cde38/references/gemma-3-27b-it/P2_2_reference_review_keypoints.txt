- Summary: Proposes a truncated EM algorithm for high-dimensional latent variable models and establishes near-optimal convergence rates and asymptotic normality for a decorrelated score statistic.

- Strength — Important Problem: Addresses the lack of theoretical guarantees for EM in high-dimensional settings, a key practical bottleneck.

- Strength — Novel Approach: Introduces a truncation step to enforce sparsity, enabling theoretical analysis in high dimensions.

- Strength — Strong Theoretical Results: Provides rigorous statistical and computational guarantees, including a near-optimal rate (p s* · log d / n) and asymptotic normality.

- Strength — Generality: Framework applies broadly to latent variable models, with concrete examples for Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions.

- Strength — Connections to Existing Work: Clearly positions contributions relative to prior results (e.g., [2]) and highlights novel extensions.

- Strength — Writing and Organization: Paper is well-written, with clear notation and concise algorithm presentation.

- Weakness — Technical Complexity: Analysis is highly involved and relies on many technical conditions, making it hard to digest.

- Weakness — Condition Verification: Verification of conditions is deferred to the appendix; practical implications and ease of satisfying them need more discussion.

- Weakness — Constant Factors: Hidden constants in asymptotic rates are not discussed, though they can crucially affect practical performance.

- Weakness — Sparsity Assumption: Reliance on strong sparsity (including w* being sparse) is restrictive and may limit applicability.

- Weakness — Limited Empirical Evaluation: Lacks empirical results to validate theoretical findings and demonstrate practical performance.

- Overall Assessment: Strong and significant theoretical contribution with thorough analysis, though practical clarifications would improve impact.

- Arguments for Acceptance: Addresses a fundamental problem, provides strong guarantees, proposes a novel truncation approach, advances prior work, and is well-written.

- Arguments Against Acceptance: High technical complexity, strong sparsity assumptions, and lack of empirical validation.

- Quality (Qualitative): Excellent — technically sound and complete.

- Clarity (Qualitative): Good — generally clear but technically demanding.

- Originality (Qualitative): Excellent — highly original approach and results.

- Significance (Qualitative): Excellent — important potential impact on research and practice.

- Recommendation: Strong Accept — accept for publication, with suggested improvements on constants and empirical validation.
