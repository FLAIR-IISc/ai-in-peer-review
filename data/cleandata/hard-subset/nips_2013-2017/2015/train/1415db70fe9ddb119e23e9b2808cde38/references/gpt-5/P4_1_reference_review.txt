Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies parameter estimation and inference for latent-variable models in the high-dimensional regime (d ≫ n). It proposes a “high-dimensional EM” (HD-EM) algorithm that augments the classical EM with a truncation step that keeps the bs largest-magnitude coordinates after each M-step (either exact maximization or a single gradient ascent step). Under a unified framework, the analysis decomposes the estimation error into (i) an optimization error that contracts geometrically (given initialization in a basin of attraction) and (ii) a statistical error of order √(s* log d/n) up to additional logarithmic factors, where s* is the sparsity of the true parameter. The paper further develops an inferential procedure based on a decorrelated score constructed from the Q-function and a Dantzig-type estimator for a nuisance direction, showing asymptotic normality with variance equal to the semiparametric efficiency bound for the low-dimensional target parameter in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of linear regressions.

This work builds on and extends population-to-sample EM analyses in the low-dimensional regime (e.g., Balakrishnan, Wainwright, Yu, 2017) by introducing sparsity via hard truncation and developing entrywise concentration for the sample M-step. For inference, it adapts decorrelated/debiased methodology (e.g., Zhang & Zhang, 2014; van de Geer et al., 2014; Javanmard & Montanari, 2014) to latent variable models using derivatives of the EM Q-function, related to the score and Fisher information (Theorem 2.1). Compared to l1-penalized EM for mixtures (e.g., Städler, Bühlmann, van de Geer, 2010; Khalili & Chen, 2007), the analysis here targets the actual algorithmic estimator (not a global optimum) and provides both estimation and inference guarantees. Tensor methods for mixtures (e.g., spectral experts) are discussed and contrasted as yielding slower statistical rates in high dimensions.

Strengths:
- Unified and clear algorithmic framework (E/M/T-steps) accommodating exact and gradient M-steps.
- Finite-sample error decomposition with geometric contraction plus near-minimax statistical term √(s* log d/n) (up to logs).
- Entrywise statistical error control tailored to the truncation step; explicit sparse support evolution considerations.
- Inference procedure based on a decorrelated score that is asymptotically normal and efficient (achieves semiparametric information bound).
- General connection between Q-derivatives and the actual score/Fisher information (Theorem 2.1).
- Instantiations for Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions with verification of conditions and concrete rates.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:

Quality (technical soundness):
- The main estimation theorem (Theorem 3.4) is proved for a resampling/split-sample version of the algorithm (Algorithm 4, not shown in the main text). It is not clear whether analogous guarantees hold for the standard HD-EM that reuses all data at each iteration. The implications for statistical efficiency and practical performance should be clarified, and ideally a proof for the non-resampling variant (with dependency handling) should be provided or at least discussed.
- Initialization: the results require an initializer within a constant relative l2-error of θ*. Practical initialization strategies in high dimensions for mixtures are not detailed. Given the critique of tensor methods, guidance on feasible initializers (and their guarantees) would strengthen the contribution.
- Choice of truncation level: bs must be on the order of s* (Assumption 3.8) to control truncation error. The method’s robustness to misspecification (bs > s* or bs < s*) and practical selection (e.g., via cross-validation or information criteria) should be discussed. Currently, theoretical guarantees rely on knowing s* up to a constant factor.
- Possible inconsistency in the Gaussian mixture example: it is stated that the Lipschitz constant 1 equals exp(C r^2) with μ=ν=1, yet Theorem 3.4 requires ρ1 = 1/ν ∈ (0,1). If 1 grows exponentially in r^2, then ρ1>1, contradicting contraction. This may be a sign/constant error (perhaps exp(−C r^2) was intended). Please check and correct.
- The Dantzig tuning parameter for inference requires bounds involving quantities like ∥w*∥1 and concentration terms (Assumption 4.5). Practical tuning guidelines and sensitivity would be helpful.
- Computational aspects of inference: solving a Dantzig selector each time adds cost; the complexity per iteration and per test (and scalability with d) should be discussed.
- The inference framework closely resembles the general decorrelated score approach for high-dimensional M-estimators (e.g., Ning & Liu, 2017, “A General Theory of Hypothesis Testing for High-Dimensional Models”). The paper should explicitly relate its approach to that line of work, emphasizing what is new in the latent-variable/EM context (use of Q-derivatives and nonconvex algorithmic estimators).

Clarity:
- Overall organization is reasonable, but several parts rely heavily on appendices (Algorithm 4; verifications for models). A concise summary of key conditions (with intuition) in the main text and a brief description of the resampling scheme would improve readability.
- Notation is dense. Clarify Tn(·) and the roles of r^2_{1,1}Q, r^2_{1,2}Q in the decorrelated score; a short derivation sketch in the main text would help.
- Provide explicit stopping criteria and how to choose T in practice (e.g., until the optimization error falls below the statistical floor).

Originality:
- The truncation-after-M-step mechanism is closely related to iterative hard thresholding; its adaptation and analysis for EM in latent-variable models in high dimensions appears novel.
- The inference component is a meaningful extension of decorrelated score ideas to the EM/Q-function setting and to nonconvex algorithmic estimators rather than global optima.

Significance:
- Provides a general blueprint for bringing both optimal high-dimensional estimation and valid inference to a broad class of latent variable models, with strong potential impact.
- The necessity of initialization and resampling assumptions may limit immediate applicability; addressing these would enhance significance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and underexplored gap: high-dimensional EM with both estimation and inference guarantees.
- Offers near-minimax rates and semiparametric efficiency.
- Conceptually clean framework applicable to multiple latent models; aligns with NeurIPS interests in statistical-computational tradeoffs.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Reliance on resampling for theory; lack of guarantees for the standard algorithm on a single dataset.
- Strong assumptions (accurate initialization, knowledge of s*, basin concavity) with limited practical guidance.
- Possible inconsistency in constants for contraction in the Gaussian mixture example.
- Limited empirical validation in the provided text.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify and, if possible, extend theory beyond the resampling version to the standard full-sample HD-EM.
- Provide practical initialization methods and selection strategies for bs and tuning parameters, with empirical sensitivity analysis.
- Correct and explain the contraction constant for the GMM example.
- Discuss computational complexity and scalability.
- Add explicit discussion of connections to general decorrelated score theory (e.g., Ning & Liu, 2017) and to iterative hard-thresholding literature.
