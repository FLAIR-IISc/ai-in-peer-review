### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a significant and long-standing gap in the literature: the lack of theoretical guarantees for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in the high-dimensional setting where the dimension `d` is much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors propose a novel "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the classic EM by introducing a truncation step after the M-step to enforce sparsity at each iteration.

The core contributions are twofold. First, the paper provides a unified theoretical framework for analyzing this algorithm, proving that with a proper initialization, the iterates converge at a geometric rate to a solution that achieves the near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`. This is a major advance over prior work, such as Balakrishnan et al. [2], which was restricted to the low-dimensional regime. Second, and perhaps more impressively, the paper moves beyond point estimation to tackle the problem of statistical inference. It introduces a "decorrelated score statistic" for the estimator produced by their algorithm, proves its asymptotic normality, and shows that its limiting variance is optimal, achieving the semiparametric information bound. The general framework is demonstrated on two important latent variable models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is deep and appears to be sound, successfully navigating the challenges posed by the non-convexity of the likelihood function and the introduction of the non-linear truncation operator. The authors adeptly combine techniques from the analysis of EM as a proximal/gradient method with tools from high-dimensional statistics. The claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs, which, while deferred to the appendix, lay out a clear and comprehensive argument. The theoretical results are sharp, establishing not only geometric convergence rates for optimization but also near-optimal rates for statistical estimation.

However, the analysis relies on a few idealizations. The theory requires a good initialization within a basin of attraction, a common but important caveat in non-convex optimization that is not fully addressed from a practical standpoint. Furthermore, the main theorems are proven for a "resampling version" of the algorithm, a theoretical device used to ensure independence across iterations. While this simplifies the proof, it creates a small gap between the analyzed algorithm and the one that would be implemented in practice. These are, however, relatively minor points in the context of the overall contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, though technically very dense. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work, clearly explaining the limitations of previous approaches and motivating their new algorithm and analysis. The problem setup, the proposed algorithm, and the main theoretical results (Theorems 3.4 and 4.6) are stated with precision. The notation, while extensive, is necessary for the topic. The main text would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the decorrelated score test, some of which is currently in the appendix. Nonetheless, for an expert reader in high-dimensional statistics, the paper is clear and the arguments are followable.

**Originality:** The originality of this work is exceptionally high. While building on the conceptual framework of analyzing EM as an optimization algorithm [2], the adaptation to the high-dimensional setting is highly non-trivial and novel. The key idea of integrating a hard thresholding step within the EM iterations and successfully analyzing the entire dynamic process is a significant conceptual leap.

Furthermore, developing a valid method for statistical inference (hypothesis testing) for the output of a non-convex iterative procedure like this high-dimensional EM is a frontier research topic. Existing methods for high-dimensional inference (e.g., debiased Lasso) typically rely on the KKT conditions of a convex program. This paper breaks new ground by developing an analogous theory for a local optimum of a non-convex problem, which is a far more challenging setting. This is a bold and innovative contribution.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is substantial. The EM algorithm is one of the most widely used algorithms in statistics and machine learning, but its application in modern high-dimensional settings has largely lacked theoretical justification. This work provides the first rigorous, end-to-end framework for both estimation and inference with EM when `d >> n`. This is a foundational result that could unlock theoretically-grounded applications of latent variable models in fields like genomics, computational neuroscience, and economics, where high-dimensional data is the norm. The proposed methods and, perhaps more importantly, the analytical techniques are likely to be influential and inspire a new line of research into other iterative algorithms for high-dimensional non-convex problems.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper provides the first comprehensive theoretical guarantees (optimal estimation and valid inference) for the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional regime. This is a breakthrough result for a fundamental algorithm.
2.  **Bold Approach:** It successfully tackles the twin challenges of non-convexity and high-dimensionality, extending complex ideas from high-dimensional inference to the output of an iterative algorithm, not a one-shot M-estimator.
3.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The analysis is rigorous and establishes sharp, near-optimal statistical and computational rates.
4.  **Broad Impact:** The results are general and have immediate implications for a wide range of popular latent variable models, addressing a critical need in many scientific fields.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Reliance on a Good Initialization:** The theory is local and depends on starting within a basin of attraction, without providing a guaranteed method for finding such an initialization.
2.  **Analysis of a Resampled Algorithm:** The theoretical guarantees are for a slightly modified version of the algorithm, which may not perfectly reflect the performance of the standard implementation.

### Conclusion

This is an outstanding paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the theory of both the EM algorithm and high-dimensional statistics. The authors tackle a difficult and important problem with a novel approach and provide a remarkably complete set of theoretical results, covering both optimal estimation and valid inference. While the analysis relies on standard (but strong) assumptions like a good initialization, this does not diminish the importance of the conceptual and technical breakthrough. The paper's novelty and potential for impact are extremely high. It is a trailblazing work that I expect to be highly influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
