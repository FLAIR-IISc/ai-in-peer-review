## Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a significant open problem: providing theoretical guarantees for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models. Existing theoretical work largely focuses on low-dimensional settings. The authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, and subsequently establish statistical and computational guarantees, including a near-optimal convergence rate and asymptotic normality of a decorrelated score statistic for low-dimensional parameters. The work builds on recent advances in understanding the convergence of EM in low dimensions (e.g., [2]) but introduces crucial innovations to address the challenges posed by high dimensionality. The paper provides a unified framework applicable to Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models, with detailed theoretical analysis.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical gap:** The lack of theoretical guarantees for EM in high dimensions is a major limitation in the field. This paper directly addresses this issue.
* **Novel algorithmic approach:** The introduction of the truncation step is a clever and effective way to enforce sparsity and enable theoretical analysis.  This is a key contribution.
* **Strong theoretical results:** The convergence rate of p√(s* log(d/n)) is near-optimal, and establishing asymptotic normality in this high-dimensional setting is a significant achievement. The separation of optimization and statistical error is also a clear and useful contribution.
* **Unified framework:** The applicability to multiple latent variable models demonstrates the generality of the proposed approach.
* **Clear connections to existing work:** The authors carefully position their work relative to the existing literature, clearly outlining the improvements and novel contributions.  The connection to perturbed gradient methods [2] is well articulated.
* **Detailed analysis:** The paper provides a comprehensive and detailed theoretical analysis, including careful conditions and proofs (deferred to appendices).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conditionality:** The results heavily rely on a number of conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5). While the authors state they will be verified for specific models in the appendix, the complexity of these conditions makes them somewhat difficult to assess at a high level.  A more intuitive explanation of their necessity and relationship to model properties would be beneficial.
* **Practicality of Tuning Parameters:** The practical selection of tuning parameters (e.g., *bs*, step size ⌘, and the parameter in the Dantzig selector) is not explicitly discussed beyond mentioning their dependence on theoretical quantities. Providing guidance on how these parameters could be estimated or chosen in practice would significantly enhance the paper's applicability.
* **Computational Cost:** While the theoretical convergence rate is promising, the truncation step introduces additional computational cost. A discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm and potential optimization strategies to mitigate this cost would be valuable.
* **Sparsity Assumption:** The assumption of sparsity (both in the true parameter and the Dantzig selector’s solution) is quite strong. While common in high-dimensional statistics, its impact on the applicability of the method should be clearly acknowledged and discussed.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-supported claims. The completeness is good; while detailed proofs are in the appendix, the main text provides a clear outline of the methodology and results. The authors demonstrate careful consideration of both strengths and weaknesses of their work.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and organized, though the theoretical sections are dense and require careful reading. The notation is relatively standard, but the numerous conditions and definitions can be overwhelming. Providing more intuitive explanations alongside the technical details would improve clarity.

**Originality:**

The combination of the truncated EM algorithm and the resulting theoretical guarantees is highly original. While building on existing work, the authors make a significant contribution to the field by extending theoretical guarantees to the high-dimensional setting.

**Significance:**

The results have the potential to be highly significant. Establishing theoretical guarantees for EM in high dimensions opens up new possibilities for applying this widely used algorithm to a broader range of problems. The development of a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference is also a valuable contribution.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental open problem in statistical learning.
* Provides a novel and theoretically justified algorithm for high-dimensional latent variable models.
* Establishes near-optimal statistical rates and asymptotic normality.
* The work is well-motivated, carefully analyzed, and clearly presented (despite some density).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong assumptions and potentially complex tuning parameter selection could limit the practical applicability of the method.
* A more thorough discussion of the computational cost and scalability of the algorithm would be beneficial.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  Despite some minor weaknesses, this paper represents a significant advancement in the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm and its application to high-dimensional latent variable models. The originality, technical rigor, and potential impact of the work make it a valuable contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly providing more practical guidance on parameter selection and discussing the computational implications of the truncation step.
