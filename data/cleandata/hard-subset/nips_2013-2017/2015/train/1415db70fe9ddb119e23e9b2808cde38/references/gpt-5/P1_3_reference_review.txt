Summary
The paper studies parameter estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models via a modified EM algorithm. The main algorithmic idea is to add a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after each E/M step to enforce sparsity along the solution path. Two M-step implementations are covered: exact maximization and a single gradient ascent step. Under high-level conditions on the population Q-function (Lipschitz-type gradients and local strong concavity/smoothness), a statistical error condition on the sample M-map in ℓ1, and an initialization within a constant relative error, Theorem 3.4 bounds the ℓ2 error at iteration t by a geometric “optimization error” plus a statistical error of order √(s*)·ε, where ε is an entrywise (ℓ∞/ℓ1) deviation between M and Mn. For the Gaussian mixture and mixture-of-regressions examples, ε ≍ √(log d / n) (up to model-dependent factors), yielding a near-minimax rate √(s* log d / n) for the final estimator.

For inference, the paper proposes a decorrelated score statistic tailored to the EM objective by exploiting identities linking rQ and the likelihood score/Fisher information (Theorem 2.1). A Dantzig-type estimator of the nuisance projection vector w is used to construct a score orthogonal to the high-dimensional nuisance. Under Conditions 4.1–4.4 and Assumption 4.5, Theorem 4.6 establishes asymptotic normality with variance achieving the semiparametric information bound, enabling valid tests/confidence intervals for low-dimensional components.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on recent analyses of EM in low dimensions (Balakrishnan et al.) by introducing sparsity-aware truncation and ℓ1/entrywise control necessary in high-d settings. It connects to debiasing/desparsified inference (Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.) by adapting decorrelation to the EM/Q-function setting where estimators are not solutions of convex programs. It contrasts with tensor methods, arguing improved rates and applicability when d ≫ n, and discusses earlier EM analyses for GMMs and mixtures. The truncation step is reminiscent of iterative hard thresholding in sparse optimization.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: A unified framework that couples a high-dimensional EM with hard thresholding and provides simultaneous computational (geometric contraction) and statistical guarantees with near-minimax rates.
- Inference contribution: A decorrelated score tailored to EM, with asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance.
- Generality: Covers both exact and gradient ascent M-steps, with model checks provided for GMM and mixture-of-regressions.
- Careful separation of optimization vs statistical errors, and explicit entrywise control enabling analysis of the truncation step.
- Clear articulation of assumptions (basin of attraction, Lipschitz-gradient variants, concavity/smoothness, and Tn concentration).

Weaknesses and limitations
- Sample splitting/resampling: Main convergence theorem refers to a resampling version (Algorithm 4 in the appendix). It is unclear whether the guarantees hold for the standard single-sample EM iterates without sample splitting. This is important both practically and theoretically; many recent works remove sample-splitting with more delicate stability arguments.
- Initialization: Results require an initializer within a constant relative error. While common in EM analyses, the paper does not provide a concrete, computationally tractable initialization strategy in high dimensions that respects sparsity (e.g., moment/tensor-based, spectral, or IHT-style warm starts), nor quantify conditions under which such an initializer exists when d ≫ n.
- Tuning and practicality: The choice of bs requires knowledge of s* up to constants. Guidance for adaptive selection (e.g., via cross-validation or information criteria) is not discussed. The gradient ascent stepsize uses unknown µ, ν. The Dantzig tuning parameter depends on quantities (ξ’s) not directly available; practical calibration is not addressed.
- Assumptions on w*: The sparsity of the nuisance projection vector w* (s*w = o(√(n/log d))) is crucial for inference. While standard in high-dimensional inference, its plausibility for general latent variable models is not discussed; only GMM/mixture regression are sketched.
- Empirical validation: No experiments are presented to assess finite-sample performance, robustness to misspecification, sensitivity to tuning/initialization, or coverage of the proposed tests. For NeurIPS, even theory-focused work typically includes simulations.
- Clarity/typos: The manuscript has numerous formatting issues (e.g., symbols “>” for transpose, missing years, garbled authors/titles in references), and heavy notation that sometimes obscures the main ideas. The role of the T-step and how bs influences support evolution could be explained more intuitively.

Quality
The theoretical development is generally careful and consistent with recent techniques. However, reliance on sample-splitting and strong initialization makes the practical impact less clear. Model-specific verifications are partially deferred; more explicit statements for mixture of regressions (beyond GMM) would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
Organization is reasonable, but readability suffers from dense notation, minor typesetting errors, and a terse main text with many details deferred. Adding a high-level roadmap, intuitive explanations of the key conditions, and practical guidance would help.

Originality
Combining EM with truncation and delivering both estimation and inference guarantees for high-dimensional latent variable models appears novel. The decorrelated score in the latent-variable/EM setting is a meaningful extension of debiasing.

Significance
If the results can be made to work without resampling and with practical initialization/tuning, this could become a standard approach for sparse latent variable models. The inference contribution is timely and potentially impactful.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you extend the analysis to the standard (no resampling) EM with a uniform concentration/stability argument? Even a weaker result would be valuable.
- Provide a concrete initialization method with guarantees (e.g., sparse spectral/tensor warm start limited to O(1) accuracy), and discuss its sample complexity.
- Give practical guidance for choosing bs, stepsize, and the Dantzig tuning. Are there data-driven rules?
- Discuss the computational cost of solving the Dantzig program for w at scale; is a Lasso surrogate adequate?
- Add simulations for GMM/mixture regression to verify rates, selection accuracy, and coverage of the decorrelated score/Wald intervals, and to compare against penalized likelihood and tensor baselines.
- Improve presentation and fix references/notation.

Pros
- Solid and novel theory for high-dimensional EM with sparsity.
- Asymptotically optimal inference for low-dimensional components.
- Applicable to important models (GMM, mixture regression).

Cons
- Depends on sample splitting and nontrivial initialization.
- Limited practical guidance and no empirical results.
- Strong sparsity assumption on w*; generality beyond examples is unclear.
- Presentation issues.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The work advances the theory of computation and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models in a meaningful way. However, the absence of experiments, reliance on resampling, and practical tuning/initialization gaps weaken the case. With added empirical validation and clarification of the above points, this would be a strong paper.
