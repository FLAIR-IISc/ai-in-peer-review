**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper presents a theoretical framework for parameter estimation and statistical inference using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in the high-dimensional sparse setting, where the dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the classic EM algorithm by adding a hard truncation (T-step) after the M-step to enforce sparsity at each iteration.

The main contributions are twofold:
1.  **Estimation Guarantees:** The paper proves that, with a suitable initialization, the proposed algorithm's iterates converge at a geometric rate to a final estimator. This estimator achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d)/n)`, where `s*` is the true sparsity level.
2.  **Inference Guarantees:** The authors develop a "decorrelated score statistic" based on the estimator produced by their algorithm. They prove this statistic is asymptotically normal, which enables hypothesis testing and the construction of confidence intervals for low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. The limiting variance of their statistic is shown to be optimal, achieving the semiparametric information bound.

This work builds directly upon recent theoretical analyses of the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional setting, particularly Balakrishnan et al. (2017) [2], which viewed EM as a perturbed gradient method. The key novelty here is the adaptation to the high-dimensional regime. This requires introducing the truncation step, which poses significant new challenges for the analysis. Unlike the `l2`-norm analysis in [2], this paper requires a more fine-grained, entrywise (`l-infinity`) characterization of the statistical error to control the effect of truncation.

Furthermore, this work significantly extends the growing literature on high-dimensional inference. Most existing methods for inference (e.g., debiased/desparsified Lasso) rely on estimators that are global optima of convex, penalized objective functions. This paper's contribution is highly original because it provides a rigorous inferential theory for an estimator that is the output of an iterative algorithm for a non-concave likelihood function, and which is not guaranteed to be a global optimum.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of fundamental importance. The EM algorithm is a workhorse in statistics and machine learning for latent variable models, yet its theoretical properties in the modern high-dimensional setting were largely unknown. Providing the first framework that guarantees both optimal estimation and valid statistical inference for a computationally feasible EM-type algorithm in this regime is a major contribution.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are novel and appear sound. The unified analytic framework, based on a set of abstract conditions that are then verified for specific models (Gaussian Mixture Models, Mixture of Regressions), is elegant and powerful. The analysis required to handle the truncation step and establish the entrywise error bounds is non-trivial. The development of a decorrelated score test for a local optimum of a non-concave problem, rather than a global optimum of a convex one, is a significant conceptual advance for high-dimensional inference.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation and clearly situates the work within the literature. The problem, proposed algorithm, and main theoretical results are presented concisely and clearly in the initial sections. The decomposition of the estimation error into an "Optimization Error" and a "Statistical Error" (Eq. 1.1) provides great intuition.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would be significantly more impactful with simulation studies. Experiments could validate the key theoretical findings, such as: (a) Does the algorithm converge at the predicted geometric rate in practice? (b) Does the final estimator achieve the `sqrt(s* log(d)/n)` error rate? (c) Do the confidence intervals constructed from the decorrelated score test achieve the nominal coverage level (e.g., 95%)? Without experiments, the practical performance of the proposed method remains undemonstrated.

2.  **Practicality of Assumptions:**
    *   **Initialization:** The theory relies on an initial estimator that is already within a constant relative error of the true parameter. This is a common assumption in non-convex optimization theory but is a critical practical hurdle. The paper provides no discussion on how to obtain such an initialization. A brief mention of potential methods (e.g., spectral methods) and their own requirements would strengthen the paper.
    *   **Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm requires a sparsity parameter `bs`, and the inference procedure requires a tuning parameter `lambda`. The theoretical choices for these parameters (e.g., Eq. 3.8 for `bs`) depend on unknown quantities like the true sparsity `s*` and other problem-dependent constants. The paper does not discuss how these could be chosen in a data-driven way (e.g., via cross-validation), which is essential for practical application.

3.  **Heavy Reliance on Appendix:** While necessary for a conference paper, many crucial details are deferred to the appendix. For example, the verification of the abstract conditions (3.1, 3.2, 3.3) for the specific models is the technical core of the application, but it is not presented in the main text. This makes it difficult to fully evaluate the correctness and implications of the results for the example models without a deep dive into the supplemental material.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a long-standing, important open problem at the intersection of optimization, statistics, and machine learning.
*   Provides the first comprehensive theoretical framework for both optimal estimation and valid inference for the EM algorithm in high dimensions.
*   The technical contributions, particularly the analysis of the truncated EM and the development of inference for its output, are highly novel and significant.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-structured, presenting a general and extensible framework.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The complete lack of experiments is a significant weakness. It leaves the practical performance and applicability of the proposed methods unevaluated.
*   The discussion on the practical feasibility of key assumptions (especially initialization and tuning parameter selection) is missing.

**Overall Recommendation:**
Despite the lack of experiments, the theoretical contributions of this paper are exceptionally strong, novel, and significant. It tackles a very difficult problem and provides a comprehensive and elegant solution. It opens up a new avenue for analyzing a classic algorithm in a modern, challenging setting. The paper's impact on future research in high-dimensional statistics and latent variable modeling is likely to be substantial. Therefore, I recommend acceptance. The authors should be strongly encouraged to include a discussion of the practical assumptions and, if possible, some simulation results in the final version.
