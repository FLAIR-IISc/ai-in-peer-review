This paper addresses the challenging problem of applying the EM algorithm to high-dimensional latent variable models, a setting where theoretical guarantees are largely absent. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and provide statistical and computational guarantees, including asymptotic normality for hypothesis testing. The work builds upon recent advances in understanding the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional regime and attempts to extend those guarantees to higher dimensions.

The paper is technically ambitious and tackles a significant problem. The proposed truncation step appears to be a reasonable approach for enforcing sparsity and achieving statistical guarantees in high dimensions. The theoretical results, particularly the convergence rate and the asymptotic normality of the decorrelated score statistic, are valuable contributions. However, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor issues, both in presentation and technical detail, that detract from its overall impact. 

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Addressing theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high dimensions is a crucial and timely research direction.
* **Novelty:** The proposed truncation step and the associated analysis represent a novel contribution.
* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The established convergence rate and asymptotic normality results are significant achievements.
* **Generality:** The framework is presented as applicable to a broad family of latent variable models.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Excessive Technicality & Notation:** The paper is dense and relies heavily on complex notation, making it difficult to follow even for experts in the field. Many definitions (e.g., k · k, numerous subscripts) are introduced without sufficient motivation or clear explanation.
* **Lack of Intuition:** While the mathematical results are presented rigorously, the underlying intuition behind the truncation step and its impact on convergence is not adequately explained.
* **Overly Stringent Conditions:** The numerous conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) feel overly restrictive and are difficult to verify in practice. The constant bombardment of conditions interrupts the flow of the paper and obscures the core ideas.
* **Formatting and Presentation:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies exist. For example, inconsistent use of boldface for variable names, unclear labeling of equations (e.g., (1.1) is immediately followed by lines of text *within* equation formatting), and redundant statements.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** Condition 3.3, in particular, feels weaker than necessary; using the `1`-norm difference might be overly strong when considering only the support of the estimator. The justification for the Dantzig selector tuning parameter in Assumption 4.5 is lacking.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the authors mention applications to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, the empirical evaluation is not presented *within* the main paper, merely referenced to an appendix. This makes it difficult to assess the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
* **Repetitive language**: Certain phrases and concepts are reiterated throughout, contributing to redundancy and hindering concise communication.

**Specific Comments:**

* **Page 1, Line 20:** "global optima" should be "global optimum". Throughout, careful editing for singular/plural consistency is needed.
* **Page 2, Line 1:** "⇤Research supported..." – This information is better suited for an acknowledgement section, not directly following the abstract.
* **Page 3, Equation (1.1):**  The notation is cramped and hard to read. Consider breaking it down or increasing spacing. The labels “Optimization Error” and “Statistical Error” are helpful but could be made more prominent.
* **Page 4, Algorithm 1:** The notation `trunc` and `supp` are not clearly defined *within* the algorithm itself.  A brief explanation alongside the function calls would improve readability.
* **Page 7, Condition 3.3:** The motivation for using the `1`-norm error in statistical error condition is weak.
* **Page 11, Assumption 4.5:** The condition on `s⇤w` seems very restrictive. Justification is needed.
* **Throughout:** The paper would greatly benefit from a more visual presentation of the key ideas. Diagrams illustrating the truncation step or the decorrelated score function could improve understanding.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to a challenging problem.
* Strong theoretical results with potentially broad applicability.
* Significant contribution to the understanding of the EM algorithm in high dimensions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Overly technical and difficult to follow.
* Excessive number of conditions and technical details.
* Formatting and presentation issues detract from the overall quality.
* Limited empirical validation.
* Numerous minor flaws that accumulate and hinder comprehension.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in high dimensions, its flaws in presentation and technical detail are substantial. The overwhelming complexity and numerous minor issues overshadow the strengths of the work. I believe that the paper, in its current form, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. A major revision addressing the clarity, presentation, and technical rigor is required. I would lean towards **rejection** at this time, but would be open to reconsidering a significantly revised version.
