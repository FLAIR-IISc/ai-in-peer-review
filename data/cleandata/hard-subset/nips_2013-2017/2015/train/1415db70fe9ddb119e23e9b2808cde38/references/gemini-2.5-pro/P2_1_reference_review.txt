### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for latent variable models in the high-dimensional setting (d >> n). The authors propose a novel "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which integrates a hard-thresholding (truncation) step into the standard EM procedure to enforce sparsity. The paper's two main contributions are: 1) providing statistical and computational guarantees for this algorithm, showing that with a good initialization, it converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves the near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`; and 2) developing a procedure for asymptotic inference (hypothesis testing) based on a "decorrelated score statistic" derived from the algorithm's output, which is shown to be asymptotically normal with an optimal limiting variance.

This work builds directly upon the line of research that provides theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional setting, most notably Balakrishnan et al. (2017) [Ref 2]. The authors adopt the same conceptual framework of viewing the EM algorithm as a perturbed version of a gradient-based method and analyzing its population-level dynamics. However, the extension to the high-dimensional regime is highly non-trivial. The key novelty is the introduction of the truncation step and the corresponding analysis, which requires a much finer-grained, entrywise (`l_inf`-norm) control of the statistical error, as opposed to the `l_2`-norm analysis sufficient for the low-dimensional case.

For the inference part, the work connects to the broader literature on high-dimensional inference for sparse models, such as the debiasing/desparsifying methods for the Lasso (e.g., Zhang & Zhang, 2014 [Ref 32]; van de Geer et al., 2014 [Ref 26]). The crucial distinction and originality lie in developing an inference theory for an estimator that is a *local optimum* of a *non-convex* optimization procedure (EM), whereas most existing work focuses on global optima of convex problems like the Lasso.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and challenging problem of high practical and theoretical importance. The EM algorithm is a workhorse in statistics and machine learning, yet its behavior in the modern high-dimensional setting has remained poorly understood. This work provides the first rigorous framework for both optimal point estimation and valid statistical inference for the EM algorithm when d >> n, a major theoretical advancement. The results could have a significant impact on how latent variable models are used and interpreted in fields like genomics, computational biology, and econometrics.

2.  **Originality:** The contributions are highly original. While building on existing frameworks, the specific techniques are novel. The modification of the EM algorithm with an iterative truncation step is a simple and elegant idea, but its theoretical analysis is complex and new. The development of a decorrelated score test for an estimator that is not a global optimum of a convex program is a particularly novel and impressive contribution.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results are strong and appear to be sound. The authors establish geometric convergence, a near-optimal statistical rate for the estimator, and asymptotic normality for their test statistic. The decomposition of the estimation error into an optimization component and a statistical component is clean and insightful. The general framework, based on a set of verifiable conditions, is powerful and demonstrates the applicability of the results to a class of models (illustrated with Gaussian Mixture Models and Mixture of Regressions).

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very dense and heavily reliant on abstract conditions and notation (e.g., Conditions 3.1, 3.2, 3.3, 4.1-4.4). While this is necessary for a general theoretical framework, it makes the core intuition difficult to grasp for a broader audience. The main text would benefit from more high-level explanations. For instance, a brief, intuitive paragraph explaining *why* the truncation step allows the analysis to go through and how it controls the error accumulation would be very helpful. Similarly, the intuition behind the decorrelated score test in Section 2.2 is deferred to the appendix; a concise summary in the main text would improve readability.

2.  **Strong Assumptions:** The analysis relies on several strong assumptions, which are common in this line of work but limit the practical applicability.
    *   **Initialization:** The theory requires an initial estimator that is already within a constant-factor relative error of the true parameter. This is a "local" analysis, and the critical question of how to *obtain* such an initialization (e.g., via a spectral method) is not addressed. A discussion of potential initialization strategies and their own theoretical guarantees would make the work more complete.
    *   **Sparsity of `w*`:** The inference theory requires the "decorrelating" vector `w*` to be sparse (Assumption 4.5). This is an assumption on the structure of the Fisher Information matrix and may not hold for all problems. It essentially assumes the nuisance parameters' influence on the parameter of interest is sparse, which is a significant structural limitation.
    *   **Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm requires knowledge of, or a good estimate for, the sparsity level `s*` to set the parameter `bs`. The inference procedure requires a tuning parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector. The paper provides theoretical guidance for their scaling but offers no discussion on how these would be chosen in practice (e.g., via cross-validation), which is a crucial aspect for practitioners.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are substantial, the inclusion of even a small simulation study would greatly strengthen the paper. It would serve two purposes: (i) to provide empirical validation of the asymptotic results, showing that the convergence rates and the normality of the test statistic hold in finite-sample regimes, and (ii) to demonstrate the algorithm's performance and robustness, especially with respect to the choice of tuning parameters and the quality of initialization.

### Final Assessment

This is a very strong theoretical paper that makes significant and novel contributions to the understanding of the EM algorithm in high dimensions. It bridges an important gap between computation and statistical theory for a widely used class of models. The technical quality is high, and the results are of great importance. The main weaknesses are the reliance on strong assumptions (particularly regarding initialization) and the complete absence of empirical results. Despite these limitations, the theoretical novelty and significance of the work are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper represents a major step forward in high-dimensional statistical theory.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, long-standing problem in high-dimensional statistics.
*   Provides the first comprehensive theoretical framework for both estimation and inference for the EM algorithm when d >> n.
*   Contributions are technically deep, novel, and significant.
*   The general framework is elegant and applicable to multiple important models.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Lack of any experimental validation to support the theory.
*   Strong, potentially restrictive assumptions about initialization and problem structure.
*   Practical aspects, such as choosing tuning parameters, are not addressed.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The theoretical contribution is outstanding and will likely inspire a great deal of follow-up work, including research that addresses the practical limitations mentioned above.
