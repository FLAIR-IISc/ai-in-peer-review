Summary
This paper studies parameter estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models via a variant of EM augmented with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step that enforces sparsity along the iterates. The authors analyze two versions of the M-step (exact maximization and a single gradient step), establish geometric contraction under suitable initialization and population smoothness/concavity conditions, and bound the final estimation error at (near-)minimax rate O(√(s* log d / n)). For inference, they construct a “decorrelated score” statistic (using a Dantzig-type estimator of a nuisance-orthogonalizing vector) and prove asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. They illustrate the general conditions on Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Relation to prior work
- Optimization/EM: Extends the population-to-sample EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) from low to high dimension using truncation (akin to iterative hard-thresholding ideas).
- High-d inference: Adapts decorrelated/debiased score methods (e.g., Zhang & Zhang 2014; van de Geer et al. 2014; Ning & Liu 2017) to latent variable settings by leveraging EM identities relating Q-function derivatives to the observed score/Fisher information.
- Alternatives: Contrasts with tensor methods and ℓ1-regularized EM for mixtures; argues improved rates/statistical efficiency.

Strengths
- Ambitious and timely: unifies optimization guarantees and valid inferential procedures for high-dimensional latent variable models.
- Clear algorithmic scaffold: EM + truncation with both exact and gradient M-step variants.
- Theoretical rates: Geometric optimization error plus entrywise statistical error leading to O(√(s* log d / n)) estimation, near minimax for the examples.
- Inference: Decorrelated score/Wald achieving semiparametric efficiency under explicit (though strong) conditions.
- General framework with model specializations (GMM, mixture of regressions).

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/methodological
- Reliance on resampling/split-sample analysis: Theorem 3.4 is proved for a “resampling” EM (Algorithm 4, in appendix). It is unclear whether the guarantees hold for the practical single-sample algorithm or how to remove sample-splitting (e.g., via uniform concentration/martingale arguments). This is a major gap.
- Initialization: Assumes an initializer within a constant relative ℓ2-error (and within a basin B), but no concrete, polynomial-time initializer is provided for the high-dimensional latent models considered. This is crucial in practice and for the theory to be actionable.
- Tuning and oracle knowledge: The sparsity level b_s must be of the same order as s*, and stepsize η=2/(ν+µ) requires knowledge of population constants. No adaptive, data-driven schemes or sensitivity analysis are provided.
- Monotonicity/descent: The truncation step breaks the EM ascent property; there is no discussion of how the Q-function behaves along the iterates and whether non-monotonicity causes practical instability.
- Scope of models/assumptions: The Gaussian mixture specialization assumes equal mixing, known/constant variance, and strong SNR; mixtures of regression assumptions are not visible in the main text. The generality claims would benefit from more diverse latent models and weaker conditions.
- Inference construction: The Dantzig problem (2.6) uses T_n(·) and a tuning parameter λ that depends on quantities (ζT, ζL, ζEM) not available in practice; guidance for choosing λ is missing. The test uses β estimated under H0 (b0=(0, b_β)); whether this requires refitting under the null and its computational impact is not discussed.
- Identities and signs: Theorem 2.1 asserts E[T_n(θ*)]=I(θ*), but typically Fisher information is −E[∇^2ℓ]/n. The sign convention for T_n and Q’s curvature should be clarified to avoid confusion (and consistently handle concavity vs convexity).

Clarity/presentation
- Formatting is severely degraded throughout: many LaTeX macros are broken (e.g., “⇤, `, ·, 1, 2, ⌘, ⌫, ⇢”), missing inequality symbols (“d n”), and ambiguous notation (r1, r2 gradients; “,1” norm). Several equations are hard to parse (e.g., (3.8)-(3.10)), with denominators like (1−√ρ1) appearing inconsistently. There is a stray footnote marker in the intro. Some definitions (e.g., matrix norms) are stated but not consistently used.
- References are incomplete/malformed (author names truncated, missing years/titles) and several key citations are missing: the Fisher/Louis identities for EM (Louis, 1982), iterative hard-thresholding (Blumensath & Davies, 2009), sparse EM variants, and recent high-dimensional inference works beyond GLMs.
- Many important details are deferred to appendices or to a “longer version,” leaving the main text underspecified. Given NeurIPS page limits, minimal proof sketches and clear statements of specialized conditions for the two instantiated models should be in the main paper.

Empirical validation
- No experiments. Even for a theory paper, small synthetic studies would demonstrate the practical viability of the truncation EM, robustness to tuning/initialization, and coverage of the proposed CIs, plus comparisons to ℓ1-EM and tensor baselines.

Quality
Technically plausible and potentially impactful, but with critical gaps (resampling vs single-sample analysis, initialization, tuning) and unclear sign/notation conventions that need correction. Many assumptions are strong.

Clarity
Currently poor due to formatting/notation issues and heavy reliance on appendices; several definitions and conditions are difficult to follow.

Originality
Moderate. The combination—EM with hard-thresholding plus decorrelated inference for latent models—is interesting, but relies on largely known ingredients (IHT-style truncation, EM identities, debiasing). The inferential extension to latent variable models is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
Potentially significant if cleaned up and validated; however, without practical guidance and experiments, it is hard to assess real-world impact.

Pros
- Unifies high-dimensional EM optimization with valid inference.
- Near-optimal estimation rates; semiparametric efficiency for inference.
- Covers both exact and gradient M-step implementations.

Cons
- Theory relies on resampling; unclear applicability to standard EM.
- No initializer, tuning guidance, or experiments.
- Severe presentation/formatting issues; missing key references.
- Strong assumptions; limited model scope in main text.

Questions for authors
1) Can you provide results without sample splitting? Or outline how to pass from resampling to single-sample guarantees?
2) What practical initializers do you recommend/prove for the two models? Under what conditions?
3) How should b_s and λ be chosen in practice? Any data-driven procedures or cross-validation strategies, with theoretical robustness?
4) Please clarify the sign conventions in Theorem 2.1/T_n and concavity assumptions, and provide a brief derivation in the main text.
5) Do you establish any monotonicity or stability guarantees despite truncation? If not, what are the observed behaviors?
6) Can you include synthetic experiments validating rates/coverage?

Overall recommendation: Reject
While the core idea is promising and the theoretical direction is valuable, the submission in its current form has too many methodological gaps (resampling vs standard EM, initialization, tuning), lacks empirical validation, and suffers from extensive clarity/formatting problems and incomplete referencing. I encourage resubmission after substantial revision, added experiments, and clearer, self-contained main-text statements. Confidence: High.
