

# Peer Review: "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings, where the dimension d exceeds the sample size n. The authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation step to enforce sparsity, enabling the first theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in such settings. The paper establishes both statistical and computational convergence rates (achieving a near-optimal $O(\sqrt{s^* \log d / n})$ rate) and develops a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference of low-dimensional components. The framework is applied to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, with detailed verifications of conditions for these specific cases. This work significantly extends the theoretical foundation of the EM algorithm beyond the well-studied low-dimensional regime, addressing a critical gap between computation and statistics.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide comprehensive proofs for all claims, with detailed conditions verified for specific models in the appendix. The paper carefully distinguishes between optimization error (geometrically decaying) and statistical error (achieving near-optimal rate), a crucial decomposition for high-dimensional analysis. The theoretical framework is well-structured and logically developed, with clear connections between the truncation step, sparsity enforcement, and statistical properties.

**Originality:** This work represents a significant advancement over existing literature. While [2] established guarantees for low-dimensional EM algorithms, this paper is the first to provide theoretical guarantees for high-dimensional EM with sparsity, addressing a fundamental limitation in the literature. The truncation step (a key innovation) is novel for EM algorithms, and the decorrelated score statistic for inference in high-dimensional latent variable models is also original. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly [2] and tensor-based methods, by addressing high-dimensional challenges that previous approaches cannot handle.

**Significance:** The paper solves an important problem with practical implications. High-dimensional latent variable models are increasingly common in modern applications (e.g., genomics, finance, and neuroscience), where theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm were previously unavailable. The authors provide both estimation guarantees and uncertainty assessment, which are essential for practical applications. The near-optimal rate of convergence and asymptotic normality of their decorrelated score statistic establish a new benchmark for inference in high-dimensional latent variable models, opening doors for future research.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and the paper's contributions. The technical conditions and main theorems are presented with appropriate precision. The notation is consistent, and the appendix provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The comparison with related work is thorough and helpful for contextualizing the contributions.

## Weaknesses

**Lack of Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper lacks empirical results to validate the proposed algorithm. Given the high-dimensional nature of the problem, a small-scale simulation study demonstrating the algorithm's practical performance and the effect of the truncation step would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Assumption Restrictiveness:** The paper relies on several strong assumptions (e.g., sparsity of the true parameter, minimum signal-to-noise ratio, and sparsity of the Dantzig selector solution). While these are standard in high-dimensional statistics, the paper could better discuss the practical implications of these assumptions for real-world applications.

**Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the high-dimensional EM algorithm in detail. While the truncation step enforces sparsity, the paper doesn't quantify how this affects the computational cost compared to the standard EM algorithm, which would be important for practitioners.

**Tuning Parameter Selection:** The choice of the sparsity parameter $b_s$ (which controls the sparsity level) is crucial for the algorithm's performance. The paper doesn't provide guidance on how to select this parameter in practice, which is a limitation for real-world application.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contributions are substantial, addressing a fundamental gap in the literature with rigorous analysis that advances the state of the art. The paper is well-written, clearly differentiates itself from prior work, and provides a general framework applicable to a broad family of latent variable models.

The weaknesses noted (lack of empirical validation, assumption restrictiveness, and computational complexity discussion) are important but do not undermine the paper's core theoretical contributions. These could be addressed in future work, and the theoretical foundation provided here is sufficient to justify acceptance.

The paper aligns well with NIPS topics, particularly in statistical machine learning, high-dimensional statistics, and optimization. It makes a clear, significant contribution to the field by bridging the gap between computation and statistics in high-dimensional settings.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- First theoretical guarantees for EM algorithm in high-dimensional regimes with sparsity
- Near-optimal statistical rate of convergence ($O(\sqrt{s^* \log d / n})$)
- First asymptotic inference framework for high-dimensional latent variable models
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with detailed conditions and proofs
- General framework applicable to multiple latent variable models

**Con Acceptance:**
- No empirical validation of the proposed algorithm
- Strong assumptions may limit practical applicability
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and tuning parameter selection
- The truncation step's impact on computational cost is not quantified

Overall, the paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and well-justified, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The authors have made a significant advance in understanding the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings, with clear implications for both theory and practice.
