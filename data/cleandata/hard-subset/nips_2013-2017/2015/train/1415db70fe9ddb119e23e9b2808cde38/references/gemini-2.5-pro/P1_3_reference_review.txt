This paper presents a theoretical framework for parameter estimation and statistical inference for latent variable models in the high-dimensional regime where the dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors propose two main contributions. First, a novel "high-dimensional EM algorithm" that incorporates a hard-thresholding (truncation) step after the standard E- and M-steps to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. Second, a "decorrelated score test" for conducting statistical inference (e.g., hypothesis testing) on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector.

The core theoretical results demonstrate that, under certain regularity conditions and with a suitable initialization, the proposed algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`. Furthermore, the proposed decorrelated score statistic is shown to be asymptotically normal, with a limiting variance that achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. The framework is general and its application is illustrated for high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

This work builds upon recent advances in the analysis of the EM algorithm in low-dimensional settings, particularly Balakrishnan et al. (2017) [2], by adapting the analytic techniques to the challenges of high-dimensionality. The key innovation is the introduction and analysis of the truncation step. For inference, the paper adeptly borrows and adapts techniques from the high-dimensional inference literature for convex models (e.g., the debiased Lasso) to the non-convex setting of the EM algorithm.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing gap in the literature: the lack of theoretical guarantees for the widely used EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. Providing a computationally tractable method for both optimal point estimation and valid statistical inference in this regime is a major contribution. Latent variable models are fundamental in machine learning, and this work provides a foundational theoretical tool for their application to modern high-dimensional data.

2.  **Novelty and Technical Depth:** The primary contributions are highly original. The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm with its truncation step is a simple and elegant modification, but its theoretical analysis is non-trivial. The authors successfully analyze the dynamics of the iterative estimates under this non-linear truncation operation. Even more impressive is the development of an inferential framework for the local optima produced by this algorithm, a problem significantly harder than for global optima of convex problems (like the Lasso). The synthesis of ideas from the EM analysis literature and the high-dimensional inference literature is novel and powerful.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis. It establishes not just a statistical rate for the final estimator but also a computational rate (geometric convergence). The statistical rate is shown to be near-optimal. The asymptotic normality result for the score test, which achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound, is a very strong and desirable result, placing the proposed inferential procedure on firm theoretical footing.

4.  **Generality of the Framework:** The authors present their theory under a general set of abstract conditions and then verify these conditions for two important and non-trivial latent variable models. This approach demonstrates the potential breadth and applicability of their framework to a wider class of models.

### **Weaknesses (Arguments for Improvement/Rejection)**

1.  **Reliance on a Strong Initialization Condition:** The theoretical guarantees are contingent on starting the algorithm within a basin of attraction of the true parameter (`||θ_init - θ*||_2 <= R`). While this is a standard assumption in the literature on non-convex optimization (including the prior work in [2]), it remains a significant practical limitation. The paper does not discuss how one might obtain such an initialization in practice, which is often the hardest part of the problem. A discussion on potential initialization strategies (e.g., via tensor methods, even if suboptimal, or spectral methods) would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

2.  **Strong Assumptions for Inference:** The inference theory (Assumption 4.5) requires sparsity of the vector `w*`, which is related to a row of the inverse Fisher information matrix. This is a common but strong assumption in the high-dimensional inference literature. It essentially assumes that the partial correlation between the parameter of interest and the nuisance parameters is sparse. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of when this assumption is likely to hold for latent variable models and what the consequences are if it is violated.

3.  **Practical Guidance on Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm and inference procedure depend on several tuning parameters: the sparsity level `bs`, the step size `η` (for the gradient M-step), and the regularization parameter `λ` for the Dantzig selector. The theory provides guidance on their scaling, but there is no discussion of how to select them in practice (e.g., using cross-validation or other data-driven methods). This omission makes it difficult for a practitioner to implement the method.

### **Detailed Comments**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proofs, deferred to the appendix, appear rigorous and the theoretical claims are well-supported by the analysis. The authors are honest about the assumptions needed for their results to hold. The work is a complete theoretical contribution.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but it is extremely dense. The introduction does an excellent job of positioning the work and motivating the problem. However, the methodology and theory sections are heavy on technical notation and conditions. A bit more intuition could improve readability. For example, the intuition behind the decorrelated score test is deferred to the appendix (§D); a short, high-level paragraph explaining why this "decorrelation" is necessary for inference in high dimensions would be very helpful in the main text of Section 2.2.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it draws on existing lines of research, the synthesis is novel and the extension to the high-dimensional EM setting for both estimation and inference is a significant leap forward. It is clearly distinguished from prior work on low-dimensional EM [2] and high-dimensional inference for convex models [26, 32].

*   **Significance:** As mentioned, the significance is very high. This work provides the first computationally feasible approach with rigorous theoretical backing for parameter estimation and uncertainty quantification in high-dimensional latent variable models. It has the potential to become a cornerstone result that future research in this area will build upon. The problem is important, the proposed solution is principled, and the results are strong.

In summary, this is a very strong theoretical paper that makes a foundational contribution to an important and challenging problem. The weaknesses, primarily concerning the initialization assumption and practical guidance on tuning, are common to theoretical work in this area and do not detract significantly from the importance of the contribution. The paper is an excellent fit for NIPS and is likely to have a substantial impact on the field.
