Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a high-dimensional EM framework that augments the classical E/M steps with an explicit truncation (hard-thresholding) step to enforce sparsity throughout the iterations. The authors analyze two M-step implementations (exact maximization and one-step gradient ascent) and provide nonasymptotic guarantees that decompose error into optimization and statistical terms. Given a suitable initialization, the algorithm contracts geometrically to a basin and achieves a near-minimax estimation rate of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to extra logs). Building on this estimator, the paper further develops a decorrelated score test that operates directly on the EM machinery (via derivatives of Qn) and, under high-dimensional nuisance parameters, yields asymptotic normality with an optimal limiting variance equal to the semiparametric efficiency bound. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

The work connects to and extends: (i) low-dimensional EM analyses (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.), (ii) sparsity-aware high-dimensional inference (desparsified/debiased Lasso and related methods), and (iii) alternative approaches for latent variable models such as tensor methods or ℓ1-penalized EM. Unlike prior low-dim EM results, this paper addresses d ≫ n with theory that is both computational (contraction of the iterates) and statistical (near-optimal rates and valid inference), and unlike convex-penalized frameworks, the analysis does not require a global optimum of a convex surrogate.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Introducing a truncation step inside EM to maintain sparsity along the entire trajectory is a simple, bold idea that enables sharp entrywise control and an analysis tailored to high dimensions.
- Unified computational and inferential theory: The optimization-statistics error decomposition, geometric contraction from a local initialization, and a decorrelated score constructed from Qn together form a coherent framework. The inference result attains the semiparametric information bound.
- Generality: The framework covers both exact and gradient M-steps and is verified for two canonical latent variable models.
- Technical contributions: Careful control of entrywise statistical errors for the sample vs population M-maps, supportive Lipschitz/concavity conditions for Q, and a Dantzig-based construction for nuisance projection in the EM setting.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Initialization and basin dependence: Guarantees require an initialization with constant relative error and a sufficiently large signal-to-noise ratio (e.g., in GMM). Practical initialization strategies and their guarantees (e.g., spectral/tensor warm-starts, carefully regularized k-means) are only briefly alluded to.
- Resampling analysis: The main estimation theorem analyzes a resampling version of EM. While standard in some recent theory, it would strengthen the contribution to prove analogous results for the single-sample setting (or to quantify the gap precisely).
- Tuning and sparsity knowledge: The truncation parameter bs needs to scale with s*. Adaptive or data-driven choices (e.g., via information criteria, stability selection) are not discussed; the theory assumes s* is known up to constants.
- Assumptions for inference: The sparsity of w* (the projection vector solving I_{η,η} w = I_{η,α}) is critical for the Dantzig step. The paper should further interpret when this holds for common latent variable models and provide empirical checks.
- Empirical validation: The submission (as provided) lacks experiments. Even small-scale simulations would help demonstrate robustness to misspecification, sensitivity to initialization/bs/step-size, and compare against penalized EM or tensor baselines.
- Scope: The models treated in detail are two-component GMM and mixtures of regressions. It would be valuable to comment on multi-component mixtures, non-spherical covariances, or other latent structures (HMMs, topic models), even if only at the level of verifiable conditions.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The analysis appears careful and credible. The conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity/smoothness in the population Q, concentration for Tn) are standard in spirit though demanding to verify; the paper sketches verifications for the two models considered.
- Completeness: The estimation theory and the inferential construction are complete; however, empirical evidence and discussion of practical hyperparameter selection are missing.
- Rigor vs practicality: The resampling assumption and knowledge of s* limit immediate plug-and-play deployment; nonetheless, the results are a substantial advance on the theory side.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized with clear algorithmic steps and explicit conditions. The paper is dense; adding intuition around the role of each condition and a short “practical recipe” (initialization, bs selection, step sizes, stopping criterion) would improve accessibility. Minor notation heavy; a figure illustrating the iterate trajectory and truncation would help.

Originality
- High. Combining a truncation-augmented EM with sharp nonasymptotic rates in d ≫ n and a decorrelated score built from the EM Q-function is, to my knowledge, novel and impactful. Positioning w.r.t. ℓ1-penalized EM and “regularized EM” variants could be expanded.

Significance
- High. High-dimensional inference for latent variable models is an important and underdeveloped area; the paper provides a principled path to both estimation and valid uncertainty quantification with optimal asymptotics.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide practical initialization strategies with theoretical guarantees compatible with your basin (e.g., simple spectral methods for mixtures) and report empirical sensitivity?
- Can the resampling analysis be replaced by a single-sample argument, or can you bound the gap? A brief discussion would help.
- Is there an adaptive rule for bs (e.g., monotone schedule, cross-validation, stability selection) that preserves theory?
- How robust is the inference to misspecification of w* sparsity? Any diagnostics?
- Comparisons to ℓ1-penalized EM (soft-thresholding in the M-step) would be illuminating, both theoretically and empirically.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, novel direction closing a key gap between computation and inference for latent variable models in high dimensions.
- Solid theory with near-optimal rates and semiparametric efficiency.
- Potentially broad impact beyond the two demonstrated models.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of empirical validation in the current version.
- Reliance on resampling for analysis and knowledge of s* for tuning.
- Strong assumptions (initialization, SNR, sparsity of w*) may limit immediate applicability.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some practical and presentation gaps, the core ideas are novel and significant, and the theory is strong. The paper opens a promising direction for high-dimensional latent variable inference.
