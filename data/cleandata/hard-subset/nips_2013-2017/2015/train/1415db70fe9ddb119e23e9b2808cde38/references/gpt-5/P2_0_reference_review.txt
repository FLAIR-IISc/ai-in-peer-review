Summary and context
This paper proposes a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm for latent variable models that integrates a hard-thresholding “truncation” step into each iteration to enforce sparsity. The authors analyze two implementations of the M-step (exact maximization and a single gradient-ascent step) and develop nonasymptotic error bounds that decompose into a geometric “optimization error” and a “statistical error” of order roughly sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to log factors), where s* is the sparsity of the true parameter. Building on the resulting estimator, they introduce a decorrelated score statistic for inference on low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, and prove asymptotic normality with variance achieving the semiparametric efficiency bound. They verify the framework for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under appropriate conditions (e.g., separation/SNR and sparsity).

Relative to prior work: It extends low-dimensional EM theory (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) to high-dimensional settings by adding hard-thresholding and establishing sharper entrywise statistical control. It contrasts with convex-penalized methods and debiasing/desparsification approaches (Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.) by targeting latent variable models where the likelihood is nonconvex and global optima are typically infeasible to compute. Compared with tensor methods, the obtained estimation rates are sharper (near minimax-optimal) and do not rely on high-order moments. The inference procedure adapts decorrelated score ideas to the EM/Q-function setting.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual advance: a principled, sparse EM procedure with nonasymptotic estimation guarantees and a unified inference framework for latent variable models.
- Solid theory: geometric convergence of the optimization error and near-optimal statistical rates; asymptotic normality achieving the semiparametric information bound.
- Generality: covers both exact and gradient EM variants; a unified set of conditions and proofs, with model-specific verifications.
- Relevance: addresses an important gap between computational algorithms (EM) and statistical guarantees in high-dimensional latent variable models.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Strong assumptions and local analysis: requires a good initialization in a basin of attraction and separation/SNR-type conditions; practical guidance for obtaining such initializations is limited.
- Resampling in analysis: the main estimation theorem is stated for a “resampling” version of the algorithm; while common as a proof device, a discussion or result for the standard single-sample EM would strengthen practicality.
- Practicality of inference step: computing the Dantzig selector for the decorrelation vector and Tn(·) can be heavy in high dimensions; no empirical evidence is provided on stability or finite-sample performance.
- Scope of verification: conditions are explicitly verified for two models; claims of broad generality would be more convincing with additional examples or a clearer recipe for checking conditions.
- Clarity and exposition: notation-heavy with many constants and technical conditions; some connections to closely related literatures (e.g., iterative hard thresholding and nonconvex sparse optimization) could be better acknowledged.

Quality
Technically strong. The decomposition into optimization vs statistical error is carefully executed, with Lipschitz-gradient and concavity/smoothness conditions tailored to the Q-function. The entrywise (l1) statistical control is appropriate for handling the truncation step, and the final rate sqrt(s* log d / n) is near-minimax. The inference result is compelling: the decorrelated score leverages identities linking gradients of Q and the likelihood to attain semiparametric efficiency. However, the reliance on resampling for the main theorem and on a strong initialization remains a theoretical-to-practical gap. I would like to see either a single-sample analysis sketch or experiments corroborating that resampling is not needed.

Clarity
The paper is well structured but dense. Algorithmic steps are precise; the role of the T-step is clear. The many constants and conditions can overwhelm; a high-level “checklist” for verifying conditions by model would help, as would a clearer discussion of how to choose bs and stepsizes in practice and how to obtain initializations. A comparison to iterative hard-thresholding methods (e.g., Blumensath & Davies; Jain et al.) would aid readers situating the approach.

Originality
Good. While both components (hard-thresholding for sparsity; decorrelated score for inference) are known, their integration into EM for high-dimensional latent variable models with provable convergence and semiparametric-optimal inference is, to my knowledge, new. The analysis via entrywise concentration tailored to the truncation step is nontrivial and differentiates this work from prior low-dimensional EM theory and convex high-dimensional inference.

Significance
High potential impact. High-dimensional latent variable models are central in modern ML. Providing both estimation and inference with near-optimal rates and efficient asymptotics is valuable and likely to inspire follow-up work, extensions to other latent models, and practical methods. The strong assumptions (separation, sparsity of the semiparametric direction w*, good initialization) limit immediate applicability but are standard in theory for these problems.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can the main estimation guarantee be extended to the standard (single-sample) iteration without resampling? If not, can you provide empirical evidence that the algorithm behaves similarly?
- Practical initialization: what concrete, computationally feasible initializers meet your basin-of-attraction requirement for the two models?
- Complexity of inference: discuss the cost and numerical stability of solving the Dantzig selector and computing Tn in high d. Would a Lasso regression surrogate suffice?
- Relation to IHT/HTP and nonconvex sparse M-estimation: please discuss connections and differences formally.
- Robustness: how sensitive are guarantees to mild model misspecification or to approximate sparsity?

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, well-motivated theory bridging a clear gap.
- Near-optimal rates and semiparametric-efficient inference.
- General framework applicable beyond the two worked examples.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy assumptions and resampling-based analysis; limited practical guidance.
- No empirical validation.
- Some missing contextualization with related sparse nonconvex optimization literature.

Overall evaluation
A solid theoretical contribution with clear novelty and potential impact on high-dimensional latent variable modeling. I lean toward acceptance contingent on clarifying the resampling issue, adding discussion of initialization and computational practicality, and strengthening connections to related work.
