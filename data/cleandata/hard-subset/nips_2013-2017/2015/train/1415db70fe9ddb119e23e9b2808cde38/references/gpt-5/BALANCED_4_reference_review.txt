Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm that appends a hard-thresholding (truncation) step after each E/M step to enforce sparsity in the parameter iterate. The authors analyze two M-step implementations (exact maximization and a single gradient-ascent step), and, under a basin-of-attraction initialization and suitable smoothness/Lipschitz conditions on the population Q function, derive a two-term error bound: an optimization error that decays geometrically and a statistical error at (near-)minimax rate O(√(s* log d / n)) (up to a log n factor). They further develop a decorrelated score/Wald inference procedure for a low-dimensional component of the parameter, constructing a Dantzig-type estimator of the orthogonalization vector w, and prove asymptotic normality with variance attaining the semiparametric efficiency bound. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, where the conditions are verified under separation/SNR assumptions.

Relative to Balakrishnan et al. (2017), which analyzed EM in low dimensions (d ≪ n) and established p(d/n) rates to a local optimum, this work targets the high-dimensional regime (d ≫ n), introduces an explicit truncation step to maintain sparsity, and provides high-dimensional inference via decorrelated scores, connecting rQ and Tn to the score and Fisher information. The inference development is related to debiasing/desparsifying methods for GLMs (Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.; Javanmard & Montanari), but extends to latent-variable models with nonconcave likelihoods where global optimization is typically infeasible. Compared to tensor/moment methods for mixtures (Anandkumar et al.), the claimed rates are sharper in high dimensions under sparsity. The truncation step is reminiscent of iterative hard-thresholding/projected gradient methods, and there are nearby works on penalized EM and alternating minimization for mixed regressions; this paper’s main novelty is a unified computational-statistical analysis (including inference) for EM with truncation in high dimensions.

Strengths
- Clear high-level framework: EM + truncation with either exact or gradient M-steps, analyzed under unified conditions.
- Sharp statistical rates: O(√(s* log d / n)) (near-minimax) once optimization error decays, for two canonical latent-variable models.
- Inference contribution: decorrelated score statistic that is asymptotically normal and semiparametrically efficient, with conditions tailored to latent-variable structure via Q and Tn.
- Addresses computation-statistics gap for latent-variable models beyond convex settings; results apply to local solutions produced by a practical algorithm.
- Verification of conditions sketched for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under explicit SNR assumptions.

Weaknesses and questions
- Initialization: The guarantees require “appropriate” initialization within a basin. Practical, computationally efficient initializers for the high-dimensional setting are not specified in the main text (e.g., spectral/tensor or method-of-moments with sparsity screening). Please make these concrete and state precise conditions under which they succeed.
- Resampling/sample-splitting: The main convergence analysis uses a resampling version to simplify dependence across iterations. Is sample splitting necessary, or can the guarantees be proved without it (perhaps with a polylog factor), as in modern IHT or EM analyses? Clarify the price paid in rate/constant.
- Tuning and adaptivity: The truncation level bs must be of the same order as s*. How should bs be chosen in practice without knowing s*? Can you provide adaptive rules (e.g., monotone decrease schedule, BIC-type selection, knockoffs/FDR control) and sensitivity analysis? Similarly, practical guidance for the Dantzig tuning parameter and step sizes is missing.
- Assumptions’ strength and transparency: Conditions 3.1–3.3 and 4.1–4.4 are abstract. For each showcased model, please state explicit constants and SNR thresholds, and isolate any dependence on unknown parameters (e.g., Fisher eigenvalues). The sparsity of the orthogonalization vector w* (Assumption 4.5) can be strong; discuss when this is realistic (e.g., near-block-diagonal information structure) and provide examples where w* is dense (and what fails).
- Scope of models and experiments: Only two latent-variable models are worked out. It would strengthen the paper to add at least synthetic experiments validating the predicted optimization/statistical rates, robustness to misspecification, and the coverage/size of the proposed tests. Extending theory/empirics to another model class (e.g., missing-data Gaussian models, HMMs) would enhance generality.
- Clarity and presentation: The manuscript has several typographical issues, incomplete citations, and overloaded notation (e.g., rQ vs r1Q). Please clean up references (years, venues), define all symbols upon first use, and summarize algorithmic complexity per iteration.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically solid at a high level, with plausible conditions and correct use of decorrelation machinery. Some gaps remain (initialization, necessity of resampling, practical tuning). Proofs are deferred; more detail on constants/rates for each model would help.
- Clarity: Generally understandable but hindered by formatting/notation and missing details on practical aspects. Appendices seem to carry essential content; some should be promoted to the main text.
- Originality: The combination of EM with hard-thresholding plus a full computational-statistical analysis and optimal inference for latent-variable models in high dimensions is novel. The truncation idea itself is known, but the framework and efficiency result in this context appear new.
- Significance: High. EM is ubiquitous; having rates and valid inference in high dimensions for mixture models is impactful and likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap (high-dimensional EM with inference).
- Near-minimax estimation rates and semiparametric optimal inference.
- Conceptually simple, implementable algorithm with two M-step variants.
- Conditions verified for canonical mixture models.

Cons for acceptance
- No empirical validation; practical guidance (initialization/tuning) is limited.
- Some assumptions may be strong (w* sparsity, SNR, basin-of-attraction).
- Analysis uses resampling; unclear if necessary in practice.
- Presentation and citations need significant polishing.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Constructive suggestions
- Provide a concrete, computationally efficient initializer with guarantees under your assumptions.
- Either remove resampling in the theory or clearly quantify the gap between analyzed and practical algorithms.
- Add simulations showing estimation error decay (optimization vs. statistical plateau), sensitivity to bs, and validity/power of the score test.
- Discuss adaptivity for bs and Dantzig tuning; provide defaults and diagnostic checks.
- Expand the verification of Conditions 4.x to clarify when w* is sparse; if possible, offer a ridge-type alternative when w* is not sparse.
