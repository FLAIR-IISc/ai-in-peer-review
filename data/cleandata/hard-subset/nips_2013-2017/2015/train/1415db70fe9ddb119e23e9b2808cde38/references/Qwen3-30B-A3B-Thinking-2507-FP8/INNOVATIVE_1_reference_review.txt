

# Review: High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality

This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm that addresses a significant gap in the literature: theoretical guarantees for EM in high-dimensional settings where dimension d exceeds sample size n. The authors introduce a truncation step in both E-step and M-step to enforce sparsity, enabling them to establish geometric convergence rates and near-optimal statistical rates for parameter estimation. They further develop a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference of low-dimensional components in high-dimensional latent variable models.

The core novelty lies in extending the classical EM algorithm to high-dimensional regimes with theoretical guarantees. Previous work (e.g., [2]) established results for low-dimensional settings (d << n), but no theoretical guarantees existed for high-dimensional cases. The paper bridges this gap by introducing a truncation step that enforces sparsity structure along the solution path, which is the key technical innovation. This approach allows the authors to establish both optimization and statistical guarantees simultaneously, proving that the algorithm converges geometrically to an estimator with the near-minimax optimal rate of s* log d / n.

The decorrelated score statistic for inference is another significant contribution. Prior work on high-dimensional inference (e.g., [13, 26, 32]) primarily focused on generalized linear models, not latent variable models. The authors' approach is unique because it applies to latent variable models where global optima are computationally infeasible to obtain. They prove the decorrelated score statistic is asymptotically normal with optimal variance, achieving the semiparametric information bound.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors effectively motivate their approach by identifying the gap between computation and statistics in high-dimensional latent variable models. They provide detailed theoretical guarantees for both estimation and inference, with careful verification of conditions for specific models (Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression).

Strengths:
1. **Novelty**: The high-dimensional EM algorithm with truncation is a bold and original approach that addresses a fundamental gap in the literature.
2. **Theoretical significance**: The paper provides the first theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings, including both estimation and inference.
3. **Technical depth**: The analysis carefully handles the challenges of high dimensionality, particularly the entrywise statistical error characterization.
4. **Practical relevance**: The approach is applicable to a broad family of latent variable models, with promising implications for real-world applications.

Weaknesses:
1. The truncation step (bs parameter) is somewhat ad hoc; the paper doesn't provide a principled method for selecting bs.
2. The theoretical guarantees depend on several conditions that need verification for each specific model, which may limit immediate applicability.
3. The paper focuses on latent variable models, which limits the scope compared to broader optimization methods.

However, these weaknesses are minor compared to the paper's significant contribution. The authors have successfully identified and addressed a major gap in the literature with a novel approach that could shape future research in high-dimensional statistical inference.

The paper's significance is substantial. It resolves a long-standing theoretical gap in the application of EM to high-dimensional problems, which has been a major limitation in applying latent variable models to modern high-dimensional datasets. The decorrelated score statistic for inference in latent variable models is particularly valuable as it enables uncertainty quantification in settings where this was previously impossible.

The paper stands out from previous work in several key ways:
- Previous work [2] established results only for low-dimensional settings (d << n), while this paper handles d >> n.
- The truncation step is a novel mechanism to handle sparsity in the EM framework, which is not present in existing approaches.
- The decorrelated score statistic is specifically designed for latent variable models, unlike methods for generalized linear models.

Compared to tensor methods [1, 9], which also handle high-dimensional latent variable models but with suboptimal statistical rates, this paper achieves near-optimal rates. The authors correctly identify this as a key advantage.

In terms of novelty, significance, and technical contribution, this paper makes a strong case for acceptance. It introduces a genuinely new approach to a fundamental problem, provides rigorous theoretical guarantees, and addresses a significant gap in the literature. The truncation step is a clever and novel solution to the high-dimensional challenge, and the decorrelated score statistic for inference is an important theoretical advance.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound, clearly written, highly original, and significantly advances the field. While the theoretical conditions could be more fully explored, the core contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance.

I recommend acceptance of this paper as it represents a significant step forward in the theoretical understanding of EM algorithms in high-dimensional settings and opens new avenues for research in high-dimensional statistical inference for latent variable models. The novelty and significance of the approach far outweigh the minor methodological limitations.
