This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in the high-dimensional setting, where the parameter dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" that augments the standard E- and M-steps with a truncation (T-step) that enforces sparsity at each iteration. The main contributions are twofold. First, the paper establishes that, given a suitable initialization, the proposed algorithm converges at a geometric rate to a solution that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d) / n)`. Second, it develops a procedure for valid asymptotic inference on low-dimensional components of the parameter vector by constructing a "decorrelated score statistic" that is shown to be asymptotically normal. This work significantly extends the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm, which was previously well-established only in the low-dimensional regime (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017), to the more challenging and practically relevant high-dimensional sparse setting. It also makes an important contribution to the high-dimensional inference literature by providing a method applicable to local optima of a non-convex procedure, in contrast to most existing methods that rely on global optima of convex programs.

### Strengths

*   **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing gap between the widespread practical use of the EM algorithm and its theoretical understanding in modern high-dimensional statistics. Providing the first set of guarantees for both optimal parameter estimation and valid asymptotic inference for EM in the `d >> n` regime is a major contribution that will be of high interest to both theoreticians and practitioners in the field.
*   **Originality and Novelty:** The proposed framework is highly original. While adding a hard-thresholding step is a natural idea for high dimensions, the associated theoretical analysis is non-trivial. The paper's main novelty lies in its unified analysis that establishes both computational (geometric convergence) and statistical (near-optimal rates) guarantees. Furthermore, the development of a decorrelated score test for an estimator that is a local, rather than global, optimum of a non-convex procedure is a sophisticated and novel contribution to the challenging area of high-dimensional inference.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The analysis framework, based on a set of clearly stated high-level conditions, is rigorous and powerful. The key insight to use the `ell_infinity`-norm to control the statistical error (Condition 3.3) to handle the truncation step is technically sound and crucial for the success of the proof. The resulting guarantees—geometric convergence to an estimator with a near-minimax statistical rate and an asymptotically efficient inference procedure—are state-of-the-art.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. It begins with a clear motivation and a concise summary of contributions. The related work is discussed thoroughly, correctly positioning the paper's advances. The methodology and main theorems are presented in a clean, general form before being applied to specific examples, which makes the framework easy to understand.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While the paper is of high quality, there are several points that could be addressed to further strengthen it.

1.  **The Initialization Assumption:** The theoretical results rely on the existence of an initial estimate `init` that is already within a constant-factor `ell_2` ball of the true parameter `theta*`. This is a common but strong assumption in the analysis of non-convex optimization. For the work to be more self-contained and of greater practical impact, it would be beneficial to include a discussion on how such an initialization might be obtained in practice. Even if a full theoretical guarantee for an initialization procedure is beyond the scope, discussing potential data-driven strategies (e.g., via spectral methods or convex relaxations) would be a valuable addition.
2.  **Analysis of the Resampling Algorithm:** The proof of the main estimation result (Theorem 3.4) is for a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4 in the appendix), where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a common theoretical device to simplify analysis by ensuring independence across iterations, but it does not reflect how the algorithm is used in practice (with the full dataset at each step). The paper should be more explicit about this limitation in the main text. A brief discussion on the challenges of extending the analysis to the standard non-resampling algorithm and whether the results are expected to hold would be highly valuable for the reader.
3.  **Guidance on Tuning Parameters:** The proposed algorithm and inference procedure depend on key tuning parameters, namely the sparsity level `bs` and the Dantzig selector regularization parameter `lambda`. The paper provides theoretical scalings for these parameters, but offers little guidance on how they should be chosen from data in a practical application. A discussion of potential methods, such as cross-validation for `bs`, and the potential theoretical implications of such data-driven choices would significantly enhance the paper's practical utility.
4.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a work with strong theoretical contributions, its claims could be made more concrete and compelling with a small simulation study. For example, demonstrating the geometric convergence rate empirically (e.g., a linear decay of error on a log-plot) and validating the asymptotic normality of the test statistic (e.g., via a QQ-plot or by checking the uniformity of p-values under the null) would provide powerful, complementary evidence for the theoretical findings.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first comprehensive theoretical framework for both estimation and inference for the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional sparse setting.
*   The results are of high quality, establishing geometric convergence, near-minimax optimal statistical rates, and asymptotically efficient inference.
*   The technical analysis is novel and rigorous, successfully navigating the challenges of non-convexity and high dimensionality.
*   The paper is very well-written and makes a significant contribution to an important area of machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The theory relies on a strong and non-constructive initialization assumption.
*   The formal proofs are for a data-resampling version of the algorithm, not the one used in practice.
*   The paper lacks any empirical results to support and illustrate the theoretical claims.

### Decision

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm. The strengths, particularly the significance and novelty of the results, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The limitations identified (initialization, resampling analysis) are common in this line of theoretical research and represent important directions for future work rather than fatal flaws. I therefore recommend acceptance. The authors are encouraged to address the points raised above, especially by adding discussion on the initialization, the resampling scheme, and tuning parameters, to further improve the manuscript.
