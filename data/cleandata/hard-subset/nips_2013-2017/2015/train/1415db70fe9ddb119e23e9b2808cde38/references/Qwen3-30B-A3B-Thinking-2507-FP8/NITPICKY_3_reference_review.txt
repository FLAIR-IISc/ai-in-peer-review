

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

This paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm with truncation steps to enforce sparsity, establishing statistical guarantees for both parameter estimation and asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors claim to fill a significant gap by providing the first theoretical guarantees for EM in high-dimensional settings, with applications to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel framework for high-dimensional EM with a clear algorithmic structure (Algorithm 1) and establishes theoretically sound convergence guarantees. The decomposition of estimation error into optimization and statistical components (Theorem 3.4) is well-structured. The decorrelated score statistic for inference (Section 2.2) is a valuable contribution that addresses a critical gap in high-dimensional latent variable models.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies (Major)
The paper exhibits numerous notational inconsistencies that undermine technical clarity. The sparsity level is denoted as "s⇤" in some places and "s^*" in others, with inconsistent use of asterisks. The paper uses "p" to denote square root (e.g., "p s⇤ · log d/n"), which is non-standard and confusing. The symbol "⇥" is used for matrices without proper definition. The expression "p bs+ C 0/ p 1  · p s⇤" in equation (3.10) is poorly formatted and ambiguous. These inconsistencies make the technical content difficult to follow.

### Technical Ambiguities
The paper claims "near-minimax-optimal" rates but doesn't precisely define "near" (e.g., within logarithmic factors, constant factors). The asymptotic regime for the normality result (Theorem 4.6) is not clearly specified (e.g., d/n → 0, d/n → constant, d/n → ∞). The paper claims to establish "the first theoretical guarantee" for high-dimensional EM, but previous work on high-dimensional EM (e.g., [23] for mixture of regression) exists, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contributions from these.

### Mathematical Errors
In Theorem 3.4, the expression "p bs+ C 0/ p 1  · p s⇤" is ambiguously written. It should be either "sqrt(bs + C0)/sqrt(1-ρ) * sqrt(s*)" or "sqrt(bs) + C0/sqrt(1-ρ) * sqrt(s*)", but the paper's notation makes this unclear. The expression "⇢t/2" in equation (3.10) is ambiguous: is it "rho^(t/2)" or "rho^t / 2"? The paper also uses "b" for estimators inconsistently (e.g., "b = (T )" versus "b (T)").

### Incomplete Technical Justification
The paper claims the truncation step "effectively enforces sparsity" but doesn't rigorously justify how the choice of "bs" affects the final estimator. The paper states that "the statistical error term is roughly of the order p s⇤ · log d/n" but doesn't provide a rigorous proof of the exact rate, only a heuristic explanation.

### Related Work
The paper cites [2] as having established "the first result that characterizes explicit statistical and computational rates of convergence" for EM, but doesn't sufficiently distinguish their low-dimensional work from the high-dimensional contributions in this paper. The claim that "there exists no theoretical guarantee for the EM algorithm" in high dimensions is overstated, as [23] provides support recovery guarantees for high-dimensional EM.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a novel framework for high-dimensional EM with theoretical guarantees, the numerous notational inconsistencies, mathematical ambiguities, and technical shortcomings significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The paper's claim of being the "first" theoretical guarantee for high-dimensional EM is questionable given existing work, and the technical presentation lacks the precision expected for a NIPS publication.

The paper's significance is overshadowed by these technical flaws. The authors have made a valuable contribution to high-dimensional inference for latent variable models, but the paper's presentation and technical execution do not meet NIPS standards. The minor methodological details and formatting inconsistencies are substantial enough to warrant rejection, as they prevent clear understanding and reproducibility of the results.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper contains significant technical and notational flaws that prevent clear understanding of the theoretical contributions, despite the potentially important topic. The authors should thoroughly revise the paper to fix the notational inconsistencies, clarify ambiguous expressions, and provide more precise technical justification before resubmission.
