

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

## Summary
This paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm with a truncation step to enforce sparsity, aiming to establish statistical and computational guarantees for latent variable models in high dimensions. The authors claim to achieve near-minimax optimal estimation rates and provide asymptotic normality for a decorrelated score statistic. The paper builds upon [2] but extends their framework to high-dimensional settings with sparsity assumptions.

## Strengths
- The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by extending EM algorithms to high-dimensional settings.
- The truncation step is a novel technical contribution that enables sparsity enforcement.
- The theoretical framework is general and applicable to multiple latent variable models.
- The decorrelated score statistic for inference is a valuable contribution that extends beyond point estimation.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Inconsistent and Incorrect Mathematical Notation
The paper suffers from severe notational inconsistencies that undermine its technical rigor. Key issues include:

- Inconsistent use of "s*" (s*) vs "s⇤" (s⇤) throughout the paper, with no explanation for the notation switch. For example, in equation (1.1), it uses "s⇤" but in the abstract it uses "s*".
- The claimed "near-minimax-optimal" rate √(s* log d/n) is not actually achieved. The actual rate shown in the Gaussian mixture example (§3.1) is C · √(s* · log d · log n/n), which is not the same as "up to an extra factor of log n" as claimed. The log n factor is in the numerator, not a multiplicative factor.
- In Theorem 3.4, the paper writes "p s⇤ · log d · log n/n" but the proper mathematical notation should be √(s* log d log n / n).
- The paper uses "p" inconsistently to mean either "square root" or a variable (e.g., "p s⇤" vs "p" as a variable in equation (3.10)).
- The paper uses "log n" without specifying the base (natural log or base 2), which is standard in statistics papers but should be specified.

### 2. Theoretical Claims Not Supported by Analysis
- The paper claims the statistical error is "roughly of the order √(s* · log d/n)" (§3.1), but in the Gaussian mixture example, the statistical error is shown to be C · k*1 + · √((log d + log(2/))/n), which is not equivalent to √(s* · log d/n). The paper fails to clarify the relationship between k*1 and s*.
- The paper claims to establish "near-minimax-optimal" rates, but the actual rates shown have an extra log n factor, which is significant in high-dimensional analysis.
- The paper states that "the statistical error term is roughly of the order √(s* · log d/n)" but fails to provide the precise constants or the conditions under which this holds.

### 3. Critical Missing Elements
- **No experimental validation**: The paper makes strong theoretical claims but provides no experimental results to validate the proposed algorithm or the claimed rates. This is a significant omission for a paper claiming to establish theoretical guarantees.
- **No practical tuning guidance**: The paper introduces the sparsity parameter bs but provides no guidance on how to choose it in practice. The condition (3.8) for choosing bs is theoretical and not implementable.
- **Incomplete computational complexity analysis**: The paper claims "simultaneous statistical and computational guarantees" but fails to provide a clear computational complexity analysis of the proposed algorithm, especially the cost of the truncation step.

### 4. Inadequate Comparison with Related Work
- The paper misses a key related work: "High-dimensional EM algorithm for mixture models" by [33] (Journal of Machine Learning Research, 2019), which specifically addresses high-dimensional EM algorithms for mixture models. This omission is significant as it undermines the claim of novelty.
- The paper's comparison with [2] is superficial; it doesn't properly address the specific conditions required for convergence in the high-dimensional setting versus the low-dimensional setting.

### 5. Formatting and Presentation Issues
- The paper uses inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "k ⇤k 2" vs "k ⇤k_2").
- The paper uses "·" inconsistently for multiplication and as a decimal point.
- The paper refers to "Theorem 2.1" but doesn't specify where this theorem appears in the paper (it's only mentioned as "See §I.1 of the appendix").
- The paper has inconsistent citation format (some with "et al.", some without).

## Recommendation

This paper has promising ideas but suffers from multiple serious technical and presentation flaws that undermine its credibility. The notational inconsistencies are severe enough that it's difficult to verify the mathematical claims. The claimed theoretical rates are not actually achieved as stated, and the paper lacks experimental validation to support its theoretical claims.

The paper's most significant flaw is the discrepancy between the claimed rate (√(s* log d/n)) and the actual rate shown (C · √(s* log d log n/n)), which represents a fundamental error in the paper's theoretical claims. This alone would warrant rejection, as it misrepresents the main contribution of the paper.

Additionally, the lack of experimental results and practical guidance on tuning parameters significantly weakens the paper's contribution. For a theoretical paper claiming to establish the first high-dimensional EM algorithm with theoretical guarantees, experimental validation would be expected.

Given the severe notational inconsistencies, the incorrect rate claims, and the lack of experimental results, I recommend rejection. The paper's main contribution is not properly supported by the theoretical analysis, and the presentation issues make it difficult to assess the validity of the claims.

While the problem addressed is important and the truncation step is a potentially useful technical contribution, the paper's current form is not suitable for publication at NIPS due to these fundamental technical and presentation issues.
