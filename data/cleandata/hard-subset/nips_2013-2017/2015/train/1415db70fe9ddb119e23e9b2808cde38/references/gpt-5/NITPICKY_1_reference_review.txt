Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional latent variable models and proposes a “high-dimensional EM” that augments the classical E/M steps with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step to enforce sparsity throughout the iterates. The main theoretical claims are twofold: (i) under suitable initialization and regularity conditions, the algorithm converges geometrically to a statistically accurate estimator achieving a near-minimax ℓ2 error of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to a log n factor), with guarantees for both exact and gradient-ascent M-steps; (ii) based on the obtained estimator, the authors develop a decorrelated score test (via a Dantzig-style estimator of a nuisance direction) that is asymptotically normal and, they claim, semiparametrically efficient for low-dimensional targets in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance. The analysis is phrased in a unified framework and illustrated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions (details deferred to appendices).

Relation to prior work is made primarily to Balakrishnan et al. (population-to-sample EM analysis in low dimensions), debiasing/desparsifying approaches for GLMs, and tensor methods for mixtures. The work positions itself as closing a computation-statistics gap for EM in high dimensions, providing both estimation and inference.

Strengths
- Ambitious and timely goal: simultaneous optimization guarantees and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models, a setting with real gaps.
- Conceptually simple modification (hard-thresholding/truncation) with clear intuition; handles both exact- and gradient-EM variants.
- Error decomposition into optimization vs statistical terms is clean; geometric convergence plus entrywise statistical control is technically non-trivial for EM with truncation.
- Asymptotic inference via a decorrelated score tailored to the EM/Q-function is interesting; the link between ∇Q and the full score/Fisher information is used neatly.
- Claimed rates match near-minimax (up to log n) and the inference claims target the semiparametric information bound.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical assumptions and practicality
- Initialization: All guarantees hinge on an initializer within a basin of attraction (relative ℓ2 error < constant). No concrete, computationally justified initialization procedure is provided for the high-dimensional setting; tensor/spectral initializers are dismissed for suboptimal rates, but they could still serve as initializers. Without a constructive initializer with probability guarantees, the main results are conditional.
- Choice of sparsity bs: Requires bs to be of the same order as s*, via inequalities (3.8)-(3.9). There is no practical guidance (CV, BIC, stability) nor robustness analysis to misspecification. This reliance is strong for practical use.
- Stepsize and constants: The gradient M-step uses η = 2/(ν+μ), which are population quantities; similar constants appear in ρ. How to set η from data (backtracking, Lipschitz estimates) is not discussed, and the effect on guarantees is unclear.
- Resampling variant: Theorem 3.4 is proved for a resampling/splitting version (Algorithm 4, not included), which reduces sample size per iteration and is rarely used in practice. Guarantees for the standard single-sample iterate are not established in the main text.
- Strong structural assumptions: The inference step assumes sparsity of w* (solution to a Fisher-information linear system) with s_w* = o(√(n/log d)), which may fail for many mixture settings; little intuition is provided on when w* is sparse for the presented models.
- Separation/signal-to-noise: For GMMs, the Lipschitz constant involves exp(-C r^2); the analysis requires large SNR. Practical regimes with moderate overlap are not addressed.

Novelty and positioning
- The truncation step is essentially hard-thresholding/projection onto the s-sparse set, closely related to Iterative Hard Thresholding (IHT) and projected gradient methods; this connection and relevant literature (e.g., Blumensath & Davies; Jain et al.; Loh & Wainwright on nonconvex M-estimators) are not discussed. Positioning as “novel high-dimensional EM” would benefit from clarifying what is fundamentally new relative to known projected/thresholded iterative schemes.
- “First” guarantees for EM in high dimensions seem somewhat overstated: there is prior work on regularized EM or gradient-EM with ℓ1 penalties for mixtures; the paper cites [23], but a broader comparison (both theoretically and algorithmically) is missing.

Clarity and completeness
- Extensive reliance on appendices/“longer version” for crucial definitions (Algorithm 4), verifications of Conditions 3.1–3.3 and 4.1–4.4, and proofs. The main text does not contain enough details to audit the core claims for the two models; only a single GMM implication is sketched.
- Notation and typesetting are seriously problematic: many TeX artifacts (⇤, ⌘,  , `-norms, misplaced subscripts/superscripts), broken formulas (e.g., bs + C0/√(·)), and hard-to-parse inequalities. Several key displays are unreadable without guessing. This substantially hinders review and would not meet NeurIPS standards as-is.
- References are heavily corrupted/incomplete (authors’ names mangled, missing years/venues, unclear mapping to in-text numeric citations). This must be fixed.
- No experiments or simulations. Even purely theoretical papers typically include small-scale experiments to illustrate finite-sample behavior, robustness to bs/initialization, and to compare against ℓ1-regularized EM or tensor initializers. The lack of empirical evidence weakens the practical significance claim.

Methodological details
- The Dantzig selector constraint for w uses Tn(·) involving second derivatives of Qn; computational cost and numerical stability are not discussed. Tuning parameter choice (λ) depends on unknown rates (ξT, ξL, ξEM); no data-driven rule is proposed.
- Variance normalization uses [Tn(·)]_{α|}; consistency requires accurate estimation of Fisher sub-blocks—assumptions are stated but guidance/diagnostics are missing.
- The extra log n factor in the rate is acknowledged but not discussed; whether it is an artifact of resampling or analysis could be clarified.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide a concrete, analyzable initializer achieving the required basin conditions for the covered models (possibly via MOM/tensor or penalized estimators), and quantify its computational and statistical cost.
2. Can you establish guarantees for the non-resampling algorithm on a single sample? Is the log n factor removable?
3. How robust is the method to mis-specifying bs? Any theory with bs ≥ Cs* but unknown s*?
4. For which mixture regimes is w* demonstrably sparse? Please give intuition and formal statements for GMM and mixture of regressions.
5. Practical tuning: how to pick η, bs, and λ from data? Any sensitivity analysis or heuristics?
6. Please relate your T-step to IHT/projected gradient literature and clarify the novelty over these methods.

Pros
- Addresses an important gap (HD EM estimation + inference).
- Clean, modular theoretical framework; near-optimal rates and asymptotic normality.
- Covers both exact and gradient M-steps.

Cons
- Presentation/typing/references are in unacceptable shape.
- Strong, unverified-in-practice assumptions (initialization, sparsity of w*, SNR).
- Guarantees rely on resampling and unknown constants; little practical guidance.
- No empirical validation; limited comparison to closely related methods.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The core idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current manuscript has substantial clarity, completeness, and practicality issues, alongside strong assumptions not fully justified. A revised version with corrected writing, complete references, explicit initializers and tuning strategies, non-resampling guarantees (or clear justification), and empirical validation would be much stronger.
