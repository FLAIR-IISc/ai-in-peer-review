

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

## Summary
This paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm with truncation steps to enforce sparsity, aiming to establish statistical guarantees for parameter estimation and uncertainty assessment in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors claim to be the first to provide theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings, establishing both estimation rates and asymptotic normality for a decorrelated score test. They apply their framework to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel framework for high-dimensional latent variable models, which is a significant contribution to the literature. The theoretical results are generally well-motivated, and the connection between the EM algorithm and proximal methods is well-articulated. The decorrelated score test for hypothesis testing is a valuable addition to the literature on high-dimensional inference.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies (Major)
The paper contains numerous formatting and notational inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism. The true parameter is denoted both as "⇤" (with a stray space) and "⇤" (without space) throughout the paper. Mathematical expressions like "k ⇤k 2" appear without proper spacing. The title should be "High-Dimensional" (with a hyphen), not "High Dimensional." The reference to "⇤Research supported by..." is a clear formatting error where the footnote marker is not properly handled.

### Theoretical and Methodological Issues (Critical)

1. **Inaccurate claim about related work**: The paper states "there exists no theoretical guarantee for the EM algorithm" in high-dimensional regimes, but [23] explicitly analyzes the gradient EM algorithm for high-dimensional mixture of regression models. This misrepresentation of related work is unacceptable in a peer-reviewed paper.

2. **Ambiguous sparsity parameter selection**: The paper describes "bs" as a "tuning parameter that controls the sparsity level" (line 1, Algorithm 1), but provides no guidance on how to select it in practice. The condition (3.8) for choosing bs is stated as "bs = Θ(C·max{...})", but the paper doesn't clarify how to compute the constants C, or how to select them.

3. **Inconsistent mathematical notation**: In Theorem 3.4, the condition (3.9) states "p bs+ C0/ p 1 · p s⇤ / (1 - p ρ1) · ϵ" which is mathematically ambiguous. The expression "p bs+ C0/ p 1" is unclear and should be written as "√(bs + C₀)/√(1 - ρ₁)" for clarity.

4. **Mischaracterization of statistical error**: The paper claims the statistical error term is "roughly of the order √(s⇤ · log d/n)" (Section 3.1), but fails to rigorously justify this claim. The statistical error ϵ is defined in Condition 3.3, but the paper doesn't prove that ϵ = O(√(log d/n)) for the specific models they consider.

5. **Inconsistent description of the M-step**: The paper states "the M-step (line 5) at the t-th iteration" (Section 2.1), but Algorithm 1 line 5 is "M-step: (t+0.5) = Mn( (t))", which suggests the output is (t+0.5), not (t). This inconsistency creates confusion about the algorithm's iteration structure.

### Technical and Theoretical Weaknesses

1. **Lack of computational complexity analysis**: The paper claims "simultaneous statistical and computational guarantees" but provides no analysis of the computational complexity (per-iteration cost, total iterations) of the proposed algorithm.

2. **Unjustified norm choice**: The paper uses L1-norm for statistical error in Condition 3.3, claiming it "characterizes the more refined entrywise statistical error," but provides no theoretical justification for why L1 is appropriate in this context compared to L2.

3. **Missing minimax comparison**: The paper claims "near-minimax-optimal" rates but doesn't explicitly compare to known minimax lower bounds for the specific models they consider.

4. **Incomplete empirical validation**: The paper claims to "illustrate its effectiveness" by applying to two models but provides no empirical results to support this claim, which is a significant omission for a theoretical paper.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a novel framework for high-dimensional EM algorithms with theoretical guarantees, but it contains numerous critical flaws that undermine its scientific contribution. The major issues include misrepresentation of related work, inconsistent notation, ambiguous mathematical expressions, and lack of empirical validation. The paper's claims about being the first to establish theoretical guarantees for high-dimensional EM are inaccurate due to the oversight of [23].

While the core idea is interesting and potentially valuable, the paper's current form does not meet the standards for publication at NIPS. The authors need to thoroughly revise the paper to address these issues, including correcting the related work section, fixing all notational inconsistencies, providing rigorous justification for their statistical error claims, and adding empirical validation.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of technical and presentation flaws that significantly detract from the paper's scientific contribution. The paper would be suitable for publication after addressing these critical issues.
