- Proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm that adds a hard-thresholding truncation after the M-step to enforce sparsity.  
- Provides joint computational and statistical guarantees: geometric contraction to a neighborhood and near-minimax ℓ2 estimation rate O(√(s* log d / n)) (up to log n).  
- Handles both exact M-step maximization and gradient-step M-steps in the theoretical results.  
- Develops a decorrelated score inference procedure using a Dantzig-type projection on Q-function derivatives, proving asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency.  
- Instantiates the framework for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, with model-specific condition verifications (e.g., separation assumptions).  
- Extends low-dimensional EM theory and debiased inference methods to nonconvex latent variable models with high-dimensional nuisance parameters.  
- Clear algorithmic idea that mirrors hard-thresholded projected methods while preserving EM structure.  
- Strong joint computational-statistical analysis decomposing optimization and statistical errors under unified conditions.  
- Novel inference contribution linking Q-function derivatives to observed score/Fisher information and achieving efficiency.  
- Concrete model instantiations suggest broad applicability.  
- Requires a strong initializer within a constant ℓ2 radius and support-size control; practical provable initializers are not provided.  
- Truncation level b_s must be of order s*, which is unknown; lacks guidance or data-driven methods for choosing b_s.  
- Truncation breaks EM monotonicity; practical convergence diagnostics and potential cycling are not discussed.  
- Main theoretical results rely on resampling/split-sample analysis rather than the single-sample iterates used in practice.  
- Empirical validation is lacking — needs synthetic and real-data experiments for rates, robustness, and inference coverage/type I error.  
- Missing comparisons and positioning relative to IHT/projected gradient literature and penalized EM (e.g., Blumensath & Davies; Jain et al.; Städler et al.).  
- Inference assumptions require sparsity of the decorrelation vector w* and several concentration/Lipschitz conditions that may be restrictive in mixtures.  
- Technical development is solid and carefully structured, with insightful use of entrywise (ℓ1) error control for truncation effects.  
- Reliance on strong initialization and resampling, plus lack of experiments, weakens practical guarantees.  
- Manuscript is dense and notation-heavy with some formatting issues; needs clearer notation and a concise "Assumptions at a glance" table.  
- Suggested additions: concrete initializer proposals, adaptive truncation or ℓ1-penalized alternatives, finite-sample Gaussian-mixture bounds, and guidance for Dantzig λ selection with simulations.  
- Pros: strong general theory, near-optimal rates, and semiparametric-efficient inference adapted to EM.  
- Cons: strong/unvalidated initialization/tuning assumptions, resampling-based analysis, no empirical results, and incomplete related-work positioning.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept — substantial theoretical contributions but needs better practical guidance, related-work context, and empirical validation.
