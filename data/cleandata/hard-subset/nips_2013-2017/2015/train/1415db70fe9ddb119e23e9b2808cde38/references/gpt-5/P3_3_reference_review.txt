Summary
The paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm by inserting a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after each E/M-step to enforce sparsity, and provides unified computational and statistical guarantees. The main estimation result shows that, starting from a suitably accurate initialization, the iterates contract geometrically up to a statistical error floor of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to log factors), which is near-minimax optimal. For inference, the paper introduces a decorrelated score (and Wald) construction tailored to EM: it leverages Fisher’s/Louis’ identities to relate derivatives of the Q-function to the score and Fisher information, estimates a projection vector via a Dantzig selector, and proves asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency for a low-dimensional component in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The framework covers both exact M-steps and gradient EM, and is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under high SNR and sparsity. The analysis uses a resampling variant of EM to control statistical error across iterations.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on Balakrishnan et al. (EM from population to sample) and Tseng (proximal point view), but extends them to the high-dimensional regime by incorporating sparsity via truncation. It also connects to the debiasing/desparsification literature (van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang, Bühlmann et al.) for inference with high-dimensional nuisance, but adapts it to nonconcave latent-variable likelihoods where global convex optima are infeasible. Compared to tensor methods, the paper argues improved statistical rates. To my knowledge, asymptotic inference with semiparametric efficiency for latent-variable models with high-dimensional nuisance via EM is new and of interest.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The decomposition of error into optimization and statistical components is clean and aligns with modern high-dimensional analysis. The contraction relies on local strong concavity and Lipschitz gradient conditions for the population Q(·; θ*), which are plausible under separation/SNR assumptions and verified (per appendix) for the examples. The transition from sample to population maps is handled via an entrywise ℓ1 control (Condition 3.3), which is the right scale to survive truncation. The inference construction correctly exploits Fisher’s identity: ∇θ Qn(θ; θ)/n equals the score, and E[Tn(θ*)] equals Fisher information, making the decorrelated score well-motivated and ultimately semiparametrically efficient.
- Assumptions and limitations: Guarantees require (i) good initialization (relative ℓ2 error < constant), (ii) a basin of attraction with strong concavity/smoothness of Q(·; θ*) and Lipschitz gradient conditions, typically enforced by high SNR and/or separation, (iii) knowledge of an upper bound on s* to set the truncation level b_s on the order of s*, and (iv) sparsity of the projection vector w* solving Iηη w = Iηα (s_w* small). The analysis uses a resampling (sample-splitting across iterations) EM for technical convenience; transfer of guarantees to standard EM without resampling is not established. The Gaussian mixture example fixes equal mixing weights and spherical components with known variance, which is a restricted setting.
- Completeness: The paper is predominantly theoretical. There are no experiments demonstrating the practicality, robustness to model misspecification, impact of truncation, or comparison with ℓ1-penalized EM or tensor methods. Given NeurIPS standards, some empirical validation would strengthen the contribution.

Clarity
The high-level ideas are clear, but the presentation is dense. Conditions 3.1–3.3 and 4.1–4.4 are abstract and deferred; it would help to bring concrete instantiations (constants, rates) for the two example models into the main text. The notation around mixed derivatives r1, r2 and Tn could be streamlined with a brief “Louis identity” summary. The bibliography is incomplete/garbled (years missing, author names mangled); this must be fixed. Some LaTeX artifacts remain.

Originality
- Methodologically, combining EM with iterative hard-thresholding and analyzing support evolution to obtain near-minimax rates is novel in this context.
- The decorrelated score tailored to EM via Q-function derivatives and Dantzig-based nuisance projection appears new for latent-variable models and achieves semiparametric efficiency.
- Prior works do not provide simultaneous computational guarantees and valid inference for high-dimensional latent-variable models; this paper fills that gap.

Significance
If the conditions are met, the results offer a blueprint for statistically optimal estimation and valid inference for a broad class of latent-variable models where convex formulations are unavailable. This could influence both theory and practice. However, the strong conditions (initialization, SNR, sparsity of w*), and lack of empirical evidence, temper immediate practical impact.

Pros
- Clear advance: simultaneous optimization and statistical guarantees for high-dimensional EM.
- Near-minimax estimation rates with explicit optimization vs. statistical error tradeoff.
- Asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency via a principled decorrelation construction.
- Coverage of both exact and gradient EM; modular conditions that can be verified per model.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on strong assumptions: accurate initialization, local strong concavity/Lipschitzness, high SNR, and sparsity of w*; equal-weight, spherical GMM in examples.
- Analysis uses resampling EM; no result for the standard single-sample iteration.
- Need to know or tightly upper-bound s* to set b_s; guidance for tuning is limited.
- No empirical results; unclear robustness to misspecification and finite-sample behavior.
- Incomplete and messy references; some presentation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide experiments on synthetic data for GMM and mixture regression: compare to ℓ1-penalized EM, gradient EM with soft-thresholding, and tensor methods; report estimation error, support recovery, and coverage/power of the proposed tests under varying SNR, n, d, and s*.
- Discuss practical initialization (e.g., spectral/tensor, K-means, method-of-moments) and quantify the basin’s size vs. SNR; include ablations showing sensitivity to initialization.
- Address the gap between resampling EM analysis and the standard EM; at least give heuristic arguments and empirical evidence that the results transfer.
- Justify and discuss the sparsity of w*: when is Iηα approximately supported on a few columns of Iηη? Provide examples and counterexamples.
- Offer practical tuning guidelines for b_s, step size, and Dantzig λ (data-driven rules).
- Clean up notation and references; include more of the model-specific verifications in the main text.

Overall recommendation
Technically strong and novel, with potentially high impact, but currently lacking empirical validation and with several strong assumptions. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can add convincing experiments and clarify the practical aspects; otherwise borderline.
