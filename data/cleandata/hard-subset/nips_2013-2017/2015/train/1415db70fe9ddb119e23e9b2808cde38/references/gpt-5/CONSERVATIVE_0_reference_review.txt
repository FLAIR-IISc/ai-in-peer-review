Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a “high-dimensional EM” procedure that augments the classical EM with a truncation step (hard-thresholding) to enforce sparsity at each iteration. The authors analyze two M-step implementations (exact maximization and gradient ascent) and, under local smoothness/strong concavity and Lipschitz-type conditions on the population Q-function, show geometric contraction of the optimization error plus an L1-based statistical error that yields a near-minimax rate O(s*^0.5 (log d / n)^0.5) (up to logs) after sufficient iterations, given a suitable initialization within a basin of attraction. For inference, they construct a decorrelated score test by estimating a “decorrelating” vector via a Dantzig selector fed by an empirical analogue of the Fisher information derived from the EM Q-function; they prove asymptotic normality with variance achieving the semiparametric information bound, under sparsity of the nuisance and of the decorrelation vector.

This extends the population-to-sample EM contraction framework of Balakrishnan et al. (low-d) to the high-d regime by adding truncation, and adapts decorrelated/debiased inference ideas (van de Geer et al., Ning & Liu, Zhang & Zhang) to latent variable models using EM-specific identities. The paper sketches implications for sparse Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under separation/SNR conditions. Related penalized EM and tensor methods are discussed; the authors argue for improved rates and broader applicability.

Strengths
- Clear high-level goal: bridge statistical guarantees and computation for latent variable models in high dimensions, addressing both estimation and inference.
- Theoretical contributions: (i) contraction analysis with a nonconvex truncation operator integrated into EM, yielding near-minimax rates; (ii) inference via a decorrelated score achieving the semiparametric bound; (iii) treatment of both exact and gradient M-steps.
- Conceptual unification: mapping EM derivatives to likelihood score/Fisher information (Theorem 2.1) is clean and facilitates inference.
- Assumptions and conditions are stated explicitly, with model-specific specializations (e.g., for GMMs with sufficient SNR).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Lack of empirical validation. There are no experiments demonstrating practical performance, robustness, or the feasibility of tuning (bs, step sizes, Dantzig λ) on finite samples. Given the nonconvexity and sensitivity of EM, this is a serious omission for NeurIPS. Comparisons to established baselines (penalized likelihood with EM or coordinate descent, proximal-gradient with ℓ1, de-biased GLM approaches where applicable, tensor/spectral) are needed.
- Reliance on a resampling (sample-splitting across iterations) analysis. While common as a proof device, it leaves a gap to the practical algorithm that reuses the full data each iteration; the paper does not close this gap or quantify the price paid by splitting.
- Strong and somewhat opaque conditions. Guarantees require: (i) good initialization within a specified basin (not explained how to obtain in high-d latent models); (ii) strong concavity/Lipschitz conditions on Q around the truth; (iii) sufficiently large SNR and sparsity; (iv) sparsity of the decorrelation vector w* (a structural condition on Fisher information). The practicality and generality of these assumptions are not adequately justified or diagnosed.
- Sensitivity to tuning and model misspecification. The truncation level bs must be on the order of the unknown s*, and thresholds/λ depend on concentration rates and unknown constants. No guidance or stability study is provided. Hard-thresholding is discontinuous and can be brittle.
- Relation to existing methods could be tighter. The truncation step is closely related to Iterative Hard Thresholding / HTP and projected gradient for sparse estimation; penalized EM has a long history for mixtures (e.g., ℓ1-penalized EM, regularized M-step). The novelty relative to “EM + ℓ1 penalty” (and their theoretical analyses) should be sharpened. For inference, connections to de-biasing in nonconvex M-estimation and to EM-based inference literature could be expanded.
- Presentation quality: numerous notation/formatting issues (symbols garbled, missing years/authors in references) hinder readability. Many essential details are deferred to the appendix; the main paper feels heavy on conditions and light on intuition/guidelines.

Quality
The theoretical development appears careful and leverages established tools (local strong concavity, concentration, Dantzig selector analysis). However, the heavy reliance on sample splitting, strong local assumptions, and the absence of experimental corroboration limit confidence in practical utility. Proof sketches in the main text are minimal; it is unclear whether all technical lemmas fit within the page limit.

Clarity
Concepts are defined, and algorithms are stated. Yet, the manuscript suffers from formatting errors, dense notation, and many deferred details. It would benefit from a streamlined exposition, clearer guidance on initialization/tuning, and illustrative figures or pseudocode examples specialized to GMM/MoR.

Originality
Moderate. Bringing hard-thresholding explicitly into EM and proving near-minimax rates is a useful synthesis, and the decorrelated score via EM identities is a nice twist. But both components build on well-established ideas (EM contraction analyses; IHT/projection for sparsity; de-biasing/Dantzig-based inference). The claim of the first inference framework for high-d latent variable models seems plausible but should be positioned more carefully against penalized EM and recent nonconvex inference works.

Significance
Potentially meaningful for theory-oriented audiences working on high-d latent variable models. Without experiments and clearer guidance, impact on practice is uncertain. The inference component could be influential if its assumptions and implementation are shown to hold in realistic settings.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide empirical studies on synthetic and real data for GMM and mixture of regressions: estimation error vs n,d,s*, robustness to mis-specified bs, effect of initialization, comparisons to ℓ1-penalized EM and tensor/spectral methods, and coverage of the proposed tests.
- Clarify how to obtain the required initialization in high dimensions (e.g., spectral/tensor starts, method-of-moments, thresholded estimators), and analyze its cost.
- Discuss whether the guarantees extend to the practical, non-resampled algorithm, or provide a stability argument without sample splitting.
- Offer data-driven tuning strategies for bs and the Dantzig parameter, and analyze sensitivity.
- Better situate the truncation step relative to IHT/HTP and penalized EM literature; add missing citations and fix the bibliography.
- Explain computational overhead of computing Tn and solving the Dantzig program per test, and how it scales with d and n.

Pros
- Solid theoretical framework combining estimation and inference.
- Near-minimax rates and semiparametric efficiency under stated conditions.
- Addresses an important class of models (latent variable, nonconvex likelihoods).

Cons
- No experimental validation.
- Strong assumptions (initialization, SNR, sparsity of w*), limited guidance for practice.
- Analysis depends on resampling; gap to the practical algorithm.
- Presentation and references need significant polishing.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contributions are interesting, but without empirical evidence and clearer practical guidance, I am not comfortable endorsing a method that departs from established (penalized) approaches. Strengthening experiments, clarifying initialization/tuning, and tightening the relation to prior art would substantially improve the paper.
