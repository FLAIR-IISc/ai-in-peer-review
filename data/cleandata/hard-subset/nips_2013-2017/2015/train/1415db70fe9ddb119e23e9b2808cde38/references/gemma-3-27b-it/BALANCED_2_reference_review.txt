This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, a setting where existing results fall short. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and establish statistical and computational guarantees, including convergence rates and asymptotic normality of a decorrelated score statistic. The work is well-motivated, addressing a significant gap in the literature.

**Summary of Contributions:** The core idea is to add a truncation step to the standard EM algorithm’s E and M-steps, enforcing sparsity and enabling theoretical analysis in the high-dimensional regime (d > n). They prove that, under reasonable conditions, the algorithm converges at an optimal rate (p s* log d/n) and that the resulting estimator allows for asymptotically normal inference via a novel decorrelated score statistic. The framework is presented generally and illustrated with Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important open problem:**  The lack of theoretical guarantees for EM in high-dimensional settings is a well-known issue, and this paper makes a substantial contribution towards closing that gap.
*   **Novel approach with truncation:** The proposed truncation step is a clever idea that allows the authors to enforce sparsity and derive meaningful results.
*   **Strong theoretical results:** The convergence rate and asymptotic normality results are mathematically rigorous and represent a significant advance. The p s* log d/n rate is a key achievement.
*   **Unified framework:**  The paper presents a general framework applicable to a broad class of latent variable models, increasing its impact.
*   **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-structured, with a logical flow of ideas.
*   **Connections to related work:** The authors clearly position their work within the existing literature, acknowledging previous contributions and highlighting the novelty of their approach. They appropriately discuss the limitations of existing methods (e.g., tensor methods) in high dimensions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The conditions required for the theorems (especially Assumption 4.5) seem rather strong and potentially difficult to verify in practice. The sparsity requirement on w* (related to the Fisher information) is particularly noteworthy and could limit the applicability of the results.  A discussion of how easily these conditions might be met for common latent variable models beyond the examples would be beneficial.
*   **Computational complexity:** While the paper establishes computational guarantees, it does not delve into the computational cost of the truncation step itself. It would be valuable to provide some analysis of the complexity of the truncated EM algorithm compared to standard EM.
*   **Limited empirical evaluation:** The paper is heavily theoretical, and lacks any empirical validation. While not strictly required for a NIPS submission, demonstrating the practical performance of the algorithm on synthetic or real datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.  Even a small-scale simulation could offer valuable insight.
*   **Notation:** While generally clear, the notation is dense, and frequent references to equations are needed to understand the various quantities defined. A table of notations would be helpful.
*   **Dependence on Initialization:** While the paper addresses initialization, the requirement of an initialization within a certain relative error bound (init ⇤ /k ⇤k 2 < ✏) could be a limitation. Discussing practical strategies to achieve this initialization, or exploring the sensitivity to initialization, would add value.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental open problem in statistical machine learning.
*   Provides a mathematically rigorous solution with strong theoretical guarantees.
*   Presents a novel and insightful approach with the truncation step.
*   The results have the potential to significantly impact the development of statistical inference procedures for high-dimensional latent variable models.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Strong assumptions that may limit practical applicability.
*   Lack of empirical validation.
*   Potential computational challenges associated with the truncation step.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The results are mathematically rigorous and the approach is novel and insightful.  However, the strong assumptions and lack of empirical validation raise some concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should address the concerns regarding the assumptions, provide a discussion of the computational complexity, and ideally include some empirical results.  Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. Specifically, exploring strategies for achieving the required initialization, and a sensitivity analysis to its quality, is crucial. A clearer discussion on the practicality of verifying the Fisher information assumptions (Assumption 4.5) would also be valuable.
