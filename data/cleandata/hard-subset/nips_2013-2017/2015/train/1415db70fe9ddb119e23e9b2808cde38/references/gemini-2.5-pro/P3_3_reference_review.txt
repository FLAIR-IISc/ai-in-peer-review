This paper presents a novel framework for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models using a modified Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The core contribution is a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" that incorporates a hard truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity along the optimization path. The authors provide a unified and rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating that under certain conditions, this algorithm achieves two key goals: (1) for parameter estimation, it converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that attains the near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d)/n)`; (2) for inference, a proposed "decorrelated score statistic" built upon the algorithm's output is asymptotically normal and its variance achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. The theory is general enough to cover both exact and approximate (gradient-based) M-steps and is applied to high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

This work significantly advances the theory of the EM algorithm, a cornerstone of statistical machine learning. It builds upon recent progress in understanding the non-asymptotic behavior of EM in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017), but the extension to the high-dimensional (d >> n) setting is highly non-trivial. The introduction of the truncation step is a natural and elegant way to incorporate sparsity, but its analysis requires a more refined, entrywise control of statistical errors, which is a key technical contribution. Furthermore, developing a valid inferential procedure for the output of an iterative algorithm on a non-convex surface is a major challenge. The paper makes a strong contribution by connecting its approach to the broader literature on debiasing for high-dimensional inference (e.g., Zhang & Zhang, 2014; van de Geer et al., 2014), but adapts it to the specific structure of the EM algorithm and the non-convex nature of the problem.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing gap between the widespread practical use of the EM algorithm and its theoretical understanding in the high-dimensional regime. Providing a computationally feasible algorithm with guarantees for both optimal estimation and valid inference is a major contribution to the field.
*   **Novelty and Originality:** The proposed High Dimensional EM algorithm with an integrated truncation step is novel, as is the accompanying unified theoretical framework. The development of a provably optimal decorrelated score test for the output of this iterative, non-convex procedure is a particularly impressive and original result.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is deep and appears to be sound. The decomposition of the error into statistical and optimization components is clean, and the proof techniques required to handle the interaction between the EM updates and the truncation step are sophisticated. The results are comprehensive, covering convergence rates, statistical precision, and asymptotic normality for inference.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly states its contributions, situates them within related work, and presents the main algorithm and theorems in an accessible manner. The use of general conditions before verifying them for specific models is an effective way to structure the theoretical argument.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **The Initialization Requirement:** A significant practical limitation, common to most analyses of non-convex methods, is the requirement of an initial estimate that is already in a local basin of attraction of the true parameter. The paper assumes `||θ_init - θ*||_2 ≤ R/2` but does not discuss how such an initialization might be obtained for high-dimensional latent variable models. Could the authors comment on potential initialization strategies (e.g., sparse spectral methods, or a regularized convex relaxation) and whether they are likely to satisfy the required conditions? This is crucial for the algorithm's practical applicability.

2.  **Theory vs. Practice (Resampling):** The main theoretical results are stated for a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4 in the appendix), where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration to ensure independence. This is a common theoretical tool but differs from the standard implementation (Algorithm 1) where the full dataset is used repeatedly. Could the authors clarify the extent to which the theoretical guarantees are expected to hold for the more practical non-resampling algorithm? Is this gap purely a matter of proof technique, or is there a fundamental obstacle?

3.  **Tuning Parameter Selection:** The algorithm's performance and its theoretical guarantees rely on the choice of the sparsity parameter `bs`. Theorem 3.4 provides a condition on `bs` that depends on the unknown true sparsity `s*`. Similarly, the decorrelated score test depends on a tuning parameter `λ` in the Dantzig selector. How should these parameters be chosen in practice? A discussion on data-driven selection methods (e.g., cross-validation) and the robustness of the results to the choice of these parameters would greatly enhance the paper's practical value.

4.  **Complexity of Conditions:** While the application to Gaussian Mixture Models is a strong point, the final sample complexity condition required for inference (`max{s_w*, (s*)^2} * (s*)^2 * (log d)^5 = o(n / (log n)^2)`) is quite complex and appears restrictive. It suggests that the method might not be applicable for models with moderately large sparsity `s*`. Could the authors provide more intuition on this condition and discuss whether it is an artifact of the analysis or fundamental to the problem?

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes fundamental contributions to the theory of high-dimensional statistics and the EM algorithm. The results are strong, novel, and significant. The weaknesses identified above are primarily related to the gap between theory and practice, which is common in this area of research. Addressing these points, even through discussion, would further strengthen an already outstanding manuscript. The paper is technically deep, clearly written, and of high quality, making it an ideal fit for NIPS.
