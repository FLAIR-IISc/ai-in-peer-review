Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models via a modified EM algorithm. The main ideas are:
- High-dimensional EM with truncation: augment the classical EM iteration with a hard-thresholding (T-step) that retains the bs largest coordinates after each M-step (either exact maximization or one-step gradient ascent). This enforces sparsity along the trajectory.
- Unified computational-statistical analysis: under local (basin-of-attraction) conditions and suitable initialization, the iterate error decomposes into a geometric optimization error plus a statistical error of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to polylog factors), where s* is the true sparsity.
- Asymptotic inference via decorrelated score: construct a Dantzig-type estimator of the nuisance “orthogonalization” vector w and form a decorrelated score using derivatives of the Q-function. The test statistic is asymptotically normal and achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound for the low-dimensional component of interest in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters.

The analysis provides two M-step implementations (exact argmax and gradient step) and verifies conditions for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, yielding near-minimax estimation rates and asymptotic normality under sparsity and SNR assumptions.

Relation to prior work: In the low-dimensional regime, [2] established geometric convergence of EM (population-to-sample) with rates O(sqrt(d/n)). The present work adapts that perspective to high dimensions by incorporating hard-thresholding and using entrywise concentration to control the statistical error, akin in spirit to iterative hard-thresholding frameworks, but tailored to the nonconvex EM map. For inference, the decorrelated/debiased literature (e.g., [13], [26], [32], [24]) provides asymptotic normality in convex M-estimation (GLMs, linear models). This paper extends such inference to nonconvex latent variable models by re-expressing scores and Fisher information through the Q-function (Theorem 2.1) and estimating the orthogonalization via a Dantzig program. Compared with tensor methods (e.g., [1], [9]), which often yield suboptimal rates and focus on low-d settings, this work targets high-d sparse settings with near-optimal rates. For high-dimensional mixtures, prior works typically gave support recovery or noiseless recovery (e.g., [31], [23]); this paper adds parameter error rates and valid inference.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual advance: integrates sparsity into EM via a simple truncation step and provides a unified analysis covering exact and approximate M-steps.
- Sharp statistical rates: achieves near-minimax sqrt(s* log d / n) rates for estimation (up to logs) under explicit conditions, improving over low-d EM analyses and over tensor-based rates for the considered models.
- Inference contribution: develops a decorrelated score built from Q-function derivatives, shows asymptotic normality, and attains the semiparametric information bound. This appears novel for nonconvex latent variable models estimated by EM.
- General framework: conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity-smoothness, and entrywise statistical error) are transparent and verified for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions. The treatment of both exact and gradient M-steps is useful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Initialization and basin assumptions: Results require an initializer within a fixed-radius basin. Practical procedures to obtain such initializers (e.g., spectral, method-of-moments, or k-means with separation conditions) are not detailed in the main text, and the sensitivity to initialization is not empirically illustrated.
- Choice of truncation level bs: Theory assumes bs is on the order of s*. Guidance for selecting bs in practice (e.g., cross-validation, information criteria, or stability selection) is not discussed. Robustness to mis-specification of bs would be valuable.
- Resampling analysis: The main estimation theorem focuses on a resampling version of the algorithm. While common in analyses, it would strengthen the paper to provide a non-resampling analysis (or at least a clear argument or experiment suggesting similar behavior without resampling).
- Assumption strength: For Gaussian mixtures, the SNR requirement is strong (large r). Inference further assumes sparsity of w* (the orthogonalization vector), i.e., s_w* = o(sqrt(n/log d)). Whether this sparsity is natural for typical latent variable models should be discussed more explicitly.
- Practical computation of inference: The Dantzig program for w can be expensive at scale (needs Tn and matrix-vector operations). Alternatives (e.g., nodewise regressions or CLIME-style approximations) and tuning (choice of λ in (2.6)) are not discussed.
- Clarity and presentation: The manuscript is dense. Some references appear incomplete or garbled. Certain constants and “extra log factors” are left implicit. The connection between the T-step and support evolution could use more intuition. It would help to centralize assumptions for each model with explicit sample size scaling and constants.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The decomposition of optimization vs. entrywise statistical error, and the control via Lipschitz-gradient plus concavity-smoothness is standard and appears sound. The inference result leverages Theorem 2.1 to connect Q-derivatives to the score/Fisher information, and the orthogonalization follows established debiasing logic adapted to EM. The assumptions are strong but transparent.
- Completeness: Proof sketches are outlined with full details deferred to appendices. Model-specific verifications are claimed. Empirical validation (even brief) is absent in the provided text.

Clarity
- The high-level structure is clear, but readability would benefit from:
  - A concise algorithm box for both implementations with all tuning parameters specified.
  - A summary table of conditions and resulting rates for each model.
  - Practical guidance on initialization and bs selection.
  - Cleanup of references and typographical issues.

Originality
- The truncation-enhanced EM with rigorous high-d rates and the decorrelated score for EM-based estimators appear novel. The combination of computational and inferential guarantees for nonconvex latent variable models is original relative to prior EM and debiasing literatures.

Significance
- Potentially significant: provides a pathway to statistically optimal estimation and valid inference for a broad class of latent variable models in high dimensions, a gap not fully addressed by convex M-estimation or tensor methods. Practical impact depends on availability of initializers and robustness to tuning.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theoretical contributions bridging computation and inference in high-d latent variable models.
- Near-optimal estimation rates and semiparametric efficiency for inference.
- General framework covering multiple models and M-step variants.

Cons against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on strong assumptions (good initialization, SNR, sparsity of w*).
- Lack of guidance on tuning and practical implementation; no empirical validation.
- Resampling-based analysis leaves open questions about the standard (single-sample) iteration.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide practical initialization strategies (with citations) and experiments demonstrating robustness to initialization.
- Offer data-driven methods to choose bs and Dantzig tuning; assess sensitivity.
- Give a non-resampling analysis or empirical evidence that the standard algorithm behaves similarly.
- Discuss computational costs and possible approximations for w estimation.
- Clarify constants, explicit sample complexity, and log factors; fix reference formatting.

Scope
- The topic fits NeurIPS areas (optimization, statistical learning theory, high-dimensional inference).

Overall assessment
- The paper makes a solid theoretical advance with clear novelty and potential impact, though practical guidance and empirical support are limited in the current draft.
