

# Peer Review: "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical gap in the literature by developing a theoretical framework for the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation step to enforce sparsity, enabling both optimal parameter estimation and asymptotic inference. The key contributions are: (1) a high-dimensional EM algorithm with theoretical guarantees for convergence to a near-minimax optimal estimator, and (2) a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components of high-dimensional parameters, with asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency. The framework is validated on Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models, with theoretical guarantees for both estimation and inference.

The paper builds upon and extends the low-dimensional EM analysis by [2], which established geometric convergence rates but only for low-dimensional settings. The authors correctly identify that existing high-dimensional methods (like Lasso-based approaches) are not directly applicable to latent variable models due to the non-convexity of the likelihood function and the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The paper distinguishes itself by providing the first theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings, along with a unified framework for both estimation and inference.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Significant Contribution**: The paper addresses a fundamental gap in the literature by providing the first theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. This is particularly important as high-dimensional latent variable models are widely used in practice (e.g., in genomics, finance, and signal processing), yet no computational-statistical guarantees existed for the EM algorithm in such settings.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical framework with well-structured theorems. The decomposition of the estimation error into optimization error (geometric decay) and statistical error (near-optimal rate) is elegant and well-motivated. The asymptotic normality of the decorrelated score statistic is a significant contribution, as it enables valid statistical inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.

3. **Careful Comparison with Related Work**: The paper thoroughly compares with existing work, particularly [2] (low-dimensional EM analysis), Lasso-based methods, and tensor-based approaches for latent variable models. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs and improves upon previous approaches, especially regarding the handling of high-dimensional nuisance parameters.

4. **Technical Rigor**: The paper demonstrates strong technical depth in establishing the theoretical guarantees. The conditions (Conditions 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.4, and Assumption 4.5) are well-motivated and carefully chosen to balance generality with tractability. The verification of these conditions for specific models (Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression) in the appendix is a strength.

5. **Practical Relevance**: The paper's framework is not just theoretically interesting but also practically relevant. The truncation step provides a natural way to incorporate sparsity assumptions in high-dimensional settings, which is crucial for many real-world applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Verification of Assumptions in Main Text**: The verification of the key assumptions (Conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.4) for the specific models (Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression) is deferred to the appendix. While this is common practice, for a conference paper, the main text should include at least the key verification steps for the most important models, especially since these are the primary applications of the framework.

2. **Lack of Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper does not provide any analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm. For high-dimensional problems, computational efficiency is as important as statistical guarantees. The paper should discuss the number of iterations required for convergence and how the truncation step affects computational complexity.

3. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't discuss how to choose the sparsity parameter bs in practice. While the theoretical analysis specifies a value for bs (equation 3.8), it's unclear how one would select this parameter in real applications. The authors should discuss cross-validation or other data-driven methods for selecting bs.

4. **Limited Discussion of Minimax Optimality**: The paper claims the statistical rate is "near-minimax optimal" but doesn't provide a minimax lower bound for comparison. While this is common in high-dimensional statistics, explicitly stating the minimax lower bound for the specific models would strengthen the claim.

5. **Sparse Structure of w***: The paper assumes that w* (defined in equation 4.1) is sparse (s*<sub>w</sub> = o(√n/log d)), but doesn't explain why this assumption is reasonable for the specific models they consider. For example, in Gaussian mixture models, why would w* be sparse? This assumption is crucial for the inference guarantees but lacks justification.

## Detailed Comments

### Quality

The paper's technical quality is generally high, with careful theoretical analysis. However, I have concerns about the practical applicability of the results. The paper establishes convergence guarantees, but the rate depends on the basin of attraction B and the initialization condition (init - θ* ≤ ε). While the paper states that "given a suitable initialization," the paper doesn't provide a practical method for obtaining such an initialization in high-dimensional settings. For example, in Gaussian mixture models, how would one obtain a suitable initialization when d >> n?

The paper's claim about the statistical error being "near-minimax optimal" is somewhat ambiguous. The paper states that the error is proportional to √(s* log d/n), which matches the minimax rate for sparse high-dimensional models. However, the paper doesn't provide a minimax lower bound for the specific latent variable models they consider, which would be necessary to claim "near-minimax optimality."

### Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to theory. However, some technical details could be clarified:

- The truncation step (line 6 of Algorithm 1) is crucial to the proposed method, but the intuition for why this step is necessary and how it affects the convergence properties is not as clearly explained as it could be.

- The definition of the decorrelated score statistic (equation 2.7) is quite dense. The paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why this specific statistic achieves asymptotic normality.

- The relationship between the population version and sample version of the M-step is sometimes confusing. For example, it's not immediately clear why Condition 3.3 uses the `1-norm for the statistical error, while the main estimation error is measured in `2-norm.

### Originality

This paper is highly original in the context of high-dimensional EM algorithms. The truncation step to enforce sparsity is a natural and effective approach that has not been previously applied to the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The development of the decorrelated score statistic for inference in latent variable models is particularly novel, as most existing high-dimensional inference methods (like Lasso-based approaches) don't apply to latent variable models due to the non-convexity of the likelihood.

The paper does a good job of distinguishing itself from related work. For example, the comparison with [2] (which focuses on low-dimensional settings) is particularly strong, as the authors correctly identify the key challenge of high dimensionality and the need for the truncation step to handle it.

### Significance

The significance of this work is high. The paper addresses a fundamental gap in the literature, providing the first theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. This is important because the EM algorithm is the most popular approach for latent variable models, yet no theoretical guarantees existed for high-dimensional settings. The framework is general and could be applied to many other latent variable models beyond those considered in the paper.

The paper's contribution to statistical inference in high-dimensional latent variable models is particularly significant. Most existing methods for high-dimensional inference (like Lasso-based approaches) don't apply to latent variable models, so the decorrelated score statistic provides a valuable new tool for researchers working with these models.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Include key verification steps in main text**: Add a few key steps from the appendix that verify the conditions for the Gaussian mixture model and mixture of regression in the main text. This will help readers quickly grasp the applicability of the framework.

2. **Add computational complexity analysis**: Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, including the number of iterations required for convergence and how the truncation step affects the per-iteration complexity.

3. **Discuss practical implementation**: Add a short section on how to choose the sparsity parameter bs in practice, possibly with a brief discussion of cross-validation or other data-driven methods.

4. **Clarify the minimax optimality claim**: Either provide a minimax lower bound for the specific models considered or rephrase the claim to be more precise (e.g., "achieves the same statistical rate as the minimax lower bound for sparse high-dimensional models").

5. **Justify the sparsity assumption on w***: Explain why w* is expected to be sparse for the specific models considered, or discuss the implications if this assumption is violated.

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to the field. The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm and decorrelated score statistic address a fundamental gap in the literature and provide valuable theoretical guarantees for high-dimensional latent variable models. While the paper has some weaknesses in presentation and practical implementation details, these are not major flaws in the theoretical contribution.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria:
- **Quality**: High technical quality with strong theoretical results
- **Clarity**: Well-written and organized, with some minor improvements needed
- **Originality**: Highly original contribution to the field
- **Significance**: Addresses a fundamental gap with high practical relevance

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the specific suggestions above.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- First theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings
- Novel truncation step effectively enforces sparsity for high-dimensional latent variable models
- First framework for asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models
- Strong theoretical results with near-optimal statistical rates and semiparametric efficiency
- Comprehensive comparison with related work, clearly distinguishing the paper's contributions

**Con Acceptance:**
- Verification of assumptions for specific models deferred to appendix
- No computational complexity analysis provided
- Limited discussion of practical implementation details (e.g., choosing sparsity parameter)
- Ambiguous claim about "near-minimax optimality" without minimax lower bound
- Sparse structure of w* not adequately justified for specific models

The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and the minor revisions suggested would substantially strengthen the paper. This is a valuable contribution to the field that should be accepted for publication at NIPS.
