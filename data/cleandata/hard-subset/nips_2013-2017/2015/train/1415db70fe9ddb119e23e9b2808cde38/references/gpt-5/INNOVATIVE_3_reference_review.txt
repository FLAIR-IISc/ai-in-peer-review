Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm that augments the classical EM with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step to enforce sparsity along the iterations. The authors provide a unified analysis showing (i) geometric decay of optimization error plus a sharp statistical error of order √(s*)·√(log d / n) (up to logs) under suitable initialization and basin conditions, and (ii) an inferential procedure based on a decorrelated score (via a Dantzig selector) that yields asymptotically normal tests for low-dimensional components with semiparametric efficiency, despite high-dimensional nuisance parameters and the nonconvexity inherent in latent variable models. The framework covers both exact and gradient-ascent M-steps and is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

This work builds on low-dimensional EM analyses (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) and on high-dimensional inference (debiased/desparsified estimators, Dantzig selector), but extends them in two key ways: (a) it treats the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional regime with an explicit sparsity-enforcing truncation in the loop, delivering near-minimax estimation rates; and (b) it develops valid asymptotic inference for latent variable models without requiring a global optimum of a convex program—addressing a gap not covered by GLM-based de-biasing or tensor methods. Compared to penalized EM or gradient-EM with ℓ1 penalties in prior work, the novelty is in the algorithmic template with truncation and in the simultaneous optimization-statistics-inference guarantees for nonconvex latent models.

Quality
- Theory: Theorems separate optimization and statistical errors, with geometric contraction and (near-)optimal rates under model-specific conditions. The inference result is strong: a decorrelated score attaining the semiparametric information bound for the target component.
- Assumptions: Conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, local strong concavity/smoothness, statistical error in ℓ1) are standard in spirit but tailored to EM with latent structures; model verifications are sketched for GMMs and mixtures of regressions. The sparsity of w* (from the Fisher information) is clearly stated and necessary for high-dimensional inference.
- Analysis caveat: The main convergence proof uses a resampling (sample-splitting across iterations) variant for technical independence. It is not fully clear in the main text whether and how guarantees extend to the practical single-sample (no resampling) implementation; an argument or citation to transfer results would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
The paper is well organized: algorithm, conditions, main theorems, and model-specific implications. However, many crucial details (verifying conditions, constants, and full proofs) are deferred to the appendix; adding brief intuition on how the T-step interacts with the M-step (e.g., connections to iterative hard-thresholding dynamics) would aid readability. Some notation is dense; a small table clarifying Qn vs Q, Tn, and the role of w would help. A section on hyperparameter selection (bs, stepsize, Dantzig tuning) would improve practical clarity.

Originality
- Conceptual: Introducing a truncation step inside EM with rigorous control of both optimization trajectory and statistical error is novel in this setting.
- Inferential: Asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency for latent variable models fitted by EM (not a convex optimum) is, to my knowledge, new and important.
- Relation to prior sparse EM / ℓ1-penalized EM: While sparsity in mixtures has been studied via penalties, the hard-thresholding-in-the-loop algorithm with provable near-minimax rates and valid inference marks a distinct and substantial step forward.

Significance
This work closes a notable gap between computation (local EM) and statistics (high-dimensional estimation and inference) for latent variable models. If the assumptions hold, researchers gain a principled, computationally feasible path to both estimation and uncertainty quantification, which has been largely missing beyond GLMs. The ideas should stimulate follow-on work on broader latent models and on tightening analysis for single-sample EM.

Strengths
- Bold, clear conceptual advance: high-dim EM with truncation plus valid inference.
- Near-minimax estimation rates with geometric optimization convergence.
- Semiparametric efficiency for target components via decorrelated score.
- Covers both exact and gradient M-steps; unifies estimation and inference.
- Instantiations for GMM and mixture of regressions; conditions are interpretable (e.g., SNR).

Weaknesses and questions
- Resampling vs practical EM: Please clarify whether convergence and rates hold for the standard, data-reuse iteration (e.g., via martingale arguments, leave-one-out stability, or bounded dependence). If not, provide empirical evidence that resampling is not essential.
- Initialization: The guarantees require being in a basin with relative error < constant. How is initialization obtained in practice for mixtures (e.g., spectral/tensor init, k-means) and how sensitive are results to misinitialization?
- Choice of bs: The theory assumes bs ≍ s*. Provide a data-driven rule (e.g., information criteria, stability selection) and sensitivity analysis. Is there a path to adaptive truncation without knowing s*?
- Dantzig tuning and computational cost: Inference requires solving a high-dimensional Dantzig program. Discuss scalability and whether nodewise regression-style alternatives apply. Guidance on λ selection would help.
- Sparsity of w*: The assumption kw*|0 = o(√(n/log d)) can be restrictive depending on the Fisher information structure. Provide examples/intuition where this holds or fails in mixtures.
- Model assumptions: For GMMs, the SNR requirement and known/unknown variance assumptions should be made explicit; robustness to variance misspecification would be valuable.
- Empirics: Even a small experimental section comparing to ℓ1-penalized EM and tensor methods on synthetic and modest real data would strengthen the submission.

Pros for acceptance
- High novelty and ambition; addresses an important open gap.
- Strong, carefully structured theory with optimal rates and efficient inference.
- Broad relevance to high-dimensional latent variable modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical gap due to resampling analysis vs single-sample EM not fully bridged in main text.
- Limited guidance for hyperparameter selection and initialization.
- No experimental validation shown in the main submission.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a significant and novel direction: principled, high-dimensional EM with integrated inference in latent variable models. While some practical aspects could be elaborated, the conceptual contribution and theoretical strength outweigh the limitations.

Confidence
High on the novelty/significance assessment; moderate on the sharpness of assumptions across all latent models due to deferred appendices.
