- Addresses lack of theoretical guarantees for EM in high-dimensional (d > n) latent variable models.
- Proposes a truncation-based high-dimensional EM algorithm enforcing sparsity to enable theoretical analysis.
- Shows geometric convergence to an estimator with near-minimax rate O(√(s* log d / n)) with suitable initialization.
- Develops a decorrelated score statistic for testing low-dimensional components and proves its asymptotic normality.
- Demonstrates semiparametric efficiency of the decorrelated score and optimality versus alternatives.
- Verifies theoretical conditions for Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models; contrasts with tensor methods that have suboptimal rates.
- Significant theoretical contribution as the first EM guarantees in high-dimensional settings.
- Novel and simple algorithmic idea (truncation) that yields strong theoretical improvements.
- Provides a unified framework for both estimation and inference in high dimensions, which is uncommon.
- Strong connection to semiparametric efficiency, achieving the information bound.
- Addresses a practically relevant problem with a computationally feasible solution.
- Highly technical with dense notation and many conditions, limiting accessibility.
- Relies on sparsity assumption (s* << d), which restricts applicability to non-sparse settings.
- Lacks practical guidance for selecting the sparsity tuning parameter bs.
- Does not provide detailed computational complexity analysis, especially cost of truncation per iteration.
- Unclear how broadly the verified technical conditions extend beyond the two example models.
- Limited comparison to other high-dimensional methods (e.g., regularized/gradient EM variants).
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and proofs are detailed and well-structured.
- Concern about opacity of the statistical error bound in Condition 3.3 and its connection to s*.
- The decorrelated score argument and asymptotic normality proof are technically sound.
- Suggest clarifying the role and selection of bs, possibly via data-dependent methods.
- Suggest adding a practical example or simulation to demonstrate empirical performance.
- Recommend simplifying dense notation (e.g., more intuitive truncation operator notation).
- Advise discussing limitations of the sparsity assumption and extensions to less-sparse regimes.
- Recommend adding an analysis or discussion of computational complexity of the algorithm.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel theoretical contribution likely to stimulate further research despite technical density.
- Recommendation: Accept.
- Key arguments for acceptance: first high-dimensional EM guarantees, strong computational/statistical results, practical relevance, rigorous proofs, and efficient inferential procedure.
- Key arguments against acceptance: technical complexity hinders accessibility, reliance on sparsity limits applicability, and insufficient practical tuning/computational guidance.
