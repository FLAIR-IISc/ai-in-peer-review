### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings (d >> n). The authors address two fundamental challenges: obtaining an estimator with optimal statistical properties and performing valid statistical inference. Their main contributions are twofold:

1.  **A High-Dimensional EM Algorithm:** They propose a modified EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation (hard thresholding) step after the M-step in each iteration. This "T-step" enforces sparsity on the parameter estimates throughout the optimization path. The authors prove that, under appropriate conditions and with a good initialization, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate. The final estimator achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of √(s*·log d/n).

2.  **Asymptotic Inference:** They develop a procedure for hypothesis testing and uncertainty quantification based on the estimator from their algorithm. They construct a "decorrelated score statistic," adapting ideas from the high-dimensional inference literature (e.g., debiased Lasso) to the score function of latent variable models. They prove this statistic is asymptotically normal, with a limiting variance that achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound.

The theory is general and is illustrated with applications to high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models. This work significantly extends the low-dimensional analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) to the high-dimensional sparse regime, providing the first comprehensive framework for both estimation and inference for the EM algorithm when d >> n.

### Strengths

*   **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a problem of great practical and theoretical importance. The EM algorithm is a workhorse in statistics and machine learning, but its theoretical guarantees in the pervasive high-dimensional setting have been lacking. Providing a computationally feasible algorithm with guarantees for both optimal point estimation and valid inference is a major contribution that could have a substantial impact on the field.
*   **Technical Novelty and Depth:** The analysis is technically sophisticated and novel. The key innovation is the integration of a hard thresholding step into the EM iteration and the rigorous analysis of its effect on the optimization and statistical errors. This is non-trivial, as the truncation complicates the dynamics of the iterates. Furthermore, the adaptation of decorrelation/debiasing techniques to the score function derived from the EM Q-function, for an estimator that is not a global optimum of a convex program, is a significant technical achievement.
*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The results are very strong. The paper establishes a geometric convergence rate for the algorithm and a near-optimal statistical rate for the final estimator (Theorem 3.4). The asymptotic normality of the decorrelated score statistic (Theorem 4.6), along with its semiparametric efficiency, provides a powerful and theoretically sound tool for inference, which has been largely missing for these models in high dimensions.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly motivates the problem, explicitly states its contributions, and separates the theory for estimation and inference into distinct sections. The use of high-level conditions (e.g., Lipschitz-Gradient, Statistical-Error) makes the main theorems and their proofs more modular and comprehensible before delving into model-specific verifications.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **The Initialization Assumption:** The analysis hinges on a suitable initialization `init` that is already within a constant factor of the true parameter `θ*` in `l2`-norm. This is a very strong assumption and a common critique of theoretical analyses of EM. In high dimensions, finding such an initialization is a challenging problem in its own right. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of how one might obtain such an initial estimate in practice (e.g., via spectral methods or other moment-based approaches), even if those methods provide suboptimal rates. Acknowledging this limitation more directly and suggesting potential solutions would improve the paper's practical relevance.
*   **Practicality of Tuning Parameters:** The proposed algorithm and inference procedure introduce crucial tuning parameters. The estimation algorithm requires `bs`, the sparsity level for truncation, which in Theorem 3.4 depends on the true (and unknown) sparsity `s*`. The inference procedure requires a tuning parameter `λ` for the Dantzig selector, whose theoretical value depends on unknown problem-dependent constants. The paper provides no guidance on how to select these parameters in practice (e.g., via cross-validation). This is a critical gap between the theory and a usable method.
*   **Analysis of the "Resampling Version":** As stated in Section 3.1 and the appendix, the analysis is performed for a "resampling version" of the algorithm, where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a standard theoretical tool to simplify proofs by ensuring independence across iterations. However, it does not reflect how the algorithm would be implemented in practice (using the full dataset at each step). The authors should state this simplification more prominently in the main body and briefly comment on the challenges of extending the analysis to the standard, non-resampling algorithm.
*   **Intuition Behind Abstract Conditions:** While the use of abstract conditions like `Concavity-Smoothness` is elegant, the paper could benefit from providing more intuition in the main text about what properties of a latent variable model would lead these conditions to hold. For instance, for the GMM, these conditions are satisfied when the mixture components are well-separated. Briefly mentioning this connection would make the abstract theory more accessible.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent and highly significant paper. It provides a landmark theoretical analysis for the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional setting, offering the first set of guarantees for both near-optimal estimation and valid inference for a computationally tractable procedure. The technical contributions are substantial and the results are of fundamental importance. While the practical applicability is limited by the strong initialization assumption and the lack of guidance on tuning parameter selection, these are common challenges in this line of research. The paper's theoretical contributions are more than sufficient to warrant publication at NIPS. It lays a strong foundation upon which future work can build to bridge the remaining gaps to practice.

**Recommendation:** Clear Accept.
