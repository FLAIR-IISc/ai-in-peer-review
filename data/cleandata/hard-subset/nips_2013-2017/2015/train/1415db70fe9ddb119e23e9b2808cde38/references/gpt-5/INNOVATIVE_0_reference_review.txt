Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm for latent variable models by inserting a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after each E/M step to enforce sparsity. The authors provide a unified computational-statistical analysis showing (i) geometric optimization convergence within a basin of attraction and (ii) near-minimax statistical error rates of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to log factors) for the final estimator. Beyond point estimation, they introduce a decorrelated score (and Wald) procedure for inference on low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, and prove asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency (achieving the information bound). The framework covers both exact and gradient-ascent M-steps and is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

This work connects to a line of results analyzing EM in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) by extending EM theory to the high-dimensional regime with sparsity. It also relates to debiasing/desparsification literature for GLMs and linear models, but adapts orthogonalization to nonconvex latent variable likelihoods via the Q-function and a Dantzig-based construction. Compared to tensor methods for latent models (e.g., Anandkumar et al.), the proposed approach targets faster statistical rates and tractable inference under sparsity. The truncation step resonates with iterative hard thresholding/projected gradient ideas but is analyzed here in the EM setting, including support evolution and entrywise statistical errors.

Strengths
- Novel integration of sparsity via a truncation step into EM with rigorous guarantees. To my knowledge, this is the first comprehensive analysis yielding both near-minimax estimation rates and valid asymptotic inference for high-dimensional latent variable models fitted by EM-like iterations.
- Clear decomposition of error into geometric optimization and statistical terms, with matching low-dimensional analogs when s* ≈ d and n is large.
- Two M-step implementations (exact and gradient), broadening applicability and connecting to practical approximate EM routines.
- Inference contribution is strong: a decorrelated score based on the Q-function that attains the semiparametric information bound. This is a meaningful advance for uncertainty quantification in nonconvex latent models where convex global optima are unavailable.
- Technical novelty in analyzing the effect of truncation: careful entrywise control (ℓ1 concentration for M vs. Mn) and support evolution, which goes beyond prior EM analyses.
- The conditions are stated in a unified way (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity-smoothness, and statistical error), and then verified for canonical models (Gaussian mixtures, mixtures of regressions).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical algorithm vs. analyzed version: the main convergence theorem is proved for a resampling (sample-splitting across iterations) algorithm, which is often impractical. It would be valuable to provide theory (even with slightly worse constants) for the standard single-sample iteration, or to quantify empirically that the behavior matches the theory without resampling.
- Initialization and basin-of-attraction assumptions: the guarantees require a good initializer and SNR-type conditions. While common in EM analyses, the paper would benefit from explicit, constructive initializers (e.g., spectral/moment-based or thresholded method-of-moments) and an empirical study of sensitivity to initialization.
- Tuning and knowledge of sparsity: the truncation level b_s must be of the same order as the true sparsity s*. Guidance for adaptive selection (e.g., via cross-validation or information criteria) and robustness guarantees when b_s is misspecified would increase practical impact.
- Conditions for inference: the sparsity of w* (from the block of the Fisher information) and Dantzig tuning are crucial and may be nontrivial to verify in some latent models. Discussion of when these conditions hold (beyond the two exemplars), or strategies to encourage approximate sparsity, would help.
- Empirical evaluation is missing in the provided text. Even synthetic experiments demonstrating finite-sample estimation rates, normality of the decorrelated score, calibration of CIs, and comparisons to ℓ1-penalized EM or tensor methods would significantly strengthen the submission.
- Notation/clarity: while the structure is logical, the paper is dense with technical notation and has several formatting glitches/references with missing details. A pass to improve readability and fix references is recommended.

Quality
The theoretical contributions appear careful and technically solid, with explicit rates, clear modular conditions, and semiparametric efficiency. However, the reliance on resampling for analysis and the lack of experiments leave some practical questions open.

Clarity
Generally well-organized, but heavy notation and some typos/formatting issues impede readability. Adding intuitive discussion of the truncation step’s effect (e.g., connections to IHT/projection) and a concise roadmap would help.

Originality
High. The marriage of truncated EM with a full convergence-rate analysis in high dimensions and a decorrelated score for latent models is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial.

Significance
High. Many practitioners use EM for latent models; bringing minimax-optimal rates and valid inference to high-dimensional settings is impactful. The framework is likely to inspire follow-ups for broader latent classes (HMMs, factor analyzers, topic models).

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you provide guarantees for the single-sample algorithm (without resampling), perhaps via martingale concentration or leave-one-out techniques?
- How sensitive is performance to misspecification of b_s? Can you propose an adaptive scheme?
- Could cross-fitting reduce some inference conditions (as in double machine learning)?
- Please include experiments: estimation error vs. n, d, s*, SNR; inference coverage and power; comparisons to ℓ1-penalized EM and tensor approaches.
- Clarify computational cost of solving the Dantzig problem for w and evaluating Tn in practice.

Pros
- Conceptually bold and novel integration of sparsity and inference into EM.
- Strong theoretical guarantees, including semiparametric efficiency.
- Applicable to multiple latent models and both exact/gradient M-steps.

Cons
- Analysis uses resampling; practical alignment not fully justified.
- Needs empirical validation and guidance on tuning/initialization.
- Assumptions for inference (sparsity of w*, SNR) may limit scope.

Recommendation
Accept (7). Despite some practical gaps, the paper introduces a significant new direction with strong novelty and potential impact. A solid empirical section and clarifications would make it even stronger. Confidence: medium-high.
