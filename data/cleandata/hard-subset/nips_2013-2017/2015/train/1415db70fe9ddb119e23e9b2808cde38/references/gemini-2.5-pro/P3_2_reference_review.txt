### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the significant challenge of performing statistically guaranteed parameter estimation and inference for latent variable models in the high-dimensional setting where the dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors propose a novel "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the classical EM algorithm by adding a hard thresholding (truncation) step after each M-step to enforce sparsity.

The key contributions are twofold:
1.  **Estimation Guarantees:** The paper provides a unified theoretical framework demonstrating that, under certain conditions and with a suitable initialization, their proposed algorithm converges at a geometric rate. The final estimator is shown to achieve a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d) / n)`, where `s*` is the true sparsity of the parameter. This is a substantial improvement over existing theory, which is limited to low-dimensional settings.
2.  **Inference Guarantees:** The paper develops a procedure for statistical inference (hypothesis testing) on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. They construct a "decorrelated score statistic" based on the output of their algorithm and prove that it is asymptotically normal. Crucially, its limiting variance achieves the semiparametric information bound, making the test statistically optimal.

The analysis is presented in a general form and then applied to two important high-dimensional latent variable models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model. The work builds upon the recent line of research analyzing the standard EM algorithm as a perturbed gradient method (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) and connects to the high-dimensional inference literature on debiased/desparsified estimators (e.g., Zhang & Zhang, 2014; van de Geer et al., 2014). The main novelty lies in extending these ideas to the non-convex optimization path of an iterative algorithm for latent variable models.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The problem tackled is of fundamental importance. While high-dimensional statistics has matured for models like GLMs, a rigorous, computationally feasible framework for latent variable models has been a major open problem. This paper makes significant strides in closing the gap between computation and statistical theory for this important class of models. The ability to perform valid inference, not just point estimation, is a particularly strong and impactful contribution.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The proposed methodology and its analysis are highly novel. Integrating a truncation step within the EM iterations and analyzing the dynamics of this procedure is a non-trivial technical challenge. The authors successfully characterize the interplay between the optimization error (which decreases geometrically) and the statistical error. Furthermore, developing an inference procedure for an estimator that is a *local* optimum of a non-convex problem, and the output of an iterative algorithm, is a significant departure from existing work that largely focuses on global optima of convex regularized problems (like the Lasso).

3.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and appears sound. The paper elegantly abstracts the core properties required for the analysis into a set of conditions (Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness, Statistical-Error). This makes the framework general and potentially applicable to other latent variable models. The decomposition of the estimation error into optimization and statistical components is clear and insightful. The proof of asymptotic normality for the decorrelated score statistic is technically deep, adapting sophisticated ideas from high-dimensional inference to this new setting.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. It clearly states its contributions, situates itself within the relevant literature, and provides a clear roadmap for the methodology and theory. The distinction between the exact maximization and gradient ascent M-steps is handled cleanly.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **The Initialization Assumption:** The analysis relies on a critical assumption: the availability of an initial estimator `init` that is already within a constant relative error of the true parameter `theta*`. This is a very strong assumption and a common limitation of theoretical analyses for non-convex optimization. The paper provides no guidance on how to obtain such an initial estimate in practice. While methods like tensor decomposition or spectral methods might provide a starting point, their own statistical properties are often suboptimal, and it is not clear if they would satisfy the required initialization condition. A discussion of potential initialization strategies and the practical implications of this assumption is needed to assess the real-world applicability of the algorithm.

2.  **Practicality of Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm requires a sparsity parameter `bs`. Theorem 3.4 provides a condition for `bs` (Eq. 3.8) that depends on the unknown true sparsity `s*`. Similarly, the Dantzig selector used for inference requires a tuning parameter `lambda` (Eq. 2.6, 4.5). The paper does not discuss how to choose these parameters in practice. This is a crucial practical detail. A discussion of data-driven selection methods (e.g., cross-validation) and the robustness of the results to the choice of these parameters would significantly strengthen the paper.

3.  **Analysis of the Resampling Version:** The main theoretical result (Theorem 3.4) is stated for a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4 in the appendix), where fresh data is used at each iteration. This is a common theoretical device to ensure independence and simplify proofs. However, the algorithm presented in the main text (Algorithm 1) uses the entire dataset at each step. The authors should be more explicit about this distinction in the main body of the paper and briefly discuss whether the results are expected to hold for the standard algorithm and what the additional technical challenges would be.

4.  **Absence of Numerical Experiments:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theory is strong, the claims would be much more compelling if supported by simulations. Numerical experiments could: (a) validate the theoretical convergence rates; (b) demonstrate the empirical performance of the estimator; (c) check the coverage of the confidence intervals derived from the decorrelated score test; and (d) investigate the sensitivity to the initialization and tuning parameters. This would provide valuable insights into the algorithm's practical behavior.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent and highly significant theoretical paper that makes a major contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and machine learning. It provides the first computationally tractable algorithm for high-dimensional latent variable models with guarantees for both near-optimal estimation and asymptotically valid inference. The technical analysis is novel and rigorous.

Despite the strengths, the paper's practical applicability is limited by the strong initialization assumption and the lack of guidance on tuning parameter selection. While these are common issues in non-convex optimization theory, their discussion is essential. The reliance on a resampling-based proof should also be made more transparent.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. The paper addresses a difficult and important problem with a novel and powerful theoretical framework. It is a landmark contribution that will likely inspire much follow-up research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
