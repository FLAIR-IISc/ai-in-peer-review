Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the EM algorithm for high-dimensional latent variable models (d ≫ n). It introduces a “high-dimensional EM” that appends a truncation step (top-k hard thresholding) after each E- and M-step to enforce sparsity. Under a unified framework allowing both exact maximization and gradient-ascent M-steps, the authors derive simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees: (i) geometric optimization error decay from a suitable initialization within a basin, and (ii) a final estimation error of order roughly sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (up to extra logarithmic factors), which is near-minimax optimal. Beyond estimation, the paper proposes a decorrelated score (and Wald) procedure for inference on low-dimensional components of the parameter in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, showing asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency.

The work builds on low-dimensional EM analyses (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) by adding sparsity-inducing truncation and high-dimensional error control. It connects the Q-function gradients to the likelihood score and Fisher information (Theorem 2.1) to enable inference, and employs a Dantzig-type estimator for the nuisance orthogonalization vector. The theory is verified for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, with conditions such as signal separation for Gaussian mixtures, and shows that the entrywise statistical error between population and sample M-steps concentrates at rate sqrt(log d / n).

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: gap between local EM convergence and statistical guarantees in high dimensions.
- Conceptual novelty: integrates hard-thresholding/truncation into EM to maintain sparsity, coupled with a decorrelated score for inference. Prior EM theory largely addresses low-dimensional settings or convex surrogates; prior high-dimensional inference primarily covers convex GLMs.
- Unified analysis: treats both exact and gradient EM, decomposes error into optimization vs statistical parts, and explicitly tracks support evolution under truncation.
- Statistical rates: near-minimax estimation rate of order sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (with mild extra logs), improving over d-dependent rates in earlier EM analyses; inference achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound for the target component.
- Conditions and verification: provides high-level Lipschitz-gradient and concavity/smoothness conditions on the population Q, a precise “statistical error” condition in ℓ1 norm tailored to high-dimensional regimes, and verifies these for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under standard separations.
- Inference contribution: decorrelated score with data-driven estimation of the orthogonalization vector via Dantzig selection; establishes asymptotic normality with optimal variance.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Initialization: Guarantees require an initializer within a basin of attraction with bounded relative error. Practical strategies (e.g., spectral/tensor or method-of-moments initializers) are not fully specified or analyzed within the high-dimensional setting; success may hinge on model-dependent procedures and SNR conditions.
- Tuning and knowledge of sparsity: The truncation level b_s must be on the order of s*, and constraints (3.8)–(3.9) link b_s and sample size in nontransparent ways. Guidance for data-driven tuning and robustness to misspecification of s* is limited.
- Resampling analysis: The main convergence theorem is proved for a resampling variant (fresh data per iteration) to facilitate independence; it is not fully clear how the theory translates to the practical single-sample EM, beyond a brief discussion.
- Practical computation: The inference step uses a Dantzig selector to estimate the decorrelation vector, which can be computationally demanding in high dimensions; computational complexity and scalability are not discussed. The hard top-k selection may be unstable when signal magnitudes are close.
- Assumptions: Some conditions (e.g., Lipschitz-Gradient, strong concavity of Q(·; θ*)) and concentration bounds are high-level and may be nontrivial to verify for broader latent variable classes. For Gaussian mixtures, relatively strong separation (SNR) is assumed.
- Experiments: No empirical validation is presented in the provided text. Finite-sample behavior of both estimation and inference (e.g., coverage, type I error, power) is not demonstrated.
- Notational/clarity issues: There are occasional typographical issues in constants (e.g., definition of ρ1), and several results depend on constants whose ranges/interpretations are not always explicit in the main text.

Quality (technical soundness)
The computational analysis follows a contraction-plus-noise template adapted to nonconvex EM with truncation, using ℓ1-based statistical error to control entrywise deviations and the support evolution. The inference analysis leverages an orthogonalized score constructed from Q-function derivatives and a Dantzig program; asymptotic normality with semiparametric optimal variance appears well-grounded given Theorem 2.1 and Conditions 4.1–4.4 plus sparsity of the decorrelation vector. While proofs are deferred, the derivations and rates are consistent with contemporary high-dimensional theory.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized: algorithms are clearly stated; key conditions are isolated; main theorems are interpretable; model-specific implications are summarized. However, the heavy notation and several technical constants reduce readability. A consolidated discussion of tuning, initialization, and sample size requirements would improve clarity. Some references and formatting appear garbled.

Originality
The combination of truncated EM for high-dimensional estimation with a provably efficient, decorrelated inference procedure for latent variable models is novel. Prior EM analyses do not provide high-dimensional rates with sparsity-enforcing iterates and asymptotic inference for low-dimensional components.

Significance
The results have potential impact for a broad class of latent variable models where convex global optima are not available. Achieving near-minimax rates and semiparametric efficiency is of clear theoretical importance. Practical significance will depend on robust initialization/tuning strategies and empirical performance.

Questions for authors
- Can the resampling analysis be extended to the single-sample EM with dependent iterates?
- What practical initialization methods are recommended for the considered models in high dimensions, and under what SNR?
- How sensitive is the algorithm to mis-specifying b_s relative to s*? Any adaptive strategies?
- Computational complexity for the Dantzig step and overall runtime scaling?
- Empirical validation of inference (type I error, power, coverage) and comparison to alternatives (e.g., penalized EM, tensor methods, de-biased estimators where applicable)?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide practical guidelines for initialization and tuning (b_s, step size, Dantzig parameter), including cross-validation or stability-based heuristics.
- Add simulations for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions to illustrate finite-sample behavior and the claimed asymptotic normality.
- Clarify constants and conditions (e.g., ρ definitions), and summarize sample size requirements in a standalone section.
- Discuss robustness to approximate sparsity and model misspecification.
- Comment on computational complexity and provide code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: provable high-dimensional EM with both estimation and inference.
- Strong theoretical guarantees with near-minimax rates and semiparametric efficiency.
- General framework applicable to multiple latent variable models and both EM implementations.

Arguments against acceptance
- Dependence on strong initialization and tuning with limited practical guidance.
- Analysis relies on resampling; guarantees for the practical one-sample algorithm are not fully established.
- Lack of empirical validation in the provided text.
- Verifying conditions beyond the showcased models may be challenging.
