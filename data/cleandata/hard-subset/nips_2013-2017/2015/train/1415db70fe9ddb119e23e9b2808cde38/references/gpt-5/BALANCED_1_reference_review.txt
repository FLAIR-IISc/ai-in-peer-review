Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models via a modified EM algorithm. The key algorithmic contribution is to augment each EM iteration with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step that keeps the top bs coordinates in magnitude, thereby enforcing sparsity along the entire iterate path. The authors analyze both exact maximization and gradient-ascent implementations of the M-step. Under local regularity (Lipschitz/concavity-smoothness) and a suitable initialization inside a basin of attraction, they prove geometric convergence of the optimization error and a (near-)minimax statistical error of order sqrt(s* log d / n), where s* is the sparsity of the true parameter. Beyond point estimation, they construct a decorrelated score statistic adapted to latent variable models by leveraging the identity that the gradient and Hessian of the Q-function match the score and Fisher information (Theorem 2.1). Under sparsity of the nuisance “orthogonalization” vector w*, they prove asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency for low-dimensional components.

This work builds on the population-to-sample EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017), extending it from low to high dimensions by introducing iterative truncation and by quantifying entrywise statistical errors. For inference, it adapts desparsification/orthogonalization ideas (e.g., van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang, Javanmard & Montanari) to nonconvex latent variable likelihoods via the EM surrogate. Compared to ℓ1-penalized EM variants for mixtures (e.g., Städler et al.), the present analysis targets the iterates of a nonconvex, non-global-optimizer algorithm and provides both rate guarantees and asymptotic inference. The paper also contrasts with tensor-method approaches that yield slower rates in finite samples.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic idea: a simple truncation step integrated into EM that maintains sparsity and enables sharp ℓ2 rates.
- Unified computational-statistical analysis providing geometric optimization error plus near-minimax statistical error for two M-step implementations.
- Inference contribution: a decorrelated score tailored to latent variable models with proof of asymptotic normality and efficiency.
- General framework formulated in terms of verifiable conditions, with instantiations for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.
- The use of entrywise (ℓ∞/ℓ1) concentration to control truncation effects is technically nontrivial and valuable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Initialization: The main results require a starting point within a constant-relative-error neighborhood of the truth. In high dimensions, obtaining such initializers for latent variable models is nontrivial and often requires strong separation/SNR assumptions or tensor methods; practical and theoretical initialization strategies are not developed in the main text.
- Resampling analysis: The core estimation theorem is presented for a resampling (fresh data per iteration) variant to ease dependence handling. This is a significant gap between theory and the practical algorithm that reuses data. If non-resampled analysis is only sketched in the appendix or not provided, the guarantees may not reflect realistic usage.
- Assumption strength and scope: The GMM example focuses on a symmetric, two-component, equal-weight model with known spherical covariance and large SNR. Extensions to more general mixtures (unequal weights, K > 2, unknown covariance) and robustness to misspecification are not addressed.
- Tuning and adaptivity: The theory requires bs ≍ s*, stepsizes, and Dantzig-selector tuning depending on unknown quantities. There is no analysis of mis-specifying bs, nor guidance on data-driven selection beyond asymptotic orders.
- Sparsity of w*: As in desparsified Lasso, inference requires w* to be sparse. Conditions ensuring this in latent variable models (and their empirical plausibility) are only discussed at a high level.
- Empirical validation: The submission appears to lack experiments. For NeurIPS, empirical demonstrations of the algorithm’s behavior and finite-sample inference (coverage, power) would substantively strengthen the case.
- Presentation: Notation is dense; some references appear incomplete; several constants/assumptions are deferred. The flow could be improved for readability.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound under stated conditions. The optimization-statistics decomposition and inference proof are careful and align with recent high-dimensional literature. However, reliance on resampling and strong initialization assumptions weakens practical relevance.
- Clarity: Generally well-organized but heavy on notation; several assumptions could be better motivated upfront. Definitions of Tn, w, and the Dantzig program are clear, but more intuition around the T-step dynamics would help. References need fixing.
- Originality: Moderate to high. Bringing hard-thresholded EM together with a rigorous high-dimensional analysis and decorrelated inference for latent variable models appears novel. The inference optimality is a nice touch.
- Significance: Potentially high, as it opens a pathway for inference in high-dimensional latent structure models where convexity is absent. The practical impact depends on addressing initialization, tuning, and non-resampled analysis.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean modification to EM with strong theoretical guarantees at near-optimal rates.
- First unified framework to deliver both estimation and semiparametrically efficient inference for high-dimensional latent variable models.
- General conditions with instantiations for canonical models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theory hinges on resampling and strong local initialization; practicality and generality are limited.
- No empirical results to corroborate finite-sample behavior or to compare with penalized EM and tensor methods.
- Narrow model instantiations (e.g., symmetric two-component GMM) and strong SNR requirements.
- Tuning/selection issues and sparsity-of-w* assumption not well justified in practice.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide non-resampled analysis (or at least sample-splitting/cyclic reuse with uniform concentration across iterates) and discuss stopping rules.
- Develop and analyze concrete initialization schemes in high dimensions (e.g., sparse spectral/tensor initializers) with sample complexity guarantees matching your assumptions.
- Add synthetic and real-data experiments comparing to ℓ1-penalized EM, gradient-EM with penalties, and tensor baselines; report estimation error, support recovery, runtime, and inference coverage/power.
- Discuss adaptivity to s* (e.g., thresholding schedules, cross-validation, information criteria) and analyze robustness to bs mis-specification.
- Clarify assumptions behind w* sparsity for the considered models, and when they may fail.
- Extend discussion to K > 2 mixtures and unknown covariance/noise variance; outline what changes in conditions and proofs.
- Clean up notation and complete citations.

Recommendation
Reject. While the theoretical contributions are interesting and potentially impactful, the current version relies on strong assumptions (initialization, resampling), has limited model scope, and lacks empirical validation. Addressing these points would make the work much stronger for a machine learning venue like NeurIPS.
