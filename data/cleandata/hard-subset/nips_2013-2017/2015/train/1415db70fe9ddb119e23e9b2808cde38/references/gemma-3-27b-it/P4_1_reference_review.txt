This paper addresses the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, a setting where existing theory breaks down. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and establish statistical and computational guarantees, including a convergence rate of p s⇤ · log d/n and asymptotic normality of a decorrelated score statistic. The work is well-motivated, tackling a significant gap in the literature, and builds upon recent advancements in understanding the EM algorithm's behavior.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial open problem – providing theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional regime where d > n. This is a significant limitation of existing EM theory.
* **Novel Approach:** The proposed truncation step is a clever idea to enforce sparsity and enable theoretical analysis. It’s a non-trivial modification to the standard EM algorithm designed specifically to address high dimensionality.
* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The authors provide rigorous convergence rate analysis (p s⇤ · log d/n) and establish the asymptotic normality of a decorrelated score statistic. This is a substantial technical achievement.
* **Unified Framework:** The framework accommodates both exact and gradient ascent implementations of the M-step, increasing its generality.
* **Applications:** Illustrating the results on Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models demonstrates the applicability of the theory.
* **Clear Connection to Related Work:** The paper carefully positions its contribution relative to prior work, specifically mentioning [2] and highlighting the differences in assumptions and results due to the high-dimensional setting. The comparisons to tensor methods and other high-dimensional inference techniques are also valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Complexity:** The paper is highly technical and requires a strong background in statistical inference and optimization. The numerous conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) can be overwhelming, and it’s not immediately clear how easily these conditions can be verified in practice for arbitrary models.
* **Condition Verification:** While the authors provide some verification for Gaussian mixture models, a more detailed discussion of the conditions' tractability for a wider range of models would be beneficial. The practical implications of needing to satisfy conditions related to the Fisher information matrix are not fully explored.
* **Constant Factors:** The convergence rates are expressed up to constant factors. A more precise characterization of these constants, even if only asymptotic, would be helpful.
* **Sparsity Assumption:** The requirement for the decorrelated score test that s⇤w be small (and related conditions in Assumption 4.5) feels restrictive. While the authors provide justification, exploring the sensitivity of the results to this assumption, or suggesting alternative approaches when it doesn't hold, would be valuable.
* **Repetitive Explanations:** Some of the explanations regarding the intuition behind the method and the comparison to prior work appear repetitive throughout the paper, especially concerning the high dimensionality challenge.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in the field.
* The proposed method is novel and well-motivated.
* The theoretical results are strong and provide valuable insights.
* The work is clearly written (despite its complexity) and provides a thorough comparison to related literature.
* The proposed framework has potential for broad applicability.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The technical complexity may limit the paper’s accessibility to a broader audience.
* The stringent conditions and sparsity assumption could limit practical applicability in some scenarios.
* A more detailed discussion of constant factors and verification of conditions for various models would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. While the technical complexity and assumptions may pose challenges, the novelty of the approach and the rigor of the results warrant acceptance. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS, stimulating further research in this important area.



**Rating:** Accept (7/10) – A strong paper with minor room for improvement. Addressing the weaknesses regarding practical verification of conditions and further elaborating on the implications of the sparsity assumption could elevate the paper to an even higher level.
