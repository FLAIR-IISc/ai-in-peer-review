Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm for latent variable models that augments each iteration with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step to enforce sparsity. The authors provide a unified analysis that simultaneously yields (i) geometric convergence to a basin near the true parameter, and (ii) near-minimax statistical error rates of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to a log n factor), under suitable initialization and regularity conditions. They treat both exact maximization and gradient-ascent implementations of the M-step. Building on the estimator, they construct a decorrelated score statistic for inference on low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. They prove asymptotic normality with variance achieving the semiparametric information bound, thereby enabling valid hypothesis testing and confidence intervals. They verify conditions for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

This work extends the low-dimensional EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) to high dimensions by incorporating sparsity via truncation and by developing an inference framework akin to debiasing/desparsification (Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.), but tailored to EM through identities connecting Q-function derivatives to the score and information (Theorem 2.1). Compared to tensor methods, the proposed approach achieves sharper rates in high dimensions and targets likelihood-based estimators. The hard-thresholding step is reminiscent of IHT/gradient hard-thresholding in sparse M-estimation, though that literature is not discussed in depth here.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: rigorous high-dimensional guarantees for EM (both computational and statistical), including asymptotic inference, for latent variable models.
- Clear optimization-statistics decomposition: geometric decay of optimization error plus a model-specific entrywise statistical error, enabling near-optimal rates.
- General framework covers exact and approximate M-steps, with explicit constants and conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity-smoothness, statistical error).
- Inference contribution is strong: decorrelated score achieving the semiparametric efficiency bound for low-dimensional parameters, adapted to latent models via Theorem 2.1.
- Concrete instantiations for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions with explicit scaling, including SNR/separation conditions.

Weaknesses and detailed comments by review criteria

Quality
- The theory appears technically careful and internally consistent; the decomposition, basin-of-attraction analysis, and concentration requirements are standard and adapted thoughtfully to EM with truncation. The asymptotic normality relies on standard high-dimensional orthogonalization but requires nontrivial verification of concentration for the Tn(·) operator.
- However, the main convergence and rate theorem (Theorem 3.4) is proved for a resampling version of the algorithm (fresh sample per iteration) to simplify dependence, while the algorithm of interest uses the same data. The paper should either provide results for the standard single-sample iteration (via uniform concentration/stability arguments) or clearly state limitations and quantify the extra log factor. As written, the practical relevance of the resampling assumption is a concern.
- The initialization requirement (within a fixed fraction of the truth) is strong in high dimensions, and while common in EM analyses, the paper does not provide constructive initializers with guarantees under its assumptions. Practical procedures and their theoretical guarantees would significantly strengthen the work.
- Choice of sparsity parameter b_s must be of the same order as s*, and conditions (3.8–3.9) require knowledge or accurate overestimation of s*. Guidance on data-driven selection and robustness to mis-specification would be valuable.
- Assumptions for inference (sparsity of w*, bounded Fisher information, sample-size scaling) can be restrictive. The paper gestures at when these hold but more explicit verification and discussion in the main text (not only appendix) for the two example models would help.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and the high-level narrative is clear. The use of Conditions 3.1–3.3 and 4.1–4.4 is helpful, and Theorem 2.1 is a nice clarifying identity.
- Nonetheless, the notation is heavy and sometimes cumbersome (e.g., multiple gradient notations, subscript/superscript conventions). A compact table of symbols and a schematic of the algorithm (E/M/T steps) would improve readability.
- References appear inconsistently formatted and incomplete in the provided version; this should be corrected. Some important related work is missing (see below).

Originality
- Combining EM with iterative hard-thresholding and providing joint computational/statistical guarantees in high dimensions is novel to my knowledge for latent variable models. The inference component is also original in this context: most debiasing work focuses on convex M-estimators; extending to nonconvex EM-based estimators is nontrivial and of interest.
- That said, the truncation step is closely related to the IHT literature (e.g., Blumensath & Davies; gradient hard-thresholding pursuit), and related projection-based sparse optimization methods should be acknowledged and contrasted. Also, L1-penalized EM variants (e.g., Stadler et al.) and their differences relative to your T-step deserve more discussion.

Significance
- If the guarantees can be established for the standard single-sample algorithm and if feasible initialization strategies are provided, the impact could be substantial: practitioners could use EM-like procedures for high-dimensional latent models with theoretical backing and valid inference.
- Currently, the lack of empirical evaluation is a notable gap. Even for a theory paper, NeurIPS audiences benefit from simulations validating finite-sample behavior, sensitivity to initialization and b_s, comparisons against L1-penalized EM, tensor methods, and debiased-Lasso baselines on mixtures.

Questions and suggestions to authors
- Can you provide non-resampling guarantees (or a sketch) for the standard algorithm? If not, please discuss the practical implications and whether cross-fitting could bridge the gap while mitigating dependence.
- What initialization procedures meet your basin-of-attraction conditions in high dimensions for GMM and mixture regression? E.g., spectral initializers adapted with thresholding? Please add constructive algorithms and guarantees.
- How sensitive are results to choosing b_s > s*? Can you propose adaptive or data-driven thresholds (e.g., based on information criteria, path following) and discuss theoretical robustness?
- For inference, please spell out when w* is sparse in your examples and provide explicit bounds on s_w* as a function of model structure and SNR.
- Could cross-fitting or sample-splitting be used in estimating w via the Dantzig selector to relax conditions in Assumption 4.5?
- Add discussion and citations to hard-thresholding methods for sparse M-estimation and to L1-penalized EM variants, clarifying the tradeoffs (computational and statistical).

Pros
- Novel, principled extension of EM to high-dimensional sparse settings with theory.
- Near-optimal estimation rates and semiparametric efficient inference.
- Covers both exact and gradient M-steps; clear decomposition of errors.

Cons
- Main analysis uses a resampling variant; unclear guarantees for the practical algorithm.
- Strong initialization and sparsity-parameter assumptions without constructive guidance.
- No experiments; related work on hard-thresholding and penalized EM under-cited.
- Notational heaviness and reference formatting issues.

Overall recommendation
Technically strong and potentially impactful theory paper, but with important practical and presentation gaps. I lean toward a weak accept if the authors can (i) clarify or extend results to the standard non-resampling setting and (ii) add empirical validation and discussion on initialization/parameter selection. Otherwise, this is borderline for NeurIPS given the current limitations.
