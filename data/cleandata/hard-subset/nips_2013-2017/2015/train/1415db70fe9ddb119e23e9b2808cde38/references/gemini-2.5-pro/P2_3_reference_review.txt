### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature: the lack of theoretical guarantees for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings where the dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors propose a novel "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the standard EM by incorporating a hard truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity. The paper's main contributions are twofold. First, for parameter estimation, it proves that under suitable initialization and regularity conditions, the proposed algorithm converges at a geometric rate to a solution that achieves a (near-)minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d) / n)`. Second, for statistical inference, it introduces a "decorrelated score statistic" based on the algorithm's output. This statistic is shown to be asymptotically normal, enabling valid hypothesis testing and confidence interval construction for low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. The theoretical framework is general and its application is demonstrated for high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

### Review

This is a theoretically deep and significant paper that provides the first comprehensive inferential framework for the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional regime. The work rigorously bridges the gap between the computational properties of a widely used iterative algorithm and the desired statistical properties of its output (optimal estimation and valid inference) in a challenging non-convex setting.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is sophisticated and appears sound. The main results (Theorems 3.4 and 4.6) are substantial.

*   **Strengths:** The decomposition of the estimation error into an optimization term that decays geometrically and a statistical term that is near-optimal is a powerful and elegant result. The development of a decorrelated score test for an estimator that is not the global optimum of any convex program is particularly impressive and novel. This is a crucial departure from much of the existing high-dimensional inference literature (e.g., debiased Lasso), which relies heavily on the KKT conditions of a convex optimization problem. The authors establish a general framework based on a set of clearly stated (though abstract) conditions and then show how these conditions can be verified for specific latent variable models, which is a strong and effective analytical approach. The connection made in Theorem 2.1 between the Q-function derivatives and the standard score function and Fisher information is a cornerstone that elegantly links the EM machinery to classical statistical inference concepts.

*   **Weaknesses/Areas for Clarification:**
    1.  **Initialization:** A key assumption, as in much of the literature on non-convex optimization, is the need for a good initialization within a basin of attraction `B` (i.e., `||init - theta*||_2 <= R/2`). The paper does not discuss how to obtain such an initial estimate in practice. While tensor methods are mentioned as having suboptimal rates, it would be useful to discuss if they (or other methods) can be proven to provide a sufficiently accurate initialization to satisfy the paper's requirements. Without a practical and theoretically-backed initialization procedure, the applicability of the main theorems is conditional.
    2.  **Resampling Algorithm:** The proof of the main estimation result (Theorem 3.4) relies on a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4 in the appendix), where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a common proof technique to simplify dependency issues, but it is not what is implemented in practice (Algorithm 1 uses the full dataset at each step). The paper should be more explicit in the main text about whether the results hold for the practical Algorithm 1, and if so, how the analysis changes. If they only hold for the resampling version, this is a limitation that should be clearly stated.
    3.  **Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm requires setting a sparsity parameter `bs` and the decorrelated score test requires a parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector. The theoretical results provide guidance on the scaling of these parameters, but a discussion of how they might be chosen in practice (e.g., via cross-validation) is missing. This is important for the potential use of this method by practitioners.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the problem, the contributions, and the related literature. The separation of the theory into estimation (Section 3) and inference (Section 4) is logical and helps the reader follow the development.

*   **Suggestions for Improvement:** While the abstract conditions (e.g., 3.1, 3.2, 3.3) are necessary for the general theory, providing more intuition about what they mean for a specific model like the Gaussian mixture model *before* presenting the main theorems could improve readability. For example, briefly explaining what `Q(·; theta*)`'s concavity/smoothness physically represents in the GMM context would be helpful.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds upon the analytical framework of Balakrishnan et al. [2] for low-dimensional EM, the extension to the high-dimensional setting is highly non-trivial.

*   The key novelty is the introduction of the truncation step and the corresponding analysis. This requires a much finer-grained, entrywise (`l-infinity`) control of the statistical error, which is a significant technical departure from the `l2`-norm analysis in prior work.
*   The development of an inferential framework for the output of an iterative non-convex solver is a major conceptual advance. It moves beyond the analysis of global optima of penalized likelihoods, which is where most high-dimensional inference research has focused. This opens the door to analyzing inference for a much broader class of practical algorithms.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is extremely high. The EM algorithm is one of the most fundamental and widely used algorithms in statistics and machine learning for dealing with latent variables. High-dimensional data is now ubiquitous. Providing the first rigorous theoretical guarantees for EM's estimation accuracy and, crucially, for statistical inference in this high-dimensional setting is a landmark contribution. Researchers and practitioners working on high-dimensional latent variable models (e.g., in genomics, neuroscience, econometrics) now have a computationally feasible procedure with strong theoretical backing for both estimating sparse parameters and assessing their uncertainty. This work is likely to be highly cited and to inspire a great deal of follow-up research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Fundamental Problem:** Addresses the high-dimensional performance of the EM algorithm, a core problem of broad interest.
2.  **Novel and Strong Theoretical Results:** Provides the first geometric convergence guarantees to a near-optimal estimator for a high-dimensional EM algorithm.
3.  **Groundbreaking Inferential Framework:** Develops a novel method for valid statistical inference based on the output of an iterative, non-convex optimization algorithm, which is a major contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics.
4.  **Generality:** The framework is presented in a general form and then applied to important models, demonstrating its potential breadth.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Practicality Concerns:** The paper is purely theoretical. The crucial assumptions about initialization are not coupled with a practical procedure, and the choice of tuning parameters is not discussed from a user's perspective.
2.  **Analysis of a Modified Algorithm:** The analysis of the resampling version in the proof might limit the direct applicability of the theoretical results to the more standard implementation. This point requires clarification.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes fundamental contributions to our understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The strengths—originality, significance, and technical quality—are outstanding and far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the practical implementation details and could be addressed in a future work or clarified in the final version. This paper represents a major advance and is a clear accept for NIPS.
