This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and establish convergence and asymptotic normality results. While the topic is undeniably significant and the authors demonstrate substantial technical effort, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet cumulatively substantial, flaws in presentation, justification, and technical detail.

The core idea – truncating the EM steps to enforce sparsity – is reasonable and intuitively appealing. However, the justification for *how* the truncation parameter is chosen (bs) feels somewhat ad-hoc, relying heavily on constants (C, C0) without sufficient explanation of their dependence on problem parameters. The stated conditions (3.8 and 3.9) relating bs to other quantities are complex and lack clear intuitive motivation. The dependency on *multiple* constants, seemingly introduced without careful analysis of their interplay, raises concerns about the practical applicability of the algorithm.

The notation is frequently dense and inconsistent. The use of multiple symbols for similar concepts (e.g., ⇢ and 1, 2) without clear differentiation is confusing. Several definitions are introduced without sufficient context or explanation, requiring the reader to constantly refer back to earlier sections, hindering comprehension. The frequent use of "…" and deferred details to the appendix, while common, feels excessive here, obscuring the flow of the core arguments.

The theoretical results, while impressive in scope, are presented with a level of generality that sacrifices clarity. The conditions (3.1-3.3) are stated without sufficient discussion of their practical verification for common latent variable models. The authors claim to provide such verification in the appendix (specifically §E), but the details provided in the main paper regarding these verifications are insufficient to assess their validity. Specifically, the authors state that “for Gaussian Mixture Model” conditions hold but provide minimal explanation.

The connection between the theoretical results and the specific latent variable models (Gaussian Mixture and Mixture of Regression) feels underdeveloped. While the authors mention applying the framework, the details of how the conditions are met for these models are largely relegated to the appendix, making it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theory. The claim of a “near-optimal” rate of convergence (p s⇤ · log d/n) is not fully substantiated, and the “extra factors” are not clearly quantified or discussed.

The asymptotic inference section (Section 4) suffers from similar issues. The conditions (4.1-4.5) are numerous and complex, and their justification feels weak. The discussion of the Dantzig selector and its tuning parameter (4.5) is particularly dense and lacks intuitive explanation. The connection to existing work in high-dimensional inference is superficial, and the claim of optimality (based on the semiparametric information bound) requires stronger justification.

Minor issues further detract from the paper's quality. Numerous typos and grammatical errors are present throughout. Several equations lack clear labels or explanations. The referencing style is inconsistent. Figures are absent.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant open problem in statistical learning.
*   Proposes a novel algorithmic approach (truncated EM).
*   Establishes theoretical guarantees (convergence and asymptotic normality).
*   Offers a unified framework applicable to various latent variable models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Overly complex and dense presentation.
*   Insufficient justification for key algorithmic choices (truncation parameter).
*   Lack of clarity regarding verification of theoretical conditions.
*   Weak connection to practical applications and specific models.
*   Numerous minor flaws (typos, notation inconsistencies, missing figures).
*   The sheer volume of conditions and their complex interdependencies make the results difficult to digest and apply.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the significant technical effort and potentially important contributions, I recommend **rejection**. The paper, in its current form, is too difficult to follow and lacks the clarity and rigor expected of a NIPS publication. While the core ideas are promising, the numerous flaws in presentation and technical detail overshadow the strengths of the work. A substantial revision addressing the aforementioned concerns – specifically, simplifying the presentation, providing more concrete justifications for algorithmic choices, and offering more detailed analyses for specific models – would be necessary for reconsideration. The paper currently reads more like a technical report than a polished, impactful research contribution.
