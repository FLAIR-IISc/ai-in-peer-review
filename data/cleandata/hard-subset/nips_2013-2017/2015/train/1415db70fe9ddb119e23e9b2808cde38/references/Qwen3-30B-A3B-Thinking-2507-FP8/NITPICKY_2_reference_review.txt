

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

This paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm with a truncation step to enforce sparsity, along with theoretical guarantees for both estimation and inference. The authors claim to establish the first theoretical framework for high-dimensional latent variable models, including statistical rates of convergence and asymptotic normality of a decorrelated score statistic.

## Summary and Context

The paper builds on recent work by [2] which established low-dimensional convergence guarantees for EM, extending it to high-dimensional settings by introducing a truncation step to enforce sparsity. The authors apply their framework to Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models, claiming (near-)minimax optimal rates of sqrt(s*log d/n) for parameter estimation and optimal semiparametric inference.

## Strengths

The paper presents a theoretically rigorous approach to high-dimensional EM, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The truncation step is a novel technical contribution that enables the analysis of high-dimensional settings. The decorrelated score statistic provides a principled approach to inference in high-dimensional latent variable models, which is a valuable contribution.

## Critical Flaws

Despite the promising theoretical contributions, the paper contains numerous technical and formatting inconsistencies that undermine its credibility and clarity:

1. **Formatting Errors**: The abstract contains a clear formatting error: "such a ⇤Research supported by..." where the symbol "⇤" should not be attached to the text. This is a basic LaTeX formatting error that should be easily fixed.

2. **Notation Inconsistency**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for sparsity levels (s* vs s⇤), which appears throughout the manuscript. This creates confusion and makes the theoretical arguments harder to follow.

3. **Mathematical Errors**: 
   - The statistical error rate is repeatedly written as "p s⇤ · log d/n" which should be "sqrt(s* log d / n)" (the paper uses "p" to mean "square root" but this is non-standard and confusing).
   - In Theorem 3.4, the expression "p bs+ C 0/ p 1  · p s⇤" is incorrectly formatted and should be "sqrt(bs + C0)/sqrt(1-ρ) · sqrt(s*)".
   - In the Gaussian Mixture Model example, the rate is stated as "C · p s⇤ · log d · log n/n" which should be "C · sqrt(s* log d log n / n)".

4. **Missing Norm Specifications**: Condition 3.3 states "M( ) Mn( ) 1 ✏" without specifying which norm is used. The text later mentions "L1-norm" but this is not clear in the condition statement.

5. **Theoretical Inconsistency**: The paper claims "the statistical error term is roughly of the order p s⇤ · log d/n" but later states "b ⇤ C · p s⇤ · log d · log n/n" in the Gaussian Mixture Model example, introducing an extra log n term without explanation.

6. **Formatting Inconsistency**: The paper uses "bs" for the sparsity parameter but later uses "s" for sparsity level, creating confusion.

7. **Typo in Key Formula**: In the decorrelated score statistic (2.7), there's a typo: "p n · Sn b 0 , ⇥ Tn b 0 ⇤ ↵| /2" should have a multiplication sign instead of a comma.

## Significance and Originality

While the paper addresses an important problem, the theoretical contributions are not as novel as claimed. The work builds directly on [2] and the truncation step is a natural extension for high-dimensional settings. The decorrelated score statistic is a standard technique in high-dimensional statistics, though applied here to a new context.

## Recommendation

Despite the promising theoretical framework, the numerous technical and formatting errors significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The mathematical inconsistencies (particularly with the statistical rates) are serious enough to cast doubt on the main results. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to fix these fundamental issues before being considered for publication.

The authors should carefully verify all mathematical expressions, standardize their notation, and ensure all rates are correctly formatted. Given the numerous minor but critical errors that permeate the manuscript, I recommend rejection. These errors are not just typographical but represent fundamental misunderstandings of statistical rates and mathematical notation that would confuse readers and reviewers alike.

The paper's potential contribution is substantial, but the current version is not sufficiently polished for publication at NIPS. The authors should address these technical issues before resubmission.
