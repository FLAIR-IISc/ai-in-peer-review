This paper tackles a challenging and important problem: theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings, where d > n. The authors make a significant contribution by proposing a truncated EM algorithm and establishing convergence rates and asymptotic normality under reasonable conditions. The work directly addresses a long-standing gap between the practical utility of EM and its theoretical understanding, particularly in the high-dimensional regime. This is a bold approach, as establishing such guarantees is notoriously difficult, and the truncation step is a clever way to enforce sparsity and enable analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm with truncation is a novel contribution.  Existing theoretical work largely focuses on low-dimensional scenarios.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides substantial theoretical analysis, including convergence rates (p√s* log(d/n)) and asymptotic normality of the estimator and a decorrelated score statistic. This level of theoretical grounding is commendable.
*   **Generality:** The framework is presented as general, applicable to various latent variable models (illustrated with Gaussian Mixture and Mixture of Regression models), showcasing broad potential impact.
*   **Practical Relevance:**  Addressing the high-dimensional case is crucial for many modern applications, making the work directly relevant to practitioners.
*   **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper clearly positions itself relative to existing literature, acknowledging prior results ([2], [30], [25]) and highlighting the novelty of the contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The conditions for the theoretical guarantees (Conditions 3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.5) appear somewhat strong and potentially difficult to verify in practice. While the authors illustrate verification for specific models, the general applicability hinges on these conditions holding. The sparsity assumption on the *gradient* w* (related to the Fisher Information) feels particularly restrictive.
*   **Constant Factors:** The rates often involve unspecified constant factors, making it hard to fully assess their practical implications. The impact of these constants could be substantial.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks a comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating the practical performance of the proposed algorithm compared to existing methods in realistic high-dimensional settings. While theory is central, some validation with simulations or real data would strengthen the paper.
*   **Complexity of Presentation:**  The theoretical derivations are dense and require careful reading. While mathematically sound, a more streamlined presentation could improve accessibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a truly novel and significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in a challenging high-dimensional setting.
*   The theoretical guarantees, while subject to assumptions, are substantial and well-supported.
*   The approach of incorporating a truncation step is clever and opens new avenues for analyzing similar algorithms.
*   The potential impact on various latent variable models is considerable.
*   The paper's clear exposition of the problem and its connection to related work demonstrate a deep understanding of the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumptions required for the theoretical guarantees raise concerns about the practical applicability of the algorithm.
*   The lack of extensive empirical validation limits the assessment of the algorithm’s real-world performance.
*   The complexity of the presentation might hinder broader adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and theoretical depth are compelling. While the assumptions are somewhat restrictive, the contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance, even with these caveats. The paper tackles a fundamental open problem in statistical machine learning and proposes a well-motivated and theoretically sound solution. The lack of extensive empirical evaluation is a concern, but the authors provide sufficient justification for focusing on theoretical guarantees at this stage. The paper's impact will likely be significant, inspiring further research in this area. The potential benefit of providing theoretical foundations for the ubiquitous EM algorithm in the high-dimensional regime outweighs the limitations.



**Rating:** Accept (Strongly)
