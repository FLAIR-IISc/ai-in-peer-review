

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings, where dimension d exceeds sample size n. The authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, establishing both statistical and computational guarantees for parameter estimation. They further develop a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference that achieves semiparametric efficiency. The work extends previous low-dimensional results (e.g., [2]) to high dimensions, where the statistical rate shifts from O(√d/n) to O(√(s* log d)/n), and provides the first theoretical framework for uncertainty assessment in high-dimensional latent variable models.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Innovation**: The truncation step in the EM algorithm (Algorithm 1) is a clever and well-motivated approach to handle high-dimensional sparsity. The paper establishes a clear decomposition of the estimation error into optimization error (geometrically decreasing with iteration count) and statistical error (near-minimax optimal), which is a significant theoretical contribution.

2. **Rigorous Framework**: The paper develops a unified analytical framework that applies to multiple latent variable models. The detailed verification of technical conditions for Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models in the appendix demonstrates the framework's generality.

3. **Novel Inference Procedure**: The decorrelated score statistic (Section 2.2) represents a substantial advance over existing methods for latent variable models. The proof that it achieves the semiparametric information bound (Theorem 4.6) is particularly strong and addresses a key gap in the literature.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with precise definitions, clear algorithm descriptions, and thorough technical development. The appendix provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: While the theoretical results are strong, the paper provides no empirical evidence to support the proposed algorithm's performance. The authors should include experiments comparing their method with existing approaches (e.g., gradient EM with sparsity constraints [23]) on synthetic and real datasets to demonstrate practical utility.

2. **Strong Assumptions for Inference**: The inference framework requires the sparsity assumption on w* (defined in 4.1), which may be difficult to verify in practice. The paper should discuss practical strategies for selecting the tuning parameters (e.g., the Dantzig selector parameter λ) and provide guidance on how to check the sparsity assumption in real applications.

3. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't address computational complexity or practical implementation details. For instance, the truncation parameter bs (Equation 3.8) depends on the unknown true sparsity s*, which would need to be estimated in practice. The authors should discuss how to choose bs without prior knowledge of s*.

4. **Limited Discussion of Model Scope**: While the paper mentions applications to Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, it doesn't discuss limitations of the framework for other latent variable models (e.g., topic models, factor analysis). A more thorough discussion of the framework's applicability would strengthen the paper.

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. Add empirical results comparing the high-dimensional EM algorithm with existing methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets. This would demonstrate the practical value of the theoretical results.

2. Provide a discussion on how to select the key tuning parameters (e.g., bs, λ) in practice, including cross-validation strategies or data-driven approaches.

3. Include a section discussing the limitations of the framework for specific latent variable models beyond those considered in the paper.

4. Add a brief computational complexity analysis, comparing the per-iteration cost of the high-dimensional EM algorithm with standard EM and gradient-based alternatives.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The proposed framework fills an important gap in the literature and provides the first rigorous guarantees for both parameter estimation and asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The theoretical results are strong and well-supported, with clear connections to existing work in the field.

The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation, which is particularly notable for a NeurIPS submission where both theoretical and empirical contributions are valued. The paper would be substantially strengthened with experimental results demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed methods.

The paper is highly original, addressing a fundamental question in statistical learning that has been largely unexplored in high dimensions. The significance of the work is high, as it provides a theoretically sound foundation for applying EM to high-dimensional problems across various domains.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. While the lack of empirical results is a notable weakness, the theoretical novelty and rigor are sufficient to warrant acceptance, especially given the paper's clear potential for practical impact. The authors should address the suggested improvements in a revised version to further strengthen the work.
