Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies EM for latent-variable models in the high-dimensional regime (d >> n), where classical MLE is ill-posed without structural assumptions and prior EM guarantees focus on low-dimensional d << n. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm that augments the E/M steps with a hard-thresholding “T-step” that enforces sparsity along the iterates. They provide a unified analysis for two M-step implementations (exact maximization, and one-step gradient ascent), giving nonasymptotic bounds that decompose optimization error (geometric contraction) and statistical error. With appropriate initialization and tuning (sparsity level bs of the order of the true sparsity s*), the estimator achieves near-minimax rate O(√(s* log d / n)) up to log factors. Building on the estimator, they construct a decorrelated score test (via a Dantzig-selector estimate of a nuisance-orthogonalization vector w) and show the resulting statistic is asymptotically normal with variance achieving the semiparametric information bound, enabling optimal inference for low-dimensional components under high-dimensional nuisance.

This extends the influential population-to-sample EM framework of Balakrishnan et al. to high-dim settings by introducing truncation and by controlling entrywise statistical errors. It also connects to debiasing/desparsification literature (Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.; Javanmard & Montanari) but moves beyond convex GLMs to nonconvex latent-variable likelihoods processed via EM. Compared to l1-penalized EM variants (e.g., Städler et al. for mixture regression), this work analyzes a different computational path (hard-thresholded EM) and, importantly, provides valid inference with semiparametric efficiency. Applications are illustrated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under signal-to-noise separation conditions.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The statements are precise, build on clear population conditions (Lipschitz-type gradient conditions for Q, local strong concavity/smoothness) and a statistical error control in l1 for sample vs. population M-steps. The optimization-statistics error decomposition is standard yet carefully adapted to the truncation step, requiring support evolution control. The inference result leverages an identity linking Q-derivatives to the score/Fisher information, and a Dantzig-based orthogonalization to derive asymptotic normality with efficient variance.
- Assumptions: Several are strong: (i) a suitable initialization within a constant relative error of the truth; (ii) knowledge (or good tuning) of bs at the order of s*; (iii) sparsity of the orthogonalization vector w* (s_w* small); (iv) SNR/separation conditions to ensure local concavity/Lipschitz properties; and (v) a resampling (or sample-splitting) analysis to simplify dependence across iterations. These restrict practical applicability without further guidance.
- Completeness: The core theoretical claims are supported, but proofs and model-specific verifications are deferred to appendices/longer version. There is no empirical or simulation study demonstrating finite-sample behavior, tuning robustness, or comparison to l1-penalized EM/tensor methods.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized with algorithms and assumptions laid out. However, the presentation is dense, notation is heavy, and there are formatting artifacts (LaTeX symbols, incomplete references). Details critical to intuition (e.g., effect of truncation on support evolution, guidelines for choosing bs, initialization strategies) are largely deferred. The reliance on a “longer version” weakens self-contained clarity.

Originality
- Novelties include: (i) a unified analysis of a hard-thresholded EM for high-dimensional latent-variable models yielding near-optimal estimation rates; (ii) an inference framework that delivers asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency for low-dim parameters despite nonconvexity and high-dimensional nuisance; and (iii) covering both exact and gradient M-steps. While hard-thresholding ideas are known in sparse optimization and l1-penalized EM variants exist, the combination with EM, proofs at entrywise level, and efficient inference in this nonconvex setting appear new.

Significance
- The theoretical contribution is substantial: it advances understanding of EM in high dimensions and provides the first inference guarantees of this type for latent-variable models. If accompanied by empirical evidence and practical guidance, it could influence both theory and practice. As written, the practical impact is limited by strong assumptions, lack of experiments, and tuning issues.

Strengths
- First unified computational-statistical theory for high-dimensional EM with sparsity-enforcing truncation.
- Near-minimax estimation rates and geometric optimization convergence under clear local conditions.
- Asymptotically normal, semiparametrically efficient decorrelated score/Wald tests for low-dimensional components.
- Covers exact and gradient M-steps; instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Weaknesses
- No empirical validation (simulations or real data), no comparisons to l1-EM or tensor methods; unclear robustness to mis-specified bs or initialization.
- Analysis uses a resampling/sample-splitting variant; guarantees for the practically used single-sample EM are not explicit.
- Strong and sometimes opaque assumptions (initialization closeness, SNR, sparsity of w*, basin parameters), with limited guidance on how to verify/achieve them in practice.
- Tuning choices (bs, stepsize, Dantzig tuning) lack data-driven procedures and sensitivity analysis.
- Presentation issues: heavy notation; several typos/formatting glitches; incomplete references; reliance on a “longer version” for key details.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide a simulation study (even small-scale) comparing truncated EM to l1-penalized EM and tensor methods for estimation and inference accuracy, sensitivity to bs and initialization, and wall-clock time.
- Discuss practical initialization strategies with theoretical justification (e.g., spectral/tensor starts, method-of-moments, l1-penalized warm starts).
- Give data-driven rules for bs (cross-validation, information criteria, stability selection) and for the Dantzig tuning; analyze sensitivity.
- Clarify whether the main convergence/inference guarantees extend to the standard, single-sample EM (without resampling), and what additional mixing or martingale arguments would be needed.
- Improve exposition: streamline notation, fix LaTeX artifacts, complete and standardize references, and move some intuition/lemmas from the appendix into the main text.

Recommendation
- Overall: borderline. The theoretical advances are strong and potentially impactful, but the lack of empirical support and heavy practical assumptions weaken the submission for NeurIPS.
- If experiments and clearer guidance on tuning/initialization are added, I would lean to acceptance. As it stands: weak reject.

Confidence: medium.
