### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the long-standing challenge of applying the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to high-dimensional latent variable models, where the dimension `d` is much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a novel "high-dimensional EM algorithm" which integrates a hard-thresholding or "truncation" step within each iteration. This forces the parameter estimates to remain sparse throughout the optimization process, which is crucial for well-posedness in the `d >> n` regime. Second, they develop a comprehensive theoretical framework that establishes, for the first time, both computational and statistical guarantees for this algorithm. They prove that with a suitable initialization, their algorithm converges at a geometric rate to a solution that achieves the near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`.

Beyond point estimation, the paper makes a significant leap by proposing a "decorrelated score statistic" to enable valid statistical inference (e.g., hypothesis testing) for low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. This is a highly non-trivial contribution, as existing high-dimensional inference techniques are typically designed for global optima of convex problems (like the Lasso), whereas the EM algorithm converges to local optima of a non-convex likelihood. The authors prove that their proposed statistic is asymptotically normal and its variance achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. The general framework is then applied to provide concrete guarantees for high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Ambition:** This paper is a bold and highly original piece of work. The theoretical analysis of the EM algorithm in the low-dimensional setting is itself a relatively recent development. Tackling the far more complex high-dimensional (`d >> n`) regime is a major step forward. The proposed solution—interleaving optimization steps with sparsity-inducing truncation steps—is an elegant and powerful new concept for adapting iterative algorithms to high-dimensional settings. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a fundamental extension of one of the most important algorithms in machine learning and statistics.

2.  **Breakthrough in High-Dimensional Inference:** Perhaps the most impressive contribution is the development of a valid inferential framework. Providing p-values and confidence intervals in high-dimensional models is notoriously difficult. Extending these ideas from convex models to the local optima found by a non-convex procedure like EM is a significant theoretical breakthrough. This greatly enhances the practical utility of the proposed method, moving it beyond a "black-box" estimator to a tool for scientific discovery and uncertainty quantification.

3.  **Strong and Comprehensive Theory:** The paper provides a rigorous and complete theoretical narrative. It establishes the geometric convergence of the algorithm, a near-optimal statistical rate for the final estimator, and the asymptotic normality of the test statistic. The decomposition of the estimation error into an optimization component that vanishes with iterations and a statistical component that depends on `s`, `d`, and `n` is particularly insightful and clearly articulates the algorithm's behavior.

4.  **High Significance and Potential Impact:** The EM algorithm is a workhorse for models with latent structure across countless scientific and engineering domains. The lack of a theoretically-sound high-dimensional version has been a major limitation. This paper provides a direct, computationally feasible, and theoretically-backed solution. This work is highly likely to be influential, spawning follow-up research on adapting other classical algorithms to the high-dimensional setting and enabling new applications of latent variable models in fields like genomics, computational neuroscience, and econometrics.

**Weaknesses (Minor points to consider):**

1.  **Reliance on Initialization:** The theoretical guarantees, particularly the geometric convergence, are contingent on starting the algorithm within a "basin of attraction" of the true parameter. This is a common requirement in the analysis of non-convex optimization, but the paper does not provide extensive guidance on how to find such an initialization in practice for high-dimensional problems. While this is a well-recognized difficulty and does not invalidate the core contribution, it is a practical limitation. I view this as a ripe area for future work rather than a critical flaw of the present paper.

2.  **Technical Assumptions:** The analysis relies on a set of technical conditions that must be verified for each specific model. While the authors successfully do this for their examples, these conditions (e.g., sparsity of the decorrelation vector `w*`, Lipschitz and concavity properties) can be quite strong and complex. This is likely an unavoidable price for the powerful guarantees obtained, but it does mean that applying this framework to a new model class is a non-trivial undertaking.

### Overall Recommendation

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It represents the best kind of submission for NIPS: it is ambitious, conceptually novel, and rigorously executed. It tackles a fundamental problem at the intersection of optimization, statistics, and machine learning, and provides a powerful and elegant solution. The work significantly advances the state of the art by providing the first comprehensive framework for both optimal estimation and valid inference for the EM algorithm in the challenging high-dimensional setting. While the reliance on a good initialization is a limitation, it is a standard one in this line of research and is heavily outweighed by the novelty and significance of the contributions. This paper opens a new direction for research and provides a tool that could have a substantial impact on both theory and practice.
