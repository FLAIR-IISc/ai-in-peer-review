**Summary:**
This paper addresses the theoretical analysis of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in the high-dimensional regime where the dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the standard EM procedure by adding a hard-thresholding (truncation) step after each M-step to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. The main contributions are theoretical. First, for parameter estimation, the paper establishes that under certain conditions, including a suitable initialization, the proposed algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator achieving a near-minimax optimal statistical error of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`. Second, for statistical inference, the authors construct a "decorrelated score statistic" based on the algorithm's output. They prove this statistic is asymptotically normal, allowing for hypothesis testing and the construction of confidence intervals for low-dimensional components of the parameter vector. The theoretical framework is presented as a general one and is instantiated for the Gaussian Mixture Model and the Mixture of Regression Model. This work extends the low-dimensional analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) to the high-dimensional sparse setting.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a significant and challenging open problem: providing theoretical guarantees for the widely used EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. Establishing a computationally tractable method with rigorous statistical guarantees for estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models is a valuable research goal.
2.  **Comprehensive Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a strong and unified theoretical analysis. It simultaneously establishes the computational rate of convergence (geometric), the statistical rate of convergence for the final estimator (near-optimal), and the asymptotic normality for statistical inference. Proving such results for an iterative, non-convex procedure is a notable technical achievement.
3.  **Generality:** The framework is developed under a set of general conditions (Lipschitz gradients, concavity-smoothness, etc.) and then verified for specific models. This suggests the approach could potentially be applied to a broader class of high-dimensional latent variable models.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Critical Initialization Assumption:** The entire theoretical framework is predicated on the availability of an initial estimator that lies within a local basin of attraction of the true parameter. This is a very strong assumption. The paper provides no discussion on how to obtain such an initial estimate in practice. While using strong assumptions to make progress on hard theoretical problems is common, the lack of a concrete and verifiable initialization procedure severely limits the practical applicability of the presented guarantees. Without a reliable initializer, the algorithm is not guaranteed to work.
2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical and lacks any form of empirical validation. As a reviewer who prefers established methods, I am skeptical of a new algorithmic proposal that has not been tested, even in simulation. There are no experiments to:
    *   Verify the convergence and estimation accuracy in finite-sample settings.
    *   Assess the practical performance of the decorrelated score test (e.g., its Type I error control and power).
    *   Compare the proposed method against any baseline methods. For instance, one could compare against a direct penalized likelihood optimization approach or even the tensor methods that the paper criticizes for their suboptimal rates.
    *   Demonstrate the robustness of the algorithm to its tuning parameters.
    The absence of any empirical results makes it impossible to judge whether the claimed theoretical properties translate into a useful and reliable method in practice. The asymptotic results may require an impractically large `n` to become relevant.
3.  **Practicality of Tuning Parameters:** The proposed algorithm and inference procedure rely on several tuning parameters, most notably the sparsity level `bs` for the truncation step and the regularization parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector. The theory requires these parameters to be chosen in relation to unknown quantities, such as the true sparsity `s*`. The paper offers no guidance on how to select these parameters from data, for example, using cross-validation. This is a significant gap between the theory presented and a usable algorithm.
4.  **Novelty of the Algorithmic Contribution:** The core modification to the EM algorithm is a truncation or hard-thresholding step. This is a very standard technique in high-dimensional statistics, commonly used in methods like Iterative Hard Thresholding (IHT). While the analysis of this step within the EM framework for latent variable models is novel and technically demanding, the algorithmic proposal itself is a combination of well-known components.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first (to my knowledge) unified framework for computationally efficient and statistically near-optimal estimation and inference for the EM algorithm in a general high-dimensional sparse setting.
*   The theoretical analysis is deep and appears to be technically sound, representing a significant contribution to the statistical theory of high-dimensional models.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The work is presented as a new algorithm, but its practical viability is completely unproven due to a total lack of experiments.
*   The theory relies on a critical assumption of a good initialization, with no method provided to satisfy it.
*   Key practical aspects, such as data-driven selection of tuning parameters, are not addressed.
*   The paper endorses an innovation without the rigorous testing and validation against established approaches that one would expect for a top-tier conference.

**Conclusion:**
While the theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial and address an important problem, the work feels incomplete from a machine learning perspective. The proposed "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" is presented without any empirical evidence of its effectiveness or comparison to existing methods. The reliance on a strong, unverified initialization assumption and the lack of guidance on tuning parameter selection raise serious questions about its practical utility. In my view, a paper proposing a new algorithm for a fundamental problem should provide more than just asymptotic theory; it should offer some evidence that the method is sound and performs well in practice. Given my preference for well-vetted and rigorously tested approaches, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The paper would be much stronger if it included a thorough empirical study to support its theoretical claims.
