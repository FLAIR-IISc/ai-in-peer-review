- Proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step to enforce sparsity during iterations.  
- Shows geometric convergence to a local optimum with estimation error decomposed into geometric optimization error and statistical error at rate √(s* log d / n).  
- Introduces a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing that is asymptotically normal and semiparametrically efficient for low-dimensional components.  
- Establishes theoretical results for Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models, verifying required conditions.  
- Extends prior low-dimensional EM analyses to high-dimensional sparse settings, filling a theoretical gap.  
- First comprehensive theoretical framework providing simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees for high-dimensional EM.  
- The truncation (T-step) is a novel and effective extension of classical EM to enforce sparsity.  
- Provides both maximization and gradient-ascent M-step analyses, demonstrating framework generality.  
- Main theorems clearly decompose optimization and statistical errors and connect the score test to semiparametric efficiency.  
- Lacks empirical validation on synthetic or real high-dimensional data to demonstrate practical performance.  
- Paper is highly technical with dense notation and many conditions, which may hinder accessibility.  
- Relies on sparsity assumptions and provides limited practical guidance for selecting the sparsity parameter bs.  
- Scope of applications is limited; extension to other latent variable models (e.g., topic models, factor analysis) is not discussed.  
- Insufficient comparison to other high-dimensional methods (e.g., tensor methods, other sparse EM variants).  
- Technically sound with rigorous proofs and careful condition verification for specific models.  
- Well-organized and clear in main-result presentation, though proofs could use more intuitive explanation.  
- Original contribution by extending EM analysis to high-dimensional settings and proposing a novel decorrelated score for inference.  
- Significant potential impact by addressing theoretical guarantees for EM in modern high-dimensional applications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: first theoretical framework for high-dimensional EM, optimal convergence rates, semiparametric-efficient testing, and unified framework for multiple models.  
- Arguments against acceptance: no experiments, possibly restrictive assumptions, unclear practical selection of bs, and limited comparison to alternatives.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution warranting acceptance despite limited empirical validation.  
- Recommendation: accept, with suggestion to add empirical demonstrations or practical implementation guidance.  
- Suggested improvements: add synthetic experiments, give practical guidance for bs selection, compare to alternative high-dimensional methods, discuss computational complexity of truncation, and provide intuition for the Dantzig selector step in the score test.
