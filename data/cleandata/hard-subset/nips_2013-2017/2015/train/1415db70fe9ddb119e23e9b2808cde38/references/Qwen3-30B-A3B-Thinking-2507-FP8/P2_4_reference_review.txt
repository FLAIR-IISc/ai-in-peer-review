

# Review: High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings where dimension d exceeds sample size n. While existing work (e.g., [2, 18, 25, 30]) has established strong guarantees for low-dimensional EM algorithms, the paper demonstrates that these results do not extend to high-dimensional regimes without additional structure. The authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, and establish both statistical optimization guarantees and asymptotic inference procedures. This work builds upon the foundational EM algorithm literature [12, 30] and extends the recent low-dimensional analysis of [2] to the high-dimensional setting with sparsity assumptions.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish two main theoretical results: (1) Theorem 3.4 shows that the proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm converges geometrically to an estimator with near-minimax optimal statistical rate of O(√(s* log d/n)), and (2) Theorem 4.6 proves the asymptotic normality of their decorrelated score statistic, achieving the semiparametric information bound. The analysis carefully addresses the challenges of high dimensionality through the truncation step, and the authors verify their conditions for specific models (Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression) in the appendix.

The paper is well-structured as a complete theoretical contribution, with clear definitions, precise theorems, and detailed proofs in the appendix. The authors demonstrate appropriate care in distinguishing their contribution from related work and acknowledging limitations. The theoretical framework is sound and the mathematical arguments appear correct.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem, the gap in existing literature, and the contributions. The methodology section presents the algorithm and inferential procedure with precise notation. The theoretical results are stated clearly with well-justified assumptions. The appendix provides sufficient detail for experts to verify the proofs.

The paper effectively uses figures of merit (optimization error vs. statistical error) to clarify the convergence behavior. The connection between the proposed decorrelated score statistic and standard asymptotic inference is well-explained, making the novel contribution accessible to readers familiar with high-dimensional statistics.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution by extending the EM algorithm to high-dimensional settings with sparsity assumptions. The key innovation is the truncation step (T-step) that enforces sparsity during the iterative process, which is novel and addresses the specific challenge of high dimensionality in latent variable models.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work: [2] covers low-dimensional regimes (d ≪ n) with a p(d/n) rate, while this paper achieves the sharper p(s* log d/n) rate in high-dimensional settings (d ≫ n). The decorrelated score statistic for inference in high-dimensional latent variable models is also novel, as existing methods [13, 15, 16, 26, 32] primarily focus on generalized linear models rather than latent variable models. The paper's framework is the first to provide simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees for EM in high-dimensional settings.

## Significance

This work is highly significant for both theoretical and practical reasons. Theoretical significance lies in establishing the first convergence guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings, filling a major gap in the literature. The paper demonstrates how to handle the fundamental challenge of high-dimensional nuisance parameters in latent variable models.

Practically, this work provides a theoretically grounded approach for practitioners working with high-dimensional latent variable models (e.g., in genomics, natural language processing, or computer vision), where traditional EM algorithms would fail. The decorrelated score statistic enables valid statistical inference for low-dimensional components, which is crucial for model interpretation and hypothesis testing.

The paper's framework is general enough to apply to a broad family of latent variable models, making it widely applicable. The near-minimax optimal rate of convergence (up to log factors) is an important theoretical achievement that advances the state of the art.

## Weaknesses

The primary limitation is the lack of empirical validation. While the paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, including some experimental results would strengthen the contribution by demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. The authors mention these will be included in a longer version, but for a NeurIPS submission, some empirical results would be valuable.

The conditions required for the theoretical guarantees (Conditions 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) may be restrictive in practice, though the authors verify them for specific models in the appendix. It would be helpful to include a discussion of how sensitive the algorithm is to violations of these conditions.

The paper could also provide more guidance on selecting the sparsity parameter bs, which is crucial for the truncation step. While the paper gives theoretical conditions for its choice (Equation 3.8), it's unclear how to set this parameter in practice.

## Overall Assessment

This is an outstanding theoretical contribution that makes significant progress on an important problem in statistical machine learning. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a well-motivated gap in the literature. The extension of the EM algorithm to high-dimensional settings with sparsity assumptions is novel and the theoretical results are strong.

The paper meets all four review criteria at a high level. The quality is excellent, the clarity is exceptional, the originality is significant, and the significance is substantial for both theory and practice. While the lack of empirical results is a minor weakness, the theoretical contribution is so strong that it would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper represents a major theoretical advance in understanding the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. It provides the first unified framework for both parameter estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models, with near-optimal statistical rates and valid asymptotic inference. The work has the potential to influence both theoretical research and practical applications in statistics and machine learning.
