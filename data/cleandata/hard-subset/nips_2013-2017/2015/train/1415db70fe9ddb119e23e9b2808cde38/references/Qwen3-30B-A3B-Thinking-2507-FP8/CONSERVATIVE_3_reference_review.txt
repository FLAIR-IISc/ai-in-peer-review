

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

This paper proposes a "high dimensional EM algorithm" with a truncation step to enforce sparsity, claiming to establish theoretical guarantees for high-dimensional latent variable models where dimension d exceeds sample size n. The authors present two main contributions: (1) a modified EM algorithm with a truncation step to achieve sparsity, and (2) a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference. The paper claims to fill a theoretical gap in high-dimensional latent variable models, where previous work (cited as [2, 25, 30]) only covers low-dimensional regimes.

The paper's main novelty appears to be the truncation step (T-step) in Algorithm 1, which preserves the top bs magnitude entries at each iteration. However, this approach bears significant resemblance to standard iterative hard thresholding methods used in sparse optimization, rather than representing a fundamentally new EM framework. The paper fails to convincingly establish how their truncation method differs from or improves upon existing sparsity-inducing approaches like L1 regularization or thresholding methods already used in high-dimensional EM contexts (e.g., [23] for mixture of regression models).

The theoretical claims require careful scrutiny. The authors claim their algorithm achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical rate of p(s* log d/n), but they do not provide a minimax lower bound proof to substantiate this claim. The conditions they impose (Conditions 3.1-3.3) appear quite strong, particularly Condition 3.2 requiring strong concavity and smoothness in the basin of attraction. For many latent variable models, such conditions may not hold without additional restrictive assumptions.

The paper's assertion that they provide "the first theoretical guarantee of parameter estimation and asymptotic inference in high dimensional regimes" is questionable. [23] already established support recovery guarantees for high-dimensional mixture of regression models using a gradient EM algorithm with L1 penalty, and the decorrelated score statistic they propose is conceptually similar to methods in [13, 26, 32] for high-dimensional inference.

Most critically, the paper lacks empirical validation. Despite claiming to establish a "general inferential theory" for high-dimensional latent variable models, the paper provides no experimental results comparing their approach to existing methods (e.g., gradient EM with L1 penalty, tensor methods) on real or simulated high-dimensional data. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether their theoretical guarantees translate to practical advantages.

The paper also fails to adequately address computational considerations. The repeated calculation of the top bs entries at each iteration (the T-step) introduces significant computational overhead that could make their approach less practical than alternatives, especially for very high-dimensional problems. The paper does not discuss how the choice of bs affects computational efficiency or whether their method scales better than existing approaches.

While the paper's theoretical framework is presented in detail (with proofs deferred to appendices), the claims about the novelty and practical significance of the approach seem overstated relative to the evidence provided. The truncation step they propose is a relatively simple modification to the standard EM algorithm, yet the paper presents it as a significant theoretical advance without sufficient justification.

The paper's main strength is its attempt to provide a unified theoretical framework for high-dimensional EM algorithms, but this is undermined by the lack of clear differentiation from existing approaches and the absence of empirical validation. The theoretical results appear to be extensions of existing techniques rather than novel contributions, and the paper fails to demonstrate how their method outperforms established baselines.

In summary, while the paper addresses an important problem, the claims of novelty and theoretical advancement are not sufficiently supported. The truncation step is not convincingly shown to be superior to existing sparsity-inducing methods, and the lack of empirical validation against established baselines makes it difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed approach. The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical justification for the truncation step, more detailed comparisons to existing methods, and empirical results demonstrating the practical advantages of their approach.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. More rigorous comparison to existing high-dimensional EM approaches (e.g., [23])
2. Empirical validation showing improvements over established baselines
3. Clearer justification for why truncation is superior to L1 regularization
4. Discussion of computational complexity and scalability

As presented, the paper makes incremental contributions rather than a significant advance in the field. The theoretical results appear to be extensions of existing techniques rather than novel insights, and the claims of novelty are not sufficiently supported by evidence.
