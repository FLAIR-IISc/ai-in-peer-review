This paper presents a theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional sparse settings, where the dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which integrates a hard-thresholding (truncation) step after each M-step to enforce sparsity along the optimization path. They prove that, under certain conditions and with a good initialization, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves the near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`. Second, they develop a procedure for statistical inference on the parameters estimated by their algorithm. They construct a "decorrelated score statistic" for testing hypotheses about low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. They show this statistic is asymptotically normal and that its limiting variance is optimal, achieving the semiparametric information bound. The framework is general and its applicability is demonstrated for high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

This work builds upon a recent line of research providing non-asymptotic guarantees for the EM algorithm, most notably Balakrishnan et al. (2017) [2], which established similar results for the low-dimensional setting. The key innovation here is the successful extension of this analytical framework to the high-dimensional regime. This requires overcoming significant technical hurdles, such as analyzing the effect of the non-linear truncation operator and establishing finer-grained `l_infinity`-norm statistical error bounds, as opposed to the `l_2`-norm bounds sufficient for low-dimensional analysis. For inference, the paper adeptly adapts the "debiasing" or "decorrelating" techniques, originally developed for convex regularized estimators like the Lasso (e.g., [26, 32]), to the non-convex, iterative setting of the EM algorithm.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a problem of fundamental importance. The EM algorithm is a workhorse in machine learning and statistics for latent variable models, yet a rigorous understanding of its performance in the prevalent high-dimensional setting has been lacking. This work provides the first comprehensive framework that guarantees both statistically optimal point estimation and valid asymptotic inference for a computationally tractable algorithm in this challenging regime. This is a major theoretical advance.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm with its truncation step is a natural but non-trivial adaptation. The theoretical analysis is highly innovative. Characterizing the interplay between the EM updates and the hard-thresholding operator within a high-dimensional statistical context is a formidable task. The authors' proof strategy, which carefully tracks the support of the iterates and relies on sharp `l_infinity`-norm concentration bounds, appears rigorous and sound. Furthermore, developing an inference procedure for an estimator that is not the solution to a convex optimization problem is a significant contribution that pushes the boundary of high-dimensional inference theory.

3.  **Clarity and Generality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors present a unified analytic framework based on a set of abstract conditions, which elegantly separates the core logic of the convergence proof from model-specific details. They then meticulously verify these conditions for important and non-trivial examples like Gaussian Mixture Models. This structure makes the paper's core ideas accessible while maintaining technical rigor. The comparison to related work is thorough and accurately contextualizes the paper's contributions.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **The Initialization Assumption:** The theoretical guarantees rely on an initialization `theta_init` that is already within a certain `l_2`-ball of the true parameter `theta*`. This is a strong assumption, common in analyses of non-convex problems, but it remains a critical practical barrier. The paper does not suggest a data-driven method for obtaining such an initial estimate. Could the authors comment on potential strategies for initialization (e.g., spectral methods) and whether they might be proven to satisfy the required conditions for the models considered? A brief discussion of this limitation would be welcome.

2.  **Practicality of Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm and inference procedure depend on several tuning parameters, including the sparsity level `bs` and the Dantzig selector parameter `lambda`. The theoretical results provide guidance on their scaling (e.g., `bs` proportional to `s*`, `lambda` based on error rates), but these depend on unknown quantities. How should these be selected in practice? Addressing this, perhaps by suggesting a cross-validation scheme or other data-dependent procedure, would significantly increase the paper's practical impact.

3.  **Analysis of the Resampling Version:** The main theorems are proven for a "resampling version" of the algorithm, where, presumably, a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a useful analytical tool for ensuring independence, but the practical algorithm (Algorithm 1) uses the full dataset repeatedly. The authors should explicitly state and discuss the gap between the analyzed algorithm and the practical one, and clarify whether the theoretical results are conjectured to hold for the standard implementation.

4.  **Sparsity of the Debiasing Vector `w*`:** The inference theory hinges on the sparsity of `w*`, which is a property of the inverse Fisher information matrix. This is a strong structural assumption on the model itself, not just the parameter vector. While standard in the high-dimensional inference literature, it's a key condition that limits the scope of the results. It would be beneficial to highlight this assumption more prominently and perhaps offer some intuition on when it might be expected to hold or fail.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. This is a landmark theoretical paper that provides a much-needed bridge between the computational reality of the EM algorithm and the statistical demands of modern high-dimensional data analysis. The technical contributions are deep and significant, and the results provide fundamental new insights. While the practical aspects concerning initialization and tuning parameter selection are not fully resolved, these are well-known challenges in the field of non-convex optimization and do not diminish the paper's profound theoretical importance. This work is of high quality, originality, and significance, and will undoubtedly be influential for future research in high-dimensional statistics and latent variable modeling.
