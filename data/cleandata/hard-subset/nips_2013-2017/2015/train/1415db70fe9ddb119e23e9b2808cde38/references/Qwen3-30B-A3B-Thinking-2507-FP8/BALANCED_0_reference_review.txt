

# Review: High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality

This paper addresses an important gap in the literature by extending the theoretical guarantees of the EM algorithm to high-dimensional latent variable models where the dimension d is much larger than the sample size n. The authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step to enforce sparsity, establishing both statistical and computational guarantees for parameter estimation, and further develop a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference. The work is well-motivated and fills a significant theoretical void in the literature.

## Strengths

**Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a clear and important contribution by extending EM theory to high-dimensional settings, where existing results are limited to low-dimensional regimes. The truncation step is a novel technical innovation that enables the authors to establish (near-)minimax optimal statistical rates of O(sqrt(s* log d/n)) for parameter estimation, which is a significant improvement over the p(d/n) rates in low-dimensional settings. The introduction of a decorrelated score statistic that achieves semiparametric efficiency for inference in high-dimensional latent variable models is particularly noteworthy. The paper is well-positioned relative to recent NIPS work (e.g., [2], [18]) and clearly establishes how it differs from prior contributions.

**Quality:** The theoretical results are technically sound and rigorously proven. The authors carefully establish both computational (geometric convergence rate) and statistical (optimal rate of convergence) guarantees. The paper provides a unified framework that applies to multiple latent variable models (Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression), with detailed verification of assumptions for these specific cases. The paper acknowledges limitations and carefully compares with related work, including tensor methods and Lasso-based approaches for inference.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear notation and logical flow. The high-level contributions are clearly stated in the introduction, and the technical conditions are carefully defined. The appendix provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The main theorems (3.4 and 4.6) are presented with clear decomposition of error terms (optimization vs. statistical error) that help readers understand the behavior of the proposed algorithm.

## Weaknesses

**Lack of Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the paper lacks empirical results to demonstrate the practical performance of the proposed method. Given the computational nature of the contribution, empirical validation on synthetic and real data would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors should consider adding such experiments to better demonstrate the practical relevance of their theoretical results.

**Implementation Complexity and Practical Considerations:** The truncation step (line 6 of Algorithm 1) introduces an additional sparsity parameter bs that needs to be tuned. The paper doesn't discuss the practical challenges of selecting bs in real applications or the computational cost of the truncation step. The authors should provide more discussion on implementation considerations and potential alternatives for selecting bs.

**Assumption Tightness:** The assumptions required for the main theorems (Conditions 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) are quite strong, particularly the sparsity assumption on w* (Condition 4.5). While the paper verifies these assumptions for specific models, it's not clear how broadly applicable they are. The authors should discuss the implications of relaxing these assumptions or provide examples where they might fail.

**Limited Discussion of Intermediate Regimes:** The paper focuses exclusively on the high-dimensional regime (d >> n) but doesn't discuss how the method might behave when d is comparable to n (the "intermediate" regime), which is often encountered in practice. A brief discussion of this could enhance the paper's applicability.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add empirical results demonstrating the performance of the proposed method on synthetic and real datasets, showing the geometric convergence rate and the statistical error bounds in practice.

2. Provide more detailed discussion of the practical implementation of the truncation step, including how to select the sparsity parameter bs and the computational cost of the truncation operation.

3. Consider extending the analysis to the intermediate regime where d is comparable to n, or at least discussing the limitations of the current approach in such settings.

4. In the discussion of assumptions, provide examples where the assumptions might fail and discuss potential ways to relax them.

5. Consider adding a brief comparison with existing high-dimensional EM implementations (e.g., [23]) on a small-scale example to demonstrate the advantages of the proposed truncation step.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending the EM algorithm to high-dimensional latent variable models with rigorous statistical guarantees for both parameter estimation and inference. The truncation step is a novel and elegant technical innovation that enables the authors to achieve optimal statistical rates. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature.

While the lack of empirical validation is a notable weakness, the theoretical contribution is substantial and well-supported. The paper's main limitation is the focus on theory without empirical demonstration of the practical benefits. With the addition of empirical results and more discussion of practical implementation considerations, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Decision:** Accept (with minor revisions to address empirical validation and implementation considerations)
