This paper addresses the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, a setting where existing results are lacking. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and establish statistical and computational guarantees, including convergence rates and asymptotic normality of a decorrelated score statistic. The work is well-motivated, tackling a significant gap in the literature, and the proposed approach seems promising.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Previous Work:**

The core idea is to address the ill-posedness of maximum likelihood estimation in high dimensions (d > n) by introducing a truncation step in the EM algorithm that enforces sparsity. This builds upon previous work establishing convergence guarantees for EM in low dimensions ([2, 18, 25, 30]). The paper directly acknowledges the limitations of [2] in the high-dimensional regime and highlights the need for sharper convergence rates than those attainable by tensor methods [1, 9].  The authors position their work as going beyond simply achieving point estimation by also providing tools for uncertainty quantification (hypothesis testing). They differentiate their approach from existing high-dimensional inference methods like debiasing [13] or post-selection inference [16, 17, 24] by explicitly tailoring the analysis to the EM algorithm's iterative nature and its lack of direct connection to a convex optimization problem.  The decorrelated score statistic builds on the idea of correcting the standard score function, similar to approaches in sparse regression [4, 15].

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** The paper tackles a genuinely important open problem in statistical machine learning – establishing theoretical guarantees for EM in high dimensions.
* **Technical Depth:** The analysis appears rigorous and technically sound, with clear conditions stated and careful attention paid to both computational and statistical aspects. The derivation of the asymptotic normality result for the score statistic is a significant achievement.
* **Novelty:** The truncation step and its theoretical analysis are novel.  The combination of establishing both estimation rates and valid inference procedures for the EM algorithm is a substantial contribution.
* **Generality:** The framework is presented in a general manner and illustrated with examples for Gaussian Mixture Models and Mixture of Regression Models, suggesting broader applicability.
* **Clear Presentation (Mostly):** While dense, the paper is generally well-structured, with clear algorithmic descriptions and logical flow.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conditionality:** The theory relies on several conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) that, while stated, can be difficult to fully grasp without a deep understanding of the underlying mathematics. More intuition regarding the practical implications of these conditions would be beneficial. The constraints on `s⇤w` in Assumption 4.5 are particularly restrictive and should be discussed in more detail.
* **Constant Factors:** The constants involved in the convergence rates and variance estimates are not explicitly characterized. Understanding the dependence on these constants is crucial for assessing the practical performance of the algorithm.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper focuses on theoretical guarantees and does not discuss the computational complexity of the truncated EM algorithm, particularly the T-step. This is an important consideration for real-world applications.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  While the focus is theoretical, some limited empirical demonstration of the performance of the algorithm (even on simulated data) would strengthen the paper. This could involve comparing the empirical convergence rates to the theoretical bounds and evaluating the performance of the hypothesis test.
* **Notation:** The notation is dense and could be simplified in places.  A more comprehensive glossary of symbols would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a substantial theoretical advance in understanding the behavior of the EM algorithm in high dimensions.
* **Addresses a Long-Standing Problem:**  The work fills a critical gap in the literature and opens up new avenues for research.
* **Potential Impact:** The results could have a significant impact on applications of latent variable models in high-dimensional settings.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The analysis appears to be mathematically sound and provides strong guarantees under reasonable conditions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Complexity and Conditionality:** The complexity of the theory and the restrictive nature of some conditions may limit the practical applicability of the results.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's performance in practice.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings.  The technical analysis is rigorous and the results are potentially impactful. While the complexity of the theory and the lack of empirical evaluation are weaknesses, the overall quality of the work is high.  I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors consider addressing the concerns regarding the conditions, constant factors, computational complexity, and empirical validation in a future revision.
