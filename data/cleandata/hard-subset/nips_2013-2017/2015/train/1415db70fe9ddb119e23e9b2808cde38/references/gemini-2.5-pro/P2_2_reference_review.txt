### Summary

This paper presents a theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings where the dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which augments the standard E- and M-steps with a truncation step (T-step) to enforce sparsity at each iteration. The paper's contributions are twofold:

1.  **Estimation:** It provides theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithm, showing that with a suitable initialization, the iterates converge at a geometric rate to a local optimum. The final estimator achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d)/n)`, where `s*` is the true sparsity level.
2.  **Inference:** It develops a procedure for statistical inference on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. The authors construct a "decorrelated score statistic" based on the estimator from their algorithm and prove that it is asymptotically normal. This allows for hypothesis testing and the construction of confidence intervals.

The framework is presented in a general form with conditions that are then verified for two important latent variable models: the Gaussian Mixture Model and the Mixture of Regression Model. The work positions itself as closing the gap between the computational reality of EM (finding local optima) and the need for statistical guarantees (estimation rates and inference) in the challenging high-dimensional regime.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a highly significant and challenging problem. The EM algorithm is a cornerstone of statistical machine learning for latent variable models, but its theoretical properties in the modern `d >> n` setting are largely unexplored. Providing a unified framework for both optimal point estimation and valid asymptotic inference is a major theoretical contribution. The results could have a substantial impact on how latent variable models are used and understood in fields like genomics, computational neuroscience, and econometrics.

2.  **Originality:** The proposed method and its analysis are novel. While adding sparsity-inducing steps to algorithms is common in high-dimensional statistics, the analysis of such a procedure within the non-convex EM framework is highly non-trivial. The key technical novelty lies in carefully tracking the interplay between the optimization dynamics (the E/M steps) and the sparsity-enforcing truncation step. Furthermore, developing an inference procedure (the decorrelated score test) for a local optimum of a non-convex algorithm, rather than for the global optimum of a convex one (like the Lasso), is a sophisticated and original contribution that extends the recent line of work on high-dimensional inference to a new and important class of problems.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper appears to be technically sound and rigorous. The theoretical results are strong, providing explicit rates for both optimization and statistical error. The decomposition of the estimation error into these two components is clear and insightful. The authors lay out their assumptions clearly (e.g., Conditions 3.1-3.3) and build their theorems upon this foundation. The proof of asymptotic normality for the score statistic is a technically demanding result, and its claimed optimality (achieving the semiparametric information bound) is a very strong theoretical guarantee.

### Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)

1.  **The Initialization Assumption:** The entire theoretical guarantee hinges on the existence of an initial estimator `init` that is already within a constant factor `R/2` of the true parameter `θ*` in `l2`-norm. This is a very strong assumption, common in analyses of non-convex optimization, but it sidesteps the critical and often most difficult problem of finding a good initial point. The paper provides no discussion on how such an initialization might be obtained in practice. While tensor methods are mentioned as suboptimal for final estimation, they are often used precisely for initialization; a more thorough discussion of this would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a strong theory paper, the absence of any simulations is a missed opportunity. Numerical experiments could have: (a) verified the theoretical rates of convergence, especially the `sqrt(s* log(d)/n)` scaling; (b) demonstrated the coverage of the proposed confidence intervals from the decorrelated score test; and (c) provided insight into the practical performance of the algorithm and its sensitivity to the crucial initialization and tuning parameters (`bs`, `λ`). Without experiments, the practical utility of the proposed framework remains speculative.

3.  **Clarity and Practical Guidance:** The paper is extremely dense and technically demanding, which can hinder its clarity. The reliance on abstract conditions in the main body, with verification for specific models deferred to the appendix, makes the core results somewhat difficult to digest. More importantly, the paper offers little practical guidance on choosing the tuning parameters. The sparsity parameter `bs` is required to be of the order of the true sparsity `s*` (Eq. 3.8), and the Dantzig selector parameter `λ` is defined in terms of unknown population quantities and error rates (Eq. 4.5). How a practitioner would set these parameters is left unaddressed.

### Detailed Comments

*   The connection between the `Q` function and the log-likelihood in Theorem 2.1 is a key insight that bridges the EM framework with classical likelihood theory, enabling the use of score functions and Fisher information. This is a very elegant part of the paper.
*   The analysis of the truncation step's effect on the solution path is impressive. The authors correctly identify that this requires a finer-grained, entrywise (`l-infinity`) control on the statistical error (Condition 3.3), which is technically more challenging than the `l2`-norm analysis in low-dimensional settings.
*   The comparison with related work is thorough and accurately situates the paper's contributions relative to low-dimensional EM analysis, high-dimensional inference for convex models, and tensor methods.

### Recommendation

This is a very strong theoretical paper that makes significant contributions to a fundamental problem in machine learning. The authors provide a novel algorithm and a complete, rigorous analysis covering both optimal estimation and valid inference for the EM algorithm in high dimensions. The significance and originality of the work are exceptionally high.

The main weaknesses—the strong initialization assumption and the lack of practical guidance or experiments—are limitations but are not uncommon for a first paper establishing a complex theoretical framework. These points should be clearly acknowledged by the authors and could be addressed in future work. Despite these limitations, the theoretical advances presented are substantial and important enough for the NIPS community.

I recommend **Acceptance**.
