- Paper extends EM theory to high-dimensional settings (d > n) by adding a truncation step to enforce sparsity and provides convergence and asymptotic normality results.  
- Addresses an important and timely problem bridging practical EM use and rigorous high-dimensional theory.  
- Truncation within EM is a novel, natural idea to handle ill-posedness and enable theoretical analysis.  
- Provides a comprehensive theoretical framework with minimax-optimal estimation rates and asymptotic normality for testing.  
- Demonstrates applicability to Gaussian Mixture Models and Mixture of Regression Models.  
- Thorough technical analysis with clear conditions and proof sketches; good connection to prior work.  
- Paper is technically dense with heavy notation, making it hard to digest quickly.  
- Some assumptions are strong (notably Condition 4.5 and sparsity requirement s_w = o(√n / log d)) and may limit applicability.  
- Limited empirical evaluation; no simulations or real-data experiments to illustrate practical performance or benefits of truncation.  
- Theoretical guarantees depend on a strong initialization requirement, but practical strategies for achieving this are not discussed.  
- Computational cost of the truncation step and comparison to other methods are not addressed.  
- Strengths: novel method, rigorous analysis, potential wide impact on latent variable modeling.  
- Risks: high technical complexity, restrictive assumptions, and lack of empirical validation reduce accessibility and practical relevance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a fundamental open problem, novel and significant results, rigorous analysis, and potential broad impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy assumptions, limited empirical work, and stringent initialization/sparsity requirements.  
- Overall assessment: strong, theoretically rigorous contribution with significant value but concerns about assumptions, accessibility, and practical validation.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions contingent on clarifying assumptions, adding empirical results, guidance on initialization, and discussion of computational cost.
