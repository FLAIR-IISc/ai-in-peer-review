Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies parameter estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models via a modified EM algorithm. The core algorithm adds a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after each E/M iteration to enforce sparsity. The authors analyze two M-step implementations: exact maximization and a single gradient ascent step. They prove that, under suitable initialization and regularity conditions (Lipschitz-type conditions on the population Q-function and a concavity/smoothness sandwich), the iterates contract geometrically toward a neighborhood of the truth, and the final estimator achieves a near-optimal statistical error of order sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (up to extra logarithmic factors). Beyond point estimation, they construct a decorrelated score statistic that is asymptotically normal and achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound for a low-dimensional component in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. A key identity (Theorem 2.1) connects gradients and Hessians of the Q-function to the score and Fisher information of the observed likelihood, enabling inference without appealing to a global optimum of a penalized likelihood. The framework is illustrated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under separation/SNR and sparsity assumptions.

This work builds on and extends: (i) population-to-sample EM analyses in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) by handling d >> n and adding sparsity truncation; (ii) debiasing/desparsifying inference (e.g., Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.) by adapting it to nonconvex latent-variable likelihoods via the Q-function; and (iii) differentiates from tensor methods by achieving near-optimal rates and providing inference under high dimensions.

Evaluation by review criteria:

Quality:
- The theoretical development is careful and technically nontrivial. Theorem 3.4 provides a clean decomposition of optimization vs. statistical error with explicit geometric contraction factors for both exact and gradient M-steps. The shift to an entrywise (ℓ1) statistical error on the M-step mapping is appropriate and key in controlling support evolution under truncation.
- The inference result (Theorem 4.6) is well-grounded: the decorrelated score leverages the Q/likelihood identities to recover semiparametric efficiency. The assumptions (sparsity of w*, concentration and Lipschitz control of Tn, and bounded Fisher spectrum) are standard in spirit for high-dimensional inference, though somewhat intricate here.
- That said, several assumptions are strong and deserve more discussion: (a) availability of an initialization within a fixed relative ℓ2 error; (b) knowledge of a good truncation level b_s ≍ s*; (c) a resampling version is used for the proof, whereas the algorithm uses the full sample—stability of bounds without resampling is not fully addressed in the main text; (d) verification of conditions relies on strong SNR/separation for mixtures.
- The paper is primarily theoretical; there are no experiments. While not strictly required, even small simulations would help validate constants, tuning, and robustness.

Clarity:
- The high-level narrative is clear and the algorithm is easy to follow. The decomposition into E/M/T steps and the two M-step variants is well organized.
- However, the exposition is dense. Several notational choices and references are hard to parse, there are typos and formatting glitches (e.g., equation breaks, reference metadata), and many key verifications are deferred to the appendix. The paper would benefit from a concise, self-contained overview of how to choose tuning parameters (b_s, Dantzig λ, step size), and a short intuitive explanation of why the truncation step preserves the basin of attraction.
- Guidance on how to obtain the required initialization (e.g., spectral/tensor or method-of-moments warm starts) should be summarized in the main text.

Originality:
- Incorporating hard-thresholding into EM with a rigorous analysis of both computational contraction and optimal high-dimensional statistical rates is, to my knowledge, new. Prior EM analyses were low-dimensional or didn’t address sparsity via truncation. The inference development that reuses the Q-function to obtain a decorrelated score for latent-variable models is a neat and original instantiation of debiasing ideas in a nonconvex, non-global-optimum context.
- The paper positions itself well relative to low-dimensional EM theory and high-dimensional inference for GLMs; related penalized EM works and tensor approaches are discussed.

Significance:
- The results address a meaningful gap: computationally tractable procedures with optimal rates and valid inference for high-dimensional latent variable models. The guarantees for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under sparsity are potentially impactful and could catalyze new methods for other latent models.
- Practical significance is tempered by the reliance on strong initialization and tuning, and by the lack of empirical evidence. Still, the theoretical contributions are substantial and likely to influence subsequent work.

Pros:
- Solid, nontrivial theory: geometric contraction plus near-minimax high-dimensional rates.
- General framework covering exact and gradient M-steps.
- Asymptotically normal, semiparametrically efficient inference in high dimensions for latent-variable models.
- Clear advancement over low-dimensional EM theory and over tensor methods’ suboptimal rates.

Cons:
- Strong assumptions: good initialization, correct sparsity level b_s, w* sparsity, SNR/separation.
- Proof relies on resampling; robustness to the standard single-sample setting is not emphasized.
- No experiments; limited practical guidance on tuning and initialization.
- Exposition issues: notation density, typos, and some opaque constants/conditions.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a short experimental section: synthetic GMM and mixed regressions showing estimation rates, support recovery, and coverage of the decorrelated score/Wald intervals; compare to ℓ1-penalized EM and tensor baselines.
- Summarize in the main text feasible initialization strategies and conditions under which they achieve the required basin proximity.
- Provide practical defaults for b_s (e.g., doubling schedule or information criteria) and λ in the Dantzig step, with sensitivity analysis.
- Clarify whether the guarantees extend to the non-resampled algorithm, or add a sketch indicating how dependence across iterations is controlled.
- Improve clarity and fix reference/formatting issues.

Overall recommendation:
This is a strong theoretical paper with novel and significant contributions to high-dimensional EM and inference. Despite the lack of experiments and some strong assumptions, the work advances the state of the art. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved clarity and some empirical validation.
