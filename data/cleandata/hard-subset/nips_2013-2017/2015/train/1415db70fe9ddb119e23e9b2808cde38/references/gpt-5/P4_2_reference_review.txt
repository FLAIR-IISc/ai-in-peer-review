Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies EM for high-dimensional latent variable models, where d can exceed n and MLE is ill-posed without structure. It proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm that augments the classical E/M steps with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step enforcing sparsity. Two M-step implementations are covered: exact maximization and a single gradient-ascent step. The paper provides a unified computational-statistical analysis: starting from an initializer within a relative ℓ2-error radius around the truth, the iterates contract geometrically up to a statistical floor of order roughly sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d/n) (up to model-dependent extra factors), where s* is the sparsity of the true parameter. In addition, the paper proposes a decorrelated score test for a low-dimensional component of the parameter in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance. The test is built from gradients/Hessians of the Q-function and a Dantzig-type estimator for the orthogonalization vector; the statistic is shown to be asymptotically normal with variance achieving the semiparametric efficiency bound. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under separation/signal-to-noise conditions.

This work extends the recent analysis of EM in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2017) to high dimensions by combining EM with sparsity enforcement and by developing inferential tools beyond point estimation. Compared to debiased/desparsified Lasso literature (e.g., van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang), this work targets non-convex latent variable models and constructs inference without requiring a global optimizer of a convex program. It also contrasts with tensor methods for latent models, which often yield slower statistical rates and typically target low-dimensional regimes.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The statements are plausible and internally consistent with standard high-dimensional theory. The contraction analysis relies on population Lipschitz/smoothness/concavity of the Q-function, a statistical error bound between sample/population M-steps measured in ℓ1, and a truncation analysis controlling support evolution. The asymptotic normality of the decorrelated score uses the identity connecting gradients/Hessians of Q to the score and Fisher information and requires concentration/Lipschitz conditions on Tn and sparsity of the orthogonalization vector w*. The claims that the score variance meets the semiparametric information bound are consistent with semiparametric efficiency theory.
- Rigor/completeness: The main theorems are stated with clear conditions and provide explicit geometric contraction plus a statistical term and the CLT for the score test. However, several pieces critical to assessing completeness are deferred to the appendix (verification of conditions for each model, resampling version of the algorithm), and the reference list is incomplete/garbled. The rate expression in the GMM example appears inconsistent with earlier claims (a term like sqrt(s*)·log d·log n/n is dimensionally stronger than sqrt(s* log d/n)); this should be clarified.
- Empirics: No empirical evaluation is provided in the excerpt; practical performance, sensitivity to tuning, and finite-sample calibration of the test are not demonstrated.

Clarity:
- The high-level flow is clear: algorithm, conditions, main results, and a sketch of model implications. Notation is largely standard. The T-step, support operator, and Dantzig subproblem are defined.
- Clarity could be improved. There are typographical issues, inconsistent symbols (e.g., norms), missing years/authors in references, and some overloaded notation (e.g., rQ and its indices). The precise choice/role of bs, step size, and tuning parameter λ for the Dantzig selector would benefit from more concrete guidance. The need for and implications of the resampling version (Algorithm 4) should be explained in the main text.

Originality:
- The combination of EM with iterative hard-thresholding to obtain sharp high-dimensional rates, along with a decorrelated score test tailored to latent variable models using the Q-function identities, appears novel. Prior work either handled low dimensions, convex penalization with local optima, or did not provide inference for latent models.
- The specific use of Tn built from Q to estimate the efficient variance and orthogonalize the score in a latent variable setting is a notable conceptual contribution.

Significance:
- Providing near-minimax rates for high-dimensional EM and a computationally feasible inference procedure that attains semiparametric efficiency addresses an important gap for widely used latent variable models. If the conditions can be verified broadly and the procedure is practical, the impact is high.
- The requirement of a good initializer, sparsity of w*, and sometimes strong SNR may limit applicability, but these are common in the area.

Pros:
- Unified analysis yielding geometric convergence plus optimal statistical error.
- Works with both exact and gradient M-steps.
- Asymptotically normal decorrelated score achieving semiparametric information bound.
- Instantiations for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.
- Clear delineation of conditions (population geometry, concentration, Lipschitz, statistical error).

Cons:
- Clarity/notation issues and incomplete references; some rate expressions appear inconsistent.
- Heavy reliance on an initializer within a constant-factor ℓ2-ball; practical initialization strategies are not discussed.
- The analysis uses a resampling/split-sample EM variant; it is unclear whether the same guarantees hold for standard single-sample EM.
- Strong assumptions (e.g., sparsity of w*, SNR/separation) may be restrictive and their necessity could be better justified.
- Lack of empirical validation and practical tuning guidance (bs, step size, Dantzig λ).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide concrete, data-driven initialization strategies (e.g., method-of-moments, spectral, k-means) and analyze their basin-of-attraction guarantees.
- Clarify whether the guarantees extend to standard EM without resampling; if not, quantify the price of resampling and discuss practical implementation.
- Fix typographical issues, unify notation, and complete/standardize references with years and correct author names; ensure the stated rates match earlier claims.
- Give practical rules for choosing bs, η, and λ, and sensitivity analyses; discuss misspecification of sparsity level.
- Add empirical studies on synthetic and real data to assess estimation error and test calibration versus baselines (penalized EM, tensor methods, debiased Lasso on proxy models).
- Discuss relations to iterative hard thresholding and proximal gradient with hard-thresholding to position the truncation step among known sparse optimization methods.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. Strong theoretical contribution with novel inference for high-dimensional latent variable models, but clarity and empirical support need strengthening.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
