## Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a fundamental problem: extending the theoretical guarantees of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to the high-dimensional setting, where the number of parameters (d) exceeds the sample size (n).  The authors propose a modified EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, and then provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis covering both parameter estimation rates and asymptotic normality of a decorrelated score statistic for low-dimensional components.  They demonstrate their theory's applicability to Gaussian Mixture Models and Mixture of Regression Models.  The work builds upon recent advances in understanding the convergence of EM in the low-dimensional regime (e.g., [2]) but significantly extends it to a more challenging and practically relevant high-dimensional scenario.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical gap:** The paper directly tackles a major limitation of existing EM theory - its lack of guarantees in high dimensions, where it is often used in practice.
* **Novel algorithmic contribution:** The truncated EM algorithm is a sensible approach to address the challenges of high dimensionality and enforce sparsity, leading to improved statistical properties.
* **Strong theoretical results:** The authors provide rigorous convergence rates for the estimator and establish asymptotic normality for a carefully constructed test statistic, achieving near-minimax optimality. The decomposition of the error into optimization and statistical components is a clear and insightful contribution.
* **Generality:**  The framework is presented in a general form applicable to various latent variable models, and the authors demonstrate this with two examples.
* **Well-connected to existing literature:** The paper acknowledges and builds upon related work in high-dimensional statistics and EM algorithm analysis. The comparison with tensor methods is particularly insightful.
* **Clear presentation (mostly):** The mathematical notation is standard, and the overall structure of the paper is logical.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical complexity:** The paper is highly technical, and the sheer volume of notation and conditions can be daunting for readers not deeply familiar with the field.  While thorough, it could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the key ideas behind the conditions and proofs.
* **Conditionality:** The theoretical results rely on a number of conditions (3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.5) which, while likely reasonable for certain models, require careful verification. The paper provides verification for Gaussian Mixture Models, but a wider range of examples would strengthen the claim of generality.
* **Practical considerations:**  The choice of the sparsity parameter `bs` and the tuning parameter `epsilon` for the Dantzig selector are crucial. The paper provides guidance, but a discussion of practical methods for selecting these parameters in real-world applications would be valuable.  The computational cost of the truncation step and the gradient ascent implementation of the M-step aren't thoroughly discussed.
* **Limited empirical evaluation:** While the theory is strong, the paper lacks extensive empirical validation. Showing the performance of the algorithm and the accuracy of the confidence intervals on synthetic or real datasets would greatly enhance the impact.




**Evaluation of Criteria:**

* **Quality (4/5):** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by rigorous analysis. The proof strategy appears carefully constructed. However, the numerous conditions make it difficult to fully assess the robustness of the results without substantial effort.
* **Clarity (3/5):** The paper is generally well-organized, but the dense mathematical notation and the many conditions detract from clarity. Some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations and worked-out examples.
* **Originality (4/5):**  Addressing the theoretical limitations of EM in high dimensions is a genuinely novel contribution. The truncated EM algorithm and the decorrelated score statistic are also original contributions.
* **Significance (4/5):** The results are significant because they provide a theoretical foundation for using EM in high-dimensional settings, which is common in many applications. The potential for improved inference on latent variables could have a broad impact.




**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Fills a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of EM algorithms.
* Provides strong theoretical guarantees for parameter estimation and inference.
* Offers a practical algorithmic improvement (truncated EM).
* Well-written (despite technical complexity) and clearly motivated.

**Against:**

* High technical complexity may limit accessibility.
* Reliance on numerous conditions requires careful verification.
* Limited empirical validation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **Acceptance** with minor revisions.  This paper represents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of latent variable modeling. The authors have successfully addressed a long-standing open problem and provided a rigorous framework for analyzing the EM algorithm in high dimensions.  Addressing the weaknesses mentioned above – particularly providing more intuitive explanations, expanding the empirical evaluation, and discussing practical parameter tuning – would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
