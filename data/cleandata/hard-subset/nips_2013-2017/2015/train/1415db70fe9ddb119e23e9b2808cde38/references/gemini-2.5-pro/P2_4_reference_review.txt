### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm: its behavior in high-dimensional settings (where dimension d >> sample size n). The authors propose a "high-dimensional EM algorithm" which augments the standard EM procedure with a truncation (hard thresholding) step after each M-step to enforce sparsity. The paper's primary contributions are twofold. First, it provides a unified theoretical framework to show that, given a suitable initialization, their proposed algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves the near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of √(s*log(d)/n). Second, it moves beyond point estimation to develop a valid procedure for asymptotic inference. Specifically, the authors construct a "decorrelated score statistic" for testing hypotheses on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. They prove this statistic is asymptotically normal and that its limiting variance achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. The framework is general and its effectiveness is demonstrated on two key latent variable models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The EM algorithm is one of the most fundamental and widely used algorithms in statistics and machine learning. Its theoretical properties in the increasingly important high-dimensional regime have remained poorly understood. This paper provides the first comprehensive theoretical guarantees, covering both optimal estimation and valid inference, for a computationally feasible EM-type algorithm in this setting. This is a major theoretical contribution that addresses a long-standing open problem.

2.  **Technical Depth and Novelty:** The analysis presented is non-trivial and technically impressive. Extending the low-dimensional analysis of Balakrishnan et al. [2] to the high-dimensional setting is a significant challenge. The key innovation is the analysis of the truncation step within the iterative procedure. This requires moving from the standard `l2`-norm analysis to a more fine-grained `l_infinity`-norm characterization of the statistical error (Condition 3.3), which is a crucial and novel aspect of their proof strategy.

3.  **Comprehensive Theoretical Framework:** The paper provides a full inferential pipeline, from a practical algorithm to guarantees on point estimation and uncertainty quantification. The development of the decorrelated score test for the local optimum of a non-convex procedure is particularly novel. Unlike most high-dimensional inference literature which focuses on global optima of convex programs (like the Lasso), this work provides a method for estimators found by an iterative algorithm, which is a much more challenging and realistic scenario for latent variable models.

4.  **Generality and Clarity:** The theoretical results are presented in a general, assumption-based framework (e.g., Condition 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.4) and then cleanly instantiated for specific models. This makes the framework potentially applicable to a broad class of high-dimensional latent variable models beyond the two examples given. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and does an excellent job of positioning its contributions relative to existing literature.

**Weaknesses (Areas for improvement):**

1.  **The Initialization Assumption:** A significant caveat, common to analyses of non-convex optimization, is the requirement of a "suitable initialization" that is already within a constant factor of the true parameter's `l2`-norm. While the authors are transparent about this, the paper provides no guidance on how to obtain such an initial estimate. For the theory to be practically relevant, a discussion of feasible initialization methods (e.g., via spectral methods or other moment-based techniques) and their own theoretical guarantees would be necessary. Without this, the practical applicability of the entire framework remains a question.

2.  **Analysis of a Resampling-Based Algorithm:** The main theoretical results (Theorem 3.4) are proven for a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4 in the appendix), where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a common theoretical device to simplify proofs by ensuring independence across steps, but it does not match the algorithm one would actually implement (Algorithm 1), which uses the full dataset at each iteration. The paper should be more upfront about this in the main text and briefly discuss the potential challenges or differences in analyzing the standard, non-resampling algorithm.

3.  **Practicality of Assumptions and Tuning:** The assumptions for the inference theory (Assumption 4.5), while likely necessary, are quite complex and involve conditions on the sparsity of the inverse Fisher information (`w*`) and specific scaling requirements between `s*`, `s_w*`, `d`, and `n`. It would be helpful to provide more intuition on when these conditions might be violated. Furthermore, the algorithm introduces tuning parameters (sparsity level `bs`, Dantzig parameter `λ`) whose practical selection is not discussed. While the theory provides their required scaling, guidance on data-driven selection (e.g., cross-validation) would strengthen the paper.

### Detailed Review

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis. The decomposition of error into optimization and statistical components is a powerful and well-executed analytic strategy. The development of the inferential theory is particularly strong, correctly identifying the need for a de-biasing approach and adapting it to the challenging setting of a local optimum from a non-convex problem. The work appears complete and theoretically sound, despite the limitations mentioned above.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, states its contributions concisely, and then systematically develops the methodology and theory. The use of general, high-level conditions before diving into model-specific verifications makes the core theoretical argument easy to follow. Notations are well-defined. An expert reader should be able to understand the main ideas behind the proofs.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on the conceptual framework of Balakrishnan et al. for low-dimensional EM and the de-biasing techniques from the high-dimensional GLM literature (e.g., van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang), its synthesis and extension are novel and significant. The analysis of an EM-like algorithm with an integrated projection/truncation step is new, and the development of an inferential theory for the resulting estimator is a groundbreaking contribution for this class of problems. It clearly distinguishes itself from prior work on tensor methods (which often have suboptimal rates) and low-dimensional EM analysis.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is very high. It provides a foundational theoretical understanding for one of the most important algorithms in machine learning when applied to modern high-dimensional problems. Researchers working with latent variable models now have a theoretically-backed, computationally-tractable tool for both estimation and inference. This work is likely to inspire a new line of research on analyzing other non-convex iterative algorithms in high dimensions and will be a key reference in the field for years to come.

### Final Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the theory of high-dimensional statistics and the EM algorithm. The work is technically deep, highly original, and addresses a problem of significant importance. While the reliance on a strong initialization assumption and the analysis of a resampling-based algorithm are limitations, they are standard in this line of theoretical work and do not detract from the importance of the core results. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
