Summary
This paper develops a high-dimensional EM algorithm for latent variable models and an associated inferential framework. The algorithm augments the classical EM with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after the M-step to enforce sparsity along the path. The theory provides joint computational and statistical guarantees: with a good initialization and under local regularity conditions on the population Q-function, the iterates contract geometrically to a neighborhood of the truth, and the final estimator achieves a near-minimax ℓ2 rate O(√(s* log d / n)) (up to log n) when the M-step is either exactly maximized or updated by a gradient step. For inference on a low-dimensional parameter component in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, the authors construct a decorrelated score statistic, estimated via a Dantzig-type projection using derivatives of the Q-function. They prove asymptotic normality with variance achieving the semiparametric information bound. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, with conditions verified in the appendix (e.g., separation assumptions). The work connects to and extends low-dimensional EM theory (Balakrishnan et al., 2017) and debiased/orthogonalized inference (Zhang & Zhang, 2014; van de Geer et al., 2014) to latent variable models with nonconvex likelihoods and high-dimensional nuisance parameters.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic idea: a truncated EM that mirrors hard-thresholded projected methods while preserving the EM structure, and allows both exact and gradient M-step implementations.
- Joint computational-statistical analysis: an explicit decomposition into optimization error (geometric) and statistical error (near-optimal) under unified conditions, extending population-to-sample EM analysis to high dimensions.
- Inference contribution: decorrelated score based on Q-function derivatives, with a clean identity (Theorem 2.1) linking those to the observed score and Fisher information, yielding asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency.
- Concrete instantiations for two important latent variable models with verification of conditions, suggesting broad applicability.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Initialization assumptions: results require an initializer within a constant ℓ2 relative error and support-size control; practical, provable initializers for high-dimensional latent variable models are not provided. Given the critique of tensor methods, it would be helpful to give constructive initialization schemes with guarantees or empirical evidence.
- Tuning and adaptivity: the truncation level b_s must be of the same order as s*, which is unknown in practice. Guidance on choosing b_s (e.g., via cross-validation, information criteria, or data-driven thresholding) and sensitivity analyses are needed.
- Monotonicity and EM flavor: the truncation step breaks EM’s monotonic ascent of Q; while the analysis handles this, discussion of practical convergence diagnostics and potential cycling would help.
- Resampling in analysis: the main theorem is proved for a resampling/split-sample variant; it would be valuable to state and prove guarantees for the single-sample iterate sequence used in practice or quantify the price paid.
- Empirics: the paper appears primarily theoretical. For NeurIPS, empirical validation on synthetic and real data (rates, robustness to misspecification, coverage of confidence intervals, type I error control) would strengthen the contribution.
- Related work: the truncation step is closely related to projected/iterative hard thresholding (IHT) and gradient hard thresholding pursuit in high-dimensional M-estimation/nonconvex optimization. Citing and positioning relative to works such as Blumensath & Davies (2009), Jain, Netrapalli & Sanghavi (2014), and more recent projected gradient with sparsity constraints would clarify novelty on the optimization side. Also discuss penalized EM (e.g., Städler et al., 2010) and how your truncation compares practically/theoretically to ℓ1-penalized M-steps.
- Assumptions for inference: asymptotic normality requires sparsity of the decorrelation vector w* and several concentration/Lipschitz conditions. While standard in debiasing literature, these can be restrictive in mixtures; more intuition on when s_w* is small for the considered models would help.

Quality
The technical development is solid and carefully structured. The decomposition in Theorem 3.4 is plausible and informative, and the use of entrywise (ℓ1) statistical errors to control the truncation effects is insightful. The inference result is well-motivated via Theorem 2.1 and matches known efficiency bounds. However, the reliance on strong initialization and resampling weakens practical guarantees, and the lack of experiments makes it hard to assess robustness.

Clarity
Overall well-organized, but the manuscript is dense and notation-heavy. Some notation is overloaded (e.g., gradients rQ with respect to different arguments) and could be streamlined. The provided text includes formatting issues (garbled references, symbols). Suggestions:
- Provide a concise “Assumptions at a glance” table mapping Conditions 3.1–3.3 and 4.1–4.4 to the concrete models with explicit rates.
- Clarify algorithmic choices: default step sizes, stopping criteria, and computational complexity per iteration.
- Add a brief discussion on how to choose b_s and λ in practice.

Originality
Combining EM with hard-thresholding and providing sharp high-dimensional rates, together with an orthogonalized inference procedure tailored to latent variable models, appears novel. The connection of Q-function derivatives to observed score/Fisher information is classical, but leveraging it for decorrelated inference in nonconvex latent models is original and useful.

Significance
High-dimensional latent variable modeling is important; providing both estimation and inference with rates and asymptotics is impactful. If complemented with practical initialization and empirical validation, this framework could be adopted by researchers and practitioners.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you propose or analyze a concrete initializer (e.g., method-of-moments on a subset of coordinates, spectral clustering for mixtures) that satisfies the basin condition?
- Can the theory accommodate adaptive truncation (e.g., threshold by universal thresholds instead of fixed b_s), or ℓ1-penalized M-steps, and how would the rates compare?
- Provide finite-sample bounds (constants) in Gaussian mixtures to illustrate required SNR and n for typical d, s*.
- For inference, discuss the stability of the Dantzig step and provide guidance for λ selection; include simulations showing coverage/size.

Pros
- Strong, general theory bridging computation and statistics in high-dimensional latent models.
- Near-optimal estimation rates with explicit optimization-statistics tradeoff.
- Semiparametric-efficient inference via decorrelated scores adapted to EM.

Cons
- Strong and unvalidated initialization/tuning assumptions.
- Analysis relies on resampling; practical algorithm analyzed less directly.
- No empirical results presented; clarity and references need polishing; missing connections to IHT/projection methods.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The theoretical contributions are substantial and timely, but the paper would benefit from clearer practical guidance, stronger related-work positioning on hard-thresholded optimization, and empirical validation. If space permits, adding experiments and a discussion of initialization/tuning would significantly strengthen the submission.
