This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, a regime where existing theory breaks down. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and, crucially, provide statistical and computational guarantees, including asymptotic normality for parameter estimation. This is a significant undertaking, as establishing such guarantees in high dimensions is notoriously difficult. The work builds upon recent advancements in understanding the EM algorithm's convergence properties in the low-dimensional setting [2] and attempts to extend them to a more realistic high-dimensional scenario.

The strengths of the paper lie in its ambition and technical rigor. The authors clearly identify a gap in the existing literature and propose a novel approach to address it. The introduction of the truncation step is a reasonable attempt to enforce sparsity and regain theoretical tractability. The mathematical analysis appears substantial, with detailed conditions and proofs sketched (with references to the appendix). The establishment of asymptotic normality, particularly in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, is a noteworthy contribution if validated.

However, I have significant concerns regarding the practical relevance and justification of certain modeling choices and assumptions. My primary skepticism stems from the rather strong conditions required to establish the theoretical results. Specifically, the dependence on a carefully chosen initialization (`init ⇤ /k ⇤k 2 < 1`), the need for a sparsity parameter `bs` chosen to a specific order, and the assumptions regarding the Fisher information matrix (`⇢ max` and `⇢ min` bounds) feel restrictive and potentially limit the applicability of the proposed method.  The statistical error term also relies heavily on the assumed smoothness and concavity of the likelihood, which may not hold in many practical scenarios.

The paper relies heavily on assumptions about the data-generating process. While the Gaussian mixture model example is helpful, it doesn’t fully address the broader applicability concerns. The analysis, while mathematically thorough, feels very tailored to the specific setup and it's unclear how easily the results would generalize to more complex latent variable models. The authors acknowledge existing work on tensor methods and other high-dimensional approaches, but their dismissal of these methods feels somewhat dismissive without a more in-depth comparison on realistic datasets. The justification for the proposed truncation step rests heavily on theoretical considerations; a demonstration of its practical benefits in terms of improved estimation accuracy compared to standard EM or other methods (like variational inference) would be valuable.

The frequent references to constants (C, C0, etc.) without specific values also make it difficult to assess the practical implications of the theoretical rates. A discussion of the magnitudes of these constants and their sensitivity to problem parameters would be helpful.  Furthermore, the reliance on a large number of technical conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) adds complexity and obscures the core ideas of the paper. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant open problem in statistical machine learning.
*   Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithm.
*   Establishes asymptotic normality for parameter estimation in high dimensions.
*   Presents a novel algorithmic approach (truncated EM) to tackle high-dimensionality.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong assumptions limiting practical applicability.
*   Theoretical results may not translate well to real-world datasets.
*   Lack of empirical validation demonstrating practical improvements.
*   Complexity of the analysis and reliance on numerous technical conditions.
*   Dismissive attitude toward competing approaches.



**Overall Assessment:**

While I acknowledge the technical sophistication and ambition of this work, I find the strong assumptions and lack of empirical validation concerning. I believe the paper falls short of NIPS standards due to its limited practical relevance and the potential difficulties in applying it to real-world problems. The theoretical contributions, while valuable, are contingent on a set of assumptions that may not be easily satisfied.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

I suggest the authors focus on providing more empirical evidence demonstrating the practical benefits of their proposed algorithm and relaxing some of the restrictive assumptions. A more thorough comparison with existing methods, including a discussion of their limitations, would also strengthen the paper.
