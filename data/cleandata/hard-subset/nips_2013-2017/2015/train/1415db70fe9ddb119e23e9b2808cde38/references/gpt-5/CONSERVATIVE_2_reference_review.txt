Summary
The paper proposes a variant of EM for high-dimensional latent variable models that appends a hard-thresholding “truncation” step after each E/M step to enforce sparsity. Under a suite of conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, local strong concavity/smoothness, and entrywise statistical error bounds), the authors establish that, with a suitable initialization and an appropriately chosen sparsity level, the iterates converge geometrically up to a statistical error of near-optimal order √(s* log d / n) (with extra log factors). They treat both exact M-step and a gradient-ascent M-step. Building on the resulting estimator, they construct a decorrelated score test (via a Dantzig-selector based nuisance projection) and show asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. They illustrate verification of conditions for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions (details deferred to the appendix).

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned relative to EM convergence analyses in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) and to high-dimensional inference methods for GLMs (de-biasing/desparsification). The truncation step is reminiscent of iterative hard thresholding in sparse regression; the inference construction follows the decorrelation/Dantzig paradigm (e.g., van de Geer et al.). Compared to tensor methods, the authors argue better rates and applicability in high dimensions under sparsity.

Quality
- Theoretical development is carefully laid out, with clear separation of population vs sample analyses and explicit conditions. The decomposition into optimization vs statistical error is standard and sensible, and the semiparametric efficiency claim is aligned with established theory.
- However, the guarantees rely on strong and partially unverifiable assumptions in practice: a good initialization within a basin of attraction; strong local concavity/smoothness; fairly strict SNR/separation for mixtures; a sparsity level bs essentially matching s*; and sparsity of the nuisance projection w*. The main convergence theorem is proved for a resampling/samplesplitting variant (Algorithm 4) rather than the single-sample algorithm typically used, which raises concerns about practical relevance and sample efficiency.
- No empirical evaluation is presented. There are no simulations demonstrating rates, sensitivity to bs, or the finite-sample performance of the decorrelated test (e.g., coverage/Type-I error), nor comparisons to standard baselines (ℓ1-penalized EM, coordinate descent, or tensor/spectral initializers followed by EM). This makes it difficult to assess robustness and practical impact.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but generally readable for an expert; the algorithm and main theorems are stated clearly, and the connections between rQ and score/Fisher information (Theorem 2.1) are helpful.
- Important implementation details are missing: how to choose bs without knowledge of s*; how to initialize within the basin; how to set T and stopping rules; and guidance on step sizes for the gradient M-step beyond the population-level choice.
- Many key verifications are deferred to the appendix; the main text would benefit from at least one fully worked-out model with explicit constants and finite-sample guidance. The references list contains formatting issues and appears incomplete.

Originality
- Combining EM with hard-thresholding is a natural but incremental idea building on iterative hard-thresholding and prior analyses of EM as a perturbed gradient method. The inference piece closely follows established decorrelated score methodology, adapted to the EM/latent variable context.
- The novelty lies in unifying these elements to provide joint computational and inferential theory for high-dimensional latent variable models. This is a worthwhile goal, but the methodological ingredients are largely established.

Significance
- If the assumptions can be met and the method is stable, the results could be useful for high-dimensional inference in mixtures and related latent variable models. Achieving near-optimal estimation rates and semiparametric efficiency is theoretically appealing.
- Without empirical evidence and given the reliance on strong model/separation assumptions and nontrivial initialization, the near-term practical impact is uncertain.

Strengths
- Clear computational-statistical tradeoff analysis with geometric decay of optimization error and near-optimal statistical error.
- Treats both exact and gradient M-steps under a unified framework.
- Asymptotically normal decorrelated score achieving semiparametric efficiency for low-dimensional components.
- Verification sketches for Gaussian mixtures/mixtures of regressions.

Weaknesses
- No experiments; no comparison to established baselines (ℓ1-penalized EM, de-biased estimators from penalized likelihood, tensor + EM pipelines).
- Assumes knowledge of sparsity level via bs and requires bs ≍ s*; practical selection is not discussed.
- Requires strong initialization within a basin; no concrete initialization procedure is provided in high dimension.
- Theoretical analysis relies on a resampling/split-sample variant; extension to the practical single-sample setting is not demonstrated.
- Hard-thresholding can be unstable and may introduce bias; robustness analyses (mis-specification, heavy tails) are absent.
- Reference list has significant formatting issues and appears incomplete.

Questions for the authors
- How do you propose to choose bs in practice? Can you provide data-driven procedures with guarantees?
- What initialization strategy do you recommend for high-dimensional mixtures that lands within the basin (e.g., spectral/tensor/k-means)? Can you quantify its radius/probability?
- Can the theory be extended to the non-resampling algorithm, or can you empirically show that resampling is unnecessary?
- How sensitive are results to SNR and to misspecification? What happens when components are not well separated?
- For inference, how does the decorrelated score perform in finite samples relative to de-biased ℓ1-EM or post-selection methods?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add synthetic experiments for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions showing estimation error vs n, d, s*, support recovery, and coverage/Type-I error of the decorrelated test. Compare to ℓ1-penalized EM and tensor+EM baselines.
- Provide practical guidance for bs, step sizes, stopping criteria, and initialization, with ablations.
- Clarify whether sample splitting is required in practice; if not, discuss how to control dependence in proofs or provide empirical evidence.
- Clean up references and ensure adequate coverage of related work (IHT, de-biasing, penalized EM for mixtures).

Minor issues
- Many references are garbled; please correct author names and venues.
- Some notation is heavy; a summary table of symbols would help.

Recommendation
Overall score: 4 (weak reject)
Confidence: 4/5

Reasons to accept: Solid theoretical development; unifies estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models; potentially impactful if made practical.

Reasons to reject: Lack of empirical validation and baselines; reliance on strong assumptions (initialization, SNR, sparsity match, resampling); incremental methodological novelty relative to established hard-thresholding and decorrelated inference techniques.
