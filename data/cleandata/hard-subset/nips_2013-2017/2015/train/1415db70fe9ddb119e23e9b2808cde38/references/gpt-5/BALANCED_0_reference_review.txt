Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm for latent variable models by augmenting each iteration with a hard truncation (T-step) that enforces sparsity. Two implementations of the M-step are considered: exact maximization and a gradient EM step. The main computational-statistical result (Theorem 3.4) shows, under suitable initialization and regularity conditions, that the iterates contract geometrically to a neighborhood of the truth and achieve an ℓ2 estimation error of order sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (up to log factors), with s* the sparsity level. Building on the resulting estimator, the paper proposes a decorrelated score (and Wald) test to perform inference on a low-dimensional component α of the high-dimensional parameter, using a Dantzig-type estimator for the orthogonalization vector. Under conditions (Conditions 4.1–4.4, Assumption 4.5), the score statistic is asymptotically normal and attains the semiparametric information bound. The analysis explicitly treats both exact and gradient M-steps, and illustrates the framework on two models (Gaussian mixtures, mixtures of regressions).

The work is positioned against: (i) classical EM theory and finite-sample rates in the low-dimensional regime (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.), (ii) proximal point perspectives on EM, (iii) high-dimensional debiasing/desparsification methods (Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.; related post-selection works), and (iv) tensor-based approaches for latent variable models, which typically yield suboptimal rates in high dimensions. The paper’s key novelty relative to [2] is the truncation step enabling sparse recovery and the extension of inference tools to nonconvex latent variable settings without relying on global optima of convex surrogates.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clear, modular recipe (E/M/T-steps) that marries EM with hard thresholding to maintain sparsity along the optimization path.
- Unified theory: Joint computational (geometric contraction within a basin) and statistical (near-minimax ℓ2 risk) guarantees, covering both exact and gradient EM.
- Inference contribution: A decorrelated score tailored to latent variable models via identities linking Q and the log-likelihood (Theorem 2.1), with asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency.
- Generality: Conditions are formulated abstractly and then instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions; the framework appears portable to other latent models.
- Clear comparison to related lines (low-dim EM, debiasing, tensor methods), and discussion of why high-dimensional latent structure complicates inference beyond GLMs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strong assumptions and initialization: The guarantees require an initializer within a constant-relative ℓ2 error in a basin B and a sufficiently large SNR (for GMM). How such an initializer is obtained in high dimensions is not addressed beyond citing “suitable initialization.” This is pivotal in practice.
- Analysis via resampling: The main contraction theorem is proved for a resampling/split-data variant to control statistical error across iterations. This is often conservative and may be impractical. A result for the practical single-sample iteration would strengthen the contribution.
- Tuning and unknown sparsity: The truncation level b_s must be on the order of s*. In practice, s* is unknown; guidance (data-driven rules, stability paths, or theory for over-/under-specification) is limited. Similarly, the Dantzig tuning parameter depends on nuisance quantities (e.g., concentration rates).
- Assumptions for inference: The sparsity of w* (solution to I(θ*)_{·,·} w* = I(θ*)_{·,α}) can be restrictive in correlated designs; the paper should discuss when this holds in the presented models and its empirical plausibility.
- Practical compute and identifiability: The T-step breaks EM’s monotonicity; a discussion of its practical impact would help. For mixtures (e.g., symmetric ±θ), label/sign non-identifiability should be addressed explicitly in the inference setup.
- Missing empirical validation: No experiments are shown to assess finite-sample behavior, sensitivity to b_s and initialization, or comparison against penalized likelihood (e.g., ℓ1-regularized EM), tensor methods, or debiased-Lasso baselines in comparable regimes.

Quality
Technically careful and largely sound. The decomposition into optimization and statistical error is clear, and the use of ℓ1-based statistical error in Condition 3.3 is appropriate for tracking entrywise errors through truncation. However, the reliance on resampling, strong basin assumptions, and nontrivial conditions for inference (especially sparsity of w*) raise questions about practical scope. Proof sketches are deferred, which is acceptable, but some dependences (e.g., on ||θ*||) and constants could be made more transparent.

Clarity
The exposition is generally clear, but notation is heavy. The paper frequently defers key specifics to appendices/long version (models, constants, Algorithm 4). Providing a succinct, self-contained statement of assumptions for the two example models in the main text would improve readability. Some minor typos and inconsistent notation appear.

Originality
High. Combining EM with truncation and delivering both optimal estimation rates and valid inference for latent variable models in high-dimensions appears novel relative to prior EM analyses and debiasing work that assumes convexity.

Significance
Potentially significant: a general recipe for high-dimensional latent variable estimation and inference. Impact depends on practical viability: initialization, tuning, and robustness beyond the stylized models. Empirical support would substantially increase perceived significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and timely theoretical contribution bridging computation and inference for nonconvex latent models in high dimensions.
- Near-optimal rates and semiparametric efficiency.
- General framework applicable to multiple models and both EM variants.

Cons for acceptance
- No experiments; practical performance, robustness, and tuning are unclear.
- Strong initialization and sparsity-of-w* assumptions; resampling analysis limits practical relevance.
- Limited discussion of identifiability and monotonicity consequences of truncation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide practical initialization strategies (e.g., spectral/tensor warm starts, method-of-moments) with theory or empirical validation.
- Replace or complement resampling analysis with a single-sample proof (e.g., via martingale or leave-one-out techniques) or at least empirical evidence that resampling is unnecessary.
- Add experiments on GMM and mixture of regressions with varying n, d, s*, SNR, comparing to ℓ1-EM, tensor methods, and debiased-Lasso baselines; report coverage and power for the proposed tests.
- Discuss data-driven selection of b_s and Dantzig tuning (cross-validation, information criteria, stability selection), and sensitivity to mis-specification of s*.
- Clarify when w* is sparse in the presented models; give interpretable sufficient conditions.
- Address identifiability in mixtures and the effect of T-step on likelihood monotonicity.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theory is strong and novel, but the lack of empirical validation and reliance on strong assumptions and resampling limit confidence in practical impact. With added experiments and some tightening of assumptions/analysis, this could be a strong paper. Confidence: medium.
