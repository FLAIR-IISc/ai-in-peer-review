

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

This paper proposes a novel high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm for latent variable models, addressing the critical gap between computation and statistics in high-dimensional settings where dimension d exceeds sample size n. The authors introduce a truncation step to enforce sparsity during both E-step and M-step iterations, enabling the development of a general inferential theory for parameter estimation and uncertainty assessment. The paper establishes geometric convergence rates for the proposed algorithm and develops a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional components, showing asymptotic normality with optimal limiting variance.

The work builds on the foundational low-dimensional results of [2] which established geometric convergence rates and p(d/n) statistical rates, but extends them to the high-dimensional regime with p(s^* log d/n) rates (where s^* is the true sparsity level). The paper distinguishes itself from related work on high-dimensional inference (e.g., debiasing methods for Lasso [13, 26, 32]) by addressing latent variable models with non-convex likelihoods, where global optimization is computationally infeasible. It also contrasts with tensor methods [1, 9] that suffer from suboptimal statistical rates in high dimensions.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for both estimation (Theorem 3.4) and inference (Theorem 4.6). The analysis of the truncation step's impact on the iterative solution path is technically sophisticated and addresses a significant challenge in high-dimensional EM algorithms.

2. **Novel Contributions:** The truncation step (T-step) in the high-dimensional EM algorithm is a novel technical contribution that enables sparsity enforcement throughout the solution path. The decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing in high-dimensional latent variable models is a significant methodological advancement.

3. **Comprehensive Comparison:** The paper thoroughly compares with related work, clearly articulating how their approach differs from low-dimensional EM extensions [2], Lasso-based inference methods [13, 26, 32], and tensor methods [1, 9].

4. **Practical Relevance:** The framework applies to multiple high-dimensional latent variable models (Gaussian mixture, mixture of regression), with specific verification of conditions for these models in the appendix.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Assumption Specificity:** The theoretical guarantees depend on several technical conditions (Conditions 3.1-3.3, Assumption 4.5) that may not hold for all high-dimensional latent variable models. While the paper verifies these conditions for specific models, the generality of the framework for other latent structures remains unclear.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis:** While the paper establishes geometric convergence rates, it does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm (particularly the truncation step), which is important for practical implementation.

3. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation:** The paper focuses on theoretical guarantees but does not discuss practical considerations for choosing the sparsity parameter b_s or the number of iterations T, which would be valuable for practitioners.

4. **Boundary Cases:** The paper does not address how the algorithm behaves when d and n are comparable (neither d << n nor d >> n), which is a common scenario in practice.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by extending the EM algorithm to high-dimensional settings with theoretical guarantees.
- The truncation step is a novel and well-motivated technical contribution that enables the development of the inferential theory.
- The decorrelated score statistic achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound, demonstrating theoretical optimality.
- The paper provides near-minimax optimal statistical rates (p(s^* log d/n)) for parameter estimation, which is a major theoretical contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The assumptions required for the theoretical results may be restrictive for some practical applications of latent variable models.
- The paper does not provide a detailed computational complexity analysis, which is important for assessing the practical feasibility of the approach.
- The connection between the theoretical results and empirical performance is limited to theoretical rates, with no experimental validation.

**Overall Assessment:**

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The proofs in the appendix appear complete, and the authors carefully address both statistical and computational aspects of their framework.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The notation is consistent, and the main contributions are well-articulated. The algorithm description is clear, though some intuitive explanations of why the truncation step is necessary could be enhanced.

Originality: The paper makes significant contributions by extending the EM algorithm to high-dimensional settings with theoretical guarantees, which has not been previously established. The truncation step and decorrelated score statistic are novel technical contributions.

Significance: The work addresses an important problem in high-dimensional statistics with practical relevance across multiple domains. The framework provides the first computationally feasible approach for optimal parameter estimation and asymptotic inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.

This paper represents a substantial contribution to the field, making a clear and significant advance in the theoretical understanding of EM algorithms in high-dimensional settings. The technical novelty is substantial, and the theoretical guarantees are strong and well-supported. The paper's focus on both estimation and inference with optimal rates represents a significant step forward in the statistical theory of latent variable models. While the assumptions may limit applicability to some specific models, the framework is general enough to be applied to a broad family of latent variable models.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS.
