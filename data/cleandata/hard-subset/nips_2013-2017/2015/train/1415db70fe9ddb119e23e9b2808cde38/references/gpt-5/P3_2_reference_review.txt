Summary
The paper studies parameter estimation and inference for high-dimensional latent variable models via a modified EM algorithm. The key algorithmic idea is to insert a hard-thresholding truncation step after each M-step (or gradient M-step), enforcing sparsity along the iterates. Under local regularity conditions on the population Q-function (local strong concavity/smoothness and Lipschitz-type properties), an initialization within a basin of attraction, and an ℓ1-type statistical error control on the sample M-step, the authors establish a nonasymptotic error decomposition: a geometric optimization error plus a statistical error of order √(s* log d / n) (up to extra log factors), which is near-minimax. Building on the EM perspective, they further construct a decorrelated score statistic based on Q and a Dantzig-type estimator for the orthogonalization vector, and prove asymptotic normality for low-dimensional components of interest with variance attaining the semiparametric efficiency bound. The framework covers exact and gradient M-step implementations and is illustrated on a two-component spherical Gaussian mixture and mixture of regressions.

Relation to prior work
- Extends the population-to-sample EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) to high-dimensional settings, adding a truncation step akin to iterative hard-thresholding to maintain sparsity.
- Inference builds on the de-biasing/desparsification line (van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang, Javanmard & Montanari) but tailors the orthogonal score to latent-variable models via EM identities (connecting ∇Q and the observed-data score, Fisher information).
- Compared to tensor methods, the proposed approach targets faster statistical rates and broader applicability when moments are poorly behaved.

Strengths
- Technically ambitious, unifying computational and statistical analyses for nonconvex, high-dimensional latent-variable estimation.
- Clear decomposition of optimization vs. statistical error; geometric convergence under both exact and gradient M-steps.
- Asymptotic inference result that is semiparametrically efficient is novel for this class of models.
- The use of entrywise (ℓ1) statistical error to control the effect of truncation is insightful and addresses a key challenge absent in low-dimensional EM analyses.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical relevance of the main convergence theorem relies on a resampling (sample-splitting across iterations) analysis. While common for simplifying dependence arguments, no theorem is stated for the standard single-sample iterate sequence, and no guidance is given on how much the rates degrade without resampling. This substantially weakens the practical message.
- Assumptions are strong and not fully transparent:
  - Basin-of-attraction initialization: requires a constant-factor relative error; no concrete, implementable initializer is provided beyond references. How to initialize for the mixture of regressions under weak SNR or overlapping components?
  - Condition 3.2 (local strong concavity/smoothness of Q(·; θ*)) is nontrivial for many latent models. For GMM, the proof relies on a strong SNR assumption; for mixture of regressions, similar clarity is needed.
  - The sparsity of the orthogonalization vector w* (Assumption 4.5) is critical for inference. This corresponds to sparsity of the regression of the α-score on the γ-score and may fail in many realistic designs; the paper should discuss when s_w* is plausibly small for the considered models.
  - Choice of bs: (3.8) requires bs to be within a constant factor of s*. In practice s* is unknown. No adaptive rule (e.g., information criterion/Lepski-type/two-stage) or robustness analysis is provided.
  - Stepsize for gradient EM requires population quantities (µ, ν) to set η=2/(µ+ν), which are unknown.
- Computational cost: Each inference step requires solving a Dantzig selector on d−1 variables; if solved every iteration this may dominate the EM cost in high d. The paper should clarify when and how often w is estimated, and whether support-restricted or approximate solvers suffice.
- Lack of experiments: No empirical results are presented to validate finite-sample performance, sensitivity to initialization and bs, or coverage of the proposed tests. Given the nontrivial constants and assumptions, empirical evidence is important for a NeurIPS audience.
- Scope of models: The analysis and examples appear to focus on the simplest GMM (two-component, spherical, equal weights) with strong SNR. It is unclear how the constants and assumptions scale for more general mixtures (unknown mixing proportions, multiple components, nonspherical covariances), missing data models, or HMMs.
- Clarity/presentation issues: The manuscript contains notational glitches and typos (e.g., repeated symbols, rendering issues), incomplete references (years/pages missing), and some heavy notation that could be streamlined. The identity in Theorem 2.1 is standard but stated without the caveat that ∇θQ_n(θ;θ)=∇ℓ_n(θ)/n (not for arbitrary second argument).

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a theorem (or at least a sketch) for the non-resampled algorithm using the full sample across iterations? What additional mixing or martingale arguments are needed, and what price in rates?
2. How to choose bs in practice without knowing s*? Is there theory for a data-driven selector or a continuation schedule?
3. For inference, how plausible is sparsity of w* in the considered models? Can you characterize s_w* for the two examples?
4. Do you estimate w only once at the end (plug-in b) or iteratively? What are the computational costs for d ≫ n in practice?
5. Can you propose/justify a concrete initializer with provable closeness for your models (e.g., spectral or method-of-moments) under the same SNR conditions?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical studies on synthetic and real data comparing against penalized EM, tensor methods, and de-biased GLM baselines where applicable. Assess estimation error, support recovery, coverage and computational time.
- Add a non-resampling result, even if slightly weaker, or discuss practical heuristics to approximate the theoretical requirements.
- Discuss adaptivity to unknown sparsity and robust tuning of bs and λ in the Dantzig program; consider cross-validation or information criteria and analyze their effect.
- Clarify the assumptions for mixture of regressions and include explicit constants/rates; consider weaker SNR regimes.
- Expand related work to include hard-thresholding/projection methods (e.g., Blumensath & Davies) and penalized EM literature, and contrast your analysis.

Evaluation
- Quality: Strong theoretical development but with strong assumptions, resampling-based analysis, and no experiments.
- Clarity: Fair but could be improved; fix notation/rendering and complete references.
- Originality: Good. The integration of truncated EM with efficient inference for latent-variable models is novel.
- Significance: Potentially high if extended beyond restrictive settings and validated empirically.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The theoretical contributions are interesting and potentially impactful, but the current version lacks empirical validation and relies on strong assumptions and resampling that limit practical relevance. With a non-resampled guarantee, clearer conditions for broader models, and experiments, this work would be a strong candidate.
