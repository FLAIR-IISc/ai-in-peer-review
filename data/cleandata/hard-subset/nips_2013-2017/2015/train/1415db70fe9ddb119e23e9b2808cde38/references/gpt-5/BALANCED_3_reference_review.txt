Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm for latent-variable models that (i) enforces sparsity via a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after each M-step, and (ii) provides an inferential procedure based on a decorrelated score. The main estimation result (Theorem 3.4) shows that, with a suitably accurate initialization and an appropriately chosen sparsity level b_s, the iterates contract geometrically toward a neighborhood of the truth and the final estimator achieves a (near-)minimax statistical error of order sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (up to log factors). The inferential result (Theorem 4.6) establishes asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency of a decorrelated score statistic for a low-dimensional component in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, using a Dantzig-type estimator of the orthogonalization vector.

The paper builds on the “population-to-sample” EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017), extending it to high dimensions via truncation to maintain sparsity, and combines ideas from de-biasing/desparsification (e.g., van de Geer et al., Javanmard–Montanari) to obtain valid inference without relying on convex global optima. It contrasts with ℓ1-penalized EM approaches (e.g., Städler et al., 2010) by providing both fast rates and a general inference framework under nonconvexity. Applications are sketched for high-dimensional Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important gap: computationally feasible estimation and valid inference for high-dimensional latent-variable models with nonconcave likelihoods.
- Conceptually clean algorithmic modification (E–M–T) and analysis that yields a decomposition into optimization error (geometric decay) and statistical error (near-optimal).
- Covers both exact and gradient M-step implementations within a unified framework (different Lipschitz conditions).
- Inferential component is well-motivated: decorrelated score achieves semiparametric efficiency for the low-dimensional target, with clear conditions mapping rQ and Tn to the score and Fisher information.
- Provides model-specific implications for GMM and mixtures of regressions, achieving sharp rates compared to tensor-method-based estimators.

Weaknesses and concerns:
Quality
- Strong assumptions for theory to go through: (a) initialization within a fixed relative error of the truth, (b) sparsity level b_s close to s*, (c) high SNR/separation for GMM, and (d) sparsity of the orthogonalization vector w* (s*_w = o(√(n/log d))). The practicality of achieving (a) and verifying (d) is not fully addressed.
- Analysis is given for a “resampling EM” that splits data across iterations to simplify concentration arguments. This is rarely used in practice and effectively reduces the sample size per iteration. There is no theoretical guarantee for the standard (full-data) iterative scheme, nor a martingale/concentration analysis to handle dependence across iterations.
- No empirical evaluation. There are no simulations or real-data experiments to assess finite-sample behavior, sensitivity to tuning (b_s, λ in Dantzig, step sizes), robustness to misspecification, or comparison to baselines (e.g., ℓ1-penalized EM, gradient EM with ℓ1 penalties, de-biased approaches applied to pseudo-complete-data formulations).
- Some technical details are deferred to appendices, and constants/choices (e.g., b_s, λ, η, stopping criteria) are not operationalized.

Clarity
- The manuscript is notation-heavy; many assumptions are stated abstractly (“possibly depends on”) making it hard for non-experts to assess when they hold. Some references appear garbled or incomplete.
- The role and verification of Conditions 3.1–3.3 and 4.1–4.4 for the showcased models could be summarized more transparently in the main text (not only in appendices).

Originality
- The combination of truncated EM for estimation with decorrelated-score inference in high-dimensional nonconvex latent-variable models is, to my knowledge, novel and of clear interest. The connection of rQ and Tn to the score and Fisher information in the latent-variable setting is neat and useful.

Significance
- If the assumptions are reasonably met and a non-resampling analysis can be established, the results would be impactful: they would provide a practical recipe for both estimation and inference in important classes of latent-variable models where convex approaches do not apply. At present, the reliance on strong initialization, resampling analysis, and lack of empirical evidence limits immediate practical significance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical advances: near-optimal rates with nonconvex algorithm; semiparametric efficient inference for low-dimensional components amid high-dimensional nuisance.
- General framework potentially applicable to many latent-variable models.
- Bridges a gap between computation and statistics for EM in high dimensions.

Arguments against acceptance:
- No experiments or empirical validation.
- Analysis relies on resampling EM and strong initialization assumptions; no guarantees for standard full-sample iterations.
- Limited practical guidance on tuning and initialization; verification of w*-sparsity may be restrictive.
- Clarity issues and heavy dependence on appendices.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a non-resampling (full-data) analysis, e.g., via uniform concentration/martingale techniques, or empirical process arguments that control dependence across iterations.
- Give concrete, model-specific initialization strategies with provable guarantees (e.g., spectral/tensor initializers only for warm start, with subsequent EM–T improving to optimal rates), and discuss how their conditions map to your basin-of-attraction requirements.
- Add comprehensive experiments: synthetic GMM and mixture-of-regressions across n, d, s*, SNR; compare to ℓ1-penalized EM/gradient-EM, de-biased Lasso baselines, and tensor methods; evaluate estimation error, support recovery, coverage/Type I error of the decorrelated score/Wald tests; assess sensitivity to tuning b_s and λ.
- Provide practical tuning rules (e.g., b_s via stability selection or cross-validation, λ ∝ √(log d/n), step size η = 2/(µ+ν)), and stopping criteria.
- Discuss approximate sparsity and robustness to mild model misspecification.
- Improve clarity: tighten notation, summarize key conditions and their scaling in the main text, and fix references.

Recommendation: Reject (weak). The theoretical contributions are promising and potentially significant, but empirical validation and a more practical, non-resampling analysis are needed for NeurIPS. Addressing the above points would substantially strengthen the paper.
