

# Peer Review: "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings where dimension d exceeds sample size n. While prior work (e.g., [2, 18, 25, 30]) established convergence guarantees in low-dimensional regimes, the paper develops a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step to enforce sparsity. The authors prove geometric convergence rates to a local optimum with near-minimax optimal statistical error O(√(s* log d/n)), and establish asymptotic normality for a decorrelated score statistic that achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound. The framework applies to Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Rigor**: The paper makes a significant contribution by bridging the computational-statistical gap in high dimensions. The truncation step (line 6 of Algorithm 1) is a clever and necessary innovation to handle high-dimensional sparsity, which previous work could not address. The proof techniques for handling the truncation's effects on the iterative solution path are technically sophisticated.

2. **Comprehensive Theoretical Guarantees**: The authors provide both computational (Theorem 3.4) and statistical (Theorem 4.6) guarantees in a unified framework. The decomposition of estimation error into optimization and statistical components is particularly insightful. The decorrelated score statistic achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound (Theorem 4.6), which is a major theoretical contribution.

3. **Applicability and Generality**: The framework accommodates both exact maximization (Algorithm 2) and gradient ascent (Algorithm 3) for the M-step, making it broadly applicable. The verification of conditions for specific models (Gaussian mixtures, mixture of regressions) demonstrates the framework's practical utility.

4. **Clear Contribution to the Literature**: The paper carefully contextualizes its work against related literature (e.g., [2] for low-dimensional EM, [13, 26, 32] for high-dimensional inference), making the novelty and significance evident.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Assumption Verification Complexity**: The conditions (3.1-3.3) and (4.1-4.4) require verification for each specific model. While the paper provides verification for Gaussian mixtures, the complexity of this verification process may limit the framework's immediate applicability to other high-dimensional latent variable models. The paper could better articulate how general the verification process is.

2. **Practical Implementation Concerns**: The truncation parameter bs (line 1 of Algorithm 1) is set to a theoretical value (3.8) that depends on unknown quantities like s* (true sparsity level). The paper lacks discussion on how to select bs in practice (e.g., via cross-validation), which is crucial for real-world application. The theoretical value of bs might be too conservative for practical use.

3. **Statistical Error Rate Justification**: The statistical error rate of O(√(s* log d/n)) is claimed to be near-minimax optimal. However, the paper does not explicitly compare with known minimax lower bounds for the specific models considered (e.g., Gaussian mixtures). While the authors reference [9] for tensor methods, they don't directly establish the minimax optimality of their rate for their models.

4. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper establishes geometric convergence but doesn't compare computational complexity with alternative high-dimensional inference methods (e.g., tensor methods [1] or gradient-based approaches for penalized likelihoods [23]). For large-scale problems, computational efficiency is as important as statistical guarantees.

## Critical Assessment

The paper's main theoretical contribution is compelling and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The truncation step is a natural and effective way to handle high dimensionality in the EM framework, and the proofs for the convergence rate and asymptotic normality are technically sound. The decorrelated score statistic's connection to semiparametric efficiency (Theorem 4.6) is particularly strong.

However, the practical impact is somewhat limited by the need for precise knowledge of model parameters (like s*) to set the truncation parameter. While the authors acknowledge this in the discussion, they don't provide practical alternatives for setting bs. The comparison with existing methods (e.g., tensor methods [1, 9]) is good, but the paper could better articulate how their method improves upon these in both statistical and computational aspects.

The theoretical framework is impressive, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. A more detailed discussion of practical implementation strategies for setting bs
2. Explicit minimax lower bound comparisons for the specific models
3. Computational complexity analysis relative to competing methods
4. A brief discussion of empirical results (even if just simulated data) to complement the theoretical claims

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to high-dimensional latent variable modeling, filling a critical gap in the literature. The technical depth and rigor are excellent, and the results have clear implications for statistical inference in high dimensions. While the practical implementation details need more attention, the theoretical framework is novel, well-justified, and advances the state of the art.

I recommend **acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the implementation concerns (particularly regarding the selection of bs) in the final version. The paper's theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant publication at NIPS, and the framework has the potential to influence future work in high-dimensional inference for latent variable models.

The paper represents a strong contribution to NIPS, with clear novelty, technical depth, and significance for the field of statistical machine learning.
