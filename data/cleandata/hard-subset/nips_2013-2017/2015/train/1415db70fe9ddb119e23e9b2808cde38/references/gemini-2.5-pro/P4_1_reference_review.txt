### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the theoretical guarantees for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings where the number of parameters `d` is much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors identify a critical gap: classical EM theory is primarily for low-dimensional regimes, while high-dimensional models require sparsity-type regularization for which no rigorous guarantees for EM-based estimators exist, particularly concerning both estimation and statistical inference.

The paper makes two primary contributions. First, it proposes a "high-dimensional EM algorithm" that incorporates a hard truncation (T-step) after the standard E- and M-steps at each iteration. This step enforces sparsity on the parameter estimates along the optimization path. The authors prove that, under certain conditions and with a proper initialization, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a (near) minimax-optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d) / n)`, where `s*` is the true sparsity level. This result holds for both exact and approximate (gradient ascent) implementations of the M-step.

Second, the paper develops a procedure for asymptotic inference on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. The authors propose a "decorrelated score test" that builds upon the estimator obtained from their high-dimensional EM algorithm. By constructing a score function and using a Dantzig selector to correct for the influence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, they derive a test statistic that is asymptotically normal. Crucially, they show that the limiting variance of this statistic achieves the semiparametric information bound, rendering the procedure statistically optimal.

The proposed theoretical framework is general and is demonstrated on two key latent variable models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model. This work extends the analytical framework of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) [2] from the low-dimensional to the high-dimensional sparse regime. The key technical novelty lies in handling the hard truncation step, which requires a more refined entrywise error analysis. For inference, it adapts ideas from the high-dimensional linear models literature (e.g., [26, 32]) to the challenging setting of non-convex optimization where the estimator is a local, not global, optimum.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly significant and challenging open problem: establishing a computationally tractable procedure with rigorous theoretical guarantees for both estimation and inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The EM algorithm is a cornerstone of statistical practice for these models, but its behavior in the `d >> n` regime has been poorly understood. This work provides the first unified framework that guarantees both near-optimal statistical rates for estimation and asymptotically valid inference for the output of an EM-type algorithm in this setting. The combination of the EM algorithm with a truncation step and a subsequent decorrelation procedure for inference is novel and impactful.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears to be sound and rigorous. The authors successfully extend the population-to-sample analysis of EM from prior work to the high-dimensional setting. Analyzing the interplay between the EM updates and the non-smooth truncation operator is technically demanding and is handled by carefully controlling the entrywise statistical error (`l_infinity`-norm), a non-trivial departure from the `l_2`-norm analysis in previous low-dimensional work. The derivation of asymptotic normality for an estimator that is not the solution to a convex problem but rather the output of an iterative algorithm is a sophisticated technical achievement.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly articulates the problem, the proposed solution, and the main theoretical results in the introduction. The methodology is presented logically with clear algorithm descriptions. The comparison with related work is comprehensive and effectively situates the paper's contributions within the literature on EM algorithms, high-dimensional estimation, and high-dimensional inference. The theoretical conditions are stated abstractly first and then verified for concrete statistical models, which makes the framework easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Experimental Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are substantial, the absence of any numerical simulations is a significant drawback. Experiments could have been used to: (a) verify that the theoretical convergence rates hold in practice for finite samples; (b) demonstrate the empirical coverage of the proposed confidence intervals and the power of the hypothesis test; and (c) compare the performance of the proposed algorithm against other potential methods for high-dimensional latent variable models. Such validation would greatly strengthen the paper's claims and demonstrate its practical relevance.

2.  **Assumptions on Initialization:** The theoretical guarantees rely on the existence of an initial estimator that is already within a constant-factor relative error of the true parameter. This is a common requirement for provable non-convex optimization, but the paper does not provide or discuss a practical, data-driven procedure for obtaining such an initialization. While the authors critique tensor methods for their suboptimal statistical rates, these methods are often used precisely for initialization. A discussion on potential initialization strategies and their theoretical/practical implications is warranted.

3.  **Practicality of Tuning Parameters:** The proposed algorithm and inference procedure depend on several tuning parameters, including the sparsity level `bs` for truncation and the regularization parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector. The theory provides guidance on the scaling of these parameters (e.g., `bs` must be on the order of `s*`). However, in a real-world application, the true sparsity `s*` is unknown. The paper offers no practical guidance on how to select these parameters from data, for instance, through cross-validation or other data-driven methods. This limits the immediate applicability of the proposed methods for practitioners.

4.  **Analysis of the Resampling Algorithm:** The main theoretical result for estimation (Theorem 3.4) is presented for a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4 in the appendix), where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a standard analytical tool to ensure independence and simplify proofs. However, in practice, one would use the standard algorithm (Algorithm 1) on the full dataset. The paper should be more explicit in the main text about this distinction and briefly discuss the challenges or outlook for extending the proof to the more practical, non-resampling version.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   Provides a novel and complete theoretical framework for estimation and inference using the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional sparse setting, a problem of major importance to the NIPS community.
*   The technical contributions are strong, involving a non-trivial extension of existing theory for non-convex optimization and high-dimensional inference.
*   The results are significant, establishing for the first time a computationally feasible method that achieves near-minimax optimal estimation rates and asymptotically efficient inference for high-dimensional latent variable models.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The complete lack of empirical results makes it difficult to assess the practical performance and finite-sample behavior of the proposed methods.
*   The strong initialization requirement is not accompanied by a practical method to achieve it, limiting the self-contained nature of the proposed procedure.
*   The lack of discussion on data-driven selection of crucial tuning parameters hinders the practical applicability of the work.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to an important area. While the weaknesses related to practical implementation and empirical validation are notable, the novelty and significance of the theoretical results are substantial.
