- Summary: Proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step to enforce sparsity, proving geometric convergence and near-optimal estimation rate O(√(s* log d / n)) and asymptotically normal decorrelated score inference, applied to Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions.

- Strength: First theoretical guarantees for EM in high-dimensional settings, addressing an important gap.

- Strength: Introduces a novel truncation step to enforce sparsity that can generalize to other latent variable models.

- Strength: Provides a unified framework for estimation and inference, including a decorrelated score achieving semiparametric efficiency.

- Strength: Theoretical results are technically rigorous with detailed verification for specific models.

- Weakness: Lacks empirical validation on real or simulated datasets to demonstrate practical performance.

- Weakness: High technical complexity and dense notation may limit accessibility to practitioners.

- Weakness: Some technical assumptions (e.g., sparsity of w*) may be restrictive in practice.

- Weakness: Limited comparison to other high-dimensional approaches such as penalized likelihood methods.

- Quality: Technically sound with careful proofs and justified assumptions.

- Clarity: Well-organized but mathematically dense; technical conditions could use more motivation and examples.

- Originality: Novel high-dimensional EM with sparsity enforcement and original decorrelated score for latent variable models.

- Significance: Addresses a fundamental theoretical gap for a widely used algorithm; likely influential for future research.

- Recommendation: Accept — significant theoretical contribution outweighs minor weaknesses.

- Pro: First framework for EM in high dimensions with sparsity and near-optimal estimation rates.

- Pro: Develops semiparametrically efficient decorrelated score statistic for inference.

- Pro: Detailed verification for Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions strengthens credibility.

- Pro: Unified approach for both computational and statistical guarantees.

- Con: No empirical results provided.

- Con: Some assumptions may limit practical applicability.

- Con: Paper is highly technical and may be challenging for non-specialists.

- Con: Insufficient comparison with alternative high-dimensional methods.
