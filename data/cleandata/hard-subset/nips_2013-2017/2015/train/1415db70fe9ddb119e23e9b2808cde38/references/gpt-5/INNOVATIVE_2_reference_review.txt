Summary
This paper develops a high-dimensional EM framework for latent variable models that augments each EM iteration with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step to enforce sparsity. The authors analyze both exact maximization and gradient-ascent implementations of the M-step and provide a unified theory that yields: (i) geometric convergence of the optimization error and a near-minimax statistical rate O(√(s* log d / n)) (up to logs) for the estimator, and (ii) a decorrelated score test for low-dimensional components that is asymptotically normal and semiparametrically efficient in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The analysis is presented under a set of Lipschitz/concavity-smoothness conditions on the population Q-function and an entrywise statistical error control between sample and population M-steps. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions. A resampling/splitting variant of the algorithm is used for the proofs.

Relation to prior work: This extends the population-to-sample analysis of EM (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) from low-dimensional to high-dimensional regimes by adding a truncation step akin to iterative hard-thresholding. Unlike convex penalized MLE approaches for mixtures (e.g., Städler et al., Khalili & Chen), the proposed estimator is not a global optimum of a convex program, and the inference theory adapts decorrelated/debiased methods (Zhang & Zhang, van de Geer et al.) to latent variable models by working with the Q-function and a Dantzig-type projection. The paper compares favorably to tensor methods, which are known to be suboptimal statistically in many settings.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: A bold and timely attempt to unify high-dimensional estimation and valid inference for latent variable models via EM. The combination of (i) a truncated EM iteration with (ii) semiparametrically efficient inference based on a decorrelated score tailored to the Q-function seems new and of broad potential impact.
- Theory with explicit rates: Clear optimization/statistics error decomposition showing geometric contraction and near-minimax rates; the inference result attains the semiparametric information bound.
- Generality: Covers both exact and gradient M-steps under a unified analysis; conditions are formulated at the Q-function level, facilitating verification across models.
- Technical contributions: Entrywise control of M-step statistical error and careful support-evolution analysis under truncation; handling of high-dimensional nuisance via a Dantzig selector built from Q-function derivatives is elegant.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and initialization: Guarantees require a basin-of-attraction condition and good initialization plus (in examples) strong separation/SNR. This is standard in EM analyses but limits practicality. Discussion of how to obtain such initializations in general latent models would help.
- Choice of sparsity level bs: The theory assumes bs is of the same order as s*. In practice s* is unknown. Guidance for selecting bs (e.g., via information criteria, stability selection, or adaptive thresholding) is needed; sensitivity analyses would strengthen the work.
- Resampling/splitting for analysis: The main theorem is proved for a resampling version (Algorithm 4). It would be valuable to state a theorem for the practical “single-sample” algorithm, or at least bound the gap, since sample splitting across iterations can be impractical.
- Computational burden: Each iteration entails an EM update plus a Dantzig selector to compute w(·) for inference; the latter can be nontrivial in high dimensions. Complexity and scalable solvers should be discussed; comparisons to ℓ1-penalized EM variants would be instructive.
- Empirical validation: The paper (as presented) lacks experiments. Demonstrations on synthetic Gaussian mixtures/mixtures of regressions comparing convergence, estimation error, support recovery, and, crucially, Type I error/power of the score/Wald tests would substantially increase confidence. Robustness to misspecification and mis-tuned bs would also be informative.
- Scope: The worked-out models are two-component Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions. While the framework is general, additional examples (e.g., mixtures with K>2, mixture of GLMs, HMMs) would better establish generality.

Quality
The technical development is careful and largely sound. The conditions are plausible and verified (per appendix) for the two models. The semiparametric efficiency claim is well-justified via the connection between Tn and Fisher information. The reliance on a resampling analysis and strong SNR/initialization is a limitation but consistent with the EM literature.

Clarity
The paper is dense but well-organized. The algorithm is clearly specified. Some suggestions:
- Provide a short “practical guide” box with default choices for bs, step sizes, and the Dantzig tuning parameter, plus remarks on initialization.
- Move key intuition on the decorrelated score (currently in the appendix) into the main text.
- Clarify the computational cost of the Dantzig step and whether it is needed at every iteration or only at the end for inference.

Originality
High. While truncation/hard-thresholding and decorrelated inference are known ideas individually, their integration into a high-dimensional EM framework for latent variable models with rigorous optimization/statistical guarantees and semiparametric efficiency appears new and impactful.

Significance
High. Latent variable models are ubiquitous, and providing both near-optimal estimation and valid inference in high dimensions fills a real gap. The ideas should inspire follow-up work on broader classes of latent models and better initialization schemes.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel conceptual contribution unifying high-dim EM with valid inference.
- Near-minimax rates and semiparametric efficiency.
- Addresses an important gap between computation and statistics for latent models.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical support is missing/limited.
- Strong assumptions (initialization, SNR) and tuning knowledge (bs).
- Proofs rely on resampling; practical algorithm guarantees are indirect.

Questions for authors
1) How should bs be chosen in practice, and how sensitive is performance to mis-specification? Can one adaptively update bs?
2) Can the resampling analysis be removed or relaxed (e.g., via leave-one-batch-out arguments) to obtain guarantees for the standard algorithm?
3) What initialization strategies do you recommend beyond the two models studied?
4) Is the Dantzig step required only for inference at the end, or during iterations? What is its computational cost in d ~ 10^4–10^5?

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some practical and assumption-related limitations, the paper introduces a significant new direction for high-dimensional EM with a compelling inference component. The novelty and potential impact justify acceptance; adding experiments would further strengthen it.
