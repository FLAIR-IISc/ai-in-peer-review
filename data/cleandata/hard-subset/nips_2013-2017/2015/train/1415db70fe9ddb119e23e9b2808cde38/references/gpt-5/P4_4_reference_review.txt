Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies statistical optimization and inference for latent-variable models in the high-dimensional regime (d >> n). It proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm that augments the classical E- and M-steps with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step to enforce sparsity along the solution path. Two M-step implementations are covered: exact maximization and a single gradient-ascent step. Under suitable initialization and model-specific smoothness/Lipschitz conditions on the population Q-function, the authors establish nonasymptotic bounds for the iterates: the estimation error decomposes into a geometrically decaying optimization term plus a statistical term of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to logarithmic factors), which is near-minimax optimal for s*-sparse parameters.

For inference on low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters, the paper develops a “decorrelated score” test. The test constructs a Dantzig-style estimator of the nuisance projection vector w that solves a block Fisher-information equation, then forms a scalar score S(·) that is asymptotically normal under the null and attains the semiparametric information bound. The theory is presented in a unified framework and specialized to two examples: spherical Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, where the needed population conditions are verified under separation/SNR assumptions.

The work is positioned relative to: (i) low-dimensional EM analyses (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. [2]) that establish geometric convergence and O(√(d/n)) rates; (ii) tensor methods for mixtures (e.g., [1,9]) with typically suboptimal statistical rates; and (iii) high-dimensional inference via debiasing/desparsification in GLMs (e.g., [13,26,32]), which require convexity and do not cover latent-variable likelihoods. The paper argues it is the first to provide both optimal high-dimensional estimation and asymptotically valid inference for EM-type estimators in latent-variable models.

Strengths
- Quality/technical soundness:
  - Clear decomposition of optimization and statistical errors with explicit rates; geometric contraction under well-stated population conditions (Lipschitz-Gradient and Concavity/Smoothness).
  - Entrywise statistical control in ℓ1 between sample and population M-steps enables sharp √(log d / n) concentration and careful handling of the truncation step.
  - Inference procedure leverages an explicit connection between derivatives of Q and the log-likelihood score/Fisher information (Theorem 2.1), leading to an asymptotically normal, semiparametrically efficient score.
  - Covers both exact and gradient M-steps; provides conditions and step-size for the latter.
- Originality:
  - Introduces a truncated/high-dimensional EM with theoretical guarantees; prior EM analyses focus on low-dimensional settings.
  - Extends decorrelated-score inference to latent-variable models where the estimator need not be a global optimum of a convex program.
- Significance:
  - Addresses a central gap between computational EM heuristics and high-dimensional statistical guarantees.
  - Near-optimal estimation rates and valid inference have broad potential impact across latent-variable modeling.
- Clarity:
  - Algorithms are clearly stated. Assumptions/conditions are delineated and tied to model examples. The roles of initialization, truncation, and concentration are explicit.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Assumptions and practicality:
  - Initialization: Guarantees require starting within a basin of attraction with relative error bounded by a constant. Practical strategies to obtain such initializations (e.g., spectral/tensor initializers, sample splitting with screening) should be detailed and evaluated.
  - Truncation level: The sparsity parameter b_s must be of the same order as s*. Guidance on choosing b_s in practice (e.g., cross-validation, information criteria, adaptive thresholding) and robustness to mis-specification is needed.
  - SNR/separation and strong concavity: For Gaussian mixtures, the analysis assumes sufficiently large signal-to-noise ratio; the scope and empirical tightness of these conditions should be discussed.
  - Sparsity of w*: The decorrelated score requires w* (solution of a block information equation) to be sparse. This is not guaranteed generically and may limit applicability; discussion of when this holds in common latent models (beyond the given examples), and what to do otherwise, would help.
- Analysis vs. implementation:
  - The main convergence theorem analyzes a resampling (sample-splitting) variant to simplify dependence. A discussion or result that transfers guarantees to the standard single-sample EM (or a practical multi-split scheme with aggregation) would strengthen the contribution.
  - Monotonicity: The truncation step can break the EM monotonic increase in Q. Clarifying whether any surrogate monotonicity or descent property holds, or how this impacts stopping criteria, would aid practitioners.
  - Tuning of the Dantzig constraint parameter for w (lambda) depends on concentration terms and unknown constants. Provide data-driven selection strategies (e.g., theoretically motivated plug-ins or multiplier bootstrap) and empirical sensitivity.
- Scope and validation:
  - Theoretical verification is provided for two models under specific settings (spherical covariance for GMM). It would be valuable to discuss extensions (e.g., non-spherical covariances, more general latent structures) and what modifications are needed.
  - Empirical evaluation (not shown here) is essential: compare to penalized EM baselines and tensor methods on synthetic and real datasets; study sensitivity to b_s, initialization, and step-size; report coverage and power for the proposed tests; and assess computational cost.

Detailed comments by criterion
- Quality: The proofs and conditions are credible and align with recent EM and high-dimensional inference theory. The decomposition into optimization and statistical error is standard and well-executed. The reliance on sample splitting and several model-specific constants could be further operationalized.
- Clarity: Algorithmic statements are clear, but the notation is heavy and many assumptions are deferred to the appendix. Adding an assumptions-to-conclusions roadmap and practical guidance on hyperparameters would improve accessibility. Minor typos in equations likely stem from formatting.
- Originality: The integration of truncation within EM with corresponding nonasymptotic analysis, plus a decorrelated score tailored to latent-variable models, appears novel beyond the low-dimensional EM literature and convex high-dimensional inference.
- Significance: The results advance the state of the art for high-dimensional latent-variable estimation and inference and could influence both theory and practice, provided practical guidance and empirical validation are strengthened.

Arguments for acceptance
- Provides the first general framework with near-optimal rates and valid inference for high-dimensional latent-variable EM estimators.
- Technically solid with clear advances over low-dimensional EM analyses and over convex debiasing methods that do not apply here.
- Semiparametric efficiency of the score is a strong and clean result.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical aspects (initialization, tuning of b_s and Dantzig parameters, step-size) are underspecified; robustness not demonstrated.
- Analysis relies on sample splitting; guarantees for the standard implementation are not explicit.
- Applicability may be limited by sparsity of w* and SNR/concavity assumptions; breadth of verified models is modest.
- The empirical component (not visible here) is critical; if weak or absent, this detracts from NeurIPS standards.

Suggestions/questions for the authors
- Provide concrete, data-driven procedures for initialization, b_s, and lambda in the Dantzig step, with sensitivity analyses.
- Clarify how the theory extends to single-sample EM (without resampling), or propose a practical multi-split algorithm with aggregation and show comparable performance.
- Discuss when w* is sparse in common latent models; if not, can group-sparsity or ridge-regularized variants yield valid inference?
- Report experiments evaluating estimation error, support recovery, coverage/power, and runtime, comparing to penalized EM and tensor methods.
- Comment on non-spherical Gaussian mixtures and other latent-variable families, and the changes needed in conditions/proofs.
- Provide guidance on stopping criteria and any surrogate monotonicity after truncation.
