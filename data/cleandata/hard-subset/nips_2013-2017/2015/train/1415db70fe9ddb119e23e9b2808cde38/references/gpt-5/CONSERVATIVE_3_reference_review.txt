Summary
The paper studies EM for latent-variable models in the high-dimensional regime (d >> n). It proposes a simple modification of EM that appends a hard-thresholding “T-step” after each M-step to enforce sparsity. Under local assumptions (basin-of-attraction, SNR/separation, suitable initialization close to the truth) and either exact M-step or one-step gradient M-step, the authors derive geometric contraction plus an l1-based statistical error, yielding an estimation rate on the order of sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to logs). For inference on a low-dimensional component, the paper constructs a “decorrelated score” based on the Q-function’s derivatives and a Dantzig-selector estimate of a nuisance projection, and proves asymptotic normality with variance equal to the semiparametric efficiency bound. Two models (Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions) are used to instantiate the assumptions. The analysis mainly follows the “from population to sample” EM framework of Balakrishnan et al., with new elements to handle hard-thresholding and entrywise errors.

Relation to prior work
- The computational analysis extends the low-dimensional EM theory in Balakrishnan et al. to a high-dimensional setting via hard thresholding. The truncation step resembles iterative hard-thresholding (IHT) literature for sparse M-estimation, which should be cited and discussed.
- For inference, the construction is closely related to de-biasing/decorrelation methods (Zhang & Zhang 2014; Javanmard & Montanari 2014; van de Geer et al. 2014; Ning & Liu 2017), now applied in latent-variable models using the EM identity ∇θ Q(θ; θ) = (1/n)∇θ ℓn(θ).
- Sparse/penalized approaches for mixtures (e.g., Städler et al. 2010, Khalili & Chen 2007) are cited; a more careful comparison to these nonconvex penalized likelihood methods would help.

Strengths
- Clear, modular analytical framework that decomposes optimization and statistical errors and treats both exact and gradient M-step implementations.
- The l1-type statistical error control and handling of support evolution under hard-thresholding are technically nontrivial and of interest.
- The inference construction connects EM quantities to score/Fisher information and establishes asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency under high-dimensional nuisance.
- Useful to see the implications worked out for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strong assumptions limit practical relevance:
  - Requires a good initialization with constant relative error, large SNR/separation, and knowledge of s* up to a constant (bs must scale with s*). No data-driven scheme for choosing bs is provided or analyzed.
  - The sparsity of w* (the “decorrelation” vector derived from the Fisher information) is assumed small; for mixtures the information matrix is typically dense, and it is unclear when s_w* is realistically o(√(n/log d)).
  - The “resampling” (fresh-sample) variant is used for the main analysis; guarantees for the single-sample algorithm are not established. This is a significant gap for practice.
- Limited novelty in the inference part: the key identity (Theorem 2.1) is standard in EM theory; the decorrelated score and Dantzig approach follow established de-biasing frameworks. The main contribution is verifying conditions, but the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the additional sparsity assumptions (on w*) hold in the presented models.
- No empirical validation. Given the sensitivity to initialization, tuning bs, and step sizes, and the computational overhead of solving a Dantzig program using second derivatives of Q, simulations are essential to assess robustness, finite-sample performance, coverage, and comparisons to established baselines (e.g., L1-penalized EM/likelihood and de-biased procedures built on those estimators).
- Clarity/organization: The manuscript is difficult to parse in places, with numerous typographical/formatting artifacts (e.g., symbols mangled, missing years and authors in references), and some key algorithms relegated to appendices. Important conditions (e.g., Condition 3.3) are strong and deserve more intuition in the main text.
- Claims of “near-optimal” rates have extra log factors and rely on model-specific constants; explicit statements of minimax lower bounds for the considered models would strengthen these claims.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Can the authors provide guarantees for the single-sample algorithm (no resampling) or at least show how to convert the resampling analysis via sample-splitting or leave-one-batch-out arguments?
- How sensitive is the algorithm to misspecifying bs? Can one use an adaptive threshold or information criterion and still retain guarantees?
- For Gaussian mixtures/mixture regressions, can you characterize when w* is sparse? Empirical evidence on the effective sparsity of w* would be helpful.
- What is the computational complexity per iteration, especially for computing Tn(·) and solving the Dantzig program? How does it scale in d and n?
- How are step sizes chosen for the gradient M-step in practice? Any backtracking or line search?

Missing or underemphasized related work
- Iterative Hard Thresholding (Blumensath & Davies, 2009) and hard-thresholded gradient methods for sparse M-estimation (e.g., Loh & Wainwright, 2015) should be discussed.
- De-biased inference beyond linear/GLMs: Javanmard & Montanari (2014), Ning & Liu (2017, “A General Theory of Hypothesis Testing under Sparsity”) are relevant to the inference framework.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and careful theoretical development of a simple, implementable modification of EM in high dimensions.
- Bridges computation and inference in latent-variable models, a topic of broad interest at NeurIPS.
- Technical tools for handling truncation and entrywise errors may be reusable.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical relevance undermined by strong assumptions (initialization, SNR, sparsity of w*), resampling analysis, and lack of experiments.
- Incremental novelty: inference component closely follows known de-biasing frameworks; the EM identity used is standard.
- Clarity and polish issues, including incomplete/broken references.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The theoretical ideas are interesting, but the paper lacks empirical validation, relies on restrictive assumptions (especially resampling and sparsity of w*), and the inference contribution is largely an adaptation of established de-biasing methods. I encourage the authors to (i) provide single-sample guarantees or clear guidance showing resampling is not needed, (ii) add comprehensive experiments with baselines and robustness analyses, (iii) clarify when the sparsity assumptions on w* hold, and (iv) improve exposition and related work coverage. With these additions, the work could be much stronger.
