### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a theoretical framework for parameter estimation and statistical inference for latent variable models in the high-dimensional setting where the dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors introduce a novel "High Dimensional EM Algorithm," which augments the classical Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with a truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. The main contributions are twofold. First, for parameter estimation, the paper establishes that under certain conditions, including a sufficiently accurate initialization, the proposed algorithm converges at a geometric rate to a solution that achieves a (near) minimax-optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d) / n)`, where `s*` is the true sparsity level. This guarantee is provided for M-steps implemented via either exact maximization or a gradient ascent step. Second, for statistical inference, the paper develops a "decorrelated score test" for testing hypotheses on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. It is shown that the proposed test statistic is asymptotically normal and that its limiting variance achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. The general framework is illustrated with applications to high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

This work significantly extends the line of research that provides theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm, most notably the work of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) [2], which established similar computational and statistical guarantees but was confined to the low-dimensional setting (`d << n`). The key novelty in moving to the high-dimensional regime is the introduction and analysis of the iterative truncation step. Furthermore, the work on inference builds upon the high-dimensional inference literature (e.g., Zhang & Zhang (2014) [32], van de Geer et al. (2014) [26]) but makes a novel contribution by developing a valid inferential procedure for an estimator that is a local optimum of a non-convex likelihood function, whereas prior work has largely focused on global optima of convex programs like the Lasso.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are substantial and are supported by a rigorous analytical framework. The authors carefully lay out the technical conditions required for their results to hold (e.g., Conditions 3.1-3.3) and provide a clear roadmap of the proofs. The analysis correctly identifies and separates the optimization error, which decreases geometrically, from the statistical error, which dictates the final estimation accuracy. The derivation of the asymptotic normality for the decorrelated score statistic is a technically challenging and complete piece of work. The application of the general theory to specific models like the Gaussian mixture model demonstrates the framework's utility.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the problem, a clear summary of the paper's contributions, and a thorough comparison with related work, effectively situating the paper in the current literature. The methodology is presented logically, with algorithms clearly stated. The separation of the general theory into sections on estimation (Section 3) and inference (Section 4) makes the paper easy to follow. While the technical content is dense and the notation is necessarily heavy, the authors have made a commendable effort to define terms and explain the intuition behind their approach, such as the connection between their `Tn` quantity and the Fisher information matrix (Theorem 2.1).

**Originality:** The work is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first paper to provide a general framework with theoretical guarantees for both optimal parameter estimation and valid asymptotic inference for the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional sparse setting. Key novel elements include:
1.  The design of the high-dimensional EM algorithm with an integrated truncation step.
2.  The corresponding theoretical analysis, which must handle the evolving support of the iterates and requires a more fine-grained, entrywise (`l-infinity`) characterization of statistical error compared to the `l2`-norm analysis used in prior low-dimensional work.
3.  The construction of a valid inferential procedure for a local optimum of a non-convex objective, which is a significant departure from existing high-dimensional inference methods that rely on convexity.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. The EM algorithm is one of the most fundamental and widely used algorithms in statistics and machine learning, and its application to high-dimensional problems is common in practice (e.g., in computational biology, finance). This paper provides the first rigorous theoretical foundation for its use in this setting, bridging a crucial gap between theory and practice. The development of a computationally feasible method for uncertainty quantification (hypothesis tests, confidence intervals) in high-dimensional latent variable models is a major breakthrough and is likely to be highly influential. Researchers and practitioners working with models like mixture models, factor analysis, or mixed-effects models in high dimensions will likely use and build upon these ideas.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Initialization Requirement:** The theoretical guarantees, both for estimation and inference, are local and depend on starting the algorithm with an initialization `init` that is already in a basin of attraction around the true parameter `theta*`. The paper does not discuss methods for obtaining such an initialization (e.g., via spectral methods), which is a crucial and often challenging step for applying non-convex procedures in practice. While this is a common feature of theoretical analyses in this area, acknowledging this practical hurdle more explicitly would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Tuning Parameter Selection:** The algorithm requires a sparsity parameter `bs` to be specified. The theoretical conditions (e.g., Eq. 3.8) provide a choice that depends on the true, unknown sparsity `s*`. The paper does not address how `bs` or the tuning parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector would be chosen in a data-driven way (e.g., via cross-validation). A brief discussion of this practical aspect would be beneficial.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper addresses a long-standing and important problem by providing the first comprehensive theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional setting.
*   **Novelty and Impact:** It introduces a novel algorithm and a highly original analysis. The results on asymptotic inference for a local optimum of a non-convex problem are particularly significant and likely to inspire future work.
*   **Technical Strength:** The analysis is rigorous, comprehensive, and technically sound.
*   **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The reliance on a strong initialization assumption without providing a method to achieve it may limit the immediate practical utility of the theoretical guarantees. This is a common but notable limitation.

In summary, the strengths of this paper—its novelty, significance, and technical quality—far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a major theoretical advance for a fundamental algorithm in machine learning and statistics.
