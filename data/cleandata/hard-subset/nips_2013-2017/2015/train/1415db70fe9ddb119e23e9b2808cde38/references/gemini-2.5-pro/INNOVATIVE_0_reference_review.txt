**Summary**

This paper addresses the significant gap between the practical use of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and its theoretical understanding in the high-dimensional (d >> n) setting. While EM is a cornerstone for fitting latent variable models, its guarantees have been largely confined to the low-dimensional regime. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm," which cleverly integrates a hard-thresholding (truncation) step into the standard EM iterations to enforce sparsity. They prove that this algorithm, given a suitable initialization, converges at a geometric rate to a solution that achieves the near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of sqrt(s*log(d)/n). Second, and perhaps more impressively, they develop a "Decorrelated Score Test" based on the algorithm's output. This provides a valid method for hypothesis testing and uncertainty quantification for individual parameters, a notoriously difficult problem for non-convex optimization in high dimensions. The authors establish the asymptotic normality of their test statistic, showing it achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. The framework is presented generally and then instantiated for Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models. This work substantially extends the recent analysis of low-dimensional EM (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) to the challenging high-dimensional sparse setting.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and ambition. It tackles a fundamental, long-standing problem with a conceptually bold approach.

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The lack of a rigorous framework for estimation *and* inference for the widely-used EM algorithm in high dimensions has been a major theoretical void. This paper provides the first comprehensive framework to fill it. The most novel aspect is the development of an inferential theory (the decorrelated score test) for the *output of an iterative algorithm that finds a local optimum* of a non-convex likelihood. This is a significant departure from the bulk of the high-dimensional inference literature, which has almost exclusively focused on global optima of convex problems (e.g., the Lasso). This new direction is highly significant and likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research.

2.  **Elegant Algorithmic and Theoretical Synthesis:** The proposed modification to the EM algorithm—adding a simple, intuitive truncation step—is elegant. The real achievement, however, is the supporting theory. The analysis successfully characterizes the complex interplay between the expectation, maximization, and truncation steps, proving geometric convergence to a statistically optimal estimator. This requires a more refined, entrywise (`l-infinity`) analysis of the statistical error, which is substantially more challenging than the `l-2` analysis sufficient for the low-dimensional case.

3.  **A Complete Theoretical Result:** The paper presents a remarkably complete theoretical narrative. It moves seamlessly from computational guarantees (geometric convergence rate) to statistical guarantees (near-minimax estimation error) and finally to inferential guarantees (asymptotic normality and optimality). Providing this full spectrum of results for such a challenging problem setting is a major accomplishment. The proof that the resulting test statistic is semiparametrically efficient is the capstone on a very strong theoretical contribution.

**Weaknesses**

While the contributions are substantial, there are a few points that could be viewed as weaknesses, though they are common in work of this nature and do not undermine the core results.

1.  **Dependence on Strong/Complex Assumptions:** The analysis rests on a series of technical conditions that, while verified for the paper's examples, may be difficult to check for other models. More critically, the theory relies on a good initialization (i.e., the algorithm must start in a basin of attraction around the true parameter). This is a standard requirement for guarantees in non-convex optimization, but it remains a practical limitation, as finding such an initialization can be a challenge in itself (e.g., requiring methods like spectral initialization).

2.  **Analysis of a "Resampling" Algorithm:** The proofs are formally for a version of the algorithm that uses a fresh, independent sample of data at each iteration. This is a common theoretical tool used to simplify the analysis of stochastic iterative algorithms. However, in practice, one would almost always iterate on the full dataset. The paper does not discuss the potential gap between the theory for the resampled algorithm and the performance of the practical algorithm. While the results are likely to hold, this is a minor theoretical looseness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and important open problem at the intersection of optimization, statistics, and machine learning.
*   Presents a highly novel and bold approach by developing an inferential framework for a local optimum of a non-convex problem.
*   The algorithmic modification is simple and practical, while the accompanying theory is deep and comprehensive.
*   The results are of high significance and establish a new direction for analyzing a wide class of practical algorithms.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theory requires a good initialization, which is a common but non-trivial prerequisite.
*   The analysis is technically demanding and relies on a theoretical resampling scheme which differs from the practical implementation.

**Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a foundational contribution. Its conceptual novelty in extending high-dimensional inference to the local optima of the EM algorithm is a major breakthrough. While the analysis relies on strong assumptions and a standard "good initialization" clause, these are acceptable trade-offs for the significance and ambition of the results. The paper is well-written, the theory is rigorous, and the potential impact is high. It pushes the boundary of our understanding of a canonical machine learning algorithm. I strongly recommend acceptance.
