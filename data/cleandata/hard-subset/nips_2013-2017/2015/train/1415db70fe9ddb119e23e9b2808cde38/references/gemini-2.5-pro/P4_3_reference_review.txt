### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the theoretical analysis of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in the high-dimensional setting where the dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors identify a critical gap: while the EM algorithm is a workhorse for latent variable models, its theoretical guarantees on convergence and statistical properties have been limited to the low-dimensional regime. To address this, the paper makes two primary contributions. First, it proposes a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" that modifies the standard EM procedure by adding a truncation step (T-step) after each maximization step (M-step). This T-step enforces sparsity on the parameter estimates at each iteration. The authors prove that, given a suitable initialization, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s*log(d)/n)`, where `s*` is the true sparsity level. Second, the paper develops a procedure for asymptotic inference. It proposes a "decorrelated score test" for hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter. The authors prove that this test statistic is asymptotically normal and its limiting variance achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound.

This work builds upon the analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) [2], extending their low-dimensional framework for EM as a perturbed gradient method to the high-dimensional, sparse setting. The inference part adapts techniques from the high-dimensional linear model literature (e.g., [26, 32]) to the more complex, non-convex optimization landscape of latent variable models where the estimator is a local, not global, optimum. The general framework is instantiated and verified for two key models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper provides what appears to be the first comprehensive theoretical framework for both estimation and inference for the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional (d >> n) regime. This is a highly significant and challenging problem, as the EM algorithm is ubiquitously used in practice, but its behavior in modern high-dimensional settings has been poorly understood. Bridging the gap between the computational procedure and statistical guarantees (both for point estimation and uncertainty quantification) is a major contribution that substantially advances the field.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results are strong and appear to be technically sound. The paper establishes a geometric rate of convergence for the algorithm and a near-optimal statistical rate for the resulting estimator. The analysis required to handle the addition of the truncation step is non-trivial, demanding a more fine-grained, entrywise (`l_infinity`-norm) control of the statistical error, which is a key technical innovation over previous `l_2`-norm analyses. Furthermore, establishing asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency for an estimator obtained via an iterative, non-convex procedure is a formidable technical achievement.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The introduction provides an excellent motivation for the problem and a clear summary of the paper's contributions and how they relate to the existing literature. The main results are stated precisely, and the general framework is presented before being applied to specific examples, which aids in understanding the scope and mechanics of the proposed method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Analysis Based on a Resampling Algorithm:** A key weakness is that the main theoretical analysis is conducted for a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4, described in the appendix). In this version, a fresh, independent sample of data is used at each iteration of the algorithm. This is a significant deviation from the standard EM algorithm (Algorithm 1), which uses the full dataset in every iteration. The main text only briefly mentions this at the start of Section 3.1. This raises questions about the direct applicability of the proven theoretical rates to the algorithm that would be used in practice. The authors should provide a more prominent discussion in the main paper about why this resampling is necessary for their proof and what challenges prevent the analysis of the standard algorithm.

2.  **Initialization Requirement:** The theory hinges on the assumption of a "suitable initialization" that lies within a basin of attraction of the true parameter. While this is a standard assumption in the theoretical analysis of non-convex optimization algorithms (including [2]), the practical problem of how to obtain such an initial estimate in a high-dimensional latent variable model is notoriously difficult and is not addressed by the paper. This limits the work's contribution as a fully self-contained, practical procedure. A discussion of potential initialization strategies or the challenges involved would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Complexity of Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on a set of high-level technical conditions (e.g., Condition 3.3, Assumption 4.5). While these conditions are subsequently verified for the paper's examples, their abstract formulation can make it difficult to assess the theory's applicability to other models. The assumption on the sparsity of `w*` in the inference section, for example, is critical but may not be immediately intuitive. A more detailed discussion of the interpretation and plausibility of these assumptions would be beneficial.

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents a strong, novel, and significant contribution to the theory of high-dimensional statistics and machine learning. It provides the first rigorous framework for analyzing the estimation and inference properties of the EM algorithm in a high-dimensional, sparse setting. The technical results are impressive and address a long-standing open problem. Despite the weaknesses concerning the reliance on a resampling-based algorithm for the proofs and the unaddressed problem of initialization, the paper's strengths are compelling. The work opens up important new avenues for research into a fundamental algorithm of machine learning. The contributions are of high quality and are very well-suited for NIPS.
