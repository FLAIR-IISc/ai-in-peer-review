This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, a significant gap in the literature. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and establish impressive results regarding convergence rates and asymptotic normality of the estimator and associated score test. The work is highly ambitious and represents a substantial contribution if the claims hold. The novelty lies in extending the analysis to the high-dimensional regime and providing theoretical justification for a practical, commonly used algorithm. The focus on both estimation *and* inference is also commendable.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Addressing theoretical guarantees for EM in high dimensions is a genuinely novel and important problem. The truncation step is a clever idea to enforce sparsity and enable analysis.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a comprehensive theoretical framework, establishing convergence rates and asymptotic normality under reasonable conditions. The rates achieved are near-optimal.
* **Generality:** The framework appears applicable to a range of latent variable models (GMM, mixture of regression), demonstrated through application examples.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The EM algorithm is widely used; providing theoretical backing in high dimensions is highly valuable to practitioners.
* **Inference:** The development of a decorrelated score test and establishing its asymptotic normality are significant additions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** The paper is *very* dense and mathematically challenging. While rigor is appreciated, accessibility could be improved. The large number of conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) feels overwhelming and makes it difficult to grasp the core assumptions quickly.
* **Condition Verification:** While the authors state they will verify conditions for specific models in the appendix, the provided excerpt doesn’t include those detailed proofs. Assessing the plausibility of these conditions is crucial, and their feasibility for real-world applications isn’t immediately apparent. The conditions feel quite strong, particularly those related to sparsity of the Fisher information.
* **Constant Factors:** The constants hidden within the “O” notation, and the specific dependence of rates on factors like `s*`, `d`, and `n`, are not fully elucidated. This makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of the theoretical results.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks empirical validation. While theory is the primary focus, some simulations demonstrating the performance of the algorithm and the score test would significantly strengthen the contribution. Demonstrating the gains (if any) over existing methods in practical settings would be very persuasive.
* **Assumptions on w*:** The requirement that `w*` (from the decorrelated score test) be sparse is a strong assumption that warrants further discussion. While the authors briefly touch on this, its implications and potential limitations should be more thoroughly addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in statistical machine learning.
* The theoretical results are impressive and, if correct, represent a substantial advance.
* The proposed algorithm and inference procedure have the potential to impact a wide range of applications.
* The paper demonstrates a deep understanding of the EM algorithm and high-dimensional statistics.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The extreme technical density and numerous conditions make the paper difficult to assess.
* The lack of empirical validation raises concerns about the practical relevance of the results.
* The strong assumptions (e.g., sparsity of `w*`) may limit the applicability of the framework.
* Without detailed condition verification in the appendix, the theoretical guarantees remain somewhat abstract.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses, I lean towards **acceptance** with a strong recommendation for the authors to address the concerns raised above, *particularly* providing detailed verification of the conditions in the appendix and including some empirical validation. The potential impact of this work is significant, and the bold approach to tackling a long-standing problem is commendable.  The high dimensionality and theoretical focus align well with the NIPS audience. While methodological perfection is not expected, the sheer ambition and potential of this work outweigh its current limitations.  I believe the program committee would benefit from a discussion of this paper even if further refinement is needed. It introduces a significant new direction for the analysis of EM algorithms.
