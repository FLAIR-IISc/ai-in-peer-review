### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for parameter estimation and statistical inference for high-dimensional latent variable models using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The core challenge addressed is the lack of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in the high-dimensional setting (d >> n), where standard maximum likelihood is ill-posed and sparsity is required. The authors make two main contributions. First, they propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the classic EM by adding a truncation step after the M-step to enforce sparsity throughout the optimization path. They prove that, with a proper initialization, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator that achieves a (near-)minimax optimal statistical error rate of √(s* log(d)/n). Second, they develop a procedure for statistical inference based on the algorithm's output. They construct a "decorrelated score statistic" for testing hypotheses on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector and prove that it is asymptotically normal. Crucially, its limiting variance is shown to be optimal, achieving the semiparametric information bound. The general framework is demonstrated on two key examples: Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

This work significantly extends the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm, building upon prior work like Balakrishnan et al. (2017) [2] which provided guarantees in the low-dimensional setting. It also makes a novel contribution to the high-dimensional inference literature (e.g., debiased Lasso), which has primarily focused on estimators from convex optimization problems, by providing a valid inferential procedure for a local optimum found by a non-convex iterative algorithm.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a fundamental and long-standing problem: providing theoretical guarantees for the most popular algorithm for latent variable models (EM) in the modern high-dimensional regime. The ability to perform not just point estimation but also valid statistical inference (hypothesis tests, confidence intervals) in this challenging setting is a major advance. These results could have a broad impact on many fields where sparse latent variable models are used.

2.  **Strong Novelty and Originality:** The proposed method and analysis are highly original. The introduction of the truncation step within the EM iterations is a simple but powerful idea to handle high dimensionality. The development of an inferential framework for the *output of an iterative algorithm* that solves a non-convex problem is a key novelty. Unlike most high-dimensional inference methods that rely on the KKT conditions of a global optimum of a convex program, this work provides guarantees for a computationally tractable local solution, bridging a critical gap between computational algorithms and statistical theory.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous and comprehensive theoretical analysis. It establishes simultaneous computational (geometric convergence) and statistical (near-optimal rate) guarantees under a unified framework. The proof strategy, which carefully tracks the evolution of the support and uses fine-grained entrywise error bounds to handle the truncation step, appears sound and technically deep. The result showing that the proposed score test is semiparametrically efficient is a strong theoretical statement of optimality.

4.  **Generality:** The framework is developed in a general form under abstract conditions, which are then verified for specific but important models like Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression. This suggests the approach is potentially applicable to a wide range of high-dimensional latent variable models.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is technically very dense, which can make it difficult to follow. The reliance on a series of abstract conditions (3.1, 3.2, 3.3, 4.1-4.4, and Assumption 4.5) makes it challenging to grasp the core intuition. I would suggest the authors add more discussion to intuitively explain what these conditions mean in the context of latent variable models. For example, how does the model's signal-to-noise ratio or the separation between mixture components affect constants like µ, ν, and ρ? This would make the results more accessible.

2.  **The Initialization Assumption:** The theory relies on an initialization that is already within a constant relative error of the true parameter. This is a common assumption for non-convex optimization theory but remains a significant practical hurdle. The paper does not provide guidance on how to find such an initialization. A short discussion of potential strategies (e.g., spectral methods) and relevant citations would significantly strengthen the paper's practical relevance, even if a full analysis is beyond scope.

3.  **Practicality of Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm and the inferential procedure depend on tuning parameters, namely the sparsity level `bs` and the Dantzig selector's `λ`. The theoretical choices given in (3.8) and (4.5) depend on unknown quantities like the true sparsity `s*` and other population-level constants. The paper lacks a discussion on how to select these parameters in practice, for instance, using cross-validation. This is a crucial step for real-world application.

4.  **Analysis of the Resampling Algorithm:** The main estimation theorem is proven for a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4), where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a standard proof technique to ensure independence and simplify analysis. However, in practice, one would use the full dataset at each step. The paper should be more explicit about this distinction in the main body and briefly comment on the challenges of extending the proof to the more standard, non-resampling version of the algorithm.

### Detailed Questions for Authors

*   In the "Implications for Gaussian Mixture Model," the final statistical rate is stated as being near-optimal. However, the text mentions a rate of O(√(s* log(d) log(n)/n)), which contains an extra log(n) factor compared to the standard minimax rate. Could you clarify the source of this additional factor?
*   The inference theory relies on the sparsity of `w*`, the vector representing the influence of nuisance parameters. Can you provide some intuition on when this assumption (`s*w` is small) is likely to hold or fail in latent variable models?
*   While the theoretical contributions are substantial, the paper would be even more convincing with a small simulation study. Demonstrating the empirical convergence rate and the actual coverage of the confidence intervals derived from your score test would provide a valuable sanity check and showcase the theory's connection to practice.

### Conclusion

This is a very strong theoretical paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the intersection of high-dimensional statistics, optimization, and machine learning. It provides the first comprehensive theoretical framework for both optimal estimation and valid inference for the EM algorithm in the challenging high-dimensional setting. Despite some weaknesses related to clarity and practical implementation details (initialization, tuning), the strengths in significance, novelty, and technical quality are overwhelming. This work successfully bridges a major gap between algorithmic practice and statistical theory for one of the most fundamental algorithms in machine learning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly important and challenging problem.
*   Presents novel and original ideas for both estimation (EM with truncation) and inference (decorrelated score for a local optimum).
*   Provides strong, rigorous, and comprehensive theoretical results (geometric convergence, near-minimax rates, asymptotic normality, and semiparametric efficiency).
*   The framework is general and applicable to a broad class of models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The paper is very dense and hard to parse due to abstract conditions.
*   Practical aspects like obtaining a good initialization and choosing tuning parameters are not addressed.
*   The analysis is for a resampling-based algorithm, which is not the standard implementation.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance are much stronger. I recommend acceptance of this paper.
