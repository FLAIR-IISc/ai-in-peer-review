Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after each M-step to enforce sparsity, together with an inferential procedure based on a decorrelated score. The main computational-statistical result (Theorem 3.4) shows that, under suitable initialization and regularity, the iterates converge at a geometric rate to a sparse estimator achieving a (near-)minimax statistical error of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to a log n factor), where s* is the sparsity of the true parameter. The analysis covers both exact maximization and a gradient-ascent approximation for the M-step. For inference, the paper proposes a Dantzig-selector-based decorrelated score constructed from derivatives of the Q-function; by leveraging identities connecting rQ and the log-likelihood score in latent variable models, the test statistic is shown to be asymptotically normal and to attain the semiparametric efficiency bound for the low-dimensional target (Theorem 4.6). The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

This work extends the recent population-to-sample EM theory in the low-dimensional regime (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2017) by addressing the high-dimensional case with sparsity. It differs from penalized EM or l1-EM approaches (e.g., Städler et al., 2010) by using hard truncation rather than convex penalties and by analyzing the dynamics of support along the EM trajectory. For inference, it builds on/departs from debiasing/desparsification (Zhang & Zhang, 2014; van de Geer et al., 2014; Javanmard & Montanari, 2014) by adapting the decorrelated score to latent variable models through Q-function derivatives. It also contrasts with tensor methods for latent variables (Anandkumar et al.), which typically yield suboptimal rates and are primarily low-dimensional.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: A simple, generic truncation step integrated into EM that yields sparse iterates and near-optimal rates in high dimension, with analysis covering both exact and gradient M-steps.
- Unified computational-statistical guarantees: Clear decomposition into optimization and statistical error; geometric contraction plus sharp entrywise control leads to sqrt(s* log d / n) rates.
- Inference contribution: A decorrelated score tailored to latent-variable likelihoods via Q-function derivatives, with asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency.
- Generality: Framework instantiated for GMMs and mixed regressions; conditions are phrased to be verifiable model by model.
- Relation to prior work: Thoughtful placement relative to low-dimensional EM theory, penalized methods, and debiasing literature.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strong assumptions and practicality: The analysis uses a resampling (sample-splitting across iterations) version of the algorithm to control statistical error per iterate. It is unclear whether the guarantees carry to the practical single-sample setting without notable degradation; a discussion or high-level argument (e.g., via martingale concentration or stability) would help.
- Initialization: The theory requires initialization within a basin of attraction with relative error < 1. Concrete, computationally feasible initializers (e.g., spectral/tensor, method-of-moments, or thresholded least squares) and conditions under which they satisfy the required accuracy should be described for each model.
- Tuning and knowledge of sparsity: The truncation level bs must be of the same order as s*. Guidance on selecting bs in practice (e.g., cross-validation, information criteria, stability selection), and robustness when bs under-/over-shoots s*, is missing. Similarly, selection of step size η, Dantzig tuning λ, and stopping rules should be discussed.
- Assumptions for inference: The sparsity of w* (the population orthogonalization vector) is critical; while standard in high-dimensional inference, its reasonableness in mixtures deserves more discussion and empirical support. The conditions in Assumption 4.5 are heavy, and the resulting sample size requirements (with poly-log factors) may be stringent.
- Scope of models: Although the framework is advertised as general, only two models are worked out. It would be valuable to at least outline verification for other common latent variable models (e.g., probit mixtures, HMMs with sparse transitions/emissions).
- Empirical evaluation: No experiments are provided. Synthetic and real-data results demonstrating estimation error rates, support recovery, geometric contraction, and inference coverage/Type I error under different SNRs and dimensions are needed to assess practicality and calibrate constants.
- Presentation and references: The paper is notation-heavy; several references in the list are incomplete. Some connections (e.g., to iterative hard thresholding and hard-thresholded gradient methods) could be acknowledged.

Quality
The theory appears technically careful and largely sound, with a clear path from population contraction to sample-level guarantees using entrywise control. The reliance on sample splitting and strong SNR/basin assumptions is a limitation. The inference result is compelling, with the key identity relating Q-derivatives to scores making the decorrelated construction natural here. Completeness would be improved by empirical validation and by addressing tuning/initialization.

Clarity
Overall clear but dense. Suggestions:
- Provide a short “proof sketch” intuition for Theorem 3.4 (support evolution under T-step) and Theorem 4.6 (orthogonalization via Q-derivatives).
- Summarize all assumptions and rates in a single table per model.
- Clarify how to pick bs, η, λ, and how to initialize. Discuss removing sample splitting (or propose cross-fitting).
- Fix typos and complete citations.

Originality
Combining EM with hard truncation and providing sharp high-dimensional rates and valid inference for latent variable models is novel. The decorrelated score adapted via Q-derivatives is a nice contribution.

Significance
Bridges a notable gap between computation and inference for latent variable models in high dimensions. If empirically validated and made practical (tuning, initialization), the approach could influence practice.

Questions for authors
- Can you remove or mitigate the resampling assumption (e.g., via stability or self-normalized concentration), or provide empirical evidence that a single-sample implementation behaves similarly?
- What concrete initializers meet the basin condition for GMM and mixed regressions?
- How sensitive is inference to misspecification of s_w* sparsity?
- How do you recommend choosing bs and λ in practice?
- Computational complexity per iteration compared to l1-penalized EM?

Pros
- Strong, general theory with near-optimal rates and valid inference.
- Simple algorithmic modification (T-step) with clear intuition.
- Covers both exact and gradient EM.

Cons
- Heavy assumptions (initialization, SNR, bs ≈ s*, sparsity of w*).
- Analysis relies on resampling; practicality not demonstrated.
- No experiments; limited model coverage; tuning guidance missing.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on adding experiments and clarifying practical aspects (initialization, tuning, single-sample analysis). The theoretical contributions are solid and potentially impactful.

Confidence: High on theory assessment; medium on practicality due to lack of experiments.
