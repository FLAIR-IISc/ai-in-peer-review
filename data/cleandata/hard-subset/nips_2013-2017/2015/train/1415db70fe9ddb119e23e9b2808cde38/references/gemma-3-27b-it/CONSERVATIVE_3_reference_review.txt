This paper addresses the challenging problem of applying the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, a regime where theoretical guarantees are largely absent. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and develop a theoretical framework to establish statistical and computational guarantees, including asymptotic normality for parameter estimation. While the problem is undeniably important and the authors attempt a rigorous analysis, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing due to a combination of technical complexity, reliance on strong assumptions, and a perceived lack of compelling novelty beyond extending existing low-dimensional results.

The core idea of truncating the E and M steps to enforce sparsity is reasonable, but its implementation and the subsequent analysis feel unnecessarily complex. The numerous conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) are a significant barrier to entry for readers, and their verification (even for the Gaussian mixture model example) appears involved and relies on parameters that are difficult to interpret intuitively. The conditions themselves are often stated without clear motivation, simply asserting their necessity for the theoretical results.

My primary concern is the lack of a clear advantage over existing high-dimensional statistical methods. The statistical rate achieved (p s⇤ · log d/n) is a well-established result for sparse estimation problems – reminiscent of LASSO or similar techniques – and framing it as a novel contribution stemming *specifically* from the EM algorithm feels strained. The argument that the truncation step is key to achieving this rate doesn't fully land; other sparsity-inducing techniques could likely be integrated with the EM framework and yield similar results. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate why this particular truncation approach is superior to, or even meaningfully different from, established methods.

The claim of establishing asymptotic normality in the high-dimensional setting is valuable, but the conditions required to achieve it are stringent and limit the practical applicability. The requirement of sparsity in the *limiting* weights (w⇤ in Section 4) seems particularly restrictive.  The connection to semiparametric efficiency is mentioned but not deeply explored, leaving the reader wondering about the practical implications of this optimality claim.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Theoretical understanding of EM in high dimensions is lacking.
*   **Rigorous approach:** The authors attempt a detailed theoretical analysis, providing convergence and asymptotic normality results.
*   **Potential for wider application:** The framework is presented as generalizable to various latent variable models.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical complexity:** The paper is dense and difficult to follow, with numerous conditions and intricate proofs.
*   **Limited novelty:** The achieved statistical rate is not novel and the specific contribution of the truncation step feels incremental.
*   **Strong assumptions:** The required conditions are stringent and may not hold in many practical scenarios.
*   **Lack of empirical validation:** The paper focuses exclusively on theoretical analysis, lacking any simulation results or real-world applications to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
*   **Unclear connection to existing high-dimensional methods:** The paper does not adequately position itself within the broader literature of sparse estimation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper attempts a theoretically rigorous treatment of a challenging problem.
*   The results *could* be useful for specific applications where the strong assumptions are satisfied.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The technical complexity and stringent assumptions limit the practical impact.
*   The novelty is incremental and the connection to existing high-dimensional methods is not sufficiently explored.
*   The lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the real-world performance.
*   The paper feels more like a technical extension of existing results than a breakthrough.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

While the authors demonstrate mathematical sophistication, the paper lacks the originality and practical relevance to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The theoretical framework is overly complex, relies on strong assumptions, and does not offer a compelling advantage over existing methods. The absence of empirical validation further weakens the contribution. I would encourage the authors to simplify their presentation, focus on specific applications, and provide evidence of the practical benefits of their approach.
