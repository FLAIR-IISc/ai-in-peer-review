Summary
The paper studies EM for high-dimensional latent variable models (d >> n) and proposes (i) a “high-dimensional EM” that appends a hard-thresholding/truncation step to each EM iteration to enforce sparsity, and (ii) a decorrelated score test for inference on a low-dimensional component of the parameter in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The analysis provides: (a) a decomposition of error into geometric “optimization error” plus a “statistical error” of order sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to extra log factors), under suitable initialization and regularity; and (b) asymptotic normality of a score statistic with variance achieving the semiparametric information bound. The framework covers both exact M-step and gradient-ascent M-step implementations and is instantiated for two models (two-component spherical GMM and mixture of regressions) under separation/SNR assumptions.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on population-to-sample analyses of EM (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.), proximal views of EM, and on de-biasing/desparsification for inference in high-dimensional GLMs (Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.). It contrasts with tensor/moment methods (Anandkumar et al.), and with penalized EM approaches for mixture models (e.g., Städler et al.). The truncation step resembles iterative hard thresholding/projection methods widely used in sparse optimization.

Strengths
- Ambitious and timely attempt to bridge computational EM analysis with high-dimensional statistical guarantees.
- Clear optimization/statistics error decomposition and geometric convergence under assumptions; near-minimax statistical rate.
- Extends decorrelated-score-based inference beyond convex GLMs to latent variable models via Fisher’s identity; semiparametric efficiency is appealing.
- Covers both exact and gradient M-steps; unified conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, concavity/smoothness, statistical error) provide a template for model-specific verification.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strong assumptions:
  - Initialization must lie in a basin with constant relative error; how to obtain such an initializer in high dimensions (with sparsity) is not addressed with guarantees. Existing spectral/tensor initializers are either low-dimensional or require large n and separation.
  - Assumes substantial separation/SNR for GMM and similar assumptions for mixtures; practical applicability unclear.
  - Requires bs ≈ s* and uses hard thresholding of top bs coefficients. In practice s* is unknown; sensitivity and data-driven selection are not addressed.
  - Inference relies on sparsity of w* (a column of the inverse Fisher information), which can be unrealistic; no discussion of approximate sparsity or nodewise-regression alternatives.
- Resampling/split-sample analysis: Main convergence theorem is proved for a resampling version to simplify dependence. There is no theorem for the standard single-sample EM iterates, nor a clear prescription/penalty for sample splitting. This limits practical relevance.
- Practicality of tuning:
  - Gradient step requires step sizes tied to unknown curvature constants.
  - Dantzig tuning parameter depends on unknown quantities; solving a high-dimensional LP per inference target may be expensive.
- Limited scope of verification: Only two relatively simple models are treated (two-component spherical GMM and mixture of regressions). Claims of generality to “a broad family of latent variable models” are not substantiated (e.g., HMMs, topic models, factor analyzers, non-spherical covariances, K>2 mixtures).
- Lack of empirical validation: No experiments demonstrating estimation error scaling, robustness to misspecification of bs, runtime, or coverage and power of the proposed tests. Given the novelty claims, empirical evidence is important.
- Novelty vs. established methods: The truncation step is close to iterative hard thresholding/projection; the paper does not position its algorithm relative to existing sparse EM variants or hard-thresholded EM-like procedures. The inference part is largely a direct adaptation of decorrelated-score ideas using Fisher’s identity.
- Clarity and presentation issues: Numerous typos and broken notation (e.g., “d n”, undefined symbols, garbled constants), incomplete and malformed references, and missing details in the main text with heavy reliance on appendices. As written, reproduction is difficult.

Quality
The theoretical direction is interesting and some results are plausible, but many claims hinge on restrictive conditions and an analysis that uses resampling. Absent empirical support and practical guidance on initialization and tuning, the work reads as an incomplete theoretical position.

Clarity
Organization is reasonable, but the manuscript suffers from notation glitches, missing constants, and reliance on appendices for key details. References need substantial cleanup; important related work on hard-thresholding and sparse EM is missing or under-cited.

Originality
Moderate. The combination of EM with hard truncation and a de-biased score for latent models is a reasonable synthesis, but both components are adaptations of established techniques. The inference extension to latent models is the more novel part.

Significance
Potentially significant if the approach can be made practical with robust initialization/tuning and broader model coverage. In its current form, impact is limited by assumptions and lack of validation.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide an initialization scheme with guarantees in the high-dimensional setting for your models, and quantify the required n and SNR to be in the basin.
- Either remove the resampling assumption and prove uniform concentration over iterates, or present a practical sample-splitting algorithm with explicit performance trade-offs.
- Analyze sensitivity to bs and propose a data-driven rule (e.g., information criteria, stability selection).
- Discuss feasibility of the w* sparsity assumption; consider approximate sparsity and nodewise regression analogues.
- Add experiments comparing to penalized EM (ℓ1/SCAD), gradient EM with ℓ1, and tensor/spectral initializations, evaluating estimation error, support recovery, runtime, and test coverage/power.
- Clarify constants/log factors in Theorem 3.4; discuss K>2 mixtures and non-spherical covariances.
- Fix notation and references; cite hard-thresholding/IHT literature and sparse EM variants.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The paper introduces interesting ideas but requires stronger theoretical grounding for the standard (non-resampled) setting, clearer/practical guidance on initialization and tuning, broader model verification, and empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards.

Confidence: high.
