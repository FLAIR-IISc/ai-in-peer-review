Summary
The paper studies EM for high-dimensional latent-variable models (d >> n) where classical MLE and low-dimensional EM theory do not apply. The authors propose a high-dimensional EM algorithm that augments the E- and M-steps with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step to enforce sparsity along the iterates. They analyze two M-step implementations (exact maximization and a single gradient ascent step) and, under a basin-of-attraction initialization, establish a nonasymptotic error decomposition: a geometrically decaying optimization error plus a statistical error of order roughly sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to log factors), leading to near-minimax rates for the final iterate. Beyond point estimation, they introduce a decorrelated score test for inference on a low-dimensional component of the parameter in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. Using a Dantzig-selector construction for the orthogonalization vector and a key identity linking derivatives of Q to the score and Fisher information, they prove asymptotic normality with variance attaining the semiparametric information bound. They illustrate the framework on Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on recent analyses of EM in the low-dimensional regime (notably Balakrishnan et al.) by recasting EM as a perturbed gradient method. It departs by introducing a truncation step to handle high dimensionality and sparsity, and by developing inference via decorrelated scores akin to desparsification/debiasing methods in high-dimensional GLMs (e.g., van de Geer, Zhang & Zhang, Javanmard & Montanari), but adapted to latent-variable settings. Compared to tensor/moment methods for mixtures, this approach targets better statistical rates and computational practicality when d is large. The connection to proximal point interpretations of EM and to classical EM convergence results is acknowledged.

Strengths
- Tackles an important gap: computationally tractable estimation and valid inference for high-dimensional latent-variable models fit by EM.
- Clean algorithmic idea (T-step) with general analysis covering both exact and gradient M-steps.
- Nonasymptotic theory with explicit optimization/statistical error decomposition and near-optimal rates for sparse parameters.
- Inference contribution: decorrelated score adapted to EM, with semiparametric efficiency under stated conditions.
- Clear mapping from conditions to two concrete models (Gaussian mixtures, mixtures of regressions), with explicit rates.
- The connections between Q-derivatives and score/Fisher information are made explicit, enabling inference.

Weaknesses
- Strong assumptions and practicality gaps:
  - Requires a good initialization in a basin of attraction with constant relative error; no concrete, computationally feasible initialization strategy is provided for the considered models (e.g., spectral/tensor or method-of-moments warm starts).
  - The sparsity level bs must be set of the same order as the unknown s*, and several bounds require knowledge of constants (μ, ν, ρ); guidance for tuning is limited.
  - The “resampling” analysis version is not practical and the implications for single-sample runs need clarification.
- Inference requires sparsity of w* (from the inverse information) which can be unrealistic in mixtures; more discussion/empirical evidence is needed.
- No empirical evaluation is presented in the submission (only theoretical claims and references to appendices/longer version). For NeurIPS, even theory papers benefit from simulations validating rates, stability to misspecification, and comparison to competitors (e.g., ℓ1-penalized EM, proximal gradient with soft-thresholding).
- Clarity/notation issues and minor errors hinder readability:
  - Several typographical artifacts, undefined/misaligned symbols (e.g., r2_{1,1}Q, r2_{1,2}Q, subscripts/superscripts), and inconsistent norms.
  - References are incomplete/garbled; many entries lack years/journals or are malformed.
- Comparison baselines: missing discussion and, ideally, theory for ℓ1-penalized EM (soft-thresholded/proximal M-step) versus hard truncation, and trade-offs in bias/variance and support recovery.

Quality
The theoretical results are plausible and technically nontrivial. The conditions (Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness, Statistical-Error in ℓ1 for s-sparse inputs) are clearly stated and, per the appendix claims, verified for the two examples. The inference result is carefully tied to Fisher information and achieves efficiency. However, the lack of experiments and the heavy reliance on unverified constants/tuning weaken the empirical credibility. Practicality of assumptions (initialization, sparsity of w*) needs more justification.

Clarity
The overall structure is reasonable, but readability suffers from notational glitches, dense presentation, and reliance on appendices/longer version for key details. The algorithm is clearly stated, yet guidance on tuning and stopping is minimal. The references must be fixed.

Originality
The truncation-EM framework and its analysis in high dimensions, coupled with decorrelated scores for latent-variable models, appear novel as a unified contribution. The T-step relates to known hard-thresholding methods, but its integration into EM and the inference component are original and valuable.

Significance
If the results hold broadly and can be implemented robustly, the impact is high: a general path to estimation and valid inference for high-dimensional latent-variable models. The analysis could stimulate further work on nonconvex inference.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide practical initialization strategies for the considered models (e.g., moment/spectral methods) with guarantees that they land in the basin B.
- Discuss adaptive choices of bs (e.g., cross-validation, information criteria) and sensitivity to mis-specifying s*; consider a final refitting/debiasing step on the selected support to reduce bias.
- Compare to penalized EM with ℓ1 penalties (soft-thresholding) both theoretically and empirically.
- Clarify the feasibility and cost of the Dantzig step; can nodewise regression variants be used? Provide guidance for tuning λ in finite samples.
- Elaborate on the plausibility of sparsity of w* in the presented models; if dense, can approximate sparsity suffice?
- Replace the resampling analysis with an argument for a single sample path, or discuss how to translate the bounds.
- Add simulations for GMM and mixture of regressions: estimation error vs n,d,s*, support recovery, inference coverage/power, and robustness to misspecification/heavy tails.

Pros
- Strong and timely theoretical contribution bridging computation and inference in high-d latent-variable models.
- General framework with clear algorithm and rates; inference is semiparametrically efficient.

Cons
- Practical gaps (initialization, tuning, w* sparsity), no experiments, and clarity/typo issues.
- Some assumptions may be restrictive for common mixtures.

Recommendation
Borderline accept (leaning accept if the camera-ready can add simulations, clarify initialization/tuning, and fix presentation and references). Confidence: moderate.
