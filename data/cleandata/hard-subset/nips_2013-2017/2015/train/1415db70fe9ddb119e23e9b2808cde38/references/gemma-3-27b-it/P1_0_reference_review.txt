## Peer Review: High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm: its behavior in high-dimensional settings where the number of parameters *d* exceeds the sample size *n*.  The authors propose a modified EM algorithm incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, and provide a comprehensive theoretical framework establishing both statistical estimation rates and asymptotic normality for the resulting estimator. They demonstrate the applicability of their framework to Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and Mixture of Regression Models (MRMs).  The work builds on recent advancements in understanding the convergence of EM in the low-dimensional regime [2, 25, 30] and addresses challenges posed by high dimensionality, particularly the lack of well-defined maximum likelihood estimators and the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical open problem:** The theoretical analysis of EM in high dimensions is a long-standing challenge, and this paper provides a substantial contribution towards filling that gap.
* **Novel algorithmic contribution:** The proposed truncation step is a clever and effective way to address the challenges posed by high dimensionality and enforce sparsity, allowing for statistical guarantees.
* **Comprehensive theoretical framework:**  The paper develops a unified framework for analyzing both the computational convergence of the algorithm and the statistical properties of the estimator. This is a significant achievement.
* **Optimal Statistical Rate:** Achieving a (near-)minimax-optimal rate of convergence (p s⇤ · log d/n) is a strong result, demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed approach.
* **Asymptotic Normality:** Establishing asymptotic normality for the estimator is crucial for uncertainty quantification and hypothesis testing, a feature often missing in high-dimensional settings. The proposed decorrelated score statistic is a particularly valuable contribution.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The notation is clearly defined, and the algorithm is presented concisely.
* **Connections to related work:** The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, including comparisons to [2] and other approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Complexity:** The paper is highly technical and requires a strong background in statistical learning theory and optimization. This may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* **Conditionality:** The theoretical results rely on several conditions (3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.5) that, while likely reasonable for many latent variable models, require careful verification for each specific application. The authors demonstrate this for GMMs, but it would be helpful to see a more general discussion of the conditions and their implications.
* **Constant Factors:** The constants appearing in the bounds (e.g., in Theorem 3.4) are not explicitly quantified. While this is common in theoretical analysis, it makes it difficult to assess the practical implications of the results.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical analysis and lacks extensive empirical validation. While the authors state that further experiments are in the longer version, demonstrating the performance of the algorithm on real or simulated datasets would strengthen the paper.
* **Sparsity Assumption:** The requirement that the nuisance parameter w* be sparse (as implied by Assumption 4.5) is a potentially restrictive assumption. While justified in the context of achieving asymptotic normality, it's important to discuss its limitations and potential impact on practical applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and important paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The proposed algorithm and theoretical framework are novel and well-developed, and the results are both theoretically sound and practically relevant. While the technical complexity and reliance on conditions may limit its accessibility, the potential impact of this work is substantial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a long-standing open problem in statistical learning.
* Provides a novel and effective algorithm for high-dimensional estimation.
* Establishes rigorous theoretical guarantees for both convergence and statistical properties.
* Offers a valuable tool for uncertainty quantification and hypothesis testing.
* Represents a significant advance in the state of the art.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* High technical complexity may limit accessibility.
* Reliance on conditions requires careful verification for each application.
* Lack of extensive empirical evaluation.
* Potential restrictions imposed by the sparsity assumption on nuisance parameters.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept**. Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its limitations. The theoretical contributions are significant and the potential impact is substantial. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the conditions and discuss the implications of the sparsity assumption.  Adding a brief discussion of experimental results, even if limited, would further strengthen the paper.

**Score:** 8.5 / 10
