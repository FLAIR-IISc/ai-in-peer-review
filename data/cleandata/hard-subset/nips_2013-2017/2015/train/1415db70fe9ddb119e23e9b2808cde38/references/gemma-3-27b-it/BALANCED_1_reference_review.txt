This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, a setting where existing results fall short. The authors make a significant contribution by proposing a truncated EM algorithm and deriving statistical and computational guarantees, including asymptotic normality for low-dimensional components. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important open problem.

**Summary of Contributions:** The authors introduce a high-dimensional EM algorithm with a truncation step to enforce sparsity. They establish a convergence rate of p s⇤ · log(d/n) for parameter estimation and demonstrate asymptotic normality for hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components, achieving optimal rates under certain conditions. The framework is general, applicable to models like Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regression models, and accommodates both exact and approximate maximization steps in the M-step.

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Problem:** Addressing the theoretical limitations of the EM algorithm in high dimensions is crucial for its reliable application to modern data analysis.
*   **Novel Approach:** The truncation step is a clever idea that effectively addresses the challenges posed by high dimensionality and enforces sparsity.
*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The convergence rate and asymptotic normality results are significant advancements, providing solid theoretical foundations for the algorithm’s performance.
*   **Generality:** The framework’s applicability to different models and M-step implementations adds to its practical value.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear notation and explanations. The inclusion of algorithms and detailed notation aids reproducibility.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors provide a thorough review of existing literature, accurately positioning their work within the broader field. Comparisons with tensor methods and other high-dimensional estimation techniques are particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Conditions:** The many technical conditions (3.1-3.3 and 4.1-4.5) while standard, can be daunting and may limit the immediate applicability of the results. Verifying these conditions for specific models requires substantial effort, as illustrated in the appendices, and a reader unfamiliar with the technical details may struggle to assess the practicality.
*   **Constant Factors:** While the authors demonstrate optimal scaling, the constant factors hidden within the big-O notation are largely unknown. These constants can significantly impact the algorithm’s practical performance, especially for moderate sample sizes.
*   **Sparsity Assumption:** The requirement for the nuisance parameter (w⇤) to be sparse in the context of asymptotic normality feels restrictive.  While motivated by achieving optimal inference, this condition needs further discussion regarding its practical implications.  Is it often met in real-world scenarios?
*   **Computational Cost:** The truncation step, while theoretically sound, might introduce computational overhead. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm compared to standard EM would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the paper focuses on theoretical guarantees, including some preliminary empirical results on synthetic or real datasets would strengthen the paper and provide a more complete picture of the algorithm’s performance.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant open problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The results are mathematically rigorous and provide valuable insights into the behavior of the EM algorithm in high dimensions.
*   The generality of the framework and the clear presentation enhance its potential impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The numerous technical conditions may limit the practical applicability of the results.
*   The lack of empirical validation leaves some questions about the algorithm’s real-world performance unanswered.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. While the technical conditions and lack of empirical validation are concerns, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the results outweigh these weaknesses. I believe the paper is suitable for NIPS, provided the authors address the minor concerns outlined above, particularly a more detailed discussion of the assumptions and computational complexity.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Discuss Practicality of Conditions:** Provide more guidance on how to verify the technical conditions for common latent variable models.
*   **Analyze Constant Factors:** Investigate the impact of the constant factors on the algorithm’s performance.
*   **Address Sparsity Assumption:** Offer a more in-depth discussion about the practical implications of the sparsity assumption on the nuisance parameter.
*   **Computational Complexity Analysis:** Include a discussion of the computational cost of the truncation step.
*   **Consider Empirical Validation:** Add some preliminary experimental results to demonstrate the algorithm’s performance on real or synthetic data.
*   **Clarify Notation:** While generally clear, a few places could benefit from more explicit definitions or explanations of notation. For example, the specific meaning of 's' in the context of (3.8) could be elaborated on.
