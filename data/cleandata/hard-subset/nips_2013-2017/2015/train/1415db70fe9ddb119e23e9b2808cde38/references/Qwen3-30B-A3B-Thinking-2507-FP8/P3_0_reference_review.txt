

# Peer Review: "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature on latent variable models: the lack of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings where the dimension d exceeds the sample size n. The authors propose a novel high-dimensional EM algorithm that incorporates a truncation step to enforce sparsity, establishing both computational and statistical guarantees. They prove that with appropriate initialization, the algorithm converges geometrically to an estimator with a near-minimax optimal statistical rate of convergence (O(√(s* log d/n)). Furthermore, they develop a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing of low-dimensional components, establishing its asymptotic normality and optimality in terms of semiparametric efficiency.

The paper builds upon and extends the work of Balakrishnan et al. [2] who established guarantees for the low-dimensional regime (d << n), but the key innovation here is the truncation step that enables handling high-dimensional settings. The authors verify their theoretical results for Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models, showing that their approach achieves the optimal statistical rate while tensor methods (which have been used for latent variable models) suffer from suboptimal rates in high dimensions.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Theoretical Contribution**: The paper fills a critical gap in the literature by providing the first theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. This is a major advancement given the widespread use of EM in latent variable models.

2. **Novel Algorithmic Framework**: The introduction of the truncation step in the EM algorithm is a clever and effective approach to handle high dimensionality. It's a simple modification that leads to profound theoretical improvements.

3. **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides a unified framework for both estimation and inference, which is rare in high-dimensional statistics. The authors establish both optimization and statistical guarantees, and they prove the asymptotic normality of their proposed decorrelated score statistic.

4. **Strong Connections to Semiparametric Efficiency**: The authors demonstrate that their decorrelated score statistic achieves the semiparametric information bound, which is a strong theoretical guarantee that few high-dimensional inference methods achieve.

5. **Practical Relevance**: The paper addresses a real-world problem where high-dimensional latent variable models are increasingly used (e.g., in genomics, natural language processing, and computer vision), and provides a computationally feasible solution.

## Weaknesses and Criticisms

1. **Technical Complexity and Accessibility**: The paper is highly technical with dense notation and numerous conditions (Conditions 3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5). While this is expected for a theoretical paper, the complexity may limit the accessibility of the results to a broader audience. A more intuitive explanation of the key technical conditions would be helpful.

2. **Sparsity Assumption**: The paper relies on the assumption that the true parameter is sparse (s* << d), which is standard in high-dimensional statistics but limits the applicability of the method. It would be valuable to discuss scenarios where this assumption might not hold and how the method might be extended.

3. **Tuning Parameter Selection**: The sparsity parameter bs is crucial for the algorithm's performance, but the paper doesn't provide a practical method for selecting it in real data applications. The authors state that bs should be chosen to be "sufficiently large and of the same order as the true sparsity level s*," but this is not helpful for practitioners.

4. **Computational Complexity**: While the paper establishes geometric convergence rates, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, particularly the cost of the truncation step at each iteration. For very large d, this could become computationally prohibitive.

5. **Verification of Conditions**: The paper verifies the technical conditions for Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models in the appendix, but it's not clear how widely applicable these conditions are to other latent variable models. The authors should discuss the broader applicability of their framework.

6. **Comparison with Alternatives**: While the paper compares with tensor methods and low-dimensional EM, it would be valuable to compare with other high-dimensional approaches for latent variable models, such as the gradient EM with regularization proposed by [23] for mixture of regression.

## Technical Evaluation

The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, with detailed proofs provided in the appendix. The main results (Theorems 3.4 and 4.6) are significant contributions to the field. The truncation step is a clever and effective innovation that enables the extension to high dimensions, and the authors provide a careful analysis of its effects on the convergence properties.

However, I have some concerns about the statistical error term in Condition 3.3. The authors use the l1-norm to quantify statistical error, which is appropriate for high-dimensional settings, but the specific form of the error bound (Equation 3.7) is somewhat opaque. The connection between the statistical error and the sparsity level s* could be made more explicit.

The decorrelated score statistic is well-motivated and the proof of its asymptotic normality (Theorem 4.6) is technically sound. The authors correctly identify that their method achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound, which is a strong theoretical guarantee.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Clarify the role of the sparsity parameter bs**: Provide more guidance on how to select bs in practice, perhaps through a data-dependent method or a discussion of its relationship to the true sparsity level s*.

2. **Add a practical example**: Include a small simulation study or real data application to demonstrate the algorithm's performance and the practical implications of the theoretical results.

3. **Simplify the notation**: The notation is quite dense. Consider introducing more intuitive notation for key quantities like the truncation operator.

4. **Discuss limitations**: Provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the sparsity assumption and how the method might be extended to less sparse settings.

5. **Add computational complexity analysis**: Include a discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm, particularly the cost of the truncation step at each iteration.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and latent variable models. The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm with truncation is a clever and effective solution to a long-standing problem. The theoretical guarantees are strong, and the extension to inferential procedures is particularly valuable.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature. While the technical complexity may limit immediate accessibility, the results are likely to be of great interest to the NIPS community and will probably stimulate further research in high-dimensional latent variable models.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. The results are novel, technically sound, and significant for both theoretical and practical aspects of latent variable modeling. The authors have clearly demonstrated the novelty of their approach through a careful comparison with existing work.

The main weaknesses are related to accessibility and practical implementation details, but these are common in highly technical theoretical papers and do not diminish the paper's scientific contribution. The paper provides a solid foundation for future work in high-dimensional latent variable models and will likely be influential in the field.

## Key Arguments for Acceptance

- **Novel contribution**: First theoretical guarantees for EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings.
- **Strong theoretical results**: Establishes both computational and statistical guarantees with near-optimal rates.
- **Practical significance**: Provides a computationally feasible approach for high-dimensional latent variable models.
- **Rigorous analysis**: Careful theoretical development with detailed proofs in the appendix.
- **Inferential framework**: Develops a decorrelated score statistic that achieves semiparametric efficiency.

## Key Arguments Against Acceptance

- **Technical complexity**: May be difficult for some readers to follow.
- **Sparsity assumption**: Limits applicability to sparse parameter settings.
- **Tuning parameter selection**: Lack of practical guidance for choosing the sparsity parameter.

Despite these concerns, the paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses, and the contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
