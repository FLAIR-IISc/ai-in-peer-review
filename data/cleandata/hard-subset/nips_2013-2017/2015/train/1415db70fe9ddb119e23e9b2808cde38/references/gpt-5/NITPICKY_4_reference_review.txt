Summary
This paper proposes a high-dimensional variant of EM that alternates standard E/M steps with a hard-thresholding “T-step” to enforce sparsity. Two M-step implementations are covered (exact maximization and a single gradient ascent step). The authors establish geometric contraction of optimization error within a basin of attraction and derive an overall ℓ2 estimation error of order sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (up to log n). Building on the estimator, they construct a decorrelated score test using a Dantzig selector for orthogonalization and prove asymptotic normality with variance matching the semiparametric efficiency bound. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, with verifications deferred to the appendix. The analysis parallels recent finite-sample EM theory in low dimensions (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) and connects high-dimensional inference to desparsified/decorrelated methods (van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang, Ning & Liu).

Strengths
- Ambitious and timely goal: unifying optimization and inference guarantees for latent-variable models in the high-dimensional regime.
- Conceptual novelty: introducing a truncation step into EM and analyzing the resulting dynamics; relating Q-function derivatives to the score and Fisher information to enable decorrelated inference in latent-variable settings.
- Generality: covers exact and gradient M-steps and aims to handle multiple latent variable models under a common set of conditions.
- Theoretical claims include geometric convergence to a neighborhood and near-minimax estimation error, and an inference procedure that achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Resampling analysis vs. practical algorithm: The main convergence theorem (3.4) analyzes a resampling version (Algorithm 4, in appendix). Guarantees for the standard single-sample iterative algorithm are not provided in the main text; it is unclear whether the same bounds hold without data splitting or how dependence across iterations is controlled. This is a major gap for practical applicability.
- Choice of truncation level bs: Theorems require bs to be of the same order as s* and even depend on unknown constants (3.8)–(3.9). No data-driven rule (e.g., information criteria, stability methods, cross-validation proxies) is provided, and theoretical robustness to mis-specification of bs is not analyzed. This limits usability and makes the claims sensitive to oracle knowledge.
- Initialization and basin-of-attraction assumptions: The analysis requires an initialization within a fixed-radius ball around θ*. How to achieve this in practice for the latent variable models considered (especially GMM) is not discussed beyond strong separation/SNR assumptions. The guarantees heavily rely on this and on model-specific constants (often implicit).
- Conditions are strong and somewhat opaque: Conditions 3.1–3.3 and 4.1–4.4 depend on population smoothness/concavity and Lipschitz constants around θ*, an ℓ1 statistical error for M vs. Mn under s-sparse inputs, and sparse “orthogonalization” vector w*. The required sparsity of w* (s_w* = o(√(n/log d))) is nontrivial and not justified for typical designs beyond high-level discussion; practical diagnostics for this assumption are not provided.
- Inference construction: The decorrelated test is essentially a transplant of the desparsified/decorrelated score framework to Q-function derivatives. While valuable, it seems incremental: the main novelty lies in showing the EM-Q derivatives coincide with score/Fisher (Theorem 2.1), which is standard in EM theory; the rest mirrors known high-dimensional inference developments.
- Missing experiments: There are no empirical results demonstrating the finite-sample behavior, sensitivity to bs and tuning parameters, or comparisons to penalized EM / tensor methods / debiasing baselines. Given the number of tuning parameters (bs, step size, Dantzig λ) and stringent assumptions, experiments are essential.
- Clarity/notation issues:
  - Tn(θ) definition (2.4) and its use in the Dantzig constraint are hard to parse; the mix of second derivatives with respect to first and second arguments looks nonstandard. If the claim is that E[Tn(θ*)]=I(θ*), a clean definition as the negative Hessian of Q(·; θ) at θ would help. Signs and dimensions should be double-checked.
  - Condition 3.1 (Lipschitz-Gradient-1): r_θ Q(M(θ); θ*) − r_θ Q(M(θ); θ) bounded by ||θ−θ*||; indexing and roles of arguments are confusing. Similarly for Lipschitz-Gradient-2. Provide intuition and a simple verification path in the main text for at least one model.
  - Multiple uses of “r” for gradients, subscripts 1,2, etc., are difficult to follow; some symbols (`, `1-norm, Id, indicators) and equations have formatting glitches (e.g., missing slashes, broken math).
- References are severely incomplete/malformed: author names are corrupted, years/journals/volume/pages missing, some titles truncated. This is below NeurIPS standards and hinders assessing related work.
- Minor technical doubts:
  - The “optimal rate up to log n” claim: the origin of the extra log n factor should be clearly identified and whether it can be removed without resampling.
  - Monotonicity: the truncation step breaks the EM monotonicity property; a discussion of practical convergence diagnostics would be useful.
  - Computational cost: computing Tn(·) and solving the Dantzig selector at each iteration may be expensive. Complexity and scalability are not discussed.

Relation to prior work
- Low-dimensional EM convergence (Balakrishnan et al.) is appropriately cited, and the paper builds on that “population-to-sample” analysis paradigm.
- Sparse EM/penalized EM for mixtures (e.g., Städler et al., Khalili & Chen) are cited, but a direct comparison (both theoretically and empirically) is missing.
- High-dimensional inference lines (van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang, Ning & Liu) are relevant; the contribution here is adapting these tools via EM identities to latent variable models, which is useful but not fundamentally new.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide guarantees for the standard, single-sample algorithm (no resampling), or clearly state limitations.
- Offer practical, data-driven procedures for choosing bs and λ, and analyze robustness to misspecification.
- Clarify Tn(·) definition, signs, and dimensions; streamline Conditions 3.1–3.2 with intuitive explanations; include at least one fully worked-out verification in the main text.
- Add synthetic experiments for GMM and mixture of regressions, including sensitivity to initialization, bs, λ, and comparisons to penalized EM and debiased-likelihood baselines.
- Discuss computation (per-iteration cost, scalability), and provide pseudocode with all tuning defaults.
- Fix references and extensive notation/formatting errors.

Pros
- Ambitious attempt to close computation–statistics gap for high-dimensional latent-variable EM.
- Clean error decomposition and near-optimal rates under strong conditions.
- Asymptotically optimal inference construction for a nonconvex, latent-variable setting.

Cons
- Practical algorithmic guarantees hinge on resampling and oracle/tuned sparsity; unclear applicability.
- Strong, somewhat opaque assumptions; limited novelty in the inference component.
- No experiments; numerous presentation and reference issues.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The ideas are promising, but the current manuscript has significant theoretical/practical gaps (resampling reliance, tuning/initialization), lacks empirical validation, and contains many clarity and formatting problems. With a strengthened non-resampled analysis, practical tuning guidance, thorough experiments, and careful editing, this work could become a valuable contribution. Confidence: high.
