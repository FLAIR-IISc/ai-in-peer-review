This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, a setting where existing results fall short. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and establish both statistical and computational guarantees, including asymptotic normality of the estimator and a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing. The work is well-motivated, addressing a significant gap in the literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The truncation step incorporated into the EM algorithm is a key innovation, designed to address the challenges of high dimensionality and sparsity. Establishing theoretical guarantees for this modified algorithm, especially asymptotic normality, is a valuable contribution.
* **Generality:** The framework is presented as general, applicable to various latent variable models (illustrated with Gaussian mixture and mixture of regression models).
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis, including conditions for convergence, statistical rates, and asymptotic properties of the estimator and test statistic. The proof structure appears well-organized (though the appendix is not fully reviewed).
* **Statistical Optimality:** The authors demonstrate that the proposed decorrelated score statistic achieves the semiparametric information bound, indicating optimality.
* **Clear Presentation of Assumptions:** The conditions required for the theorems are clearly laid out, along with discussion regarding their implications and verification for specific models.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Complexity:** The paper is highly technical and relies on numerous conditions and assumptions. While necessary for rigor, this makes it difficult to grasp the core ideas without significant effort. A more intuitive explanation of the truncation step's impact would be helpful.
* **Dependence on Conditions:** The statistical rates and asymptotic results are contingent on strong conditions, especially regarding the sparsity level and the Fisher information matrix. The practicality of these conditions in real-world applications warrants further discussion. Specifically, the requirement that `s⇤w` (sparsity of the w vector related to the Fisher information) is small might be restrictive.
* **Computational Cost:** The truncation step potentially adds computational overhead to the EM algorithm. A discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm compared to standard EM would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper focuses exclusively on theoretical results. Including some simulation studies or a small-scale application to demonstrate the performance of the algorithm in practice would strengthen the work. While the authors acknowledge deferring further models to a longer version, some empirical validation is desired in the main manuscript.
* **Notation:** While generally clear, the abundance of symbols and notation can be overwhelming.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in statistical learning: theoretical guarantees for EM in high dimensions.
* The proposed approach is novel and offers a potential solution to the challenges of local optima and high-dimensional nuisance parameters.
* The rigorous theoretical analysis provides strong support for the validity of the proposed algorithm and its statistical properties.
* The result concerning the optimality of the decorrelated score statistic is a significant contribution to statistical inference in latent variable models.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The high level of technical complexity and strong assumptions may limit the paper's accessibility and practical relevance.
* The lack of empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's performance in real-world scenarios.
*  The conditions on `s⇤w` being small might limit the scope of the method.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound and theoretically significant paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. While the paper is complex and relies on strong assumptions, the novelty of the approach and the rigor of the analysis outweigh these concerns.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NeurIPS, contingent on the authors considering adding a small-scale empirical evaluation to bolster the theoretical findings.
