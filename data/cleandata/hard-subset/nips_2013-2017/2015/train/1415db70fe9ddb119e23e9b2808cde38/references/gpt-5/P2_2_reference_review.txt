Summary
This paper studies EM for high-dimensional latent variable models and aims to bridge the computation–statistics gap when d ≫ n under sparsity. The authors propose a high-dimensional EM algorithm that appends a hard-thresholding (“truncation”) step after each E/M step to enforce sparsity, and develop an inferential procedure based on a decorrelated score. They provide a unified analysis showing (i) an optimization–statistics error decomposition with geometric contraction from suitable initialization and (near-)minimax estimation rate O(s*^1/2 (log d / n)^1/2) up to logs; and (ii) asymptotic normality of a decorrelated score for a low-dimensional component with variance attaining the semiparametric efficiency bound, despite high-dimensional nuisance parameters. Two M-step implementations are covered (exact maximization and one-step gradient ascent). The authors verify the conditions for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under separation/SNR and sparsity, and relate gradients/Hessians of Q to those of the log-likelihood (Theorem 2.1). The analysis uses a resampling version of the algorithm for technical convenience.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on population-to-sample EM analyses (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) that establish geometric convergence in low dimension and connects to high-dimensional inference via debiasing/desparsification (van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang) and Dantzig/nodewise approaches. It contrasts with tensor methods (Anandkumar et al.) which yield suboptimal rates and primarily low-dimensional guarantees. Compared to penalized EM variants, the hard-thresholded EM with entrywise error control and a decorrelated score for latent variable models appears novel.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main theorems are clearly stated, assumptions are explicit (Lipschitz-gradient and concavity/smoothness of the population Q; entrywise statistical error for the M-step; concentration and Lipschitz properties of Tn), and the arguments are plausible given current theory. The optimization bound cleanly separates geometric optimization error from statistical error and yields near-minimax rates when ε ≍ (log d / n)^1/2. The inference result is careful about nuisance adjustment and links to Fisher information, attaining semiparametric efficiency.
- Caveats/assumptions: Guarantees require (a) a good initialization within a basin of attraction; (b) strong SNR/separation (e.g., for GMM); (c) truncation level b_s of the same order as s*; and (d) sparsity of the “decorrelation” vector w* defined via the Fisher information. These are standard but nontrivial in practice. Moreover, the main estimation theorem is proved for a resampling/split-sample variant of EM; it is not clear whether the same rates hold for the single-sample iterative algorithm used in practice. The inference conditions involve several quantities (ξEM, ξG, ξT, ξL) and sample-size scaling; while verifiable for the two examples, they are somewhat opaque.
- Empirical evaluation: None is presented in the provided text. For NeurIPS, experiments demonstrating finite-sample performance (estimation error, support recovery, type-I error and power, coverage) and comparisons with alternatives (ℓ1-penalized EM/gradient EM, tensor/spectral methods, debiased-lasso baselines where applicable) would substantially strengthen the paper.

Clarity
The high-level ideas and algorithm are easy to follow; the role of the truncation step and the decorrelated score are well motivated. However, the presentation is notation-heavy and the draft contains many formatting artifacts (e.g., stray symbols, missing reference details), making it harder to parse. Practical guidance on choosing b_s, stepsizes, and Dantzig tuning is limited; computational costs of the Dantzig step and Hessian evaluations are not discussed. The reliance on resampling for theory vs. the algorithm used in practice should be clarified.

Originality
Combining hard-thresholded EM with a unified computational-statistical analysis in high dimensions and extending debiased-score inference to latent variable models with nonconvex likelihoods is, to my knowledge, novel. Achieving semiparametric efficiency for mixture models with high-dimensional nuisance is a strong conceptual contribution.

Significance
The work addresses an important and timely problem: statistically optimal estimation and valid inference for high-dimensional latent variable models computed by EM-like procedures. If broadly applicable, the framework could influence both theory and practice. The reliance on strong conditions and the lack of empirical validation temper the immediate impact.

Pros
- Addresses a clear gap: high-dimensional EM with both estimation and inference guarantees.
- Clean optimization–statistics error decomposition with near-minimax rates.
- Covers both exact and gradient M-steps under a unified framework.
- Decorrelated score attains semiparametric efficiency; inference beyond point estimation.
- Conditions verified for two canonical latent variable models.

Cons
- Theory for a resampling variant; unclear guarantees for standard single-sample EM iterations.
- Strong assumptions: good initialization, high SNR, correct order of b_s, sparsity of w*.
- Practical aspects underdeveloped: tuning, computational cost of Dantzig/Hessian, robustness to mis-specified sparsity.
- No empirical results or comparisons; limited guidance for practitioners.
- Presentation issues: heavy notation, formatting glitches, incomplete references.

Questions for authors
1) Do your estimation and inference guarantees extend to the standard, non-resampling EM iterates? If not, can you provide a stability argument or experiments showing similar behavior?
2) How should b_s be chosen without knowing s*? Is there robustness to over/under-specification?
3) What initialization strategies with guarantees (e.g., spectral, method-of-moments) do you recommend for the two models?
4) Can nodewise regressions replace the Dantzig step for w, and what is the computational complexity at scale?
5) How sensitive are your results to model misspecification and label switching in mixtures?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contributions are strong and, if complemented with empirical validation and clarified assumptions (especially removing the resampling gap), would be a compelling NeurIPS paper. In its current form, the lack of experiments and some practical gaps limit its suitability for NeurIPS. Constructive improvements include adding thorough experiments, clarifying applicability to standard EM, and improving presentation.
