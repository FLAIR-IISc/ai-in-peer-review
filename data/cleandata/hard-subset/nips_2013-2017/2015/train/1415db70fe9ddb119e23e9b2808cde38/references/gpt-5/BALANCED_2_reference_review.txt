Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies EM for high-dimensional latent variable models and proposes a “high-dimensional EM” (HD-EM) algorithm that augments the classical EM with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step to enforce sparsity after each M-step. Two implementations are covered: exact maximization and a single gradient-ascent step. The authors establish nonasymptotic optimization and statistical error bounds under local (basin of attraction) assumptions, showing geometric contraction of the optimization error and a final estimation rate of order sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (up to log n factors), which is near-minimax. Building on the HD-EM estimator, they develop a decorrelated score test (via a Dantzig-type estimator of a nuisance-orthogonal direction) and prove asymptotic normality with variance attaining the semiparametric efficiency bound. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under separation/SNR conditions.

The paper fits into a line of work connecting computation and statistics for EM: Wu (1983) and Tseng (2004) on convergence; Balakrishnan et al. on population-to-sample analysis and geometric convergence in low dimensions; and recent survey work on EM. It also relates to high-dimensional inference via de-biasing/desparsification (Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.; Javanmard & Montanari), but extends these ideas to latent-variable likelihoods where global convex optima are unavailable. Compared to tensor/moment methods (Anandkumar et al.), the authors argue improved statistical rates and broader applicability in high dimensions.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: theoretical guarantees for EM in the high-dimensional regime, including both estimation and inference.
- Clean, unified analysis: explicit decomposition of optimization vs. statistical error; covers both exact and gradient M-steps with clear conditions (Lipschitz-gradient, local strong concavity/smoothness, sample vs. population M-step discrepancy).
- Near-optimal estimation rates: sqrt(s* log d / n) up to log n factors under sparse truth, and geometric convergence from a good initialization.
- Inference contribution: decorrelated score based on the Q-function’s derivatives; asymptotic normality attaining the semiparametric information bound, which is nontrivial for latent-variable models with high-dimensional nuisance.
- Broad potential applicability beyond the two worked examples.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Analysis via resampling: The main estimation theorem is proved for a “resampling” (data-splitting per iteration) variant to simplify dependence across iterates. While standard in some analyses, this leaves a gap to the practical single-sample algorithm. Please clarify whether your proofs can be extended to the standard reuse-of-data EM (e.g., via uniform concentration over iterates, covering arguments, or stability/martingale tools), or provide empirical evidence that resampling is not necessary in practice.
- Initialization: Guarantees require an initializer within a constant relative error basin. Practical, high-dimensional initializers are not detailed. For the considered models, specify concrete, computationally feasible initialization schemes (e.g., method-of-moments/tensor for a coarse start, spectral clustering, or thresholded method-of-moments) along with conditions ensuring they land in the basin.
- Choice of truncation level b_s: The theory assumes b_s is of the same order as s*. How sensitive are results to mis-specification? Can you propose a data-driven rule (e.g., path over b_s with information criteria, stability selection, or thresholding by universal/Benjamini–Hochberg-like rules)? A robustness study would strengthen the practical message.
- Assumptions for inference: Asymptotic normality relies on sparsity of the orthogonalization vector w* (s_w* = o(√(n/log d))) and bounded eigenvalues of Fisher information. While common in de-biasing literature, these may be strong for mixture models where Fisher blocks can be dense or ill-conditioned. It would help to (i) discuss when s_w* is small for the two models, (ii) quantify SNR/separation required for Condition 4.x and Assumption 4.5, and (iii) comment on the feasibility and numerical stability of estimating T_n in practice.
- Model specifics: In the Gaussian mixture example, is σ² assumed known? If not, how is it estimated and incorporated in the M-step and in T_n? Also, the proof sketches rely on local strong concavity of Q(·; θ*), which is nontrivial in mixtures; a clearer statement of separation/SNR conditions and their impact on constants (e.g., ρ) would help.

Clarity
- Overall presentation is technically solid but dense. A high-level roadmap figure and a brief intuition for why the truncation step does not derail EM’s contraction would aid accessibility. The role and construction of T_n and its relationship to the Fisher information (Theorem 2.1) are useful—consider moving some of that intuition from the appendix into the main text.
- Some references appear incomplete or have typos; please proofread the bibliography and unify notation (there are occasional formatting artifacts in the equations).
- Explicitly separate assumptions required for the estimation theorem vs. those unique to inference.

Originality
- The combination of EM with iterative hard-thresholding-like truncation for high-dimensional latent variable models and a de-correlated score built from the Q-function appears novel. It would be fair to discuss connections to iterative hard thresholding/HTP and sparse EM variants (e.g., ℓ1-penalized EM) to position the truncation step relative to penalization approaches (Städler et al., Khalili & Chen).
- Achieving semiparametric efficiency in latent-variable settings with high-dimensional nuisance is a strong originality point.

Significance
- If solidly validated, the framework could influence practice for a broad class of latent models where convex surrogates are unavailable. The ability to provide both rates and valid inference is valuable.
- However, the lack of empirical evaluation is a notable limitation for NeurIPS. Demonstrations on simulated Gaussian mixtures/mixtures of regressions and comparisons to ℓ1-penalized EM, tensor methods, and low-dimensional EM (when applicable) would substantiate the claims and illuminate constants and finite-sample behavior.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide a non-resampling analysis or at least experiments comparing resampled vs. standard HD-EM.
- Specify practical initialization methods and conditions ensuring basin entry.
- Propose and test data-driven tuning for b_s and the Dantzig selector’s λ.
- Clarify treatment of unknown variance and discuss computational complexity per iteration (e.g., cost of sorting for truncation, solving the Dantzig LP).
- Add experiments illustrating estimation accuracy, support recovery, and coverage/power of the score/Wald tests under varying n, d, s*, and SNR.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with theoretically strong and conceptually elegant results.
- Provides both estimation and inference, with optimal rates and semiparametric efficiency.
- General framework applicable to multiple latent-variable models and both exact and gradient M-steps.

Arguments against acceptance
- Resampling-based analysis leaves a gap to the practical algorithm.
- Heavy assumptions (good initialization, knowledge of sparsity level, sparsity of w*) without concrete practical guidance.
- No empirical validation.

Recommendation
Borderline reject (leaning to weak accept if empirical results and practical guidance are added). The theoretical contributions are strong and potentially impactful, but the paper would benefit from bridging the theory–practice gap with experiments, clearer initialization/tuning strategies, and a discussion of extending the analysis beyond the resampling setting.
