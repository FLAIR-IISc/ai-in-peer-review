**Summary:**
This paper develops a theoretical framework for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional settings (d >> n), where standard theory does not apply. The authors propose a "high-dimensional EM algorithm" which augments the classical EM steps with a truncation (T-step) that enforces sparsity on the parameter estimate at each iteration. The paper's two main contributions are: 1) establishing that this algorithm, under certain conditions and with a suitable initialization, converges geometrically to a solution that achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d)/n)`; and 2) proposing a decorrelated score statistic for performing asymptotic inference (e.g., hypothesis testing) on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter, and proving its asymptotic normality. The theoretical framework is presented generally and its application is illustrated for Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance and Ambition:** The paper tackles a long-standing and critical problem: the lack of theoretical guarantees for the widely-used EM algorithm in the high-dimensional regime. The scope is ambitious, providing a unified analysis for both point estimation (computational and statistical rates) and uncertainty quantification (asymptotic inference), which is a comprehensive theoretical contribution.
2.  **Novelty of Results:** To my knowledge, this is the first work to establish near-optimal estimation rates and asymptotic normality for a computationally tractable EM-type algorithm for general latent variable models in a high-dimensional setting. Proving such results for a local optimum of a non-concave likelihood function, rather than a global optimum of a convex one, is a significant technical achievement.
3.  **General Framework:** The analysis is presented in a general form, based on a set of verifiable conditions, and accommodates both exact maximization and approximate gradient-ascent M-steps. This makes the framework potentially applicable to a broad class of latent variable models beyond the examples provided.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the impressive theoretical contributions, the paper suffers from a number of methodological gaps, lack of clarity in key areas, and formatting inconsistencies that detract from its overall quality.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   A critical point is that the main theoretical result for estimation (Theorem 3.4) is established not for Algorithm 1 as presented, but for a "resampling version" (Algorithm 4) detailed in the appendix. The main text mentions this only in passing ("To simplify the technical analysis..."). This is a crucial detail that significantly changes the context of the result. An algorithm that uses fresh data at each iteration is fundamentally different from one that iterates on a fixed dataset. The paper provides no justification for why the analysis of the resampling version should extend to the practical, non-resampling version. This lack of transparency is a major flaw.
    *   The paper repeatedly defers critical information—such as the verification of its core conditions for specific models and the details of the resampling algorithm—to the appendix or a "longer version of this paper". While appendix material is standard, the current manuscript feels incomplete and difficult to assess on its own merits.
    *   There are minor but distracting formatting and notational issues. The research support footnote `*` is not attached to any author. The reference list shows inconsistent formatting (e.g., some author names are in full, others are initials). Notation for the truncation operator is defined within the algorithm block rather than in the preceding text. The statement in Section 2.2 that `bθ` can be used instead of `bθ_0` in the score statistic with no change to the theoretical results is presented without justification.

2.  **Methodological and Theoretical Gaps:**
    *   **The Initialization Problem:** The entire theory relies on the existence of an initial estimator `init` that is already within a constant factor of the true parameter `θ*` in `l2`-norm. This is a very strong requirement. The paper offers no method, discussion, or citation for how to obtain such an initializer for high-dimensional latent variable models. This is a well-known bottleneck for EM theory, and ignoring it renders the practical relevance of the algorithm and its guarantees questionable.
    *   **Oracle Tuning Parameters:** The proposed algorithm and inference procedure depend on several tuning parameters (`bs`, `T`, `λ`). The theoretical analysis provides prescriptions for these parameters (e.g., Eq. 3.8, 4.5) that depend on unknown, true model quantities such as the sparsity `s*` and properties of the Fisher information matrix. The paper does not discuss how these could be selected in a data-driven manner (e.g., via cross-validation), which is essential for any practical application.
    *   **Strength and Plausibility of Assumptions:** The theory for inference (Section 4) hinges on Assumption 4.5, which includes a sparsity assumption on the vector `w*` (related to the inverse of a submatrix of the Fisher information). This type of assumption, while used in other high-dimensional inference literature, is very strong. The paper provides no justification for why one should expect this structure to hold for the latent variable models under consideration. Furthermore, the sample size requirements in (4.6) are highly complex and asymptotic, giving little intuition for finite-sample performance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a highly significant open problem in statistical machine learning.
*   The theoretical results on estimation rates and asymptotic inference for a local EM solution are novel, technically deep, and represent a clear advance in the field.
*   The general framework has the potential for broad impact on the analysis of algorithms for latent variable models.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The analysis is performed on a resampling-based algorithm, while the paper presents a standard (non-resampling) algorithm, creating a significant disconnect between theory and practice.
*   The assumption of a high-quality initial estimate is a critical gap that is not addressed, severely limiting the applicability of the results.
*   The reliance on oracle tuning parameters and strong, unmotivated assumptions (e.g., sparse `w*`) further distances the theory from practical implementation.
*   Numerous issues with clarity and presentation make the paper feel incomplete and not yet ready for publication.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents novel and significant theoretical ideas, the combination of a major disconnect between the presented algorithm and the one analyzed, the unaddressed problem of initialization, and the reliance on oracle assumptions prevents me from recommending acceptance. The work is promising, but in its current state, it is not a complete piece of work and its claims are not fully supported in a practical context. I therefore recommend **rejection**. The authors should focus on bridging the gap between their theory and a practical algorithm by addressing the initialization and tuning parameter issues, and by clarifying the relationship between the resampling analysis and the standard EM procedure.
