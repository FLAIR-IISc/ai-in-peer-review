This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for parameter estimation and statistical inference for high-dimensional latent variable models using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The core problem addressed is the lack of theoretical guarantees for the widely-used EM algorithm in the high-dimensional regime where the dimension `d` is much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors make two main contributions. First, they propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the standard EM algorithm by adding a hard-thresholding (truncation) step after each M-step to enforce sparsity. They prove that, under certain conditions and with a suitable initialization, this algorithm converges at a geometric rate to an estimator achieving a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d) / n)`. This work directly extends the low-dimensional analysis of Balakrishnan et al. [2] to the sparse high-dimensional setting. Second, for statistical inference, the authors develop a "decorrelated score test" for low-dimensional components of the parameter vector. This adapts the debiasing/desparsifying ideas from the high-dimensional generalized linear model literature (e.g., [26, 32]) to the challenging context of latent variable models where the estimator is the output of an iterative algorithm for a non-concave problem, rather than the global optimum of a convex one. They establish the asymptotic normality of their proposed test statistic. The framework is demonstrated on high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and important problem. The EM algorithm is a workhorse for fitting latent variable models, yet its behavior in the prevalent high-dimensional setting has remained poorly understood from a theoretical standpoint. This work provides the first comprehensive theoretical guarantees for both optimal parameter estimation and valid statistical inference for an EM-type algorithm in this regime. This is a significant step towards bridging the gap between theory and practice for a broad class of models.

2.  **Originality:** The contributions are highly original. While building upon the low-dimensional EM analysis of [2] and the high-dimensional inference literature for convex models, the synthesis is non-trivial and technically innovative. The introduction of the truncation step within the EM iterations and the corresponding analysis are novel. More impressively, the development of a valid inferential procedure for a local solution of a non-concave problem is a major conceptual advance over existing methods that rely on the KKT conditions of a convex global optimum (like the Lasso).

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper appears to be technically sound and the theoretical results are strong. The authors provide a unified framework that cleanly separates the optimization error (which decays geometrically) from the statistical error (which achieves the near-optimal rate). The proof of asymptotic normality for the decorrelated score statistic is a key achievement. The conditions required by the theory are laid out explicitly and are verified for two non-trivial and important examples, lending credibility to the framework's generality.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the proposed algorithm is presented straightforwardly, and the main theoretical results are stated precisely in Theorems 3.4 and 4.6. The authors do a good job of situating their work within the relevant literature and highlighting their key departures and contributions.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions (for improvement):**

1.  **Initialization:** The theory hinges on the availability of an initial estimator `init` that is already in a basin of attraction, i.e., within `R/2` of the true parameter `theta*` in `l2`-norm. This is a strong assumption and a potential limitation for the practical application of the results. The paper does not provide guidance on how to obtain such an initial estimator. I would strongly encourage the authors to add a discussion on this point. Could spectral methods or tensor-based methods (which the authors contrast with) be used to find a suitable initialization? Discussing potential initialization strategies and whether they are likely to meet the theoretical requirements would significantly strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

2.  **Analysis of the Resampling Version:** The theoretical analysis is performed for a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4 in the appendix), where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a standard technique for simplifying proofs by ensuring independence across iterations. However, the practical algorithm (Algorithm 1) uses the full dataset at each step. The authors should be more transparent about this discrepancy in the main text. A short discussion justifying why the theoretical results derived for the resampling scheme are expected to be a good proxy for the practical algorithm would be very valuable for the reader.

3.  **Tuning Parameter Selection:** The proposed method requires specifying tuning parameters: the sparsity level `bs` for the algorithm and the regularization parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector in the inference procedure. The theoretical guidance (e.g., Eq. 3.8 and 4.5) depends on unknown quantities like the true sparsity `s*` and properties of the Fisher information matrix. The paper would be improved by a discussion of practical methods for selecting these parameters, such as cross-validation, and the potential theoretical challenges associated with data-driven tuning.

4.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a strong theory paper, the claims would be more compelling with some numerical simulations. Even a small-scale simulation study on one of the example models (e.g., Gaussian mixtures) could: (a) demonstrate that the estimation error of the proposed algorithm scales with `n`, `d`, and `s*` as predicted by Theorem 3.4, and (b) show that the empirical distribution of the decorrelated score statistic under the null hypothesis is indeed close to a standard normal distribution, validating Theorem 4.6. This would provide valuable evidence that the asymptotic theory provides a good description of the method's behavior in finite samples.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a substantial theoretical contribution to a core topic in machine learning and statistics. The authors provide a novel and powerful framework for analyzing the EM algorithm in the challenging high-dimensional setting, offering strong guarantees for both estimation and inference. The originality and significance of the results are high. While the weaknesses regarding initialization, the resampling analysis, and the lack of experiments are not insignificant, they represent common challenges in this area of theoretical research and are addressable. The paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and is likely to be highly influential for future research on computational and statistical trade-offs in non-convex optimization. I recommend acceptance.
