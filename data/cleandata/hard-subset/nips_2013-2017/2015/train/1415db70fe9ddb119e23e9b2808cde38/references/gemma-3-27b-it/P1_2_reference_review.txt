## Peer Review: High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, where the number of parameters *d* is significantly larger than the sample size *n*.  Existing theoretical work is largely limited to the low-dimensional setting. The authors propose a novel “high-dimensional EM” algorithm, incorporating a truncation step to enforce sparsity, and develop a comprehensive inferential theory providing statistical and computational guarantees. Specifically, they demonstrate convergence to a statistically optimal rate of p√s* log(d/n) for parameter estimation and establish asymptotic normality for a decorrelated score statistic for testing hypotheses related to low-dimensional components. The framework is general, applicable to models like Gaussian Mixture Models and Mixtures of Regression, and accommodates both exact and gradient ascent M-step implementations.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds on recent progress in understanding the EM algorithm’s convergence properties (Balakrishnan et al., 2019, and references therein). However, it significantly extends these results to the high-dimensional regime, a critical gap in the literature. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches (e.g., those based on tensor methods) and position their work as a unique contribution addressing both estimation *and* inference in this setting. They acknowledge and differentiate their work from related areas like high-dimensional inference in generalized linear models (e.g., using Lasso) and highlight the novel challenges posed by latent variable models and the non-concavity of their likelihood functions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a Crucial Gap:** The paper tackles a significant open problem in the theoretical foundations of latent variable modeling – understanding EM in high dimensions.
*   **Strong Theoretical Framework:** The authors develop a rigorous mathematical framework, providing convergence rates and asymptotic normality results.  The conditions imposed appear reasonable and are, in principle, verifiable.
*   **Novel Algorithm:** The proposed truncated EM algorithm offers a practical approach to leveraging sparsity in high-dimensional models.
*   **General Applicability:** The framework’s generality (applicable to different M-step implementations and multiple models) is a substantial strength.
*   **Statistical Optimality:** Establishing the asymptotic optimality of the proposed score test is a key contribution.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough and well-structured analysis, including careful consideration of the statistical and computational trade-offs.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Complexity:** The paper is mathematically dense and will be challenging for readers without a strong background in statistical optimization and high-dimensional statistics. While thorough, the numerous conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) can be overwhelming.  A more intuitive explanation of these conditions, beyond their formal statement, would be beneficial.
*   **Constant Factors:** The convergence rates and asymptotic properties are expressed with constant factors (C, C0, etc.). The dependence of these constants on various parameters (e.g., sparsity level, signal-to-noise ratio) isn't always explicitly stated, making it difficult to assess the practical implications of the results.
*   **Implementation Details:**  While the algorithms are presented, practical considerations for implementing the truncation step and choosing the sparsity parameter *bs* are not fully discussed.  Guidance on these aspects would enhance the paper’s applicability.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical results. While the authors mention applying the framework to Gaussian Mixture Models and Mixtures of Regression, there’s a lack of empirical validation – even a small simulation study demonstrating the performance of the algorithm and the validity of the asymptotic normality results would significantly strengthen the work.
* **Condition 4.6:** Condition 4.6 is rather stringent and requires that the sparsity level of the true parameter w* be sufficiently small (w* = o(sqrt(n/log n)). This limits the applicability of the theorem as in many realistic scenarios the true parameter has a large number of non-zero elements.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Significant Theoretical Advance:** The paper presents a substantial theoretical contribution, filling a critical gap in the literature on the EM algorithm.
*   **Potential for Impact:** The results have the potential to impact the development of more robust and reliable latent variable models in high-dimensional settings.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The proposed algorithm and inferential procedure are well-motivated and theoretically justified.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **High Technical Barrier:** The paper’s complexity may limit its accessibility to a broad audience.
*   **Lack of Empirical Evidence:** The absence of empirical validation weakens the confidence in the practical relevance of the results.
* **Stringent condition 4.6**


**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant theoretical contribution to the field. While the technical complexity and lack of empirical validation are weaknesses, the importance of the problem addressed, the rigor of the analysis, and the potential impact of the results outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance contingent on the following minor revisions:

1.  **Clarify Technical Conditions:** Provide more intuitive explanations of the technical conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5).
2.  **Discuss Constant Factors:** Discuss the dependence of the constant factors in the convergence rates and asymptotic properties on key parameters.
3.  **Address Implementation Issues:** Provide some guidance on practical considerations for implementing the truncation step and choosing the sparsity parameter *bs*.
4. **Discuss Condition 4.6** Explain why this condition is necessary and its impact.
5.  **Consider Adding Simulation Results:** If possible, include a small simulation study to demonstrate the algorithm’s performance and validate the asymptotic results.



**Confidence:** 8/10
