### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for parameter estimation and statistical inference in high-dimensional latent variable models. The authors identify a critical gap between the computational reality of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which typically finds local optima, and the existing statistical theory, which often assumes access to a global optimum. This is particularly problematic in the high-dimensional (d >> n) setting, where the standard EM algorithm lacks any guarantees.

To address this, the authors propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm," which modifies the standard EM by introducing a truncation (T-step) after the maximization (M-step) to enforce sparsity at each iteration. Their main contributions are twofold:

1.  **Estimation:** They prove that, with a suitable sparse initialization, their algorithm converges at a geometric rate. The final estimator achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of approximately `sqrt(s* log(d) / n)`, where `s*` is the true sparsity level. This is a significant improvement over the `sqrt(d/n)` rate from low-dimensional analyses, which is meaningless when d > n.
2.  **Inference:** They develop a "decorrelated score test" for conducting hypothesis tests on individual components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. They prove this statistic is asymptotically normal, with a limiting variance that achieves the optimal semiparametric information bound. This provides a valid method for uncertainty quantification (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) based on the output of their algorithm, which is not a global optimum of a convex program.

The framework is shown to be applicable to important models like high-dimensional Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The paper tackles a long-standing and important problem at the intersection of computational methods and statistical theory for latent variable models in the high-dimensional regime. Providing a computationally tractable algorithm with rigorous guarantees for both estimation and inference is a major step forward.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Theoretical Results:** The paper delivers strong theoretical guarantees. The geometric convergence rate and the near-optimal statistical rate for the estimator are state-of-the-art. More impressively, the paper provides a framework for valid asymptotic inference, which is a much harder problem, especially given that the estimator is a local optimum of a non-convex likelihood.
3.  **Novel and Insightful Analysis:** The technical approach is innovative. The authors successfully extend the view of EM as a perturbed gradient method [2] to the high-dimensional setting. This requires a much finer-grained analysis (controlling the `l_inf`-norm of the statistical error) to handle the effects of the novel truncation step. The adaptation of decorrelation/debiasing techniques from the high-dimensional linear model literature to the non-convex, latent variable setting is highly non-trivial and a significant technical contribution.
4.  **Generality:** The framework is presented through a set of general conditions, which are then verified for specific models. This suggests the approach could be applied to a wider class of high-dimensional latent variable problems beyond the examples given.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **The Initialization Assumption:** The theory critically relies on the existence of an initial estimator that is already within a constant relative error of the true parameter. This is a very strong assumption and a common, yet significant, limitation of theoretical analyses for non-convex optimization. The paper does not discuss practical methods for obtaining such an initialization, which remains a challenging open problem in itself (e.g., via tensor methods, which the paper notes have suboptimal rates).
2.  **Reliance on a Resampling Algorithm for Proofs:** The main theoretical results (Theorem 3.4) are proven for a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4 in the appendix), where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a proof technique used to ensure independence and simplify the analysis. However, it is not the algorithm that would be used in practice (Algorithm 1). The paper does not explicitly state whether the results hold for the practical, non-resampling version, which is an important gap between the theory and practice.
3.  **Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm and inference procedure depend on tuning parameters, namely the sparsity level `bs` for the T-step and the parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector. The paper provides theoretical guidance on their scaling but omits any discussion of how to select them in practice (e.g., via cross-validation), which is essential for practitioners.
4.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** The main results are stated under a set of abstract technical conditions (e.g., Lipschitz-Gradient, Concavity-Smoothness). While this makes the theorems general, it can obscure the actual requirements on the model. For instance, the verification for the GMM example shows that some of these conditions depend on a sufficiently high signal-to-noise ratio, which is a strong assumption.

### **Detailed Comments on Reviewing Criteria**

**Quality:** The paper is of very high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, deep, and appears to be sound. The decomposition of the estimation error into optimization and statistical components is clean and powerful. The development of an inferential procedure for a local optimum of a non-convex problem is a top-tier technical achievement. The claims are well-supported by detailed proofs in the appendix. This is a complete and substantial piece of theoretical work.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but it is extremely dense. The core ideas are presented clearly in the introduction and methodology sections. However, the intuition behind key components, such as the decorrelated score test, is deferred to the appendix. The reliance on abstract conditions in the main body makes the paper hard to parse for those not deeply familiar with the area, as one must dig into the appendix to understand the concrete model assumptions. The notation is necessarily complex but is mostly standard. A minor suggestion would be to briefly discuss the resampling algorithm and its relation to the practical algorithm in the main text.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds upon existing lines of research—the analysis of EM in low dimensions [2] and high-dimensional inference for convex models [26, 32]—its contribution is far from incremental. The synthesis of these ideas and their extension to handle the interplay between the EM updates, high-dimensional statistical noise, and the non-convex landscape is novel. The proposed algorithm with its truncation step, and the associated theory for both estimation and inference, is a first for this class of problems. The paper correctly positions itself against related work and clearly articulates its unique contributions.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is very high. It provides the first computationally feasible approach with strong, end-to-end theoretical backing for estimation and uncertainty quantification in high-dimensional latent variable models. These models are widely used in many fields (e.g., computational biology, economics, machine learning). This work provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for using the EM algorithm in modern data settings. It is highly likely that researchers will build upon this framework, either by applying it to new models or by working to relax its assumptions (e.g., the initialization requirement). It substantially advances the state of the art.

In conclusion, this is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a foundational contribution to a challenging and important area. Despite the strong assumptions regarding initialization, it provides a landmark result that bridges computation and theory for high-dimensional latent variable models.
