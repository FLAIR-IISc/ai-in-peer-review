Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm that integrates a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after each M-step (or gradient M-step) to enforce sparsity along the entire trajectory. Under suitable initialization and local regularity conditions on the population Q-function, the authors establish (i) simultaneous computational and statistical guarantees: geometric decay of optimization error and a near-minimax statistical rate O(√(s* log d / n)) up to log factors; and (ii) an inferential framework based on a decorrelated score constructed from Qn and a Dantzig estimate of the orthogonalization vector, yielding asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency for low-dimensional components in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. The theory covers both exact and gradient implementations of the M-step and is illustrated on Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions.

This work explicitly builds on the population-to-sample EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (low-dimensional), extends the debiasing/desparsification literature (Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.) to latent variable models, and contrasts with penalized EM approaches (e.g., Städler, Bühlmann & van de Geer) and tensor methods. The identities connecting rQn and Tn to the score and Fisher information are classical in EM (Fisher’s identity; Louis’ missing information principle) and are leveraged to justify the inference construction for latent models.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A simple but powerful idea—injecting a truncation step into EM—to maintain sparsity throughout iterations and enable sharp, entrywise error control. This is a clean bridge between iterative hard-thresholding and EM.
- Unified computation-statistics analysis: Clear separation of optimization vs. statistical error; geometric convergence plus near-minimax statistical rate that scales with s* rather than d.
- Generality: Framework covers both exact and gradient M-steps; conditions are verified (at least outlined) for two canonical latent-variable settings (GMM and mixture regression). The results plausibly extend to other latent models.
- Inference contribution: Decorrelated score for latent-variable models with high-dimensional nuisance parameters, achieving the semiparametric information bound. This is a meaningful advance beyond point estimation.
- Clarity of the main message: The bounds explicitly show how truncation, initialization, and statistical fluctuations interact; the roles of Lipschitz/concavity and sample-splitting are transparent.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practicality of resampling/split-EM: The main convergence theorem is proved for a resampling variant (fresh sample per iteration). This simplifies analysis but can be statistically and computationally wasteful and is not reflective of practice. Is there a no-resampling guarantee (with uniform concentration) or at least an iterate-averaged variant?
- Initialization: The results require an initializer within a constant L2-relative error. How to obtain such an initializer under high-dimensional latent models without supervision? Are tensor/spectral initializers or method-of-moments feasible at high d with s* ≪ d? Please make this concrete per model.
- Tuning and knowledge of sparsity: The bounds require bs of the same order as s*, and additional tuning for the Dantzig selector and step-size. How sensitive is the method to mis-specified bs? Can one propose a data-driven rule (e.g., cross-validation, information criteria, or stability selection) compatible with the theory?
- Assumptions for inference: The sparsity of w* (nodewise regression analogue) and numerous small-o conditions are standard but somewhat opaque. It would help to specialize these to concrete scaling laws (s*, s_w*, d, n) for each model so the feasibility region is explicit.
- Missing classical citations: Theorem 2.1 follows from Fisher’s identity and Louis (1982). Please cite these to position the contribution correctly.
- Empirical validation: The submission emphasizes theory; the provided text does not show experiments. NeurIPS readers will expect simulations comparing (i) EM+T-step vs L1-penalized EM and tensor methods, (ii) exact vs gradient M-step, (iii) coverage/Type I error of the decorrelated score under finite n, and (iv) sensitivity to initialization and bs.
- Scope of models: While the framework is claimed general, only two models are worked out. A third example (e.g., mixed GLMs or HMMs) would further substantiate generality.

Quality
- The technical development is careful, with clear decomposition of errors and explicit dependence on model constants. Some proofs rely on strong local regularity (concavity/smoothness in a basin), SNR separation, and good initialization—reasonable for EM analyses, but limitations should be highlighted.
- The inference section is sound and aligns with established high-dimensional inference techniques adapted via EM identities.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, though notation is heavy. A figure/pseudocode summary of the three-step iteration and a roadmap of assumptions/implications per model would help. Please fix typographical issues and ensure consistent notation for gradients/Hessians.
- Make the dependence on log n factors explicit whenever possible.

Originality
- The synthesis—hard-thresholded EM with provable high-dimensional rates and semiparametric efficient inference for latent models—is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. It advances beyond penalized EM analyses and low-dimensional EM theory.

Significance
- The results could become a template for high-dimensional estimation and inference in latent variable models where global convex programs are unavailable. If complemented by empirical evidence and broadened model coverage, this will be impactful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a no-resampling analysis (or at least a discussion) using uniform deviation bounds to bring theory closer to practice.
- Detail initialization strategies and their provable guarantees under your assumptions.
- Add experiments for GMM and mixture regression demonstrating estimation error, support recovery, and valid inference (coverage/Type I error), comparing to L1-EM and tensor baselines.
- Discuss robustness to model misspecification and heavy tails; can truncation be combined with robust gradients?
- Cite Fisher’s identity and Louis (1982) for the score/Hessian identities.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and bold approach bridging a theoretical gap for EM in high dimensions.
- Strong, general theory with optimal rates and semiparametric efficient inference.
- Broad applicability and clear potential impact.

Cons against acceptance
- Reliance on resampling for theory; unclear practical performance without it.
- Heavy assumptions (initialization, w* sparsity) and multiple tuning knobs.
- Limited empirical validation in the current form.

Recommendation
Weak accept leaning to accept. The conceptual contribution is significant and opens a promising direction; I’m willing to overlook some incompleteness in experiments if the camera-ready addresses practicality (no-resampling discussion), adds empirical validation, and clarifies initialization and tuning.
