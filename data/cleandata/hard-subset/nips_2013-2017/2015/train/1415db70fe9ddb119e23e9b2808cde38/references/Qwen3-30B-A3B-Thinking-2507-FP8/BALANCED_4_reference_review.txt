

# Review of "High Dimensional EM Algorithm: Statistical Optimization and Asymptotic Normality"

## Summary and Context
This paper proposes a high-dimensional extension of the EM algorithm for latent variable models, addressing the significant gap in theoretical guarantees for high-dimensional regimes where d >> n. The authors introduce a truncation step to enforce sparsity in both E-step and M-step, enabling the establishment of near-optimal statistical rates of convergence and asymptotic normality for the decorrelated score statistic. The work builds upon recent low-dimensional EM analyses [2, 18, 25, 30] but extends them to high-dimensional settings, which is a substantial contribution given the increasing importance of high-dimensional latent variable models in modern applications.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first comprehensive inferential framework for high-dimensional EM algorithms. The truncation step is a clever and novel approach to handle high dimensionality, distinguishing this work from previous low-dimensional analyses.

2. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper provides thorough theoretical guarantees, including geometric convergence rates and near-minimax optimal statistical rates (O(√(s* log d/n))). The decorrelated score statistic achieves the semiparametric information bound, demonstrating theoretical optimality.

3. **Comprehensive Framework**: The unified framework applies to multiple latent variable models (Gaussian mixtures, mixture of regressions), with detailed verification of conditions for specific models in the appendix. The separation of computational and statistical guarantees is particularly well-executed.

4. **Clarity and Structure**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear notation, well-motivated theorems, and detailed proofs in the appendix. The connection between the classical EM algorithm and the high-dimensional extension is well-explained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper lacks empirical results demonstrating the practical performance of the proposed algorithm on real or simulated datasets. While theoretical guarantees are strong, the practical utility would be better established with experiments showing how the algorithm performs compared to alternatives.

2. **Strong Assumptions**: The decorrelated score inference requires strong assumptions (e.g., sparsity of w* in equation 4.1). While these are standard in high-dimensional inference, the paper could better motivate why these assumptions are reasonable for the specific models considered.

3. **Limited Discussion of Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't address practical considerations for implementation, such as sensitivity to the choice of the sparsity parameter bs, or how to select it in practice. The relationship between the theoretical requirements and practical implementation could be strengthened.

4. **Comparison with Alternative High-Dimensional Methods**: While the paper contrasts with low-dimensional EM work, it could more thoroughly discuss how the proposed method compares with other high-dimensional approaches (e.g., tensor methods [1, 9], or other high-dimensional inference techniques [26, 32]).

## Constructive Feedback

1. **Add Empirical Validation**: Including simulation studies would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors could demonstrate how the high-dimensional EM algorithm performs compared to existing methods in terms of estimation accuracy, computational efficiency, and inference quality.

2. **Clarify Practical Implementation**: The paper should discuss practical implementation details, including how to choose the sparsity parameter bs, and provide guidelines for practitioners. A brief discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm would also be valuable.

3. **Strengthen Assumption Justification**: For the decorrelated score inference, the paper could provide more intuition for why the sparsity assumption on w* is reasonable in the context of latent variable models. The authors might also explore how the method behaves when this assumption is violated.

4. **Expand Comparison with Related Work**: The paper could more thoroughly discuss the relationship with tensor-based methods [1, 9], which also address high-dimensional latent variable models but with different theoretical guarantees. A clear comparison of the statistical rates and computational requirements would help contextualize the contribution.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the literature on high-dimensional latent variable models. The proposed high-dimensional EM algorithm with truncation provides the first comprehensive theoretical framework for both estimation and inference in high-dimensional settings, with near-optimal statistical rates and optimal inference properties.

While the theoretical results are strong, the lack of empirical validation limits the paper's impact. The authors should address this in a revised version by adding simulation studies demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed algorithm. The paper would also benefit from more detailed discussion of implementation considerations and a more thorough comparison with alternative high-dimensional methods.

Given the paper's theoretical novelty and significance, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to address the empirical validation gap and strengthen the practical implementation discussion. The work represents an important step toward closing the computational-statistical gap in high-dimensional latent variable models.
