Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional latent-variable models and proposes a sparsity-aware variant of the EM algorithm that appends a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after each M-step to enforce sparsity. Two M-step implementations are covered: exact maximization and a single gradient-ascent update. Under population smoothness/strong-concavity and Lipschitz-gradient conditions (localized to a basin around the truth) and a sample-to-population l1 error bound for the M-step mapping, the authors establish a nonasymptotic bound for the iterate error that decomposes into (i) a geometric “optimization” term and (ii) a “statistical” term scaling as sqrt(s* log d / n) (up to logs). Building on the EM identity linking derivatives of Q to the score and Fisher information, they construct a decorrelated score statistic via a Dantzig program for the nuisance projection; they prove asymptotic normality with variance attaining the semiparametric efficiency bound, enabling valid testing/confidence intervals for low-dimensional components. The framework is instantiated for two canonical latent models (two-component spherical Gaussian mixtures with known variance and mixtures of linear regressions), with verification of the technical conditions and explicit rates under SNR conditions.

Relation to prior work
The work extends the population-to-sample EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al. by incorporating high-dimensional sparsity via truncation, reminiscent of iterative hard-thresholding ideas, and by switching from l2 to l1 control of sample-to-population deviations. It differs from l1-penalized EM approaches (e.g., Städler et al., and recent analyses for penalized mixture regression) by analyzing a nonconvex, non-penalized procedure with explicit iterate sparsification. The inference component adapts decorrelated/debiased-score methodology (van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang) to latent-variable models by exploiting EM identities; to my knowledge, asymptotic normality and efficiency for EM-based estimators in high-dimensional latent models has not been established previously. Tensor-method literature is appropriately contrasted.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic idea with broad appeal: a simple T-step added to EM yields sparse iterates and enables near-optimal estimation rates.
- Nonasymptotic computational-statistical guarantees with explicit geometric contraction plus optimal (up to logs) sqrt(s log d / n) error; coverage of both exact and gradient M-steps.
- Inference contribution is substantial: decorrelated score built from Q/Hessian quantities achieves semiparametric efficiency in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance, beyond GLMs.
- Technical novelties include l1 control of the sample vs. population M-step mappings and careful tracking of support evolution under truncation.
- Concrete verification for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions, with explicit SNR regimes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Resampling analysis: The convergence theorem is proved for a “resampled EM” that uses fresh data at each iteration. This avoids dependence between iterates and the empirical process but diverges from practice. No theory is provided for the standard single-sample iterate sequence; this is a significant gap.
- Initialization: Results require an initializer within a constant relative error of θ*. In high dimensions and for nonconvex latent models, obtaining such an initializer with guarantees is nontrivial. Practical, theoretically justified initialization strategies (e.g., sparse spectral/tensor methods) are not provided.
- Tuning and practicality: The truncation level bs must be chosen on the order of the (unknown) true sparsity s*. Guidance for data-driven selection, sensitivity to mis-specification (over/under truncation), step-size choice, and stopping rules is limited. Conditions (3.8)–(3.9) are opaque and difficult to check in practice.
- Assumptions: The sparsity of the “decorrelation” vector w* is strong and model-dependent; feasibility of estimating w* via a Dantzig program hinges on this. The mixture examples assume known variance and balanced components with high SNR; extensions to unknown weights/covariances or weaker separation are not discussed.
- Computational burden for inference: Computing Tn (involving Hessians of Q) and solving a high-dimensional Dantzig program adds nontrivial cost, with little discussion of scalability or numerics.
- Scope vs claims: While the framework is positioned as general, only two models are fully treated. It would be valuable to see verification for other latent structures (e.g., HMMs, mixed membership/topic models) or a clear statement of limits.
- Empirical validation: No experiments are presented to assess finite-sample behavior, robustness to tuning and initialization, or comparisons against l1-penalized EM/tensor methods. For NeurIPS, this undermines practical significance.
- Clarity and presentation: The notation is heavy; several conditions and constants are introduced without intuition. Some references appear garbled/incomplete; a careful pass is needed. The main text could benefit from proof sketches and clearer guidance on verifying assumptions.

Quality
Technically solid overall, with careful nonasymptotic analysis and a novel inference component. However, the reliance on resampling, strong initialization and sparsity assumptions, and lack of empirical evidence weaken the practical impact.

Clarity
Readable for experts but dense; several conditions could be better motivated. The paper would benefit from a roadmap figure of the algorithm and assumptions, and from consolidating constants. The references need cleaning.

Originality
Moderate to high. The hard-thresholded EM with l1-based perturbation control and the decorrelated-score inference for latent-variable EM estimators are, in combination, novel and valuable.

Significance
Potentially high: if the standard (non-resampled) EM analysis can be established and practical guidance plus experiments are added, this could become a reference for high-dimensional latent-variable inference. As it stands, the impact is more theoretical.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theory for the standard EM without resampling (e.g., via leave-one-out stability/martingale arguments).
- Present a concrete, theoretically justified initialization scheme in the high-dimensional latent setting.
- Offer data-driven procedures for bs and step-size selection, with sensitivity analysis.
- Extend the mixture results to unknown weights/variances or discuss identifiability/label-switching more explicitly.
- Include simulations comparing to l1-penalized EM and tensor methods, evaluating estimation error and inference coverage.
- Discuss computational complexity and practical computation of Tn and the Dantzig step; consider scalable approximations.
- Improve exposition and references.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, original theory at the intersection of EM, sparsity, and inference.
- Semiparametric efficiency for low-dimensional components in latent models.
- Clear, simple algorithm easy to adopt.

Cons for acceptance
- No experiments; limited practical guidance.
- Resampling assumption; lack of guarantees for standard EM iterates.
- Strong assumptions (initializer, SNR, sparsity of w*) limit applicability.

Overall: A promising theoretical contribution; with added empirical validation and addressing the resampling gap, it would be a strong NeurIPS paper.
