Summary
This paper studies high-dimensional latent variable models and proposes a “high-dimensional EM” algorithm that augments each EM iteration with a hard-thresholding (truncation) step to enforce sparsity. The authors analyze two variants (exact M-step and gradient M-step), proving geometric contraction of the optimization error within a basin of attraction, and a statistical error of order sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (up to log factors), where s* is the sparsity of the true parameter. On top of the estimator, they construct a decorrelated score for inference on low-dimensional components and show asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency (efficient information bound), under a set of conditions. The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixture models and mixtures of regressions, under separation/SNR and other regularity conditions. The analysis leverages population- vs sample-level Q-functions, Lipschitz and concavity/smoothness conditions, and a Dantzig step to estimate the decorrelating vector.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on the population-to-sample EM analysis of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) in low dimensions, extends it to high dimensions via sparsity/thresholding (akin in spirit to iterative hard-thresholding for M-estimation), and connects to debiasing/desparsification for inference (van de Geer et al., Javanmard & Montanari, Zhang & Zhang, etc.). Compared to tensor-methods for mixtures, the paper aims for sharper rates and inference. It also relates to penalized EM and sparse mixture literature (e.g., Stadler et al., Khalili & Chen), but replaces explicit penalties with iterative truncation.

Strengths
- Technical ambition: A unified framework that couples computational convergence (geometric contraction) with high-dimensional statistical rates and valid inference is valuable.
- Clear decomposition of optimization vs statistical error, and coverage of both exact and gradient M-steps.
- Asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency for a decorrelated score in latent variable models is interesting and (to my knowledge) less developed than for convex GLMs.
- The analysis emphasizes entrywise control and l1-type statistical error in the M-step, which is the right scale for high-dimensional control with truncation.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strength of assumptions and practicality: Many results hinge on strong model-specific conditions (Lipschitz-gradient around the truth with the second argument fixed at θ*, local strong concavity, SNR/separation for mixtures) and on being initialized inside a basin with a constant relative error. The paper gives no practical initialization scheme with guarantees in high dimensions beyond citing assumptions. This limits practical impact.
- Resampling analysis: The main theorem is stated for a resampling/split-sample variant (fresh data per iteration) to simplify dependence. This is a standard analytical crutch but weakens the direct relevance to the actual (single-sample) EM. The paper should provide a full single-sample analysis or, at least, precise uniform concentration arguments that recover analogous bounds without resampling.
- Tuning and oracle knowledge: The threshold level b_s is assumed to be of the same order as s*, with bounds depending on unknown constants and the contraction factor. In practice s* is unknown and the guidance for selecting b_s, step sizes, and the Dantzig tuning λ is minimal. The theory currently assumes near-oracle choices.
- Comparison to established baselines: There is no empirical validation, and no comparison to established methods such as ℓ1-penalized EM (e.g., Stadler et al., 2010), SCAD/MCP-EM variants, or modern likelihood-penalization for mixtures, all of which are used in practice and have some theory. Similarly, for inference, the practical performance of the decorrelated score is not demonstrated (coverage, type-I error) and its computational cost (solving a Dantzig program involving Tn) is not discussed relative to alternatives.
- Breadth of models: Although the framework is framed as general, verification is only given for two mixture models under fairly strong conditions (e.g., high SNR). It remains unclear how broadly Condition 3.1–3.3 and 4.1–4.4 can be checked for other latent variable models (e.g., HMMs, topic models).
- Clarity and presentation: The draft contains many notation glitches and copy/paste artifacts (misplaced symbols, malformed equations, inconsistent subscripts/superscripts, undefined constants, dangling references). References are incomplete and in several cases appear corrupted. Critical definitions and assumptions are deferred to an appendix not provided here; as written, it is hard to reproduce or audit the proofs.
- Limitations not discussed: The method relies on sparsity of both θ* and the decorrelation vector w*; when w* is dense, inference fails. Thresholding introduces bias and can be unstable when b_s is misspecified or when signals are near the noise level; there is no robustness analysis. The gradient M-step uses a fixed step size tied to population curvature, which is not available in practice.

Quality
- The theoretical direction is sound and aligns with modern high-dimensional analysis, but the current form relies on strong assumptions, resampling analysis, and lacks empirical validation or comparisons. Key practical aspects (initialization, tuning, computational burden) are not addressed.

Clarity
- The paper is difficult to read due to notation overload, missing definitions in the main text, and numerous typographical/reference issues. A careful rewrite with self-contained statements, explicit constants where possible, and cleaned references is needed.

Originality
- Combining EM with hard thresholding is a natural extension of IHT ideas; the estimation rates are consistent with known minimax rates. The inference component via decorrelated score in latent variable models is the more original contribution, though it follows the now-standard debiasing paradigm.

Significance
- If strengthened (single-sample analysis, broader model verification, and empirics), the work could be impactful for high-dimensional latent variable estimation and inference. In its current form, practical significance is limited.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide single-sample (non-resampling) convergence and error bounds, or uniform concentration results that justify the practical algorithm.
- Give concrete, data-driven procedures for tuning b_s, step sizes, and λ, with sensitivity analyses.
- Add empirical studies: synthetic mixtures with controlled s*, d, n showing estimation error vs baselines (ℓ1-EM, SCAD/MCP-EM, tensor methods), and inference coverage/type-I error under null/alternative.
- Clarify initialization strategies with theoretical guarantees in high dimensions (e.g., spectral/tensor warm starts) and how they interact with your conditions.
- Discuss computational complexity per iteration, especially the Dantzig step and evaluation of Tn, and scalability.
- Clean up notation, state all assumptions in the main text, and fix the references.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical ideas are promising, particularly the inference component, but the reliance on strong/locals assumptions, the resampling analysis, lack of empirical validation and comparisons to established penalized-EM baselines, and clarity issues make it premature for NeurIPS in its current form.

Pros
- Ambitious, unified theory for estimation and inference with EM in high dimensions
- Near-optimal rates and semiparametric efficiency
- Covers both exact and gradient M-steps

Cons
- Strong assumptions, reliance on resampling, unclear initialization
- No experiments or baseline comparisons
- Oracle-like tuning and computational overhead not addressed
- Presentation and references need major revision
