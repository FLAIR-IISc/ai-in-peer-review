Summary
The paper proposes a high-dimensional variant of the EM algorithm that appends a truncation (hard-thresholding) step to the E- and M-steps to enforce sparsity of iterates. Two M-step implementations are allowed: exact maximization and a single gradient ascent step. The authors provide nonasymptotic convergence bounds that decompose error into geometric optimization error and a statistical error of order roughly sqrt(s*)·sqrt(log d / n) (up to log n factors) under suitable initialization and a basin-of-attraction assumption. They also propose a decorrelated score test tailored to latent variable models, using a Dantzig-type estimator of the orthogonalizing vector based on a Hessian-like Tn(·), and establish asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency for a low-dimensional component in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters. They instantiate conditions for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions in the appendix.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: EM in high-dimensional latent variable models with both estimation and inference.
- Clean idea: add hard-thresholding to each EM iterate to maintain sparsity; unify exact and gradient M-step analyses.
- Error decomposition and rates are plausible and align with minimax-optimal (up to logs) performance for sparse problems.
- Decorrelated score construction is natural (Fisher’s identity links Q to log-likelihood) and targets semiparametric efficiency.
- Attempts to verify general conditions for two canonical latent variable models.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness/clarity:
- Numerous notation errors and inconsistencies make it hard to verify claims. Examples:
  - Condition 3.1 “Lipschitz-Gradient-1” as written is self-cancelling (both sides are the same expression); presumably the second term should involve θ, not θ*.
  - Theorem 2.1: I(θ*) is defined with confusing “/n” placement (“E[r^2 ℓn(θ*)] n”). The scaling of Tn(·) vs the Fisher information seems off by factors of n unless carefully defined; please clarify exact scaling.
  - Tn definition (2.4): r^2_{1,1}Qn + r^2_{1,2}Qn is presented without clear block notation; later you use Tn(θ)_{·,α}. The shapes/mappings need to be made precise.
  - Mixed use of rQ, r_1 Q, r^2_{1,2} Q, and r_{,2}^2 Q without clear definitions leads to ambiguity.
- Many key assumptions are only stated abstractly (Conditions 3.1–3.3, 4.1–4.4) and verification is deferred. In the main text, there is insufficient detail to assess tightness or necessity; for NeurIPS, more self-contained statements would help.
- Algorithm 4 (resampling/split-sample EM) is used for analysis but is not the proposed procedure. It is not shown clearly whether the guarantees for the resampling version transfer to the practical (single-sample) Algorithm 1 without degradation.
- Initialization: results require “appropriate initialization with relative error ≤ δ,” but how this is obtained in the high-dimensional latent variable setting is not addressed (beyond general remarks). This is crucial given nonconvexity.
- Choice of sparsity level b_s: theorems require b_s ≍ s*, but s* is unknown. There is no guidance on data-driven selection or robustness to misspecification; the error bounds (3.8)–(3.9) depend on unknown constants and s*.
- The Dantzig selector tuning λ in (2.6) depends on unknown quantities (||w*||∞, concentration rates, Lipschitz constants). Practical calibration is not discussed; the theory also presumes w* is sparse, which is a strong condition on the nuisance-block precision structure that may fail in many latent models.
- For Gaussian mixtures, guarantees require a large SNR (||θ*||/σ ≥ r). This limits applicability; the impact on rates and feasibility under moderate SNR is not discussed.
- The claimed optimal variance for the score statistic rests on semiparametric efficiency; but the construction uses plug-in based on b_0 (α set to 0). Using b in place of b_0 is claimed “theoretical results remain the same” without proof sketch; uniformity under local alternatives is not discussed.
- Computational complexity: each iteration requires forming Qn and solving either a maximization step or a gradient step plus a Dantzig LP in dimension d for w, which can be comparable in cost to high-dimensional nodewise regressions each iteration. Complexity and scalability are not analyzed.

Empirical evaluation:
- No experiments. For a NeurIPS audience, at least small-scale empirical studies (synthetic GM/mixture regression) demonstrating finite-sample behavior, sensitivity to b_s/λ, initialization, and comparisons to penalized EM or debiased-likelihood baselines would substantially strengthen the paper.

Presentation/formatting:
- The manuscript has many typographical and formatting issues that hinder readability: LaTeX artifacts (⌘, ⇤, ·,  , 1 subscripts), inconsistent equation numbering, incorrect punctuation in formulas, and numerous broken references (authors split by spaces, missing years/titles). E.g., many references list only surnames/initials with missing bibliographic information; [2], [27] appear misattributed; “Journal names” and years are often absent. NeurIPS formatting standards are not met.

Originality and relation to prior work:
- The thresholded-iterate approach is reminiscent of iterative hard thresholding and proximal gradient with truncation; a deeper comparison (e.g., to projected gradient EM variants, hard-thresholded EM, or to high-dimensional M-estimation with nonconvex penalties) is missing.
- For inference, close connections to de-biased Lasso literature are acknowledged; however, discussion of recent latent-variable inference approaches (beyond GLMs) is limited. The comparison to tensor methods is somewhat dated; more recent sample-efficient methods for mixtures are not discussed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all notation errors; precisely define gradients/Hessians, block indices, and the scaling linking Qn to ℓn. Provide a clean statement of Theorem 2.1 with conditions (dominated convergence, interchange of differentiation/integration).
- Bring key model-specific verifications (at least for Gaussian mixtures) into the main text, including explicit constants/rates (ε in Condition 3.3) instead of “possibly with extra factors.”
- Discuss practical initialization strategies and provide theoretical conditions under which simple schemes succeed.
- Provide guidance for choosing b_s and λ (theory-backed, cross-validation, stability), and study robustness to misspecification.
- Clarify whether the main bounds hold for Algorithm 1 without resampling and, if not, quantify the gap.
- Add computational complexity analysis and experiments.
- Substantially revise and complete the references.

Pros
- Addresses a timely and challenging topic (high-dimensional EM with inference).
- Conceptually simple algorithmic modification with meaningful theoretical guarantees.
- Asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficiency is compelling.

Cons
- Numerous clarity/notation/formatting issues; several statements appear incorrect as written.
- Heavy and sometimes unverifiable assumptions (initialization, SNR, sparsity of w*).
- Practical aspects (tuning, complexity) and experiments are missing.
- Reliance on resampling for analysis without clear transfer to the practical algorithm.

Recommendation: Reject (4/10). The contribution is promising, but the current form has too many technical and presentation deficiencies, and lacks empirical validation.

Confidence: 0.8 (familiar with high-dimensional EM and debiasing literature).
