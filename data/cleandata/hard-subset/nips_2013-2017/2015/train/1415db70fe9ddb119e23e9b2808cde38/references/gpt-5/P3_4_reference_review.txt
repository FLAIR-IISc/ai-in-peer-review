Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies estimation and inference for latent-variable models in the high-dimensional regime (d ≫ n). It proposes a high-dimensional EM algorithm that augments the classical E/M steps with a truncation (hard-thresholding) step to enforce sparsity along the solution path. The authors analyze two variants of the M-step: exact maximization and a single gradient-ascent step. Under local curvature and stability conditions on the population Q-function, they establish a nonasymptotic error decomposition for the iterate sequence: geometric optimization contraction plus a statistical term of order √(s* log d / n) (up to logarithmic factors), culminating in a near-minimax-optimal rate at stationarity (Theorem 3.4). Beyond point estimation, they construct a “decorrelated score” test that achieves asymptotic normality with semiparametric efficient variance for a low-dimensional component in the presence of high-dimensional nuisance parameters (Theorem 4.6). The framework is instantiated for Gaussian mixtures and mixtures of regressions under standard separation/signal-to-noise assumptions.

This advances the line of work on EM theory (Wu; Balakrishnan et al.; Dempster et al.; Tseng), especially the recent population-to-sample EM analyses in low dimensions, by handling d ≫ n with sparsity and providing an inference procedure. It also connects to desparsified/decorrelated inference (Zhang & Zhang; van de Geer et al.; Javanmard & Montanari) by showing that the Q-function gradient equals the normalized score and linking Tn to Fisher information.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A simple but effective modification of EM with a truncation step that mirrors hard-thresholding schemes, yielding sparse iterates and sharp entrywise control needed in high dimensions.
- Theory: Clear decomposition into optimization versus statistical errors with geometric contraction and √(s* log d / n)-type rates; accommodates both exact and gradient M-steps; careful control of support evolution under truncation.
- Inference: A decorrelated score built on Q/Tn that attains semiparametric efficiency under high-dimensional nuisance; conditions are stated in a unified way and verified for two canonical latent-variable models.
- Scope: The framework is model-agnostic in form and can, in principle, extend to other latent structures.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Resampling/samples-splitting: The main convergence result is proved for a resampling version of the algorithm (Algorithm 4, appendix), using fresh data per iteration. This is statistically inefficient and not how EM is used in practice. It is unclear whether the claimed rates hold when reusing the full sample across iterations; a discussion or proof sketch without resampling (e.g., via empirical process/martingale arguments) would greatly strengthen the work.
- Initialization: The results require an initializer within a constant relative L2 accuracy. How to obtain such an initializer for the considered models at high dimension, and with the stated SNR/separation? The paper is critical of tensor methods for suboptimal rates, but they remain a practical initializer. Please specify concrete, computationally feasible initializers with guarantees in the stated regimes.
- Choice of sparsity level b_s: The theory requires b_s ≳ s* up to constants depending on contraction parameters (Eq. 3.8), but s* is unknown. How should b_s be chosen in practice? Can the algorithm be made adaptive (e.g., via data-driven thresholds, model selection along a path, or stability selection)?
- Strength of assumptions: The decorrelated score requires sparsity of w* = Iγγ^{-1} Iγα (Eq. 4.1) and multiple concentration/Lipschitz conditions on Tn in ℓ∞ norm. The sparsity of w* is a strong structural assumption on Fisher information that may not hold generically for mixtures; please discuss when it is plausible and provide examples (or negative examples).
- Model scope and parameters: The GMM analysis assumes known isotropic covariance and equal mixing. What happens with unknown covariance, unequal mixing proportions, or more than two components? Similarly, for mixtures of regression, which parameters are unknown and how does that affect conditions and rates?
- Practicality and computation: The Dantzig program for w involves a high-dimensional linear program. What is the computational cost and numerical stability when d is large? Are there scalable approximations (e.g., nodewise regressions) compatible with the latent-variable structure?
- Empirics: The paper presents no empirical results. Synthetic experiments demonstrating the estimation rates, the contraction behavior with/without resampling, robustness to mis-specified b_s, and the coverage/power of the decorrelated test would be very valuable. Comparisons to penalized EM (e.g., ℓ1-penalized M-steps) and to tensor initializers would also help.
- References and presentation: Many references are malformed or incomplete, and some relevant high-dimensional nonconvex/sparse optimization works (e.g., iterative hard thresholding, projected gradient for nonconvex M-estimators) are not discussed. The writing is generally clear, but the paper is condition-heavy; pointers from conditions to concrete verifications earlier in the main text (not only appendix) would aid readability.

Quality
The technical development appears sound and well-motivated. The population-to-sample translation via ℓ∞-type control is appropriate for hard-thresholded updates. The link between Qn’s derivatives and the score/Fisher information is neat and enables the inference result. However, the reliance on sample splitting for theory, strong structural assumptions for inference, and the lack of experiments limit the completeness.

Clarity
The paper is organized and readable for an expert audience. Suggestions: add an overview table mapping assumptions (3.1–3.3, 4.1–4.4) to model-specific bounds; clarify initialization strategies; and fix references.

Originality
Combining EM with hard thresholding and delivering both optimal estimation rates and semiparametric-efficient inference for latent-variable models in high dimensions is, to my knowledge, novel. The inference construction adapts decorrelation to the EM/Q-function context in a principled way.

Significance
If the non-resampling analysis can be established (or at least justified empirically) and the method shown to be practical, this would be a meaningful step toward statistically optimal and inferentially valid procedures for high-dimensional latent-variable models.

Pros
- Addresses an important gap: high-dimensional EM with rates and inference.
- Solid theory with clear optimization/statistics interplay and efficiency guarantees.
- Applicable to canonical latent-variable models; likely extensible.

Cons
- Theory hinges on iterative sample-splitting; unclear behavior with standard EM on a fixed dataset.
- Strong assumptions (w*-sparsity, SNR, known covariance/mixing) and limited model scope.
- No empirical validation; practical guidance (initialization, tuning, computation) is sparse.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you provide theory or at least empirical evidence that the same rates hold without resampling?  
2) What practical initializers do you recommend, and under what conditions do they meet the required basin proximity?  
3) How sensitive is performance to b_s; can you propose an adaptive thresholding rule?  
4) Can the inference be implemented via nodewise regressions to avoid linear programming, and does the theory still hold?  
5) How do your results extend to unknown covariance/mixing proportions or K > 2 components?

Recommendation
Borderline accept if the authors can (i) justify the non-resampling setting and (ii) add convincing experiments; otherwise, lean reject due to missing empirical validation and practical guidance despite strong theoretical contributions.
