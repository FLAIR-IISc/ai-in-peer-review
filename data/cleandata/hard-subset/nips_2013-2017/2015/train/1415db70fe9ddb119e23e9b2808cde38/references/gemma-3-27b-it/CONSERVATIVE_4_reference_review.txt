This paper addresses the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, a significant gap in the existing literature. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and establish convergence rates and asymptotic normality, which, if correct, would represent a substantial contribution. However, despite the ambitious goals and technical effort, I have serious concerns about the novelty, clarity, and ultimately, the convincingness of the presented results.

The core idea of truncating the EM iterates to enforce sparsity is not entirely novel. Sparsity-inducing penalties are commonly used with EM in related areas, and this work’s truncation step feels like a relatively straightforward adaptation. The authors acknowledge related work but downplay the connection, framing the truncation as a key innovation rather than a standard regularization technique. While demonstrating theoretical properties *with* this truncation is valuable, the presented justification for its necessity feels insufficient given existing literature.

My primary concern lies with the conditions required to achieve the stated results. The numerous conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) and the associated parameters (⇢, ✏, ⇣EM, etc.) make the applicability of these theorems highly questionable. The conditions are stated abstractly, and the detailed discussion of how these translate to specific models (GMM, mixture of regression) is largely relegated to the appendix, hindering evaluation. The requirement that `s⇤w` be small (related to the sparsity of the Fisher information) feels particularly restrictive and limits the practical impact. Furthermore, the dependency of the convergence rate on `log d/n` while seemingly “optimal” feels weak, especially given the strong assumptions. Existing low-dimensional results often achieve rates of `d/n`, and the degradation in performance with high dimensionality doesn't seem sufficiently justified, given the imposed complexity.

Clarity is another significant weakness. The notation is dense, and the presentation is overly technical. Key definitions are scattered throughout the text, making it difficult to follow the logical flow. The motivation behind certain choices, such as the specific form of the truncation step, remains unclear.  Algorithm 1, in particular, is difficult to parse without constant reference to definitions elsewhere in the paper.  The connection between the theoretical results and their practical implications is also insufficiently explored. How does one choose `bs` in practice? What sensitivity analyses have been performed regarding the choice of `?` The paper lacks a clear discussion of these practical considerations.

While the authors attempt to connect their work to existing literature, they often portray prior results as weaker than they are.  For instance, the characterization of [2]’s results feels somewhat selective, focusing on the lower statistical rate without acknowledging the nuances of their contributions.  The comparison to tensor methods feels similarly superficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem is important and challenging.
*   The authors demonstrate a significant technical effort.
*   If the theoretical results hold under realistic conditions, they could be impactful.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – the truncation step feels like a standard regularization technique.
*   Overly restrictive conditions significantly limit the applicability of the results.
*   Lack of clarity in presentation and insufficient discussion of practical implications.
*   Overstated claims of superiority compared to existing methods.
*   The complexity of the theory, combined with the numerous assumptions, raises concerns about the robustness and practical value of the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the paper tackles an important problem, the overly strong assumptions, lack of clarity, and limited novelty make it unsuitable for NIPS at this time. The authors should significantly revise the paper, focusing on relaxing the conditions, providing a more comprehensive analysis of practical considerations, and clarifying the connection to existing work. A more detailed discussion of how these results translate to realistic datasets would also be beneficial.  The presented work feels more like a preliminary investigation into a complex theoretical landscape rather than a complete and convincing contribution.
