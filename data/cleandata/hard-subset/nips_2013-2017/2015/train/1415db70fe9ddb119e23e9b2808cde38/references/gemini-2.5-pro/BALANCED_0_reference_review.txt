This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for parameter estimation and uncertainty quantification for latent variable models in the high-dimensional regime (d >> n). The authors propose a "High-Dimensional EM Algorithm" which modifies the classical EM algorithm by incorporating a truncation (hard-thresholding) step after each M-step to enforce sparsity. The paper's two main contributions are a non-asymptotic analysis of this algorithm and a procedure for asymptotic inference.

First, for parameter estimation, the authors prove that under certain regularity conditions and given a suitable initialization, their algorithm converges at a geometric rate. The final estimator is shown to achieve a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d) / n)`, where `s*` is the true sparsity level. This result elegantly bridges the gap between the computational properties of the EM algorithm and the statistical properties of the regularized MLE in a high-dimensional context. The analysis extends the work of Balakrishnan et al. (2017) [2], which studied the EM algorithm in low dimensions, to the more challenging high-dimensional setting. The key technical novelty lies in handling the non-linear truncation operator, which requires a more fine-grained, entry-wise (`l-infinity`) analysis of the statistical errors, a departure from the `l-2` analysis sufficient for the low-dimensional case.

Second, for statistical inference, the paper proposes a "decorrelated score test" to perform hypothesis tests on individual components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. This is a significant contribution, as inference in high-dimensional, non-convex models is notoriously difficult. Drawing inspiration from the debiasing literature for high-dimensional convex models (e.g., the desparsified Lasso), the authors construct a test statistic that is asymptotically normal under the null hypothesis. Crucially, they show that its limiting variance achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound, indicating statistical optimality. This provides the first computationally feasible procedure for valid inference based on the EM algorithm in this setting.

The general framework is demonstrated on two important examples: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a fundamental and long-standing problem: providing theoretical guarantees for the widely-used EM algorithm in the modern high-dimensional setting. Establishing rates for a computationally tractable algorithm and providing a valid method for inference are major advances that will likely be influential for both theorists and practitioners working with high-dimensional latent variable models.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The proposed High-Dimensional EM algorithm, which integrates hard thresholding into the EM iterations, is a natural and well-motivated approach. The theoretical analysis, however, is highly non-trivial. Adapting the "perturbed gradient" view of EM to accommodate the truncation step and developing an inference framework for the *output of an iterative algorithm* (which is not necessarily a global optimum) are significant conceptual and technical contributions.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous and comprehensive theoretical analysis. The decomposition of the estimation error into an optimization component (which decays geometrically) and a statistical component (which determines the final error rate) is clear and insightful. The assumptions are laid out explicitly, and the proofs (provided in the appendix) appear to be sound.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a clear motivation and a thorough review of related work, effectively positioning its contributions. The methodology and main theoretical results are presented before diving into the specific model applications, which helps in understanding the general framework.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong theoretical paper, its practical impact and completeness could be further enhanced by addressing the following points:

1.  **Initialization:** The entire theory hinges on the availability of an initial estimator `init` that lies within a basin of attraction of the true parameter `theta*`. This is a strong assumption, common in non-convex optimization theory, but the paper provides no guidance on how to obtain such an initializer. A discussion of practical strategies—for instance, using spectral methods or tensor-based methods (even if their rates are suboptimal, they might suffice for initialization)—would greatly strengthen the paper. Without this, the applicability of the proposed algorithm remains purely theoretical.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theory is compelling, the claims would be significantly bolstered by numerical simulations. Even a simple set of experiments could: (a) demonstrate the geometric convergence of the algorithm in practice; (b) verify that the empirical estimation error scales with `s*`, `d`, and `n` as predicted by Theorem 3.4; and (c) for the inference part, evaluate the finite-sample performance of the decorrelated score test by checking its type-I error control and the coverage of the corresponding confidence intervals. This would provide valuable evidence that the asymptotic theory is relevant for realistic sample sizes.

3.  **Analysis of a Resampling Algorithm:** The main estimation result (Theorem 3.4) is proven for a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4), where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a standard proof technique used to ensure independence and simplify the analysis. However, in practice, one would use the entire dataset at each step. The authors should be more upfront about this in the main text and briefly discuss whether the results are expected to hold for the standard (non-resampling) algorithm and what the technical challenges are in extending the proof.

4.  **Tuning Parameter Selection:** The algorithm and the inference procedure rely on key tuning parameters: the sparsity level `bs` for the algorithm and the regularization parameter `lambda` for the Dantzig selector used in the score test. The theory provides their required scaling, but offers no advice for their selection in practice. A brief discussion on practical methods like cross-validation would be very helpful for any user of this method.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly significant and challenging problem at the intersection of statistics and computation.
*   Provides the first computationally feasible EM-based approach with guarantees for both optimal estimation and valid inference in high-dimensional latent variable models.
*   The theoretical contributions are substantial, novel, and technically deep.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly positions its contributions relative to a vast body of literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The strong initialization assumption is not discussed from a practical standpoint.
*   The complete absence of experiments makes it difficult to gauge the practical performance and the finite-sample relevance of the asymptotic results.
*   The main estimation theorem is for a resampling-based algorithm, which is not the practical implementation.

### Recommendation

Overall, this is an excellent theoretical paper that makes significant advances on an important problem. The strengths in originality, significance, and technical quality are compelling. While the weaknesses regarding practical implementation details and the lack of experiments are notable, they do not undermine the core theoretical contribution. They represent important directions for future work that build on this strong foundation. Therefore, I recommend accepting this paper. I strongly encourage the authors to incorporate a discussion of the practical aspects (initialization, tuning parameters, and the resampling scheme) in their final version to broaden the paper's impact.
