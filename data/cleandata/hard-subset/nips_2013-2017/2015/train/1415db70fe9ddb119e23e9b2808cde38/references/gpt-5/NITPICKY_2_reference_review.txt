Summary
This paper studies EM for high-dimensional latent variable models. The core idea is to augment EM with a truncation step (T-step) that hard-thresholds the parameter to the top-bs entries at each iteration, enforcing sparsity along the path. The authors provide (i) optimization and statistical guarantees: with suitable initialization and under population/sampling conditions, the iterates contract geometrically and the final estimator achieves a near-minimax rate O(√(s* log d / n) up to log n); and (ii) an inferential procedure: a decorrelated score (via a Dantzig-style estimator of a nuisance projection vector w) that yields asymptotically normal statistics for low-dimensional components, with variance matching the semiparametric information bound. The framework treats both exact maximization and gradient M-step variants, and gives examples for Gaussian mixtures and mixture of regressions (details deferred to the appendix). The analysis leverages a resampling (sample-splitting across iterations) variant of EM to simplify concentration arguments.

Relation to prior work: This builds on low-dimensional EM analysis (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) and on high-dimensional inference via de-biasing/desparsification (van de Geer et al., Zhang & Zhang), adapting those ideas to latent variable models where likelihoods are non-concave. The truncation step is the key addition to enforce sparsity within EM; inference is based on a decorrelated score constructed from derivatives of the Q-function.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: computational-statistical guarantees for EM in high dimensions and valid inference with high-dimensional nuisance.
- Derives geometric convergence and near-optimal estimation rates under explicit conditions; covers both exact and gradient M-step variants.
- Provides an inference method attaining semiparametric efficiency for target components despite nuisance parameters.
- The link between rQn and the full-data log-likelihood (Theorem 2.1) is clean and underpins the inference construction.
- The framework is conceptually general and illustrated on two canonical latent models.

Weaknesses / Concerns
Technical assumptions and practicality:
- Strong initialization requirement (within a fixed-radius basin, relative error < constant). The paper does not provide a concrete, data-driven, high-dimensional initializer for the considered latent models; obtaining such an initializer is often the main challenge. Please discuss practical initializations (e.g., spectral/tensor, method-of-moments, k-means for GMMs) and the conditions under which they meet the basin requirements in high dimension.
- Tuning and unknown constants: bs must be of the same order as s*. This presupposes knowledge of s*, with no data-driven selection rule (cross-validation? stability selection?). The gradient M-step requires step size 2/(ν+µ), and the Dantzig selector needs λ depending on ζT, ζL, ζEM and ∥w*∥∞, none observable. Practical guidance is missing.
- Resampling EM: Main theory is for a sample-splitting version that uses n/T samples per iteration to regain independence. This is statistically wasteful and not how EM is used. The paper does not provide guarantees for the standard single-sample version nor quantify the trade-off between T (to kill optimization error) and the shrinking per-iteration sample size (increasing statistical error). This is a major limitation for practical relevance.
- Monotonicity and ascent: The T-step breaks the standard EM monotonicity, yet the paper does not analyze or discuss the loss of ascent in the observed-data likelihood/Q and its practical implications.

Assumptions and verification:
- Condition 3.2 (Q(·; θ*) is strongly concave and smooth) is asserted for classes of latent models. For general mixtures, Q(·; θ*) being globally strongly concave is nontrivial. Only GMM is briefly instantiated in the main text; mixture of regressions and other models are deferred. It would help to state the precise parameter regimes (e.g., separation/SNR, known/unknown variance) needed for these conditions in the main paper.
- The l1 statistical error control for M(θ)−Mn(θ) with s-sparse inputs requires uniform high-probability bounds over supports encountered along the path. The paper uses resampling to avoid dependence but does not discuss uniformity for the non-resampling case.
- Inference requires sparsity of w* (support size s*w), a structural assumption on the Fisher information that may be strong/opaque in mixtures. Please justify when s*w ≪ d holds for your examples.

Missing experiments:
- There are no simulations or empirical studies. Given the number of tuning parameters, the reliance on initialization, and the asymptotic nature of inference guarantees, empirical evaluation is crucial to assess finite-sample performance, sensitivity to bs/λ/η, and coverage.

Clarity and presentation:
- The manuscript contains many typesetting/notation issues that materially hinder readability: frequent occurrences of garbled symbols (e.g., ⇤, , ·, `), inconsistent norms (ℓ vs `), inconsistent subscripts/superscripts (r1 vs r, r2), and broken equations (e.g., stray “p” outside radicals, missing inequality signs “d n”). Algorithms 1–3 have misrendered variable names (bSinit, trunc init). Some definitions (e.g., Tn in (2.4)) have confusing indexing (1,1; 1,2) and dimension statements. Please thoroughly proofread and fix all LaTeX artifacts.
- References are severely corrupted: author names truncated/shuffled, missing years/venues, and incomplete titles. This must be corrected for a NeurIPS submission.
- Many essential model-specific details are deferred to the appendix; at least one full instantiation (assumptions, constants, and final bounds) for each example should appear in the main paper.

Quality
- The high-level arguments are plausible, and the claimed rates/limits align with prior literature; however, the reliance on sample-splitting and strong, partly opaque assumptions, plus the absence of experiments, weakens the empirical and practical credibility.

Originality
- The truncation-within-EM idea in high dimension and the adaptation of decorrelated score to latent-variable EM are novel combinations of known tools. The inference component borrows heavily from existing de-biasing frameworks but applied to the Q-function. Overall, moderate novelty.

Significance
- If polished and complemented with non-resampling guarantees and experiments, this could be impactful for high-dimensional latent-variable estimation/inference. In current form, practical significance is limited.

Questions for authors
1. How to obtain an initializer satisfying the basin condition in high dimension for your two models? Provide concrete, verifiable procedures and conditions.
2. Can you avoid sample splitting by establishing uniform concentration over the basin, and thus give guarantees for the standard EM?
3. How should bs, η, and λ be chosen in practice? Any data-driven rules or heuristics? Sensitivity?
4. For mixture of regressions, under what variance/Design assumptions are Conditions 3.1–3.3 satisfied?
5. Do you have finite-sample inference results (coverage/error bars) from simulations?

Pros
- Addresses an important gap; general framework
- Near-minimax rates; semiparametric efficiency
- Covers exact/gradient M-steps

Cons
- Theory hinges on resampling; no guarantees for standard EM
- Strong initialization and sparsity-of-w* assumptions; tuning unspecified
- No experiments
- Numerous formatting/notation/reference issues

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The cumulative technical/practical gaps and extensive presentation issues outweigh the theoretical promise. A revised version with (i) non-resampling guarantees or a clear justification, (ii) concrete initialization/tuning strategies, (iii) thorough experiments, and (iv) polished writing/references would be much stronger.
