### Summary of the Paper

This paper develops a theoretical framework for parameter estimation and statistical inference for latent variable models in the high-dimensional regime where the dimension `d` can be much larger than the sample size `n`. The authors propose a "High Dimensional EM Algorithm," which modifies the classic EM algorithm by adding a hard-thresholding step (T-step) after each M-step to enforce sparsity in the parameter estimates. The paper provides a unified analysis for two M-step implementations: exact maximization and a single gradient ascent step.

The main contributions are twofold:
1.  **Estimation:** The authors prove that, given a suitable initialization within a local basin of attraction, the proposed algorithm converges at a geometric rate. The final estimator achieves a near-minimax optimal statistical error rate of `sqrt(s* log(d) / n)`, where `s*` is the true sparsity level. This is a significant extension of prior work, such as Balakrishnan et al. (2017), which provided similar guarantees but only for the low-dimensional setting.
2.  **Inference:** The paper introduces a decorrelated score statistic for hypothesis testing on low-dimensional components of the high-dimensional parameter vector. It is shown that this statistic is asymptotically normal under the null hypothesis. Crucially, its limiting variance achieves the semiparametric information bound, establishing its statistical optimality.

The general theoretical framework is presented under a set of abstract conditions, which are then verified for two important high-dimensional latent variable models: the Gaussian mixture model and the mixture of regression model.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a fundamental and long-standing problem: the lack of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings. This is a critical gap, as both EM and high-dimensional models are ubiquitous in machine learning and statistics. The proposed algorithm, incorporating a simple and intuitive truncation step, and its comprehensive theoretical analysis represent a major step forward. The extension to valid statistical inference is also a highly significant contribution, as uncertainty quantification in these complex, non-convex settings is notoriously difficult.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a complete theoretical treatment, covering both computational aspects (geometric convergence rate) and statistical aspects (near-optimal estimation error and asymptotically efficient inference). The analysis successfully navigates the challenges posed by the non-convexity of the likelihood, the iterative nature of the algorithm (which is not a direct solver for an M-estimator), and the high dimensionality. The result that the decorrelated score test achieves the semiparametric efficiency bound is particularly strong and impressive.

3.  **General and Well-Presented Framework:** The authors develop their theory under a general set of high-level conditions (e.g., Concavity-Smoothness, Statistical-Error). This makes the framework potentially applicable to a broad class of latent variable models beyond the two examples provided. The paper is clearly written and well-structured, effectively communicating the problem, the proposed methods, and the main theoretical results. The relationship to and distinction from prior work is thoroughly and accurately explained.

### Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance) and Constructive Feedback

1.  **The Initialization Assumption:** The analysis relies on a strong assumption that a good initial estimate `init` is available, satisfying `||init - θ*||_2 ≤ R`. While common in the analysis of non-convex problems, the paper does not provide a practical, data-driven procedure for obtaining such an initialization. This limits the practical applicability of the theoretical guarantees.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a discussion of potential initialization strategies. For instance, could spectral methods or tensor-based methods (despite their potentially suboptimal rates, as noted by the authors) be used to find an initializer that satisfies the required condition with high probability? Even a discussion of this point would add considerable value.

2.  **Analysis of a Resampling-Based Algorithm:** The theoretical analysis is conducted for a "resampling version" of the algorithm (Algorithm 4 in the appendix), where a fresh batch of data is used at each iteration. This is a standard analytical tool that simplifies proofs by ensuring independence across steps. However, in practice, one would use the entire dataset at each iteration.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should explicitly state this distinction in the main paper and briefly discuss the potential challenges in extending the proof to the more practical setting where data is reused. While a full proof is likely beyond the scope of this paper, acknowledging this gap and providing some intuition would be helpful for the reader to understand the scope of the current theoretical guarantees.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are substantial, the claims would be more compelling if supported by simulations. Numerical experiments could be used to:
    *   Verify that the estimation error scales with `s*`, `d`, and `n` as predicted by Theorem 3.4.
    *   Demonstrate the empirical coverage of confidence intervals constructed from the proposed decorrelated score test, showing that they achieve the nominal level.
    *   Investigate the algorithm's sensitivity to the quality of initialization and the choice of tuning parameters (e.g., `bs`).

4.  **Practical Selection of Tuning Parameters:** The algorithm requires setting the sparsity level `bs`, and the inference procedure requires a tuning parameter `λ` for the Dantzig selector. The theory provides guidance on their scaling, but it does not address how to choose them from data in practice.
    *   **Suggestion:** A brief discussion on practical methods for parameter tuning, such as cross-validation or information criteria, would be a valuable addition for practitioners wishing to apply this method.

### Decision

This paper presents a novel and significant theoretical contribution to a fundamental problem in machine learning. It provides the first comprehensive framework for both optimal estimation and efficient inference using the EM algorithm in a high-dimensional context. The theoretical results are strong, and the framework is general. While the work has limitations, particularly regarding the practical issues of initialization, tuning, and the reliance on a resampling scheme for the analysis, these are common in foundational theoretical work on such difficult problems. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This is a high-quality paper that is likely to have a substantial impact on the field.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
