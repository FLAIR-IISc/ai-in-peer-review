- Proposes a sparse EM algorithm that inserts a hard-thresholding truncation step after each M-step to enforce sparsity.  
- Analyzes two M-step implementations: exact maximization and a single gradient-ascent step.  
- Main theorem decomposes estimation error into a geometrically contracting optimization term plus a statistical term of order √(s*)·√(log d / n) (up to logs).  
- Shows near-minimax estimation rates once optimization error vanishes.  
- Develops a decorrelated score (and Wald) inference procedure for low-dimensional components with high-dimensional nuisance.  
- Constructs the decorrelating vector w via a Dantzig-type estimator from Q-function derivatives, achieving asymptotic normality and semiparametric efficiency.  
- Instantiates the framework for Gaussian mixture models and mixtures of regressions with explicit rates and SNR conditions.  
- Builds on Balakrishnan et al. (2017) by extending population-to-sample EM analysis to high dimensions.  
- Connects to debiased/desparsified inference literature but adapts the decorrelated score to latent-variable, nonconvex settings.  
- Claims superiority over tensor methods in high-dimensional sparse regimes, achieving sharper rates under sparsity.  
- Theoretical analysis is careful: requires smoothness/strong concavity in a basin, Lipschitz M-step conditions, and fine-grained ℓ1 control between sample and population M-steps.  
- Truncation requires entrywise control, a nontrivial refinement beyond prior ℓ2 analyses.  
- Inference result leverages identities between Q-derivatives, likelihood score, and Fisher information under concentration and ℓ∞-rate conditions.  
- Key assumptions: good initialization in a basin, sparsity of parameter and w*, correct tuning of bs ≈ s*, and use of a resampling variant for analysis.  
- Some assumptions (initialization, sparsity of w*, resampling) are strong or nontrivial in practice.  
- Clarity: high-level ideas are clear but the paper is notation-heavy, has deferred definitions, typos, formatting glitches, and insufficient explanation of the resampling assumption.  
- Novelty: introduces sparsity-enforcing truncation within EM with entrywise support analysis and provides first high-dimensional inference for EM estimators under latent structures.  
- Significance: potentially impactful for high-dimensional latent-structure applications; theoretical advances in computation+inference are timely, but lack of empirical evaluation weakens practical impact.  
- Strengths include explicit computational-statistical tradeoff, unified treatment of two M-steps, semiparametrically efficient decorrelated score, and verification on two canonical models.  
- Weaknesses: theory assumes basin initialization—needs data-driven initialization guarantees; bs selection and robustness to misspecification are not addressed.  
- Weaknesses: inference requires s*w = o(√(n/log d))—needs intuition, verification, and discussion of failure modes.  
- Weaknesses: analysis uses resampling for decoupling iterates; unclear extension to single-sample algorithm—proof sketch or empirical evidence needed.  
- Weaknesses: computational burden of solving Dantzig programs per iteration not discussed; alternatives (nodewise Lasso) and complexity should be addressed.  
- Weaknesses: no experiments—recommended simulations comparing truncated EM, ℓ1-penalized EM, gradient EM with soft-thresholding, and spectral/tensor methods, with ablations on initialization, bs, and SNR.  
- Weaknesses: relation to IHT/projection methods should be explicitly discussed and differences clarified.  
- Weaknesses: some population Q Lipschitz/concavity conditions may be strong—need intuition and scaling discussion, especially under moderate overlap.  
- Weaknesses: provide finite-sample tuning guidance for step sizes, iterations T, and Dantzig parameters, plus clean up references and notation.  
- Overall recommendation: strong theoretical contribution meriting acceptance pending clarity improvements and at least modest empirical validation.
