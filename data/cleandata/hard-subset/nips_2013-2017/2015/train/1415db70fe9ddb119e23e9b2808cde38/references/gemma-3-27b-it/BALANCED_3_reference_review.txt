This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, a regime where existing results break down. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and provide statistical and computational guarantees, including convergence rates and asymptotic normality for hypothesis testing. This is a significant contribution as it bridges a gap between computation and statistical theory in a practically relevant setting. The paper is well-motivated, and the proposed approach seems promising.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Addressing the lack of theoretical guarantees for EM in high dimensions is a crucial and timely research direction.
* **Novel Approach:** The truncation step in the EM algorithm is a clever idea to enforce sparsity and achieve theoretical results.
* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides rigorous statistical and computational guarantees, including convergence rates and asymptotic normality, which are highly valuable. The convergence rate of p sqrt(s* log(d/n)) is near-optimal.
* **Generality:** The framework is general enough to be applied to Gaussian mixture models and mixture of regression models, demonstrating its broad applicability.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear notation and algorithmic descriptions. The outline of the proof in the appendix is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technicality & Readability:** While generally well-written, the level of technical detail can be quite dense, making it challenging for readers not deeply familiar with the relevant theoretical tools. Some sections, like the introduction of notation in section 2.2, could benefit from simplification.
* **Condition Verification:** While the authors mention verifying the conditions for specific models in the appendix, the details are deferred to a longer version. Providing more details in the main paper, or at least a more thorough discussion of the assumptions and their plausibility, would strengthen the work.  For example, how sensitive are the results to violations of the assumptions?
* **Constant Factors:** The constants involved in the convergence rates and asymptotic normality (e.g., C, C0) are not fully characterized.  This limits the practical interpretability of the results. Understanding the dependence of these constants on model parameters and sample size would be useful.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical guarantees. Discussion of practical implementation issues, such as the choice of the truncation parameter `bs`, the stepsize `⌘`, and the impact of initialization sensitivity, would enhance the paper's relevance to practitioners.  How robust is the algorithm to different initialization strategies?
* **Comparison to Existing High-D Methods:** The paper briefly mentions related work but could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other high-dimensional latent variable model estimation techniques (e.g., variational inference, spectral methods), especially regarding computational complexity and statistical efficiency.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in statistical machine learning with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The results are significant and advance the state of the art in understanding the behavior of the EM algorithm in high dimensions.
* The theoretical guarantees are strong and provide valuable insights into the performance of the algorithm.
* The generality of the framework makes it applicable to a wide range of latent variable models.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The technical complexity of the paper might limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* The lack of detailed verification of conditions and characterization of constant factors reduces the practical impact of the results.
* The limited discussion of practical implementation considerations may hinder the adoption of the proposed algorithm.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. While the technical details and lack of thorough practical considerations are weaknesses, the importance of the problem addressed and the strength of the results outweigh these concerns. I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance, but I recommend the authors address the weaknesses by providing more detailed explanations of the assumptions, characterizing the constant factors, and discussing practical implementation aspects.  Specifically, expanding the discussion around sensitivity to initialization and providing guidance on parameter selection (especially `bs`) would be beneficial.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
