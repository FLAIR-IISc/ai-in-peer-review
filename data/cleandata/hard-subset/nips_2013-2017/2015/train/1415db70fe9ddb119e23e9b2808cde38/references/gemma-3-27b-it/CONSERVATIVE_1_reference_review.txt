This paper addresses the challenging problem of theoretical guarantees for the EM algorithm in high-dimensional latent variable models, a significant gap in the existing literature. The authors propose a truncated EM algorithm and establish convergence and asymptotic normality results under fairly general conditions. While the problem is important and the authors demonstrate considerable technical skill, I find the paper ultimately falls short of convincing me of its novelty and impact, primarily due to the substantial assumptions required and the lack of empirical validation beyond illustrative examples.

The core idea of truncating the EM update to enforce sparsity is intuitively reasonable, and the associated theoretical analysis is impressive. The stated convergence rate of p s⇤ · log d/n is indeed optimal, given the sparsity level. However, the numerous conditions (3.1-3.3, 4.1-4.5) needed to achieve this rate seem overly restrictive. The dependence on parameters like ⇢, ✏, and the required sparsity structure of the w⇤ vector (related to the Fisher information) raises concerns about the practical applicability of the method. The paper acknowledges the need for careful tuning of parameters but doesn't provide concrete guidance on how to do this in realistic scenarios. 

The theoretical framework appears heavily influenced by existing work on high-dimensional estimation, particularly Lasso and related methods. While the authors correctly point out differences, the approach feels more like an adaptation of known techniques to the EM framework than a genuinely novel contribution. The connection to existing literature on tensor methods feels somewhat superficial, and the claim of overcoming their limitations needs stronger justification. 

My biggest concern is the complete lack of substantial empirical validation. The paper mentions applying the framework to Gaussian mixture and regression models but doesn’t present comprehensive experimental results demonstrating its performance on real-world datasets or comparing it to standard EM implementations or other high-dimensional estimation techniques. The illustrative examples discussed in the appendix are insufficient to establish the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. Without such validation, it is difficult to assess whether the theoretical guarantees translate into tangible benefits.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses an important open problem in statistical inference.
*   Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the high-dimensional EM algorithm.
*   Establishes optimal convergence rates under specific conditions.
*   Develops a decorrelated score statistic for asymptotic inference.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The numerous assumptions and conditions required for the theoretical results are overly restrictive and may limit practical applicability.
*   The lack of substantial empirical validation hinders the assessment of the algorithm's performance.
*   The proposed method feels like an adaptation of existing techniques rather than a genuinely novel contribution.
*   The paper is dense and challenging to read, with a lot of technical detail.
*   Limited discussion of computational cost or scalability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a fundamental theoretical problem and provides a mathematically rigorous solution.
*   The results offer valuable insights into the behavior of the EM algorithm in high-dimensional settings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The restrictive assumptions and lack of empirical validation cast doubt on the practical relevance of the work.
*   The originality of the approach is questionable.
*   The paper is overly technical and lacks a clear demonstration of its benefits.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the theoretical contributions are noteworthy, the numerous assumptions and lack of empirical validation significantly detract from the paper's overall impact. I believe that the authors need to address the concerns regarding practical applicability and provide more convincing evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. A major revision with substantial experimental results and a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of the assumptions is required. The current manuscript feels incomplete and more akin to a theoretical exploration than a fully developed research contribution.
