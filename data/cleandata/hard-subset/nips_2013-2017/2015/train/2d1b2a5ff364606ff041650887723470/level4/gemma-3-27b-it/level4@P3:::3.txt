This paper considers the Generalized Lasso with non-linear measurements and Gaussian design. A known heuristic often used is that non-linear observations may be treated as noisy linear observations. Building on the work of Plan and Vershynin [17], the authors extend their results and, by moving to an asymptotic regime, provide novel and precise explicit expressions for the estimation error. The accuracy of these predictions is demonstrated through several special cases, including the Lasso, group Lasso, and nuclear norm regularization. The application to the design of optimal quantizers in the context of q-Bit compressive sensing and its relation to the Lloyd-Max quantizer is particularly insightful.

**Quality:** This is a strong piece of work. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the results are significant, providing a precise characterization of estimation performance in a challenging setting. The authors carefully consider both theoretical aspects and practical implications. 

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. However, the level of mathematical detail sometimes necessitates a strong background in high-dimensional statistics and convex optimization to fully grasp the intricacies of the arguments. While this is typical for NIPS, briefly expanding on certain technical aspects could improve accessibility. The notation is consistent throughout, which aids clarity. The paper could benefit from a more balanced treatment of different sections, with perhaps a slightly more detailed explanation of the connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While building upon previous work by Plan and Vershynin [17], it significantly extends their results by providing precise asymptotic error expressions, which were previously unknown. The generalization to various regularizers (Lasso, group Lasso, nuclear norm) and the insightful application to q-bit compressive sensing are also novel contributions. The connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer is a particularly interesting and original result.

**Significance:** The results are significant for several reasons. First, they provide a theoretical justification for the widely used heuristic of treating non-linear measurements as noisy linear measurements. Second, the precise error expressions allow for a more informed design of estimation algorithms and a better understanding of the factors influencing performance. Third, the application to q-bit compressive sensing has practical implications for designing efficient analog-to-digital converters. The work addresses a difficult problem and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The derived expressions will likely be valuable for researchers and practitioners working in areas such as compressed sensing, signal processing, and machine learning.

**Specific Comments:**

1.  Page 4, line 193: The phrasing regarding the regularized and penalized versions being equivalent could be more precise. It would be helpful to explicitly state the correspondence between the regularization parameter and the penalty parameter.
2.  Caption of Figure 1:  Correct the formatting to '$n$=768' for consistency.
3.  Page 5, line 247: There appears to be a minor typographical error ("When ...").
4.  Page 5, line 294: The authors' use of “proximal function” is somewhat unconventional. While the concept is related, the term typically refers to the proximal *operator*, and the described function is more accurately referred to as the Moreau envelope. Suggest changing the terminology to avoid confusion.
5.  Page 5, Assumption 1: Providing a more formal interpretation of this assumption using concepts from variational analysis would enhance the rigor and clarity of the presentation.
6.  Throughout the paper: Avoid repeatedly calling the Generalized Lasso an “algorithm.” It is more accurately described as a convex program or a decoder.
7.  The discussion of experimental validation (towards the end of the provided excerpt) feels slightly disjointed. A more cohesive connection to the theoretical results would strengthen this section.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics.
*   The results are rigorously proven and supported by numerical experiments.
*   The work has practical implications for various applications, including compressive sensing and quantization.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized (with minor improvements suggested).
*   The connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer is a particularly insightful and original contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The mathematical depth of the paper might make it inaccessible to a broader audience.
*   The presentation could be more balanced, with some sections receiving more detailed explanation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the specific comments provided above. This work is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.
