

This paper investigates the estimation of structured signals from nonlinear measurements using the Generalized LASSO. The authors extend the recent work of Plan and Vershynin (2015) by providing precise asymptotic characterizations of the Generalized LASSO's performance when measurements follow the form y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0), where g is a nonlinear link function. Their key insight is that, for i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, the estimation error of the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that with appropriately scaled and noisy linear measurements. Specifically, they prove that the error ‖x̂ - μx_0‖_2^2 is asymptotically identical to the error when using linear measurements of the form y_lin = μA x_0 + σz, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2] for γ ~ N(0,1).

The authors' main contribution is Theorem 2.1, which establishes this equivalence, and Theorem 2.2, which provides an explicit, asymptotically precise expression for the estimation error. These results significantly strengthen previous work by Brillinger (1980s) and Plan and Vershynin (2015), who established only qualitative results about the estimator's behavior. The paper also demonstrates the practical implication that the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error is the celebrated Lloyd-Max quantizer, a novel insight with important applications in quantized compressed sensing.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The authors provide rigorous proofs for their claims, supported by numerical experiments (Figures 1-2). The theoretical analysis is particularly strong, as it establishes precise error expressions that hold for all values of the regularization parameter λ and for a wide class of regularizers f (including ℓ_1, group ℓ_1, and nuclear norms). This level of precision represents a substantial advance over previous work that provided only upper bounds.

The paper could be strengthened by better connecting to the rich literature on single-index models, which studies exactly this measurement model (y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)) under the assumption that g is unknown. While the authors briefly mention this in the introduction, they do not adequately discuss how their work relates to or extends this literature. For instance, the classical work by Ichimura (1993) on Semiparametric Least Squares (SL) and Weighted SL Estimation of Single-Index Models provides a relevant framework for this problem.

Additionally, the paper would benefit from addressing the recent work by Yi et al. (2015), "Optimal Linear Estimation under Unknown Nonlinear Transform," which also studies the equivalence between linear and nonlinear measurements. Yi et al. establish a similar equivalence under different conditions, particularly for cases where μ = 0 (which the current paper excludes). The authors should discuss how their results relate to and extend Yi et al.'s work.

The assumption that the measurement matrix has i.i.d. Gaussian entries is standard in this literature and necessary for the technical analysis, but the authors could provide more justification for why this assumption is critical for their results. While the paper mentions this in the modeling assumptions, a brief discussion of whether the results might extend to more general measurement matrices would be helpful.

Strengths:
- Provides precise asymptotic characterization of Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements
- Establishes a clean theoretical equivalence between nonlinear and linear measurements
- Derives explicit error expressions that capture the role of all relevant parameters
- Demonstrates practical implications (optimal quantizer is Lloyd-Max)
- Well-structured and technically sound

Weaknesses:
- Inadequate connection to single-index model literature
- Does not discuss related work by Yi et al. (2015)
- Limited discussion of the necessity of Gaussian measurement matrix assumption

The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding Generalized LASSO under nonlinear measurements. The precise error expressions are a notable advance over previous work, and the practical implication about the optimal quantizer is valuable. While the connection to related literature could be strengthened, the paper's technical contributions are substantial and well-supported.

Overall, this is a strong paper that advances our understanding of Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements. The theoretical results are novel, precise, and well-motivated, with clear practical implications. I recommend acceptance.
