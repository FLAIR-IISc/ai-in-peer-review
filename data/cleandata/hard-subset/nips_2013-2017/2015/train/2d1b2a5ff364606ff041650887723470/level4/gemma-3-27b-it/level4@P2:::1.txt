This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to measurements corrupted by non-linear functions. The authors establish a key result: under certain conditions, the performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of a linear model with scaled and noisy measurements. This is a significant contribution, offering a precise characterization of error bounds and insights into optimal quantization strategies. The paper is well-written and presents a thorough analysis, building upon previous work by Brillinger, Plan & Vershynin, and recent advances in LASSO analysis.

The core idea is to analyze the Generalized LASSO, which minimizes a squared error plus a regularization term, when the measurements are not directly the signal but a function of it (yi = gi(aTi x0)). The authors demonstrate that, in the large-sample limit with Gaussian measurement matrices, the non-linearity can be effectively accounted for by scaling and adding noise to a linear model. This allows leveraging the existing theoretical framework for linear models to precisely predict the estimation error. They derive explicit expressions for the mean-squared error, providing valuable insights into the roles of the link function, regularization, and measurement parameters. Notably, they identify the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error as the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper provides a novel and theoretically rigorous analysis of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, filling a gap in the existing literature.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears technically sound, with clear derivations and careful consideration of asymptotic behavior. The derivation of explicit error expressions is particularly strong.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally very well-written and clearly explains the motivations, assumptions, and results. The organization is logical and facilitates understanding.
*   **Significance:** The results have the potential to be impactful in applications where measurements are inherently non-linear, such as compressive sensing with quantized measurements. The identification of the optimal quantizer is a valuable practical contribution.
*   **Connections to Prior Work:** The paper effectively connects to and builds upon previous work in statistics, econometrics, and signal processing, properly citing relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Typographical Errors:** While generally well-written, the paper contains a noticeable number of typographical errors, particularly within the proof sections. While these do not affect understanding, they detract from the overall polish.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the paper provides theoretical results, it would benefit from more extensive empirical validation to demonstrate the practical relevance and accuracy of the derived error expressions. Figures 1 and 2 offer some insight, but more comprehensive experiments would strengthen the claims.
*   **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on strong assumptions (e.g., Gaussian measurement matrices, specific properties of the link function). The sensitivity of the results to deviations from these assumptions is not fully explored.
*   **Single-Index Model Literature:** The authors acknowledge limited familiarity with the single-index model/sufficient dimension reduction literature. It's possible that key insights or related results may already exist within that field, which the authors could benefit from exploring further. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound analysis of an important problem.
*   The derived results provide valuable insights into the performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and contributions.
*   The potential impact of the work in areas such as compressive sensing and signal processing is significant.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The presence of numerous typographical errors detracts from the overall quality.
*   The lack of extensive empirical validation limits the practical relevance of the results.
*   The sensitivity to assumptions and potential overlap with the single-index model literature require further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field. The theoretical results are novel, technically sound, and clearly presented. While the presence of typographical errors and the limited empirical validation are drawbacks, they do not outweigh the significant strengths of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** Specifically, the authors should carefully proofread the paper to correct the typographical errors. Additionally, I recommend adding some further empirical validation to support the theoretical findings. Addressing the reviewer concerns about the single-index model and providing a thorough literature review would further strengthen the paper.
