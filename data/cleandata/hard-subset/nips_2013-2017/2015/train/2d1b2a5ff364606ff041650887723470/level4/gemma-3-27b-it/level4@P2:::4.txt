This paper investigates linear estimation problems in the presence of nonlinear link functions. Building upon the work of Plan and Vershynin, the authors analyze the asymptotic estimation error achieved using the generalized LASSO for arbitrary link functions with a non-zero proportionality constant, µ. A key result, Theorem 2.1, demonstrates that the asymptotic error is equivalent whether the measurements are linear or nonlinear.

The paper establishes a precise connection between nonlinear and linear measurements, revealing that the performance of the generalized LASSO is governed by parameters µ and σ, which characterize the properties of the nonlinear function *g*. The derivation of explicit expressions for the estimation error, particularly in Theorem 2.2, represents a significant contribution, offering precise insights into the role of the link function, signal structure, regularizer, and regularization parameter. The authors also explore applications to quantized compressive sensing, identifying the optimal quantizer as the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper provides a significant theoretical advance by establishing a precise equivalence between nonlinear and scaled linear measurement models for the generalized LASSO. This simplifies the analysis of estimation errors in nonlinear settings.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous, with explicit derivations of the asymptotic error expressions. The extension of existing results to the nonlinear case is well-motivated and carefully executed.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, presenting a clear line of reasoning from the problem setup to the main results. The notation is mostly consistent.
*   **Practical Implications:** The application to quantized compressive sensing and the identification of the optimal Lloyd-Max quantizer demonstrate the potential practical impact of the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Gaussian Assumption:** The reliance on Gaussian measurement vectors (entries i.i.d. standard normal) is a significant limitation. The paper would benefit from a discussion of the robustness of the results to deviations from this assumption or an exploration of alternative measurement distributions. Justifying this assumption's necessity for the conclusions would strengthen the paper.
*   **Regularizer Choice:** While the paper acknowledges the importance of the regularizer *f* and its connection to the signal structure, it does not delve into the selection of the appropriate regularizer given a specific structural constraint on *x₀*. Expanding on this aspect would increase the paper's practical value.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper omits key references to related areas. Specifically:
    *   The extensive literature on single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction, which address linear and subspace estimation under unknown link functions, is not discussed.
    *   The recent work of Yi et al. ("Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform," 2015) presents an alternative algorithm for sparse recovery even when the link function has zero µ. This paper also demonstrates the equivalence of linear and nonlinear estimation in high dimensions under specific conditions and should be acknowledged.
*   **Dependence on µ and σ:** The authors should discuss the implications of the dependence on µ and σ. While they characterize the hardness of estimation, further elaboration on the impact of these parameters on algorithm design or performance bounds would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of high-dimensional estimation.
*   The results have the potential to significantly advance our understanding of the generalized LASSO in nonlinear settings.
*   The clarity of the presentation and the potential practical applications enhance the paper’s value.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong Gaussian assumption limits the generality of the results.
*   The lack of discussion on regularizer selection and the omission of relevant related work weaken the paper’s overall impact.
*   Further elaboration on the role of µ and σ would strengthen the analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written and technically sound paper with potentially significant contributions. While the Gaussian assumption and omissions in related work are concerns, the novelty and theoretical depth of the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly incorporating the missing references and discussing the limitations of the Gaussian assumption. A brief discussion on guidance for choosing the regularizer would also be beneficial.
