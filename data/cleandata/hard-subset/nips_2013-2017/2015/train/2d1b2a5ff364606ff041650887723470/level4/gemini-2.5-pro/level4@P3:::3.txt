This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO estimator for recovering structured signals from non-linear measurements under a Gaussian design matrix. The measurement model considered is yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where g is a potentially unknown and random non-linear link function. The authors analyze the standard Generalized LASSO, which naively fits a linear model to these non-linear observations.

The central and compelling result of the paper is that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime (where the number of measurements m and signal dimension n grow to infinity with a fixed ratio), the estimation performance of this approach is identical to that of a LASSO problem with linear measurements of the form yi = μa_i^T x0 + σzi. Here, μ and σ are constants that depend only on the link function g. This work builds upon and significantly strengthens the classical results of Brillinger and more recent non-asymptotic bounds by Plan and Vershynin [17]. The key contribution is moving beyond upper bounds to provide novel, asymptotically precise, and explicit expressions for the estimation error. The authors validate their theoretical predictions across several important special cases, including `l1` (LASSO), `l1,2` (Group LASSO), and nuclear-norm regularization. A particularly insightful application of the theory is presented for the design of optimal quantizers in q-bit compressive sensing, where the authors establish a connection to the classic Lloyd-Max quantizer.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper's main finding is elegant, powerful, and provides a rigorous justification for the heuristic that non-linear measurements can often be treated as scaled and noisy linear ones. This equivalence result provides a potent analytical tool, simplifying the analysis of a complex, non-linear system into a well-understood linear counterpart. This has the potential to impact how researchers analyze and design systems involving signal processing under hardware non-linearities.

2.  **Technical Novelty and Depth:** The primary contribution—providing the first asymptotically precise error characterizations for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements—is a significant advance. It moves the state-of-the-art beyond the non-asymptotic upper bounds of prior work [17], offering an exact understanding of the estimator's behavior. The derived expressions explicitly capture the interplay between the signal structure, the regularizer, the link function (via µ and σ), and the problem dimensions.

3.  **Generality and Broad Applicability:** The theoretical framework is presented in a general form that applies to a wide class of convex regularizers and structured signals. The paper demonstrates this by successfully applying the main theorem to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems, showcasing the framework's versatility.

4.  **Practical Insights:** The application to optimal q-bit quantizer design is an excellent demonstration of the theory's practical utility. By showing that minimizing the LASSO estimation error is equivalent to minimizing the σ²/μ² ratio, the authors translate a complex problem into a much simpler one. The resulting connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is both insightful and of practical value for system design.

5.  **Empirical Validation:** The provided simulations show an impressively accurate match between the theoretical predictions and the empirical performance of the LASSO estimator, lending strong support to the validity of the asymptotic analysis.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of Technical Assumptions:** Assumption 1 is central to the derivation of the main results, but its presentation is quite abstract. It would be highly beneficial for the reader if the authors provided a more intuitive explanation or a more formal interpretation of this condition, perhaps by connecting it to established concepts in variational analysis or convex geometry. This would make the paper's technical core more accessible.

2.  **Precision of Terminology:** The paper's clarity could be enhanced by adhering more strictly to standard terminology from the convex analysis and optimization literature.
    *   The Generalized LASSO is consistently referred to as an "algorithm." It is more accurately described as an estimator, a convex program, or an optimization problem. An algorithm would be a specific method used to solve this problem (e.g., ISTA, FISTA, ADMM).
    *   On page 5, the function `ef,τ(v)` is what is widely known as the Moreau envelope (or Moreau-Yosida regularization) of f. The "prox" notation used is potentially confusing, as `prox` is almost universally reserved for the proximal operator itself, not its value. Using the standard term "Moreau envelope" would improve clarity and better connect the work to the relevant literature.

3.  **Presentation and Intuition:** While well-written, the paper is dense due to conference space limitations. The main theorems are stated concisely with proofs deferred to the appendix. While necessary, this leaves the reader with little intuition about why the non-linear problem should converge to the specific linear one. Including a brief, high-level sketch of the proof argument in the main text could significantly improve the paper's pedagogical value.

### Minor Comments and Typos

*   The draft's comment about the equivalence of regularized and penalized versions is valid, but this is a well-known aspect of LASSO-type problems and not a major issue.
*   In the caption of Figure 1, 'n=768' should be typeset in math mode as '$n$=768' for consistency.
*   Page 5, line 247: There appears to be a typo at the start of the sentence "When measurements are linear...".
*   As mentioned above, I strongly suggest changing the notation and terminology related to the Moreau envelope on page 5 (line 294) to align with standard convex analysis literature.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper with significant contributions. The results are novel, impactful, and well-supported. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to presentation and terminology, and addressing them would further improve the quality and clarity of this excellent work.
