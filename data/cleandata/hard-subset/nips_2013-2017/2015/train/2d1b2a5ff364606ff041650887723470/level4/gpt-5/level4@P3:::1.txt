After reading the authors’ response and other reviews, my overall assessment remains largely unchanged.

Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies estimation under single-index/nonlinear measurement models y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian measurement vectors a_i, where g may be unknown and random. The authors analyze the generalized square-root LASSO (minimize ||y − A x||_2 + λ f(x), with f convex and often nonsmooth) for recovering structured signals (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank). The main result shows that, asymptotically, using the LASSO on nonlinear measurements is equivalent to using it on an effective linear model y = μ A x0 + σ z, with μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). This establishes that nonlinearity affects performance only through (μ, σ^2), yielding precise predictions for the LASSO error.

Building on the CGMT framework and related precise analyses for linear models (e.g., Bayati–Montanari; Stojnic; Thrampoulidis–Oymak–Hassibi), the paper derives an explicit scalar convex optimization whose unique solution α* gives the asymptotic error ||x̂ − μ x0||_2 → α*. The authors then specialize to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank settings, producing closed-form characterizations in the sparse case (including a critical λ phenomenon for δ < 1). The results refine and substantially strengthen prior work by Brillinger (who handled least squares with fixed n, m → ∞) and by Plan–Vershynin (who provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO). An appealing application shows that for q-bit quantized sensing, minimizing the LASSO error reduces to optimizing σ^2/μ^2, and that Lloyd–Max quantizers are stationary points of this objective.

Quality:
The technical development is strong and careful. Theorems 2.1–2.2 provide a clean equivalence and an exact error characterization via a low-dimensional convex program, which appears correct and novel. The specialization to sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3) yields transparent fixed-point equations, matches prior special cases for Gaussian signals, and aligns well with simulations. Assumption 1 (limit of a scaled Moreau envelope of the convex conjugate) is reasonable in the showcased examples and connects naturally to standard choices of f and priors for x0. The reliance on i.i.d. Gaussian designs is standard for CGMT-based precise asymptotics. A finite-sample discussion is limited, but the asymptotic predictions typically track well in moderate dimensions in this literature.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and well organized, with a clear narrative from model to equivalence to precise predictions and applications. However, there are numerous typos and small grammatical issues, especially in the proofs. The terminology around “generalized LASSO,” “L2-LASSO,” and “square-root LASSO” should be unified and made consistent with standard definitions (the objective here uses the ℓ2 norm, not the squared loss). It would help to provide intuitive explanations for μ and σ^2 (e.g., link to Hermite expansions/Stein’s lemma), and to summarize how to compute the function F for common regularizers within the main text (with code or pseudocode in the supplement).

Originality:
The equivalence result and precise asymptotic error characterization for LASSO under nonlinear measurements appear to be the first of their kind. The work both generalizes Brillinger to high-dimensional regularized estimators and sharpens Plan–Vershynin by converting loose bounds into exact asymptotics. The extension to a broad class of convex regularizers (beyond ℓ1) is a clear advance.

Significance:
The results advance our theoretical understanding of LASSO under model mismatch/nonlinear sensing and provide actionable guidance: performance can be read off from the corresponding linear model with effective (μ, σ^2), enabling principled choices of λ and even quantizer design. This will be valuable to researchers in high-dimensional statistics, signal processing, and learning with quantized/censored/1-bit data.

Strengths:
- Clear equivalence between nonlinear and effective linear models with precise asymptotic error predictions.
- General framework covering many convex regularizers and structures.
- Sharp specialization for sparse recovery; verification by simulations.
- Useful application to quantizer design and connection to Lloyd–Max.
- Bridges and extends multiple lines of prior work (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin; CGMT/AMP literature).

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Gaussian design: Results require i.i.d. N(0,1) A. A discussion of universality to sub-Gaussian or structured designs (partial Fourier, subsampled orthogonal) would broaden impact; even empirical evidence would help.
- Assumption μ ≠ 0: Clarify implications and alternatives when μ = 0 (e.g., purely odd/even links), and relate to methods tailored for 1-bit CS that can recover direction without this condition.
- Assumption 1: Provide more guidance on verifying it for common f and x0 models (including low-rank), and, where possible, furnish explicit formulas or numerical recipes for F(c1, c2, c3).
- Finite-sample behavior: Add experiments quantifying convergence to the asymptotic predictions and sensitivity to λ, δ, and noise level. A comparison with the constrained LASSO of Plan–Vershynin would be informative.
- Notation and terminology: Harmonize the use of “generalized LASSO,” “L2-LASSO,” and “square-root LASSO”; define all symbols on first use; ensure constants and scaling (e.g., 1/√n factors) are consistent throughout.
- Quantizer optimization: The Lloyd–Max connection provides stationarity; briefly discuss global optimality and initialization, and include empirical comparisons across different initializations.
- Minor: Clean up typos and grammar throughout, especially in the proofs; fix small inconsistencies (e.g., missing subscripts/superscripts, spacing, and punctuation).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution with precise, broadly applicable results.
- Clear advancement over and unification of prior work.
- Practical insights (e.g., quantizer design) backed by analysis and experiments.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Restriction to Gaussian designs and asymptotic regime; limited discussion of universality and finite-sample guarantees.
- Technical assumption (Assumption 1) may feel abstract to some readers; more guidance/examples would help.

Overall, this is a high-quality and impactful paper that, with minor revisions for clarity and additional discussion of scope and assumptions, would make a valuable contribution. My confidence is moderate-high.
