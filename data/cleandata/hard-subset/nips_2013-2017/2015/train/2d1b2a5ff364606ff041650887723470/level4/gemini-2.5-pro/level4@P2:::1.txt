### Review

**Summary**

This paper analyzes the performance of the Generalized LASSO (or square-root LASSO) for estimating a structured signal `x_0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)`. The measurement matrix `A` is assumed to have i.i.d. standard Gaussian entries, and the link function `g` can be non-linear, random, and even unknown. The authors' main contribution is to demonstrate, in a precise asymptotic sense (as dimensions `m, n` grow large), that using the standard LASSO objective—which naively assumes a linear model—yields an estimate `x̂` whose performance is equivalent to that of an estimator operating on linear measurements.

Specifically, the paper shows that the mean squared error `||x̂ - μx_0||^2` under the non-linear model converges to the same value as the error from a corresponding linear model `y_lin = A(μx_0) + σz`, where `z` is standard Gaussian noise. The scaling factor `μ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the link function `g`, via `μ = E[γg(γ)]` and `σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2]` for a standard normal variable `γ`.

This result significantly strengthens prior work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, moving beyond consistency proofs and upper bounds to provide exact, deterministic expressions for the asymptotic estimation error. The framework is general, accommodating various convex regularizers (e.g., `l1`, nuclear norm, group LASSO) and signal structures. As a practical application, the authors show that their result simplifies the problem of designing optimal q-bit quantizers to the more tractable problem of minimizing the ratio `σ^2/μ^2`, connecting it to the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance:** The central result establishing the equivalence between the non-linear and a corresponding linear measurement model is both elegant and powerful. It provides a deep insight into why a "mismatched" estimator like the LASSO can be surprisingly effective for non-linear observations. This fundamentally advances our understanding of high-dimensional estimation in settings beyond the standard linear model.

2.  **Originality and Quality:** The paper provides the first *precise* asymptotic characterization of the LASSO's performance with non-linear measurements. While building on the ideas of Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, the move from upper bounds to exact formulas is a major technical leap. The derived error expressions (e.g., in Theorem 2.2 and 2.3) are explicit and quantitatively capture the interplay between the number of measurements (`δ`), the signal structure, the regularizer (`f`), the regularization parameter (`λ`), and the properties of the non-linearity (via `μ` and `σ`). The theoretical claims are well-supported by numerical simulations that show a remarkable match with the predictions.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the problem, clearly positioning the work within the context of related literature (classical single-index models, recent high-dimensional analysis of LASSO). The main results are stated clearly, and the application to compressive sensing with q-bit quantization serves as a compelling demonstration of the theory's utility.

4.  **Generality:** The theoretical framework is impressively general. It applies to a broad class of convex regularizers, which allows the results to cover sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems under a unified lens. The theorems appear to be novel even in the special case of linear measurements for certain regularizers (e.g., group-sparse and nuclear norm).

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on standard but strong assumptions common in this line of work, namely i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices and a large-system asymptotic regime (`m, n -> ∞` with `m/n` fixed). The practical performance for finite dimensions and for structured or non-Gaussian measurement ensembles remains an open question. A brief discussion on the limitations imposed by these assumptions would be beneficial.

2.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** The manuscript, particularly the appendix containing the proofs, would benefit from a thorough proofreading to correct several typographical errors. While this does not detract from the core results, it would improve the overall quality and readability of the paper.

3.  **Connection to Related Fields:** The paper is well-situated within the high-dimensional statistics and signal processing literature. However, the problem formulation falls under the classical "single-index model" in statistics and econometrics. While the authors cite some key works, the connection to the broader literature on sufficient dimension reduction could potentially be strengthened. (Disclaimer: this is not my primary area of expertise, so I may be unaware of existing connections or distinctions).

**Overall Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper presenting a significant and surprising theoretical result. The authors provide a precise and general characterization of the performance of the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements, proving its asymptotic equivalence to a simple linear model. The strengths—in terms of significance, technical quality, and clarity—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper represents a substantial contribution to the field and is very well-suited for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
