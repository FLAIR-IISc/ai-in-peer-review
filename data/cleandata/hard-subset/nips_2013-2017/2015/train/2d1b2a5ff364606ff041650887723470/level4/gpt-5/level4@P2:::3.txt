Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies recovery of structured signals from nonlinear observations of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian measurement vectors. It proposes using the (regularized) generalized LASSO and shows that, asymptotically, the estimation error under nonlinear measurements matches that of a corresponding linear model with an effective gain and noise level: yi ≈ μ a_i^T x0 + σ z_i, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2] for γ ∼ N(0,1). This strengthens Brillinger’s classical result for least squares (fixed n, m → ∞) and significantly extends the non-asymptotic constrained-LASSO bounds of Plan and Vershynin by providing precise asymptotic error predictions for penalized LASSO with general convex regularizers. The main theorem gives an exact scalar convex program whose optimizer yields the asymptotic MSE. Specializations cover sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) models. An application to quantized sensing shows that optimizing the LASSO error leads to the Lloyd–Max quantizer.

Quality (technical soundness):
The results appear technically solid and consistent with recent CGMT/AMP-based analyses for linear models. The equivalence between nonlinear and corresponding linearized measurements is clearly stated, and the scalar optimization formula provides a concrete, checkable prediction. Assumptions (i.i.d. Gaussian A, μ ≠ 0, bounded moments, linear asymptotics) are explicit. It would be helpful to add a brief proof sketch to guide readers through the main steps.

Clarity (presentation):
The paper is generally well written and well organized, with clear positioning relative to [5, 17] and the CGMT literature. Some notation could be streamlined (see detailed comments), and certain parts (e.g., Assumption 1 and its interpretation) would benefit from more explanation. Space constraints understandably push details to the appendix.

Originality:
High. To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact characterization of the LASSO error under general nonlinear single-index measurements and general convex regularizers. The quantizer-design consequence is novel and insightful.

Significance:
High. The work unifies a widely used heuristic (“treat nonlinear as linear with effective parameters”) with precise theory. It offers practical guidance for regularization and quantizer design and should influence both theory and applications in compressed sensing, statistics, and signal processing.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual contribution: precise equivalence of nonlinear and effective linear models for LASSO.
- General framework covering multiple structures and regularizers.
- Exact scalar convex program for asymptotic MSE; interpretable and computable.
- Specializations to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank cases with accurate empirical validation.
- Practical application to q-bit quantization, recovering Lloyd–Max as optimal under the proposed criterion.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Gaussian design assumption is restrictive; robustness to sub-Gaussian or structured designs is not explored.
- Assumption μ ≠ 0 excludes important links (e.g., even functions); a brief discussion of alternatives when μ = 0 would be useful.
- Results are asymptotic; finite-sample accuracy and deviations are only empirically illustrated.
- Assumption 1 is technical; more intuition and references from variational analysis would aid understanding.
- The approach presumes knowledge (or estimability) of μ and σ; guidance on estimating these from data would be valuable.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
- Constrained vs. penalized forms: Please clarify the equivalence and give the mapping between the constraint radius and λ (Page 4, around line 193).
- Figure 1 caption: typeset consistently; “n=768” should be “n = 768”.
- Page 5, line 247: there is a typo in the sentence beginning “When …”.
- Page 5, line 294: What is called the “proximal function” is the Moreau envelope (with index τ). Consider renaming it “Moreau envelope” and reserving prox for the proximal operator.
- Assumption 1: Provide a more formal interpretation using tools from variational analysis (e.g., stability of Moreau envelopes and epi-convergence; standard references such as Rockafellar–Wets). Concrete examples verifying the assumption (beyond ℓ1 and ℓ1,2) would help.
- Terminology: Avoid calling the generalized LASSO an “algorithm.” It is a convex program/estimator; algorithms solve it.
- Experiments:
  - Add results for sub-Gaussian designs (e.g., Rademacher) to test robustness.
  - Explore finite-sample deviations from the asymptotic predictions and the effect of λ tuning (e.g., via cross-validation vs. theory-driven choices).
  - Discuss sensitivity to mis-specification or empirical estimation of μ and σ.
  - Consider including the constrained LASSO comparison, since [17] focused on that variant.
  - Provide code or pseudocode for computing the scalar optimization and for reproducing figures.

Questions for the authors:
- Can the equivalence extend to sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant designs? What breaks in the proof?
- How do you recommend estimating μ and σ when g is unknown? Are method-of-moments consistent and does this affect the asymptotic MSE?
- Is there a path to finite-sample, high-probability error bounds informed by your asymptotic characterization?
- For μ = 0, can pre-processing (e.g., monotone transformations) or alternative estimators recover directionality?

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, novel theory with clear practical implications.
- Broad applicability across structured models and regularizers.
- Convincing empirical validation.
- Insightful connection to optimal quantization (Lloyd–Max).

Cons for acceptance:
- Gaussian design assumption and μ ≠ 0 restriction limit scope.
- Heavy reliance on asymptotics; limited finite-sample theory.
- Some notational and terminological issues; Assumption 1 needs more clarity.

Overall evaluation:
This is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution that will interest the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance after minor revisions addressing clarity, terminology, and expanded discussion of assumptions and robustness.
