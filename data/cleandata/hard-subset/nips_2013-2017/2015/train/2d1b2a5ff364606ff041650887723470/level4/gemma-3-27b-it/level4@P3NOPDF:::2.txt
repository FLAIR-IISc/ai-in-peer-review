This article extends the established theoretical understanding of the l2-lasso to the more general setting of single-index models, and importantly, relaxes the common Gaussian assumption on the prior distribution of the input features. This represents a worthwhile contribution to the literature concerning the precise asymptotic behavior of lasso-type estimators. The paper is generally well-written and presents its results clearly.

However, there are a couple of points that would benefit from further attention. First, the connection between single-index models and linear models with scaled signals and non-standard noise, as originally highlighted by Brillinger (1982), is rigorously demonstrated in Appendix A. Mentioning this equivalence briefly in the main text – particularly when introducing the single-index model – would provide a useful heuristic justification for the statement made in the blockquote on line 140. This would strengthen the reader’s intuition regarding the theoretical framework.

Second, the paper specifically focuses on the l2-lasso (also known as the square-root lasso). A key advantage of the l2-lasso over the more commonly used l2^2-lasso is that its theoretically optimal regularization parameter does not depend on knowledge of the noise variance (σ). While the paper provides a detailed statistical characterization, a potentially impactful extension of this work would be to investigate whether this advantage – the variance-independence of the optimal parameter – translates into practical benefits in the single-index model setting. Exploring this question could significantly strengthen the practical justification for employing the l2-lasso, and would be of substantial interest to practitioners. 

Specifically, it would be valuable to understand if, in practical scenarios, the l2-lasso consistently outperforms the l2^2-lasso even when the noise variance is estimated (which is often the case in applications). Even a discussion of the limitations of the theoretical advantage in finite-sample settings would be a valuable contribution.

In summary, this is a solid theoretical paper that makes a clear contribution to the understanding of high-dimensional statistical estimation. Addressing the above points would further enhance the impact and practical relevance of the work.
