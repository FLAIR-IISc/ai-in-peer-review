

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to non-linear measurements of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), where the link function g is potentially unknown and non-linear. The authors build upon the seminal work of Brillinger [5] and Plan and Vershynin [17], who established that under Gaussian measurement matrices, the solution to the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to a linear measurement model with appropriately scaled noise. The key contribution of this paper is providing precise asymptotic expressions for the estimation error that hold for a wide range of regularizers (L1, group L1, nuclear norm) and link functions, demonstrating that the estimation performance with non-linear measurements is asymptotically identical to that with linear measurements of the form yi = μai^T x0 + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2] for γ ~ N(0,1).

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it extends the results of Plan and Vershynin [17] by providing precise error expressions rather than upper bounds, which allows for a complete characterization of the estimation performance. Second, it establishes that the optimal quantizer for q-bit compressive sensing is the Lloyd-Max quantizer, a significant insight with practical implications for signal acquisition systems. Third, it generalizes the analysis to various structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) using the convex Gaussian min-max theorem framework.

The quality of the paper is excellent. The theoretical results are rigorously developed and supported by comprehensive numerical validation. The authors carefully establish the precise asymptotic behavior of the error under the high-dimensional regime where m/n = δ ∈ (0,∞), which is a significant improvement over previous work that either considered fixed dimension (Brillinger [5]) or provided only upper bounds (Plan and Vershynin [17]). The proofs are technically sound, and the assumptions are well-justified.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though the treatment of Assumption 1 could be strengthened. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the interpretation of Assumption 1 in terms of variational analysis, as suggested in the draft review. The notation for the Moreau envelope (referred to as "proximal function" in the paper) should be clarified to align with standard convex analysis terminology. The paper would also benefit from a clearer distinction between the Generalized LASSO (a convex optimization program) and algorithms that solve it.

The originality of the work is strong. The precise error expressions for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements represent a significant theoretical advance. While Plan and Vershynin [17] established that the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements behaves like a linear measurement model with appropriate scaling, this paper provides the exact characterization of the error that was previously missing. The connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer is particularly novel and insightful.

The significance of the work is substantial. The results provide a deeper theoretical understanding of how structured signal recovery algorithms behave with non-linear measurements, which is crucial for applications in sensor networks, medical imaging, and other fields where measurement non-linearities are common. The precise error expressions enable better design of recovery algorithms and provide practical guidance for parameter selection. The application to optimal quantizer design for q-bit compressive sensing is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates a direct connection between theoretical analysis and practical engineering solutions.

Strengths:
- Provides precise asymptotic error expressions for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements
- Generalizes results to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems
- Establishes a novel connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer for optimal q-bit compressive sensing
- Rigorous theoretical analysis supported by comprehensive numerical validation
- Clear demonstration of the equivalence between non-linear and appropriately scaled linear measurements

Weaknesses:
- Assumption 1 could be better motivated and interpreted
- Some technical notations (e.g., "proximal function") could be clarified to align with standard convex analysis terminology
- The distinction between the Generalized LASSO (a convex program) and algorithms that solve it could be clearer
- The paper could provide more intuition about the practical implications of the precise error expressions

The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the understanding of structured signal recovery with non-linear measurements. It aligns well with the NIPS scope, addressing a fundamental theoretical question with important practical implications. The results are novel, well-validated, and represent a clear advancement over previous work.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution with precise error expressions, comprehensive validation across multiple structured recovery problems, practical application to optimal quantizer design.

Con: Some technical aspects could be clarified for better accessibility, though this is a minor issue given the paper's technical nature.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field, providing a precise characterization of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements that was previously unavailable. The results have clear theoretical significance and practical implications for signal recovery in the presence of measurement non-linearities. The paper is well-written and rigorously developed, making it suitable for publication at NIPS.
