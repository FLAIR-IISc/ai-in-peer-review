### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper analyzes the performance of the Generalized LASSO (or square-root LASSO) for recovering a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. The measurement matrix `A` has i.i.d. Gaussian entries, and the link function `g` can be non-linear, random, and even unknown. The core contribution is a powerful and elegant theoretical result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit (where `m, n -> infinity` with `m/n -> δ`), the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimate in this non-linear setting is precisely equivalent to the error of the LASSO in a corresponding *linear* measurement setting, `yi = μ a_i^T x0 + σ z_i`. Here, `μ` and `σ` are constants that depend only on the link function `g`, defined as `μ = E[γg(γ)]` and `σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2]` for a standard normal variable `γ`.

Building on this equivalence, the paper provides an exact asymptotic characterization of the estimation error, `‖x̂ - μx0‖^2`, via a small, deterministic convex optimization program. The authors then specialize this general result to several important structured recovery problems, including sparse signals (`l1`-norm), group-sparse signals (`l1,2`-norm), and low-rank matrices (nuclear norm). As a practical application, they demonstrate that their framework can be used to analyze `q`-bit quantized compressive sensing, concluding that the optimal quantizer that minimizes the LASSO's estimation error is the classic Lloyd-Max quantizer.

This work builds upon the classical results of Brillinger for the unregularized, low-dimensional case and the more recent high-dimensional analysis of Plan and Vershynin. It significantly strengthens these previous results by replacing non-asymptotic upper bounds with asymptotically exact error characterizations for the more commonly used regularized LASSO formulation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The main result establishing the asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear and a specific linear model is a significant theoretical contribution. It provides a clean, intuitive, and powerful explanation for why a seemingly misspecified model (applying LASSO directly to non-linear observations) is effective. This insight is likely to be influential for researchers studying similar problems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper delivers precise, asymptotically exact formulas for the estimation error. This represents a major advance over the existing literature, which has primarily focused on upper bounds that often contain unknown constants. The derived formulas explicitly capture the interplay between the number of measurements (`δ`), the signal structure (via the function `F`), the link function (via `μ` and `σ`), and the regularization parameter (`λ`). The theoretical predictions are convincingly validated by numerical simulations.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the context of prior literature, and succinctly summarizes the main contributions. The progression from the general theorem to specific applications (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) is logical and effective.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The application to `q`-bit compressive sensing is a compelling demonstration of the theory's utility. The finding that the optimal quantizer design problem simplifies to minimizing the `σ^2/μ^2` ratio, and that this is achieved by the Lloyd-Max quantizer, is an interesting and practical result.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Positioning Relative to Single-Index Models:** The paper positions itself mainly with respect to the high-dimensional statistics and signal processing literature. While the introduction acknowledges the connection to the classical single-index model from statistics and econometrics, a more detailed discussion of how these results relate to or advance that extensive body of work could strengthen the paper's contribution and clarify its novelty in that context.

2.  **Typos in Proofs:** The main body of the paper is very polished, but the supplementary material containing the proofs would benefit from careful proofreading. There are a number of typos and minor grammatical errors. While these do not appear to affect the correctness of the results, correcting them would improve the manuscript's overall quality.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a fundamental and elegant theoretical result that is a significant advance in the analysis of high-dimensional estimation with non-linearities.
*   **Pro:** It provides precise, verifiable formulas for estimation error, replacing previous, looser bounds.
*   **Pro:** The work is clearly written, well-motivated, and the theoretical findings are supported by strong numerical evidence.
*   **Pro:** The application to quantizer design provides a concrete and valuable practical outcome of the theory.
*   **Con:** The manuscript could better articulate its contribution in relation to the extensive literature on single-index models.
*   **Con:** The appendix requires proofreading to fix minor errors.

In my view, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses, which can be easily addressed in a final version. The paper makes a substantial and insightful contribution to the field.
