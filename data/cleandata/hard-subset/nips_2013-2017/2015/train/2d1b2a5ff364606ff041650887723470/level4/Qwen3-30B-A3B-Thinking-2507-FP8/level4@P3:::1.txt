

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper addresses the important problem of signal estimation from non-linear measurements using the Generalized LASSO framework. The authors consider the scenario where measurements take the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), with ai being rows of a Gaussian measurement matrix and gi being independent copies of a possibly unknown link function g. They establish that the Generalized LASSO, designed for linear measurements, can be effectively applied in this nonlinear setting, with the key insight that the estimation error asymptotically matches that of a linear measurement model with appropriately scaled noise.

The paper builds upon and significantly extends the work of Brillinger (1980s) and Plan and Vershynin (2013). While Brillinger showed that the LS solution is a consistent estimator of x0 up to a constant μ, and Plan and Vershynin provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained version of the LASSO, the authors deliver the first precise asymptotic characterization of the estimation error for the regularized Generalized LASSO. Their main result (Theorem 2.1) establishes that the error for non-linear measurements is asymptotically identical to that of a linear measurement model with scaled and noisy measurements, where the scaling factor μ and noise variance σ² are determined by the link function g through μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ) - μγ)²] for γ ~ N(0,1).

**Strengths:**

*Quality*: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs establishing precise asymptotic guarantees. The authors carefully develop the theoretical framework and provide precise characterizations of the estimation error that hold for all values of the regularization parameter λ. The results are well-supported by theorems and are applicable to a wide range of structured signals (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) with various regularizers (ℓ₁, nuclear norm, etc.). The authors appropriately address the limitations of their approach, noting that the signal can only be estimated up to a constant of proportionality when the link function is unknown.

*Clarity*: The paper is well-organized and generally clearly written. The connection between the nonlinear and linear measurement models is explained with care, and the main results are presented in a logical progression. The paper is accessible to researchers familiar with the LASSO literature, though some technical details (particularly in the proofs) could be presented more clearly. The authors provide illustrative examples and figures that effectively demonstrate the accuracy of their theoretical predictions.

*Originality*: The paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first precise asymptotic analysis of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. While previous work established qualitative results (that the LASSO performs well up to scaling), this paper delivers quantitative error expressions that explicitly capture the role of the link function, signal structure, and regularization parameter. The application to q-bit compressive sensing, showing that the optimal quantizer is the Lloyd-Max quantizer, represents a novel and practically relevant consequence of their general framework.

*Significance*: The results have important theoretical and practical implications. The precise error characterization provides deeper understanding of the LASSO's behavior in nonlinear settings, which is valuable for both theoretical development and practical applications. The connection between nonlinear measurements and scaled linear measurements simplifies the analysis of LASSO-type algorithms in nonlinear scenarios. The application to optimal quantization design (Lloyd-Max) has immediate relevance for measurement system design in compressed sensing.

**Weaknesses:**

*Clarity*: While generally well-written, the paper contains several typos, particularly in the proof sections, which slightly diminish its readability. The presentation of Assumption 1 could be clarified with more intuitive explanation of why this condition is natural and how it is satisfied in common cases.

*Originality*: The paper does not sufficiently address the relationship to the single-index model literature, which is the primary context for the measurement model considered. While the authors reference some works, they could better position their contribution within this well-established statistical framework. The connection to sufficient dimension reduction problems is mentioned but not fully leveraged.

*Significance*: The practical implications could be strengthened by providing more concrete examples of applications where the nonlinear measurement model is relevant (beyond the mentioned 1-bit quantization), and by discussing how the precise error characterization can guide practical parameter selection in real-world applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the analysis of the LASSO with non-linear measurements. The precise error characterization is novel, technically sound, and provides valuable insights into the behavior of LASSO-type algorithms in nonlinear settings. The connection between non-linear measurements and appropriately scaled linear measurements is elegantly established, and the application to optimal quantization design is particularly noteworthy.

The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field. The minor issues with typos and the need for better contextualization within the single-index model literature do not detract from the substantial theoretical advances presented. The results represent a meaningful extension of previous work on the LASSO and have both theoretical and practical significance.

I recommend acceptance. This paper advances our understanding of the LASSO in nonlinear settings with rigorous, precise results that will be valuable to researchers working at the intersection of signal processing, statistics, and machine learning.
