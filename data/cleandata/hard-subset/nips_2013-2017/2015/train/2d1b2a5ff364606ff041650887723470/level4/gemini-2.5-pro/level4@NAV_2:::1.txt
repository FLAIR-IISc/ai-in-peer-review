This paper analyzes the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimating a structured signal `x0` from measurements `y` that have undergone a non-linear transformation of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. The central and compelling result is that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit, the estimation error of this LASSO problem is precisely equivalent to that of a standard LASSO problem with linear measurements corrupted by additive Gaussian noise, specifically `yi = μ a_i^T x0 + σ z_i`. The effective signal scaling `μ` and noise level `σ` are determined solely by the statistical properties of the non-linear link function `g`. This work builds upon and significantly strengthens key lines of previous research. It generalizes the classical results of Brillinger, which were limited to unregularized least-squares and a fixed-dimension setting, to the modern high-dimensional, regularized setting. Furthermore, it refines the recent work of Plan and Vershynin by replacing their non-asymptotic upper bounds with asymptotically exact characterizations of the estimation error.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Quality:** The main finding—that the LASSO with non-linear measurements behaves identically to one with specific linear measurements—is an elegant and powerful theoretical contribution. It provides a deep insight into why a method designed for linear models works so well in this non-linear setting. The practical application to designing optimal Lloyd-Max quantizers is a strong demonstration of the theory's utility.
*   **Originality and Technical Depth:** The paper provides explicit, asymptotically exact formulas for the mean-squared error (Theorems 2.2 and 2.3). These results represent a major technical achievement and appear to be the first of their kind for LASSO-type programs with non-linear observations. The derivations are rigorous, and the results appear novel even for some of the specialized linear measurement scenarios considered (e.g., group-sparse and low-rank recovery).
*   **Clarity and Empirical Validation:** The paper is very well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively situates the problem within the literature, and the main results are stated with precision. The numerical simulations presented in Figures 1 and 2 provide excellent corroboration of the theoretical predictions, demonstrating their remarkable accuracy.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** The manuscript would benefit from a careful proofreading, as there are a number of typographical errors, particularly in the appendices. Correcting these would improve the overall quality of the presentation.
*   **Scope of Assumptions:** The analysis relies on standard but strong assumptions, namely an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix and an asymptotic (m, n → ∞) regime. While typical for this line of work, a brief discussion on the potential for relaxing these assumptions to other measurement ensembles or providing finite-sample insights would strengthen the paper.
*   **Relation to Single-Index Models:** The paper connects well to the high-dimensional statistics literature but could be strengthened by further clarifying its relationship to the extensive literature on single-index models in statistics and econometrics. While some key works are cited, it is not entirely clear if this equivalence result (or a similar one) is known in that field under different assumptions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a highly significant and novel theoretical result establishing a precise equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models for the LASSO.
*   Offers the first asymptotically exact performance characterization for the Generalized LASSO in this setting, a substantial advance over existing upper bounds.
*   The analysis is technically deep, and the results are rigorously derived and convincingly validated by experiments.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and addresses a problem of broad interest to the machine learning and signal processing communities.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The technical assumptions (i.i.d. Gaussian matrix, asymptotics) are restrictive, though common in this research area.
*   The manuscript contains several typographical errors that require correction before publication.
