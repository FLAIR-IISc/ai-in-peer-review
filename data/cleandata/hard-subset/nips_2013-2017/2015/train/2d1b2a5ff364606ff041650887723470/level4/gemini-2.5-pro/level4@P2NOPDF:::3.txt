**Summary**

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized Lasso for non-linear measurements with a Gaussian design. The work is motivated by the common heuristic that non-linear observations can be approximated as linear observations corrupted by noise. Building upon the foundational work of Plan and Vershynin [17], the authors extend these results to an asymptotic regime, deriving novel, precise, and explicit expressions for the estimation error. The theoretical predictions are shown to be highly accurate across several important special cases, including the standard Lasso, the group Lasso, and nuclear norm minimization. A particularly insightful contribution is the application of these results to the design of optimal quantizers in q-bit compressed sensing, establishing a clear connection to the classic Lloyd-Max quantizer.

**Quality**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of non-linear inverse problems. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the results are significant.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, the depth of the treatment is somewhat unbalanced across different sections. While this is likely a consequence of strict space limitations, certain theoretical arguments and derivations would benefit from more detailed explanations.

**Originality**

While the paper builds upon the framework established by Plan and Vershynin [17], it introduces significant and novel contributions. Foremost among these are the new, explicit expressions for the estimation error, which represent a substantial advance over existing results that typically rely on looser, order-wise bounds.

**Comments**

1.  On page 4, line 193, it would be helpful to explicitly state that the regularized and penalized versions of the problem are equivalent under an appropriate correspondence of their respective parameters.

2.  In the caption of Figure 1, for consistency with standard mathematical notation, 'n=768' should be formatted as '$n=768$'.

3.  There appears to be a typo on page 5, line 247, in the sentence beginning with "When...".

4.  On page 5, line 294, the function referred to as the "proximal function" is well-known in convex analysis as the Moreau envelope of index \(\tau\). The notation `prox` is potentially confusing, as it is standardly reserved for the proximal operator, not the Moreau envelope itself. I strongly suggest adopting the standard terminology and notation to improve clarity and avoid ambiguity.

5.  Regarding Assumption 1 on page 5, the presentation would be enhanced by providing a more formal interpretation, perhaps by connecting it to established results from variational analysis.

6.  Throughout the manuscript, the generalized Lasso is occasionally referred to as an "algorithm." It would be more precise to describe it as a convex program, an estimator, or a "decoder" (using compressed sensing terminology), as it defines a solution rather than the specific computational procedure for finding it.
