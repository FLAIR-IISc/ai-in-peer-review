This paper investigates linear estimation problems in the presence of a nonlinear link function. Building upon the work of Plan and Vershynin, the authors analyze the asymptotic estimation error achieved using a generalized lasso approach for arbitrary link functions when the mean parameter, μ, is non-zero. A key finding is that the asymptotic error remains identical to that presented in Theorem 2.1, regardless of whether the link function is linear or nonlinear.

However, Theorem 2.1 relies on the assumption of a normally distributed linear measurement vector, which may be restrictive in certain applications. The authors should provide further justification for the necessity of this assumption to reach their conclusions.

Several questions remain. First, how should the regularization parameter be selected based on the set, *kai*, containing the true parameter vector, x₀? While the paper discusses the use of convex regularizers, it does not explicitly address this practical consideration. Second, while the results demonstrate equivalence between linear and nonlinear measurements, the resulting error characterization depends on μ and σ, parameters that quantify the difficulty of estimation under the nonlinear function *g*. It would be valuable to understand the authors’ perspective on the necessity of this dependence. 

The manuscript would benefit from including references to related work. Specifically, the paper does not cite research on linear vector/subspace estimation under unknown link functions within the context of single index models and sufficient dimension reduction. Additionally, the recent paper by Yi et al. ("Optimal linear estimation under unknown linear estimation") introduces a novel algorithm for sparse recovery, even when the link function has μ = 0, and establishes the equivalence of high-dimensional linear estimation with both linear and nonlinear link functions under specific conditions – a result supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical validation.

Overall, this is a well-written paper presenting interesting and potentially significant results.
