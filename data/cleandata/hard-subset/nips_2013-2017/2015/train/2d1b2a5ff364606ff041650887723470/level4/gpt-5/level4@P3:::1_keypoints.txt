Paper studies LASSO under single-index/nonlinear measurements and shows asymptotic equivalence to an effective linear model y = μ A x0 + σ z with μ = E[γg(γ)], σ^2 = E[(g(γ)−μγ)^2].

Main theorem: the asymptotic LASSO error equals α*, the unique solution of a scalar convex program; nonlinearity affects performance only via (μ, σ^2).

Derivation uses CGMT and related precise-asymptotics literature, yielding closed-form characterizations for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank cases.

Sparse specialization (Theorem 2.3) gives transparent fixed-point equations and a critical-λ phenomenon for δ < 1; matches simulations and prior Gaussian signal cases.

Application to q-bit quantized sensing reduces LASSO performance to optimizing σ^2/μ^2, with Lloyd–Max quantizers as stationary points.

Technical development is strong and careful; Theorems 2.1–2.2 are novel and appear correct.

Assumption 1 (Moreau envelope limit) is reasonable for showcased examples but is somewhat abstract and needs more guidance.

Results rely on i.i.d. Gaussian designs (standard for CGMT) which limits generality; finite-sample discussion is limited.

Paper is generally well written and organized but contains numerous typos, grammatical issues, and inconsistent terminology for LASSO variants.

Recommend adding intuitive explanations for μ and σ^2 (e.g., Hermite/Stein) and summarizing how to compute F for common regularizers (code/pseudocode in supplement).

Originality: first precise asymptotic error characterization for LASSO under nonlinear measurements, sharpening Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin and extending to general convex regularizers.

Significance: advances theoretical understanding of LASSO under model mismatch and provides actionable guidance for λ selection and quantizer design.

Strengths: clear equivalence result, broad framework for convex regularizers, sharp sparse results, quantizer application, and strong connections to prior work.

Weaknesses: needs discussion/empirical evidence for universality to sub-Gaussian or structured designs (e.g., partial Fourier).

Weaknesses: clarify implications and alternatives when μ = 0 and relate to methods that recover direction (e.g., 1-bit CS).

Weaknesses: provide more guidance on verifying Assumption 1 for common f and x0 (including low-rank) and give explicit/numerical recipes for F(c1,c2,c3).

Weaknesses: include finite-sample experiments showing convergence to asymptotics and comparisons with constrained LASSO (Plan–Vershynin).

Weaknesses: harmonize notation/terminology, define all symbols on first use, and ensure consistent scaling/constants.

Weaknesses: for quantizer optimization, discuss global optimality, initialization, and provide empirical comparisons across initializations.

Minor: clean up typos, grammar, missing subscripts/superscripts, spacing, and punctuation throughout proofs.

Arguments for acceptance: strong, novel theoretical contribution that unifies and advances prior work and offers practical insights.

Arguments against acceptance: Gaussian-design and asymptotic restrictions plus abstract technical assumption (Assumption 1) warrant more scope/clarity.

Overall: high-quality, impactful paper that should be accepted after minor revisions for clarity and expanded discussion of scope and assumptions; reviewer confidence moderate–high.
