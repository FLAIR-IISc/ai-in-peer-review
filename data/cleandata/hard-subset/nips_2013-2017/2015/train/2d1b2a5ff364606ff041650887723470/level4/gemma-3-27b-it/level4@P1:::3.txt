This paper considers the Generalized Lasso with non-linear measurements and Gaussian design. A well-known heuristic is that non-linear observations can be treated as noisy linear observations. Building on the work of Plan and Vershynin [17], the authors extend the results of [17] and, by moving to an asymptotic regime, provide novel precise explicit expressions for the estimation error. The accuracy of their predictions is confirmed through several special cases (including the Lasso, group Lasso, and nuclear norm regularization). The application to the design of optimal quantizers in the context of q-Bit compressive sensing and its relation to the Lloyd-Max quantizer is particularly insightful.

**Quality:** This is a strong piece of work. The technical analysis appears sound, and the results are well-motivated and supported. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the relevant literature and contribute significantly to the field.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, though the treatment is somewhat dense, likely due to the complexity of the subject matter. While the authors provide a clear overview of their contributions, the lack of space occasionally prevents a fully detailed explanation. The notation is mostly consistent and accessible.

**Originality:** The paper builds upon the work of Plan and Vershynin [17] and expands upon it in several significant ways. For instance, the explicit expressions for the estimation error performance are novel and represent a valuable contribution. The connection to optimal quantizer design is also a noteworthy original aspect.

**Comments:**

1) Page 4, line 193: The regularized and penalized versions of the LASSO are equivalent with appropriate correspondence.
2) Caption of Figure 1:  ‘n=768’ should be ‘$n$=768’.
3) Page 5, line 247: There is a typo at “When…”.
4) Page 5, line 294: The function the authors call the “proximal function” is, in fact, well-known in convex analysis as the Moreau envelope with index τ. The notation used is somewhat awkward, as “prox” is generally used for the proximal point/operator, not the Moreau envelope. I suggest changing the terminology to align with standard usage.
5) Page 5, Assumption 1: Providing a more formal interpretation of this assumption using results from variational analysis could be instructive.
6) The authors should avoid referring to the Generalized Lasso as an “algorithm” throughout the text. It is more accurately described as a (convex) program or a decoder (a term widely used in compressive sensing).
7) The paper demonstrates that, asymptotically, the estimation performance of the Generalized Lasso with non-linear measurements is equivalent to that of a linear and noisy (Gaussian) measurement model. The authors provide novel, precise, explicit expressions for the estimation error and confirm their findings with numerous special cases, including the Lasso, group Lasso, and nuclear norm. The application to designing optimal quantizers in the context of q-Bit compressive sensing, and its relation to the Lloyd-Max quantizer, is particularly insightful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in signal processing and statistics – estimation with non-linear measurements.
*   The theoretical results are novel and provide valuable insights into the performance of the Generalized Lasso in this setting.
*   The precise error analysis is a significant contribution, offering a deeper understanding of the factors affecting estimation accuracy.
*   The connection to optimal quantizer design has practical implications for compressive sensing.
*   The authors demonstrate a strong technical expertise and a clear understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is mathematically dense, and some readers may find it challenging to follow all the details.
*   The asymptotic setting may limit the direct applicability of the results in some practical scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper. While the technical details are demanding, the contribution is significant and the results are well-supported. The paper addresses an important problem, provides novel theoretical insights, and has potential practical applications. The clarity is sufficient for an expert in the field, and the minor suggestions above could further improve the presentation.
