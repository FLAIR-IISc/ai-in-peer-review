

Summary: This paper extends the work of Plan and Vershynin [17] to the Generalized Lasso under non-linear measurements with Gaussian design. The authors leverage the heuristic that non-linear observations behave like noisy linear ones, and through an asymptotic analysis, derive precise closed-form expressions for estimation error. These predictions are validated across multiple special cases (Lasso, group Lasso, nuclear norm) and yield a particularly insightful application to optimal quantizer design in q-bit compressed sensing, with connections to the Lloyd-Max quantizer.  

Quality: This is a strong contribution to the field.  

Clarity: The paper is well-written but omits some technical details due to space constraints, resulting in an unbalanced treatment of certain aspects.  

Originality: The work builds on Plan and Vershynin [17] and provides novel closed-form expressions for estimation error under the considered framework, representing a meaningful extension.  

Comments:  
1. Page 4, line 193: The regularized and penalized formulations are equivalent under a suitable parameter correspondence; this should be explicitly stated.  
2. Figure 1 caption: Correct "n=768" to "$n$=768".  
3. Page 5, line 247: Fix the typo in "When ...".  
4. Page 5, line 294: The term "proximal function" is misleading; this is standardly termed the Moreau envelope of index $\tau$ in convex analysis. The notation conflicts with the common use of "prox" for the proximal operator. The authors should adopt the standard terminology.  
5. Page 5, Assumption 1: It would be instructive to provide a more formal interpretation of this assumption using tools from variational analysis.  
6. The Generalized Lasso is a convex optimization program (or decoder, as commonly used in compressed sensing), not an algorithm. The text should avoid calling it an "algorithm" to prevent confusion.  
7. The experimental section effectively demonstrates the Generalized Lasso applied to non-linear measurements, with theorems accurately predicting estimation error. The key insight—that asymptotic performance matches that of a linear-noisy model—should be highlighted more concisely to avoid repetition with the summary.
