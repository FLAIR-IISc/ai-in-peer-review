This paper provides a precise asymptotic characterization of the statistical performance of the l2-lasso (also known as the square-root lasso) when applied to single-index models. A key contribution is the extension of this type of analysis beyond standard linear models to a non-linear setting. Furthermore, the authors relax the common assumption of a Gaussian prior on the signal, broadening the applicability of their theoretical results.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. The extension of precise asymptotic analysis from linear models to the single-index model framework is a significant and valuable contribution to the literature on high-dimensional statistics and lasso-type estimators. The relaxation of the Gaussian prior assumption is also a noteworthy technical achievement that enhances the generalizability of the findings.

While the paper is strong, there are a couple of areas where its clarity and impact could be enhanced:

1.  **Connection to Linear Models in the Main Text:** The authors correctly establish the connection between the single-index model and an equivalent linear model with a scaled signal and non-standard noise, as originally explored by Brillinger (1982). While this relationship is rigorously detailed in Appendix A, its intuition is crucial for understanding the main results. I recommend that the authors briefly introduce this connection in the main body of the paper. Doing so would provide valuable context and a more direct heuristic justification for the key statement presented in the blockquote around line 140, making the central argument more self-contained and accessible to the reader.

2.  **Practical Implications for Model Choice (l2-lasso vs. l2^2-lasso):** The paper focuses exclusively on the l2-lasso. A primary motivation for preferring the l2-lasso over the more conventional l2^2-lasso (standard Lasso) is its theoretical advantage: the optimal regularization parameter is independent of the unknown noise variance, σ. This property is often cited as a significant practical benefit. The precise asymptotic framework developed in this paper is perfectly suited to investigate whether this theoretical justification holds in practice. For instance, how does the performance of the l2-lasso with its 'oracle' parameter compare to a cross-validated l2^2-lasso? A discussion on this point, even if brief, would substantially increase the paper's impact by connecting its deep theoretical results to a long-standing practical question in the field. It would be a valuable service to practitioners to understand if the theoretical elegance of the l2-lasso indeed translates to a robust performance advantage.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of high-dimensional estimators. The work is rigorous and the results are significant. My recommendations are primarily aimed at improving the paper's clarity and impact by better motivating the core arguments in the main text and by exploring the practical implications of the theoretical findings. Addressing these points would strengthen an already solid piece of work.
