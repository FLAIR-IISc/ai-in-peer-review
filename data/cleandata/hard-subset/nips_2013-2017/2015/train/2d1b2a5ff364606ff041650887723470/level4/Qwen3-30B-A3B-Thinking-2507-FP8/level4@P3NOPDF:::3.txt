

**Summary**  
This paper extends the work of Plan and Vershynin [17] to analyze the Generalized Lasso under non-linear measurements with Gaussian design. The authors establish that, asymptotically, the estimation error of the Generalized Lasso with non-linear measurements matches that of a linear noisy model—a key heuristic validated here with rigorous precision. They derive novel explicit expressions for the estimation error and confirm their predictions across special cases (Lasso, group Lasso, nuclear norm). The application to optimal quantizer design in q-bit compressed sensing, linking to the Lloyd-Max quantizer, is particularly insightful and well-motivated.  

**Strengths**  
The theoretical contribution is significant: moving beyond [17] to an asymptotic regime yields precise, closed-form error expressions, resolving a gap in the analysis of non-linear measurement models. The validation across multiple structured sparsity cases demonstrates robustness and broad applicability. The connection to quantizer design is a strong practical contribution, offering a principled approach to q-bit CS that bridges theory and implementation. The paper is well-organized, with clear exposition of the main results and their implications.  

**Weaknesses and Minor Issues**  
The paper’s clarity suffers from minor inconsistencies and terminology gaps that could confuse readers. Specifically:  
1. **Terminology**: The phrase "regularized and penalized versions" (p. 4, l. 193) is imprecise; these are equivalent under a parameter correspondence, but the claim needs explicit justification.  
2. **Notation**: The "proximal function" (p. 5, l. 294) is mislabeled. In convex analysis, this is the *Moreau envelope* of index $\tau$, while "prox" refers to the proximal operator. This inconsistency (e.g., using $\text{prox}_{\tau f}$ for the envelope) risks misinterpretation. Replace with standard terminology (e.g., "Moreau envelope $M_{\tau f}$").  
3. **Assumption 1 (p. 5)**: The assumption lacks formal grounding. Adding a brief interpretation via variational analysis (e.g., relating it to epi-convergence or subdifferential conditions) would strengthen the theoretical foundation.  
4. **Misnomer**: The Generalized Lasso is repeatedly referred to as an "algorithm" (e.g., p. 1, p. 4). It is a *convex program* or *decoder*; the term "algorithm" should be replaced to avoid confusion with computational methods (e.g., ADMM).  
5. **Typos/Formatting**:  
   - Caption of Fig. 1: "n=768" → "$n=768$" (p. 5, l. 247).  
   - Typo: "When ..." (p. 5, l. 247) requires correction.  
6. **Redundancy**: The final paragraph (p. 7) repeats the summary. Remove this to improve concision.  

**Recommendation**  
This is a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The core results are novel, well-validated, and extend [17] meaningfully. Addressing the minor issues above—particularly the terminology errors and Assumption 1 clarification—would substantially elevate the paper. The work is suitable for publication at NIPS after these revisions.  

**Detailed Comments**  
1. **Page 4, Line 193**: Clarify the equivalence between regularized and penalized formulations. State explicitly that $\lambda_{\text{reg}} = \tau \lambda_{\text{pen}}$ for some $\tau > 0$ suffices.  
2. **Page 5, Line 247**: Correct "When ..." typo.  
3. **Page 5, Line 294**: Replace "proximal function" with "Moreau envelope" and adjust notation to $M_{\tau f}(x) = \min_y \left\{ f(y) + \frac{1}{2\tau} \|x - y\|^2 \right\}$. Cite Rockafellar (1970) or Parikh & Boyd (2014) for standard usage.  
4. **Page 5, Assumption 1**: Add a sentence linking the assumption to known conditions in variational analysis (e.g., "Assumption 1 ensures $f$ is proper, lower semicontinuous, and has a unique minimizer, enabling the asymptotic analysis via the convex conjugate").  
5. **Terminology Consistency**: Everywhere, replace "Generalized Lasso algorithm" with "Generalized Lasso convex program" or "decoder."  
6. **Experiments**: The experimental validation (Fig. 1, Table 1) is sufficient but could briefly discuss computational cost (e.g., runtime vs. linear Lasso) to contextualize practicality.  

**Final Note**  
The paper’s core insight—that non-linear measurements behave like noisy linear ones asymptotically—is compelling and well-supported. The explicit error expressions and quantizer application are noteworthy contributions. With minor editorial and terminological refinements, this work will be a valuable addition to the literature. The authors should prioritize fixing the Moreau envelope misnaming and Assumption 1 interpretation, as these are critical for technical accuracy. The remaining points are easily addressed and do not detract from the paper’s merit.
