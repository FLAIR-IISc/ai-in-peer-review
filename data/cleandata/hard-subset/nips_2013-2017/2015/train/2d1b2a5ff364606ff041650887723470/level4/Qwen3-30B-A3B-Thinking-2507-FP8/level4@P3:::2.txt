

The paper investigates the estimation performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to non-linear measurements of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), extending previous work by Brillinger (1982) and Plan & Vershynin (2015). The authors demonstrate that the estimation error of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that with linear measurements of the form yi = μai^T x0 + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ) - μγ)²] for γ ~ N(0,1). This equivalence holds for a wide class of link functions g and regularizers f, and the authors provide precise asymptotic error expressions that capture the role of the structure of x0, the regularizer f, and the measurement parameters.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it generalizes previous results by providing precise error expressions rather than loose upper bounds, which is a major advance in the field. The authors derive these expressions using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, building on recent advances in precise analysis of the Generalized LASSO with linear measurements. Second, they apply these results to specific structured signal cases (sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery), demonstrating that their framework can handle a variety of common structured signal models. Third, they provide a novel application to q-bit compressive sensing, showing that the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error is the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from the general case to specific applications. The technical development is sound, and the results are thoroughly validated through experiments. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach, noting that the signal is assumed to have unit norm (a common simplification in high-dimensional analysis) and that the link function g must satisfy certain technical conditions.

The main strengths of the paper include its theoretical precision, broad applicability to different structured signal models, and practical significance for applications with non-linear measurements. The precise error characterization provides deeper insights than previous work, which only offered upper bounds. The connection between non-linear measurements and linear measurements with scaled signal and noise is particularly elegant and has important implications for practitioners.

A minor weakness is that the connection between non-linear and linear measurements, which is central to the paper's contribution, is not sufficiently emphasized in the main text. While the rigorous connection is provided in Appendix A, it would be helpful to include a more heuristic explanation in the main body (e.g., on line 140) to better justify the key insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements.

The paper makes an important contribution to the literature on structured signal recovery. The precise error analysis extends previous work in several directions: it generalizes to the regularized case (not just constrained or unregularized), provides exact asymptotic expressions rather than upper bounds, and handles a wide class of link functions. This work is clearly related to previous NIPS papers on the LASSO, such as those by Thrampoulidis et al. (2013, 2015) and Donoho et al. (2013), but provides significant new results that advance the state of the art.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a practically important problem: many measurement systems (e.g., 1-bit quantizers, optical sensors) produce non-linear measurements. The authors show how to analyze and optimize recovery algorithms for these systems using existing linear measurement theory, which has immediate applications in signal processing and machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides precise asymptotic error characterization for Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements
- Generalizes previous work by Plan & Vershynin to the regularized case
- Demonstrates the equivalence to scaled linear measurements, simplifying analysis
- Contains novel applications to q-bit compressive sensing with optimal quantizer derivation
- Well-written with thorough theoretical development and experimental validation

Arguments against acceptance:
- The connection between non-linear and linear measurements could be better explained in the main text
- The assumption of unit-norm signal is somewhat restrictive (though common in high-dimensional analysis)
- The technical conditions on the link function g could be more thoroughly discussed

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances our understanding of structured signal recovery with non-linear measurements. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant theoretical contribution with practical implications. I recommend acceptance.
