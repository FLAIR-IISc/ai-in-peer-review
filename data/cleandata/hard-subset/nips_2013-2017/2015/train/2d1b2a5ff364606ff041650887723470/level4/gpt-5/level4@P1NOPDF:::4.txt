This paper studies linear estimation with an unknown nonlinear link, inspired by recent work of Plan and Vershynin. It analyzes the asymptotic estimation error of the generalized Lasso for arbitrary link functions with μ ≠ 0, and shows—surprisingly—that the asymptotic error is exactly the same as in the linear case (Theorem 2.1), regardless of whether the link is linear or nonlinear.

Theorem 2.1 assumes that the measurement vectors are Gaussian. This assumption can be restrictive in practice. The authors should explain why Gaussianity is necessary for the conclusion (e.g., which parts of the proof use rotational invariance or exact Gaussian properties) and whether the result extends to more general designs (e.g., sub-Gaussian, isotropic, or heavy-tailed distributions), possibly with modified constants or additional conditions.

Questions and requests for clarification:
1) Choice of regularizer from a known set K containing x0: The paper assumes a convex regularizer but does not discuss how to construct or select an appropriate regularizer based on prior knowledge that x0 ∈ K. Guidance on practical choices (e.g., when K encodes sparsity, group structure, low rank, or other convex geometry) and on how sensitive the guarantees are to misspecification would be valuable.
2) Dependence on μ and σ: While the main message is that nonlinear measurements are effectively equivalent to linear ones, the bounds explicitly depend on μ and σ, which encode the difficulty of estimation under the nonlinear link g. Are these dependencies information-theoretically necessary, or are they artifacts of the analysis and/or the generalized Lasso? In particular, do the authors foresee procedures or conditions under which the dependence on μ and σ could be mitigated or removed? Any insight into tightness would help interpret the result.

Related work: 
- The paper does not discuss the extensive literature on single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction, which also studies linear vector/subspace estimation under unknown link functions. A brief comparison would help situate the contribution.
- The recent paper “Optimal linear estimation under unknown link function” by Yi et al. proposes algorithms for sparse recovery even when μ = 0 and establishes an equivalence between high-dimensional linear estimation with linear and nonlinear links under suitable conditions, with both rigorous proofs and empirical support. This seems closely related and should be cited and contrasted with the present results.

Overall, this is a well-written paper with interesting results. Clarifying the necessity of the Gaussian design, providing guidance on regularizer selection from a known set K, and discussing the necessity of the μ and σ dependencies—together with a more complete positioning relative to single-index/sufficient-dimension-reduction work and the results of Yi et al.—would substantially strengthen the paper.
