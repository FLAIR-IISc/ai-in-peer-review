This paper analyzes the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimating a structured signal from non-linear measurements with a Gaussian design matrix. The work builds upon the foundational results of Brillinger and more recent work by Plan and Vershynin, which established that non-linear observations can be treated as noisy linear ones, allowing for signal recovery up to a scaling constant. The main contribution of this paper is to move beyond existing non-asymptotic bounds into an asymptotic regime where the signal dimension and number of measurements grow large. In this setting, the authors derive novel, precise, and explicit expressions for the estimation error.

The central finding is a rigorous proof that the estimation performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically identical to that of a LASSO problem with corresponding linear measurements corrupted by additive Gaussian noise. The paper explicitly characterizes the scaling factor and noise variance of this equivalent linear model. The accuracy of these theoretical predictions is empirically validated across several important special cases, including standard LASSO for sparse recovery, Group LASSO for group-sparse signals, and nuclear-norm regularization for low-rank matrix recovery. As a compelling application, the authors show how their framework can be used to design optimal quantizers for q-bit compressive sensing, drawing an insightful connection to the classic Lloyd-Max quantizer.

### Quality and Originality

This is a strong, technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution. While it builds on existing work, it pushes the analysis to a new level of precision. The key strengths are:

*   **Novelty of Results:** The paper provides the first asymptotically exact characterizations of the estimation error for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. Deriving explicit, closed-form expressions for the error is a major advance over the non-asymptotic bounds in prior work.
*   **Rigorous Equivalence:** The formal establishment of the asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear problem and a specific noisy linear problem is an elegant and powerful result. It elevates a common heuristic into a precise, quantitative tool.
*   **Generality:** The framework is quite general, applicable to a wide class of convex regularizers (l1, group l1, nuclear norm, etc.) and various signal structures.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and organized. The main ideas are communicated effectively, and the problem setup and contributions are clearly stated. However, the presentation is somewhat dense, likely due to page limitations, which may make some sections challenging for non-experts. A few minor suggestions could further improve clarity:

*   The treatment feels slightly unbalanced, with some parts being more detailed than others. Expanding on the intuition behind some of the key theoretical steps, perhaps in the appendix, could enhance readability.
*   Some minor adjustments to terminology would align the paper more closely with standard conventions in convex analysis (see detailed comments below).

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are important and likely to be influential. By providing exact performance predictions, the work offers a deeper understanding of how non-linearities in the measurement process affect regularized recovery methods. This has both theoretical and practical implications. The application to optimal quantizer design is particularly insightful and demonstrates the practical utility of the theoretical findings. Researchers and practitioners working on signal recovery from quantized or otherwise non-linear data are likely to build upon these ideas.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

1.  **Terminology for Moreau Envelope:** On page 5, the function derived from the Fenchel conjugate is referred to using `prox` notation. In convex analysis, this function is standardly known as the Moreau envelope. The notation `prox` is almost universally reserved for the proximal operator. Using non-standard terminology could be confusing. I would strongly recommend adopting the standard term "Moreau envelope" and its associated notation.

2.  **Interpretation of Assumption 1:** On page 5, it might be instructive to provide a more formal interpretation of Assumption 1 from the perspective of variational analysis. This could help readers better understand its implications and the conditions under which it holds.

3.  **LASSO as a Program vs. Algorithm:** Throughout the text, the Generalized LASSO is occasionally referred to as an "algorithm." It would be more precise to call it a "convex program," an "estimator," or a "decoder," as it defines a solution rather than a specific computational procedure for finding it.

4.  **Regularized vs. Penalized Forms:** On page 4, line 193, where the constrained LASSO is introduced, it might be worth briefly noting that the regularized (penalized) and constrained versions of the LASSO are equivalent under an appropriate correspondence of their parameters.

5.  **Minor Typo:** In the caption of Figure 1, for mathematical consistency, 'n=768' should be formatted as '$n$=768'.
