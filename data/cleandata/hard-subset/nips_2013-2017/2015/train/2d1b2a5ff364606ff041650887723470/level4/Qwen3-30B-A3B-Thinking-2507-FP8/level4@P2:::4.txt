

This paper investigates the asymptotic behavior of the Generalized LASSO when applied to non-linear measurement models of the form $y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)$, where $g_i$ are independent copies of a potentially unknown link function $g$. The authors demonstrate that, under appropriate conditions, the estimation error of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of a linear measurement model with appropriately scaled and noisy measurements. Specifically, Theorem 2.1 establishes that $\| \hat{x} - \mu x_0 \|_2^2 = \| \hat{x}^{lin} - \mu x_0 \|_2^2$ in the limit as $n \to \infty$, where $\mu = \mathbb{E}[\gamma g(\gamma)]$ and $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[(g(\gamma) - \mu \gamma)^2]$ with $\gamma \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. This result extends the classical work of Brillinger (1981) and the recent contributions of Plan and Vershynin (2013), providing precise asymptotic error predictions rather than the loose upper bounds previously established.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper provides technically sound results with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish precise asymptotic error expressions (Theorems 2.1-2.2) that generalize previous work on the LASSO with linear measurements. The proofs are well-structured and build upon recent advances in the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework. The paper includes multiple concrete applications (sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery) that demonstrate the broad applicability of the results.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-written with clear exposition of the main contributions. The technical framework is carefully developed, and the main results are presented in a logically organized manner. The figures effectively illustrate the accuracy of the theoretical predictions. The paper would benefit from slightly more detailed explanations of the assumptions underlying Theorem 2.1, particularly regarding the role of the link function.

*Originality:* This work provides the first asymptotically precise analysis of the Generalized LASSO for non-linear measurements. While Plan and Vershynin (2013) established that the LASSO can handle non-linear measurements with appropriate scaling, this paper provides precise error predictions that were previously unknown. The connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer for optimal quantization (Section 2.3) is particularly novel.

*Significance:* The results have important implications for practical applications involving non-linear measurements, such as 1-bit quantization, censored observations, and other measurement systems with nonlinearities. The precise error characterization enables better design of measurement systems and regularization parameters. The theoretical framework provides a unified understanding of how non-linearities affect estimation performance.

**Weaknesses:**

*The assumption of Gaussian measurement matrices, while standard in high-dimensional statistics, should be more thoroughly justified. The authors should explain why this assumption is necessary for their precise error characterization, particularly given that many practical applications might use non-Gaussian measurement matrices.

*The paper does not adequately address the relationship to the single-index model literature. The authors should discuss how their work relates to classical results in this area (e.g., [4,5,14,15]) and clarify how their results differ from previous approaches to single-index models.

*The connection to the recent paper by Yi et al. (2015) "Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform" is insufficiently addressed. This work also establishes equivalence between linear and nonlinear measurement models under certain conditions, and the authors should discuss how their results relate to and differ from Yi et al.'s approach, particularly regarding the case when $\mu = 0$.

*The paper does not sufficiently address how to choose the regularizer based on the structure of $x_0$. While the paper assumes convex regularizers, it would be valuable to discuss practical considerations for selecting the appropriate regularizer for specific structured signal classes.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first precise asymptotic error analysis for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements.
*The results are technically sound and rigorously proven, extending previous work with precise error characterizations.
*The theoretical results have clear practical implications for measurement system design (e.g., optimal quantization via the Lloyd-Max quantizer).
*The paper is well-written and clearly presents a novel contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*The assumption of Gaussian measurement matrices, while common, is not adequately justified in the context of the paper's contribution.
*The paper fails to properly contextualize its work within the single-index model literature and the recent Yi et al. (2015) paper.
*The paper does not sufficiently address how to select the regularizer based on the structure of $x_0$, which is a critical practical consideration.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that provides precise asymptotic error characterizations for the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements. The results represent a meaningful extension of previous work by Plan and Vershynin, and the theoretical framework is technically sound. While the paper has some shortcomings in contextualizing its work within the broader literature and justifying certain assumptions, these can be addressed through minor revisions. The paper makes an important contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistical estimation and should be accepted with minor revisions.
