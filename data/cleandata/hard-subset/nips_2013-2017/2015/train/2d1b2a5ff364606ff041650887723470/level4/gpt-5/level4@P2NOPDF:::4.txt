This paper studies linear parameter estimation with an unknown nonlinear link, building on recent work by Plan and Vershynin. It analyzes the asymptotic estimation error of the generalized Lasso for arbitrary link functions with μ ≠ 0 and shows—perhaps surprisingly—that the asymptotic error matches that of the linear model, as stated in Theorem 2.1. In other words, whether the link is linear or nonlinear, the asymptotic error is identical under the stated conditions.

Assumptions and scope. Theorem 2.1 requires Gaussian design for the measurement vectors. This may be restrictive in practice. The paper would benefit from a clear justification of why Gaussianity is essential for the proof or conclusion, and a discussion of whether the result extends (or fails to extend) to sub-Gaussian or more general designs.

Questions and clarifications:
1) Choice of regularizer. The paper assumes a convex regularizer but does not explain how to choose it given prior knowledge that x0 lies in a set K (e.g., a structured or low-complexity set). Practical or theoretical guidance—such as connections to atomic norms, descent cones, or compatibility with K—would improve applicability.
2) Dependence on μ and σ. While the result equates the nonlinear and linear settings, the final error expressions depend on μ and σ, which quantify the difficulty induced by the link g. Are these dependences information-theoretically necessary? Can the authors comment on lower bounds or show that these parameters are unavoidable, or alternatively, identify regimes where their influence vanishes?

Related work. Some relevant literature appears to be missing:
- Single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction study estimation of a direction or subspace under an unknown link. A brief review and positioning relative to this body of work would strengthen the paper.
- A recent paper by Yi et al., “Optimal linear estimation under unknown linear estimation,” proposes new algorithms for sparse recovery that remain effective even when μ = 0. It establishes an equivalence between linear and nonlinear high-dimensional estimation under appropriate conditions, supported by both theory and experiments. A comparison and citation would provide useful context and highlight the novelty here.

Overall assessment. This is a clearly written paper with interesting and nontrivial results. Addressing the above points—especially the necessity of the Gaussian design, guidance on regularizer selection tied to K, and the role of μ and σ—would enhance both the theoretical clarity and practical relevance. Including the missing references would also improve the positioning of the work within the existing literature.
