- Studies recovery from nonlinear observations yi = g(ai^T x0) with Gaussian ai and shows generalized LASSO asymptotic error equals that of a linear model with effective μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ)−μγ)^2].  
- Main theorem yields an exact scalar convex program whose optimizer gives the asymptotic MSE, with specializations to ℓ1, ℓ1,2, and nuclear-norm regularizers.  
- Application to quantized sensing shows optimizing LASSO error leads to the Lloyd–Max quantizer.  
- Strengthens Brillinger’s result and extends Plan & Vershynin by providing precise asymptotic error predictions for penalized LASSO under general convex regularizers.  
- Technical soundness: analysis appears solid and consistent with CGMT/AMP literature; assumptions are explicit though a proof sketch would help readers.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well written and organized, but some notation and Assumption 1 need clearer explanation; many details are deferred to the appendix.  
- Originality: high — first asymptotically exact characterization of LASSO error under general single-index models and convex regularizers.  
- Significance: high — unifies the heuristic of treating nonlinear as linear with precise theory and offers practical guidance for regularization and quantizer design.  
- Strength: clear conceptual contribution establishing equivalence between nonlinear and effective linear models for LASSO.  
- Strength: general framework covering multiple structured models and regularizers.  
- Strength: provides an interpretable, computable scalar convex program for asymptotic MSE.  
- Strength: empirical validation for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank special cases.  
- Strength: practical insight recovering Lloyd–Max quantizer as optimal under the proposed criterion.  
- Weakness: restrictive Gaussian design assumption; robustness to sub-Gaussian or structured designs is not explored.  
- Weakness: assumption μ ≠ 0 excludes important cases (e.g., even link functions); alternatives for μ = 0 are not discussed.  
- Weakness: results are asymptotic; finite-sample accuracy and probabilistic deviations are not theoretically addressed.  
- Weakness: Assumption 1 is technical and needs more intuition, examples, and connections to variational analysis.  
- Weakness: approach presumes knowledge or estimability of μ and σ; guidance on practical estimation is lacking.  
- Suggestion: clarify equivalence between constrained and penalized LASSO and give mapping between constraint radius and λ.  
- Suggestion: fix minor typos/formatting, rename “proximal function” to Moreau envelope, and avoid calling LASSO an “algorithm.”  
- Suggestion: provide formal interpretation and more examples for Assumption 1 using Rockafellar–Wets and variational-analysis tools.  
- Experimental suggestions: add sub-Gaussian design experiments, study finite-sample deviations and λ tuning, test sensitivity to μ/σ mis-specification, include constrained LASSO comparisons, and provide code/pseudocode.  
- Questions: can equivalence extend to sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant designs, how to estimate μ and σ from data, is there a route to finite-sample high-probability bounds, and what to do when μ = 0?  
- Pros for acceptance: strong, novel theory with broad applicability, convincing empirical validation, and insightful quantization connection.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited scope due to Gaussian design and μ ≠ 0, heavy reliance on asymptotics, and some clarity/assumption issues.  
- Overall evaluation: high-quality, original, and significant work recommended for acceptance after minor revisions addressing clarity, terminology, and robustness.
