This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimating signals from measurements corrupted by nonlinear link functions. The authors demonstrate that, asymptotically, the estimation performance with these nonlinear measurements is equivalent to that of linear measurements with scaled and noisy observations, providing explicit expressions for the estimation error. This is a significant extension of previous work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin. The results are technically sound, well-motivated, and offer a valuable contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing.

**Summary of Contributions:** The paper builds upon recent advances in the precise analysis of the Generalized LASSO and extends them to the non-linear measurement setting. The key contribution is a precise characterization of the estimation error for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, establishing an equivalence to a linear measurement model with appropriate scaling and noise.  This enables the application of existing results from linear estimation theory to the non-linear case. The authors also demonstrate a surprising connection to optimal quantization, identifying the Lloyd-Max quantizer as optimal for minimizing estimation error.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Originality:** The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem – estimation with non-linear measurements – and provides novel and insightful results.  Establishing the asymptotic equivalence to a linear model is a significant theoretical contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical analysis appears rigorous and well-justified. The derivation of explicit error expressions is impressive.  The authors carefully consider asymptotic regimes and provide clear modeling assumptions.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the connections to prior work are well-explained.  The notation is consistent, and the key ideas are presented in a logical manner.
* **Significance:** The results have practical implications for applications where measurements are subject to non-linear distortions, such as compressive sensing with low-precision ADCs.  The identification of the optimal quantizer is particularly valuable.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper explores several special cases (sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, low-rank matrix recovery) and provides concrete examples and illustrations of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope (Potential):** While the authors acknowledge the connection to the single-index model and sufficient dimension reduction literature, a more extensive discussion of existing work in those fields might be beneficial. As the reviewer notes, there’s a possibility of unawareness regarding potentially relevant prior work.  Specifically, a more detailed comparison with techniques developed in econometrics for handling non-linear single-index models would strengthen the paper.
* **Typographical Errors:**  The reviewer correctly points out the presence of typos, particularly in the proof sections. These should be carefully addressed before publication. While not a major issue, they detract from the overall polish of the manuscript.
* **Assumption 1:** The assumption regarding the convergence of a specific function related to the regularizer (Assumption 1) feels somewhat restrictive and lacks intuitive justification.  A clearer explanation of why this assumption is necessary and under what conditions it holds would be helpful.
* **Computational Complexity of Optimal Quantizer:** While identifying the Lloyd-Max quantizer as optimal is theoretically interesting, the computational cost of implementing it in practice might be significant. A discussion of the practical limitations of this approach would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics.
* The results are rigorously derived and have significant theoretical and practical implications.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.
* The analysis provides a precise understanding of the performance of the Generalized LASSO in the presence of non-linear measurements.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The potential lack of comprehensive coverage of related work in the single-index model literature.
* The presence of typos detracts from the overall quality of the manuscript.
* The restrictive nature of Assumption 1 and the lack of intuitive justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.  The strengths of the work significantly outweigh the weaknesses.  Addressing the typographical errors and providing a more detailed justification for Assumption 1 would further strengthen the manuscript. The paper represents a significant advancement in our understanding of estimation with non-linear measurements and is a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.  The clarity of writing and the thoroughness of the analysis make it a particularly commendable submission.
