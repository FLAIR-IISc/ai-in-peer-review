

This paper extends the precise asymptotic analysis of the Generalized LASSO to the setting of non-linear measurements of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), where gi are independent copies of a link function g. The authors demonstrate that the estimation performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that with linear measurements of the form yi = μai^T x0 + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ)-μγ)²] for γ ~ N(0,1). This extends earlier work by Brillinger (1982) and Plan and Vershynin (2013), who established similar results but only for the unconstrained least squares estimator and with upper bounds rather than precise error characterizations. The authors provide explicit expressions for the asymptotic squared error that hold for all values of the regularization parameter λ and for a wide class of convex regularizers f(x) corresponding to structured signals (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank).

Quality: The paper presents technically sound results with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors build upon recent advances in the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework to derive precise error characterizations rather than just upper bounds. The proofs are thorough, and the authors carefully address the strengths and limitations of their approach. The key assumption of i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices is justified in the context of high-dimensional statistics, and the authors provide clear conditions under which their results hold. The paper also provides the first-known precise results for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate technical depth for the NIPS audience. The main results are presented in a logical sequence, and the authors provide helpful illustrations of their error predictions in Figures 1-2. However, the connection between non-linear and linear measurement models (a key insight of the paper) is only rigorously established in Appendix A, while the main text mentions it only in a blockquote without sufficient heuristic justification. This connection should be explicitly discussed in the main text to better guide readers through the paper's central insight.

Originality: This work makes a substantial contribution by extending precise asymptotic analysis to the non-linear measurement setting. While Plan and Vershynin provided upper bounds for the constrained Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, this paper establishes precise error formulas that hold for the regularized Generalized LASSO (the more commonly used version in practice). The results are novel for the specific problem setting and provide new theoretical insights for structured signal recovery with non-linear measurements. The application to q-bit compressive sensing, demonstrating that the Lloyd-Max quantizer is optimal, is particularly noteworthy.

Significance: The results have important implications for practical applications involving non-linear measurements, such as 1-bit quantization in sensor networks and compressive sensing. The precise error characterizations provide theoretical guidance for designing measurement systems and selecting regularization parameters. The demonstration that the optimal quantizer is the Lloyd-Max quantizer (a well-known result in signal processing) bridges theoretical and practical knowledge in this domain.

Strengths: The paper provides the first precise asymptotic characterizations of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, which significantly advances the theoretical understanding of this problem. The results are general enough to apply to various structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) with novel contributions in each case. The connection between non-linear and linear measurement models is elegant and has practical implications for measurement system design.

Weaknesses: The paper focuses exclusively on the l2-lasso (square root LASSO) but does not thoroughly address how the results compare to the more commonly used l2^2-lasso. The key theoretical advantage of the l2-lasso (the oracle property where the regularization parameter λ doesn't depend on the unknown noise variance) is not examined in the context of non-linear measurements. This is a significant omission given the practical importance of this property. Additionally, the connection between non-linear and linear measurement models is only fully explained in the appendix rather than in the main text.

Arguments for acceptance: This is a theoretically significant contribution that extends precise asymptotic analysis to a challenging non-linear setting. The results are novel, rigorous, and have clear practical implications for signal processing applications. The paper is well-written and makes a substantial contribution to the literature on LASSO-type estimators.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper would be stronger with a more thorough comparison to the l2^2-lasso and a more explicit discussion of the non-linear/linear measurement connection in the main text. The focus on the l2-lasso without addressing its relationship to other LASSO variants limits the paper's practical relevance.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of structured signal recovery with non-linear measurements. With minor revisions to address the connection to linear measurements in the main text and a more thorough discussion of the l2-lasso vs. l2^2-lasso comparison, this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS. The work represents a significant step forward in the precise analysis of LASSO-type estimators under non-linear measurement models.
