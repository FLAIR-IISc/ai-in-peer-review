

This paper investigates the estimation of structured signals from nonlinear measurements using the Generalized LASSO framework. Building upon the classical work of Brillinger and more recent contributions by Plan and Vershynin, the authors establish that the asymptotic estimation error of the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements is equivalent to that with appropriately scaled and noisy linear measurements. The key result (Theorem 2.1) demonstrates that for Gaussian measurement matrices, the estimation error under nonlinear measurements matches exactly with that under linear measurements of the form yi = μa_i^T x0 + σz_i, where μ and σ are determined by the link function g.

The paper makes a significant contribution by providing precise asymptotic characterizations of the estimation error for a wide class of link functions and regularizers, extending previous non-asymptotic bounds. The authors derive explicit expressions for the error that capture the roles of the link function, signal structure, regularizer, and regularization parameter. The results are supported by rigorous theoretical analysis and empirical validation through various examples.

A notable strength is the comprehensive treatment of different structured signal cases (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) with precise error predictions. The application to q-bit compressive sensing, where the authors identify the optimal quantization thresholds using the Lloyd-Max algorithm, is particularly compelling and demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.

The main limitation is the assumption of Gaussian measurement matrices, which might restrict applicability in some practical scenarios. While the authors acknowledge this as a standard assumption in high-dimensional statistics, it would strengthen the paper to discuss why this assumption is necessary for their precise characterization and whether the results could be extended to other measurement ensembles.

Two important questions arise from the paper:
1. The paper assumes convex regularizers but does not address how to select the appropriate regularizer based on the structure of x0 (i.e., the set κ in which x0 lies). This is a crucial consideration for practical applications.
2. The characterization of estimation performance depends on μ and σ, which are determined by the link function g. It would be valuable for the authors to elaborate on why these dependencies are necessary and whether they can be interpreted as measures of the "hardness" of the nonlinear measurement process.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough review of the single-index model literature, particularly the extensive work on sufficient dimension reduction that studies vector/subspace estimation under unknown link functions. Additionally, the recent work by Yi et al. ("Optimal linear estimation under unknown linear estimation") should be cited, as it addresses sparse recovery with link functions having μ = 0 and establishes a similar equivalence between linear and nonlinear estimation under proper conditions. This work provides complementary insights and would strengthen the paper's position within the literature.

Overall, this is a well-written paper with significant theoretical contributions that advance our understanding of how structured signal estimation behaves under nonlinear measurements. The results are clearly presented, the analysis is rigorous, and the practical implications are well articulated.
