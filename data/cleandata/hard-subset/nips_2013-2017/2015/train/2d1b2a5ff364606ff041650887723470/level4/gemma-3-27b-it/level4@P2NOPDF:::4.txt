This paper investigates linear estimation problems in the presence of a nonlinear link function. Building on the work of Plan and Vershynin, the authors analyze the asymptotic estimation error achieved using a generalized lasso regularizer for arbitrary link functions with non-zero mean, µ ≠ 0. A key finding is that the asymptotic error is identical for both linear and nonlinear link functions, as formalized in Theorem 2.1.

The authors’ reliance on a Gaussian assumption for the linear measurement vector in Theorem 2.1 warrants further discussion. While this assumption is standard, its necessity for the stated conclusion is not explicitly justified. Elucidating *why* this specific distributional assumption is crucial would strengthen the theoretical foundation of the result.

I have several questions regarding the practical application and theoretical implications of this work. First, the paper discusses the choice of regularizer under the assumption of convexity, but does not offer guidance on how to select an appropriate regularizer based on the properties of the underlying parameter space, specifically the set 𝜅 containing the true parameter vector x₀. Addressing this practical consideration would enhance the paper’s utility. Second, while the results demonstrate equivalence in asymptotic error between linear and nonlinear measurements, the resulting error expression still depends on µ and σ, parameters that characterize the difficulty of estimation associated with the nonlinear link function *g*. It would be valuable to understand the authors’ perspective on whether this dependence is inherent and necessary, or if it arises as a consequence of the analysis.

Finally, the paper would benefit from acknowledging related work. Specifically, the substantial literature on single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction – which addresses linear vector/subspace estimation under unknown link functions – is not discussed. Furthermore, the recent paper by Yi et al. (“Optimal linear estimation under unknown linear estimation”) presents a novel algorithm for sparse recovery, even when the link function’s mean (µ) is zero. This work also establishes an equivalence between high-dimensional linear estimation with linear and nonlinear link functions under certain conditions, supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. Incorporating these references would provide a more complete context for the contributions of this paper. 

Overall, this is a well-written paper presenting interesting and potentially significant results. The equivalence demonstrated between linear and nonlinear estimation errors under the given conditions is a noteworthy contribution to the field. Addressing the points raised above regarding justification of assumptions, practical considerations for regularizer selection, interpretation of the µ and σ dependence, and acknowledgment of related work would further strengthen the manuscript.
