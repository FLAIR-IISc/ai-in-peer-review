This paper provides a precise asymptotic characterization of the performance of the Generalized LASSO (also known as the square-root LASSO or `l2`-LASSO) for estimating a structured signal from non-linear measurements. The measurement model, `y_i = g(a_i^T x_0)`, is known as the single-index model. The authors' main contribution is to demonstrate that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime, the estimation error of the LASSO in this non-linear setting is equivalent to the error of the same estimator in a corresponding linear model with additive Gaussian noise, `y_i = μa_i^T x_0 + σz_i`. The scaling factor `μ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the non-linear link function `g`.

This work builds upon and significantly strengthens two lines of research. First, it generalizes the classical work of Brillinger, which established a similar equivalence for unregularized least squares in a fixed-dimension (`n`) asymptotic setting. Second, it refines the recent high-dimensional analysis of Plan and Vershynin, which provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO variant, by delivering asymptotically *exact* error characterizations for the more common regularized LASSO. The theoretical results are specialized for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems and are convincingly validated by numerical simulations.

### Strengths

**Significance and Originality:** The paper's central result—that the LASSO with non-linear measurements behaves identically to one with specific linear measurements—is both elegant and powerful. It provides a clear and precise theoretical foundation for applying a linear model estimator to a fundamentally non-linear problem. Moving from the upper bounds of prior work to exact asymptotic formulae represents a substantial advance in the theoretical understanding of these estimators. The application to optimal quantizer design is a compelling demonstration of the practical utility of the theory.

**Quality:** The paper appears to be technically sound. The claims are well-supported by a rigorous theoretical framework (Theorem 2.2), which provides a general error formula based on a scalar optimization problem. This general result is then successfully applied to derive more explicit characterizations for specific, widely-used regularization schemes like the `l1`-norm and group `l1,2`-norm. The extension to signals `x_0` with non-Gaussian priors is also a valuable generalization. The numerical experiments align perfectly with the theoretical predictions, lending strong empirical support to the analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and structured. The introduction effectively situates the work within the existing literature and clearly articulates its contributions. The problem setup and main results are stated precisely.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

The paper is strong, and the following points are intended as constructive suggestions for further improvement.

1.  **Intuition for the Main Result:** The core equivalence between the non-linear and linear models is rigorously derived. However, the intuition behind this equivalence could be highlighted more prominently in the main text. While Brillinger's work is cited, a brief, heuristic argument in the introduction could help the reader appreciate why such a result is expected. For example, explaining that for i.i.d. Gaussian measurement vectors `a_i`, the projection `a_i^T x_0` is a Gaussian scalar, which allows the effect of the non-linear function `g` to be summarized by its first two moments with respect to a Gaussian measure (leading to `μ` and `σ`), would provide valuable context before the formal statements.

2.  **Implications for `l2`-LASSO vs. `l2^2`-LASSO:** The authors focus on the `l2`-LASSO (square-root LASSO). A key motivation for this estimator over the more traditional `l2^2`-LASSO is that the optimal regularization parameter `λ` is independent of the noise variance. Your results establish an effective noise variance `σ^2` that depends on the (possibly unknown) link function `g`. This creates an interesting parallel: just as the `l2`-LASSO is robust to unknown noise variance in linear models, is it also robust to the unknown non-linearity `g` in this setting? A discussion on this point would significantly strengthen the paper's practical implications. Does your analysis suggest that the `l2`-LASSO is particularly well-suited for single-index models for this reason?

3.  **Details on Low-Rank Recovery:** Section 2.2.4 on low-rank matrix recovery is slightly less developed than the sections on sparse and group-sparse recovery. The section concludes by stating that details are left for future work. It would be beneficial to either briefly elaborate on the conditions required for the convergence of the singular value distribution or to clarify the scope of the claim being made in the current draft.

### Summary

Overall, this is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a significant and elegant contribution to the high-dimensional analysis of LASSO-type estimators. It provides precise, actionable insights into the behavior of a standard algorithm in a non-standard setting. The weaknesses are minor and relate to opportunities for improving clarity and expanding the discussion of the results' implications. I recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first asymptotically precise error characterization for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements.
*   The main equivalence result (non-linear = linear) is insightful, powerful, and simplifies analysis.
*   The framework is general, covering a wide range of signal structures, regularizers, and non-linearities.
*   The theory is rigorously supported by analysis and validated by convincing numerical experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The discussion could be improved by providing more intuition and exploring the practical implications of the results more fully (e.g., `l2` vs. `l2^2`-LASSO choice).
