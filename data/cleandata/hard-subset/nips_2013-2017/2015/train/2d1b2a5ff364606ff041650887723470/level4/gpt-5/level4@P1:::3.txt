Summary:
This paper studies the Generalized LASSO for recovering a structured signal x0 from non-linear measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian design. Building on Brillinger’s classical results for least squares and the more recent work of Plan and Vershynin on constrained generalized LASSO, the authors move to a high-dimensional asymptotic regime and derive precise, explicit expressions for the estimation error. A central contribution is an exact equivalence: asymptotically, the performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements matches that with appropriately scaled noisy linear measurements yi = μ a_i^T x0 + σ z_i, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). They further provide a scalar convex program that yields the asymptotic MSE for a wide class of convex regularizers f and link functions g and for all λ > 0. The theory is specialized to sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) settings, and is validated by simulations. An application to q-bit compressive sensing shows that optimizing the quantizer to minimize the LASSO estimation error recovers the classical Lloyd–Max quantizer.

Quality:
This is a solid piece of work. The analysis is technically sound, the claims are supported by clear asymptotic results, and the empirical results align well with the theory across multiple structural priors and link functions. Some details are necessarily omitted due to space, but the main arguments are convincing.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, though the presentation is somewhat unbalanced in places. Space limitations appear to have forced the omission of some technical details and formal interpretations that would aid readability.

Originality:
While the work builds on Plan and Vershynin [17], the contributions are substantial and novel. In particular, the asymptotically exact equivalence to linear measurements with explicit μ and σ, and the precise error characterizations for a broad class of convex regularizers, are new.

Significance:
The results provide the first asymptotically precise performance characterizations for LASSO-type estimators with non-linear measurements under Gaussian design. The insights into how μ and σ alone capture the impact of non-linearities are likely to inform both theory and practice (e.g., tuning and quantizer design). The application to optimal q-bit quantization is especially insightful.

Pros:
- Clear, precise asymptotic characterization of the error via a low-dimensional convex program.
- Broad applicability to multiple regularizers (ℓ1, ℓ1,2, nuclear norm) and non-linear link functions.
- Strong empirical validation.
- Useful application to quantizer design, recovering Lloyd–Max as optimal for this criterion.

Cons:
- Presentation is somewhat unbalanced; some definitions and assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1) would benefit from a more formal treatment.
- Minor terminology and notation issues (e.g., “algorithm” vs “program,” Moreau envelope notation).
- A few typographical and formatting issues.

Detailed comments:
1) Page 4, line 193: The penalized and constrained formulations are equivalent under appropriate parameter correspondence. Please clarify the mapping and cite standard results.

2) Caption of Figure 1: “n=768” should be typeset as “n = 768” (e.g., use $n$ if in LaTeX).

3) Page 5, line 247: There is a typo starting at “When ...”. Please correct.

4) Page 5, line 294: What is called the “proximal function” is commonly known in convex analysis as the Moreau envelope (with index τ). The current notation is awkward, as prox typically denotes the proximal operator, not the envelope. I suggest renaming and using standard notation for the Moreau envelope.

5) Assumption 1: It would be instructive to give a more formal interpretation (and sufficient conditions) using results from variational analysis (e.g., epi-convergence, properties of Fenchel conjugates and Moreau envelopes). This would help readers verify the assumption for new regularizers.

6) Throughout: Please avoid calling the generalized LASSO an “algorithm.” It is a convex program (or “decoder” in the CS literature), while specific solvers (e.g., proximal algorithms) are algorithms.

7) Experiments: The empirical section is strong. You show that applying the generalized LASSO to non-linear measurements yields estimation errors that match the theorems across several structures (LASSO, group LASSO, nuclear norm). The application to q-bit compressive sensing, and the conclusion that the Lloyd–Max quantizer minimizes the resulting estimation error via the σ2/μ2 criterion, is particularly insightful.

Overall recommendation:
This paper offers precise and broadly applicable insights into generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements, with strong theoretical and empirical support and a useful practical application. I recommend acceptance after addressing the minor clarity, terminology, and notation issues noted above.
