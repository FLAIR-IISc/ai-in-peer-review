This paper investigates linear estimation in the presence of nonlinear link functions, building upon recent work by Plan and Vershynin. The authors study the asymptotic estimation error achievable using the generalized LASSO for arbitrary link functions with µ ≠ 0, demonstrating that the asymptotic error is equivalent whether the underlying measurements are linear or nonlinear, as shown in Theorem 2.1. The paper provides precise expressions for this error, capturing the roles of the link function, signal structure, regularizer, and regularization parameter. 

The assumption of i.i.d. Gaussian entries for the measurement matrix A is a potentially limiting factor. While justified in many scenarios, a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to deviations from this assumption, or potential extensions to other matrix distributions, would strengthen the work.

Several questions remain. First, while the paper discusses the role of convex regularizers, it doesn't fully address how to *choose* the appropriate regularizer based on the underlying structure of x0. Further guidance on this selection process would be valuable. Second, while the result demonstrates equivalence between nonlinear and linear measurements, the conclusion relies on parameters µ and σ, which characterize the difficulty of estimation posed by the nonlinear function g. Exploring the implications of this dependence – specifically, whether the performance gains from using knowledge of g are limited – would be insightful.

The related work section could benefit from expanding its coverage. The paper overlooks relevant literature in single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction, which extensively study linear vector/subspace estimation under unknown link functions. Additionally, the recent work by Yi et al. ("Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform," 2015) introduces an algorithm for sparse recovery even when the link function has µ = 0 and establishes a similar equivalence between linear and nonlinear estimation under different conditions. This paper provides a complementary perspective and should be discussed. 

Despite these points, this is a well-written paper presenting fairly interesting and technically sound results. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements, yielding precise and novel error bounds.
*   The result establishing equivalence between linear and nonlinear measurements is significant and offers a useful simplification for analysis.
*   The derivation of the error expressions captures the critical factors influencing estimation performance.
*   The connection to optimal quantization (discussed in Section 2.3) provides a practical application of the results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The assumption of Gaussian measurement matrices might be restrictive.
*   The discussion of regularizer selection is limited.
*   The related work section is not exhaustive, omitting relevant literature in single-index models and related areas.
