- Problem: studies single-index model y_i = g(a_i^T x0) with structured x0 and Gaussian A using generalized (square-root) LASSO with convex regularizer.  
- Main result: asymptotic equivalence—generalized LASSO under nonlinear measurements has same error as linear model y = μ A x0 + σ z with μ = E[γ g(γ)], σ^2 = E[(g(γ)−μγ)^2].  
- Precise formulas: derives asymptotic MSE via a scalar convex optimization (Theorem 2.2) for general convex regularizers, with closed-form cases (e.g., Theorem 2.3).  
- Specializations: treats sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank regularizers; simulations match theory; shows Lloyd–Max quantizer minimizes error for q-bit quantized CS.  
- Prior work: extends Brillinger (1982) and Plan & Vershynin (2015) by providing asymptotically exact error characterizations for regularized LASSO under nonlinear single-index models.  
- Strength: clear main message that nonlinear SI measurements reduce to a scaled-noise linear model for LASSO analysis.  
- Strength: first asymptotically precise error characterizations for a broad class of regularizers, valid for all λ and explicit in δ, λ, f, and g.  
- Strength: concrete specializations and practical implication for quantizer design.  
- Weakness: nonlinear-to-linear equivalence is relegated to the appendix; needs a brief, intuitive main-text explanation.  
- Weakness: focuses on ℓ2-LASSO—should compare to ℓ2^2-LASSO, assess whether variance-independence advantage holds, examine λ sensitivity, and offer λ-selection guidance.  
- Weakness: strong reliance on Gaussian design and assumption μ ≠ 0; robustness to sub-Gaussian/correlated designs and the μ=0 case should be discussed.  
- Weakness: practical use may require estimating μ and σ from data—discussion of estimators and their effect is missing.  
- Clarity/reproducibility: request compact recipes and code for computing F(c1,c2,c3) and solving the scalar optimization (10), plus lighter notation and earlier highlighting of the main optimization.  
- Evaluation: technically strong, careful proofs leveraging modern tools; clarity good but could improve intuitions; originality and significance high.  
- Pros: asymptotically exact, interpretable results that bridge classic and modern theory and yield practical insights.  
- Cons: restricted to Gaussian/asymptotic regime, no finite-sample guarantees, limited discussion of λ tuning and μ,σ estimation.  
- Recommendation: accept after minor revisions to elevate the nonlinear-to-linear equivalence, compare ℓ2 vs ℓ2^2 LASSO and λ selection, and comment on robustness and μ,σ estimation.
