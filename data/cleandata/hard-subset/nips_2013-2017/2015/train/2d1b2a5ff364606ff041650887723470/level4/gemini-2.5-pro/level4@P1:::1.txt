This paper analyzes the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimating a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`. The central and compelling finding is that naively applying the standard LASSO estimator—which is designed for linear models—to this non-linear problem is not only effective but also yields a performance that can be characterized with high precision.

The authors demonstrate that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime with i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, the estimation error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is equivalent to that of a LASSO problem with linear measurements of the form `y_i = μ * a_i^T x0 + σ * z_i`. The paper provides explicit formulas for the scaling constant `μ` and the effective noise standard deviation `σ`, showing they depend only on the properties of the link function `g`. This work significantly extends the classical results of Brillinger (which applied to unregularized least squares in a fixed-dimension setting) and the more recent work of Plan and Vershynin (which provided non-asymptotic bounds for a constrained LASSO). The authors derive precise, deterministic expressions for the limiting mean-squared error for a general class of convex regularizers and demonstrate the theory's applicability to specific cases, including sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank signal recovery.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's main result, which establishes an asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear measurement model and an effective linear one, is elegant, powerful, and highly insightful. It provides a precise characterization of the LASSO's performance, moving beyond the upper bounds common in prior literature and offering a deeper understanding of why a seemingly mismatched estimator works so well.

2.  **High Technical Quality:** The analysis provides asymptotically exact formulas for the estimation error (Theorems 2.2 and 2.3), which represents a major technical advance. The derivations, which build on the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, appear solid. Furthermore, the theoretical predictions are convincingly validated by numerical simulations that show an excellent match across various settings (Figures 1-2).

3.  **Generality and Scope:** The analysis is conducted within a general framework that accommodates a wide variety of signal structures (via the convex regularizer `f`) and non-linearities (`g`), including important practical cases like 1-bit quantized measurements. This broad scope makes the results widely applicable.

4.  **Clear Practical Implications:** The theory has direct and valuable practical consequences, as compellingly demonstrated by the application to optimal q-bit quantizer design. The authors show that the complex goal of minimizing the final estimation error can be reduced to the much simpler problem of minimizing the ratio `σ²/μ²`, thereby connecting the problem to the well-known Lloyd-Max quantizer. This provides a concrete and useful design principle derived directly from the theory.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Contextualization with Related Literature:** The paper effectively connects its results to the work of Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin. However, as the authors note, the single-index model is a classical topic with an extensive literature in statistics and econometrics. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of how this work relates to, and advances upon, other key results in the single-index model and sufficient dimension reduction literature. This would help readers who are not specialists in that area to better appreciate the novelty and context of the contribution.

2.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** While the paper is generally very well-written and clear, it would benefit from a final round of proofreading. There appear to be several typos, particularly in the supplementary material and appendices containing the proofs. Correcting these would improve the overall polish of the manuscript.

3.  **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices and a specific high-dimensional asymptotic limit. These are standard assumptions in this line of research but are also limitations. It would be helpful for the authors to briefly comment on the potential for extending these results to other matrix ensembles or providing non-asymptotic guarantees.

In summary, this is a very strong paper presenting a surprising, elegant, and significant theoretical result. The authors provide a precise asymptotic characterization for the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements, with clear connections to a corresponding linear model. The work is technically impressive, and the results have immediate practical relevance. The paper is well-written and the claims are well-supported. I recommend acceptance.
