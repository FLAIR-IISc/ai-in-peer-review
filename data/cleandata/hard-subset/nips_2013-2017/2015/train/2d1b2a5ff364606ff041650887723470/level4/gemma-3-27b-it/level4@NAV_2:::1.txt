This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to measurements corrupted by nonlinear transformations. The authors establish precise asymptotic expressions for the estimation error, demonstrating that, surprisingly, the performance is asymptotically equivalent to that of a linear measurement model with appropriately scaled measurements and noise. This is a significant contribution, building upon previous work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, and providing much tighter error bounds. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and the technical analysis appears sound.

The core idea—characterizing the nonlinear measurement scenario through an equivalent linear one—is insightful and potentially quite useful. The authors carefully lay out their assumptions and proceed with a rigorous analysis, culminating in Theorem 2.2, which provides a precise error formula. The subsequent specialization to sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3) is a valuable addition, and the connection drawn to q-bit compressive sensing and the Lloyd-Max algorithm demonstrates the practical relevance of the results. The inclusion of supporting figures is helpful in illustrating the accuracy of the derived formulas.

One potential weakness lies in the strong assumption of Gaussian measurement matrices. While common in theoretical analyses, this limits the applicability of the results in practical scenarios where other measurement matrix designs are employed. Exploring the robustness of the findings to deviations from Gaussianity would be a valuable direction for future work. Additionally, the assumption of unit norm for x0, while simplifying the presentation, might not always hold. Although the authors acknowledge this and provide a path to generalization, explicitly addressing the impact of arbitrary norms would strengthen the work.

The paper demonstrates strong originality by providing precise asymptotic error characterizations in a setting where previously only loose bounds were available. The significance is considerable, as it advances our understanding of the fundamental limits of estimation with nonlinear measurements and provides a theoretical foundation for designing and analyzing algorithms in such scenarios. The results are likely to be of interest to researchers in signal processing, statistics, and machine learning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides precise asymptotic error bounds for Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements, a significant improvement over existing results.
*   Establishes a clear connection between nonlinear and equivalent linear measurement models.
*   The analysis is rigorous, and the technical contributions are substantial.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The results have potential practical implications, as demonstrated by the application to q-bit compressive sensing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies on the strong assumption of Gaussian measurement matrices.
*   The assumption of unit norm for x0, while acknowledged, restricts the generality of the results.
*   The connection to the single-index model literature, while mentioned, could be explored more deeply.



Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper. The technical contributions are significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the results are likely to be influential in the field. While the assumptions are somewhat restrictive, they are clearly stated, and the potential impact of the work outweighs these limitations.
