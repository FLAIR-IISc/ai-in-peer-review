Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation from nonlinear observations using the Generalized LASSO. The observation model is yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where A has i.i.d. N(0,1) rows, x0 is structured (sparse, block-sparse, low-rank, etc.), and the link functions gi are i.i.d. copies of a possibly random nonlinearity g that may be unknown. Building on Brillinger’s classical results for least squares with fixed n and m → ∞ and the non-asymptotic bounds of Plan and Vershynin for constrained estimators, the authors establish asymptotically exact error formulas for the Generalized LASSO in the proportional regime m/n → δ ∈ (0,∞).

The main result (Theorem 2.1) shows that, for Gaussian designs, the estimation error of the Generalized LASSO under nonlinear observations is asymptotically identical to that under an equivalent linear model with effective gain μ = E[γ g(γ)] and effective noise variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], where γ ∼ N(0,1). Thus, the nonlinear model behaves, for LASSO purposes, like a linear model ylin = A(μx0) + σz. Theorem 2.2 further provides a single-letter convex optimization whose unique minimizer gives the exact asymptotic squared error, explicitly capturing δ, λ, the regularizer f through a functional F defined via a Moreau-envelope limit, and g through (μ, σ^2). The paper then specializes the formula to: (i) sparse recovery with ℓ1 regularization (Theorem 2.3), (ii) group-sparse recovery via ℓ1,2, and (iii) low-rank recovery via nuclear norm (with a roadmap to compute F). An application to quantized compressed sensing demonstrates that minimizing the LASSO error over quantizers reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, and that Lloyd–Max iterations converge to stationary points of this objective, agreeing with the paper’s equivalence principle.

Relation to prior work: The paper extends Brillinger’s fixed-n LS asymptotics and strengthens Plan–Vershynin’s non-asymptotic bounds by delivering precise asymptotics and covering regularized estimators. It connects to the body of precise-error analyses for linear measurements via AMP and CGMT (Bayati–Montanari; Donoho–Maleki–Montanari; Stojnic; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.). The work is also related to single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction (e.g., Ichimura; Li & Duan) and to recent results on estimation under unknown nonlinear transforms, including cases with μ = 0 (Yi et al., 2015).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The results are clearly stated and (based on the appendices) appear rigorous. The equivalence theorem and the precise asymptotics are plausible given Gaussian rotational invariance and CGMT-type arguments.
- Assumptions: The i.i.d. Gaussian design is central; Assumption 1 (existence of the limiting functional F) is reasonable and verified for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2. The low-rank case is outlined but leaves some details to future work.
- Experiments: Simulations align with the theory and include nonlinearities (quantization) and multiple regularizers. More non-Gaussian design experiments would further bolster robustness claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with helpful figures. That said:
- Clarify why Gaussianity is essential (rotational invariance enabling exact reduction to the linear surrogate via CGMT) and discuss to what extent sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant designs should inherit the results.
- Streamline notation (e.g., consistent use of μ, σ, σ^2; minor typos such as “mesaurements”, “eventhough”).
- Explicitly state the identifiability issue and the unit-norm convention up front, and clearly separate the roles of direction vs. scale.

Originality
The equivalence of nonlinear and linear measurement models for Generalized LASSO with precise error characterization in the proportional regime is, to my knowledge, new and important. The specializations beyond ℓ1 (group-sparse, nuclear norm) and the quantizer-design consequence are novel and useful.

Significance
The results offer practical guidance: one can analyze and tune Generalized LASSO under nonlinear observations by analyzing a linear problem with effective parameters (μ, σ^2). The precise formulas can inform choices of λ, measurement budget m, and quantizer design. This is likely to impact both theory and practice in compressed sensing, quantization, and high-dimensional statistics.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean equivalence principle with precise, single-letter error characterization.
- Broad coverage of regularizers and structures; concrete specializations for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2.
- Insightful quantizer-design application (reducing design to σ^2/μ^2; Lloyd–Max relevance).
- Strong connections to and extensions of both classical (Brillinger) and modern (Plan–Vershynin, AMP/CGMT) literature.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Gaussian design assumption: Please discuss necessity (rotational invariance) and expected robustness to sub-Gaussian, orthogonally invariant, or design with mild correlations. Any partial extensions or conjectures would be valuable.
- μ ≠ 0 requirement: The paper assumes μ ≠ 0. Please comment on the μ = 0 case (e.g., odd/even nonlinearities, 1-bit sign without dithering) and relate to approaches that handle μ = 0 (e.g., Yi et al., 2015), even if outside the current scope.
- Choice of regularizer: The paper assumes a convex regularizer but does not discuss how to select f for a given structured set K. A short discussion (or references) on matching convex gauges/atomic norms to structure would help practitioners.
- Assumption 1: Provide more intuition, broader classes where F exists, and explicit formulas beyond the given examples (e.g., more details for the nuclear-norm case).
- Finite-sample guidance: The asymptotics are precise; please add a short discussion on finite-n accuracy, suggested λ tuning, and sensitivity to model misspecification.
- Related work: Expand the discussion of single-index and sufficient dimension reduction literature (e.g., Ichimura; Li & Duan) and include Yi et al. (2015) more prominently, noting their handling of μ = 0 and differences in goals and methods.

Minor comments
- Fix typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., σ vs. σ^2 in the abstract; small grammatical issues).
- Ensure all references are complete and correctly formatted; add missing SDR/single-index citations.
- Clarify the distinction between ℓ2-LASSO vs. square-root LASSO terminology and ensure the objective in (2)–(9) is consistent throughout.
- Number figures and equations consistently; ensure footnotes are properly placed.

Questions for the authors
- Do your results extend to sub-Gaussian designs or orthogonally invariant ensembles? What breaks without exact Gaussianity?
- Can you provide guidance for choosing λ based on the formula in Theorem 2.2 (e.g., minimizing α*(λ))?
- How sensitive are the predictions to estimation of μ and σ^2 when g is unknown?
- For the low-rank case, can you provide a worked example of F under a standard spiked model?

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, precise theoretical contribution with clear practical implications.
- Solid integration and extension of influential lines of work (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin; AMP/CGMT).
- Broad applicability across structured regimes and a compelling application to quantizer design.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on Gaussian design with limited discussion of robustness.
- μ ≠ 0 limitation excludes important nonlinearities (e.g., pure sign without dithering).
- Some technical details (Assumption 1 for nuclear norm) deferred; related-work coverage incomplete.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper provides a compelling and technically strong equivalence principle with precise error formulas that should interest the NIPS community. Addressing the scope limitations and related-work gaps would further strengthen the contribution.
