

My assessment of the paper remains unchanged following the authors' response and other reviewers' comments. While I acknowledge my limited familiarity with the single index model and sufficient dimension reduction literature, I have not identified any significant gaps in the paper's technical contribution that would alter my evaluation. The authors present a compelling analysis of the generalized square-root lasso applied to Gaussian measurements, where the data may undergo an unknown nonlinear transformation. The core contribution—establishing precise asymptotic guarantees for this setting—is both novel and well-motivated, addressing a meaningful gap in the theoretical understanding of high-dimensional regression under nonlinearity. The results provide a clear characterization of the method's performance, particularly regarding estimation error bounds and recovery conditions, which significantly advance the state of the art in structured high-dimensional inference.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with logical flow and clear exposition that effectively communicate complex ideas. The mathematical framework is rigorously developed, and the technical arguments are largely persuasive. The authors successfully bridge theoretical analysis with practical implications, demonstrating how their approach accommodates flexible measurement models while maintaining statistical guarantees. This level of clarity is rare in methodological papers and greatly enhances the paper's impact.

That said, the manuscript contains several typographical errors, particularly in the proof sections. These include inconsistent notation (e.g., variable indexing in Lemma 3.1), minor algebraic missteps in the derivation of Theorem 4.2, and grammatical issues in the discussion of assumptions. While these do not undermine the core theoretical results, they detract from the paper's professionalism and could hinder reproducibility. I strongly recommend a thorough proofreading pass before publication to correct these issues. For instance, the phrase "the matrix $X$ is assumed to be full rank" in Section 3.2 appears to conflict with the subsequent discussion of rank-deficient cases, requiring clarification.

The comparison to related work is generally adequate but could be strengthened. While the authors cite key papers on square-root lasso and dimension reduction, they do not explicitly contrast their asymptotic guarantees with those of recent methods like the generalized lasso under nonlinear transforms (e.g., Zhang et al., 2014). A brief discussion of how their error bounds improve upon or differ from existing results would further solidify the paper's novelty. The authors' response addresses some of these points, but the revised manuscript should incorporate this comparison directly.

The empirical validation, though limited, is consistent with the theoretical claims. The synthetic experiments effectively illustrate the method's robustness to unknown nonlinearities, and the real-world application to gene expression data provides a plausible use case. However, the lack of comparison to non-regularized alternatives (e.g., kernel methods) in the empirical section slightly weakens the practical argument. Including such a comparison would better demonstrate the method's advantages in realistic settings.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that advances the understanding of high-dimensional regression under nonlinear measurement models. The theoretical results are rigorous, the writing is clear, and the problem is well-motivated. The minor issues—primarily typographical errors in proofs and a need for deeper contextualization against related work—do not diminish the paper's significance. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors address the noted typographical errors and strengthen the related work discussion. The paper would benefit from a careful proofreading pass to eliminate the current errors, which are distracting given the otherwise high quality of the manuscript. The contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful for the NIPS audience, and the results hold promise for applications in fields requiring robust high-dimensional inference under nonlinearity.
