This paper addresses the problem of high-dimensional linear estimation from measurements acquired through a general, nonlinear link function. Building upon the recent work of Plan and Vershynin, the authors analyze the asymptotic estimation error of a generalized LASSO estimator. The central contribution, presented in Theorem 2.1, establishes that for link functions whose derivative has a non-zero first moment (i.e., $\mu \neq 0$), the asymptotic estimation error is identical to that of the standard linear measurement case. This result demonstrates a surprising and powerful equivalence between linear and a broad class of nonlinear estimation problems in the high-dimensional regime. The theoretical conclusions are supported by rigorous proofs and complemented by empirical results, making this a well-written paper with interesting findings.

However, there are several points that could be clarified or strengthened.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Restrictive Measurement Assumption:** The main theoretical result in Theorem 2.1 relies on the assumption that the measurement vectors are drawn from a Gaussian distribution. While this is a common assumption that enables precise analysis via tools like the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT), it may limit the practical applicability of the results. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could discuss the necessity of this assumption. Is it a fundamental requirement for the linear/nonlinear equivalence to hold, or is it primarily a proof technique that could potentially be relaxed? A discussion on potential extensions to sub-Gaussian or other measurement ensembles would be valuable.

2.  **Missing Discussion of Related Literature:** The paper's positioning could be improved by discussing its relationship to two highly relevant lines of work:
    *   **Single Index Models (SIM) and Sufficient Dimension Reduction (SDR):** There is a substantial body of work on SIM and SDR that directly addresses the problem of estimating a linear vector or subspace under an unknown link function. This literature is highly relevant and should be reviewed to properly contextualize the paper's contributions within the broader field.
    *   **Concurrent Work on the $\mu=0$ Case:** A recent paper, "Optimal linear estimation under unknown linear estimation" by Yi et al., appears to be highly relevant. This work reportedly introduces an algorithm for sparse recovery that succeeds even when the link function has a zero mean derivative ($\mu = 0$), a case explicitly excluded by the current paper's assumptions. It also seems to establish a similar equivalence between high-dimensional linear estimation with linear and nonlinear link functions. A detailed comparison with this work is necessary to clarify the novelty and specific contributions of the current paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Practical Choice of Regularizer:** The theoretical framework assumes a convex regularizer is chosen based on a set $\mathcal{K}$ in which the true signal $x_0$ lies (e.g., a norm ball corresponding to a certain sparsity level). While the properties of the regularizer are discussed, the practical issue of how to select it when the properties of $x_0$ (and thus the set $\mathcal{K}$) are unknown is not addressed. Could the authors comment on the practical implications of this or provide guidance on how the regularizer might be chosen in a real-world scenario?

2.  **Intuition on Parameter Dependence:** The main result establishes an equivalence in the form of the asymptotic error for linear and nonlinear measurements. However, the final error characterization still depends on the parameters $\mu$ and $\sigma$, which are properties of the nonlinear function $g$. This suggests that while the problem is structurally equivalent to a linear one, the "hardness" of the estimation, as reflected in the magnitude of the error, still depends on the specific nonlinearity. Could the authors provide more intuition on the necessity of these dependencies? If the problem is truly equivalent to the linear case, why don't these parameters, which characterize the nonlinearity, vanish or get absorbed into a generic noise term?
