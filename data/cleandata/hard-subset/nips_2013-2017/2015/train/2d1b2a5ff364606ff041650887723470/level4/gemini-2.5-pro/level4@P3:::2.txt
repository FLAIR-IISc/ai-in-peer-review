This paper presents a precise asymptotic analysis of the Generalized LASSO estimator for recovering a structured signal from non-linear measurements, a setup commonly known as the single-index model. The central contribution is an elegant and powerful equivalence principle: in the high-dimensional limit with a Gaussian measurement matrix, the performance of the LASSO (which naively assumes a linear model) on data from a non-linear model `y_i = g(a_i^T x_0)` is identical to its performance on data from an equivalent linear model `y_i = μ a_i^T x_0 + σ z_i`. The scaling factor `μ` and effective noise standard deviation `σ` are determined solely by the properties of the link function `g`.

This work builds upon and significantly strengthens two lines of research. First, it extends the classical work of Brillinger on single-index models from the fixed-dimension, unregularized setting to the modern high-dimensional, regularized regime. Second, it advances the recent results of Plan and Vershynin for the same problem by replacing their non-asymptotic upper bounds with precise asymptotic characterizations of the mean-squared error. The analysis, which appears to be based on the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework, is general enough to accommodate a wide variety of signal structures (e.g., sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and non-Gaussian signal priors, thereby also extending recent works on the precise analysis of LASSO in the standard linear setting. The authors demonstrate the practical utility of their theoretical result with an application to optimal quantizer design.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Fundamental and Significant Result:** The main finding—that the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to one with linear measurements—is a clean, powerful, and insightful result. It provides a definitive characterization of how a standard algorithm behaves under model mismatch, showing that the effect of the non-linearity can be neatly captured by two scalar parameters, `μ` and `σ`. This simplifies the analysis and understanding of a complex problem.

2.  **Technical Novelty and Quality:** The paper provides the first asymptotically exact error predictions for a LASSO-type estimator in the single-index model setting. Moving from upper bounds to precise formulas is a significant technical achievement. The theoretical framework is general and rigorously applied to specific cases of interest, such as sparse and group-sparse recovery, with theorems that appear novel even when specialized to the linear measurement case (e.g., for non-Gaussian signal priors).

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The main results are stated precisely in Theorems 2.1 and 2.2, and their implications are effectively demonstrated through specific examples and numerical simulations that impressively validate the theoretical predictions.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The application to optimal `q`-bit quantizer design is a compelling example of the theory's utility. By reducing a complex optimization problem over estimator performance to a simple problem of minimizing the ratio `σ^2/μ^2`, the authors provide a clear and actionable design principle. The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is particularly interesting.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Upfront Intuition:** The core idea that, for a Gaussian design matrix `A`, the single-index model is approximately equivalent to a scaled linear model is a known concept, dating back to Brillinger (1982). While the paper rigorously establishes this equivalence for the LASSO estimator's performance in the appendix, the main text would benefit from discussing this heuristic connection earlier. Briefly mentioning this in the introduction would provide valuable intuition and help the reader anticipate why the main result (Theorem 2.1) is plausible, thereby strengthening the paper's narrative flow.

2.  **Limited Discussion on the Choice of `l2`-LASSO:** The paper focuses exclusively on the `l2`-LASSO, also known as the Square-Root LASSO. A primary motivation for using this variant over the more common `l2^2`-LASSO in standard linear models is that the optimal regularization parameter `λ` is independent of the unknown noise variance. This paper's results could shed new light on this choice. The non-linearity `g` induces an *effective* noise level `σ` and signal scaling `μ`. A crucial practical question is whether the `l2`-LASSO retains its "noise-blind" advantage in this setting. Does the optimal choice of `λ` depend on `μ` and `σ`, which are derived from the (potentially unknown) link function `g`? A discussion on this point would significantly enhance the paper's practical impact by providing guidance on the choice and tuning of LASSO variants for non-linear measurement problems.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a significant and elegant contribution to the literature on high-dimensional statistics. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to improving the exposition and discussion rather than to any fundamental flaws in the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Establishes a fundamental and clean equivalence principle for LASSO under non-linear measurements.
*   Provides the first precise asymptotic performance characterizations for this problem, a significant technical advance over existing bounds.
*   The results are general, covering various structures and signal priors.
*   The paper is well-written and the theory is convincingly validated by experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The exposition could be improved by providing more intuition for the main result upfront.
*   The practical implications regarding the choice between `l2`-LASSO and `l2^2`-LASSO in this new context are not fully explored.
