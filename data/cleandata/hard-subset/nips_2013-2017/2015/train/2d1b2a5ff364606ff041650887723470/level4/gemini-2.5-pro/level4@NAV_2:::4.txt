This paper investigates the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal, `x0`, from measurements that have passed through a non-linear link function, i.e., `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. The authors analyze the performance of the Generalized LASSO, an estimator ostensibly designed for linear models, in this non-linear setting. Drawing inspiration from the classical work of Brillinger and more recent results by Plan and Vershynin, the paper's central contribution is to show that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit and for i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, the LASSO's performance is precisely equivalent to its performance on a corresponding linear model with additive Gaussian noise: `yi = μa_i^T x0 + σz_i`. The effective signal scaling `μ` and noise level `σ` are shown to depend only on the moments of the non-linear link function `g`. This powerful equivalence theorem allows the authors to derive exact asymptotic expressions for the mean-squared estimation error for a broad class of convex regularizers, including those promoting sparsity, group-sparsity, and low-rank structure.

**Strengths:**

*   The key result of the paper—the asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear measurement problem and a specific noisy linear one—is elegant, insightful, and represents a significant theoretical advance. It cleanly decouples the effect of the non-linearity from the underlying structured recovery problem.
*   The analysis provides asymptotically *precise* characterizations of the estimation error, moving beyond the non-asymptotic upper bounds found in prior work. This offers a much deeper and more quantitative understanding of the LASSO's behavior in this setting. The provided figures demonstrate that these theoretical predictions are remarkably accurate in practice.
*   The framework is general and powerful, covering a wide variety of structures via general convex regularizers. The specific applications to `l1`, `l1,2`, and nuclear-norm regularization are well-developed and provide novel, concrete results for these important cases.
*   The paper demonstrates the practical utility of its theoretical findings through an interesting application to `q`-bit compressive sensing. The discovery that the optimal Lloyd-Max quantizer minimizes the LASSO's final estimation error is a non-obvious and valuable consequence of the main theorem.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   The analysis relies on the assumption that the measurement matrix `A` has i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a common assumption in the literature that enables the use of powerful analytical tools, but it is restrictive. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the necessity of this assumption and the potential challenges in extending the results to broader matrix ensembles, such as those with sub-Gaussian entries.
*   While the paper correctly identifies its connection to the single-index model, the literature review in this area could be more comprehensive. The problem is closely related to a large body of work in statistics and econometrics on single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction, and a more thorough positioning with respect to this classical literature would better contextualize the contribution.
*   A more detailed comparison to highly relevant contemporary work is needed. For example, the paper's analysis is restricted to link functions where `μ ≠ 0`. The work of Yi et al. [24] proposes an alternative estimation strategy that succeeds even when `μ=0`. A discussion comparing the performance and applicability of the standard LASSO (as analyzed here) versus such specialized methods would provide readers with a more complete view of the state-of-the-art for this problem.
*   The paper convincingly shows that `μ` and `σ` govern the equivalent linear model. Could the authors provide more physical or geometric intuition as to why these specific moments, `E[γg(γ)]` and `E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2]`, are precisely the quantities that emerge as fundamental in the high-dimensional limit?

**Evaluation Summary:**

*   **Quality:** The paper appears to be of high technical quality. The claims are substantial and appear to be supported by a rigorous asymptotic analysis framework. The work is presented as a complete and polished contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The main ideas, assumptions, and results are stated with precision. The use of figures to validate the theoretical formulae is effective.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on a clear line of previous research, the derivation of exact asymptotic error formulae for the regularized LASSO under general non-linear measurements is a novel and significant contribution to the field.
*   **Significance:** The results are of considerable significance. They provide a fundamental and precise understanding of how a ubiquitous algorithm behaves in a challenging, non-standard setting. The established equivalence is a powerful theoretical tool that is likely to be used and built upon by other researchers. The findings also have direct implications for the practical design of systems involving signal quantization.
