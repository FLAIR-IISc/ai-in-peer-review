This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimating structured signals from non-linear measurements, building upon the classical work of Brillinger and more recent contributions by Plan and Vershynin. The core result demonstrates that, asymptotically, the estimation error with non-linear measurements is equivalent to that of a linear model with scaled measurements and additive Gaussian noise. The authors derive precise expressions for this error, a significant advancement over existing bounds, and explore implications for optimal quantization strategies.

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The motivation is well-established, and the connections to existing literature (Brillinger, Plan & Vershynin, and recent work on precise LASSO analysis) are appropriately highlighted. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant theoretical concepts and effectively position their contribution within the broader field of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The precise asymptotic characterization of the estimation error in the presence of non-linear measurements is a significant contribution. Existing results were either less precise or focused on constrained versions of the problem.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the authors carefully state their assumptions and limitations. The extension of prior work to the high-dimensional regime (m/n → δ) is also noteworthy.
*   **Practical Implications:** The result relating non-linear measurements to scaled linear measurements has practical consequences, as demonstrated by the application to q-bit compressive sensing and the analysis of the optimal quantizer. The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is a valuable insight.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making the complex mathematical arguments accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of l2 vs. l2^2-LASSO:** While the paper appropriately identifies the l2-LASSO (square-root LASSO) as the focus, it doesn’t fully explore the implications of the result for justifying the choice of l2 over the more common l2^2-LASSO. The advantage of the l2-LASSO – independence of the regularization parameter from the noise variance – is mentioned, but a deeper discussion of whether this holds true in the non-linear setting would be beneficial.
*   **Heuristic Justification:** The core idea – the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements – relies on the work of Brillinger. While rigorously proven in the appendix, a more intuitive explanation within the main body of the paper would improve readability for a broader audience. Briefly mentioning the core argument in Brillinger’s proof in the introduction (e.g., concerning the characteristic function) would be helpful.
*   **Assumption 1 Complexity:** Assumption 1, regarding the limit of a complex expression involving the Fenchel conjugate, feels somewhat opaque. While mathematically sound, it could benefit from a more intuitive explanation or a simpler condition that guarantees its satisfaction in common scenarios.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the literature on high-dimensional estimation.
*   The derived results are precise and have potential practical implications, particularly in areas such as compressed sensing with quantized measurements.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the implications for the choice between l2 and l2^2-LASSO.
*   The heuristic justification for the core equivalence result could be strengthened within the main body of the paper.
*   The complexity of Assumption 1 might limit the accessibility of the results to a broader audience.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed with further clarification and discussion. I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2015, provided the authors address the points raised above. I recommend a score of **7/10** and a recommendation of **Accept**.
