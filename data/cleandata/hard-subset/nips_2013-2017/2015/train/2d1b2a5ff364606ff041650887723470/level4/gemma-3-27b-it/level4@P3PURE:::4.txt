This paper investigates linear estimation problems with a nonlinear link function. Building upon the recent work of Plan and Vershynin, the authors analyze the asymptotic estimation error when using a generalized lasso for arbitrary link functions with μ ≠ 0. A key finding is that the asymptotic error is identical for both linear and nonlinear link functions, as formally stated in Theorem 2.1.

However, Theorem 2.1 relies on the assumption that the linear measurement vector is sampled from a normal distribution, which may be restrictive in certain applications. The authors should provide further justification for this assumption and explain why it is necessary for the derived results to hold.

I have a few questions for the authors. First, how should the regularization parameter be chosen based on the set Kai, within which the true parameter vector x₀ lies? While the paper discusses the assumption of a convex regularizer, the specific problem of parameter selection based on Kai remains unaddressed. Second, while the results demonstrate equivalence between linear and nonlinear measurements, the conclusion still depends on μ and σ, which characterize the difficulty of estimation from the nonlinear function g. I am curious whether the authors have considered the necessity of these dependencies, and whether they provide insights into the role of the nonlinear function’s properties.

Finally, the paper would benefit from acknowledging related work. Specifically, the literature on single index models and sufficient dimension reduction studies linear vector/subspace estimation under unknown link functions, yet these are not discussed. Furthermore, the recent paper “Optimal linear estimation under unknown linear estimation” by Yi et al. introduces a novel algorithm for sparse recovery, even when the link function has μ = 0, and establishes the equivalence of high-dimensional linear estimation with linear and nonlinear link functions under appropriate conditions. This work provides strong theoretical and empirical support for similar conclusions.

Overall, this is a well-written paper presenting interesting and potentially significant results.
