### Summary

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimating a structured signal from non-linear measurements with a Gaussian design matrix. The work builds upon a common heuristic that treats non-linearities as additive noise and formalizes this notion in an asymptotic regime. Drawing inspiration from the work of Plan and Vershynin [17], which provided non-asymptotic performance bounds, this paper provides a significant extension by deriving precise, explicit expressions for the estimation error. The central result demonstrates that, asymptotically, the mean-squared error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is equivalent to that of a LASSO problem with linear measurements, where the signal is appropriately scaled and corrupted by Gaussian noise. The parameters of this equivalent linear model are shown to depend only on the first and second moments of the non-linear link function. The authors validate the accuracy of their theoretical predictions across several important special cases, including `l1`-norm (LASSO), `l1,2`-norm (Group LASSO), and nuclear-norm regularization. Furthermore, the paper presents an insightful application of the theory to the design of optimal quantizers for q-bit compressive sensing, establishing a connection to the classic Lloyd-Max quantizer.

### Quality and Significance

This is a high-quality and significant theoretical contribution. The paper tackles an important problem at the intersection of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. While the idea that non-linearities can be modeled as noise is not new, this work provides a rigorous and precise characterization of this phenomenon for regularized estimators in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution—deriving precise asymptotic formulas for the estimation error—is a clear and important advance over the existing non-asymptotic upper bounds found in [17]. This provides a much deeper and more exact understanding of the LASSO's behavior in this setting.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical results appear to be sound and are built upon established, state-of-the-art analysis techniques for high-dimensional inference. The claims are well-supported by the derivations (deferred to the appendix) and corroborated by numerical simulations.
*   **Generality:** The framework is quite general, covering a wide class of convex regularizers (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and non-linear link functions.
*   **Practical Application:** The application to optimal quantizer design is particularly compelling. By showing that the complex problem of minimizing estimation error can be reduced to optimizing the effective signal-to-noise ratio (`µ²/σ²`), the paper provides a clear and practical guideline for system design and connects it to the well-known Lloyd-Max algorithm.

### Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors do a good job of situating their work within the relevant literature and clearly stating their contributions. However, the presentation is dense, and clarity could be improved in a few areas.

**Weaknesses/Areas for Improvement:**
*   The treatment of some technical aspects is brief, likely due to space constraints. For instance, Assumption 1 is central to the main theorems but may be opaque to readers not intimately familiar with this line of work. A more intuitive explanation or connection to more standard assumptions in the main text would be helpful.
*   The use of non-standard terminology for some concepts from convex analysis (see detailed comments) could cause confusion. Adhering to conventional naming would enhance readability.

### Originality

The paper is original and makes a distinct contribution. While it builds directly on the foundations laid by Brillinger [5] and Plan and Vershynin [17], it moves beyond their results by shifting from performance bounds to exact asymptotic characterizations. To the best of my knowledge, these precise error formulas for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements are novel.

### Detailed Comments

1.  **Terminology for the Estimator:** The paper frequently refers to the Generalized LASSO as an "algorithm." It is more accurately described as an estimator, a convex optimization problem, or a decoder. An "algorithm" refers to a specific procedure for solving this optimization problem (e.g., ISTA, ADMM). I suggest refining this terminology throughout the manuscript for greater precision.

2.  **Terminology for Moreau Envelope:** On page 5, line 294, the paper uses a notation `ef,τ(v)` and refers to it as related to the "proximal function." This function is well-known in convex analysis as the Moreau envelope (or Moreau-Yosida regularization) of `f`. The term `prox` is almost universally reserved for the proximal operator, not the envelope itself. Using the standard term "Moreau envelope" would improve clarity and prevent confusion.

3.  **Interpretation of Assumption 1:** While technically precise, Assumption 1 could benefit from a more intuitive or formal interpretation. For example, could the authors connect this assumption to established concepts in variational analysis or provide more insight into which classes of regularizers `f` and signal distributions `px0` satisfy it? This would help readers better grasp the scope of the results.

4.  **Constrained vs. Penalized LASSO:** On page 4, line 193, the text discusses the constrained LASSO. It might be worth briefly noting that the penalized (Lagrangian) form and the constrained form are closely related and often equivalent under an appropriate correspondence of the tuning parameter, which helps connect the results here to prior work on the constrained version.

5.  **Minor Typos:**
    *   **Figure 1 Caption:** 'n=768' should be typeset in math mode as `$n=768$'.
    *   **Page 5, line 247:** There appears to be a typo at the beginning of the sentence "When clear from the context...". Please review.
