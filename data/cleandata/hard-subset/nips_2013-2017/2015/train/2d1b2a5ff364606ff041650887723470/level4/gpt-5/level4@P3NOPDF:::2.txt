Summary
This paper provides a precise asymptotic characterization of the statistical performance of the ℓ2-Lasso (square-root Lasso) for single-index models (SIMs), while relaxing the common Gaussian prior assumption on the signal x0. The work extends recent progress on exact asymptotics for Lasso-type estimators and contributes to understanding their behavior under model misspecification induced by unknown link functions. The paper is clearly written and technically careful.

Strengths
- Extends sharp risk characterizations of the ℓ2-Lasso to SIMs, a widely used and practically important class beyond linear models.
- Relaxes the Gaussian prior on x0, broadening the applicability of the results.
- The analysis appears rigorous, with well-organized proofs and clear notation.
- The topic is timely and relevant for both theory and practice, particularly for tuning and method selection in high-dimensional regression with unknown noise levels.

Main comments and suggestions
1) Emphasize the linear-model equivalence of SIMs in the main text.
As noted by Brillinger (1982), a single-index model can be recast as a linear model with a scaled (unknown) signal and a non-standard (generally heteroskedastic and possibly correlated with the covariates) noise term. You already formalize this connection in Appendix A; however, it should also be discussed in the main text, ideally early on. Doing so would:
- Provide an immediate heuristic for why square-root Lasso analyses for linear models are relevant under SIMs.
- Help justify the statement in the blockquote on line 140 and guide the reader’s intuition about how the link function influences effective noise and signal scaling.
A short remark or figure summarizing the mapping (slope of the link at the relevant projection, effective noise variance, and any induced correlation structure) would be very helpful.

2) Clarify the practical advantage of ℓ2-Lasso versus ℓ2^2-Lasso under SIMs.
A central motivation for the square-root Lasso is that the oracle tuning parameter is independent of the (typically unknown) noise variance σ in linear models. Your results for SIMs are well-placed to answer whether this variance-independence—and its practical benefits—persist under model misspecification:
- Does the oracle choice of the ℓ2-Lasso regularization parameter remain independent of σ in the SIM setting, once the SIM is expressed as a linear model with non-standard noise? If not, which problem-dependent quantities replace σ, and how do they depend on the link and the distribution of x0?
- Can you provide an explicit comparison (analytical or empirical) between optimally tuned ℓ2-Lasso and ℓ2^2-Lasso under SIMs? Even a corollary or a brief discussion would be valuable. For example, do your formulas imply regimes where ℓ2^2-Lasso is competitive once σ is estimated, or where ℓ2-Lasso is provably preferable due to robustness to link-induced noise?
- It would be particularly informative to translate your asymptotic formulas into concrete guidance for tuning λ in practice (e.g., how to set λ to achieve near-oracle risk if the link is unknown), and to comment on whether cross-validation recovers the oracle λ in finite samples for SIMs.

3) Scope and assumptions.
Because the paper relaxes the Gaussian prior on x0, it would help to state early and prominently:
- The exact moment and tail assumptions needed on x0 (e.g., finite second moment, sub-Gaussian tails, bounded fourth moment). Do the results tolerate heavy-tailed priors, and if not, where do the arguments require stronger control of moments?
- The conditions on the link function (e.g., monotonicity, differentiability, Lipschitz continuity) and how they enter the asymptotic risk only through a small number of scalar parameters. If feasible, summarize these parameters in the main text (with pointers to derivations in the appendix), and connect them to the Brillinger linearization to bridge intuition and theory.
- The design assumptions (presumably Gaussian i.i.d.). If the results hinge on Gaussian design via CGMT or related tools, a short comment on whether one expects robustness to sub-Gaussian designs would improve clarity.

4) Presentation and intuition.
- Consider adding a brief “takeaways” paragraph after the main theorem(s) that distills the dependence of the optimal risk and tuning on: sample-to-dimension ratio, sparsity (if relevant), link properties, and prior on x0. A concise bullet list can substantially aid readers.
- Since Appendix A carries important intuition, include a standalone remark or proposition in the main text summarizing the equivalence and its implications for tuning and risk.
- If feasible, include a small simulation illustrating: (i) the accuracy of the asymptotic formulas under SIMs; (ii) the practical tuning of λ for ℓ2-Lasso; and (iii) a comparison to ℓ2^2-Lasso under the same setting. Even a modest experiment would anchor the theoretical insights.

Minor comments
- Please reference Brillinger (1982) in the main text where you discuss the SIM-to-linear equivalence, and explicitly connect that discussion to the blockquote on line 140.
- It would be helpful to state the proportional asymptotics (n,p → ∞ with fixed ratio) upfront in the introduction, to set reader expectations.
- Where possible, reduce notational overload by collecting key symbols in a short glossary or table.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution to the precise asymptotic analysis of modern high-dimensional estimators. The extension of square-root Lasso risk characterizations to SIMs and non-Gaussian priors is both novel and useful. With a clearer integration of the SIM-to-linear reduction in the main text and a more explicit discussion of the practical advantage of ℓ2-Lasso over ℓ2^2-Lasso in this setting—including guidance on tuning and, ideally, a brief empirical illustration—the paper would be even more impactful. I recommend acceptance after addressing the points above.
