Summary
This paper studies high-dimensional estimation from nonlinear measurements of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where x0 is structured and A has i.i.d. standard Gaussian entries. The authors analyze the generalized LASSO with a convex regularizer f and show a striking equivalence: asymptotically, the estimation error of the generalized LASSO under nonlinear measurements is exactly the same as that under a corresponding linear model with scaled signal and additive Gaussian noise, ylin = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], with γ ∼ N(0,1). Theorem 2.1 formalizes this equivalence, and Theorem 2.2 provides an exact asymptotic error formula via a three-scalar convex optimization whose objective depends on δ = m/n, λ, μ, σ, and a function F that encodes the structure of x0 and the regularizer via a Moreau-envelope limit. The paper specializes these results to sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3), group-sparse recovery, and low-rank matrix recovery, and applies them to quantized compressive sensing, showing that the optimal quantizer (for LASSO estimation error) coincides with the Lloyd–Max quantizer.

Relation to prior work: The results significantly strengthen Brillinger’s classical analysis (consistent LS under nonlinear link, with proportionality μ, in fixed n and m → ∞) and extend recent non-asymptotic bounds by Plan and Vershynin for constrained LASSO under nonlinear link. Methodologically, the work connects to precise error analyses for LASSO under linear measurements (AMP-based and CGMT-based), and generalizes them to broad classes of convex regularizers and nonlinear links.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The nonlinear-to-linear reduction with explicit μ and σ is clean and powerful, and Theorem 2.1 establishes equality of asymptotic errors, not just bounds.
- Generality: The framework covers a wide class of convex regularizers (including ℓ1, group ℓ1,2, and nuclear norm) and link functions g, with precise dependence on δ, λ, μ, and σ.
- Precision: Theorem 2.2 gives an exact scalar convex program for the limiting error; the specializations (e.g., Theorem 2.3) are insightful and match simulations.
- Impact: The quantizer design consequence (optimality of Lloyd–Max under this criterion) is both interesting and practically relevant.

Weaknesses and questions
- Gaussian measurement assumption: The equivalence and precise CGMT-based analysis require A with i.i.d. N(0,1) entries. This is strong. Are there universality results or extensions to sub-Gaussian, isotropic, or rotationally invariant ensembles? A discussion of necessity vs. proof technique would help readers interpret scope.
- Assumptions on μ and σ: The main results assume μ ≠ 0 and σ^2 bounded. While this is standard in the single-index literature, it excludes important cases (e.g., some even links) and some nontrivial g with μ = 0. The paper briefly notes this and cites alternatives for special cases. It would be useful to elaborate on whether the dependence on μ and σ is information-theoretically necessary (it seems so) and whether one can estimate μ and σ from data when g is unknown. Since LASSO does not require μ, σ to run, guidance on post-scaling and practical estimation (e.g., moment-based estimates or calibration when g is known up to scale) would be helpful.
- Choice of regularizer and λ: The analysis accommodates general convex f, but the paper does not provide guidance on how to pick f given a model class K (e.g., the relation between K in constrained formulations and penalized f via descent cones/Gaussian width) or how to tune λ in practice. Since Theorem 2.2 yields exact risk as a function of λ, the authors could discuss data-driven selection (e.g., using the derived equations or approximate risk minimization) and the mapping between constrained and penalized forms in this nonlinear setting.
- Assumption 1 and F: The Moreau-envelope limit F(c1,c2,c3) is central. While the paper works out F for several key cases (sparse, group-sparse), more intuition on when Assumption 1 holds and how to compute F beyond these examples (e.g., for nuclear norm with general spectral distributions) would improve clarity.
- Clarity and related work: The paper is generally well written but dense. A more explicit summary of the proof strategy (CGMT pipeline from the nonlinear model to its linear surrogate and then to the scalar problem) would aid readability. Also, the literature review on single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction is quite sparse; it should cite foundational SDR work and relevant single-index estimation methods. In addition, Yi et al., “Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform” (arXiv:1505.03257), proposes algorithms that can handle μ = 0 and is directly relevant.

Quality
The theoretical results are strong, precise, and appear technically sound, with proofs leveraging modern tools for sharp asymptotics. The simulation studies (as described) align with theory. Clarifying the necessity of the Gaussian assumption and the role of Assumption 1 would further strengthen the technical presentation.

Clarity
Overall clear, but the paper could benefit from:
- A brief, high-level roadmap of the proof.
- More discussion of Assumption 1 and concrete recipes for F in the low-rank case.
- Minor proofreading (typos: “mesaurements,” “redifining,” etc.) and consistent notation.

Originality
To the best of my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact error analysis for generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements and general convex regularizers, extending beyond ℓ1. The quantizer-design consequence via Lloyd–Max is a nice byproduct.

Significance
High. The nonlinear-to-linear equivalence with exact risk characterization is likely to influence both theory and practice (e.g., quantized sensing, one-bit CS, censored models), and it unifies disparate observations in the single-index literature with modern high-dimensional analysis.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Please discuss whether the Gaussianity of A is essential or a proof artifact, and whether universality is plausible.
- Provide guidance on choosing f for a given set K and on tuning λ (e.g., leveraging Theorem 2.2 or the λcrit characterization in Theorem 2.3).
- Expand on the necessity and estimation of μ and σ when g is unknown; clarify how post-scaling should be handled in practice.
- Add related work in single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction, and discuss Yi et al. (2015) which addresses μ = 0 cases.
- Consider additional experiments with non-Gaussian A to probe robustness, and with group-sparse/low-rank settings to illustrate the generality.

Pros
- Precise, general, and elegant equivalence result (nonlinear ≡ linear with μ, σ).
- Exact asymptotic error characterization; covers multiple structures and regularizers.
- Practical implication for quantizer design (Lloyd–Max optimality).
- Strong alignment between theory and experiments.

Cons
- Strong Gaussian measurement assumption; limited discussion of universality.
- Limited practical guidance on choosing f and tuning λ.
- Sparse coverage of related single-index/SDR literature; omission of closely related recent work handling μ = 0.
- Dense presentation; Assumption 1 and F could be further demystified.
