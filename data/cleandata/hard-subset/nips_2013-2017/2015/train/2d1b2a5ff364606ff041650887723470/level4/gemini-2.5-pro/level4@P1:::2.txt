This paper provides a precise, asymptotic characterization of the performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to single-index models, where measurements are non-linear functions of linear projections of a signal. Building upon the classical work of Brillinger and more recent results by Plan and Vershynin, the authors demonstrate a remarkable equivalence: in the high-dimensional limit, the estimation error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is identical to that of a LASSO applied to a corresponding linear model with a rescaled signal and an effective noise level. These scaling and noise parameters (μ and σ) are shown to depend only on the non-linear link function. The analysis covers a wide class of convex regularizers—including those for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank signals—and notably relaxes the common assumption of a Gaussian signal prior. The theoretical predictions are validated through numerical simulations, showing excellent agreement.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The main result, establishing a precise asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear measurement model and a corresponding scaled linear model, is both elegant and powerful. It provides a concrete analytical tool for understanding and predicting the performance of LASSO-type estimators in a broad class of challenging non-linear settings. This extends a significant line of research on the precise analysis of high-dimensional estimators into a new and important domain.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper appears technically sound, leveraging sophisticated tools from high-dimensional probability to derive its results. The derived formulas for the mean squared error are explicit and provide clear insights into the interplay between the number of measurements, the signal structure, the choice of regularizer, and the properties of the non-linear link function.
*   **Generality:** The framework is quite general. The ability to handle various regularizers and non-Gaussian signal priors makes the results broadly applicable and of interest to a wide audience in machine learning, statistics, and signal processing. The application to optimal quantizer design is a nice demonstration of the theory's practical utility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is well-written and the contributions are clear. The following points are suggestions for further improving the manuscript's clarity and impact.

1.  **Motivating the Core Equivalence:** The paper's central finding—that the single-index model is asymptotically equivalent to a scaled linear model—is rigorously proven. However, the intuition behind this result, which has roots in the work of Brillinger (1982), could be introduced more explicitly in the main text. While the connection is detailed in the appendix, highlighting this heuristic justification earlier in the paper would better motivate the approach and make the core claim more immediately intuitive for the reader.

2.  **Discussion on the Choice of l2-LASSO:** The authors focus on the l2-LASSO (or Square-Root LASSO), a choice often motivated by the fact that the optimal regularization parameter λ is independent of the noise variance. This is a significant practical advantage over the standard l2²-LASSO when the noise level is unknown. In the context of this work, the effective signal scaling (μ) and noise variance (σ²) are functions of the (potentially unknown) link function `g`. This raises a compelling practical question: does the l2-LASSO retain its advantage in this setting? A discussion on whether this "noise-blind" property is still practically useful, given that the effective noise level σ depends on `g`, would be a valuable addition and would significantly strengthen the paper's practical implications.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper presents a strong, novel, and significant theoretical contribution to the literature on high-dimensional estimation. The results are precise, general, and provide a fundamental understanding of how the popular LASSO estimator behaves with non-linear measurements.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The weaknesses identified are minor and relate to improving the exposition and discussion; they do not undermine the core technical contributions of the work.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a worthwhile contribution to the literature on the precise asymptotic behavior of LASSO-type estimators. I recommend its acceptance.
