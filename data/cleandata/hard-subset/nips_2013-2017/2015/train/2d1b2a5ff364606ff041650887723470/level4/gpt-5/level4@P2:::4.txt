Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies recovery of a structured vector x0 from nonlinear observations yi = gi(a_i^T x0) using the generalized LASSO min_x ||y − A x||_2 + λ f(x), where f is a convex regularizer matched to the structure (e.g., ℓ1, group ℓ1,2, nuclear norm). Building on Brillinger’s classical result for least squares and Plan–Vershynin’s bounds for constrained LASSO under single-index models, the authors prove an asymptotically exact equivalence: with i.i.d. Gaussian design, the estimation error of generalized LASSO under nonlinear link g is identical to that under an effective linear model with scaled signal and additive Gaussian noise, y_lin = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). Theorem 2.1 formalizes this equivalence; Theorem 2.2 provides a precise scalar optimization that yields the asymptotic squared error, and specializations are given for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank settings, with simulations validating the formulas. A notable application is to q-bit compressed sensing: optimizing the quantizer to minimize the LASSO error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, which leads to Lloyd–Max quantization.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean and practically useful equivalence between nonlinear and linear observation models for generalized LASSO, capturing the role of g only through (μ, σ).
- Precise, asymptotically exact error characterizations (not just bounds), extending the CGMT-based line of work from linear to nonlinear links.
- Broad applicability across regularizers and structures; specialization to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank cases is helpful.
- The quantizer design consequence (Lloyd–Max optimality for minimizing LASSO error) is elegant and actionable.
- The writing is generally clear, with well-motivated comparisons to Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin, and informative figures.

Weaknesses and questions:
1) Gaussian design assumption: The main theorems require A to have i.i.d. N(0,1) entries. Is rotational invariance essential, or do the results extend to subgaussian designs, general rotationally invariant ensembles, or structured designs (e.g., partial Fourier)? Some discussion of what breaks (or can be salvaged) beyond Gaussian would strengthen the impact.

2) Dependence on μ and σ: The results assume μ ≠ 0 and use μ, σ in the equivalent linear model. Since the algorithm itself does not require knowledge of g, how should μ and σ be estimated from data when g is unknown? Can method-of-moments or Stein-type identities provide consistent estimators, and how sensitive are the asymptotic predictions and tuning recommendations to estimation error in μ and σ? A brief treatment would improve practical relevance.

3) Choice of regularizer and tuning: The paper assumes a convex f matched to the structure of x0. It would be useful to provide guidance on mapping structural assumptions (e.g., a known set K or prior on x0) to a choice of f, and to discuss how Theorem 2.2 can inform optimal or near-optimal selection of λ (e.g., via the scalar program).

4) Assumption 1 and verification: While the paper verifies the key envelope-conjugate limit for several important f (ℓ1, ℓ1,2, nuclear norm), additional intuition and general conditions ensuring the existence of F(c1,c2,c3) (e.g., via properties of proximal operators and distributional limits) would help readers apply the framework to other regularizers.

5) μ = 0 regime: The main results exclude μ = 0, which arises in important cases (e.g., odd/even symmetries or certain quantizers). Please discuss the μ = 0 regime and connections to recent work that explicitly handles it, such as Yi et al. (2015), which proposes algorithms for sparse recovery under unknown nonlinear transforms, including cases with μ = 0.

6) Statement of Theorem 2.1: The equality “with probability one” between the two errors appears to be an equality of asymptotic limits (i.e., both converge almost surely to the same deterministic value), not a pathwise identity for finite n. Clarifying this phrasing would avoid confusion.

Quality:
- The theoretical development appears careful and builds on the CGMT framework and prior precise analyses; the specialization to canonical regularizers is convincing. The empirical validation aligns well with predictions.
- To aid reproducibility, consider adding more details on numerical evaluation of the scalar programs (e.g., initialization, convexity checks, and runtime).

Clarity:
- The exposition is generally strong. A pass to fix typos and minor issues would help (e.g., “mesaurements,” “expecation,” “redifining,” consistent naming of LASSO variants, consistent notation for g vs. g_i).
- It may help to collect the definitions of μ, σ, δ, and F in one place and keep notation consistent across sections.

Originality:
- To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact error characterization for generalized LASSO under nonlinear single-index measurements, generalizing precise linear-model results to this setting. The quantizer design corollary is novel and insightful.

Significance:
- The results yield immediate, interpretable prescriptions for performance prediction, regularization tuning, and measurement system design under nonlinearities, and should be of broad interest to the NIPS community working on high-dimensional inference and inverse problems.

Relation to additional literature:
- While the paper cites Brillinger, Plan–Vershynin, and parts of the single-index literature (e.g., Ichimura; Li and Duan), a more explicit discussion situating this work within the single-index and sufficient dimension reduction literature would benefit readers less familiar with that area.
- Please also discuss the connection and differences to Yi, Wang, Caramanis, and Liu (2015), which addresses optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transforms, including μ = 0.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical contribution with precise, broadly applicable results.
- Clear conceptual equivalence simplifying nonlinear measurement analysis to linear.
- Practical impact on quantizer design and parameter tuning.
- Empirical validation supports theory.

Cons for acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on i.i.d. Gaussian designs without a clear path to more general matrices.
- Limited discussion of μ estimation and robustness when g is unknown.
- Limited guidance on regularizer selection and λ tuning beyond the theory.

Overall assessment:
A well-written and rigorous paper with interesting and useful results. Addressing the scope beyond Gaussian designs, clarifying μ/σ estimation, and better connecting to related single-index/SDR and μ = 0 literature would further strengthen the submission.
