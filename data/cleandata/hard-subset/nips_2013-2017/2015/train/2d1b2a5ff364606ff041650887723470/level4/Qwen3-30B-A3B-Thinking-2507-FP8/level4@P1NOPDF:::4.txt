

This paper investigates linear estimation with a nonlinear link function, building on Plan and Vershynin's recent work. It analyzes the asymptotic estimation error of generalized lasso for arbitrary link functions with μ ≠ 0, revealing a surprising result: the asymptotic error is identical whether the link function is linear or nonlinear, as established in Theorem 2.1.  

Theorem 2.1 assumes the linear measurement vector is sampled from a normal distribution. While this is a standard technical assumption, the authors should clarify why this specific distributional requirement is necessary for their conclusion to hold, as it may be restrictive in certain applications.  

Two key questions remain unaddressed:  
1. **Regularizer selection**: The paper discusses convex regularizers but does not specify how to choose the regularizer based on the set κ containing the true signal x₀. This gap limits the practical applicability of the framework.  
2. **Role of μ and σ**: Although the result shows equivalence between linear and nonlinear measurements, the asymptotic error depends on μ and σ—parameters characterizing the difficulty of estimation under the nonlinear link g. The authors should elaborate on the necessity of these dependencies and whether they reflect inherent properties of the nonlinear setting.  

The paper also omits relevant literature. First, the single-index model and sufficient dimension reduction literature extensively studies linear vector/subspace estimation under unknown link functions, yet this work provides no discussion of these foundational approaches. Second, Yi et al.'s recent paper "Optimal Linear Estimation Under Unknown Link Functions" (ICML 2015) establishes a similar equivalence between linear and nonlinear estimation for sparse recovery under proper conditions, including cases where μ = 0. Yi et al. support their claims with rigorous theory and empirical validation, offering a complementary perspective that merits comparison.  

Overall, the paper presents an interesting theoretical result with clear implications for high-dimensional estimation. However, addressing the distributional assumption, clarifying the regularizer selection mechanism, resolving the dependence on μ and σ, and incorporating key related work would significantly strengthen the contribution. The technical arguments are sound, but the current presentation would benefit from deeper engagement with the broader literature and practical considerations.
