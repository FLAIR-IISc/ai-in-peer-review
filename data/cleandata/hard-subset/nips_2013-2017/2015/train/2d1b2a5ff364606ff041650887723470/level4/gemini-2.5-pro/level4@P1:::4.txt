This paper addresses the problem of estimating a structured signal (e.g., sparse, low-rank) from non-linear measurements using the Generalized LASSO. The measurement model is of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where g is a potentially unknown and random non-linear link function. Building upon the foundational work of Brillinger and more recent results by Plan and Vershynin, the authors move beyond upper bounds to provide an asymptotically exact characterization of the estimation error. The central and insightful result (Theorem 2.1) is that the performance of the LASSO estimator in this non-linear setting is asymptotically identical to its performance on an equivalent linear model yi = µ a_i^T x0 + σ z_i. The scaling factor µ and noise level σ are determined by the first and second moments of the link function g with respect to a standard Gaussian input. The paper derives a precise formula for this asymptotic mean-squared error (Theorem 2.2) and demonstrates its accuracy for various signal structures, including sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery. An interesting application to the optimal design of multi-bit quantizers is also presented.

### Strengths:
*   The paper's main contribution is a powerful and elegant theoretical result. The equivalence between the non-linear measurement problem and a corresponding linear one provides deep insight into the behavior of the LASSO. This simplifies the analysis of a complex problem, reducing it to the more well-understood linear setting.
*   The analysis is precise, yielding exact asymptotic formulas for the estimation error, in contrast to the upper bounds common in prior work. These formulas explicitly capture the roles of the problem parameters (m, n, λ), the signal structure (via the function F), and the link function (via µ and σ).
*   The results are quite general, holding for a wide class of convex regularizers and link functions, as demonstrated by the specific applications to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank models.
*   The paper is well-written, and the theoretical claims are supported by numerical simulations that show a remarkable match between the theory and practice.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:
1.  **Gaussian Measurement Matrix:** The analysis relies on the assumption that the entries of the measurement matrix A are i.i.d. Gaussian. This is a strong assumption that may limit the practical applicability of the results. While common in this line of theoretical work, it would be valuable for the authors to comment on the necessity of this assumption for their proof techniques. Is it possible to extend these results to a broader class of sub-Gaussian measurement ensembles?

2.  **The Role of µ and σ:** The results elegantly show that the entire effect of the non-linearity `g` is captured by the two scalars µ and σ. This is a fascinating simplification. Could the authors provide more intuition as to why these specific first- and second-order moments are sufficient to fully characterize the asymptotic error, and why higher-order properties of `g` do not appear to play a role?

3.  **Choice of Regularizer:** The framework is general with respect to the convex regularizer `f(x)`. However, the paper provides little guidance on how one might choose a regularizer for a given signal structure beyond mentioning standard pairings (e.g., L1-norm for sparsity). Some discussion on this topic would be beneficial.

4.  **Missing Related Work:** The literature review could be strengthened by acknowledging two related areas more thoroughly:
    *   The paper mentions the "single-index model," but the broader literature on this topic and on "sufficient dimension reduction," which also deals with estimation under unknown link functions, seems under-cited.
    *   More importantly, the authors seem to have missed a highly relevant contemporary paper: "Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform" by Yi et al., arXiv:1505.03257 (2015). That work also establishes an equivalence between non-linear and linear high-dimensional estimation problems. Critically, it introduces an algorithm that succeeds even when µ=0, a case explicitly excluded by the current submission. A discussion of this work is necessary to properly position the current paper's contributions and limitations.

### Overall Assessment:
This is a high-quality paper presenting a significant and interesting theoretical result. The established equivalence between non-linear and linear models for the LASSO is a fundamental contribution that advances our understanding of high-dimensional estimation.

*   **Quality:** The work appears technically sound, providing precise, non-trivial results supported by simulations.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While building on prior work, the move from bounds to exact asymptotic characterizations for the regularized LASSO in this setting is a novel and important contribution. However, the originality should be contextualized against the work of Yi et al.
*   **Significance:** The findings are likely to be influential for both theorists and practitioners working on structured signal recovery. The equivalence theorem is a powerful tool for analysis and could guide practical applications, as demonstrated by the quantizer design example.

My main reservations concern the strong Gaussian assumption and the omission of key related work, particularly Yi et al. (2015), which addresses the important µ=0 case. Addressing these points, especially the latter, in a revised version would substantially strengthen the paper. Despite these points, the core contribution is strong, and I recommend acceptance.
