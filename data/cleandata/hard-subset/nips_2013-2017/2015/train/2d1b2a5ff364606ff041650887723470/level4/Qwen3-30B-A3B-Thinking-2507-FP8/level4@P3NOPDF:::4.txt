

This paper investigates linear estimation under a nonlinear link function, extending recent work by Plan and Vershynin. The core contribution is Theorem 2.1, which establishes that the asymptotic estimation error for generalized Lasso remains identical regardless of whether the link function is linear or nonlinear, provided the linear measurement vector is drawn from a Gaussian distribution. The result is theoretically significant, as it demonstrates equivalence between linear and nonlinear settings under the specified conditions.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear theoretical derivation and the non-trivial insight that nonlinearity does not inherently increase estimation difficulty in the asymptotic regime. The presentation is generally well-structured, and the technical arguments are sound. However, several critical points require clarification and expansion to strengthen the contribution.

First, the assumption that measurement vectors follow a Gaussian distribution is restrictive for practical applications. While this simplifies the analysis, the authors should explicitly justify why this assumption is necessary for Theorem 2.1 to hold. Without this, the result’s applicability to real-world scenarios—where measurements may arise from non-Gaussian distributions—remains unclear. The authors should address whether the equivalence in error rates persists under weaker distributional assumptions or discuss the limitations imposed by Gaussianity.

Second, the paper does not resolve a key practical question: how to select the regularizer based on the unknown set $\mathcal{K}$ containing the true signal $x_0$. While the paper assumes a convex regularizer, it fails to specify *how* this regularizer should be chosen for a given $\mathcal{K}$ or how the choice impacts the asymptotic error. This omission is significant, as the regularizer’s design directly influences the feasibility of the approach in practice. The authors should elaborate on the relationship between $\mathcal{K}$, the regularizer, and the conditions required for Theorem 2.1 to apply.

Third, the dependence of the error bound on $\mu$ and $\sigma$ (which characterize the link function $g$) warrants deeper discussion. Theorem 2.1 shows that the error scales with these parameters, yet the paper does not clarify *why* this dependence is necessary. Specifically:  
1. Are $\mu$ and $\sigma$ unavoidable for characterizing the estimation difficulty under nonlinear links?  
2. Could the error bound be expressed without these terms under alternative assumptions?  
A brief analysis of these parameters’ role would strengthen the theoretical interpretation and highlight the conditions under which nonlinear links *do* introduce additional complexity.

The paper also overlooks important related work. It fails to cite foundational literature on single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction, where linear or subspace estimation under unknown link functions has been extensively studied (e.g., [references]). More critically, it neglects Yi et al.’s recent NIPS 2014 paper *"Optimal Linear Estimation under Unknown Link Functions"*, which establishes an equivalence between linear and nonlinear estimation under similar conditions, including the case $\mu = 0$. Yi et al. provide rigorous proofs and empirical validation, making their work highly relevant. The current paper should engage with this parallel contribution, clarifying how its results differ or align with theirs.

In summary, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by showing asymptotic error equivalence for linear/nonlinear settings under Gaussian measurements. However, the restrictive measurement assumption, unresolved regularizer selection, and unclear role of $\mu$/$\sigma$ need addressing. The omission of key related work further diminishes the paper’s impact. If these points are resolved—particularly by justifying the Gaussian assumption, detailing regularizer selection, and contextualizing the results against Yi et al.—the paper would be significantly strengthened. As it stands, the core insight is compelling but its scope and practical relevance require clarification. The writing is clear and the results are interesting, but the discussion of limitations and related work must be expanded to meet NIPS standards.
