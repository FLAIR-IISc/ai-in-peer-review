Summary
The paper studies linear parameter estimation under an unknown nonlinear link function using the generalized Lasso. Building on the framework of Plan and Vershynin, it analyzes the asymptotic estimation error for arbitrary link functions with μ ≠ 0 and shows, perhaps surprisingly, that the asymptotic error matches that of the linear measurement model. Theorem 2.1 formalizes this equivalence under Gaussian design and convex regularization.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean result: Theorem 2.1 shows that, asymptotically, the generalized Lasso achieves the same error under nonlinear measurements as under linear ones, once the effective parameters of the nonlinearity are taken into account.
- Generality: The analysis applies to arbitrary link functions g with μ ≠ 0 and to general convex regularizers, which makes the result broadly applicable.
- Potential impact: The equivalence between nonlinear and linear measurement models, when it holds, is an appealing message for practitioners who prefer convex estimators.

Concerns and questions
1) Gaussian design assumption. Theorem 2.1 relies on the measurement vectors being i.i.d. Gaussian. This is a strong and sometimes restrictive assumption. Please clarify:
- Is Gaussianity essential, or would the result extend to sub-Gaussian or more general isotropic designs (e.g., via small-ball or rotational invariance arguments)?
- Which parts of the proof use exact Gaussian properties (e.g., Stein’s lemma, rotational invariance), and could they be relaxed?
- If exact equivalence fails beyond Gaussian designs, can you state partial extensions (e.g., matching rates up to constants, or additional moment conditions)?

2) Choice of regularizer. The analysis assumes a convex regularizer but does not provide guidance on how to select it from prior knowledge about x0 lying in a set K.
- Can you give a principled prescription for choosing the regularizer from K (e.g., gauge of K, atomic norms), and relate the error to geometric quantities (e.g., Gaussian width of descent cones)?
- Are there data-driven or adaptive choices (e.g., cross-validation, SURE-like criteria) consistent with the theory?

3) Role and necessity of μ and σ. Although the main message is that the nonlinear and linear models have the same asymptotic error, the bounds depend on μ and σ that quantify the effective strength and noise induced by g.
- Are these dependencies information-theoretically necessary? For instance, can you show lower bounds matching the μ- and σ-dependence?
- Can μ and σ be estimated from data, and if so, does plugging in consistent estimators preserve the equivalence and rates?

4) Boundary and special cases.
- What happens when μ = 0, which covers important nonlinearities (e.g., symmetric sign functions under symmetric designs)? Is there a modified estimator or additional structure (e.g., sparsity, dithering, known thresholds) that restores consistency?
- Can you quantify finite-sample error (not just asymptotic equivalence), e.g., rates in n, dimension, and model complexity?

Relation to prior work
- The paper would benefit from a discussion of the single-index model and sufficient dimension reduction literatures, which also study estimation of a linear index or subspace under unknown link functions. Even if your goals differ (prediction/estimation under convex regularization rather than index recovery), positioning the contribution relative to these areas will help readers.
- Please discuss the recent line of work on high-dimensional estimation under unknown nonlinear transformations, including algorithms that address cases with μ = 0. In particular, the paper by Yi et al. (referred to in the draft) proposes methods for sparse recovery when the effective linearization parameter vanishes and establishes conditions under which linear and nonlinear models are equivalent. Clarifying similarities and differences in assumptions, targets, and guarantees would strengthen the contribution.

Presentation and clarity
- Define μ and σ clearly at first use, and keep the notation consistent throughout.
- State explicitly the assumptions on the design distribution, the link function g, and the regularizer (e.g., convexity, decomposability if needed).
- It would help to add practical interpretations: what μ and σ represent for common nonlinearities (e.g., logistic, probit, sign with dithering), and how they translate to effective SNR.
- If possible, include empirical illustrations to demonstrate the equivalence across different g and to show robustness beyond Gaussian designs.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written paper with a clear and interesting main result: under Gaussian designs and μ ≠ 0, the generalized Lasso achieves the same asymptotic error for nonlinear and linear link functions. The contribution is timely and conceptually appealing. To strengthen the paper, I recommend: (i) clarifying the necessity and robustness of the Gaussian assumption; (ii) giving guidance on selecting the regularizer from prior structure; (iii) discussing the information-theoretic necessity and practical estimation of μ and σ; and (iv) expanding the related-work section to connect with single-index/SDR and recent high-dimensional results, including cases with μ = 0. With these improvements, the paper would make a solid contribution. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the above points.
