Summary
This paper studies generalized LASSO estimation under non-linear single-index measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian design. Building on Brillinger’s classical result for least squares and the non-asymptotic bounds of Plan and Vershynin, the authors prove that, in a high-dimensional linear-asymptotic regime (m/n → δ), the estimation performance of the generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically identical to that of a generalized LASSO with linear Gaussian measurements y = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2] with γ ∼ N(0,1). Beyond this equivalence, the paper provides asymptotically exact, explicit expressions for the squared error via a low-dimensional convex program whose inputs are δ, λ, the regularizer f, and the structure of x0 (encapsulated through a functional F). The theory covers popular regularizers (l1, group l1,2, and nuclear norm), and is validated numerically. An application to quantizer design in q-bit compressed sensing shows that minimizing the LASSO error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 over quantizer thresholds and levels, which connects to the Lloyd–Max quantizer and establishes it as stationary for the proposed criterion.

Relation to prior work
- Brillinger (1980s) analyzed LS with non-linear measurements in the classical m → ∞, fixed n regime; this paper extends to high dimensions and to regularized estimators.
- Plan and Vershynin provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements; this paper strengthens those results to precise asymptotics for the regularized LASSO and makes the μ, σ dependence exact.
- The analysis leverages modern precise-error tools developed for linear measurements (AMP/CGMT-based works), generalizing them to non-linear observations and to broader classes of regularizers.

Evaluation
Quality: The results are technically sound and carefully stated. The equivalence theorem (non-linear = linear with μ, σ) is crisp, and the main error formula is a convex scalar optimization that matches simulations. Specializations to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank cases are well worked out. Assumptions are standard for precise asymptotics with Gaussian design, though Assumption 1 could be better motivated.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized and readable. Some terminology is nonstandard (proximal function vs. Moreau envelope), and a few typos/notation choices detract from polish. The role and interpretation of the scalar optimization variables (α, β, τ) could use more intuition, and Assumption 1 would benefit from a more formal explanation.

Originality: Strong. To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact characterization of generalized LASSO error under non-linear single-index measurements, covering a broad family of regularizers. The quantizer-design consequence is insightful and practically relevant.

Significance: High. The results unify the treatment of non-linearities under generalized LASSO, provide actionable design guidance (e.g., quantizer thresholds/levels), and clarify the impact of λ, δ, and the regularizer on performance. The precise characterization can inform tuning and system design in compressed sensing and single-index models.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptual contribution: a sharp and broadly applicable equivalence between non-linear and linear-noisy models for generalized LASSO.
- Precise, practically usable error predictions across multiple structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank).
- Clear identification of μ and σ as the sufficient statistics of the non-linearity for LASSO error.
- Insightful application to q-bit quantization, with a concrete design prescription linked to Lloyd–Max.
- Strong experimental validation across regimes and regularizers.

Cons and limitations
- Gaussian design is essential; robustness to sub-Gaussian or structured designs is not addressed.
- Assumption 1 is somewhat opaque; readers may struggle to verify it beyond the provided examples.
- The case μ = 0 (e.g., odd/even symmetries) is excluded from the main development; a brief discussion of alternatives would help.
- Limited finite-sample guidance (e.g., choice of λ, estimation of μ and σ when g is unknown).
- Experiments could report more details (trial counts, error bars) and explore non-Gaussian measurement matrices.

Specific comments and suggestions
1) p.4, line 193: Please clarify that, under appropriate conditions, the constrained and penalized formulations are equivalent via Lagrange multipliers, and state the mapping between λ and the constraint radius.

2) Figure 1 caption: Use consistent mathematical notation, e.g., “n = 768.”

3) p.5, line 247: Typo in the sentence beginning “When …”; please correct.

4) p.5, around line 294: What is called the “proximal function” is more commonly known as the Moreau envelope (with index τ). The current notation overloads “prox,” which is typically reserved for the proximal operator. I recommend renaming to “Moreau envelope” and adjusting notation accordingly.

5) Assumption 1: Provide a more formal interpretation using variational analysis/epi-convergence. For instance, relate the pointwise limits of normalized Moreau envelopes of f* to distributional limits of (h, x0) and invoke known results (e.g., Attouch–Wets/epi-convergence) to justify when F(c1, c2, c3) exists.

6) Terminology: Avoid calling the generalized LASSO an “algorithm.” It is a convex program/estimator/decoder; different algorithms can be used to solve it.

7) Experiments:
- Add brief guidance on λ selection in practice and show sensitivity plots versus λ to complement the theory.
- Report the number of Monte Carlo trials and include error bars or confidence intervals.
- If possible, include one experiment with sub-Gaussian or mildly structured A to probe robustness.
- In the quantizer design section, explicitly show that minimizing σ^2/μ^2 tracks the empirical error across q and (t, ℓ), and clarify the optimization procedure and convergence checks for Lloyd–Max.

8) Practical estimation of μ and σ: When g is unknown, discuss whether μ and σ can be estimated from data (e.g., via sample moments of y and a_i^T x̂), and whether the theory suggests consistent plug-in strategies.

9) Intuition for Theorem 2.2: A short paragraph explaining the roles of α (asymptotic error), β (effective correlation), and τ (dual/regularization parameter) would help readers parse the scalar program.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution that advances our understanding of generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements from qualitative bounds to precise asymptotic characterizations. The results are broadly applicable, technically solid, and well validated. Addressing the clarity and minor presentation issues above would further improve the paper. I recommend acceptance.
