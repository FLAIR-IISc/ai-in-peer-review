This paper investigates linear estimation in the presence of a nonlinear link function. Building upon the recent work of Plan and Vershynin, the authors analyze the asymptotic estimation error achieved using generalized lasso regularization for arbitrary link functions with non-zero mean shift (\mu \ne 0). A key finding is that, surprisingly, the asymptotic error is identical for both linear and nonlinear link functions, as formalized in Theorem 2.1.

The analysis in Theorem 2.1 relies on the assumption that the linear measurement vector is sampled from a normal distribution. While this is a common assumption, it may be restrictive in certain applications. The authors should provide a more detailed justification for the necessity of this assumption for the derived conclusions to hold, and potentially discuss the implications of relaxing it. A brief discussion of alternative distributions or conditions under which the results might still be valid would strengthen the paper.

I have two primary questions for the authors. First, the paper discusses the choice of regularizer assuming a convex function, but it remains unclear how to optimally select the regularization parameter based on the set \(\mathcal{K}\) within which the true parameter vector \(x_0\) is known to lie. Further elaboration on this practical aspect of implementation would be valuable. Second, while the results demonstrate equivalence in asymptotic error between linear and nonlinear measurement scenarios, the error expression itself depends on \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\), parameters that characterize the difficulty of estimation when the link function is nonlinear (g). I am curious if the authors have considered the necessity of this dependence. Does it reflect a fundamental difference in the statistical properties of the nonlinear case that manifests in the error variance, or is it simply a consequence of the specific analysis techniques employed?

The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive review of related work. Specifically, the established literature on single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction, which addresses linear vector/subspace estimation under unknown link functions, is not acknowledged. This area represents a significant body of work directly relevant to the problem considered here. Furthermore, the recent paper by Yi et al. ("Optimal linear estimation under unknown linear estimation") introduces a novel algorithm for sparse recovery that operates effectively even when the link function has zero mean shift (\mu = 0). This work demonstrates an equivalence between high-dimensional linear estimation with linear and nonlinear link functions under specific conditions, supported by both theoretical results and empirical validation. Incorporating this related work would provide valuable context and highlight the contribution of the present paper.

Overall, this is a well-written paper presenting interesting and potentially valuable results. The equivalence between linear and nonlinear estimation errors, under the stated conditions, is a noteworthy finding. Addressing the points raised regarding the normality assumption, regularization parameter selection, the dependence on \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\), and a more thorough literature review would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and completeness.
