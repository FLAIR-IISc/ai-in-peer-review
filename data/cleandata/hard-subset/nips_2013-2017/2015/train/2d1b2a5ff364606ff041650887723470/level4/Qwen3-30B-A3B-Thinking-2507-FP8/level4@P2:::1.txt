

This paper addresses a fundamental question in high-dimensional signal estimation: how well does the Generalized LASSO perform when measurements are nonlinear functions of the signal? The authors extend seminal work by Brillinger (1980s) and Plan and Vershynin (2013) to provide precise asymptotic characterizations of estimation error for the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements. The key insight is that, for Gaussian measurement matrices and under appropriate conditions, the performance of the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements yi = gi(ai^T x0) is asymptotically equivalent to its performance with linear measurements yi = μ(ai^T x0) + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ)-μγ)²] for γ ~ N(0,1). This equivalence holds for all values of the regularization parameter λ and for a wide class of regularizers and link functions g.

The paper builds upon recent advances in precise error analysis of the Generalized LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 21]), extending them to the nonlinear setting. The authors derive a precise error formula that captures the role of the link function g, the signal structure, the regularizer f, and the regularization parameter λ. Their main theoretical contribution is Theorem 2.1, which establishes the equivalence between nonlinear and linear measurements, and Theorem 2.2, which provides an explicit asymptotic expression for the estimation error. The authors further illustrate their results with special cases including sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, and low-rank matrix recovery.

Strengths: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first precise asymptotic characterization of the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements. This fills a critical gap in the literature, as previous work by Plan and Vershynin [17] only provided loose upper bounds. The authors' approach is elegant and general, with results applicable to a wide range of regularizers and link functions. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with thorough illustrations of the theoretical results in Figures 1-2. The application to q-bit compressive sensing, where they show that the optimal quantizer is the Lloyd-Max quantizer, is particularly compelling and demonstrates the practical relevance of their theoretical findings.

Quality: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The proofs are rigorous, and the authors carefully address the necessary assumptions. The asymptotic results are precise and hold for all values of λ, which is a significant improvement over previous work. The authors provide a clear connection between their results and existing literature on the Generalized LASSO with linear measurements, demonstrating how their results generalize and extend prior work.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear exposition of the problem, results, and their significance. The notation is consistent, and the structure follows a logical progression from the problem statement to the main results and their implications. The technical details are presented with sufficient clarity for readers familiar with high-dimensional statistics and optimization. The paper would benefit from a few minor clarifications regarding the assumptions on the link function g (e.g., why g cannot be even), but these are minor points.

Originality: This is a highly original contribution. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first paper to provide precise asymptotic error expressions for the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements. While the equivalence between nonlinear and linear measurements was suggested by Plan and Vershynin, the authors provide the first rigorous and precise characterization of this phenomenon. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the single-index model literature, extending the classical results of Brillinger to the structured signal setting.

Significance: The results have significant theoretical and practical implications. The precise error characterization enables better understanding of the Generalized LASSO's behavior under nonlinear measurements, which is crucial for applications like 1-bit compressive sensing, quantized measurements, and other scenarios with nonlinear measurement devices. The demonstration that the optimal quantizer for the Generalized LASSO is the Lloyd-Max quantizer provides a concrete design principle for practical applications. The paper's findings will likely influence future work on estimation under nonlinear measurements.

Weaknesses: The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the assumptions required for the results to hold, particularly regarding the link function g. For example, the authors assume μ ≠ 0, but it would be helpful to understand what happens when μ = 0. The paper focuses on Gaussian measurement matrices, but it would be valuable to discuss how the results might extend to other measurement ensembles. The experimental section is somewhat limited, with only two figures illustrating the theoretical predictions; more extensive numerical validation would strengthen the paper.

The paper contains several minor typos and formatting issues, particularly in the proof sections (e.g., missing spaces, inconsistent notation), which should be corrected in the final version.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in understanding the behavior of the Generalized LASSO under nonlinear measurements. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the literature. The results have both theoretical importance and practical relevance for applications involving nonlinear measurements. I recommend acceptance.
