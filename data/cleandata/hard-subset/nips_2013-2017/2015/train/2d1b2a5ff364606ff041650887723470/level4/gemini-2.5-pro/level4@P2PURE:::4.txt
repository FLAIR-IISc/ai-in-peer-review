This paper considers the problem of linear estimation from measurements obtained via a nonlinear link function. Drawing inspiration from recent work by Plan and Vershynin, the authors analyze the asymptotic estimation error of the generalized LASSO for arbitrary link functions where the parameter $\mu \ne 0$. The central result, presented in Theorem 2.1, is that this asymptotic error is identical for both linear and nonlinear link functions.

A potential limitation is that Theorem 2.1 relies on the assumption that the linear measurement vectors are sampled from a Gaussian distribution. This assumption may be restrictive in some practical scenarios, and it would strengthen the paper if the authors explained why this condition is necessary for their conclusions to hold.

A few questions for the authors:
1. The paper discusses the use of a convex regularizer related to a set $\mathcal{K}$ containing the true signal $x_0$. However, it does not address the practical question of how to choose this regularizer based on prior knowledge of this set. Could the authors provide some guidance on this point?
2. The main result establishes an equivalence in asymptotic error between linear and nonlinear measurements. Yet, this error characterization still depends on $\mu$ and $\sigma$, which are parameters of the nonlinear link function $g$ that seem to quantify the problem's difficulty. Could the authors elaborate on the necessity of this dependence? It suggests that while the form of the error is identical, the inherent difficulty of the nonlinear problem is still encoded in the final result.

The paper could also be improved by including several missing references:
1. The literature on Single Index Models and Sufficient Dimension Reduction is highly relevant, as these fields also study the estimation of linear vectors or subspaces under unknown link functions. The current draft does not review any work from this area.
2. A recent paper by Yi et al., "Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear measurement," appears particularly relevant. It introduces an algorithm for sparse recovery that works even for link functions with $\mu = 0$ and establishes the equivalence of high-dimensional estimation for linear and nonlinear link functions under appropriate conditions. This work, supported by rigorous theory and empirical results, should be discussed in relation to the current submission.
