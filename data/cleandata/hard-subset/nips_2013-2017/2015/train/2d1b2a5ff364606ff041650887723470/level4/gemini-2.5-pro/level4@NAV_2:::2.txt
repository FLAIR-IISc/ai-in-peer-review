This paper studies the problem of structured signal recovery from non-linear measurements, a setup also known as the single-index model. The authors analyze the performance of the Generalized LASSO (or `l2`-LASSO) estimator, which naively treats the measurements as if they were linear. Building upon the classical work of Brillinger and more recent results by Plan and Vershynin, the central contribution is an asymptotically precise characterization of the LASSO's mean-squared estimation error in the high-dimensional regime where the number of measurements and the signal dimension grow large proportionally. The main result elegantly demonstrates that the estimation performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to its performance on a corresponding linear model with a scaled signal and a specific level of additive Gaussian noise. The scaling and noise variance parameters are shown to depend only on the non-linear link function. The analysis is general, covering a wide class of convex regularizers (e.g., `l1`, group, and nuclear norms) and relaxing the common assumption of a Gaussian signal prior.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** This work provides a significant theoretical contribution to the literature on high-dimensional statistics. It moves beyond the non-asymptotic upper bounds of prior work and derives the first precise asymptotic error characterizations for the regularized LASSO in the single-index model setting. This provides an exact understanding of how the non-linearity, signal structure, and problem dimensions interact to determine estimator performance.
*   **Quality:** The technical analysis appears solid and the derived formulae are shown to be remarkably accurate via numerical simulations. The paper successfully extends a powerful analysis framework (based on the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem) to this new problem domain. The application of the results to the design of optimal quantizers is a compelling demonstration of the theory's practical relevance, leading to the interesting conclusion that the Lloyd-Max quantizer is optimal for minimizing the LASSO's estimation error.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and structured. The main results are stated clearly, and their implications are explored for several important special cases, including sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Contextualizing the Main Result:** The key insight that a single-index model can be related to an effective linear model is a classical one, dating back to Brillinger (1982). While the paper's rigorous derivation of this equivalence for the regularized LASSO in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime is novel and highly non-trivial, the main text could do more to provide this heuristic intuition upfront. Briefly mentioning this connection in the introduction would help frame the paper's contribution more as a precise, modern characterization of a known phenomenon, rather than the discovery of the phenomenon itself. This would improve the narrative flow and better manage reader expectations.

2.  **Discussion of the `l2`-LASSO Choice:** The paper focuses specifically on the `l2`-LASSO, also known as the Square-Root LASSO. A primary motivation for using this estimator over the more conventional `l2^2`-LASSO is that its "oracle" regularization parameter `λ` is independent of the (often unknown) noise variance. The results herein show that the non-linear measurement model is equivalent to a linear one with an effective noise variance `σ^2` that depends on the link function `g`, which may also be unknown. This seems to make the `l2`-LASSO an especially suitable choice for this problem. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this point. A brief comment on how the authors' findings further validate the choice of the `l2`-LASSO over the `l2^2`-LASSO in this setting would be a valuable addition.
