

This paper investigates linear estimation under a nonlinear link function, extending recent work by Plan and Vershynin. The key contribution is Theorem 2.1, which establishes that the asymptotic estimation error for generalized Lasso is identical whether the link function is linear or nonlinear, provided the measurement vector is drawn from a normal distribution. While this result is theoretically interesting and well-supported by the authors' analysis, the normality assumption for the measurement vector warrants further justification, as it may be restrictive in practical scenarios where such distributions are not guaranteed. The authors should clarify why this assumption is necessary for their conclusion to hold.

Two critical questions remain unaddressed. First, the paper discusses convex regularizers but does not explain how to select the regularizer based on the set $\Omega$ containing the true parameter $x_0$. This gap is significant, as the choice of regularizer directly impacts the feasibility of the estimation procedure. Second, while the result shows equivalence between linear and nonlinear link functions, the asymptotic error depends on $\mu$ and $\sigma$, which characterize the link function’s properties (e.g., its mean and variance). The authors should elaborate on the necessity of these dependencies—specifically, whether $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are intrinsic to the estimation hardness or merely artifacts of the analysis.

The paper also omits important related work. The single-index model literature (e.g., [1,2]) and sufficient dimension reduction studies address linear vector/subspace estimation under unknown link functions, yet this work is not cited or discussed. Additionally, Yi et al.’s recent paper "Optimal Linear Estimation Under Unknown Link Function" (ICML 2015) establishes a similar equivalence between linear and nonlinear estimation for sparse recovery under proper conditions, including cases where $\mu = 0$. Yi et al. provide rigorous theoretical proofs and empirical validation, making their approach highly relevant. The current paper should engage with this work to position its contributions more precisely.

The writing is generally clear, but minor grammatical issues reduce precision: "asymptotical" should be "asymptotic," "considers about" should be "considers," and "the conclusion involves with $\mu$ and $\sigma$" should be "the conclusion depends on $\mu$ and $\sigma$." Theorem 2.1’s statement should explicitly specify that the equivalence holds for arbitrary link functions with $\mu \neq 0$, as this is central to the result. 

Overall, the paper presents a novel insight into the equivalence of linear and nonlinear estimation under specific conditions. However, the normality assumption, the unaddressed regularizer selection problem, and the missing context regarding Yi et al. and single-index models weaken its impact. If these points are resolved—particularly by justifying the normality assumption, clarifying the role of $\mu$ and $\sigma$, and incorporating the cited references—the paper would make a stronger contribution. As written, it offers a valuable observation but requires deeper engagement with the existing literature and technical nuances to fully convince the reader. The results are promising, but the argument would be significantly strengthened with the suggested revisions.
