This paper investigates the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal from non-linear measurements using the Generalized LASSO estimator. The measurement model considered is `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`, where `x0` is the unknown signal, `A` is a measurement matrix with i.i.d. Gaussian entries, and `g` is a potentially unknown and random non-linear link function.

The core contribution is a precise, asymptotic characterization of the LASSO's estimation error in the high-dimensional limit where the number of measurements `m` and the signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio. The main result (Theorem 2.1) demonstrates a remarkable equivalence: the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically identical to its performance with an equivalent linear model `yi = μa_i^T x0 + σz_i`. The parameters `μ = E[γg(γ)]` and `σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2]` (where `γ` is a standard normal random variable) elegantly capture the complete effect of the non-linearity. This result substantially refines and generalizes the classical work of Brillinger and the more recent high-dimensional bounds of Plan and Vershynin by providing an exact asymptotic expression for the mean-squared error. The authors further provide a general formula for this error (Theorem 2.2) and specialize it to important cases such as sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery. Finally, they showcase the utility of their theory with an interesting application, demonstrating that the Lloyd-Max quantizer is optimal for minimizing the LASSO estimation error in quantized compressive sensing.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper's main result, establishing the precise asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear measurement model and a corresponding linear one, is both elegant and powerful. It provides a definitive answer to the question of how the standard LASSO behaves with non-linearities. Moving beyond the non-asymptotic bounds of prior work to exact characterizations is a significant theoretical advance.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The analysis appears to be technically sound, building upon a strong line of recent work that provides precise analyses for high-dimensional estimators. The derivation of a closed-form, computable expression for the asymptotic error (Theorem 2.2) is a key strength, as it makes the theoretical result highly practical for predicting performance. The specialization of this result to various structured signal models demonstrates the generality of the framework.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem setup, main results, and their implications are explained effectively. The illustrative figures convincingly support the theoretical predictions, showing a near-perfect match between the theory and simulations.

4.  **Impact:** The findings are likely to be influential. By reducing a complex non-linear problem to a much better-understood linear one, this work provides researchers and practitioners with a clear principle for analyzing and designing systems involving non-linear data acquisition. The application to optimal quantizer design is a compelling example of the theory's practical relevance.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Gaussian Measurement Assumption:** The analysis relies on the assumption that the entries of the measurement matrix `A` are i.i.d. Gaussian. While this is a common assumption in this line of research, it is also a significant limitation. It would strengthen the paper if the authors could comment on the necessity of this assumption for their proof technique and discuss the potential challenges or possibilities of extending their results to other matrix ensembles (e.g., sub-Gaussian).

2.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper should acknowledge and discuss the highly relevant work of Yi et al. (2015) in "Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform." This work also studies high-dimensional estimation with non-linearities and proposes methods that can handle cases where `μ=0`, a scenario explicitly excluded by this paper's assumptions. A comparison would provide valuable context and better position the paper's contribution within the current literature.

3.  **Discussion of Single-Index Models:** While the paper correctly identifies the problem as a single-index model in a footnote, this connection could be more prominently discussed in the main text. Briefly elaborating on how this work contributes to the extensive literature on single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction would help contextualize the results for a broader audience, particularly from the statistics community.

4.  **Intuition behind the `μ-σ` Equivalence:** The result that the entire effect of the function `g` is captured by just two parameters, `μ` and `σ`, is powerful but perhaps counter-intuitive. The paper would be more accessible if the authors could provide a short, intuitive explanation for why this simplification occurs in the high-dimensional Gaussian setting. This would complement the formal proof and deepen the reader's understanding.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant and interesting theoretical contribution. It rigorously establishes a fundamental equivalence between LASSO estimation with non-linear and linear measurements in the high-dimensional regime. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the scope of the discussion and positioning relative to other fields, and they can be addressed in a revised version. The results are novel, technically solid, and likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
