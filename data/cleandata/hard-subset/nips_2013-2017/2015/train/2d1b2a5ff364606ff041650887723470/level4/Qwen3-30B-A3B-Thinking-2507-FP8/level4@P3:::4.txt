

This paper investigates the asymptotic performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to non-linear measurement models of the form y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0), where g_i are independent copies of a link function g. The authors establish a striking equivalence between the non-linear and linear measurement settings: under Gaussian measurement matrices (a standard assumption in high-dimensional statistics), the estimation error of the Generalized LASSO for non-linear measurements is asymptotically identical to that of a Generalized LASSO applied to scaled and noisy linear measurements y_i = μa_i^T x_0 + σz_i, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2] with γ ~ N(0,1). This extends the earlier work of Plan and Vershynin (NIPS 2013) who established non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained versions of the Generalized LASSO, and generalizes Brillinger's classical results from the 1980s. The paper's key contribution is providing precise asymptotic characterizations of the estimation error rather than merely establishing bounds, which is a significant advancement over previous work.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through rigorous asymptotic analysis using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework. The authors derive explicit expressions for the estimation error that capture the precise roles of the link function g, the signal structure, the regularizer f, and the regularization parameter λ. The results are validated through extensive numerical experiments across multiple settings (sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery), showing excellent agreement between theoretical predictions and empirical observations. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is particularly compelling, where the authors show that the optimal quantization thresholds correspond to the Lloyd-Max quantizer, a well-known result in signal processing.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The mathematical presentation is precise, and the authors carefully explain the technical assumptions and their implications. The connection between non-linear and linear measurements is elegantly presented, and the special cases (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank recovery) are well-motivated and developed. The writing is professional and accessible to readers familiar with high-dimensional statistics.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first precise asymptotic characterization of the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements. While Plan and Vershynin (2013) established the equivalence in a more limited sense, this paper delivers the precise error formulas that have been missing from the literature. The result represents a substantial theoretical advancement over previous work in this area.

The significance of the work is substantial. The equivalence between non-linear and linear measurements provides a powerful theoretical foundation for understanding and analyzing non-linear measurement systems, which are ubiquitous in real-world applications (e.g., 1-bit quantization, sensor networks with nonlinearities). The paper's theoretical insights directly inform practical design choices, such as optimal quantizer design in q-bit compressive sensing.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses that could be addressed. First, while the Gaussian measurement matrix assumption is standard in this line of work, the authors should more explicitly discuss why this assumption is necessary for their precise characterization (rather than merely stating it as a condition). Second, the paper does not adequately address the case when μ = 0, which is important for certain link functions (e.g., g(x) = sign(x)). The recent paper by Yi et al. (NIPS 2015) addresses this case and provides alternative algorithms for sparse recovery when μ = 0; this should be acknowledged and compared with the current work. Third, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work in the single-index model literature, which has extensively studied similar problems under unknown link functions.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Provides the first precise asymptotic characterization of Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements
- Theoretical results are rigorously derived and well-validated
- Offers significant practical insights (e.g., optimal quantizer design)
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Extends previous work by Plan and Vershynin in a meaningful way

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Limited discussion of the μ = 0 case, which is important for certain applications
- Some missing references to related work in single-index models
- The Gaussian measurement assumption, while standard, is restrictive for some applications

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements. The results are well-motivated, technically sound, and have clear practical implications. The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature and would be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
