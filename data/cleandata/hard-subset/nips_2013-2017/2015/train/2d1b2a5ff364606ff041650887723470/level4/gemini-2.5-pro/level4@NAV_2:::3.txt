This paper analyzes the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimating a structured signal from non-linear measurements, assuming a Gaussian measurement matrix. The authors formalize and rigorously prove a powerful heuristic: that in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime, the non-linear measurement model is equivalent to a linear one with a scaled signal and additive Gaussian noise. Building on the foundational work of Brillinger and more recent results by Plan and Vershynin [17], this work moves beyond upper bounds to provide novel, precise, and explicit expressions for the mean-squared estimation error. The theoretical predictions are shown to be accurate across several important special cases, including LASSO for sparse recovery, Group LASSO for group-sparse signals, and nuclear-norm regularization for low-rank matrix recovery. A particularly insightful application of the theory is presented in the context of q-bit compressive sensing, where it is shown that the problem of designing an optimal quantizer is equivalent to minimizing an effective signal-to-noise ratio, thereby connecting it to the classical Lloyd-Max quantizer.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper provides a significant theoretical contribution by deriving asymptotically exact performance characterizations for the Generalized LASSO with non-linearities. This is a notable advance over prior work, which primarily offered non-asymptotic upper bounds. The explicit error formulas provide deep insight into the interplay between the non-linear link function, the signal structure, the regularizer, and the problem dimensions.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears sound and establishes an elegant equivalence between the non-linear problem and an effective linear, noisy counterpart. The claims are well-supported by this analysis, and the derived formulas are validated convincingly through numerical experiments.
*   **Practical Implications:** The application to optimal quantizer design is compelling. By simplifying the complex optimization problem to one of minimizing the `σ²/μ²` ratio, the authors provide a clear and actionable principle for system design, which is a valuable contribution for practitioners.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, the presentation is somewhat dense in places, which may be a consequence of page limitations. Expanding on the intuition behind some of the technical assumptions and results could further improve readability.
*   **Terminology and Notation:**
    *   On page 5, the function `ef,τ(v)` is referred to with a notation (`prox`) that is more commonly associated with the proximal operator itself, rather than its value. It would be clearer to use the standard term from convex analysis, the "Moreau envelope" of `f` with index `τ`.
    *   Throughout the text, the Generalized LASSO is sometimes referred to as an "algorithm." It would be more precise to call it a "convex program," "estimator," or "optimization problem," as it defines a solution rather than a procedure to compute it.
*   **Theoretical Presentation:** Assumption 1 is central to the main results. While functional, its presentation could be enhanced by providing a more formal interpretation, perhaps by connecting it to established concepts in variational or convex analysis. This could make the condition more intuitive for readers.
*   **Minor Points:**
    *   For completeness, the authors could briefly mention that the regularized LASSO in (9) and its penalized counterpart (with a squared-error loss term) are equivalent under an appropriate mapping of the regularization parameter.
    *   There is a minor typo in the caption of Figure 1, where 'n=768' should be typeset as '$n$=768'.
    *   There appears to be a typo on page 5, line 247, starting with "When...".
