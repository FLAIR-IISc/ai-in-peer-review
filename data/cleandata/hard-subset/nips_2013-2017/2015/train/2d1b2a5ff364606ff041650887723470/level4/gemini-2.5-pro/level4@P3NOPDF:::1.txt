I have reviewed the authors' response and the comments from the other reviewers. I thank the authors for their detailed clarifications. While the discussion was helpful, my overall assessment of the paper remains largely unchanged.

**Summary:**

This paper analyzes the performance of the generalized square-root lasso in a setting where a sparse signal is to be recovered from Gaussian measurements that have been transformed by a potentially unknown, nonlinear link function. This corresponds to a high-dimensional single-index model. The primary contribution is the derivation of precise asymptotic guarantees for the estimator, providing a sharp characterization of its performance as the problem dimensions grow.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The core strength of this work lies in its theoretical results. The authors provide a precise, asymptotic characterization of the estimator's performance, which is a significant step beyond typical consistency guarantees. This level of precision offers a more nuanced and valuable understanding of the method's behavior and its dependence on key problem parameters. The results are technically strong and appear to be novel.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem statement is clearly articulated, the motivation is compelling, and the main theoretical results are presented in an accessible manner in the main body. Despite the technical density of the topic, the authors have done an excellent job of making the paper's contributions understandable.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Typographical Errors:** The manuscript would benefit from a careful round of proofreading. I noticed a number of typos, which were particularly prevalent in the proofs section in the supplementary material. While these errors did not prevent me from following the core arguments, correcting them is essential to polish the paper for publication.

2.  **Comment on Novelty:** It is worth noting that my expertise is primarily in high-dimensional statistics and sparse recovery, and I am less familiar with the specific literature on single index models and sufficient dimension reduction. Consequently, while the results seem highly novel from my perspective, I cannot fully gauge their relationship to all potentially relevant prior work within that specific subfield. My positive evaluation is based on the technical quality of the analysis and its clear significance to the machine learning community interested in sparse methods.
