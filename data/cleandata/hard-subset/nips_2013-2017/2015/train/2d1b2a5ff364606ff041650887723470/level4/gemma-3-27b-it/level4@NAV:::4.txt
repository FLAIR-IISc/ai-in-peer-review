This paper investigates linear estimation in the presence of a nonlinear link function. Building upon the recent findings of Plan and Vershynin, the authors analyze the asymptotic estimation error achieved using a generalized lasso approach for arbitrary link functions when μ ≠ 0. Notably, the study demonstrates that the asymptotic error remains consistent, mirroring the result presented in Theorem 2.1, regardless of whether the link function is linear or nonlinear.

A potential limitation lies in Theorem 2.1’s reliance on a normally distributed linear measurement vector, which may represent a strong assumption in certain applications. Further clarification regarding the necessity of this assumption for the theorem’s validity would be beneficial. 

I have a few questions for the authors. First, how should the regularization parameter be selected based on the set κ containing the true parameter vector x₀? While the paper discusses the requirement for a convex regularizer, the issue of optimal regularization selection with respect to κ remains open. Second, although the result establishes equivalence between linear and nonlinear measurement scenarios, the resulting error characterization still depends on μ and σ, parameters that quantify the difficulty of estimation due to the nonlinear function g. I am curious if the authors have considered the reason for this dependence.

Finally, the manuscript would benefit from including references to related work. Specifically, the authors should acknowledge the existing literature on linear/subspace estimation under unknown link functions, as developed within the single index model and sufficient dimension reduction frameworks. Additionally, the recent work by Yi et al. ("Optimal linear estimation under unknown linear estimation") presents a novel algorithm for sparse recovery, even when the link function's μ is zero, and demonstrates the equivalence of high-dimensional linear estimation with both linear and nonlinear link functions under specific conditions. This paper provides compelling theoretical and empirical support for its conclusions and should be discussed.

Overall, this is a well-written paper presenting interesting and potentially significant results.
