Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under a single-index model y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0), where the design vectors a_i are Gaussian and g_i are i.i.d. draws of a (possibly random) nonlinear link. The authors analyze the generalized LASSO with an L2 residual (square-root LASSO) and a general convex regularizer f, and show two main results:

1) Nonlinear = linear equivalence (Theorem 2.1): For Gaussian designs, in the high-dimensional limit the squared error of the LASSO with nonlinear measurements matches that of the LASSO with an equivalent linear model having scaled signal µx0 and additive Gaussian noise with variance σ^2, where µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2] for γ ~ N(0,1). This formalizes in a precise asymptotic sense earlier insights by Brillinger (for LS in fixed-n), and by Plan & Vershynin (non-asymptotic upper bounds).

2) Precise asymptotic error (Theorem 2.2): Using a CGMT-style program, the authors derive an exact deterministic characterization of the asymptotic error ‖x̂ − µx0‖^2 as the solution of a three-scalar convex optimization whose ingredients separate the roles of δ = m/n, the regularizer parameter λ, the link g (through µ, σ^2), and the structure/regularizer (through a function F derived from the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate). They then specialize to:
- Sparse recovery with ℓ1 (Theorem 2.3), yielding explicit equations in terms of the soft-thresholding operator, recovering and extending prior precise analyses for the linear case.
- Group-sparse recovery with ℓ1,2 and a vector soft-thresholding characterization.
- Low-rank matrix recovery with nuclear norm (sketching how F can be computed from the spectral distribution).
- Quantized compressive sensing: leveraging the equivalence, they formulate optimal threshold/level design as minimizing σ^2/µ^2 and argue that Lloyd–Max iterations yield stationary points for this new objective.

This work builds directly on: Brillinger’s asymptotics for LS under link violation (fixed n), Plan & Vershynin’s non-asymptotic bounds for constrained LASSO with non-linear observations, and precise CGMT/AMP-based analyses for linear measurements (e.g., Bayati & Montanari; Stojnic; Oymak et al.; Thrampoulidis et al.). The paper extends those to precise asymptotics for generalized LASSO under nonlinear measurements and offers new closed-form characterizations for several structured settings.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A clean equivalence principle reducing nonlinear measurement analysis to a linear surrogate via two scalars (µ, σ^2). This is simple, compelling, and aligns with known phenomena while providing exact asymptotics rather than bounds.
- Technical depth and generality: The CGMT-based scalar optimization appears broadly applicable across convex regularizers; the sparse and group-sparse instantiations are thorough and practically interpretable.
- Practical angle: The quantized sensing application gives a concrete design rule (minimize σ^2/µ^2) and ties to Lloyd–Max optimization, offering actionable guidance for quantizer design.
- Clarity of the role of λ and δ: The characterization illuminates different regimes (e.g., the λcrit phenomenon when δ<1) and unifies disparate lines of prior work.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Gaussian design assumption: The results require i.i.d. Gaussian A. It is unclear how robust the equivalence and precise formula are to sub-Gaussian designs, correlated designs, or structured sensing matrices common in practice.
- Assumption 1 and function F: The general theorem hinges on existence of a limit F defined via the Moreau envelope of f*. While verified for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2, the low-rank case is only sketched and “left for future work,” weakening the completeness of claims for nuclear norm.
- µ ≠ 0 and identifiability: The analysis assumes µ, σ^2 nonzero and fixes ‖x0‖=1. This excludes even link functions and some important cases; the paper notes this but does not quantify behavior or propose alternatives when µ≈0 (beyond citing specialized methods for binary sensing).
- Clarity and precision: Theorem 2.1 is phrased as an equality of errors “with probability one” in the limit; presumably the statement concerns equality of limiting errors, not finite-sample equality—this should be clarified. There are typos and minor notation issues that slightly hinder readability.
- Empirical support: While figures are referenced, the text (as provided) offers limited experimental breadth across link functions, regularizers, and finite-sample regimes. More extensive validation (and finite-n accuracy) would strengthen the case.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The work appears technically sound and careful, leveraging modern precise-analysis tools. The main assumptions are standard for CGMT-style results. Some parts (low-rank F evaluation) are incomplete, and the strong statement in Theorem 2.1 needs precise wording.
- Clarity: Generally well-organized with a clear narrative, but could improve in exposition of Assumption 1, rigorous statement of Theorem 2.1, and proofreading. Including a self-contained guide to computing F for common regularizers would help.
- Originality: High. To my knowledge, this is the first precise asymptotic analysis of generalized LASSO under nonlinear measurements, and the nonlinear-to-linear equivalence with exact error predictions is new beyond prior upper bounds.
- Significance: High. The results consolidate and extend understanding of a broad class of inverse problems, offer practical guidance (e.g., quantizer design), and will likely influence both theory and practice in high-dimensional statistics and signal processing.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel equivalence and precise asymptotics for nonlinear measurements.
- Broad applicability across structures/regularizers with concrete specializations.
- Clear connection to and advancement over influential prior work (Brillinger; Plan & Vershynin; CGMT line).
- Practical design implications for quantized sensing.

Cons against acceptance
- Strong Gaussian design restriction; no robustness study.
- Incomplete low-rank development and reliance on a technical assumption that may be opaque to readers.
- Some clarity issues in theorem statements and typos; limited experimental breadth.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 2.1 as equality of limiting errors (and state the precise mode of convergence).
- Expand on Assumption 1: provide sufficient conditions and worked-out F for nuclear norm beyond a sketch; include a small appendix with recipes for common f.
- Discuss robustness to non-Gaussian/sub-Gaussian designs and present empirical evidence; comment on required modifications to the proof.
- Address the µ≈0 regime: either characterize failure modes or suggest estimators tailored to such links.
- Add practical guidance on estimating µ and σ^2 from data when g is unknown, and how to tune λ in practice under nonlinear measurements.
- Improve proofreading and notation consistency.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a clear, technically solid, and impactful theoretical advance with practical consequences. Addressing clarity and completeness issues (especially around Assumption 1 and Theorem 2.1’s phrasing) would further strengthen the submission.
