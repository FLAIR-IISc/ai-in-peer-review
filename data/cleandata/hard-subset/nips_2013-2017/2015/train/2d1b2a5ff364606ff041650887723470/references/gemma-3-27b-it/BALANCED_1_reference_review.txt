This paper investigates the performance of the LASSO algorithm when applied to a single-index model with non-linear measurements. The core result demonstrates a surprising equivalence: in high dimensions, the LASSO effectively treats non-linear measurements as scaled and noisy linear measurements, characterized by a proportionality constant µ and a noise variance σ². The authors provide precise asymptotic error formulas, generalizing previous work on linear measurements and extending Brillinger’s early results. They also explore applications to q-bit compressive sensing, proposing an algorithm for optimizing quantization levels.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The equivalence result between non-linear and scaled linear measurements is a significant and unexpected finding. It provides a valuable theoretical understanding of the LASSO’s behavior in a more general setting. The extension of existing LASSO analysis techniques to non-linear measurements is a substantial contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper is rigorously developed with clear mathematical derivations. The proofs seem well-structured and build upon established techniques in high-dimensional statistics and random matrix theory. The careful handling of asymptotic regimes and the use of the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) are commendable.
* **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and summarizes the contributions. The notation is consistent, and the flow of ideas is logical. The sections on sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, and q-bit compressive sensing are well-integrated.
* **Completeness:** The authors provide a thorough literature review, positioning their work within the broader context of single-index models, compressed sensing, and LASSO analysis.  They address relevant prior work and clearly highlight the novelty of their approach.
* **Practical Implications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical findings and suggests a potential algorithm for optimizing quantization parameters.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** Assumption 1, regarding the convergence of a function involving the Fenchel conjugate, feels somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from further justification or discussion of its generality.  It's not immediately clear how easily this assumption will hold for all practical regularizers.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the paper proposes an algorithm for optimal quantization in the q-bit case, the computational cost of evaluating F and solving the optimization problem (even with the Lloyd-Max algorithm) is not discussed. Practical considerations for implementation would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While Figures 1 and 2 provide some validation, the empirical results are relatively limited. A more comprehensive set of simulations, exploring different non-linear functions *g* and varying signal dimensions, would strengthen the claims.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper primarily focuses on Gaussian measurement matrices.  A discussion of the robustness of the results to deviations from Gaussianity or the impact of different measurement matrix structures would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and significant theoretical result with potential implications for a range of applications.
* The technical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated.
* The paper builds upon and extends a substantial body of existing research in a coherent and insightful manner.
* The clarity of presentation makes the complex material accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Assumption 1 requires further justification.
* The lack of extensive empirical validation leaves some questions about the practical impact of the results unanswered.
* The computational aspects of the proposed algorithm in the q-bit case are not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of the LASSO algorithm in a non-linear measurement setting. The equivalence result is particularly striking and opens up new avenues for research. While the paper has some minor weaknesses, the strengths far outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Provide a more detailed justification for Assumption 1 and/or discuss its limitations.
2.  Expand the empirical validation with more comprehensive simulations and a broader range of non-linear functions *g*.
3.  Briefly discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm in the q-bit case and potentially suggest strategies for mitigating any computational bottlenecks.
4.  Include a brief discussion of the robustness of the results to deviations from Gaussianity of the measurement matrix.
