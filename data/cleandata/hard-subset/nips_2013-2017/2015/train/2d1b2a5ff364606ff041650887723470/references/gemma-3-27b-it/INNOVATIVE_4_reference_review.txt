This paper presents a surprising and impactful result: the Generalized LASSO applied to non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to one applied to scaled and noisy linear measurements. This is a strong claim with potentially broad implications, as it justifies the continued use of LASSO-type methods in scenarios where the measurement process is non-linear, a common situation in many applications. The core idea, building on prior work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, is elegantly demonstrated through precise asymptotic analysis, providing a clear characterization of the scaling factor and noise variance that govern the equivalence.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The main result is genuinely novel. Establishing this equivalence – that non-linear measurements can be effectively treated as scaled linear measurements by the LASSO – is a significant theoretical contribution. It moves beyond simply *using* LASSO with non-linear data and provides a justification for *why* it might work well.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is mathematically sound, with careful derivations and clear assumptions. The derivation of the scaling factor µ and noise variance σ2 is particularly well done.
* **Generality:** The result applies to a broad class of link functions *g* and regularizers *f*, making it widely applicable. The analysis doesn’t restrict itself to specific types of non-linearities or sparsity structures.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors effectively place their work in the context of prior research, particularly the work of Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, and highlight how their results extend and generalize these earlier findings.
* **Practical Implications:** The analysis provides a clear pathway for adapting existing LASSO analysis tools to non-linear measurement settings. The discussion of q-bit compressive sensing and the Lloyd-Max algorithm application is a concrete example of this.
* **Clear Presentation:** Despite the mathematical complexity, the paper is relatively well-written and organized. The authors make an effort to clarify the key assumptions and notations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Asymptotic Nature:** The results are asymptotic, which limits their immediate applicability to finite-dimensional problems. While this is typical in high-dimensional statistics, it’s a limitation to acknowledge.
* **Unit Norm Assumption:** The assumption that x0 has unit Euclidean norm, while simplifying the presentation, feels somewhat restrictive. The authors acknowledge this and mention how to generalize, but it would be beneficial to briefly outline the implications for the core results without this assumption.
* **Complexity of F(c1, c2, c3):** While the authors acknowledge the computational challenges associated with evaluating F(c1, c2, c3), a more detailed discussion of practical approaches or approximations would be helpful.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical analysis. While Figure 1 illustrates the results, more extensive numerical experiments, especially with different link functions *g*, would strengthen the validation of the claims. The simulation settings are a bit limited.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically strong result with potentially broad implications.
* The analysis is rigorous and builds upon a solid foundation of existing work.
* The paper demonstrates a deep understanding of the theoretical underpinnings of LASSO and its applicability to non-linear measurement settings.
* The results open up new avenues for research in areas such as compressed sensing with quantized measurements.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The asymptotic nature of the results might limit their immediate practical impact.
* The limited empirical validation could raise concerns about the robustness of the theoretical findings.
* The assumption of unit norm signal and the evaluation of function *F* need some clarifications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a highly promising paper that presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. While the asymptotic nature of the results and the limited empirical validation are minor weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and potential impact of the work strongly outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program and recommend **acceptance**.  The paper prioritizes novelty and a bold theoretical direction over exhaustive empirical evaluation, which aligns well with NIPS's emphasis on cutting-edge research. I encourage the authors to address the minor weaknesses in a revised version of the paper, particularly by providing a more detailed discussion of practical approaches for evaluating F(c1, c2, c3) and including more comprehensive numerical experiments.
