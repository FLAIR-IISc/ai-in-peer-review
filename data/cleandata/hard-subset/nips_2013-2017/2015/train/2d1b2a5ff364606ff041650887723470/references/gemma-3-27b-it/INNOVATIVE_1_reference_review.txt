This paper presents a surprising and significant result: the Generalized LASSO, when applied to measurements corrupted by a non-linear link function, behaves asymptotically as if the measurements were scaled and noisy linear measurements. This is a powerful statement, effectively demonstrating a robustness of the LASSO to non-linearity that hasn't been previously understood. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, building on recent advances in high-dimensional statistics and leveraging connections to Brillinger’s earlier work. The authors expertly connect their findings to existing literature, providing a comprehensive overview of related work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core result is genuinely surprising and offers a new perspective on the applicability of LASSO to non-linear measurement models. This isn’t incremental work; it’s a fundamental re-evaluation of the assumptions underlying the method's success.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a precise asymptotic analysis, deriving explicit error bounds and characterizing the role of the link function via the parameters µ and σ².
*   **Generality:** The result holds for a wide class of link functions *g* and regularizers *f*, significantly broadening its potential impact.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors do an excellent job of situating their contribution within the broader context of single-index models, compressive sensing, and statistical learning.
*   **Practical Implications:** The work suggests a pathway for analyzing non-linear systems using well-established linear LASSO tools, and the application to q-bit compressive sensing is a compelling example. The Lloyd-Max algorithm application is a nice touch.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Asymptotic Setting:** The results are asymptotic, which limits their direct applicability to finite-sample scenarios. While this is common in theoretical analysis, it would be helpful to briefly discuss the practical implications of this limitation.
*   **Assumption 1:** Assumption 1, concerning the limiting behavior of the Fenchel conjugate, feels somewhat opaque. While the authors indicate how it holds for sparse recovery, a more general discussion of its verification would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the theoretical results are elegant, translating them into practical algorithms for optimizing the error expression (Theorem 2.2) may be challenging.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While Figure 1 provides some illustration, more comprehensive numerical experiments demonstrating the accuracy of the predictions across different link functions and signal structures would strengthen the paper. The q-bit sensing example is good, but could be expanded.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a truly novel and significant theoretical result with potentially broad implications.
*   The analysis is rigorous and well-motivated.
*   The connection to existing work is thorough and insightful.
*   The potential for practical application is promising.
*   The paper’s contribution is of high quality and meets the standards of NIPS.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The asymptotic nature of the results limits their immediate practical impact.
*   Assumption 1 could be better justified and explained.
*   A more comprehensive empirical validation would be desirable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a valuable and surprising result. While the asymptotic setting and some technical details (like Assumption 1) could be further clarified, the novelty and theoretical depth of the work outweigh these minor concerns. The authors have made a significant contribution to our understanding of the LASSO and its robustness to non-linearities. I lean strongly towards acceptance, despite the lack of extensive empirical validation; the theoretical contribution is strong enough to warrant publication.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with encouragement to expand the empirical validation in a future version).
