### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO estimator for recovering a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. This measurement setup, known as a single-index model, includes important special cases like 1-bit compressed sensing and censored regression. The authors analyze the standard LASSO algorithm, which is designed for linear models, when it is applied directly to this non-linear setting, potentially without knowledge of the link function `g`.

The central contribution is a precise, asymptotic characterization of the estimator's mean-squared error (MSE). The main result (Theorem 2.1) establishes that in the high-dimensional limit (where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio `m/n`), the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically identical to its performance on an equivalent linear model. This equivalent model takes the form `y_lin = µ * A * x0 + σ * z`, where `z` is a vector of i.i.d. standard Gaussian noise. The scaling factor `µ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the statistics of the link function `g`, defined as `µ = E[γg(γ)]` and `σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - µγ)^2]` for `γ ~ N(0, 1)`.

This equivalence principle allows the authors to leverage the recently developed Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework to derive an explicit, deterministic formula for the asymptotic MSE (Theorem 2.2). This formula precisely captures the interplay between the signal structure, the regularizer, the number of measurements, and the properties of the non-linear link function (via `µ` and `σ^2`). The general result is then specialized to provide concrete error predictions for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems. Finally, the paper demonstrates the utility of this theory by applying it to the design of optimal quantizers for q-bit compressive sensing.

This work significantly generalizes the classical result of Brillinger [5], which established a similar scaling phenomenon for unregularized least-squares in a fixed-dimension, large-sample setting. It also builds upon the recent work of Plan and Vershynin [17], which provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO variant, by delivering asymptotically exact characterizations for the more common regularized LASSO, thereby confirming the fundamental role of the parameters `µ` and `σ^2`.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper appears to be technically sound. The claims are supported by a rigorous theoretical framework based on the CGMT, which is a state-of-the-art tool for this type of analysis. The modeling assumptions (i.i.d. Gaussian matrix `A`, specific asymptotic regime) are standard in the high-dimensional statistics literature that this work belongs to. The authors are careful to state their assumptions clearly. The provided experimental results in Figures 1 and 2 show a remarkably accurate match between the theoretical predictions and simulation outcomes for various settings, which strongly supports the validity of the asymptotic analysis even for moderate dimensions.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, a clear summary of contributions, and a thorough review of the relevant literature, effectively situating the work. The progression from the main equivalence theorem to the general error formula and its specific applications is logical and easy to follow. The notation is consistent, and the key concepts are explained well.

**Originality:**
The main contribution—the precise asymptotic equivalence between the LASSO under non-linear measurements and an equivalent linear model—is highly original and constitutes a significant conceptual advance. While previous work [17] had hinted at the importance of the constants `µ` and `σ`, this paper is the first to prove this exact equivalence and provide a general and precise characterization of the resulting error. Furthermore, some of the specialized error formulae (e.g., Theorem 2.3 for sparse recovery with general non-zero distributions) appear to be novel contributions in their own right, even for the linear measurement case.

**Significance:**
The significance of this work is high. The established equivalence principle is both elegant and powerful. It unifies the analysis of a wide range of non-linear measurement problems under a single theoretical umbrella, mapping them to the better-understood case of linear measurements with Gaussian noise. This result allows a vast body of recent, highly-precise analytical tools and results for the LASSO to be immediately extended to problems like quantized compressed sensing, probit/logit regression, and censored data models. The application to quantizer design is a compelling demonstration of the practical utility of the theory, transforming a complex optimization problem over estimators into a much simpler one involving only the parameters of the link function. This work is likely to be influential and built upon by researchers in high-dimensional statistics, signal processing, and machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Reliance on Gaussianity:** The analysis is heavily dependent on the assumption that the measurement matrix `A` has i.i.d. Gaussian entries. While this is a common starting point for such precise analyses, the practical applicability of the results would be broader if the theory could be extended to sub-Gaussian or other more general matrix ensembles. A discussion on the potential challenges or possibilities for such extensions would be valuable.

2.  **Scope of Assumption 1:** The main theorems rely on "Assumption 1," which concerns the convergence of a Moreau envelope expression. The paper demonstrates that this assumption holds for several important regularizers (`l1`, `l1,2`, nuclear norm). However, a more general characterization of the class of regularizers and signal priors for which this assumption is valid would strengthen the paper's theoretical foundations.

3.  **Proof Intuition:** The proofs are deferred to the appendix. While this is standard practice, the main text would benefit from a short, high-level proof sketch or intuitive argument explaining *why* the non-linear function `g`'s impact asymptotically collapses to only its first two moments with a Gaussian input. This would provide the reader with deeper insight into the core mechanism behind the main result.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel, fundamental, and elegant equivalence principle connecting non-linear measurement models to linear ones.
*   Provides the first asymptotically exact performance characterization for the Generalized LASSO in this broad setting, a significant advance over prior work.
*   The results are general and applicable to a wide variety of structured signal recovery problems.
*   The paper is technically strong, well-written, and the theory is convincingly validated by numerical simulations.
*   The work has high potential for impact across multiple fields.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis relies on the restrictive assumption of an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix.
*   The scope of a key technical assumption (Assumption 1) could be more clearly delineated.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses, which are largely limitations common to the current state of the art in this research area. The paper makes a fundamental and significant contribution to the understanding of high-dimensional estimation.
