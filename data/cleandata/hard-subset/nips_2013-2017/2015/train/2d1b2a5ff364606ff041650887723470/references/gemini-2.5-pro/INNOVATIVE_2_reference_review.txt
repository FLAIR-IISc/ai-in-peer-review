### Summary

This paper presents a remarkable theoretical result concerning the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm for signal recovery from non-linear measurements. The measurement model considered is the classical single-index model, `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`, where `gi` are i.i.d. non-linear link functions. The authors' central and striking contribution is to show that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit, the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimate in this non-linear setting is *exactly equivalent* to the error of the same algorithm applied to an appropriately defined *linear* model: `y_lin = µ * A * x0 + σ * z`. The scaling factor `µ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the statistical properties of the non-linear link function `g`.

This result elegantly generalizes a classical finding by Brillinger for un-regularized least squares in the fixed-dimension setting. More recently, it provides a precise characterization that sharpens the non-asymptotic upper bounds of Plan and Vershynin. By establishing this equivalence, the paper effectively allows the entire modern toolkit of precise asymptotic analysis for the LASSO (developed for linear models via frameworks like CGMT and AMP) to be directly ported to this non-linear domain. The authors demonstrate the power of this result by deriving explicit error formulas for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems, and by showing how it simplifies a practical design problem in q-bit quantized compressive sensing.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The main result is a profound conceptual breakthrough. The idea that the complex, varied, and potentially unknown non-linearities in the measurement process simply collapse into an effective scaling and an effective Gaussian noise level in the high-dimensional limit is both powerful and beautiful. This is a bold and significant claim that provides a new and surprisingly simple way to understand a complex problem. This kind of unifying insight is precisely what drives the field forward.

2.  **High Significance and Impact:** This work forges a strong, formal bridge between the classical statistics of single-index models and the modern, high-dimensional analysis of structured signal recovery. By demonstrating this equivalence, the paper makes a vast body of recent, highly technical results on linear LASSO immediately applicable to a much broader class of non-linear problems. Researchers and practitioners working with quantized, censored, or other non-linear data can now leverage these precise performance characterizations. The potential for follow-up work building on this core insight is immense.

3.  **Strong and General Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides precise, asymptotically exact formulas for the estimation error, moving far beyond the loose upper bounds that previously existed in the literature for this problem. The analysis is general, covering a wide range of convex regularizers (l1, group-l1, nuclear norm) and signal structures. The provided simulation results show an excellent match with the theoretical predictions, confirming the accuracy of the asymptotic theory even in finite dimensions.

4.  **Clear Practical Relevance:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing in Section 2.3 is an excellent demonstration of the theory's utility. The authors show that their result transforms the difficult problem of optimizing quantizer thresholds and levels to minimize recovery error into a much simpler, tractable problem of minimizing the `σ^2/µ^2` ratio. This provides a concrete example of how the theoretical insight can directly guide practical system design.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on Asymptotic Analysis:** The results are derived in an asymptotic regime where the problem dimensions (`m, n`) go to infinity. While standard for this line of work and validated by compelling simulations, this does not provide formal guarantees for finite-dimensional problems.

2.  **Gaussian Measurement Matrix Assumption:** The analysis relies on the assumption that the entries of the measurement matrix `A` are i.i.d. Gaussian. This is a critical requirement for the CGMT proof technique used. While a common assumption in the field, it limits the direct applicability of the theorem to practical scenarios where matrices may have different structures (e.g., Fourier, Hadamard).

These weaknesses are, in my view, minor in the context of the paper's contribution. They represent standard limitations of the powerful analytical framework being employed, rather than fundamental flaws in the work itself. The conceptual leap made by the authors far outweighs the constraints of the setting in which it is formally proven.

### Recommendation

This is an outstanding theory paper that presents a novel, elegant, and highly significant result. The central claim—that LASSO with non-linear measurements behaves identically to LASSO with an equivalent linear model—is a major conceptual advance. It provides a beautiful simplification of a complex problem and makes a whole suite of modern analytical tools available for a new class of problems. While the analysis rests on standard assumptions (asymptotic regime, Gaussian matrices), the sheer novelty and power of the core idea make this a landmark contribution. This work is likely to be highly influential and to stimulate a great deal of future research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
