

# Peer Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a precise equivalence between the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements and the same algorithm with appropriately scaled linear measurements. The authors consider measurements of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), where gi are independent copies of a non-linear link function g. They prove that when the measurement matrix has i.i.d. Gaussian entries and the signal dimension n grows large, the asymptotic estimation error of the Generalized LASSO for non-linear measurements is equivalent to that of the same algorithm applied to linear measurements with scaled noise: yi = μ(ai^T x0) + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ)-μγ)²] for γ ~ N(0,1).

The paper builds on the earlier work of Plan and Vershynin (2015) who considered a constrained version of the Generalized LASSO for non-linear measurements and derived loose upper bounds. The authors extend this to the regularized LASSO (which is more commonly used in practice) and provide precise error expressions rather than loose bounds. Their main theoretical contribution is Theorem 2.1, which shows the equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement regimes, and Theorem 2.2, which provides a precise error formula involving a convex optimization program.

The paper then applies these results to several important special cases: sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3), group-sparse recovery, and low-rank matrix recovery. As a practical application, they analyze q-bit compressive sensing and show how the precise error prediction can guide the optimal design of quantization thresholds and levels.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by establishing the precise asymptotic equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement regimes for the LASSO. This is a significant extension of the recent work by Plan and Vershynin (2015), which only provided loose upper bounds. The authors provide precise error formulas that are computationally tractable (involving only scalar optimization variables).

2. **Technical Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs that follow the CGMT framework. The assumptions are well-motivated and the results are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The authors carefully address the challenges of extending from the linear to non-linear case, including the lack of closed-form solutions for the LASSO.

3. **Novelty and Originality**: The paper makes a clear distinction from previous work. While Brillinger (1983) and Plan & Vershynin (2015) showed that non-linear measurements can be treated as linear measurements with scaling factor μ, this paper provides the first precise characterization of the error for the regularized LASSO (not just constrained versions) and for general convex regularizers. The authors also derive novel error formulas for specific structured signal recovery problems.

4. **Significance**: The result has important implications for practitioners who use LASSO-type algorithms in settings with non-linear measurements. The precise error formula allows for better parameter tuning and performance prediction. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is particularly relevant for practical implementations.

5. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The main results are presented in a logical sequence, with the general result first, followed by specialized cases. The notation is consistent and well-defined.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: While the paper provides theoretical results and some numerical illustrations (Figures 1-2), there is insufficient experimental validation of the theoretical predictions. The authors claim "the error with non-linear measurements matches well with the error of the corresponding linear ones," but they don't provide detailed numerical comparisons or statistical significance tests. A more thorough experimental section would strengthen the paper.

2. **Assumption Limitations**: The paper assumes that the signal x0 has unit Euclidean norm (as noted in Footnote 4). While the authors acknowledge that this can be generalized, it's a restrictive assumption for some applications. For example, in sparse recovery with binary measurements, the assumption that µ ≠ 0 (which is required for the scaling) may not hold, and the paper notes that "for a few special cases... different methodologies than the LASSO have been recently proposed."

3. **Computational Complexity of F**: The error formula in Theorem 2.2 depends on the function F, which is defined through the Moreau envelope of the regularizer. While the paper computes F for specific cases (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank), for general regularizers, computing F may be challenging. The paper doesn't provide guidance on how to efficiently compute F for arbitrary regularizers.

4. **Comparison to Alternative Approaches**: The paper focuses on the LASSO approach but doesn't sufficiently compare with alternative methods that have been proposed for non-linear measurements (e.g., the q-bit compressive sensing work by Plan & Vershynin, or the recent work by Yi et al. on optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transforms). This weakens the argument for the superiority of the LASSO approach in non-linear settings.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of the LASSO with non-linear measurements. The main result (Theorem 2.1) is a substantial extension of previous work by Plan and Vershynin, providing precise error predictions rather than loose upper bounds. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in high-dimensional statistics.

While the paper has some weaknesses in experimental validation and assumption limitations, these do not undermine the core theoretical contribution. The significance of the work is high, as it provides a principled way to analyze and apply the LASSO to non-linear measurement models, which are common in practice.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of the LASSO in non-linear measurement settings. The results are novel, technically sound, and have important implications for both theory and practice. While some additional experimental validation would strengthen the paper, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Provides the first precise characterization of the Generalized LASSO error for non-linear measurements
- Extends previous work (Plan & Vershynin) from loose bounds to exact error prediction
- Derives novel error formulas for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery
- Has clear practical implications for q-bit compressive sensing and other non-linear measurement problems
- Well-written and well-structured with rigorous proofs

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited experimental validation beyond theoretical claims
- Restricted to the case where x0 has unit norm (though this is acknowledged)
- Computational complexity of evaluating F for general regularizers is not addressed
- Doesn't sufficiently compare with alternative approaches for non-linear measurements

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a more detailed experimental section with numerical comparisons between the predicted error and empirical error for various non-linear link functions and signal structures.

2. Discuss the implications of the unit norm assumption more thoroughly, including potential extensions for cases where µ = 0.

3. Provide a brief discussion of how to efficiently compute F for general regularizers, or at least note the computational challenges.

4. Expand the comparison with alternative methods for non-linear measurements, particularly those that have been proposed for specific non-linearities (e.g., binary measurements).

The paper is strong enough to be accepted as is, but these minor improvements would make it even more compelling.
