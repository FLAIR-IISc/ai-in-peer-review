Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under the single-index model yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with i.i.d. Gaussian measurement vectors and structured x0 estimated via the generalized LASSO. The main result (Theorem 2.1) shows that, asymptotically, the generalized LASSO applied to non-linear measurements behaves exactly as if the measurements were linear with an effective gain µ = E[γ g(γ)] and noise variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). Theorem 2.2 provides an exact asymptotic MSE characterization via a three-scalar convex optimization involving δ = m/n, λ, (µ,σ^2), and a function F capturing the regularizer and signal structure. Specializations are given for sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) settings; for sparse signals, Theorem 2.3 yields explicit equations that coincide with known linear-measurement results when g is linear and extend them otherwise. An application to q-bit compressive sensing shows that optimal quantizer design reduces to minimizing σ^2/µ^2, with Lloyd–Max iterations yielding stationary points.

Relation to prior work: The equivalence to scaled linear regression extends Brillinger’s classical consistency (fixed n, m→∞) to high dimensions and to regularized LASSO. It sharpens the non-asymptotic upper bounds of Plan–Vershynin by delivering precise asymptotic formulas and by covering regularized (not only constrained) LASSO. Compared to precise LASSO analyses for linear measurements (AMP-based and CGMT-based), the paper extends precision results to non-linear output channels and to a broader class of convex regularizers.

Strengths
- Clear and impactful conceptual reduction: a wide class of non-linear measurement models is shown to be equivalent, for LASSO, to a linear model with simple effective parameters (µ,σ^2).
- Precise asymptotics: Theorem 2.2 gives a computable scalar optimization characterizing the MSE for general convex regularizers; Theorem 2.3 yields explicit predictive equations for ℓ1.
- Broad applicability: treats sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank regularization, and suggests a principled approach to quantizer design.
- Advances over prior bounds: turns Plan–Vershynin’s scaling insights into exact limits, and generalizes beyond ℓ1 to other convex penalties.
- Useful practical takeaway: performance depends on σ^2/µ^2, enabling design/tuning (e.g., quantization thresholds).

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions: i.i.d. Gaussian A and independence of the gi’s are restrictive; no universality claims. The equivalence may fail for sub-Gaussian or structured matrices; empirical evidence would help.
- µ = 0 case is excluded (e.g., symmetric 1-bit without dithering). This is a common and important regime; the paper should discuss workarounds or identify which parts break and why.
- Theorem 2.1 wording suggests pathwise equality “with probability one” rather than equality of limiting MSEs; this needs careful restatement to avoid confusion.
- Assumption 1 (existence of the limit F) is technical and, while plausible for common penalties, is left to the reader in some cases (low-rank). More concrete conditions/examples would improve accessibility.
- Limited empirical validation in the draft (Figures referenced but not shown); finite-sample behavior and robustness to model mismatch are not extensively explored.
- Missing related work: the generalized AMP (GAMP) literature provides precise state-evolution predictions for generalized linear models with separable output channels under i.i.d. Gaussian A, including MSE of MAP/penalized estimators. A comparison/discussion would situate this paper better and nuance the “first precise” claim for non-linear observations.
- Practical aspects: estimating µ and σ from data when g is unknown, and guidance for tuning λ in practice, are not discussed.

Quality
The technical contribution appears solid and nontrivial, likely based on CGMT. The derivations specialized to ℓ1 (Theorem 2.3) are consistent with earlier precise analyses in the linear case, increasing confidence. However, some proofs are deferred and parts (low-rank F, universality) are left undeveloped. Empirical validation could be strengthened.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, but there are typos and stylistic issues (e.g., “mesaurements,” “eventhough”), inconsistent referencing, and footnotes embedded awkwardly. Theorem 2.1 needs a precise statement of what converges to what. Assumptions and normalizations (e.g., of f in (b)) would benefit from clearer explanation.

Originality
High. While the equivalence in spirit traces to Brillinger and the role of (µ,σ^2) appears in Plan–Vershynin, delivering precise asymptotics for regularized LASSO under non-linear measurements and general convex penalties is novel. The quantizer-design angle is a nice addition.

Significance
High. The results offer a unifying lens on non-linear measurements for convex estimators and enable immediate transfer of precise linear-LASSO results. This can influence theory and practice in quantization, saturation, and 1-bit sensing.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, general, and elegant theoretical contribution with clear practical implications.
- Extends precise-error methodology beyond linear outputs and beyond ℓ1.
- Bridges and sharpens prior results; likely to be influential.

Arguments against acceptance
- Reliance on Gaussian A and independence assumptions without universality discussion.
- Exclusion of µ = 0 and limited treatment of low-rank case details.
- Clarity issues and incomplete empirical validation; missing comparison to GAMP literature.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 2.1 to state convergence of errors (or equality of asymptotic limits) and specify the precise mode of convergence.
- Discuss the µ = 0 regime: when does LASSO fail, and can pre-processing or alternative losses help? Cite relevant works (e.g., 1-bit CS with dithering).
- Expand on Assumption 1: provide sufficient conditions for common penalties, and a worked-out F for nuclear norm under a standard spiked model with numerical evaluation.
- Add experiments: finite-n comparisons across several g (sign, ReLU, quantization), sensitivity to non-Gaussian A, and to mismatch; compare predicted vs observed errors and to GAMP predictions when applicable.
- Address universality: either add theory or empirical evidence for sub-Gaussian/partial Fourier A.
- Provide guidance for estimating (µ,σ^2) from data when g is unknown, and practical tuning of λ; discuss the λcrit phenomenon and its implications.
- Clean up writing and references; ensure figures are informative and reproducible; consider releasing code for evaluating (10).

Recommendation
Accept, contingent on clarifying statements and strengthening empirical and related-work sections. The conceptual reduction and precise analysis are significant and broadly useful.
