

# Peer Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper investigates the problem of estimating a structured signal x₀ ∈ Rⁿ from non-linear measurements yᵢ = gᵢ(aᵢᵀx₀), where gᵢ are independent copies of a link function g. The authors establish that the Generalized LASSO (used for linear measurements) can be precisely analyzed in the non-linear setting, showing that the estimation error is asymptotically equivalent to that of the LASSO applied to appropriately scaled and noisy linear measurements. Specifically, they prove that:

‖x̂ - μx₀‖₂² = ‖x̂lin - μx₀‖₂²

where x̂ is the solution to the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, x̂lin is the solution with linear measurements ylin = A(μx₀) + σz, and μ and σ² are defined as μ := E[γg(γ)], σ² := E[(g(γ) - μγ)²] for γ ~ N(0, 1).

This result extends earlier work by Brillinger (1980s) and Plan & Vershynin (2015), who established only upper bounds on the estimation error. The authors provide a precise characterization of the error in the asymptotic regime where m/n = δ ∈ (0, ∞) and n → ∞. They then apply their results to specific structured signal recovery problems: sparse recovery (ℓ₁-regularization), group-sparse recovery (ℓ₁,₂-regularization), and low-rank matrix recovery (nuclear-norm regularization). Finally, they demonstrate an application to q-bit compressive sensing, showing how to optimize quantization thresholds to minimize estimation error.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution. The authors provide rigorous proofs of their main results (deferred to the appendix), which are well-motivated and build upon established frameworks like the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT). The analysis carefully addresses the key challenge of extending previous results from LS (Plan & Vershynin) to the Generalized LASSO. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the paper carefully discusses the limitations of the results (e.g., the unit norm assumption for x₀, which is standard in high-dimensional asymptotics).

**Originality (High):** The core insight that non-linear measurements can be precisely treated as scaled and noisy linear measurements is novel. The paper extends the work of Plan & Vershynin (2015) from upper bounds to precise characterization, which is a significant theoretical advance. The precise error formula (Theorem 2.2) appears to be new, especially for regularizers beyond the ℓ₁-norm. The application to q-bit compressive sensing provides a practical method for optimizing quantization thresholds that hasn't been previously established.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The main results are clearly stated and the connections to previous work are well-explained. The authors use consistent notation throughout and provide sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with the single-index model or the CGMT framework. The paper's structure effectively guides the reader through the theoretical development.

**Significance (High):** The results have substantial implications for practitioners using LASSO-type methods with non-linear measurements. The precise error formula provides guidance for tuning the regularizer parameter λ, and the equivalence result allows practitioners to leverage the extensive body of work on the LASSO with linear measurements to analyze non-linear settings. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is particularly relevant to practical signal processing problems.

## Weaknesses

**Quality (Moderate):** While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper lacks experimental validation. The authors derive precise error formulas, but there are no numerical experiments to validate the predictions against actual LASSO performance. Given the practical nature of the problem, some empirical evidence would strengthen the paper significantly.

**Originality (Moderate):** The paper could better distinguish its contributions from the recent work by Thrampoulidis et al. (e.g., "The squared-error of generalized lasso" and "Regularized linear regression"). While the authors cite these works, they don't explicitly contrast their precise error characterization with these previous results, which is important for establishing novelty.

**Clarity (Moderate):** The paper is somewhat dense in places, particularly in the statement of Theorem 2.2 and the subsequent special cases. Some readers might find the optimization program in (10) challenging to parse. The authors could provide a more intuitive explanation of the role of the function F(c₁, c₂, c₃) that appears in the error formula.

**Significance (Moderate):** The paper assumes i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, which is standard but might limit applicability to certain practical scenarios. The authors could briefly discuss how their results might extend to other measurement ensembles (e.g., partial Fourier matrices).

## Recommendations

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
1. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending precise LASSO analysis to non-linear measurements.
2. The equivalence result is elegant and has clear practical implications for practitioners.
3. The precise error formula generalizes previous results and applies to a wide range of structured signal recovery problems.
4. The application to q-bit compressive sensing provides a useful new method for optimizing quantization thresholds.

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
1. Lack of experimental validation weakens the practical significance of the theoretical results.
2. The paper could better distinguish its contributions from recent work on LASSO with linear measurements.
3. The unit norm assumption for x₀ might be restrictive for some applications (though the authors note it's for simplicity).

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. The main result that LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to LASSO with linear measurements (after appropriate scaling) is elegant and has important implications for both theory and practice. The precise error characterization extends previous work from upper bounds to exact expressions, which is a significant advance.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a problem of practical importance. While the lack of experimental validation is a notable weakness, the theoretical contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in understanding the behavior of the LASSO in non-linear measurement settings.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Confidence: 4/5** (I am confident in the technical soundness of the paper, but would have liked to see some empirical validation to better assess the practical significance.)
