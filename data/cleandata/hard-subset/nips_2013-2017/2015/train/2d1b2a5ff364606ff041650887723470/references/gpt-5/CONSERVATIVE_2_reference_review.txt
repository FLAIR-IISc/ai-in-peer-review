Summary
The paper studies LASSO under single-index, non-linear measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0), with A Gaussian. The main claim (Theorem 2.1) is that, in high dimensions, the squared error of the generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements asymptotically matches that of the same LASSO applied to an effective linear model y_lin = µ A x0 + σ z, with µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µ γ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Building on CGMT-style tools, Theorem 2.2 gives a precise scalar optimization predicting the asymptotic error for general convex regularizers, and Theorem 2.3 specializes to `1-regularization with explicit fixed-point equations. The paper also sketches extensions to group-sparse and low-rank settings, and an application to quantized compressed sensing: optimizing quantizers reduces to minimizing σ^2/µ^2, with a Lloyd–Max-based algorithm yielding stationary points.

Relation to prior work: Brillinger established consistency of least squares with single-index models (fixed n, m→∞). Plan–Vershynin provided non-asymptotic bounds for constrained LASSO under unknown nonlinearity and Gaussian A, identifying the same µ, σ. A substantial body of precise asymptotics exists for LASSO with linear measurements via AMP/SE and CGMT. This paper attempts to bridge these by providing asymptotically precise, general-regularizer results for non-linear measurements under Gaussian designs.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling high-level insight: for Gaussian designs, LASSO “sees” non-linear single-index measurements as scaled noisy linear observations with parameters (µ, σ^2). This unifies Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin with modern precise LASSO analyses.
- The precise scalar optimization (Theorem 2.2) is a valuable addition; the specialization for sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3) matches and generalizes AMP-based results for linear models.
- Coverage of multiple structures (sparse, block-sparse, low-rank) via a single framework is attractive.
- The quantized-CS application translates a complex design problem into a simple criterion (minimize σ^2/µ^2), with a practical algorithmic suggestion.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Heavy and restrictive assumptions: A must be i.i.d. Gaussian, g_i i.i.d., µ ≠ 0, σ^2 bounded. No robustness claims to sub-Gaussian/rotationally invariant designs or correlated/structured A. In practice, sensing matrices are often non-Gaussian.
- Assumption 1 (existence of a limiting F for the normalized Moreau envelope of f* along Gaussian directions and x0) is opaque and nonstandard. While verified for a few cases, general sufficient conditions are not provided; the core theorems hinge on it.
- Practical identifiability and usage: when g is unknown, µ and σ are unknown; scaling ambiguity is acknowledged, but the paper does not provide estimators for µ, σ or guidance on tuning λ in that setting. Claims of practical utility would require this.
- Validation is weak. The text mentions figures but provides no thorough empirical study. There is no finite-sample evaluation across diverse g, f, δ, λ, nor comparisons to estimators that exploit a known link (e.g., GLMs, one-bit CS methods). The quantizer design claim is not validated against competing designs or with finite-sample simulations.
- Clarity issues: Theorem 2.1 states “in the limit of n→∞, with probability one, ‖x̂−µx0‖^2 = ‖x̂_lin−µx0‖^2.” This reads as equality, not equality of limits; please clarify that the limits coincide. The optimization in (10) is dense; more intuition and derivation sketches would help. Assumption 1 should be isolated, motivated, and accompanied by concrete verifiable conditions.
- Literature coverage and references: Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues), and some directly relevant works on GLMs/single-index estimation and one-bit compressed sensing could be more comprehensively discussed. The connection and differences to AMP/state evolution for generalized linear models should be clarified.
- Scope of novelty: The qualitative reduction to a linear effective model with (µ, σ) is known (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin). The novelty lies in precise asymptotics under Gaussian A and general convex f. This is incremental relative to existing CGMT/AMP results for linear models; the non-linear extension is meaningful but confined to the Gaussian design and asymptotic regime.

Quality
- The theoretical direction is sound and aligns with established methods (CGMT). However, the work feels incomplete without comprehensive experiments, robustness analysis, and clearer conditions for Assumption 1. Some claims (“wide class of functions f and g”) are stronger than the stated assumptions justify.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, but notation heavy. Assumption 1 and Theorem 2.2 deserve more explanation. Fix typos and complete references. Include the missing figures and provide reproducibility details.

Originality
- Moderate. The precise asymptotics for non-linear single-index measurements with LASSO under Gaussian designs extend prior qualitative/bounds-based results. The framework across regularizers is useful, but much builds on known CGMT machinery and prior linear-model exact analyses.

Significance
- For the theory community working on precise high-dimensional asymptotics, this is potentially useful. Practical impact is limited by strong assumptions and lack of empirical validation. The quantizer design insight is interesting but needs experiments.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide finite-sample simulations across multiple g (e.g., sign, ReLU, Tobit, quantization levels), varying δ and λ, and compare to GLM-based estimators when g is known.
- Discuss estimation of µ and σ from data when g is unknown; analyze the effect of plug-in estimates on the error.
- Clarify Assumption 1: give general sufficient conditions (e.g., conditions on f and on the empirical distribution of x0) and a proof sketch for group-sparse/low-rank cases.
- Explore robustness to sub-Gaussian/rotationally invariant A, and to µ near zero.
- Improve clarity of Theorems 2.1–2.2; state precisely that limits coincide; provide intuition for the scalar program and its variables.
- Complete and correct references; add related work on generalized linear estimation and one-bit CS methods beyond Plan–Vershynin.
- Provide guidance on λ selection in the non-linear setting.

Pros
- Clean conceptual reduction to an effective linear model with precise asymptotics.
- General framework across convex regularizers; nontrivial specialization to sparse case.
- Potentially useful application to quantizer design.

Cons
- Strong Gaussian and identifiability assumptions; Assumption 1 opaque.
- Limited novelty over prior qualitative/bounds-based results; incremental over CGMT/AMP linear-model analyses.
- Insufficient empirical validation and clarity; incomplete references.

Recommendation
- Weak reject. The paper has a solid theoretical core and could be impactful with stronger validation, clearer assumptions, and robustness results. As it stands, the contribution feels under-validated and narrowly scoped to Gaussian designs. Confidence: high.
