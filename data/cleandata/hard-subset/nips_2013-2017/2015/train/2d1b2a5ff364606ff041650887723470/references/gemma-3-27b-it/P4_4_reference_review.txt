This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimation in a single-index model with non-linear measurements. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, asymptotically, the non-linear measurement problem can be treated as a scaled and noisy linear measurement problem, characterized by parameters µ and σ² defined by the link function. This allows the authors to leverage existing tight results for linear measurements to provide precise characterizations of the estimation error for a wide class of regularizers and link functions. The paper also provides specific results for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery, and offers insights into optimal quantization strategies for q-bit compressive sensing.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a clear line of reasoning. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, building upon existing literature on the LASSO and single-index models. The derivation of the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements is well-motivated. The extension of existing results to non-linear measurements is a significant contribution. The inclusion of specific results for different structured signals (sparse, low-rank, group-sparse) and a practical application to quantized compressive sensing enhances the impact of the work. The proofs, while not included in the main body, are mentioned to be in the appendix. However, a complete review would require examining those proofs for complete verification. The authors acknowledge limitations and assumptions (e.g., unit norm x0, Gaussian measurement matrix).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is mostly consistent, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a reasonably clear manner. However, the density of mathematical expressions could be challenging for readers not already familiar with the field. The introduction provides adequate context and motivates the problem effectively. The sections are logically structured, building from the general theoretical results to specific applications. The discussion of relevant literature is extensive and helps to position the work within the existing body of knowledge. A more detailed explanation of Assumption 1 and its implications could improve clarity.

**Originality:** The problem of applying LASSO to non-linear measurements is not entirely new, as the authors acknowledge. However, the paper’s key contribution – the precise characterization of the asymptotic error and the equivalence to a scaled linear problem – is novel. The application of this result to different structured signals and the analysis of q-bit compressive sensing further enhance the originality. The precise error formula (Theorem 2.2) and its specialization to sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3) are significant advancements over previous work, which often relies on upper bounds or asymptotic approximations.

**Significance:** The results have the potential to be significant for several reasons. First, they provide a theoretical justification for using LASSO with non-linear measurements, a common practice in many applications. Second, the precise error characterization allows for more informed tuning of the regularization parameter and a better understanding of the performance limits. Third, the application to q-bit compressive sensing offers practical insights into optimal quantization strategies. The connection to existing work on linear measurements allows researchers to leverage existing tools and techniques for analyzing non-linear problems. The results are likely to be of interest to researchers in signal processing, statistics, machine learning, and related fields.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The precise error characterization and the equivalence to a scaled linear problem are original contributions.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous and builds on established theoretical foundations.
*   **Significance:** The results have the potential to impact the design and analysis of algorithms for non-linear measurement problems.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The results are applicable to various signal structures and measurement models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Mathematical Density:** The paper is mathematically dense and may be challenging for some readers.
*   **Proof Verification:** A complete assessment requires reviewing the proofs in the appendix.
*   **Assumption 1:** While stated, a more intuitive explanation of the conditions of Assumption 1 and a discussion of its limitations could be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant and original contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. The theoretical results are well-motivated, technically sound, and have the potential to be impactful. Despite some minor issues with mathematical density and the need for a full proof review, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses.  I recommend acceptance to NeurIPS.
