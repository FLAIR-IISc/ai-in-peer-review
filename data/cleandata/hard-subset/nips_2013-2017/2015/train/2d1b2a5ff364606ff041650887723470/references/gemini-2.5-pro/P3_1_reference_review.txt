### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x_0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)`, where `g_i` are random link functions and `a_i` are Gaussian measurement vectors. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, which is nominally designed for linear models. The central and powerful result of the paper is that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit, the performance of the LASSO with these non-linear measurements is *exactly equivalent* to its performance on a corresponding linear model `y_i = µ a_i^T x_0 + σ z_i`, where `z_i` are i.i.d. standard Gaussian noise. The parameters `µ` and `σ^2` are determined solely by the statistics of the link function `g` via `µ = E[γg(γ)]` and `σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - µγ)^2]` for `γ ~ N(0,1)`. This equivalence allows the authors to leverage recent advances in the precise asymptotic analysis of the LASSO for linear models to provide exact characterizations of the mean-squared error (MSE) `||x̂ - µx_0||^2` for the non-linear case.

This work builds upon two distinct but related lines of research. First, it is a significant generalization of Brillinger's classical result from the 1980s, which established a similar linear equivalence for unregularized least squares in the fixed-dimension (`n`), large-sample (`m -> ∞`) regime. This paper extends this concept to the modern high-dimensional setting where `n, m -> ∞` with `m/n = δ`, and crucially, incorporates regularization to handle the `m < n` case. Second, it sharpens and extends the very recent work of Plan and Vershynin (2015), who first demonstrated the role of the same `µ` and `σ` in the high-dimensional setting. However, Plan and Vershynin provided non-asymptotic *upper bounds* with unspecified constants for a constrained LASSO formulation. The current paper moves beyond this by providing asymptotically *exact* predictions for the more common regularized LASSO, thereby cementing the equivalence principle in a precise, quantitative manner. The methodology is rooted in the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, which has been the powerhouse behind recent precise analyses of LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., works by Stojnic, Oymak, Thrampoulidis, Hassibi). The main contribution here is successfully applying this sophisticated machinery to the non-linear measurement setting.

### Strengths

1.  **Fundamental and Elegant Result:** The core finding—that LASSO effectively "linearizes" the single-index model in high dimensions—is a beautiful and fundamentally important result. It provides deep insight into why a seemingly misspecified model works so well. The equivalence to a linear model with an effective signal-to-noise ratio governed by `µ^2/σ^2` is both intuitive and powerful.

2.  **Precise and General Analysis:** The paper provides exact asymptotic characterizations of the estimation error, moving beyond the order-wise bounds common in much of the literature. The analysis, presented in Theorem 2.2, is general and applies to a wide class of convex regularizers (not just the `l1`-norm) and signal structures. The specializations for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery are compelling and demonstrate the breadth of the framework.

3.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper successfully bridges the gap between the classical statistics of single-index models and the modern theory of high-dimensional regularized estimation. Proving that the complex, non-linear problem decouples into a much simpler scalar optimization problem (Eq. 10) that precisely predicts the error is a major technical achievement. The specific results for non-Gaussian sparse signals (Theorem 2.3) and group-sparse recovery appear to be new even for the linear measurement case, which further highlights the strength of the authors' framework.

4.  **Clear Practical Implications:** The application to `q`-bit quantized compressive sensing is an excellent demonstration of the theory's utility. By reducing the complex LASSO error minimization problem to the simple task of minimizing `σ^2/µ^2`, the authors provide a principled and computationally tractable method for optimizing quantizer design (thresholds and levels). This connects a highly abstract theory to a concrete signal processing problem.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Reliance on Gaussian Measurements:** The entire analysis is predicated on the measurement matrix `A` having i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a standard assumption in the CGMT literature as it leverages Gordon's comparison inequality. However, it is a significant limitation for practical applications where measurement ensembles may be structured (e.g., Fourier, Hadamard) or sub-gaussian. While likely a very difficult extension, the authors should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and perhaps briefly comment on whether they foresee any path to extending their results to other matrix ensembles.

2.  **Clarity of Assumption 1:** Assumption 1 is the key technical condition required for the main theorems to hold. While it is shown to be satisfied for standard cases, its definition in terms of the point-wise limit of a Moreau envelope is highly technical and may be opaque to a broader NIPS audience. It would improve clarity if the authors could provide a more intuitive explanation of what this assumption entails (e.g., that certain empirical averages related to the regularizer's dual converge as expected) in the main text.

3.  **Asymptotic Nature:** The results are strictly asymptotic. The provided figures show remarkable agreement between theory and simulations even for moderate `n`, which is very encouraging. However, the theory itself provides no finite-sample guarantees or rates of convergence to the asymptotic limit. This is a characteristic of this entire line of research, but it is a weakness worth noting.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent theoretical paper that presents a significant, elegant, and impactful result. It provides a definitive answer to the question of how the LASSO behaves with non-linear measurements in high dimensions, unifying it with the well-developed theory for linear models. The work is technically deep, builds upon and significantly advances the state-of-the-art, and even delivers novel results for the simpler linear case as a byproduct. The practical relevance is clearly demonstrated through the quantizer design application. The weaknesses, primarily the reliance on Gaussian matrices and the asymptotic nature of the analysis, are inherent to the powerful techniques used and do not detract significantly from the importance of the contribution. The paper is well-written, clearly situated within the literature, and represents a complete and polished piece of research. It is a perfect fit for NIPS and I strongly recommend acceptance.
