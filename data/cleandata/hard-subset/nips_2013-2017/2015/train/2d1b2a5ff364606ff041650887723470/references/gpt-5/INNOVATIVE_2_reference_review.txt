Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation with non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) using the generalized LASSO with a convex regularizer f. The central result (Theorem 2.1) establishes an asymptotic equivalence: for i.i.d. Gaussian designs and broad classes of link functions g and regularizers f, the LASSO’s estimation error under non-linear measurements equals that of an appropriately scaled noisy linear model ylin = µ A x0 + σ z, where µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2] with γ ∼ N(0,1). Building on this, Theorem 2.2 provides an exact asymptotic MSE formula as the solution of a convex scalar optimization involving δ = m/n, λ, µ, σ^2, and a problem-dependent function F capturing the joint effect of f and the distribution of x0 via a Moreau-envelope limit. The paper then specializes to sparse (Theorem 2.3, including non-Gaussian signals), group-sparse, and low-rank settings, and illustrates an application to q-bit quantization design, showing that minimizing σ^2/µ^2 suffices and that Lloyd–Max iterations produce stationary points for this surrogate objective.

Relation to prior work
This work extends the classical Brillinger (’82) result for least squares with non-linear link functions (consistency up to a factor µ) and the non-asymptotic bounded-error guarantees of Plan and Vershynin (constrained LASSO with constants hidden and no precise formulas). It leverages modern precise error analysis for LASSO under linear measurements via AMP/state evolution and CGMT (Bayati–Montanari; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.) and generalizes those to the non-linear single-index setting. Conceptually, it provides the first asymptotically exact characterization (to my knowledge) of LASSO error under non-linear link functions and explains precisely how g influences performance only through (µ, σ^2).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: the “non-linear = linear with (µ, σ^2)” equivalence is simple, unifying, and powerful. It provides an actionable reduction of a broad class of non-linear observation models to a linear noisy surrogate.
- Technical contribution: precise asymptotics for the generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements for all λ, a general convex f, and a wide class of x0 distributions. The scalar convex program (Theorem 2.2) is practical to evaluate and reveals the roles of δ, λ, f, and g.
- Breadth: covers sparse, group-sparse, and outlines low-rank cases; Theorem 2.3 provides new exact formulas beyond Gaussian sparse priors (improving over earlier results limited by Gaussianity assumptions or high-SNR).
- Practical angle: application to quantized compressed sensing, showing optimal quantizer design reduces to minimizing σ^2/µ^2, with a justification for Lloyd–Max as a solver for the surrogate objective.
- Improves upon Plan–Vershynin by turning loose bounds into exact asymptotics and giving a sharper constrained-LASSO bound in the asymptotic regime.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Assumptions: The equivalence and precise analysis hinge on i.i.d. Gaussian designs. It is not clear how far the results extend to sub-Gaussian or structured matrices (e.g., partial Fourier), a common setting in practice. A discussion or conjecture with preliminary evidence would strengthen impact.
- Assumption 1 (existence of the limit F): While natural for many f,x0 pairs, it is currently a black box. More explicit verification beyond the provided examples (e.g., nuclear norm) and guidance on computing F numerically would improve clarity. For the low-rank case, the paper defers details; providing at least one worked-out instance would be valuable.
- µ ≠ 0 requirement: Important cases like 1-bit sign links yield µ = 0 under symmetric priors; the paper mentions this but largely sidesteps it. Can the framework cover dithering or known asymmetric links to recover µ ≠ 0, or does the equivalence break in such regimes? A more explicit treatment or limitations statement would help.
- Practical estimation of µ and σ^2: When g is unknown, the reduction is still conceptually valid but µ,σ^2 are not given. Can they be consistently estimated from data (e.g., via Stein-type identities or moment matching) and does plug-in analysis preserve the asymptotic error?
- Finite-sample behavior: The results are asymptotic a.s.; empirical figures are suggestive but limited. Some finite-n guidance (e.g., convergence rates or concentration statements) would help practitioners.
- Notational and presentation issues: There are typos and some confusing terminology (e.g., calling ‖y − Ax‖2 + λ f(x) “`2-LASSO or square-root LASSO”; traditionally square-root LASSO uses the 2-norm of residual, as here, but clarity about the differences to the least-squares formulation would help). The main text could better signpost where assumptions are used in proofs and provide brief proof sketches.

Questions for the authors
- Can you characterize classes of non-Gaussian designs where Theorem 2.1 still holds, perhaps via universality arguments?
- For µ = 0, do you foresee an alternative normalization or two-stage method that restores a meaningful equivalence (e.g., using higher-order moments)?
- In the low-rank case, can you provide a concrete F for a simple spiked model (random orthonormal singular vectors and fixed singular values)?
- How sensitive is the quantizer design (minimizing σ^2/µ^2) to mismatch in the prior on a^T x0 or to small deviations from Gaussianity?

Pros
- Bold, unifying equivalence principle with precise asymptotics; first of its kind for non-linear LASSO.
- Broad applicability across regularizers and signal models.
- Practical takeaway for quantizer design and tuning λ via the scalar program.

Cons
- Strong Gaussian design assumption; limited discussion of universality.
- Assumption 1 left abstract; low-rank case incomplete.
- Limited guidance on µ,σ estimation when g is unknown and on finite-sample performance.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a significant and novel conceptual advance with solid technical underpinnings and clear implications for practice. While some assumptions are strong and parts (low-rank F, µ = 0) are underdeveloped, the core contribution—the precise equivalence and resulting error formulas—opens a new direction likely to influence both theory and applications. I encourage the authors to tighten the exposition, expand discussion on universality and µ,σ estimation, and include at least one fully worked low-rank example.
