This paper addresses the problem of estimating a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`, a setup known as the single-index model. The authors analyze the performance of the popular Generalized LASSO estimator in this setting. The main contribution is a powerful and elegant theoretical result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit (where the number of measurements `m` and the signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio `m/n`), the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is precisely equivalent to its performance with a corresponding linear model `y_i = μa_i^T x0 + σz_i`, where `z_i` is standard Gaussian noise. The parameters `μ` and `σ` are determined solely by the link function `g`.

This work builds upon a rich body of literature. The connection between single-index models and equivalent linear models was first established for unregularized least squares in the classical, fixed-`n` asymptotic setting by Brillinger in the 1980s. More recently, Plan and Vershynin revisited this problem in the high-dimensional regime for a *constrained* version of the LASSO, providing non-asymptotic upper bounds that highlighted the key role of `μ` and `σ`. This paper significantly advances these prior works by analyzing the more common regularized LASSO and, most importantly, by replacing the upper bounds with asymptotically *exact* characterizations of the estimation error. The methodology is rooted in the powerful Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, which has been central to the recent progress in the precise analysis of LASSO with linear measurements. This work successfully extends that framework to the non-linear setting.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Novelty of the Main Result:** The central finding—that the complex, non-linear measurement model behaves identically to a simple, noisy linear model from the perspective of the LASSO estimator—is a profound and insightful contribution. This equivalence provides a powerful simplification, allowing a difficult problem to be understood through the lens of a much more tractable one. This moves the field's understanding from the existence of an approximate relationship (as suggested by Plan and Vershynin's bounds) to a precise, asymptotic equivalence.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The paper presents a rigorous and technically deep analysis. It provides an explicit formula (Theorem 2.2) for the asymptotic mean-squared error (MSE) as the solution to a low-dimensional convex program. This formula precisely captures the interplay between all key system parameters: the measurement ratio `δ=m/n`, the signal structure (via the function `F`), the regularizer, and the non-linearity (via `μ` and `σ`). The specialization of this general result to sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3) yields a more intuitive characterization that reveals phase transition phenomena, which is a hallmark of this line of research.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent motivation for the problem and a clear summary of the contributions, carefully situating the work in the context of prior literature. The progression from the general theory to specific examples (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and finally to a practical application makes the paper easy to follow and appreciate.

4.  **Empirical Validation and Practical Application:** The theoretical predictions are convincingly validated by numerical simulations (Figures 1 and 2), which show a striking match between theory and experiment. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate the practical utility of their theory through an application to quantizer design for compressive sensing. By showing that optimizing the quantizer is equivalent to the much simpler problem of minimizing the `σ²/μ²` ratio, they provide a tangible example of how this fundamental theory can guide practical engineering decisions.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a very strong paper, a few aspects could be clarified or strengthened to further increase its impact.

1.  **Intuition behind the Equivalence:** The paper proves the equivalence result but provides little intuition as to *why* it holds. The Gaussianity of the measurement matrix `A` is critical. A brief, high-level paragraph explaining how the high-dimensional geometry and the properties of Gaussian projections cause the non-linear function `g` to effectively be "averaged out" into its first two moments (`μ` and `σ`) would be highly beneficial for the reader and make the result less magical.

2.  **Discussion of Assumptions:** The analysis relies on an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix `A`. This is a standard assumption in this line of work but is a key limitation for practical application. A brief discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for extension to more general random matrix ensembles (e.g., sub-Gaussian) would make the paper more complete. Similarly, the results are asymptotic. While the simulations show good performance at finite `n`, a short comment on the expected non-asymptotic behavior or convergence rates would be welcome.

3.  **The Case of `μ=0`:** Footnote 3 correctly notes that the theory requires `μ ≠ 0`, which excludes even link functions like `g(x) = |x|`. This is an important limitation. It would be beneficial to briefly expand on this in the main text, explaining that if `μ=0`, the LASSO estimator fails to recover the signal direction, and perhaps citing alternative approaches for this specific scenario (if known, such as [24] which is already cited).

4.  **Low-Rank Matrix Recovery Section:** Section 2.2.4 on low-rank recovery feels less developed than the sections on sparsity. The analysis is conditioned on the convergence of an empirical singular value distribution, and the final evaluation of the key function `F` is left for future work. While a perfectly valid direction, this section could be strengthened by either working through a simple concrete example or by more explicitly framing it as an illustrative outline for future extensions of the main theorem.

### Summary

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant and elegant contribution to the theory of high-dimensional inference. It establishes a precise asymptotic equivalence between LASSO with non-linear measurements and LASSO with linear measurements, a result that is both fundamentally important and practically useful. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and convincingly supported by experiments. The weaknesses are minor and relate primarily to opportunities for further clarification and discussion of the result's context and limitations. The work represents a clear advance in our understanding of structured signal recovery and is highly suitable for publication at NeurIPS.
