Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation from non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) using the generalized LASSO with convex regularization. For Gaussian design (A with i.i.d. N(0,1) entries), the authors show a precise asymptotic equivalence: the error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements matches that of the same LASSO applied to an effective linear model with scaled signal and additive Gaussian noise, yi = μ a_i^T x0 + σ zi, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Building on CGMT-based techniques, they derive a scalar convex optimization whose unique optimizer α* gives lim ||x̂ − μx0||^2 = α*^2. They then specialize to sparse (l1), group-sparse (l1,2), and briefly to low-rank (nuclear norm) recovery, obtaining closed-form characterizations (fixed-point equations) for the sparse case. As an application, they propose optimizing q-bit quantizers by minimizing σ^2/μ^2 and argue that Lloyd–Max iterations converge to stationary points of this objective.

Relation to prior work
- Brillinger (80s) analyzed least squares under link violation and showed consistency up to scale μ in fixed-n, m→∞. This paper generalizes to high-dimensional regimes and to regularized LASSO, providing exact asymptotics.
- Plan & Vershynin (2015) gave non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO under non-linear observation; the current work moves to regularized LASSO and replaces loose bounds by exact limiting error expressions.
- For linear measurements, precise LASSO error characterizations via AMP and CGMT are known (Bayati–Montanari; Stojnic; Thrampoulidis et al.). This paper shows that, asymptotically, non-linearities are fully captured by μ and σ^2, allowing reuse of the linear theory.
- Yi et al. (2015) studied optimal linear estimators under unknown non-linear transforms; here the result is stronger for non-linear regularized estimators.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and significance: the “non-linear = linear with effective μ,σ” message is powerful and actionable. It collapses a wide class of non-linearities to two scalar parameters for performance prediction and design.
- Technical depth: The CGMT-based derivation leading to a three-variable convex program (Eq. 10) is elegant and appears broadly applicable across convex regularizers meeting Assumption 1. The specialization to sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3) provides interpretable fixed-point relations that align with state evolution intuition and past results.
- Scope and generality: Applies to many convex regularizers (l1, group l1,2, nuclear norm) and many link functions, including quantization, sign, and ReLU-like.
- Practical implications: The quantizer design application is well-motivated; reducing the design to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 is a useful guideline, and the Lloyd–Max connection is interesting.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Statement of Theorem 2.1: As written, it asserts almost-sure equality of the errors in the limit, ‖x̂ − μx0‖2 = ‖x̂lin − μx0‖2. This reads like equality of the limits but is phrased as equality of random variables “in the limit.” Please restate precisely as convergence of the difference to zero (or equality of deterministic limits), and clarify whether it is convergence almost surely or in probability.
- Assumptions and generality:
  - Gaussian design is essential for the proof (CGMT and Gaussian rotational symmetry). Plan–Vershynin covered isotropic sub-Gaussian designs; can any universality extension be proved here? At least discuss limitations and whether the μ,σ reduction is expected to persist beyond Gaussian A.
  - Assumption 1 (existence of F via the Moreau envelope of f* under scaling) is nontrivial. For l1 and l1,2, verification is straightforward via LLN; for nuclear norm, the paper defers evaluation and conditions (spectral distribution convergence) to future work. This weakens the low-rank contribution: at minimum, include a concrete example (e.g., spiked model) and a numerical evaluation of F to demonstrate predictive accuracy.
- Unknown μ,σ in practice: The analysis assumes μ,σ are known (or at least nonzero and bounded). In many applications g is unknown. It would be very valuable to discuss simple estimators of μ and σ from data. For Gaussian A, cross-moments E[y_i a_i] = μ x0 suggest spectral or method-of-moments initializers (cf. Yi et al., phase retrieval literature). Providing a plug-in strategy for λ tuning using estimated μ,σ would increase practical impact.
- μ = 0 case: The paper excludes μ=0 (even g). A brief discussion of what breaks and whether variants (e.g., quadratic measurements with lifting, or alternative convex losses) could be analyzed would help scope the results.
- Finite-sample behavior: While asymptotics are the focus, include more empirical studies to show how large n,m must be for the predictions to be accurate across different g and f. The current text references figures but includes limited detail; adding quantitative plots for several δ, λ and multiple link functions would strengthen the experimental section.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Normalize the objective scaling consistently; the 1/√n factor in (9) is unusual—explain why it is convenient and how it maps to standard formulations.
  - Improve exposition around F(c1,c2,c3): briefly interpret c1,c2,c3 and provide a short recipe for computing F for common f (l1, l1,2, nuclear norm).
  - Bibliography entries are incomplete and should be fixed.
  - Minor typos: “mesaurements”, “redifining”, “eventhough”, “with gaussianity assumption”, etc.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and elegant theoretical contribution with clear message and broad relevance.
- Extends precise LASSO error analysis to non-linear measurement models in a principled way.
- Practical design insight for quantized compressive sensing.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited to Gaussian designs; lack of universality discussion.
- Low-rank case underdeveloped; no concrete evaluation of F or experiments.
- Some statements (Theorem 2.1) need clarification; dependence on unknown μ,σ not addressed empirically.

Questions for authors
1) Can you provide a precise statement and proof sketch for the mode of convergence in Theorem 2.1?  
2) Do you anticipate a universality extension to sub-Gaussian designs? Any empirical evidence?  
3) For the nuclear-norm case, can you evaluate F in a spiked model and include a simulation?  
4) How would you estimate μ,σ when g is unknown, and how sensitive is performance to estimation error?

Overall evaluation
This is a high-quality and potentially impactful contribution. Addressing the clarity issues and strengthening the low-rank/empirical sections would make it a strong accept. As is, I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying Theorem 2.1 and expanding experiments.
