This paper presents a significant theoretical result concerning the performance of the Generalized LASSO for signal estimation from non-linear measurements, a setup known as the single-index model. The authors demonstrate that, in a high-dimensional asymptotic regime with i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, the mean-squared error (MSE) of the LASSO estimator is exactly equivalent to the MSE of the same estimator applied to a corresponding *linear* measurement model. This linear model has a signal scaling factor `µ` and an effective noise variance `σ^2`, both of which are determined solely by the non-linear link function `g`. This elegant equivalence principle allows the direct application of a powerful body of recent work on the precise asymptotic analysis of LASSO to the non-linear setting. The paper provides a general formula for the asymptotic MSE and specializes it to important cases like sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery.

This work builds upon a rich history. The connection between single-index models and linear models was first established in the classical, fixed-dimension setting by Brillinger (1983). More recently, Plan and Vershynin (2015) revisited this in a high-dimensional context, providing non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO variant, which first highlighted the crucial role of the parameters `µ` and `σ`. The current paper's primary contribution is to elevate these findings from loose upper bounds to a precise, asymptotic characterization for the more commonly used regularized LASSO, leveraging the state-of-the-art Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The central result (Theorem 2.1) is powerful and conceptually clean. The "Non-linear = Linear" equivalence provides a profound insight that simplifies a complex problem. It bridges the gap between the analysis of linear inverse problems and the more challenging single-index models. This allows researchers and practitioners to immediately import intuitions and results from the well-understood linear LASSO literature to problems involving quantization, censoring, and other non-linearities, which are ubiquitous in real-world systems. The application to optimal q-bit quantizer design is an excellent example of the practical utility of this theory.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The paper provides a precise, asymptotic characterization of the estimation error, which is a major advance over the order-wise correct upper bounds in prior work. The mathematical framework, presumably based on CGMT, represents the state of the art for this type of analysis. The resulting error prediction formula (Eq. 10) is explicit and captures the interplay between all key system parameters: the measurement ratio (`δ`), the link function (`µ`, `σ^2`), the signal structure, the regularizer (`f`), and the regularization parameter (`λ`). The specializations for sparse and group-sparse signals are valuable and demonstrate the framework's versatility.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction effectively situates the work within the literature and clearly articulates the main contributions. The core idea is presented with admirable clarity, and the progression from the general theorem to specific applications is logical. The figures provide compelling visual evidence that the theoretical predictions precisely match simulation results under the assumed model.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on a standard but restrictive set of assumptions: an asymptotic regime where `m, n -> ∞` with `m/n -> δ`, and an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix `A`. While this is a necessary starting point for this type of precise analysis, the paper would benefit from a discussion on the potential robustness or fragility of the results with respect to these assumptions. For instance, how might the equivalence principle be affected by structured measurement matrices (e.g., partial DCT/FFT) or by finite, practical problem dimensions?

2.  **Intuition behind the General Formula:** While Theorem 2.2 is the technical core, the optimization problem in Eq. (10) is abstract and may be opaque to readers not deeply familiar with the CGMT literature. The paper does an excellent job of simplifying this for the sparse case (Theorem 2.3), which is much more interpretable. It would be beneficial to provide more intuition behind the general formula itself. For example, a brief explanation of the physical meaning of the optimization variables (`α`, `β`, `τ`) could greatly enhance readability and understanding.

3.  **Assumption 1:** This is a crucial technical condition for the main result to hold. The authors show it holds for several key regularizers, but the assumption itself is stated formally without much intuition. A short discussion on what this assumption means—perhaps relating it to the stability or concentration of the Moreau envelope of the dual norm—would be helpful. It would also be interesting to know if there are common regularizers for which this assumption might fail.

4.  **Novelty of Proof Technique:** The novelty of this work lies in the result and its implications, rather than the development of a fundamentally new proof technique. The paper correctly cites the line of work on precise LASSO analysis via CGMT. It would be appropriate to be explicit that the contribution is the masterful application and extension of this existing framework to a new and important problem domain, which leads to a highly novel insight.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent theoretical paper with significant practical implications. It addresses an important problem at the intersection of high-dimensional statistics, signal processing, and machine learning. The main result is strong, novel, and provides a beautiful unification of linear and non-linear measurement models for LASSO-type estimation. The technical quality is very high, and the presentation is clear and compelling. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to the inherent limitations of the asymptotic framework and opportunities to provide deeper intuition. The work significantly advances the state of the art and is highly likely to be influential.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
