## NIPS Review: LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements

**Summary:**

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the behavior of the Generalized LASSO when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements. Building upon recent work by Plan & Vershynin (2015) and drawing parallels to Brillinger’s (1980s) work on least squares, the authors demonstrate that, under Gaussian measurement matrices and in high dimensions, the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of a LASSO applied to scaled and noisy linear measurements. This equivalence is characterized by a constant of proportionality (µ) and noise variance (σ²) that depend solely on the link function *g*. The paper derives a precise error expression, extending previous results to the non-linear case, and provides insights into the roles of regularization, measurement number, and the link function itself.  They further illustrate the results with specific applications to sparse recovery and q-bit compressive sensing.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper establishes a rigorous theoretical framework for analyzing the LASSO under non-linear measurements. The asymptotic analysis is well-motivated and clearly presented.
* **Novelty and Insight:** The key result – the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements – is a significant insight. It provides a powerful tool for analyzing non-linear estimation problems using the well-developed theory for linear models.
* **Generality:** The results hold for a wide class of link functions *g* and regularizers *f*, making the contribution broadly applicable.  The analysis is not limited to specific sparsity assumptions.
* **Precise Error Analysis:**  The derivation of precise error expressions, extending existing bounds, is a crucial achievement. This goes beyond simply showing consistency and provides quantitative insights.
* **Applications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing is a practical illustration of the theory and demonstrates its potential impact. The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm for quantization is insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** Despite the technical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent and the derivations are presented logically.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption of i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices is strong, although standard.  Exploring the robustness of the results to other matrix ensembles would be valuable. The assumption of unit norm x0, while acknowledged and readily generalizable, adds a slight constraint on immediate applicability.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the theoretical results are precise, computing the optimal parameters (e.g., in the q-bit sensing application) might still be challenging in practice.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While Figure 1 is provided, the empirical validation feels somewhat limited. More extensive simulations showcasing the theoretical predictions across a wider range of scenarios would strengthen the claims.  Specifically, showing the accuracy of the error formulas for varying values of λ, m/n, and different link functions would be beneficial.
* **Assumption 1:** The technical condition Assumption 1 appears somewhat restrictive and the justification for its applicability in the sparse recovery case is a bit hand-wavy.  A more detailed explanation or a broader class of functions that satisfy this condition would be helpful.




**Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** Excellent. The paper is technically sound, claims are well-supported by mathematical analysis, and the results appear complete within the stated scope. The authors are careful in presenting their assumptions and limitations.
* **Clarity:** Good. The paper is generally well-written, but some sections (e.g., the discussion around Assumption 1) could benefit from further clarification.
* **Originality:** Excellent. The equivalence result is a novel contribution. The combination of existing techniques to analyze the non-linear case, and the precise error analysis, represent significant originality.
* **Significance:** Excellent. The results are likely to be influential in the field of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. The findings provide a fundamental understanding of the behavior of the LASSO in non-linear settings and open up new avenues for research.  The connection to compressive sensing has practical implications.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*  Significant theoretical breakthrough with a clear and impactful result.
*  Rigorous mathematical analysis and precise error bounds.
*  Broad applicability to various estimation problems.
*  Potential for practical applications in areas like compressive sensing.

**Con:**

*  Strong assumptions regarding the measurement matrix.
*  Limited empirical validation.
*  Technical complexity may make it challenging for some readers.
*  Assumption 1 may be restrictive.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**

This paper represents a significant advance in our understanding of the LASSO and its application to non-linear measurement models. The theoretical results are compelling, the analysis is rigorous, and the potential impact is substantial. While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.  I strongly recommend accepting this paper to NIPS.
