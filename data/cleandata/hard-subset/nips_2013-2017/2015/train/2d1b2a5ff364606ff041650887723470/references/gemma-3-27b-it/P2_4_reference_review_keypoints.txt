- Paper proves that Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements yi = g(ai^T x0) is asymptotically equivalent to LASSO on scaled noisy linear measurements, characterized by scaling µ and noise variance σ^2.  
- Authors derive a precise asymptotic error formula and validate it with simulations.  
- Work builds on Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem and recent high-dimensional LASSO analyses.  
- Application to q-bit compressive sensing shows practical utility and links to the Lloyd–Max algorithm.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and technically sound.  
- The equivalence result is novel and significant, simplifying analysis of non-linear measurement problems.  
- Results are general, holding under broad assumptions on the link function g and regularizer f.  
- Practical implications are clear through the quantization application and optimization framework.  
- Introduction and theorem statements are mostly clear and well organized.  
- Paper is notationally dense and may be hard to follow for non-expert readers; some definitions lack intuition.  
- Empirical validation is limited in scope and would benefit from more diverse signal structures and link functions.  
- Figures are hard to interpret without careful reading of the text.  
- The impact of violating key assumptions (especially Assumption 1) is not sufficiently discussed.  
- Literature review is extensive but reads like a list; the narrative on how this work advances prior results is weak.  
- Overall quality and originality are strong; clarity is moderate.  
- The results are likely to be influential and significant for compressive sensing and estimation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical contribution, rigorous analysis, and practical application.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility concerns due to technical density, insufficient empirical breadth, and need for clearer relation to prior work.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity and empirical validation.  
- Suggested revisions: add intuitive explanations, broaden empirical examples, focus discussion of advances over prior work, and discuss consequences of violating Assumption 1.
