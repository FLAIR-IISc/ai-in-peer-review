### Review

**Summary:**

This paper analyzes the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator for recovering a structured signal from non-linear measurements, a setup known as the single-index model. The authors' central and most striking contribution is to prove that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit, using the LASSO on measurements of the form `y_i = g(a_i^T x_0)` is precisely equivalent to using it on measurements from an effective linear model `y_i = mu * a_i^T x_0 + sigma * z_i`. Here, `z_i` is standard Gaussian noise, and the constants `mu` and `sigma` are determined solely by the properties of the non-linear link function `g`.

This powerful equivalence theorem allows the authors to port the entire machinery of recent, highly precise analytical tools for the linear LASSO (namely, the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem) to the non-linear setting. The result is an exact, deterministic formula for the asymptotic mean-squared error `||x_hat - mu * x_0||^2`, expressed as the solution to a simple, low-dimensional convex program. The authors demonstrate the generality of their framework by applying it to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank signal recovery. They further showcase its practical utility by applying it to the problem of designing optimal q-bit quantizers for compressive sensing, showing that the complex design problem reduces to a simple optimization of the `sigma^2 / mu^2` ratio. The work builds upon classical results by Brillinger and recent work by Plan and Vershynin, but moves significantly beyond them by providing exact characterizations for the regularized LASSO in the high-dimensional regime, replacing loose upper bounds with precise formulae.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Significance:** The paper's core finding—that the LASSO treats a wide class of non-linear measurements *as if* they were simply scaled and noisy linear measurements—is a profound and non-obvious insight. This is a bold, elegant, and unifying theoretical result. It provides a rigorous explanation for the empirically observed robustness of LASSO-type estimators when applied to misspecified models. This kind of foundational result, which changes our understanding of a widely used tool, is precisely what the community should value most. The implications are significant, as it provides a concrete analytical tool for many problems in signal processing, statistics, and machine learning where non-linearities are common (e.g., quantization, censoring, binary observations).

2.  **Theoretical Precision and Power:** The work provides asymptotically *exact* predictions for the estimation error, a significant step up from the more common order-wise bounds. This precision is not merely a theoretical curiosity; the application to optimal quantizer design demonstrates its power. By reducing a complex stochastic problem to a simple, deterministic optimization over `mu` and `sigma`, the theory provides clear and actionable design principles. This represents a major advance over prior work which could only offer indicative bounds with unknown constants.

3.  **Generality:** The framework is impressively general. It holds for a wide class of convex regularizers beyond the standard `l1`-norm (including group LASSO and nuclear norm) and for a very broad family of non-linear link functions `g`, which can even be unknown. The paper effectively generalizes the classical work of Brillinger to the modern high-dimensional, regularized setting and provides a much sharper characterization than the recent results of Plan and Vershynin.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Idealized Theoretical Setting:** The analysis is conducted under a set of standard but idealized assumptions: the high-dimensional asymptotic limit (`m, n -> infinity` with a fixed ratio) and the use of i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices. While the provided simulations show an excellent match, these assumptions do not cover all practical scenarios. The performance of the LASSO and the validity of the equivalence principle for finite dimensions or for structured/non-Gaussian measurement ensembles remain open questions. A brief discussion of these limitations would strengthen the paper. However, given the radical nature of the main contribution, this is a minor and acceptable simplification for a foundational work.

2.  **Lack of Comparison to Specialized Methods:** The paper focuses on analyzing the "vanilla" LASSO, which is mismatched to the non-linear problem. It does not compare the performance of this approach to other algorithms that might be specifically designed for single-index models. While such a comparison is not the central goal—the paper aims to *understand* the LASSO's behavior, not propose a new state-of-the-art algorithm—it would have provided additional context on the practical trade-offs.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation:**

This is an outstanding paper that presents a truly novel and significant theoretical result. The central idea is both surprising and powerful, providing a fundamental new understanding of how the LASSO behaves in the presence of non-linear measurements. It connects two previously disparate lines of research in a beautiful way. While the analysis relies on an idealized asymptotic framework, the conceptual clarity and the power of the resulting predictions are undeniable. This work prioritizes a bold new idea over incremental methodological refinement, and it succeeds spectacularly. It is a complete, well-executed piece of research that is likely to be highly influential and to inspire follow-up work.

I strongly recommend acceptance, and I believe this paper is an excellent candidate for a spotlight or oral presentation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel, non-obvious, and elegant theoretical equivalence result.
*   The results are of high significance, providing fundamental understanding and practical design tools for a broad class of problems.
*   Moves beyond standard error bounds to provide asymptotically exact and verifiable predictions.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis is confined to an asymptotic regime with Gaussian matrices, which may limit direct practical applicability in some settings. (In my view, this is a minor weakness completely overshadowed by the strengths).
