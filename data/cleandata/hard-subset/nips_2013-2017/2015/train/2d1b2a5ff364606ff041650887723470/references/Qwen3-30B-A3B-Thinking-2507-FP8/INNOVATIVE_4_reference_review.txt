

# Review for "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper presents a striking theoretical insight: the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to the Generalized LASSO with linear measurements that are scaled and noisy. The authors establish that for a broad class of non-linear link functions, the estimation error of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements (y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)) is identical to the error of the same algorithm applied to linear measurements of the form y_lin = μa_i^T x_0 + σz_i, where μ and σ^2 are determined by the link function g.

The main contribution (Theorem 2.1) is a precise equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement regimes, extending previous work by Plan and Vershynin [17] who only provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained version of the LASSO. The authors' key innovation is moving from loose upper bounds to precise error characterizations using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework. Theorem 2.2 provides a deterministic convex optimization program that exactly predicts the asymptotic error, generalizing previous results for linear measurements to the non-linear case.

The paper makes several significant contributions: First, it establishes a theoretical equivalence that simplifies the analysis of non-linear measurement models by reducing them to well-studied linear measurement problems. Second, it provides a precise error formula for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, which was previously unknown. Third, it demonstrates practical implications through applications to sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3), group-sparse recovery, and q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3).

The theoretical foundation is strong, building on recent advances in LASSO analysis while extending them to the non-linear setting. The paper carefully establishes the connection between non-linear and linear measurements through the parameters μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ)-μγ)^2] for γ ~ N(0,1), showing how the link function g influences the effective noise level and scaling. The results are rigorously proven and validated through numerical experiments (Figures 1-2).

This work is highly novel in its conceptual approach—it reframes the analysis of non-linear measurement models in terms of linear ones with appropriately scaled noise. While related work exists (Brillinger [5], Plan and Vershynin [17]), this paper provides the first precise characterization of LASSO performance under non-linear measurements, rather than just upper bounds. The authors convincingly demonstrate how their framework extends to various structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) with minimal additional effort.

The paper's significance is substantial. It provides a theoretical foundation for using LASSO with non-linear measurements without requiring algorithmic modifications, which has implications for applications like 1-bit quantized measurements, censored observations, and q-bit compressive sensing. The application to q-bit compressive sensing in Section 2.3 is particularly compelling, showing how the theoretical insights lead to practical design principles for optimal quantization thresholds.

While the paper makes the standard Gaussian measurement matrix assumption and focuses on asymptotic results, these limitations are well-justified given the nature of the theoretical contribution. The authors clearly articulate the scope of their results and demonstrate their practical relevance.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel theoretical insight that advances the field. It transforms a complex problem (LASSO with non-linear measurements) into a familiar one (LASSO with linear measurements) through a simple yet profound equivalence. The results are not just incremental—they establish a new conceptual framework for understanding LASSO in non-linear settings.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of structured signal recovery. The novelty is clear, the significance is substantial, and the technical quality is high.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Bold theoretical insight establishing equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement regimes
- First precise characterization of Generalized LASSO error for non-linear measurements
- Extends previous work [17] from upper bounds to exact error expressions
- Generalizes to multiple structured recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank)
- Demonstrates practical application to q-bit compressive sensing
- Rigorous proofs with clear theoretical connections to prior work

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited to asymptotic analysis (though this is standard for such theoretical results)
- Requires i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix (common assumption in this literature)
- Some technical conditions on the regularizer and signal distribution (though well-justified)

This paper represents a significant theoretical advance with clear conceptual novelty. The insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements for LASSO analysis is elegant and transformative. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field and should be accepted.
