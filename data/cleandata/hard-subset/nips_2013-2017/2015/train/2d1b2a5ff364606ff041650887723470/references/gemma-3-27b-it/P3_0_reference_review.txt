## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements of the form y = g(a<sup>T</sup>x<sub>0</sub>). The central and highly significant result is a precise characterization showing that, asymptotically, the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is *equivalent* to that of a LASSO applied to scaled and noisy linear measurements. The scaling and noise parameters are explicitly defined in terms of the link function *g*.  The authors build on recent advancements in high-dimensional statistics and sparse recovery, extending existing results to the non-linear measurement setting and providing precise error bounds. The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis and illustrates the results with insightful examples, including 1-bit compressed sensing.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds upon Brillinger's (1980s) initial observation regarding consistency of least squares with non-linear measurements, and Plan & Vershynin’s (2015) constrained LASSO analysis. Importantly, it leverages the recent surge in precise analysis of the Generalized LASSO with *linear* measurements (Bayati et al., 2011; Stojnic, 2013; Thrampoulidis et al., 2014, 2016) and extends those tools to the non-linear case. The paper appropriately situates itself within the broader context of the single-index model and sufficient dimension reduction, referencing key works in those areas. It also connects to the literature on q-bit compressive sensing, offering practical implications for optimal quantization strategies.  The authors do an excellent job highlighting how their results generalize and refine existing bounds, providing a more complete and accurate picture.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The core result—the equivalence of non-linear and scaled linear measurements for LASSO performance—is a powerful and surprising finding. It provides a fundamental understanding of how the LASSO behaves in a more general measurement model.
* **Precision & Generality:**  The paper offers *precise* asymptotic error formulas, not just upper bounds, providing a much deeper understanding of the performance characteristics.  The results are also quite general, applying to a wide class of link functions *g* and regularizers *f*.
* **Rigorous Analysis:**  The mathematical derivations appear thorough and well-justified, drawing upon established techniques from high-dimensional statistics and random matrix theory.
* **Practical Implications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing, including the proposed algorithm based on the Lloyd-Max principle, demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.
* **Clear Presentation:** Despite the technical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and organized.  The motivation is clearly explained, and the key ideas are presented in a logical manner. The connections to related work are also well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1:** The assumption regarding the limit of  `n e√n(f∗)(n),c3 (c1h+ c2x0)` is somewhat opaque and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation and/or conditions under which it holds for commonly used regularizers. While the authors demonstrate its validity for the l1 norm, clarifying its broader applicability would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the derivation of the error formulas is a major achievement, actually *computing* those formulas for specific settings (especially those beyond simple cases like sparse recovery) might be challenging.  More discussion of the computational aspects, and potential approximation strategies, would be helpful.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While Figure 1 shows a good match between theory and simulation, the empirical validation feels somewhat limited. Expanding the numerical experiments to include a wider range of link functions *g*, regularizers *f*, and signal structures would further bolster the claims.
* **Discussion of Practical Constraints:** The paper focuses on the asymptotic regime. A brief discussion of how the results translate to practical scenarios with finite dimensions and potentially non-Gaussian measurement vectors would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a fundamentally new and significant theoretical result with broad implications for sparse recovery and non-linear estimation.
* The analysis is rigorous and the results are precise, offering a substantial advance over existing work.
* The practical connection to q-bit compressive sensing highlights the real-world relevance of the findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas and connections to related work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Assumption 1 is not entirely intuitive and could limit the applicability of the results without further clarification.
* The limited empirical validation leaves some questions about the robustness of the theoretical predictions in practical settings.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The core result is both surprising and highly valuable, and the rigorous analysis provides a solid foundation for further research. While there are a few minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh them.  

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  I recommend this paper for acceptance to NIPS. The authors have presented a high-quality, original, and significant work that will undoubtedly be of interest to a broad audience of researchers in machine learning, statistics, and signal processing.  Addressing the minor weaknesses mentioned above in a revision would further enhance the paper's impact.



**Rating:** 8.5 / 10
