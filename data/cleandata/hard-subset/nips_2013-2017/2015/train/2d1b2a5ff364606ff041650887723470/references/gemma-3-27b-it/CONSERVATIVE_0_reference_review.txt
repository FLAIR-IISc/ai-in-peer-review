This paper presents a theoretical analysis demonstrating the equivalence, in the asymptotic limit, of performing LASSO regression with non-linear measurements and performing LASSO on scaled, noisy linear measurements. The core result establishes that the error characteristics are governed by constants μ and σ², defined based on the non-linear link function. While the authors build upon previous work in this area (Brillinger, Plan & Vershynin), they extend it to a more general, high-dimensional setting and provide precise error characterizations. The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical rigor and careful connection to existing literature.

However, despite the technical soundness, I find the paper’s significance questionable for NIPS. The result, while mathematically interesting, feels largely *predictive* rather than *prescriptive*. Establishing that a non-linear problem behaves *as if* it were linear doesn’t, in itself, offer new algorithmic solutions or insights beyond what's already known for linear LASSO. The paper spends considerable effort deriving precise error formulas, but the practical impact of knowing these formulas, given they reduce to the linear case, remains unclear.

The paper's clarity is also a concern. The notation is dense, and the presentation relies heavily on asymptotic analysis. While mathematically precise, it makes grasping the core intuition challenging for readers not already deeply familiar with the field. The jump between general theory and specific applications (q-bit compressive sensing) feels abrupt, and the connection isn’t fully motivated. The discussion of the Lloyd-Max algorithm feels tacked on, lacking sufficient justification for why it’s particularly well-suited to this problem.  

Regarding originality, the paper extends existing results, but the extension feels incremental. The core idea of linearizing a non-linear problem isn’t new. The value-add is the precise error characterization, which, while technically impressive, doesn't fundamentally alter our understanding of how LASSO operates.  The extensive literature review is thorough, but it also highlights the relative lack of truly *novel* contributions. The reliance on asymptotic analysis, while standard in this field, also limits the applicability of the results to very large-scale problems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis and precise error bounds.
*   Well-connected to existing literature, building upon established foundations.
*   Demonstrates a deep understanding of statistical estimation theory.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Limited practical impact – the equivalence to a linear problem doesn’t suggest new algorithms or improvements.
*   Incremental originality – extends existing work without a significant breakthrough.
*   Clarity could be improved, particularly regarding intuition and motivation.
*   The q-bit compressive sensing application feels underdeveloped.
*   The asymptotic nature of the results limits real-world applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While technically sound and rigorously analyzed, the lack of significant novelty and limited practical implications do not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. The paper feels like a mathematical exercise that, while interesting to specialists, doesn’t offer a compelling contribution to the broader machine learning community. I believe the authors would benefit from focusing future work on exploiting the established equivalence to *develop* new algorithms or optimization techniques specifically tailored to the non-linear setting, rather than simply characterizing the error in the asymptotic limit.
