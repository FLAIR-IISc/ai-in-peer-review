**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a structured high-dimensional signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO algorithm, which is naively applied to this problem as if the measurements were linear. The main and striking contribution is a precise, asymptotic characterization of the mean-squared error (MSE). The paper proves that, in the high-dimensional limit where the number of measurements `m` and the signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio `m/n`, the performance of LASSO with non-linear measurements is *exactly equivalent* to its performance on a corresponding linear model with additive Gaussian noise.

The parameters of this equivalent linear model, specifically a scaling factor `μ` and a noise variance `σ^2`, are determined solely by the statistical properties of the non-linear link function `g`. This elegant result generalizes a classical finding by Brillinger for standard least squares to the regularized, high-dimensional setting. The analysis relies on the powerful Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework. The authors then specialize their general formula to provide concrete error predictions for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their theory with a compelling application to q-bit compressive sensing, showing how the problem of designing optimal quantizers can be simplified to optimizing the `σ^2/μ^2` ratio of the equivalent linear model.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper is well-situated within the existing literature. It builds upon two main threads of research:
1.  **Single-Index Models:** The work directly extends Brillinger's classical result [5] from the 1980s, which established the linearizing effect of Gaussian measurements for unregularized least-squares in a low-dimensional (`n` fixed, `m` -> ∞) setting. This paper modernizes that result for the high-dimensional (`n,m` -> ∞) and regularized (LASSO) context.
2.  **Precise Asymptotics for LASSO:** The paper follows a recent line of highly successful research on the precise asymptotic analysis of convex optimization-based estimators. This includes work by Bayati & Montanari using Approximate Message Passing (AMP), and a parallel body of work by Stojnic, Oymak, Thrampoulidis, Hassibi, and others using the CGMT framework, which is the tool used here. While those works provided precise error characterizations for LASSO with *linear* measurements, this paper is the first to achieve this level of precision for the *non-linear* measurement setting. It also improves upon recent non-asymptotic *bounds* by Plan & Vershynin [17], turning their loose upper bounds into exact predictions for the regularized LASSO.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant and Elegant Main Result:** The core finding—that LASSO with non-linear measurements is equivalent to one with linear measurements—is both profound and practical. It provides a clean, intuitive, and powerful conceptual tool for understanding and analyzing a complex problem. This simplifies analysis and allows leveraging the vast body of existing results for the linear case.
2.  **Technical Soundness and Generality:** The paper appears technically very solid, employing state-of-the-art tools from high-dimensional probability (CGMT). The theoretical framework is quite general, applicable to a wide class of convex regularizers `f`, signal structures `x0`, and link functions `g`.
3.  **Precise and Verifiable Predictions:** Unlike results that provide only order-wise bounds with unknown constants, this paper delivers precise formulas for the asymptotic MSE. The provided simulations (Figs 1-2) show an excellent match between the theoretical predictions and empirical performance, validating the theory's accuracy.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction provides an excellent motivation and summary of contributions, clearly positioning the work within the literature. The main results are stated cleanly, and the structure is logical and easy to follow.
5.  **Compelling Application:** The application to optimal quantizer design in Section 2.3 is a strong point. It demonstrates that the theory is not merely an academic exercise but can be used to solve practical design problems by transforming a difficult, implicit optimization over the LASSO solution into a much simpler, explicit optimization over the parameters `μ` and `σ^2`.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature:** The results are strictly asymptotic. While the simulations suggest the predictions are accurate even for moderate dimensions, the theory provides no finite-sample guarantees on the estimation error or on how large `n` must be for the asymptotics to kick in. This is a common limitation of this line of analysis but should be acknowledged.
2.  **Gaussian Measurement Matrix Assumption:** The analysis relies critically on the assumption that the entries of the measurement matrix `A` are i.i.d. Gaussian. This is a standard requirement for the CGMT framework but restricts the direct applicability of the results to other types of measurement ensembles commonly used in practice (e.g., subsampled Fourier or Hadamard matrices).
3.  **Exclusion of the μ=0 Case:** The theory requires that `μ = E[γg(γ)] ≠ 0`. As noted by the authors, this excludes certain link functions, most notably even functions (e.g., `g(x) = |x|`). This is a significant class of problems for which this analysis does not apply.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel, fundamental, and highly insightful result connecting non-linear and linear measurement models in high-dimensional estimation.
*   Achieves the first precise asymptotic error characterization for the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements, advancing the state-of-the-art in the theory of high-dimensional statistics.
*   The results are general, covering a wide range of structures and non-linearities.
*   The paper is technically strong, well-written, and the theoretical predictions are convincingly validated by numerical experiments.
*   The application to quantizer design demonstrates the practical utility of the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The primary limitations are the reliance on asymptotic analysis and the i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix assumption, which are common to this entire field of study but may limit immediate practical transferability to all scenarios.
*   The novelty is in the application of existing powerful tools (CGMT) to a new problem setting, rather than the development of fundamentally new analytical machinery. However, the resulting insight is highly novel and significant.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a significant and elegant contribution to our understanding of high-dimensional estimation. The central result is clean, powerful, and likely to be influential. It bridges the gap between the analysis of linear and non-linear models for a widely used algorithm. Despite the standard limitations of asymptotic analysis and the Gaussian design assumption, the quality, originality, and significance of this work are very high. I strongly recommend acceptance.
