This paper presents a surprising and significant result: the Generalized LASSO applied to non-linear measurements behaves asymptotically as if the measurements *were* linear, after appropriate scaling. This is a bold claim, and the authors provide a rigorous theoretical justification, building on recent work in high-dimensional statistics and compressed sensing. The core idea – that non-linearities can be effectively “linearized” in a high-dimensional setting by the LASSO – is novel and has potentially broad implications.

The paper is mathematically sophisticated, drawing from diverse fields like single-index models, convex optimization, and random matrix theory. The authors successfully navigate this complexity and present their results with commendable clarity, given the technical depth. The connection to Brillinger’s earlier work and the extension to the high-dimensional regime are well-articulated. The careful handling of asymptotic regimes and the clear statement of assumptions are strengths. The inclusion of specific examples (sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, low-rank recovery) to illustrate the generality of the results is also helpful. The application to q-bit compressive sensing and the suggested use of the Lloyd-Max algorithm are interesting extensions.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is thorough, the empirical validation is limited to a few figures. More extensive experiments, especially exploring different non-linear functions *g* and varying signal structures, would strengthen the claims. The discussion surrounding the application to q-bit compressive sensing feels somewhat tacked on; while the connection is logical, it lacks a detailed exploration of practical implications. The assumption of unit norm signals, while acknowledged and justified, still feels restrictive. While the authors provide references, some key works in non-linear signal processing are missing (e.g., works on functional links in signal detection).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The core result – the equivalence of non-linear and scaled linear measurements under the LASSO – is genuinely novel and unexpected.
*   **Significance:** This finding has the potential to fundamentally alter how we approach signal recovery problems with non-linear measurements. It suggests that established linear analysis techniques can be adapted to these scenarios.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The mathematical analysis appears sound, with careful attention to asymptotic regimes and assumptions.
*   **Generality:** The results are shown to hold for a wide class of regularizers and link functions.
*   **Potential Impact:** The result opens up new avenues of research in areas such as compressed sensing, sparse recovery, and machine learning with non-linear models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are sparse and could be more comprehensive.
*   **Restricted Assumptions:** The unit norm signal assumption, while handled, is a simplification.
*   **Application Depth:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing lacks in-depth analysis.
*   **Missing References:** A few relevant works in non-linear signal processing are not cited.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, the paper's strengths – particularly its novelty and theoretical significance – heavily outweigh its flaws. The result is a significant contribution to the field and has the potential to inspire further research. The paper demonstrates a bold approach and a deep understanding of the underlying mathematical principles. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to NIPS and recommend **acceptance**. While further empirical validation and more in-depth explorations of practical applications would be beneficial, the core theoretical contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance even in its current form. I would encourage the authors to address the empirical validation concerns in a future version of the work.
