

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper claims to extend previous work on LASSO with non-linear measurements by establishing precise asymptotic error formulas. The authors show that for measurements of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), the Generalized LASSO behaves asymptotically as if the measurements were linear with scaling factor μ and noise variance σ² defined in terms of the link function g. The main contribution is Theorem 2.1, which states that the error under non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to the error under appropriately scaled linear measurements.

The paper builds on Brillinger (1980s) and Plan & Vershynin (2015), who established related results for least squares and constrained LASSO with upper bounds. The authors claim to advance these results by providing precise error formulas rather than upper bounds, particularly for the regularized Generalized LASSO. They verify their claims with specific cases (sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery) and provide theoretical derivations.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written, with clear organization and consistent notation. The theoretical development is technically sound, with proper references to related work. The authors provide a thorough discussion of assumptions and limitations, including the requirement for Gaussian measurement matrices and asymptotic analysis. The specific error formulas for sparse and group-sparse recovery (Theorems 2.2-2.3) are well-motivated and extend previous results.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the claim of novelty, the core insight that non-linear measurements can be transformed to linear measurements with appropriate scaling is not new. Brillinger (1980s) already showed this for least squares without regularization, and Plan & Vershynin (2015) extended it to constrained LASSO with upper bounds. The main advance appears to be moving from upper bounds to precise error formulas, but the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why this precise characterization is significantly more valuable than the upper bounds provided by Plan & Vershynin.

Most critically, the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation. While the authors reference Figures 1-2 as illustrations of their results, they do not provide comprehensive experimental results across diverse problem settings, measurement models, or regularization parameters. The absence of practical validation makes it difficult to assess the real-world utility of the theoretical results. The strong assumptions (Gaussian measurement matrices, asymptotic regime, unit-norm signals) further limit the applicability of the findings.

The paper's claim of being "the first to derive asymptotically precise results" for non-linear LASSO-type programs appears overstated. The error formulas for linear measurements have been well-studied (e.g., in Thrampoulidis et al., 2013; Donoho et al., 2013), and the extension to non-linear measurements seems like a natural theoretical progression rather than a major innovation. The authors do not sufficiently distinguish their precise error formulas from the existing upper bounds in terms of practical value.

**Significance:** While the theoretical result is interesting, its significance for the machine learning community is limited. The asymptotic regime and Gaussian measurement assumptions are far removed from typical applications. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how practitioners would use these precise formulas to improve their LASSO implementations with non-linear measurements. The "precise" error formulas may be valuable for theoretical understanding but do not seem to offer substantial practical advantages over the existing upper bounds.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a technically sound theoretical extension of previous work, but the incremental contribution is insufficient for NIPS. The lack of empirical validation, strong assumptions limiting practical applicability, and overstated novelty claim weaken the paper significantly. While the results are mathematically interesting, they do not advance the field in a way that justifies publication in a top-tier venue like NIPS.

Pro: Clear theoretical development, well-organized, builds on solid previous work
Con: Overstated novelty, lacks empirical validation, strong assumptions limit practical relevance, incremental contribution

I recommend rejection. The paper would be better suited for a specialized statistics or signal processing journal where the theoretical contribution to non-linear measurement models would be more appreciated. For NIPS, the paper does not demonstrate sufficient novelty, practical significance, or empirical validation to meet the high standards of the conference.
