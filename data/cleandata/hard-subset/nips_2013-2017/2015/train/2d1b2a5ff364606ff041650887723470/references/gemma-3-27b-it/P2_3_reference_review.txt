## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the behavior of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(aTix0)`. Building upon prior work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, the authors demonstrate a surprising and powerful result: under certain conditions (Gaussian measurement vectors, large dimensions), the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of the LASSO with *scaled and noisy linear* measurements.  They derive a precise characterization of this equivalence, identifying scaling and noise parameters (`µ` and `σ2`) dependent on the non-linear link function `g`. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis and demonstrates the accuracy of their predictions through simulations. They also highlight practical implications, specifically in the context of q-bit compressive sensing.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The core result – the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements for LASSO – is highly original and potentially significant. It offers a powerful simplification for analyzing and utilizing LASSO in scenarios with non-linearities, opening avenues for leveraging existing linear LASSO theory.  The application to q-bit compressive sensing is a compelling example of practical relevance.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is built on a solid theoretical foundation. The authors clearly state their assumptions and provide rigorous proofs (deferred to the appendix, but referenced). The derivation of the precise error formula (Theorem 2.2) is a substantial contribution.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper explores various scenarios, including sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, and low-rank recovery, showcasing the generality of the result.
* **Detailed Comparison to Existing Work:** The authors provide a thorough and well-structured literature review, carefully positioning their contribution relative to Brillinger, Plan & Vershynin, and more recent advancements in LASSO analysis.  They explicitly address how their work generalizes and improves upon prior results.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the key concepts are explained clearly, although some sections (e.g., Assumption 1) are dense and may require careful reading.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1:** The stated Assumption 1, while technically sound, feels somewhat opaque and could benefit from further clarification and discussion of its practical implications. It’s not immediately clear how easily this assumption will hold in various real-world scenarios.  More examples of cases where it *does* hold would be helpful.
* **Computational Complexity of Theorem 2.2:** While the theoretical result (Theorem 2.2) is powerful, the optimization problem involved in computing the error bound might be challenging to solve in practice. The authors acknowledge this, but discussing potential approximation techniques or computational strategies could strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the simulations in Figures 1 and 2 provide some validation, the empirical evaluation feels somewhat limited.  Exploring a wider range of non-linear functions `g` and demonstrating the accuracy of the error prediction in more diverse settings would increase confidence in the results.
* **Focus on Gaussian Measurements:** The work is heavily reliant on the assumption of Gaussian measurement vectors.  While this is a common assumption in high-dimensional statistics, exploring the robustness of the result to deviations from Gaussianity would be a valuable extension.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core theoretical result is genuinely novel and has the potential to significantly impact the analysis and application of LASSO in non-linear measurement settings.
* The paper demonstrates a high level of technical rigor and provides a thorough comparison to existing literature.
* The application to q-bit compressive sensing highlights the practical relevance of the work.
* The authors clearly identify the limitations of their work and suggest potential directions for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Assumption 1 may be restrictive in some practical applications.
* The computational complexity of the error bound derived in Theorem 2.2 might limit its immediate practical utility.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty, rigor, and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. The paper meets all four reviewing criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance) to a high degree.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS, contingent upon the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1.  **Clarify Assumption 1:** Provide more detailed explanations and examples illustrating when this assumption holds and its implications for practical applications.
2.  **Discuss Computational Aspects:** Briefly discuss potential strategies for approximating or efficiently solving the optimization problem in Theorem 2.2.
3.  **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Consider including simulations with a wider range of non-linear functions `g` to further validate the accuracy of the error prediction.
