

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper makes a bold and novel theoretical contribution by establishing a precise equivalence between LASSO with non-linear measurements and LASSO with linear measurements under a specific scaling. The authors show that for measurements of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), the LASSO estimator behaves asymptotically as if the measurements were linear with scaling factor μ and noise variance σ², where μ and σ² are defined in terms of the link function g. This insight generalizes previous work by Plan and Vershynin [17], who provided only upper bounds on the error for a constrained LASSO, to the more commonly used regularized LASSO with precise error characterization.

The key contribution is Theorem 2.1, which states that the error of the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements is equivalent to the error when using appropriately scaled linear measurements. Theorem 2.2 then provides a precise error formula for this asymptotic behavior, which is a deterministic convex optimization problem involving only three scalar variables. The paper further demonstrates the power of this framework by specializing to sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3), group-sparse recovery, and low-rank matrix recovery. The authors also apply their theory to q-bit compressive sensing, providing a principled way to optimize quantization thresholds.

This work stands out for its conceptual clarity and elegance in connecting non-linear measurement models to linear ones through a simple scaling transformation. The insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements with specific parameters is both surprising and powerful, as it allows practitioners to leverage the extensive existing literature on linear measurement models for non-linear scenarios. The paper's theoretical approach using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) is well-suited to the problem and provides tight results where previous work offered only loose bounds.

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and effective use of figures to validate the theory. The authors carefully position their work in relation to the literature, acknowledging previous contributions while clearly articulating their novel contributions. The special cases (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) are well-motivated and demonstrate the broad applicability of the framework.

Strengths:
- The core insight is highly novel and conceptually elegant, establishing a precise equivalence between two seemingly different measurement models
- The framework is general and applies to a wide class of regularizers and link functions
- The precise error formula is a significant improvement over previous upper bounds
- The application to q-bit compressive sensing provides a concrete example of practical utility
- The paper builds meaningfully on prior work while making a clear conceptual leap

Weaknesses:
- The assumption that x0 has unit norm is somewhat restrictive (though the authors note how to generalize this)
- Some technical conditions (Assumption 1) may require careful verification for specific applications
- The numerical evaluation of F(·) for certain cases (e.g., low-rank recovery) is left for future work

This paper addresses a fundamental question in signal processing and statistics: how do non-linear measurements affect the performance of structured estimation algorithms? The authors provide a complete theoretical answer that is both surprising and practically useful. The equivalence result is not just a theoretical curiosity but has clear implications for designing and analyzing algorithms in real-world scenarios with non-linear measurements.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a unifying theoretical framework that simplifies the analysis of LASSO with non-linear measurements, which is a common scenario in applications like 1-bit quantization, censored data, and other non-linear sensing models. The precise error characterization can guide practical parameter selection and algorithm design, as demonstrated in the q-bit compressive sensing application.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this paper exceptionally strong. The central insight is bold and transformative, turning a complex non-linear problem into a familiar linear one with precise error characterization. The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of LASSO and has clear practical implications.

The paper is well-positioned for acceptance at NIPS. It presents a novel theoretical framework that significantly advances our understanding of LASSO with non-linear measurements, and it does so with rigorous mathematics and clear exposition. The authors have built on prior work in a meaningful way while making a clear conceptual leap that will be valuable to both theoretical and applied researchers.

I recommend acceptance. The novelty of the core insight, the elegance of the theoretical framework, and the practical significance of the results make this paper a strong contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses noted above do not detract from the paper's overall strength and significance.
