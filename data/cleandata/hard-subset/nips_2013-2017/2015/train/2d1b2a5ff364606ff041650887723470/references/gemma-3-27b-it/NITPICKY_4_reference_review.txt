This paper investigates the equivalence of LASSO with non-linear measurements to one with linear measurements, building upon prior work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin. The core contribution lies in establishing a precise asymptotic error characterization for the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements, demonstrating it behaves similarly to a scaled and noisy linear measurement scenario. The authors derive a precise formula relating the error to the link function *g*, the signal structure, and the regularization parameter. The paper meticulously connects the results to existing literature on sparse recovery, group sparsity, low-rank recovery, and q-bit compressive sensing, providing concrete examples and algorithmic insights.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper presents a mathematically rigorous analysis, leveraging concepts from convex analysis and high-dimensional statistics. The derivation of Theorem 2.2 and its subsequent specialization to sparse recovery is impressive.
*   **Precise Results:** The derivation of the precise error formula is a significant contribution, going beyond existing upper bounds and providing a tight characterization of performance.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The results are generalized to various signal structures (sparse, low-rank, group-sparse) and measurement settings (q-bit quantization).
*   **Connections to Existing Work:**  The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature and carefully position their work within the existing body of knowledge. The discussion of related work is thorough and provides valuable context.
*   **Practical Implications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing and the development of an algorithm for optimal quantization levels demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** Assumption 1, while stated, feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a clear justification for its broad applicability. The dependence of the result on the limit F(c1, c2, c3) and the requirement to *compute* this limit, even if asymptotically, is a potential practical bottleneck. The convergence argument for the Lloyd-Max algorithm feels somewhat tacked on and lacks a detailed convergence proof.
*   **Notational Burden:** The paper is densely packed with notation, making it challenging to follow the derivations. While mathematically precise, a more streamlined presentation could enhance clarity. For example, consistently using subscripts to denote sequence elements (e.g., x(n)0) can be confusing.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While Figure 1 is included, the empirical validation appears limited, especially given the theoretical depth of the analysis.  More extensive simulations demonstrating the accuracy of the error formula across different parameter regimes and signal structures would strengthen the paper. Specifically, it is not immediately obvious what the behavior is for a variety of sparsity levels.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** While the authors acknowledge limitations, a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges in applying the results, such as the computational cost of finding the optimal quantization levels, would be valuable. 
*  **Minor Formatting Issues**: Throughout the document, there is inconsistent spacing after periods and commas. The asterisk notation (*) on page 1 for funding acknowledgement is inconsistent with common practices, and is better presented in a dedicated acknowledgements section.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advancement in understanding the behavior of the LASSO with non-linear measurements.
*   The precise error characterization is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   The broad applicability to various signal structures and measurement settings increases the impact of the work.

**Con:**

*   The somewhat opaque Assumption 1 and the lack of detailed practical considerations may limit the immediate applicability of the results.
*   The heavy notational burden and limited empirical validation make it difficult to fully assess the practical impact of the work.
*   A multitude of minor formatting and presentation issues detract from the overall clarity of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. However, the paper is hampered by several minor flaws, including the somewhat ad-hoc assumption, heavy notation, limited empirical validation, and numerous formatting inconsistencies. While the theoretical results are impressive, the lack of a clear path to practical implementation and the stylistic issues detract from the overall impact.  I am inclined to **reject** the paper, despite the strength of its theoretical contributions, due to the accumulation of these minor but significant flaws. Addressing these issues, particularly by providing more extensive empirical validation and simplifying the presentation, would significantly improve the paper's quality and increase its likelihood of acceptance.
