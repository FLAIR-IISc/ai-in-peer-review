- Main result: Shows generalized LASSO with measurements y_i = g(a_i^T x0) is asymptotically equivalent to LASSO with linear measurements y_i = μ a_i^T x0 + σ z_i, with μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ)-μγ)^2].
- Scope: Precise error predictions for a wide range of structured signals (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and regularizers under specified asymptotic conditions.
- Methodology: Rigorous theoretical analysis using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, with detailed proofs (Theorems 2.1, 2.2).
- Relation to prior work: Extends Plan and Vershynin (2015) by replacing loose upper bounds with exact asymptotic error characterizations.
- Experiments: Empirical results (Figures 1–2) show strong agreement between theoretical predictions and simulations across link functions and signal structures.
- Limitation—norm assumption: Assumes ||x0||2 = 1, which excludes some cases (e.g., even link functions); paper discusses handling ||x0||2 ≠ 1.
- Limitation—regime: Results are asymptotic; finite-sample behavior is not fully characterized.
- Clarity: Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise setup and logically presented specialized cases.
- Technical accessibility: Proofs may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with CGMT, but this is expected for a theoretical NIPS paper.
- Originality: Novel insight that nonlinear measurements reduce to scaled noisy linear measurements; significant theoretical advance.
- Practical impact: Results guide practitioners using LASSO without knowing g, and apply to real settings like 1-bit/censored measurements.
- Application: Demonstrates practical use in q-bit compressive sensing, including optimizing quantization thresholds and levels.
- Strengths (summary): Significant, precise theoretical contribution; clear non-linear/linear connection; broad applicability; strong experiments; practical q-bit application.
- Weaknesses (summary): ||x0||2 = 1 assumption, focus on asymptotics over finite-sample analysis, and technically dense proofs.
- Recommendation: Strong accept — rigorous, original, significant, and relevant to the NIPS community.
