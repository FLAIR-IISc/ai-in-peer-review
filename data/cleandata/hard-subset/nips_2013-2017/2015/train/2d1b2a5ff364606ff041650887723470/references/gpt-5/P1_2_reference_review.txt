Summary
The paper studies generalized LASSO under non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian design A. The central claim is an asymptotic equivalence: for a broad class of convex regularizers f and link functions g, the mean-squared error of the generalized LASSO with non-linear observations equals that of the same estimator applied to an effective linear model ylin = µ A x0 + σ z, with µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µ γ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Theorem 2.1 formalizes this non-linear = linear equivalence in the high-dimensional limit m/n → δ, assuming mild normalization on f and a technical assumption (Assumption 1) ensuring existence of a limit of a Moreau-envelope expression. Theorem 2.2 gives an exact asymptotic error characterization as the solution of a three-scalar convex program involving µ, σ^2, δ, λ and a function F encapsulating the geometry induced by f and the distribution of x0. The paper specializes the formula to sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) settings; for ℓ1, Theorem 2.3 yields simpler equations and identifies a critical λcrit regime when δ < 1. An application to q-bit quantized compressed sensing shows the equivalence implies that minimizing σ^2/µ^2 over thresholds and levels is asymptotically optimal for the LASSO, and the authors connect this to Lloyd–Max as a practical design.

Relation to prior work
- Brillinger (1982) analyzed least squares under link violation for m → ∞, n fixed, showing consistency up to a scaling µ with variance parameter σ^2. This paper extends to high-dimensional regimes and to regularized estimators.
- Plan & Vershynin (2015) established non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO with non-linear observations, exposing the role of µ and σ (and ζ), but with loose constants. The present work provides asymptotically exact characterizations for the regularized LASSO and shows the precise reduction to a linear model with parameters (µ, σ^2).
- Precise LASSO risk with linear measurements has been developed via AMP and CGMT for ℓ1 and related regularizers. This paper leverages CGMT-style ideas to extend precision to non-linear observation models and to broader regularizers via the F function.

Quality
- The main results are technically strong and plausible given the Gaussian design and CGMT framework. The equivalence in Theorem 2.1 is striking and, if proofs in the appendix are correct, an important contribution. The convex scalar program (Theorem 2.2) is well-posed and reduces to known special cases; the ℓ1 specialization (Theorem 2.3) matches prior Gaussian-signal results and extends them to general sparse priors.
- Assumptions are stated, but Assumption 1 is abstract; the paper helpfully verifies it for ℓ1, ℓ1,2, and sketches for nuclear norm. The Gaussian A requirement and µ ≠ 0 are clearly stated. Theorems explicitly quantify dependence on δ, λ, f, pX0, and g (via µ, σ^2).
- Experiments (referenced in figures) reportedly validate the predictions across link functions and structures; more details on finite-sample accuracy and variability would strengthen the empirical case.
- One subtle point worth clarifying: in the original non-linear model, the “noise” g(a^T x0) − µ a^T x0 is dependent on A; the linear surrogate uses independent Gaussian noise. Theorems claim equality of asymptotic errors regardless—an explanation of why dependence vanishes for the LASSO risk under CGMT would improve clarity.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, with a clear motivation and progression from general theory to specializations and an application. However:
  - Assumption 1 deserves more intuition and a checklist for common f and x0 models (beyond examples).
  - Notation around Theorem 2.2 (equation (10)) is terse; a brief derivation sketch and variable roles would help readers implement the scalar program reliably.
  - Minor typographical/formatting issues (footnote markers, reference formatting) should be cleaned. Some statements about the µ = 0 case could be made more explicit (e.g., even link functions).
  - The low-rank section defers evaluation of F; at least one concrete worked example (e.g., spiked model) would help.

Originality
- The equivalence principle for generalized LASSO under non-linear observations, with an exact asymptotic error equal to its linear counterpart with (µ, σ^2), appears new and materially advances the state of the art beyond Plan & Vershynin’s bounds and prior precise linear-only analyses.
- The general convex-regularizer treatment via the F functional broadens applicability beyond ℓ1, and the λcrit phenomenon for δ < 1 in the ℓ1 case aligns with recent linear results but now extends to non-linear links.

Significance
- Conceptually important: it reduces a wide class of non-linear single-index observation problems for generalized LASSO to a linear model with an effective SNR. This can guide algorithm choice, hyperparameter tuning, and measurement/quantizer design (as shown for q-bit quantization).
- Practically useful within its scope (Gaussian designs); the σ^2/µ^2 criterion provides a simple target for quantizer optimization and other link design problems.
- Limitations (Gaussian design, µ ≠ 0, asymptotics) restrict immediate deployment in all settings, but the theoretical insight is likely to influence follow-up work on robustness to non-Gaussian A and finite-sample guarantees.

Suggestions and questions
- Clarify when µ = 0 arises (even g) and whether any variant estimator can recover direction in those cases; relate to known 1-bit CS algorithms and results (e.g., BIHT, logistic regression, Plan–Vershynin one-bit).
- Discuss robustness to sub-Gaussian or structured designs; can the equivalence extend with modified parameters?
- Provide guidance on estimating µ and σ^2 when g is unknown (e.g., calibration via moments/Stein’s lemma), since the equivalence suggests only these two scalars matter.
- Include more experimental detail: sensitivity to λ, finite-n performance across δ, and non-Gaussian priors.
- For the low-rank case, add at least one instance where F can be computed numerically (e.g., Marchenko–Pastur plus spikes).

Pros
- Strong, precise asymptotic theory; elegant equivalence result.
- Broad coverage of convex regularizers and structural priors.
- Connects neatly to quantizer design with a practical algorithmic implication.
- Improves and unifies understanding of LASSO under link misspecification.

Cons
- Heavily relies on Gaussian design and µ ≠ 0.
- Abstract Assumption 1 and limited worked-out low-rank example.
- Limited discussion of estimating µ, σ in unknown-g settings.
- Some clarity/notation issues and missing related work on 1-bit CS.

The paper provides a substantial and novel theoretical contribution of clear relevance to NeurIPS’s theory and learning community, with practical implications for measurement design.
