### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO estimator for recovering a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. The central contribution is a powerful and elegant result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio `m/n`, the performance of the LASSO is exactly equivalent to its performance on a corresponding linear model `yi = µ*a_i^T x0 + σ*z_i`, where `z_i` are i.i.d. standard Gaussian noise components. The crucial parameters `µ` and `σ^2` are constants that depend only on the statistics of the (possibly unknown) non-linear link function `g`.

This equivalence allows the authors to leverage recent advances in the precise analysis of the LASSO (e.g., using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework) to derive asymptotically exact, deterministic expressions for the mean squared error `||x̂ - µx0||^2`. The paper provides a general formula for this error (Theorem 2.2) that holds for a wide class of convex regularizers and signal structures. This general result is then specialized to provide more explicit characterizations for important practical cases, including sparse recovery (`l1`-norm), group-sparse recovery (`l1,2`-norm), and low-rank matrix recovery (nuclear norm). Finally, the utility of the theory is demonstrated through an application to q-bit compressive sensing, where the authors show that the problem of designing an optimal quantizer simplifies to minimizing the ratio `σ^2/µ^2`.

This work significantly extends the classical result of Brillinger [5], which established a similar equivalence for unregularized least squares in a low-dimensional (`n` fixed, `m` -> inf) setting. It also sharpens the recent results of Plan and Vershynin [17], moving from non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO to asymptotically exact predictions for the more commonly used regularized LASSO.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The main result establishing the equivalence between the non-linear and an effective linear model is a fundamental contribution. It provides a simple, intuitive, and powerful lens through which to understand the behavior of the LASSO—an algorithm designed for linear models—in a non-linear setting. This is likely to be an influential result that researchers will build upon.

2.  **Novelty and Technical Quality:** The paper provides the first asymptotically *precise* performance characterization for the LASSO under a general class of non-linear measurements. Moving beyond the existing upper bounds [17] to exact formulae is a major technical advance. The analysis appears to be sound, rigorously building upon the sophisticated and modern CGMT framework. The derived formulae for sparse recovery with general non-Gaussian priors (Theorem 2.3) and for group-sparse/low-rank settings are also novel contributions in their own right, even for the linear measurement case.

3.  **Generality:** The theoretical framework is impressively general. It applies to a wide variety of convex regularizers (not just the `l1`-norm), signal structures, and non-linear link functions `g`. The fact that `g` does not need to be known by the algorithm is a key strength, highlighting the robustness of the LASSO.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, provides an excellent survey of the relevant literature, and precisely states the paper's contributions. The main results are presented clearly, and the assumptions are made explicit. The illustrative figures convincingly support the theoretical predictions.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **The `µ ≠ 0` Assumption:** The analysis is contingent on `µ = E[γg(γ)] ≠ 0`. This is a significant limitation as it excludes, for example, any even link function (`g(x) = g(-x)`), where `µ` is guaranteed to be zero. The paper acknowledges this in a footnote but does not discuss the implications. The review would be more complete with a brief discussion on what happens when `µ = 0`. Does the analysis break down entirely? Does the LASSO fail to recover any information about `x0`? Clarifying this boundary condition would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2.  **Comparison to Model-Aware Approaches:** The paper analyzes the "vanilla" LASSO, which is agnostic to the non-linearity. However, in many settings where `g` is known (e.g., binary measurements from `sign(·)`, or Poisson counts), a standard approach would be to use a Generalized Linear Model (GLM) with a loss function matched to the data (e.g., logistic or Poisson loss). It would be highly beneficial to include a discussion, and perhaps a numerical comparison, of the performance of the model-agnostic LASSO versus a model-aware GLM-based approach. This would provide valuable context for practitioners on the trade-off between the LASSO's robustness/simplicity and the potential performance gains of a correctly specified model.

3.  **Accessibility of the Main Theoretical Result:** Theorem 2.2 presents the final error as the solution to a three-variable optimization problem. While numerically tractable, its form is not immediately intuitive. The main text would benefit from a high-level sketch of the proof or some intuition on how this specific optimization problem arises from the underlying CGMT analysis. This would make the core technical result more accessible to a broader audience without needing to delve into the appendix.

4.  **Clarity of Assumption 1:** Assumption 1, concerning the convergence of the Moreau envelope, is technically dense and its scope is not fully explained. While the authors demonstrate that it holds for common regularizers, a brief discussion on what types of regularizers might *not* satisfy this assumption would be helpful for understanding the limits of the theory.

### **Decision**

This is an excellent paper presenting a highly significant, novel, and technically deep result. The central finding—that the LASSO in a non-linear setting behaves precisely as in an equivalent linear one—is both beautiful and impactful. The paper is well-written, thoroughly situated in the literature, and its claims are convincingly supported. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the discussion of boundary conditions and comparisons with alternative methods, rather than fundamental flaws in the results themselves. These are points that can be addressed in a final version to further strengthen an already outstanding contribution. The paper clearly meets the high standards of NIPS for quality, originality, and significance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
