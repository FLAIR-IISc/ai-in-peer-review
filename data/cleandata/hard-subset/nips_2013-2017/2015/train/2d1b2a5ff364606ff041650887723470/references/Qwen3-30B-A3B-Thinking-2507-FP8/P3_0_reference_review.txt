

# Peer Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending the precise analysis of the Generalized LASSO from linear to non-linear measurement models. The authors establish that for non-linear measurements of the form $y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)$, the estimation error of the LASSO solution behaves asymptotically as if the measurements were linear with scaling factor $\mu$ and noise variance $\sigma^2$, where $\mu = \mathbb{E}[\gamma g(\gamma)]$ and $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[(g(\gamma)-\mu\gamma)^2]$ for $\gamma \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. This generalizes Brillinger's early result [5] and Plan and Vershynin's recent work [17], providing precise error formulas rather than loose upper bounds.

The paper's key technical contribution is Theorem 2.1, which establishes the equivalence between non-linear and appropriately scaled linear measurements, and Theorem 2.2, which provides a precise error formula that depends on the link function $g$ (through $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$), the signal structure (through the regularizer $f$), and measurement parameters. The authors specialize their results to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery, and apply them to q-bit compressive sensing, showing how to optimize quantization thresholds.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Depth and Rigor**: The paper builds on recent advances in LASSO analysis using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework [19] to provide a precise asymptotic characterization. This is a substantial improvement over Plan and Vershynin's [17] upper bounds, which lacked precision.

2. **Novel Insight**: The key insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled and noisy linear measurements with parameters $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ is both elegant and powerful. It provides a unifying framework for understanding LASSO performance across different measurement models.

3. **Comprehensive Specialization**: The paper carefully specializes their general results to important cases (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank recovery) and provides concrete error formulas for each, demonstrating the practical applicability of their framework.

4. **Practical Application**: The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) is particularly strong, showing how their theory can guide the design of optimal quantization schemes. The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm for optimizing quantization thresholds is insightful.

5. **Clear Relation to Prior Work**: The paper provides an excellent discussion of how their results relate to and extend existing literature, particularly [5, 17] and the recent precise analysis of LASSO with linear measurements [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 21].

## Weaknesses

1. **Measurement Matrix Restriction**: The paper assumes i.i.d. Gaussian measurement entries throughout. While this is standard for theoretical analysis, it would be beneficial to discuss whether the results extend to other measurement ensembles (e.g., sub-Gaussian, structured matrices) or if there are known counterexamples where the results fail.

2. **Link Function Knowledge Assumption**: The analysis assumes knowledge of $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$, which requires knowledge of the link function $g$. In some applications (e.g., when $g$ is unknown), this might not be feasible, and the authors should discuss how to estimate these parameters from data.

3. **Unit Norm Assumption**: The assumption that $\|x_0\|_2 = 1$ (Footnote 4) is a significant simplification. While the authors note it can be handled by redefining $g$, it's worth discussing how this affects practical implementation, particularly when $x_0$ is not normalized in the data.

4. **Computational Complexity**: While the error formula in Theorem 2.2 involves only three scalar variables, computing the function $F(c_1, c_2, c_3)$ for general regularizers $f$ could be challenging. The paper should discuss the computational complexity of evaluating $F$ for the cases they consider.

5. **Finite-Sample Behavior**: The asymptotic analysis is rigorous, but the paper lacks discussion of the finite-sample behavior. It would be helpful to include numerical experiments showing how quickly the asymptotic predictions converge for moderate $n$.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in understanding the behavior of the LASSO under non-linear measurements. The results are technically sound, the analysis is rigorous, and the insights are valuable for both theoretical understanding and practical applications in signal processing and statistics. The paper successfully extends recent precise analysis of the LASSO with linear measurements to the non-linear case, providing a unifying framework that has important implications for a wide range of problems.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that will be of interest to the NeurIPS community. The results are novel, well-motivated, and rigorously established. The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the practical relevance of the theory. While the paper has some limitations (e.g., the Gaussian measurement assumption), these are common in theoretical analyses and do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

The paper advances the state of the art in understanding structured signal recovery under non-linear measurements, providing precise error formulas that were previously unavailable. This work has the potential to influence both theoretical and applied research in compressed sensing, high-dimensional statistics, and signal processing.

## Specific Suggestions

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the results might extend to non-Gaussian measurement matrices, perhaps citing recent work on structured matrices [26].

2. Clarify the assumption about the link function $g$ - specify what "generically random" means in this context.

3. Include a short discussion of the finite-sample behavior in the experimental section or as a future work direction.

4. For the unit norm assumption, explicitly state how to handle the case where $\|x_0\|_2 \neq 1$ in practice.

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of structured signal recovery. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings.
