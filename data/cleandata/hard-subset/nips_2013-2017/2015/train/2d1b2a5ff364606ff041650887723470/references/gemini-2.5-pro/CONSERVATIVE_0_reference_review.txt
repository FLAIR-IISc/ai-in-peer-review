**Summary**

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to non-linear measurement models of the single-index form, `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)`. The central and striking contribution is the claim that, in a high-dimensional asymptotic regime (where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` go to infinity with a fixed ratio), the performance of the LASSO is equivalent to its performance on a corresponding *linear* model with additive Gaussian noise. The parameters of this equivalent linear model—a signal scaling factor `μ` and a noise variance `σ^2`—are shown to depend only on the statistical properties of the non-linear link function `g`. The authors leverage this equivalence to derive precise, deterministic formulas for the asymptotic mean-squared error (MSE) for various regularizers, including the `l1`-norm (sparse recovery), `l1,2`-norm (group-sparse recovery), and the nuclear norm (low-rank matrix recovery). The analysis builds upon the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, extending recent work on the precise analysis of LASSO with linear measurements to this more general non-linear setting, and making prior results on this problem (e.g., by Plan and Vershynin) exact rather than providing upper bounds.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Elegance and Significance:** The main result, establishing an equivalence between the non-linear and a specific linear measurement model for LASSO, is a powerful and elegant theoretical statement. It provides a clean, intuitive, and unifying framework for understanding how a standard linear method behaves in the face of a broad class of non-linearities. This is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Precise Performance Characterization:** The paper moves beyond the typical order-wise bounds found in high-dimensional statistics to provide asymptotically exact formulas for the estimation error. The resulting expressions, while complex, are deterministic and depend on the solution of a low-dimensional convex program, allowing for precise predictions of performance as a function of key problem parameters (`m/n`, `λ`, signal structure, and the `μ`, `σ^2` of the link function).

3.  **Generality:** The theoretical framework is presented for general convex regularizers and a wide class of link functions, with specific, non-trivial applications worked out for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery. This demonstrates the broad applicability of the core theoretical result.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Restrictive and Idealized Assumptions:** The analysis relies critically on the assumption that the measurement matrix `A` consists of i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a common assumption in random matrix theory and the CGMT literature, but it is a strong idealization that limits the direct applicability of the results to practical scenarios, where measurement matrices are often structured (e.g., Fourier, Hadamard) or have heavy-tailed entries. The paper does not discuss the robustness of its results to deviations from this assumption.

2.  **Focus on Asymptotic Regime:** The results are purely asymptotic. While the provided simulations show a good match between theory and practice for the chosen problem sizes, the paper offers no theoretical guarantees or empirical investigation into the pre-asymptotic behavior or the rate of convergence to the predicted limits. For practitioners, it remains unclear how large the problem dimensions must be for these formulas to be reliable, which is a crucial piece of information for any potential application.

3.  **Lack of Validation Against Competing Methods:** The paper's core message is that the standard LASSO is surprisingly effective for non-linear measurements, and its performance is now precisely predictable. However, this claim is not substantiated by comparing LASSO's performance to other methods specifically designed for such problems (e.g., specialized algorithms for 1-bit compressed sensing, or methods for generalized linear models). The experiments only serve as an internal consistency check—verifying that the derived formulas match the empirical performance of LASSO itself. To establish the *significance* of this result, one must demonstrate that LASSO is not just predictable but also competitive with or superior to established, specialized alternatives. Without this comparison, the practical utility of the analysis remains an open question.

4.  **Limited Scope of the Application:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing in Section 2.3 is an interesting theoretical consequence of the main result, showing that the complex problem of optimizing quantizer design can be simplified. However, this section stops short of being a compelling application. It presents an algorithm for optimizing the quantizer parameters based on the theory but does not include any experiments to show that this optimization actually leads to improved signal recovery compared to standard or heuristic quantizer designs.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper is a technically impressive piece of theoretical work. The equivalence principle it establishes is a beautiful result that deepens our understanding of the LASSO algorithm. The mathematical rigor appears to be high, and the generalization from linear to non-linear measurements within the precise analysis framework is a solid contribution.

However, as a submission to NeurIPS, the paper's weaknesses are significant. The reliance on idealized assumptions (Gaussian matrix, asymptotic regime) without a discussion of their limitations is a major concern for a field that values practical relevance. More importantly, the work evaluates its own theory in a vacuum, failing to benchmark the performance of the analyzed method (LASSO) against any relevant baselines. While the analysis is novel, the algorithm itself is not. The paper makes a strong, implicit claim about the utility of an established method in a new domain, but this claim is not supported with the necessary comparative evidence.

The work is a strong contribution to the theory of high-dimensional statistics, but its direct significance to the machine learning community is not clearly demonstrated. I am therefore cautious about recommending its acceptance in its current form. A stronger version of this paper would include a discussion on the limitations of the assumptions and, crucially, an empirical section comparing LASSO's performance to specialized methods for non-linear models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly elegant, novel, and significant theoretical result.
*   Provides the first asymptotically exact performance analysis for LASSO with general non-linear measurements.
*   The framework is general and applicable to many popular structured signal models.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Relies on strong, idealized assumptions (i.i.d. Gaussian matrix) that limit practical relevance.
*   Results are purely asymptotic, with no analysis of finite-sample performance.
*   Critically lacks any empirical comparison to alternative methods designed for non-linear models, making it impossible to judge the practical significance of the analyzed method.
