

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary
This paper extends the analysis of the Generalized LASSO to non-linear measurement models of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0). The authors claim that under appropriate scaling and noise modeling, the LASSO with non-linear measurements behaves asymptotically identical to the LASSO with linear measurements. They build on previous work by Brillinger [5] and Plan and Vershynin [17], claiming to provide the first precise asymptotic error analysis for LASSO with non-linear measurements. The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 2.1, which states that the error under non-linear measurements matches the error under appropriately scaled linear measurements.

## Strengths
The paper makes a valuable contribution by formalizing the relationship between non-linear and linear measurement models for structured signal recovery. The theoretical framework extends previous results from Plan and Vershynin [17] by providing precise asymptotic error predictions rather than loose upper bounds. The paper demonstrates the applicability of these results to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems, and provides an interesting application to q-bit compressive sensing.

## Weaknesses
The paper's main weakness is its lack of empirical validation. The authors claim that "Figure 1 serves as an illustration; the error with non-linear measurements matches well with the error of the corresponding linear ones," but the absence of concrete experimental results in the main text is problematic. In a field like NIPS where empirical validation is paramount, this is a significant omission. The paper makes strong theoretical claims but provides no evidence that the asymptotic results hold in practical, finite-dimensional settings.

The novelty claim is overstated. The paper asserts that "to the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first to derive asymptotically precise results on the performance of any LASSO-type program" for non-linear measurements. However, Plan and Vershynin [17] already provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained version of the LASSO under similar measurement models. The current paper's main contribution is to provide precise error expressions rather than just upper bounds, but this is a refinement rather than a fundamental new insight.

The paper also fails to address the practical question of how to estimate μ and σ² in real-world applications. The theory assumes knowledge of these parameters, but the paper provides no guidance on how to estimate them from data. For the q-bit compressive sensing application, the authors claim to have derived an algorithm for optimal quantization levels, but they only show that Lloyd-Max algorithm converges to stationary points of the objective, without providing evidence that this actually improves performance in practice.

The theoretical assumptions are quite restrictive. The paper assumes i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, a specific asymptotic regime (m/n = δ), and unit norm signals. While these are standard in the literature, the authors should acknowledge how these assumptions limit the practical applicability of their results.

## Critical Assessment
As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's theoretical contribution somewhat incremental rather than transformative. The main insight—that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements with appropriate noise—has been suggested by earlier work (Brillinger [5] for LS, Plan and Vershynin [17] for constrained LASSO). The paper provides a more precise characterization, but this precision comes at the cost of significant theoretical complexity.

The paper's primary weakness is its lack of empirical validation. In a field that values both theoretical rigor and practical relevance, the absence of concrete experiments demonstrating the claimed equivalence in finite-dimensional settings is a serious shortcoming. The paper would be much stronger if it included comparisons between the theoretical predictions and actual LASSO performance on synthetic and real datasets.

The authors also overstate the novelty of their results. While the precise error formulae for general regularizers may be new, the core insight that non-linear measurements can be mapped to linear measurements with scaling is not novel.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the analysis of LASSO under non-linear measurements, it lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its claims. The theoretical results are incremental rather than transformative, and the paper fails to address the practical question of how to estimate the scaling parameters in real-world applications. The absence of concrete experiments is particularly problematic for a paper making strong claims about the equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models.

For future work, the authors should provide extensive empirical validation demonstrating that their theoretical results hold in practical, finite-dimensional settings. They should also address the practical issue of estimating μ and σ² from data, and provide a clearer explanation of how their approach differs from and improves upon the results of Plan and Vershynin [17].
