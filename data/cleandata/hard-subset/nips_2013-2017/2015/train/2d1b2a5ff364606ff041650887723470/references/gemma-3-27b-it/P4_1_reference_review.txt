This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements. The core contribution is demonstrating that, surprisingly, the non-linear measurement problem can be effectively reduced to a scaled and noisy linear measurement problem, allowing the use of existing, well-developed theoretical tools for linear models to analyze the non-linear case. The authors derive precise asymptotic error bounds, capturing the influence of the link function, signal structure, regularizer, and regularization parameter. Several specific cases, including sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, and q-bit compressive sensing, are explored and results are validated with simulations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core result – the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements – is a significant and potentially impactful finding. It provides a novel perspective on a common problem and justifies the application of tools developed for linear models to non-linear settings.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper employs rigorous mathematical analysis, building upon existing literature on the Generalized LASSO and related areas. The derivations appear careful and well-motivated.  The connection to Brillinger’s earlier work is appropriately highlighted.
*   **Generality:** The framework is quite general, accommodating a wide range of link functions and regularizers.  The authors appropriately address the case where the link function is unknown.
*   **Completeness:** The paper provides a comprehensive treatment of the topic, including a detailed analysis, specific applications, and illustrative examples.  The inclusion of an algorithm for optimal quantization is a practical addition.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the key ideas are clearly presented. The connection to related work is explicitly discussed.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumption 1:** The necessity and broad applicability of Assumption 1 (regarding the limit of the Fenchel conjugate) aren’t fully explained. While the authors acknowledge its importance, providing more intuition or examples of situations where it might *not* hold would strengthen the presentation.  It feels somewhat opaque and could be a barrier to understanding for readers less familiar with convex analysis.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention the numerical evaluation of the optimization problem in Theorem 2.2, a more detailed discussion of its computational complexity, or potential approximations, would be valuable.  The practical implications of this optimization in high-dimensional settings are not fully explored.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The provided figures primarily demonstrate the accuracy of the theoretical predictions. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation, comparing the performance of the LASSO with other methods specifically designed for non-linear measurements, would strengthen the practical significance of the work.
*   **Overly Dense Presentation:** While generally clear, some sections, especially those detailing the specific applications (e.g., sparse recovery), are quite dense and require careful reading.  Breaking down complex derivations into smaller steps could improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result with potentially broad implications for signal processing and statistical estimation.
*   The analysis is rigorous and builds upon a strong foundation of existing literature.
*   The generality of the framework and the inclusion of practical applications add to the value of the work.
*   The connection to existing analysis of linear LASSO provides a clear path for leveraging existing results and tools.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The somewhat restrictive Assumption 1 and the lack of detailed discussion regarding its practical limitations.
*   The limited empirical evaluation and the lack of comparison with alternative methods.
*   The potential computational complexity of the optimization problem arising from the theoretical results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The equivalence result is insightful and the derived error bounds are precise and informative. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract substantially from the overall quality of the work. The paper is well-suited for presentation at NIPS and has the potential to stimulate further research in this area.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly clarifying the role and limitations of Assumption 1 and expanding the empirical evaluation.
