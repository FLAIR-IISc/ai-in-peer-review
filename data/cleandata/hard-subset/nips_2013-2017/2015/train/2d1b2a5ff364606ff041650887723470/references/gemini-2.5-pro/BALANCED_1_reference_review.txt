### Paper Summary

This paper studies the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimating a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`. The central contribution is a powerful and elegant theoretical result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio `m/n`, the performance of the LASSO is asymptotically equivalent to its performance on an associated *linear* measurement model `y_lin = µ A x0 + σ z`, where `z` is standard Gaussian noise. The scaling factor `µ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the properties of the link function `g`.

This equivalence allows the authors to leverage the powerful Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, previously used for precise analysis of LASSO with linear measurements, to the non-linear setting. The authors derive a general and asymptotically exact formula for the mean squared error `||x̂ - µx0||^2` as the solution to a simple, three-variable scalar optimization problem. This general result is then specialized to important practical cases including sparse recovery (`l1` norm), group-sparse recovery (`l1,2` norm), and low-rank matrix recovery (nuclear norm). The theoretical predictions are shown to be remarkably accurate through numerical simulations. Finally, the utility of the theory is demonstrated in an application to q-bit compressive sensing, where the complex problem of designing optimal quantizers is simplified to optimizing the ratio `σ^2/µ^2`.

This work builds upon and significantly extends two main lines of research. First, it generalizes a classical result by Brillinger (1982) for un-regularized least squares from the `n`-fixed regime to the modern high-dimensional setting, and for the regularized LASSO. Second, it refines recent work by Plan and Vershynin (2015), which provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO variant, by delivering precise asymptotic characterizations for the more common regularized LASSO, thereby making the roles of `µ` and `σ^2` exact rather than just indicative.

### Strengths

1.  **Fundamental and Elegant Main Result:** The core finding that, for the purpose of LASSO estimation, a non-linear measurement model is asymptotically equivalent to a simple, noisy linear one (Theorem 2.1) is a significant conceptual breakthrough. It provides a clear and powerful intuition for why a linear model-based estimator like LASSO can be effective even under severe non-linearities.

2.  **Precision and Generality:** The paper moves beyond the upper bounds of previous work to provide asymptotically exact predictions for the estimation error. The framework is highly general, accommodating a wide class of convex regularizers (not just the `l1`-norm), signal structures, and link functions. This makes the results broadly applicable.

3.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The paper is technically very strong. The application of the sophisticated CGMT framework to this problem is novel and expertly executed. The claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs (deferred to the appendix) and the modeling assumptions are clearly stated.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation and context. The main results are stated clearly and then instantiated with concrete examples (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) that make the general theory accessible and highlight its implications. The figures provide compelling empirical validation of the theoretical formulae.

5.  **Practical Significance:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing is a compelling demonstration of the theory's value. It shows how the theoretical insights can transform a seemingly intractable design problem (optimizing quantizer thresholds and levels for minimal final reconstruction error) into a much simpler and decoupled optimization problem (minimizing `σ^2/µ^2`).

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, there are a few areas where further clarification or discussion could strengthen it.

1.  **Discussion of Assumptions:** The analysis relies on i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices and a specific high-dimensional asymptotic limit. These are standard assumptions in this line of work but are nevertheless idealizations. A brief discussion on the potential robustness of the results to departures from Gaussianity (e.g., for sub-gaussian matrices) or the applicability of the asymptotic predictions to finite-dimensional problems would be valuable for the reader.

2.  **Intuition for Assumption 1:** Assumption 1 is a key technical condition required for the main theorems. While the authors show it holds for several important regularizers, it would be helpful to provide the reader with more intuition. What does this assumption mean? Are there common regularizers for which it might fail? Clarifying whether this is primarily a proof artifact of the CGMT method or a more fundamental requirement would be beneficial.

3.  **Details on the Low-Rank Case:** Section 2.2.4 on low-rank matrix recovery is less developed than the other examples. The authors state that the results apply if the empirical singular value distribution converges, but defer details to future work. The paper would be stronger if a specific, non-trivial example of a matrix `X0` for which this condition holds could be analyzed, solidifying this part of the contribution.

4.  **The Case of `µ=0`:** The analysis requires `µ ≠ 0`, which excludes, for example, even link functions. This is an important limitation mentioned in Footnote 3. It would be valuable to add a brief comment in the main text about this case. Is recovery impossible with LASSO, or would an entirely different analysis be needed? For instance, perhaps a higher-order moment would play the role of `µ`.

5.  **Generalization to Unknown `||x0||`:** Footnote 4 notes that the results can be generalized to `||x0|| ≠ 1`. However, this involves absorbing `||x0||` into the link function, which would make `µ` and `σ` dependent on the norm of the unknown signal. This seems to complicate the interpretation, as the parameters of the "equivalent" linear model are now tied to the unknown `x0`. A short discussion of the implications of this would be helpful.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a fundamental, original, and significant contribution to the understanding of structured signal recovery from non-linear measurements. The central result is both surprising and insightful, and the technical analysis is rigorous and state-of-the-art. The paper is clearly written, and its theoretical claims are convincingly supported by simulations. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily suggestions for improving the discussion and completeness. This work is likely to be highly influential and to inspire follow-up research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
