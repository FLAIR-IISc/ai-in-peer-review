## NIPS Review: LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems involving non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(aTi x0)`. Building on recent work by Plan & Vershynin (2015) and extending Brillinger's (1980s) earlier findings, the authors demonstrate a remarkable equivalence: under Gaussian measurements, the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of a LASSO applied to scaled and noisy *linear* measurements.  This equivalence is captured by two parameters, μ and σ², which characterize the link function *g*.  The paper provides precise error analysis, extending previous results from the linear case and offering novel error formulas for various structured signals (sparse, low-rank, group-sparse). A specific application to q-bit compressive sensing is also presented, demonstrating how the theoretical results can guide the optimization of quantization levels and thresholds.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Result:** The core finding – the equivalence between non-linear and appropriately scaled linear measurements for LASSO performance – is a powerful and insightful result. It greatly simplifies analysis of non-linear measurement scenarios.
* **Precise Asymptotic Analysis:** The paper goes beyond bounding error, delivering *precise* asymptotic expressions for the error, building upon recent advances in the field (e.g., CGMT framework, AMP). This is a major contribution.
* **Generality & Broad Applicability:** The work applies to a broad class of non-linear link functions *g* and a variety of regularizers *f* capturing different signal structures. The results are presented in a general framework.
* **Connections to Existing Literature:** The paper carefully contextualizes its contribution within the broader landscape of single-index models, compressive sensing, and structured sparsity.  It appropriately references related works.
* **Practical Application:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing showcases the practical relevance of the theoretical findings and provides a concrete example of how they can be used to optimize system parameters.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally clearly written and logically organized, although some sections (especially relating to the q-bit case) could benefit from further streamlining.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption of i.i.d. Gaussian measurement vectors, while common, limits the applicability of the results. Exploring the robustness to deviations from this assumption would be valuable.
* **Assumption 1:** The Assumption 1 concerning the limit of the Fenchel’s conjugate of *f* might be strong for some regularizers. More discussion of its applicability and potential relaxation would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention the complexity of solving the optimization program in (10) for the general case, more discussion on practical methods for approximation or efficient computation would be helpful.
* **q-bit Sensing Section:** While interesting, the section on q-bit sensing feels somewhat rushed and the connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is presented a bit abruptly. More detailed explanation and perhaps a simple experiment validating the algorithm’s performance would strengthen this section.
* **Limited Numerical Validation:** While Figures 1 and 2 are provided, the numerical validation could be more comprehensive, showcasing the results across a wider range of parameters and link functions.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and provides a rigorous analysis. The claims are well-supported by mathematical derivations and the connection to existing results is clear. It is a complete piece of work, presenting a substantial contribution to the field. (Strongly Positive)
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, though the mathematical notation is dense and requires careful attention. The organization is logical, but some sections (e.g., q-bit sensing) could be more streamlined. (Positive)
* **Originality:** The core result – the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements – is novel and represents a significant advance. The extension of existing error analysis techniques to the non-linear case is also original. (Strongly Positive)
* **Significance:** The results are highly significant as they provide a fundamental understanding of the behavior of the LASSO in non-linear measurement scenarios. This has broad implications for a variety of applications, including compressive sensing, signal processing, and statistical inference.  Researchers and practitioners will likely build upon these findings. (Strongly Positive)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Groundbreaking theoretical result with strong mathematical backing.
* Broad applicability to various signal structures and non-linear measurements.
* Clear connection to existing work and a well-defined contribution.
* Potential for significant impact on research and practical applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on Gaussian measurements is a limitation.
* Assumption 1 might be restrictive for some regularizers.
* The q-bit sensing section could benefit from more detail and validation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The results are novel, well-supported, and have the potential to impact a wide range of research areas.  While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract from the overall quality and importance of the work. I **strongly recommend acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above. In particular, I suggest adding a discussion of the robustness of the results to deviations from the Gaussian measurement assumption and providing more detail regarding the q-bit sensing application.
