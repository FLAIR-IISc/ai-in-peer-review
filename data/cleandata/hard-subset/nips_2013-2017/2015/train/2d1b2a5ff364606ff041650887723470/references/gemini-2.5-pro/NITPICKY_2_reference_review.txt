**Summary**

This paper provides a precise asymptotic analysis of the Generalized LASSO algorithm for structured signal recovery from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)`. The central contribution is an elegant proof that, in the high-dimensional limit with i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimator is equivalent to that of an estimator in a corresponding linear model with additive Gaussian noise. The scaling factor (`µ`) and effective noise variance (`σ^2`) of this equivalent linear model are determined by the first and second moments of the link function `g`. This key result allows the authors to leverage recent advances in the precise analysis of the linear LASSO (based on the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem) to derive exact asymptotic error formulas for the non-linear case. The general formula is specialized for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank signals. The utility of the theory is demonstrated through an application to q-bit compressive sensing, where it is used to reframe the problem of designing optimal quantizers as a simpler problem of minimizing an effective signal-to-noise ratio.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The main result, establishing an exact asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear measurement model and a canonical linear one, is both powerful and elegant. It provides a deep insight into why the standard LASSO remains effective even when its underlying linear model assumption is violated. This significantly extends prior work by Brillinger to the high-dimensional setting and provides an exact characterization that sharpens the upper bounds of Plan and Vershynin.

2.  **Generality and Precision:** The framework is quite general, applicable to a wide class of convex regularizers, signal structures, and link functions. The derived error predictions (e.g., Theorem 2.2 and 2.3) are not just bounds but precise asymptotic formulas, which represent the state of the art in this line of theoretical research.

3.  **Practical Relevance:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing is a compelling demonstration of the theory's utility. By reducing the complex problem of optimizing quantization thresholds and levels to minimizing the `σ^2/µ^2` ratio, the paper provides a concrete and actionable design principle.

**Weaknesses**

While the core theoretical ideas are strong, the paper is undermined by a significant number of flaws in its presentation, clarity, and completeness.

1.  **Formatting and Presentation Errors:** The manuscript is replete with typographical and formatting errors that detract from its professionalism. Examples include "mesaurements" (Sec 1.1), "expecation" (Eq. 3), "uknown" and "eventhough" (Footnote 4), "is principle is possible" (Sec 1.2), and multiple broken references in the bibliography (e.g., [1]). The footnote numbering restarts on each page, which is non-standard and confusing. Such a high density of minor errors suggests a lack of careful proofreading.

2.  **Inconsistent and Ambiguous Notation:**
    *   The definition of the LASSO estimator is inconsistent. Equation (2) presents the standard form, while Equation (9) introduces a `1/√n` scaling factor in the objective function without explanation. This changes the interpretation of the regularization parameter `λ` and should be clarified.
    *   The modeling of the signal `x0` in Section 2.1 is confusing. It states `x0 = x0/‖x0‖2` where `x0` is sampled from a density `px0`. This is circular. It then claims `x0` is "deterministically of unit Euclidean-norm" while also being sampled from a density, which is contradictory for a continuous random vector in `R^n`. The authors likely mean that the direction is random on the unit sphere, but this critical modeling assumption is stated imprecisely.

3.  **Lack of Clarity on Key Technical Points:**
    *   **Assumption 1:** This assumption is central to the main theorems but is presented in a highly abstract manner. The paper asserts it can be "naturally met" but provides little intuition beyond a brief calculation for the `l1`-norm. A more thorough discussion of the classes of regularizers that satisfy this condition is needed to understand the scope of the results.
    *   **Theorem 2.1:** The statement `‖x̂− µx0‖2^2 = ‖x̂lin − µx0‖2^2` is claimed to hold "with probability one". It is unclear if this means the two quantities (which are random variables) are equal almost surely for finite `n`, or if their *limits* as `n→∞` are equal almost surely. The latter is more plausible but the phrasing is ambiguous.
    *   **Theorem 2.2:** The optimization program in (10) is presented without justification for its convexity or intuition for the optimization variables. While familiar to experts in this specific literature, it is opaque to a broader audience.

4.  **Incomplete Sections:**
    *   The section on **Low-rank Matrix Recovery (2.2.4)** is particularly weak. It relies on an unstated assumption about the convergence of an empirical singular value distribution and concludes with "We leave the details and the problem of (numerically) evaluating F for future work." This is not acceptable for a conference publication, which should present a complete piece of work.
    *   The application to **q-bit Compressive Sensing (2.3.3)** makes a strong, non-obvious claim that the Lloyd-Max algorithm finds stationary points of the `σ^2/µ^2` objective, with the proof deferred to an appendix. This central claim requires at least a proof sketch or strong justification in the main body of the paper.

5.  **Overlooked Limitations:** The entire analysis is predicated on the measurement matrix `A` having i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a very strong assumption that is critical for the CGMT framework. The paper mentions it once and then proceeds "without further reference," failing to discuss this as a major limitation or comment on the significant challenges of extending the results to other matrix ensembles.

**Recommendation**

I recommend **rejection** for this paper.

The core result is undoubtedly novel and significant. However, the manuscript in its current form does not meet the high standards of NIPS. The work is marred by a pervasive lack of polish, from numerous typos to imprecise technical statements. Crucially, key sections feel rushed and incomplete (particularly the low-rank recovery analysis), and important claims are not adequately substantiated within the paper itself. While the ideas are of high quality, the execution is sloppy. A thorough revision is required to fix the notational inconsistencies, clarify the technical assumptions, complete the underdeveloped analyses, and meticulously proofread the text. The paper has the potential to be excellent, but it is not there yet.
