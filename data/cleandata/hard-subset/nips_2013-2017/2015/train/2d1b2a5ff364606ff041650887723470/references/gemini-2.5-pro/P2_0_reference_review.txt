### **Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"**

#### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`. While the standard Generalized LASSO is designed for linear models, the authors analyze its performance in this non-linear setting. The central and striking contribution is a theorem establishing that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit (where `m, n -> infinity` with `m/n -> delta`), the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimator is exactly equivalent to the error it would achieve on a corresponding *linear* measurement model: `y_lin = mu * A * x0 + sigma * z`. The scaling factor `mu` and effective noise variance `sigma^2` are determined solely by the properties of the (possibly unknown) link function `g`.

Building on this equivalence, the paper provides a precise, deterministic formula for the asymptotic estimation error, expressed as the solution to a low-dimensional scalar optimization problem. This general result is then specialized to important cases of structured signals, including sparse (l1-norm), group-sparse (l1,2-norm), and low-rank (nuclear norm) recovery. Finally, the practical utility of the theory is demonstrated through an application to optimizing the thresholds and levels in q-bit compressive sensing.

The work connects and extends two major lines of research: the classical statistics of single-index models (e.g., Brillinger, 1982) and the modern, precise high-dimensional analysis of regularized estimators (e.g., using AMP or CGMT frameworks).

---

#### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of very high technical quality.

*   **Soundness:** The theoretical claims are strong, precise, and appear to be rigorously derived. The use of a high-dimensional asymptotic framework is standard for this type of sharp analysis, and the assumptions (Gaussian matrix `A`, properties of `x0` and the regularizer `f`) are clearly articulated. The main result, Theorem 2.1, is a complete and powerful statement of equivalence. Theorem 2.2 provides a concrete, computable prediction for the error.
*   **Completeness:** This is a complete piece of work. It establishes a general principle, provides a precise analytical formula, and then validates this theory across several important practical instances with numerical simulations that show excellent agreement. The application to quantizer design further underscores the maturity of the ideas.
*   **Evaluation:** The authors are careful in their evaluation. The experimental results in Figures 1 and 2 are not just qualitative but provide compelling quantitative evidence for the accuracy of their theoretical predictions across different signal structures, link functions, and problem parameters.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

*   **Motivation:** The introduction provides excellent context, clearly motivating the problem and situating the work with respect to key prior results by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin. The distinction between this work (asymptotically exact predictions) and prior work (asymptotic consistency or non-asymptotic bounds with loose constants) is made effectively.
*   **Organization:** The flow is logical: from the general problem setup to the main equivalence theorem, then to the precise error formula, and finally to specific instantiations and applications. This structure makes a complex theoretical result highly accessible.
*   **Readability:** The central message—"non-linear measurements are equivalent to scaled and noisy linear measurements for the LASSO"—is simple, elegant, and memorable. This narrative clarity is a significant strength.

**Originality:** The work is highly original and represents a significant conceptual advance.

*   **Novelty:** While the analytical tools (likely related to the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem) have been developed in recent years for linear models, their application to establish this general equivalence principle for non-linear models is novel and impactful. It is not merely an incremental extension.
*   **Relation to Prior Work:** The authors do an outstanding job of relating their contributions to previous work. They correctly frame their result as a high-dimensional, regularized generalization of Brillinger's classical result. Furthermore, they significantly sharpen the recent bounds of Plan and Vershynin by providing exact characterizations instead of upper bounds with unspecified constants.
*   **New Results:** The paper notes that some of its specialized results (e.g., for general regularizers beyond the l1-norm even in the linear case) are novel in their own right, which adds to the paper's contribution.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial for both theorists and practitioners in machine learning, signal processing, and statistics.

*   **Impact on Theory:** The paper provides a powerful "reduction" tool. It allows a vast and growing body of sophisticated, precise analytical results for the linear LASSO to be immediately transferred to a wide class of non-linear measurement models. This will simplify future analyses of such problems immensely. Instead of re-deriving complex results for each new non-linearity, researchers can simply compute the corresponding `mu` and `sigma` and plug them into existing formulas.
*   **Impact on Practice:** The results have direct practical implications. The q-bit quantization example is excellent: it transforms a difficult, simulation-heavy problem of optimizing quantizer parameters into a much simpler problem of minimizing the ratio `sigma^2 / mu^2`. This provides a clear, principled methodology for system design.
*   **Advancement:** This work demonstrably advances the state of the art. It moves the understanding of LASSO with non-linear measurements from loose bounds to precise, verifiable predictions, offering a much deeper and more accurate understanding of the algorithm's behavior.

#### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** The core equivalence result (Theorem 2.1) is an elegant, fundamental, and surprising contribution that simplifies the understanding of a complex problem.
2.  **Technical Excellence:** The paper is technically deep, providing precise, state-of-the-art analysis that is rigorously validated by experiments.
3.  **High Impact:** The findings are significant for researchers in multiple fields and have clear practical applications, such as in the design of quantized sensing systems.
4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is a model of clear scientific communication, making a sophisticated topic accessible.
5.  **Novelty:** The work significantly extends and sharpens key results in both classical statistics and modern high-dimensional analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The reliance on an asymptotic framework means the results are not guaranteed to hold for small `m` and `n`, though the simulations suggest the predictions are accurate even for moderate dimensions. This is a standard and well-understood limitation of this line of work, not a fundamental flaw.
*   The proofs are deferred to an appendix, which is standard practice but means a full verification of the claims relies on supplementary material.

---

#### **Overall Recommendation**

This is an outstanding paper that presents a foundational result with significant theoretical and practical implications. It is technically deep, highly original, and exceptionally well-written. It resolves a key question about the behavior of a widely used algorithm in a non-standard setting by revealing a simple and beautiful underlying structure. The contribution is of the highest caliber and will undoubtedly be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
