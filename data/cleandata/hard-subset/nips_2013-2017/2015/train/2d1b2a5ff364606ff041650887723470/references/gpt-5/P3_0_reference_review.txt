Summary
The paper studies generalized LASSO with non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) under i.i.d. Gaussian design A. It proves a striking equivalence: asymptotically, for convex regularizers f and fixed λ, the mean-squared error of LASSO with such non-linear measurements is exactly the same as that of LASSO under a corresponding linear model with an effective gain and noise, y_lin = µ A x0 + σ z, where µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Building on CGMT-type techniques, the authors provide a general three-scalar convex optimization that yields the precise asymptotic error (Theorem 2.2), and work out explicit specializations for sparse signals with ℓ1 regularization (Theorem 2.3), as well as formulations for group-sparse and low-rank recovery. An application to q-bit quantized compressed sensing follows: the equivalence reduces quantizer design to minimizing σ^2/µ^2 and motivates using Lloyd–Max to find stationary points of that objective.

Relation to prior work
- Extends Brillinger’s classical LS result for fixed n, m→∞ to proportional-growth asymptotics and, crucially, beyond LS to generalized LASSO.
- Sharpen Plan and Vershynin’s non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO with non-linear links by giving asymptotically exact error expressions for the regularized LASSO, elucidating the exact role of µ and σ^2.
- Builds on precise LASSO analyses under linear measurements via AMP/CGMT (Bayati-Montanari; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.), generalizing to broad classes of convex regularizers and to non-linear links.
- Connects to one-bit and quantized compressed sensing by turning non-linearities into effective SNR parameters.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean, rigorous reduction of LASSO under non-linear single-index links to a linear model with effective gain/noise that depend only on g through µ and σ^2.
- Technical depth: a precise error characterization via a low-dimensional convex program with clear separation of roles: δ, λ, g (through µ, σ^2) and the structure/regularizer (through an envelope functional F).
- Generality: covers many convex regularizers; provides explicit derivations for ℓ1 (and group ℓ1,2), and a roadmap for nuclear norm.
- Significance: unifies and strengthens several threads (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin; precise LASSO asymptotics), and gives actionable guidance for quantizer design.
- The specialized sparse recovery result (Theorem 2.3) is informative, recovers known Gaussian-sparse cases, and elucidates the λcrit phase transition for δ<1.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Assumption 1 and F: The key functional F is defined via the limit of the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate. While natural for CGMT analyses, the assumption is opaque. For ℓ1 the dependence on c3 seems to vanish in the derivation, yet F(c1,c2,c3) is passed with c3 arguments in (10). Please reconcile the scaling/normalization so readers can compute F for common f without ambiguity. For nuclear norm, the section acknowledges leaving details and numerical evaluation to future work; given the general claims, at least one concrete instance (e.g., spiked model) would strengthen the contribution.
- Theorem 2.2 expression: Equation (10) has ambiguous parentheses and factors (e.g., “− ατ + µ2τ2α − αλ 2 τ F(·)”). Please typeset precisely; the current form makes reproduction difficult.
- µ and σ^2 assumptions: The modeling section assumes “µ and σ^2 are nonzero,” yet earlier discussion admits µ can be zero for even links (where only direction is identifiable). The main theorems require µ≠0; be explicit, and discuss what can be said when µ=0 (e.g., is there a corresponding result for direction-only recovery or is LASSO provably ineffective?).
- Scope of A: Results are for i.i.d. Gaussian A. Given the importance of sub-Gaussian/rotationally invariant designs, please discuss robustness and which steps of the proof need exact Gaussianity (e.g., orthogonal decomposition of Ax0, Gordon/CGMT).
- Finite-sample behavior: The asymptotic equivalence is compelling; still, rates of convergence or concentration around the limit (standard in CGMT analyses) would increase practical value.
- Practical implications: The results depend on µ and σ^2, but when g is unknown, practitioners cannot compute these directly. Can they be estimated from data (e.g., via Stein/score function identities or moment methods), and would plugging in consistent estimates leave the asymptotic unchanged?
- Experiments: While the text references figures, the manuscript as provided contains no plots. A minimal experimental section validating the general program (beyond sparse Gaussian/Bernoulli) and the quantizer design (e.g., comparing LM-based designs versus uniform quantization across q and δ) would strengthen the paper.
- Minor presentation issues: Several typos and grammatical issues (e.g., “mesaurements,” “redifining”), inconsistent reference formatting, and a few incomplete citations; these should be fixed.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a precise statement (and proof sketch) of the “linearization” step that yields the µ, σ^2 reduction? Does it rely solely on Gaussian rotational invariance and Stein’s lemma?
- For the group-sparse and low-rank cases, can you give explicit F and error predictions for at least one concrete distribution on blocks/singular values?
- Is the scalar program (10) strictly convex/concave in the stated variables ensuring uniqueness of α*? Under what conditions?
- In the q-bit application, do LM iterations converge to global minimizers of σ^2/µ^2 for symmetric thresholds/levels? Empirical evidence?

Pros
- Strong, elegant theoretical contribution that unifies disparate results.
- Precise characterization for a broadly used estimator under realistic non-linearities.
- Potentially high impact for practitioners designing front-ends (quantizers).

Cons
- Some technical assumptions opaque and not fully instantiated for important cases (nuclear norm).
- Presentation/notation issues hinder reproducibility.
- Limited empirical validation and discussion of robustness beyond Gaussian design.

Recommendation: Accept (7/10)
A well-motivated, technically solid paper with a clear main message and significant theoretical contribution. With improved clarity on assumptions/equations and some additional examples/experiments, it would be an excellent NeurIPS paper.

Reviewer confidence: High.
