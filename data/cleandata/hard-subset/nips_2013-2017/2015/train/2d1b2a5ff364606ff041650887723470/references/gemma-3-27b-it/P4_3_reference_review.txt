This paper investigates the Generalized LASSO for estimation with non-linear measurements, demonstrating a surprising equivalence to a linear measurement model with appropriately scaled noise. The core contribution is establishing precise error bounds, extending previous work on linear measurements and generalizing Brillinger’s early results. The authors rigorously show that, asymptotically, the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements mirrors that of a corresponding linear model, characterized by a proportionality constant µ and a noise variance σ².  They also derive a precise error formula, providing insights into the roles of the link function, signal structure, regularizer, and regularization parameter. Applications to q-bit compressive sensing are discussed, and an algorithm leveraging the Lloyd-Max approach is proposed for optimizing quantization parameters.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The equivalence result—showing that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled noisy linear measurements—is a significant finding. Extending precise error analysis from linear to non-linear settings represents a substantial contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper appears mathematically rigorous. The derivation of the error formulas builds upon established results (e.g., [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 21]) and provides a clear connection to existing literature. The assumptions are clearly stated and justified.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations, while complex, are presented in a logical manner.
* **Significance:** The results have potential implications for a wide range of applications involving non-linear measurements, particularly in areas like compressed sensing and signal processing. The precise error analysis provides a deeper understanding of the LASSO’s behavior in these scenarios. The connection to q-bit compressive sensing and proposing an algorithm for parameter optimization shows practical relevance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1:** While the authors acknowledge the difficulty in verifying Assumption 1 in general, providing more guidance or examples on how to check it for specific regularizers and distributions would be helpful. The statement of the assumption itself is quite technical and might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the relevant mathematical concepts.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the application to q-bit compressive sensing is interesting, the computational cost of implementing the proposed algorithm, especially for higher-dimensional problems, isn't discussed. A brief analysis or simulation results demonstrating its feasibility would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper relies heavily on theoretical analysis. While Figures 1 and 2 provide some validation, expanding the empirical evaluation with more diverse datasets and experimental settings would bolster the confidence in the results.
* **Extensive Background Knowledge Required:**  The paper assumes a strong background in high-dimensional statistics and optimization. Readers less familiar with concepts like Fenchel duality, Moreau envelopes, or the CGMT framework may struggle to fully grasp the technical details.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and mathematically rigorous solution to an important problem in signal processing and statistics.
* The derived error bounds are precise and provide valuable insights into the behavior of the LASSO with non-linear measurements.
* The results have the potential to impact a wide range of applications.
* The paper builds upon and extends existing literature in a meaningful way.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Assumption 1 may be difficult to verify in practice.
* The computational complexity of the proposed algorithm for q-bit compressive sensing is not discussed.
* The empirical validation is limited.
* The paper's technical depth may make it inaccessible to a broader audience.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The equivalence result and the precise error analysis are noteworthy achievements. While the paper has some minor weaknesses, the strengths outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding Assumption 1 and computational complexity, and perhaps expanding the empirical evaluation. A small section briefly explaining key concepts (e.g., Fenchel duality) could also improve accessibility.
