

# Peer Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper establishes a precise asymptotic equivalence between the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements and a corresponding LASSO under linear measurements with scaled noise. The authors build on foundational work by Brillinger (1980s) and Plan & Vershynin (2015), extending their results to provide exact error predictions rather than just upper bounds.

The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 2.1, which shows that the squared error of the LASSO with measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0) is asymptotically identical to the error of the LASSO with linear measurements y_lin = μa_i^T x_0 + σz_i, where μ and σ^2 are defined as E[γg(γ)] and E[(g(γ)-μγ)^2] for γ~N(0,1). Theorem 2.2 provides an explicit error formula depending on μ, σ^2, measurement ratio δ = m/n, and signal structure through function F. The framework is applied to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems, with a compelling application to q-bit compressive sensing for optimal quantization threshold design.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical novelty:** This is the first precise error characterization for LASSO with non-linear measurements, significantly advancing beyond Plan & Vershynin's upper bounds. The results unify previous findings under a single framework.

2. **Technical soundness:** The proofs follow established CGMT techniques, and the numerical validation in Figures 1-2 demonstrates strong empirical support for the theoretical claims.

3. **Practical relevance:** The q-bit CS application (Section 2.3) provides a principled method for designing optimal quantization thresholds, with clear implications for hardware implementation of compressed sensing systems.

4. **Broad applicability:** The framework handles a wide range of regularizers (including nuclear norm) and link functions, making it broadly useful across structured signal recovery problems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Restrictive signal norm assumption:** The paper assumes ‖x_0‖_2 = 1 (footnote 4), which excludes important cases where signal magnitude matters (e.g., when g is unknown or even). While the authors mention this can be generalized, the technical details are missing, and it's unclear how the error formula would change.

2. **Incomplete related work comparison:** The claim that this is "the first to derive asymptotically precise results" overlooks Yi et al. (2015) "Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform," which also considers non-linear measurements. The relationship between these works should be clarified.

3. **Lack of computational discussion:** The paper doesn't address how the error formula (a convex optimization over three variables) would be implemented in practice, particularly for large-scale problems where computational complexity matters.

4. **Questionable performance metric:** The authors acknowledge that direction (not magnitude) of x_0 matters when g is unknown, yet their analysis assumes unit norm. The choice of squared error as the performance metric needs stronger justification for their application context.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of LASSO under non-linear measurements. The results are of high quality, well-supported by rigorous analysis, and have clear practical implications. The main limitations are the restrictive signal norm assumption and incomplete comparison with related work, but these are not fatal flaws.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a valuable contribution to the field. The results are original, building on but significantly extending previous work. The significance is high, as the framework can be applied to a wide range of structured signal recovery problems with non-linear measurements.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper is a valuable contribution that advances both theory and practice in compressed sensing with non-linear measurements. The authors should address the limitations mentioned above in the final version, particularly the comparison with Yi et al. (2015) and the discussion of the signal norm assumption. The theoretical results are robust and have clear potential for impact in both theoretical and applied signal processing communities.
