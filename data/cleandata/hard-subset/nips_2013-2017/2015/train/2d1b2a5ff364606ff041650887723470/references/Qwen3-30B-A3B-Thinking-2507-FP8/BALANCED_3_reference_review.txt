

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper addresses an important question in high-dimensional statistics: how does the Generalized LASSO behave when used with non-linear measurements (yi = gi(ai^T x0) rather than linear measurements? The authors make a significant theoretical contribution by showing that, under certain conditions, the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that with appropriately scaled and noisy linear measurements. Specifically, they prove that the estimation error is identical to that of the LASSO with measurements yi = μai^T x0 + σzi, where μ and σ² are defined in terms of the link function g. This equivalence is established through precise asymptotic error predictions for structured signal recovery (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework.

The paper builds on recent advances in analyzing the Generalized LASSO for linear measurements [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 21] and extends them to the non-linear setting. It also relates to earlier work by Brillinger [5] and Plan and Vershynin [17], generalizing their results to the regularized LASSO case with precise error characterization rather than loose upper bounds. The authors provide novel theoretical results for the LASSO with non-linear measurements, including the first asymptotically precise error analysis for this setting.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical contribution:** The main result (Theorem 2.1) is a significant theoretical advance that connects non-linear and linear measurement models. This equivalence simplifies the analysis of non-linear measurement systems by reducing them to well-studied linear cases.
2. **Precision:** The paper provides precise error predictions (Theorem 2.2) rather than loose upper bounds, which is valuable for understanding the exact behavior of the LASSO under non-linear measurements.
3. **Applicability:** The results are derived for a wide class of regularizers (including `1, group-LASSO, nuclear norm) and link functions, making the framework broadly applicable.
4. **Practical relevance:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) demonstrates how the theory can guide optimal quantization design, providing a concrete example of the framework's utility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Assumptions:** The assumption that x0 has unit Euclidean norm (footnote 4) is restrictive for some applications, though the authors note it can be handled by redefining g. This limitation should be more thoroughly discussed.
2. **Gaussian measurements:** The analysis is restricted to Gaussian measurement matrices, which may limit applicability to certain practical scenarios where measurements might not be Gaussian.
3. **Technical accessibility:** The CGMT framework used for the proofs is highly technical, and the paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the key ideas for readers unfamiliar with this approach.
4. **Computational aspects:** The paper does not address how the non-linear measurement case might affect the computational complexity of solving the LASSO compared to the linear case.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that extends previous work on the LASSO to a more general measurement model.
- The results are rigorous, well-motivated, and supported by numerical experiments.
- The precise error predictions provide valuable insights for both theoretical understanding and practical applications.
- The framework has broad applicability to various structured signal recovery problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The restriction to Gaussian measurement matrices might limit the practical impact of the results.
- The unit norm assumption for x0 could be problematic in some applications without sufficient discussion of its implications.
- The paper does not explore the computational aspects of implementing the LASSO with non-linear measurements.

**Constructive suggestions for improvement:**
1. Expand the discussion of the unit norm assumption (footnote 4), explaining more clearly when this is restrictive and how to handle cases where x0 has unknown norm.
2. Consider briefly discussing how the results might extend to non-Gaussian measurement matrices, or at least acknowledging the limitation in the discussion.
3. Add a short section explaining the key intuition behind the CGMT framework to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.
4. Include a brief discussion of computational considerations for solving the LASSO with non-linear measurements, comparing it to the linear case.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that makes an important advance in understanding the behavior of the LASSO under non-linear measurements. The results are technically sound, well-justified, and have clear significance for both theory and practice. While the assumptions restrict the immediate applicability in some scenarios, the framework itself is powerful and could inspire further work addressing these limitations. The paper is well-written and makes a valuable addition to the literature on structured signal recovery.

I recommend acceptance as it represents a significant theoretical advance with clear importance for the field. The minor limitations noted above are common in highly technical theoretical work and could be addressed in future extensions of this research.
