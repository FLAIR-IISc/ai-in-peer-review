### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm for estimating a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. This setup, known as the single-index model, is common in statistics and machine learning, with examples including 1-bit compressed sensing and censored regression. While the LASSO objective is designed for a linear model, the authors analyze its performance in this non-linear setting.

The main contribution is a powerful and elegant theoretical result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit (where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio `m/n`), the mean-squared error (MSE) of the LASSO estimator with non-linear measurements is *exactly equivalent* to the MSE of the LASSO estimator applied to an effective linear model. This equivalent linear model is `y_lin = µ * A * x0 + σ * z`, where `z` is standard Gaussian noise, and the constants `µ` and `σ^2` are determined solely by the link function `g` via `µ = E[γg(γ)]` and `σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - µγ)^2]` for `γ ~ N(0, 1)`.

Building on this equivalence, the paper provides a precise, deterministic formula for the asymptotic MSE. This formula is the solution to a low-dimensional convex optimization problem, which explicitly captures the roles of the measurement ratio, the regularization parameter, the signal structure, the chosen regularizer, and the non-linearity (through `µ` and `σ`). The general theory is then specialized to important cases like sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery. Finally, the utility of the theory is demonstrated in an application to q-bit compressive sensing, where it is used to formulate a simple objective for optimizing quantizer design.

This work connects to and significantly extends several lines of research. It generalizes a classical result by Brillinger on un-regularized least squares from a fixed-`n` to a high-dimensional setting. It also builds upon recent work by Plan and Vershynin, which provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO variant, by transforming their insightful but inexact bounds into precise asymptotic characterizations for the more common regularized LASSO. Most importantly, it bridges the gap between the non-linear measurement setting and the rich, recent literature on the precise asymptotic analysis of LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., works using the Approximate Message Passing or Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem frameworks).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are supported by a rigorous theoretical framework (the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem), and the main results (Theorems 2.1, 2.2, and 2.3) provide precise, falsifiable predictions. The authors validate these theoretical predictions with numerical simulations (Figures 1 and 2), which show remarkable agreement between the predicted MSE and the empirically observed MSE across different signal structures and link functions. This provides strong evidence for the correctness of the asymptotic theory, even in moderately-sized finite dimensions. The paper presents a complete piece of work, from the general theorem to its specialization and a practical application.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem, positioning the work within the existing literature, and clearly summarizing the main contributions. The problem setup and modeling assumptions are stated precisely. The main theorems are presented cleanly, making the central message of the paper—the equivalence between non-linear and an effective linear model—immediately apparent. The application to q-bit sensing further clarifies the practical implications of the theoretical findings. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the setup and the implications of the results.

**Originality:** The paper's central contribution is highly original. While prior work had suggested a connection between the non-linear problem and an effective linear one, this paper is the first to establish a precise, asymptotic equivalence for the LASSO estimator's performance. This is a significant conceptual leap that simplifies the analysis of a broad class of problems. By showing that one only needs to compute two scalars (`µ` and `σ`) to translate a non-linear problem into a well-understood linear one, the authors introduce a novel and powerful analytical tool. The paper also notes that its specific results for group-sparse and low-rank recovery are new even in the context of linear measurements, further enhancing its originality.

**Significance:** The results presented are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact on both theoretical and applied research. The key equivalence result provides a unifying framework for understanding regularized estimation under a wide variety of non-linear measurement models. Researchers and practitioners working with models involving quantization, censoring, or other non-linearities can now leverage the extensive body of work on the precise analysis of the linear LASSO to predict performance, tune parameters, and optimize system designs. The application to q-bit quantizer design is a compelling example of this, transforming a complex optimization problem over a LASSO estimator into a simple optimization over the `σ^2/µ^2` ratio. This work significantly advances the state of the art in the theoretical understanding of high-dimensional estimation.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature:** The analysis is purely asymptotic (`n -> ∞`). While this is a hallmark of the powerful analytical tools used, the theory does not provide non-asymptotic guarantees or characterize the rate of convergence to the asymptotic limit. The practical performance for small `n` is not covered by the theory, although the simulations suggest it is already accurate for `n` in the hundreds.

2.  **Gaussian Design Assumption:** The theoretical results rely on the assumption that the measurement matrix `A` has i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a standard assumption in this line of theoretical work but limits the direct applicability of the precise formulae to other types of measurement ensembles, which are common in practice. A discussion of the potential robustness or limitations of the results with respect to this assumption would be beneficial.

3.  **Technical Assumptions:** The general result in Theorem 2.2 relies on "Assumption 1," which concerns the convergence of the Moreau envelope of the regularizer's Fenchel conjugate. While the paper demonstrates this assumption holds for standard regularizers, its full scope is not detailed, which may leave readers wondering about its applicability to more exotic or newly proposed regularizers.

In summary, the strengths of this paper in terms of quality, clarity, originality, and significance are substantial. The weaknesses are primarily limitations inherent to the powerful asymptotic methodology employed and do not detract from the importance of the core contribution. This is an excellent theoretical paper that provides fundamental new insights into a broad and important class of estimation problems.
