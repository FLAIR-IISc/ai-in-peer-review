Summary
This paper studies the generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0), with A Gaussian and gi i.i.d. copies of an unknown or random link function g. The main result shows that, asymptotically and for a broad class of convex regularizers f and structured signals x0, the behavior of the generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is equivalent to that of the same LASSO applied to linear, noisy measurements with an effective gain µ = E[γ g(γ)] and noise variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2], γ∼N(0,1). Precisely, Theorem 2.1 proves that the asymptotic squared error ‖x̂ − µx0‖^2 matches that of the LASSO with y_lin = A(µx0) + σ z. Theorem 2.2 then provides an exact characterization of this error as the solution to a scalar convex optimization problem, making explicit the role of δ = m/n, λ, f, px0, and g (via µ, σ). The paper further specializes the formula to sparse (l1), group-sparse (l1,2), and sketches a path for low-rank (nuclear norm), yielding more explicit expressions (e.g., Theorem 2.3 for sparse signals). An application to q-bit quantization uses the equivalence to reduce quantizer design to minimizing σ^2/µ^2, with the Lloyd–Max procedure shown to converge to stationary points for this objective.

Relation to prior work
This advances Brillinger’s classical single-index result (consistency up to scaling for LS with fixed n and m→∞) to high-dimensional regimes and to regularized LASSO, yielding precise asymptotic errors. It sharpens Plan–Vershynin’s non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO under non-linear observations by providing exact asymptotics and extending to the regularized form. For linear measurements, it connects to and generalizes precise LASSO analyses via AMP and CGMT (e.g., Bayati–Montanari; Stojnic; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.). The “nonlinear-to-linear equivalence” is consistent in spirit with Bussgang-type decompositions and with insights from generalized linear models and AMP/GAMP for generalized output channels; citing that signal processing literature would strengthen context.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main theorems are plausible and align with known phenomena for Gaussian designs. The CGMT-based route is a rigorous and appropriate tool for precise asymptotics. The reduction to µ, σ captures the exact way non-linearities affect the LASSO objective in Gaussian designs.
- Assumptions: The Gaussian design is critical. Assumption 1 (existence of a limit involving the Moreau envelope of f*) is somewhat opaque but is verified for l1 and l1,2 and is plausibly verifiable for other standard convex penalties. The requirement µ≠0 is crucial; the paper acknowledges the identifiability limitation and focuses on direction when appropriate.
- Completeness: The sparse and group-sparse specializations are complete and validated empirically. The low-rank case is set up but evaluation of F is deferred, which weakens completeness for that case. The quantization application is neat and logically follows.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and well-structured, with a clear progression: model, main equivalence, precise characterization, specializations, and application. The notation is heavy but standard for this literature. A few points could be clearer:
- Assumption 1: Provide a user-facing checklist of sufficient conditions for popular f (e.g., total variation/fused lasso) and for px0 beyond i.i.d. entries.
- The role and estimation of µ, σ when g is unknown: while the method does not require knowing g, practitioners would benefit from guidance on how to estimate µ, σ from data and robustness to their misspecification.
- Minor typos (e.g., mesaurements, redifining) and a few missing references (AMP/GAMP, Bussgang) could be addressed.

Originality
The main equivalence and the precise scalar optimization for the LASSO error under non-linear observations are, to my knowledge, novel and important. The extension to general convex regularizers via CGMT beyond l1 is a notable contribution. The quantization-design application that reduces to σ^2/µ^2 minimization is insightful and practical.

Significance
High. The result provides an actionable and rigorous bridge: one can analyze/tune LASSO under non-linear (even unknown) link functions by reusing the extensive theory for linear noisy measurements with derived (µ, σ). This can inform algorithm design and parameter tuning (e.g., λ selection), and the quantizer design angle is of practical interest. The work will likely be influential for both theory and applications in compressed sensing, single-index models, and quantized sensing.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and powerful equivalence (non-linear to effective linear), rigorously proved.
- Precise asymptotics via a low-dimensional convex program with clear dependence on δ, λ, f, px0, g.
- Coverage of multiple structures (sparse, group-sparse) with accurate empirical validation.
- Concrete and useful application to quantizer design.
- Advances over prior bounds (Plan–Vershynin) and unifies with precise LASSO analyses.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Gaussian design restriction: discuss extensions to sub-Gaussian rows or rotationally invariant ensembles; at least provide conjectures or empirical evidence.
- Assumption 1: broaden and make more constructive, with additional examples (e.g., fused lasso/TV and nuclear norm with explicit spectral assumptions), and include a recipe for computing F numerically for low-rank.
- Unknown g: provide an estimator for µ and σ from the data (e.g., via method-of-moments using y and Ax) and study sensitivity to estimation error.
- Finite-sample: include a brief discussion or experiments on finite-n accuracy and how discrepancies scale with n, δ, and λ.
- Quantization section: clarify the proof sketch that Lloyd–Max yields stationary points for σ^2/µ^2; discuss initialization strategies, and show empirical gains versus naïve uniform quantization.
- Minor presentation issues: unify terminology for the LASSO variant; fix typos; expand related work to include Bussgang and GAMP.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution with clear impact.
- Rigorous, general framework that subsumes important special cases.
- Practical insights (λ tuning, quantizer design) and good empirical support.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited to Gaussian designs; low-rank case left partially unresolved.
- Assumption 1 is abstract and may limit accessibility.
- Limited guidance for practitioners when g is unknown (estimating µ, σ).

Overall recommendation: accept (strong).
