Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies LASSO estimation when measurements are non-linear single-index observations yi = gi(a_i^T x0), with Gaussian design A and i.i.d. link functions gi. Its central claim is an asymptotically exact equivalence: for generalized LASSO with convex regularizers, the estimation error under non-linear measurements matches that under a corresponding scaled and noisy linear model ylin = µ A x0 + σ z, with µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µ γ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Theorem 2.1 formalizes this non-linear = linear correspondence. Theorem 2.2 then provides a precise characterization of the asymptotic squared error as the unique solution α*^2 of a three-variable convex optimization (10), in which the structure/regularizer enters through a scalar function F derived from a normalized Moreau-envelope limit (Assumption 1). The paper specializes these results to sparse (`1), group-sparse (`1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) settings; for sparse recovery, it gives a concrete fixed-point characterization (Theorem 2.3). An application to q-bit compressive sensing shows that minimizing the error reduces to minimizing σ^2/µ^2, and connects this to Lloyd–Max quantizer design.

This work generalizes Brillinger’s classic LS single-index result (consistency up to µ for m→∞, fixed n) to high-dimensional LASSO and provides precise asymptotics beyond upper bounds. It refines the Plan–Vershynin constrained LASSO bounds by giving exact asymptotic error (including the explicit role of µ, σ) for the regularized LASSO and improves upon prior precise analyses for linear measurements via CGMT/AMP by extending them to the non-linear setting and to broader classes of convex regularizers.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The claims are well-motivated and follow a line of tight Gaussian comparison (CGMT) analyses that have proved reliable in related LASSO problems. The equivalence via µ, σ is consistent with prior heuristic and bounded results (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin). The main theorems are sensible under the stated assumptions (Gaussian A; i.i.d. link; nonzero, bounded µ, σ; linear asymptotics m/n→δ). The paper carefully verifies Assumption 1 for `1 and `1,2 via LLN and provides a plausible path for nuclear norm (via singular value shrinkage), although that case is less complete.
- Proof completeness: Proofs are deferred to the appendix; within the main text the statements are precise and testable. The sparse specialization (Theorem 2.3) yields explicit fixed-point equations that can be numerically checked and matches known special cases; the figures (not included here) reportedly validate predictions.
- Limitations: The equivalence and precise formulas require Gaussian A; extension to sub-Gaussian or structured designs is not addressed. Assumption 1, while standard in CGMT analyses, is technical and its scope outside the demonstrated cases could be discussed more. The low-rank case is outlined but lacks a fully worked out F for common spectral priors. The statement of Theorem 2.1 could be clarified to emphasize equality of asymptotic limits rather than equality of finite-n errors.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized: it introduces the problem, connects to prior work, states general results, and provides specializations and an application. The role of µ and σ is made explicit and intuitive.
- However, there are several typos and minor grammatical issues (e.g., “mesaurements,” “eventhough,” “redifining”), and some references are incomplete. The function F and Assumption 1 are central; providing more intuition/examples and a brief recipe to compute F for common regularizers would improve readability and reproducibility. The phrasing “with probability one, ‖x̂−µx0‖^2 = ‖x̂_lin−µx0‖^2” should be reworded to “their asymptotic limits coincide almost surely.”

Originality:
- The non-linear = linear equivalence for regularized LASSO with precise asymptotics appears novel and strengthens prior non-asymptotic bounds for constrained LASSO. The general scalar optimization (10) that captures arbitrary convex regularizers via F is a useful unifying contribution. The sparse specialization without Gaussian prior on nonzeros extends prior precise results that assumed Gaussianity. The quantization design angle (minimizing σ^2/µ^2 and linking to Lloyd–Max) is insightful.

Significance:
- Conceptual significance: Establishes a clear and usable reduction from non-linear to linear models for LASSO, making a broad class of non-linear observation problems tractable with existing linear-LASSO theory and tools.
- Practical significance: Provides actionable guidance for tuning and design (e.g., λ selection, quantizer thresholds/levels via σ^2/µ^2). The scalar optimization enables efficient prediction of error across λ, δ, and structures.
- Scope limitations (Gaussian A, µ≠0, i.i.d. link) constrain applicability, but within high-dimensional inference with random projections, these are standard.

Strengths:
- Precise asymptotic error characterization for LASSO under non-linear measurements.
- Clear identification of µ and σ^2 as the only link-dependent quantities.
- General framework covering several convex regularizers; concrete sparse/group-sparse derivations.
- Useful application to quantized sensing and principled quantizer design.
- Builds coherently on and advances CGMT-based precise analyses.

Weaknesses:
- Strong Gaussian design assumption; no results for sub-Gaussian or dependent designs.
- Technical Assumption 1 is central; its verification beyond shown cases is not fully discussed.
- Low-rank case is only sketched; no empirical or fully explicit F presented.
- Limited experimental evidence in the main text; figures are referenced but not detailed here.
- Minor clarity issues (typos; precise meaning of Theorem 2.1’s “equality”).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify Theorem 2.1 as equality of almost-sure limits; add a brief finite-n discussion.
- Expand on conditions guaranteeing Assumption 1; provide a table of F for common regularizers.
- Flesh out the low-rank case with a concrete spectral prior and numerical validation.
- Discuss prospects for sub-Gaussian designs or robustness to mild model violations.
- Improve proofreading and complete references.

Recommendation:
- I lean toward acceptance. The paper makes a clear, technically solid, and novel contribution with both theoretical and practical impact. The Gaussian design and technical assumptions are standard in this line of work, and the framework/general insight (reduce to linear with µ, σ) is likely to be influential and useful. Addressing clarity and completeness (especially for low-rank) would further strengthen the submission.
