### Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

**Summary:**
This paper analyzes the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to a non-linear measurement model, specifically the single-index model `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)`. The central and striking claim is that, in a high-dimensional asymptotic regime with i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, the mean-squared error (MSE) of the LASSO estimate is precisely equivalent to the MSE of the same LASSO algorithm applied to a corresponding linear model with additive Gaussian noise. The scaling factor (`µ`) and noise variance (`σ^2`) of this equivalent linear model depend only on the statistical properties of the non-linear link function `g`. The authors leverage the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework to derive a precise, deterministic expression for this asymptotic MSE. They specialize this general result to cases of sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery, and demonstrate its utility in an application to optimal q-bit quantizer design. The work extends the classical results of Brillinger and provides precise characterizations that refine recent non-asymptotic bounds by Plan and Vershynin.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Theoretical Depth and Precision:** The main contribution is a strong theoretical result that replaces loose upper bounds with an exact asymptotic characterization of the estimation error. The equivalence established in Theorem 2.1 is an elegant and powerful conceptual tool, providing a clear intuition for why a method designed for linear models can succeed in a non-linear setting. The final error prediction (Theorem 2.2) is given as the solution to a low-dimensional convex program, which is a hallmark of this line of analysis.
2.  **Generality:** The framework is quite general. It accommodates a wide class of convex regularizers (`f`), structured signals (`x_0`), and link functions (`g`). The specialization to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank settings demonstrates the breadth of its applicability.
3.  **Connection to and Extension of Prior Work:** The paper is well-situated within the literature. It provides a clear high-dimensional generalization of Brillinger's classical work and sharpens the recent results of Plan and Vershynin. The connection to the established CGMT framework for LASSO analysis lends credibility to the technical approach.
4.  **Clear Practical Implication:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing is a compelling example of the theory's utility. By reducing the complex problem of optimizing quantization thresholds for signal recovery to the much simpler problem of optimizing the `σ^2/µ^2` ratio, the authors provide a tangible and useful design principle.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Restrictive Assumptions:** The paper's conclusions hinge on a set of strong, idealized assumptions. The most significant of these is the requirement that the measurement matrix `A` consists of i.i.d. Gaussian entries. While this is a standard assumption in the CGMT literature that makes the analysis tractable, it severely limits the direct applicability of the results. Many practical applications involve structured measurement matrices (e.g., Fourier, Hadamard) or matrices with non-Gaussian entries, and the paper provides no discussion or empirical evidence on whether these precise predictions are robust to deviations from the Gaussian assumption.
2.  **Asymptotic Nature:** The results are strictly asymptotic (`n, m -> ∞`). While the provided simulations (Figures 1-2) show a good match between theory and practice for the chosen problem dimensions, the paper does not investigate the finite-sample behavior or the rate of convergence to the asymptotic limit. Without this, it is difficult to know how large `n` must be for these "precise" formulas to be reliable guides for practitioners.
3.  **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper convincingly argues that the standard LASSO is a viable tool for non-linear measurements. However, it completely omits any comparison to methods specifically designed for this setting. The literature on single-index models and generalized linear models is vast. A thorough evaluation would require comparing the performance of the off-the-shelf LASSO against specialized estimators. The current experiments only validate the theory under its own idealized conditions, rather than demonstrating its competitiveness. This omission is a critical weakness, as it leaves the question of whether this is a *good* approach unanswered, even if its performance is now well-understood.
4.  **Novelty of Technique:** While the application of the CGMT framework to this problem is novel and insightful, the analytical machinery itself is established. The contribution lies in the successful application of a powerful but known tool to a different problem setting, rather than in the development of a new analytical method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a precise and elegant theoretical result for a fundamental problem.
*   The "non-linear = linear" equivalence is a powerful and intuitive concept that simplifies understanding.
*   The framework is general and covers many popular structured signal models.
*   The q-bit quantization application demonstrates clear practical relevance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The reliance on i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices is a major practical limitation that is not discussed.
*   The analysis is purely asymptotic, with limited exploration of finite-sample performance.
*   The paper fails to compare the proposed approach (using standard LASSO) to any established, specialized methods for non-linear models, making it impossible to judge its practical competitiveness.
*   The work is more of a sophisticated application of an existing theoretical framework than a fundamental methodological advance.

**Conclusion:**
This is a technically sound and well-written paper presenting an important theoretical result. The characterization of the LASSO's performance under non-linear measurements is precise and insightful. However, the work is presented in a theoretical vacuum. The heavy reliance on idealized assumptions (particularly the Gaussian matrix) and the complete absence of comparisons to alternative, specialized methods make it difficult to assess the true significance of the contribution for the broader NIPS community. While the theory is strong, the lack of rigorous validation against established baselines and discussion of the assumptions' limitations falls short of the standards I would expect for a paper making such a definitive claim. It is an excellent piece of theory, but its practical implications remain largely unproven.
