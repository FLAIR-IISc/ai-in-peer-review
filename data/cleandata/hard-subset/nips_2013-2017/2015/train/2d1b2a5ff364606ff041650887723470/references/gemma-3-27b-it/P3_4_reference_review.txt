## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements of the form *y<sub>i</sub> = g<sub>i</sub>(a<sub>i</sub><sup>T</sup>x<sub>0</sub>)*. Building upon prior work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, the authors demonstrate a surprising and powerful result: under Gaussian measurement matrices and a suitable asymptotic regime, the LASSO effectively "linearizes" the problem. Specifically, the estimation error is asymptotically equivalent to that obtained with *scaled and noisy linear measurements*, where the scaling factor and noise variance are characterized in terms of the link function *g*. They provide precise error formulas for various regularizers (sparsity, low-rank, etc.) and illustrate the result with concrete examples, including 1-bit quantization and the Tobit model.  The work also extends recent precise analysis results for linear measurements to the non-linear case.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel and Significant Result:** The equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements is a remarkable finding. It offers a fundamentally new perspective on the behavior of the LASSO with non-linearities, and simplifies the analysis considerably.
* **Precise Asymptotic Analysis:** The paper goes beyond existing upper bounds and provides *precise* characterizations of the estimation error, capturing the influence of the link function, the regularizer, and the regularization parameter. This level of detail is crucial for practical applications and theoretical understanding.
* **Generality and Scope:**  The results are quite general, applying to a broad class of link functions *g* and regularizers *f*. The analysis doesn’t require knowledge of *g*, which is particularly useful in applications where *g* is unknown.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The analysis is rigorously presented, building on established tools like the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem and careful asymptotic arguments. The assumptions are clearly stated and justified.
* **Illustrative Examples:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing with the Lloyd-Max algorithm provides a compelling illustration of the practical implications of the theoretical results. The figures further support the validity of the derived formulas.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper carefully positions its contributions within the broader context of single-index models, compressive sensing, and high-dimensional statistics, citing relevant work appropriately.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:**  The paper is mathematically demanding. While rigor is commendable, some sections might be difficult for readers unfamiliar with the specific tools used (e.g., the CGMT framework). More intuitive explanations, or signposting of key steps, would be beneficial.
* **Assumption 1:** The assumption regarding the limiting behavior of the Fenchel conjugate (Assumption 1) feels somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from further justification or discussion. It's not immediately clear why this assumption is necessary or how easy it is to verify in practice.  A more detailed discussion of conditions under which it holds would be useful.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the paper shows how to *predict* the error, actually *computing* the optimal regularization parameter, or the derived error bounds, for complex problems could still be computationally challenging. This limitation isn’t explicitly discussed.
* **Practical Implications Beyond q-bit Sensing:** While the q-bit sensing example is well-developed, the paper doesn’t fully explore other potential applications of the linearity result. Expanding on these could further highlight the significance of the work.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a significant theoretical advancement in our understanding of the LASSO with non-linear measurements.
* The results are mathematically rigorous, general, and provide precise error bounds.
* The connection to compressive sensing and the proposed algorithm for optimal quantization levels are practical and demonstrate the utility of the theory.
* The work is well-written (despite its technical density) and clearly positions itself within the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical complexity might limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* Assumption 1 requires further justification or discussion.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The core result – the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements – is surprising, insightful, and opens up new avenues for research. While the technical density and a somewhat abstract assumption are minor concerns, the strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** This paper is of high quality and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS. I recommend its acceptance with a request that the authors consider adding more intuitive explanations of key concepts and a more detailed discussion of Assumption 1.
