Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under single-index (non-linear) measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian design A and structured x0 estimated via the generalized LASSO (square-root LASSO): min_x ||y − Ax||_2 + λ f(x). The core contribution is an asymptotically precise equivalence principle: for a broad class of convex regularizers and link functions g, the estimation error of LASSO with non-linear observations is, in the large n,m regime (m/n→δ), identical to that of LASSO under an effective linear model y_lin = μ A x0 + σ z, with μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], γ∼N(0,1). Building on CGMT-based analysis, the paper (i) proves this non-linear-to-linear reduction (Theorem 2.1) and (ii) derives an explicit scalar convex program (Theorem 2.2) that yields the exact asymptotic squared-error ||x̂ − μ x0||_2^2 for any fixed λ. The framework specializes to sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) settings; in the sparse case, a more interpretable characterization is given via a pair of fixed-point equations (Theorem 2.3) involving soft-thresholding. As an application, the paper uses the equivalence to reduce optimal q-bit quantizer design for compressed sensing to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, and connects the Lloyd–Max algorithm to stationary points of this objective.

Relation to prior work: The result extends Brillinger’s classical single-index LS consistency (fixed n, m→∞) to high-dimensional regimes and general convex regularization. It sharpens Plan–Vershynin’s non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO by providing asymptotically exact characterizations (and for the regularized, not constrained, formulation). It builds on precise LASSO analyses for linear models (AMP and CGMT lines) and generalizes them to non-linear observations via the μ,σ reduction.

Evaluation

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of CGMT to obtain sharp asymptotics is appropriate and in line with recent precise LASSO analyses. The equivalence principle is compelling and clean. Assumption 1 (existence of a limit involving the Moreau envelope of f* under Gaussian perturbations) is technical but natural and verified in key examples (ℓ1, ℓ1,2). The specialization to sparse case yielding fixed-point equations aligns with prior precise analyses in the linear setting and appears consistent with simulations.
- Scope and assumptions: The Gaussian design assumption is central. The link functions are i.i.d. across measurements; μ≠0 and σ^2 finite are assumed. The low-rank case is outlined but not fully developed (evaluation of F left for future work).
- Empirics: Figures demonstrate close agreement with theory; more breadth would help but is not essential given the theoretical focus.

Clarity
- Generally well organized with a clear narrative from intuition to general theorem to specializations. The role of μ and σ is well highlighted and linked to prior literature.
- Minor clarity issues: Theorem 2.1 states equality of errors “with probability one” without “in the limit” phrasing; presumably this means the limits coincide almost surely. Some notation typos and footnote formatting detract slightly. The definition and intuition of Assumption 1 could be expanded in the main text.

Originality
- High. To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact characterization of generalized LASSO error under non-linear single-index observations, for broad convex regularizers, via a non-linear-to-linear equivalence that is precise (not just upper bounds). The sparse and group-sparse specializations and the quantizer design connection are valuable.

Significance
- Conceptual impact: The equivalence principle is powerful and simple: to understand LASSO under non-linearities, analyze the corresponding linear problem with (μ,σ) from g. This unifies disparate models (1-bit, Tobit, quantization) under a single umbrella.
- Practical implications: Provides concrete guidance on tuning and on quantizer design (minimize σ^2/μ^2), and lets practitioners reuse linear-model precise error formulas.
- Likely to stimulate follow-up on universality beyond Gaussian designs, estimation of μ,σ from data when g is unknown, and extensions to μ=0 cases.

Strengths
- Bold, clean central idea with broad applicability.
- Precise asymptotics via a compact scalar convex program valid for general convex regularizers.
- Clear specialization to sparse/group-sparse and connection to well-known thresholding operators.
- Bridges to classical and modern results (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin; AMP/CGMT), positioning the work in a coherent way.
- Insightful application to quantizer design with an actionable criterion.

Weaknesses
- Strong Gaussian design assumption; no universality claims or evidence for sub-Gaussian/structured designs.
- Assumes μ≠0; important cases like odd/even symmetries or symmetric quantization leading to μ=0 are excluded (though discussed).
- Assumption 1 is abstract; verification beyond showcased cases (e.g., nuclear norm) is deferred.
- Empirical section is limited and low-rank case is not fully instantiated.
- Practical estimation of μ,σ when g is unknown is not addressed; yet the equivalence suggests such calibration would be valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 2.1 to state equality of the almost-sure limits; specify whether finite-n deviations concentrate and at what rate.
- Discuss universality prospects: which parts of the proof hinge critically on Gaussianity? Any numerical evidence for sub-Gaussian designs?
- Provide guidance or a simple estimator for μ,σ from data when g is unknown (e.g., moment-based via Stein’s lemma or cross-fitting), and discuss how to tune λ in this setting.
- Flesh out the low-rank specialization with at least one concrete X0 model where F can be computed numerically.
- Release code for solving the scalar optimization and reproducing figures.
- Add experiments for additional link functions (e.g., ReLU, logistic, saturation) and for the quantizer design to validate the σ^2/μ^2 criterion empirically.

Recommendation
Accept. This paper introduces a compelling and broadly useful equivalence that reduces non-linear measurement LASSO analysis to an effective linear problem, and provides asymptotically exact error characterizations across important structured settings. The idea is novel and impactful, outweighing the reasonable limitations in assumptions and empirical breadth. Confidence: high.
