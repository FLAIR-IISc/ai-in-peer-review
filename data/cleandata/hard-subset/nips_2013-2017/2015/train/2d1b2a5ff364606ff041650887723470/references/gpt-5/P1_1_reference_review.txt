Summary
The paper studies generalized LASSO when observations are non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with i.i.d. Gaussian measurement vectors a_i. The central result (Theorem 2.1) shows that, in the high-dimensional proportional regime, the asymptotic squared error of the generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements equals that of the same LASSO run on an equivalent linear model with effective scaling and noise: ylin = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2] for γ ~ N(0,1). Thus, up to a scale μ, the non-linear LASSO behaves as if the measurements were linear with additive Gaussian noise of variance σ^2.

Theorem 2.2 provides an asymptotically exact scalar convex optimization (three variables) whose unique solution α* equals the limiting error ‖x̂ − μ x0‖. The formula captures the role of δ = m/n, λ, g (only via μ, σ^2), the structure of x0 and the regularizer f through a function F defined by the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate of f. Specializations include:
- Sparse recovery with ℓ1 regularization (Theorem 2.3), yielding closed-form fixed-point equations in terms of the soft-thresholding operator, a critical λcrit, and precise error for all λ.
- Group sparse recovery with ℓ1,2 regularization (vector soft thresholding).
- Low-rank recovery with nuclear norm (framework set, details for computing F deferred).

The paper connects to and sharpens: (i) Brillinger’s classical fixed-n asymptotics for LS under link misspecification, generalizing to proportional growth (δ > 1) and to LASSO; (ii) Plan–Vershynin’s non-asymptotic bounds for constrained LASSO under single-index models by yielding precise asymptotic expressions (and an improved bound for the constrained case). As an application, the paper studies q-bit compressive sensing: the optimal quantizer (levels and thresholds) for LASSO error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, and the Lloyd–Max algorithm converges to stationary points of this objective.

Quality
- The results are technically strong and carefully positioned within the AMP/CGMT line of precise LASSO analyses. The equivalence result is clean and powerful; the precise formula is consistent with earlier linear-measurement results and recovers known special cases. Assumptions are stated, and concrete verification is provided for sparse and group-sparse settings. The LS case is correctly reconciled with Brillinger.
- A limitation is that many proof details are deferred, and the general Assumption 1 (existence of the limiting F via the Moreau envelope of f*) is somewhat abstract; the low-rank case is outlined but not fully developed (evaluation of F “left for future work”).
- Experiments are mentioned (Figures 1–2) and reportedly match theory well; however, from the text it is unclear how broad the empirical coverage is (e.g., finite-sample behavior, sensitivity to λ selection).

Clarity
- Overall, the exposition is clear and well organized: context, main theorems, specializations, and an application. The contribution of μ and σ^2 is conveyed crisply.
- Notational choices (e.g., scaling by 1/√n in the objective) are unconventional but harmless; a brief motivation could help readers.
- The statement of Theorem 2.1 would be clearer if phrased as equality of limits or convergence of errors, rather than equality of random quantities; currently it reads as exact equality “with probability one” as n → ∞.
- There are several typos and minor grammatical errors (“mesaurements,” “redifining,” “expecation,” “eventhough,” capitalization of Gaussian, etc.) that should be cleaned up.

Originality
- The equivalence principle for generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements in the proportional regime and the resulting precise error expressions appear new. Prior work either handled constrained LASSO with non-asymptotic bounds (Plan–Vershynin) or precise asymptotics but only for linear measurements (AMP/CGMT literature).
- The unification across regularizers via F and the clear specialization to ℓ1 and ℓ1,2 is novel and useful. The quantizer-design angle via σ^2/μ^2 and Lloyd–Max is an interesting and practical byproduct.

Significance
- The results are likely to be influential for both theory (precise characterization under link misspecification) and practice (justifying LASSO on quantized/censored data; guiding quantizer design).
- The reduction to μ, σ^2 is appealingly simple; it gives immediate insight into when and how non-linearities hurt/help, and it enables porting existing precise linear results to non-linear settings.

Main strengths
- Conceptually clean equivalence (Theorem 2.1).
- Precise, optimization-based error characterization valid for general convex regularizers (Theorem 2.2).
- Meaningful specializations with explicit fixed-point forms (Theorem 2.3) and extension of Brillinger to proportional asymptotics.
- Practical application to quantizer design and a link to Lloyd–Max.

Main weaknesses and limitations
- Gaussian A assumption; applicability to sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant ensembles is not discussed.
- Requirement μ ≠ 0 excludes important even links (e.g., sign without dithering); the paper notes alternatives but gives no guidance for LASSO in μ = 0 regimes.
- Assumption 1 is abstract; low-rank case lacks a worked-out F or numerical recipe.
- Limited discussion on estimating μ and σ^2 from data when g is unknown (even though the algorithm does not use g).
- Empirical evaluation appears limited in scope in the current text; finite-sample accuracy and λ selection guidance would increase impact.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can the equivalence extend to rotationally invariant/sub-Gaussian designs or designs with correlated rows? What breaks in CGMT/analysis?
- Provide a simple data-driven estimator of μ and σ^2 and assess robustness (e.g., using sample correlations y^T A x / m).
- Clarify Theorem 2.1 as convergence of errors; specify any mild regularity for uniqueness of limits.
- For μ = 0 links, can you quantify the LASSO error (e.g., diverging, or characterize a non-trivial limit under additional structure)?
- Expand the low-rank section with a concrete model and computation of F (e.g., spiked model with Marchenko–Pastur).
- Add a short section guiding λ selection using your formulas (e.g., compute λcrit and discuss regimes).
- Include more experimental details (finite n, different g, mismatch in g, non-Gaussian A) to support practical relevance.

Minor comments/typos
- Fix typos and grammar (examples above); ensure consistent notation for norms and expectations; define all symbols on first use; standardize references (years, venues).

Recommendation
This is a strong theoretical paper with a clean, impactful main message and rigorous development, well aligned with NeurIPS theory. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying Theorem 2.1’s statement, improving exposition, and expanding the empirical and low-rank parts as feasible.
