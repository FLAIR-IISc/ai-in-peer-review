### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`, where `g_i` are random link functions and `a_i` are Gaussian measurement vectors. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, an algorithm designed for linear models, when applied directly to this non-linear problem.

The main contribution is a powerful and elegant theoretical result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit (`m, n -> infinity` with `m/n -> δ`), the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimator for the non-linear problem is *exactly equivalent* to the error of the same estimator on a corresponding linear problem with measurements `y_i = µa_i^T x0 + σz_i`. The scaling factor `µ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the statistics of the link function `g`. This result effectively bridges the analysis of a broad class of non-linear measurement models with the well-developed, precise theory of LASSO under linear Gaussian noise.

This work builds upon and significantly extends two key lines of research:
1.  **Classical work on single-index models:** The paper generalizes a seminal result by Brillinger (1983), which established a similar `µ`-proportionality for standard least squares in the classical, fixed-`n` asymptotic regime. This work extends the principle to the high-dimensional, regularized setting.
2.  **Modern high-dimensional analysis:** The paper directly improves upon the recent work of Plan and Vershynin (2015), who first showed that LASSO-type estimators could succeed in this setting. However, their results were non-asymptotic upper bounds containing unknown constants. The present work replaces these loose bounds with exact asymptotic characterizations, thereby proving in a precise manner the role of `µ` and `σ`. The analysis leverages the powerful Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, which has been instrumental in deriving precise error characterizations for LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., works by Stojnic, Oymak, Thrampoulidis, Hassibi).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of very high technical quality. The main theoretical claim (Theorem 2.1) is a strong, non-trivial statement. The subsequent derivation of the precise error formula (Theorem 2.2) via a scalar optimization problem is characteristic of state-of-the-art results in this field and appears technically sound. The authors are careful to state their assumptions, such as the Gaussianity of the measurement matrix `A` and the asymptotic regime, which are standard for this type of sharp analysis. The specialization of the general formula to important cases like sparse and group-sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3) demonstrates the utility of the framework and provides more intuitive results. The claim that the results are validated by simulations (Figs 1-2 are referenced) further strengthens the paper, although the figures themselves are not included in the provided text.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the problem, an excellent summary of the main contributions, and a thorough discussion of the relevant literature that effectively positions the work. The problem setup and assumptions are laid out systematically. The main theorems are stated with precision. The authors do a commendable job of taking a very complex analytical result (Theorem 2.2) and making it concrete by working through specific examples like `l1`, `l1,2`, and nuclear norm regularization. The application to optimal q-bit quantizer design is a compelling example that showcases the practical implications of the theory.

**Originality:**
The paper is highly original. While it stands on the shoulders of giants (Brillinger, Plan & Vershynin, the CGMT literature), the core result—that a large class of non-linear problems is asymptotically *equivalent* to a single linear one—is a novel and profound insight. Moving from the upper bounds of Plan and Vershynin to an exact characterization is a significant technical and conceptual leap. This equivalence principle is a new contribution that unifies the understanding of many disparate measurement models (e.g., 1-bit, quantized, censored) under a single theoretical umbrella. The derived error formulas for group-sparse and low-rank recovery under this model appear to be novel contributions in their own right.

**Significance:**
The significance of this work is potentially very high for both theorists and practitioners in machine learning, signal processing, and statistics.
*   **For theorists:** It provides a powerful "meta-theorem." Instead of re-deriving complex performance analyses for each new non-linear measurement model, researchers can now often simply compute the corresponding `µ` and `σ` and plug them into the existing, rich theory for the linear LASSO. This dramatically simplifies analysis and provides a unifying perspective.
*   **For practitioners:** The theory provides concrete, actionable insights. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is a prime example. The authors show that the complex problem of optimizing quantizer thresholds and levels to minimize final signal recovery error can be reduced to the much simpler problem of optimizing the ratio `σ^2/µ^2`. This is a valuable design principle that follows directly from their main theoretical result.

The main limitation is the reliance on the asymptotic regime and the i.i.d. Gaussian design matrix. While standard for this line of inquiry, these assumptions mean the results are not finite-sample guarantees and may not hold for structured measurement matrices. However, such theory often provides remarkably accurate predictions for moderate-sized finite problems and serves as an essential foundation for further analysis.

### Summary of Strengths/Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Strong, Elegant Main Result:** The equivalence between non-linear and linear models is a fundamental and surprising finding.
*   **Technical Depth:** The paper is technically strong, leveraging state-of-the-art analysis tools to provide precise, not just bounded, results.
*   **High Significance and Impact:** The work unifies the analysis of many important problems and provides tangible design guidelines for practical applications like quantization.
*   **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, motivated, and situated within the literature, making a complex topic accessible.
*   **Novelty:** It is a clear and substantial advance over prior work, moving from bounds to exact characterizations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Asymptotic Nature:** The results are asymptotic and may not apply to small-scale problems.
*   **Gaussian Assumption:** The reliance on i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices is a limitation for applications involving other types of sensing matrices.

Overall, the strengths of this paper overwhelmingly outweigh its weaknesses, which are standard limitations for this type of foundational theoretical work. It represents a significant contribution to our understanding of high-dimensional inference. I strongly recommend acceptance.
