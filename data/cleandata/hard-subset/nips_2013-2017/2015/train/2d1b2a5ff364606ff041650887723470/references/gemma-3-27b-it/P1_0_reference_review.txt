## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems involving non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(aTi x0)`. Building on recent work by Plan & Vershynin, the authors demonstrate a surprising and powerful result: under Gaussian measurement vectors and a high-dimensional asymptotic regime, the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of a LASSO applied to *scaled and noisy linear* measurements.  This equivalence is captured by constants µ and σ², which characterize the link function `g`. The authors derive a precise error formula for the LASSO in this non-linear setting, providing insights into the roles of the link function, signal structure, regularizer, and regularization parameter. They also explore implications for quantized measurements (q-bit compressive sensing) and provide an algorithm for finding optimal quantization levels.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work correctly positions itself within the broader context of single-index models, compressive sensing, and sparse recovery. It builds directly on Brillinger’s early work on least squares with non-linear measurements and extends the more recent constrained LASSO analysis by Plan & Vershynin.  The authors appropriately highlight connections to recent precise analyses of the LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., [2, 10, 16, 20]) and demonstrate how their results generalize those findings.  They also relate their work to sufficient dimension reduction. The extensive referencing is a strong point, demonstrating familiarity with the relevant literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel and Surprising Result:** The equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements is a significant theoretical contribution. It simplifies the analysis of LASSO with non-linear models and allows leveraging existing results from linear models.
* **Precise Error Analysis:**  The derivation of a precise error formula is a major accomplishment.  This allows for a deeper understanding of the factors influencing performance and facilitates the design of better algorithms.
* **Broad Applicability:** The results are applicable to a wide class of link functions and regularizers, making them broadly useful. The example of q-bit compressive sensing is a compelling application.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper employs a rigorous mathematical analysis, establishing asymptotic results with clear assumptions.
* **Well-Written and Organized:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, though dense.  The notation is consistent, and the authors make an effort to explain complex concepts.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The figures (even though only shown in brief) provide convincing evidence supporting the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** The paper is mathematically demanding and may be inaccessible to readers not already familiar with high-dimensional statistics and compressive sensing. More intuition could be provided for some of the key steps.
* **Assumptions:** The reliance on Gaussian measurement matrices and the unit norm constraint on x0 (though acknowledged as removable) limit the immediate applicability of the results.  A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions would be helpful.
* **q-bit Compressive Sensing Section:** While intriguing, the application to q-bit compressive sensing feels somewhat rushed. The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm could be further elaborated. The practical impact of the theoretical result for optimal quantization is not fully explored.
* **Limited Numerical Experiments:** While the authors mention figures validating the theory, the paper lacks detailed numerical results demonstrating the performance of the algorithm in more realistic scenarios.
* **Proof Complexity:** The proofs are deferred to appendices, making it difficult for a quick assessment of their validity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Significant Theoretical Advance:** The equivalence result is a major step forward in understanding the behavior of the LASSO with non-linear measurements.
* **Impact on Practice:** The precise error formula and the insights gained from the analysis have the potential to improve the design and analysis of estimation algorithms in various applications.
* **Rigor and Clarity:** Despite its technical complexity, the paper is well-written and rigorously analyzed.
* **Strong Connection to NIPS Community:** The topic of high-dimensional statistics, compressive sensing, and regularization is central to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Technical Barrier:** The high level of mathematical sophistication may limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The lack of extensive numerical experiments weakens the practical impact of the results.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The equivalence result is a surprising and powerful theoretical finding with potential implications for a wide range of applications. While the technical density and limited empirical validation are weaknesses, the strengths of the paper, particularly its theoretical rigor and originality, outweigh these concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the following minor revisions:

1. **Improve Intuition:** Add more intuitive explanations for key theoretical concepts, particularly in the derivation of the error formula.
2. **Discuss Assumption Sensitivity:**  Include a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to violations of the Gaussianity assumption and the unit norm constraint.
3. **Expand q-bit Section:** Elaborate on the connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm and provide a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the results for q-bit compressive sensing.
4. **Consider adding more detailed numerical experiments to demonstrate the results**

This paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program and will likely stimulate further research in this area.
