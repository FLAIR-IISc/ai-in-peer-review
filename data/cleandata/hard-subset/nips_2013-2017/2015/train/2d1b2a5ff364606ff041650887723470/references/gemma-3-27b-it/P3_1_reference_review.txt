## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a surprisingly clean and powerful result: the Generalized LASSO applied to non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to a Generalized LASSO applied to scaled and noisy linear measurements. Specifically, the authors identify scaling and noise parameters (µ and σ²) that characterize this equivalence. Building on prior work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, they derive a precise error formula for the LASSO in the non-linear setting, demonstrating that the performance is governed by these parameters and the structure of the signal. The authors validate their theoretical findings with simulations, showcasing the accuracy of their predictions for sparse recovery and demonstrating applicability to q-bit compressive sensing. 

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The main result – the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements – is a profound and insightful simplification. This allows leveraging the extensive literature on LASSO performance with linear measurements directly to analyze non-linear settings.
* **Precise Asymptotic Analysis:**  The paper goes beyond simply stating equivalence and provides a precise characterization of the error in the non-linear case, improving on prior upper bounds.  The derivation of the error formula (Theorem 2.2) is impressive.
* **Generality:** The work applies to a wide class of link functions 'g', regularizers 'f', and measurement matrices 'A', making it broadly applicable.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors carefully position their work within the existing literature, properly acknowledging and building upon contributions from Brillinger, Plan & Vershynin, and the broader LASSO analysis community.  The comparison with constrained LASSO (equation 7) is particularly helpful.
* **Practical Implications:**  The application to q-bit compressive sensing is a compelling example of the practical relevance of the theory. The discussion of the Lloyd-Max algorithm for optimal quantization demonstrates a path towards utilizing the theoretical result in real-world scenarios.
* **Clear Presentation:** Despite the technical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and organized.  The authors make a clear effort to explain the key concepts and assumptions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1:** The assumption regarding the limit of `n e√n(f∗)(n),c3 (c1h+ c2x0)` (Assumption 1) feels somewhat restrictive and its justification is limited. While the authors demonstrate it holds for the `l1` norm case, the broader applicability hinges on this assumption being generally satisfied. More discussion on the types of regularizers for which this assumption holds, or potential strategies to handle cases where it doesn’t, would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Complexity of Error Evaluation:**  While the theoretical error formula is precise, evaluating it in practice (solving the convex program in Theorem 2.2) might be computationally challenging for complex regularizers or high-dimensional problems. The authors acknowledge this but could elaborate on potential approximation strategies.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The simulations focus heavily on sparse recovery. While the q-bit sensing example is insightful, expanding the empirical evaluation to other regularizers (e.g., low-rank) and different types of non-linearities would further bolster the claims.
* **Lack of Discussion on Finite Sample Behavior:** The analysis is entirely asymptotic.  A brief discussion of the limitations of applying the results to finite-dimensional settings, and perhaps some insights into how the performance deviates from the asymptotic predictions, would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance with the potential to impact a broad range of research areas dealing with non-linear measurements and sparse recovery.
* The precision of the error analysis is a substantial improvement over existing work.
* The generality of the results, coupled with the practical application to q-bit compressive sensing, makes this a valuable contribution to the field.
* The paper is well-written and carefully positioned within the existing literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The restrictive Assumption 1 raises some concerns about the generality of the results.
* The computational cost of applying the theoretical results in practice could be a limitation.
* The empirical validation could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a highly original and significant contribution. The theoretical results are impressive, and the connection to practical applications like q-bit compressive sensing is compelling. While the Assumption 1 and the computational complexity of error evaluation are legitimate concerns, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality and impact of the work. 

**Recommendation:**  **Strong Accept**.  I believe this paper is a worthy addition to NIPS and will stimulate further research in the area of non-linear measurements and sparse recovery. I recommend the authors address the reviewers’ comments regarding Assumption 1 and potentially expand the empirical validation to further strengthen their claims.
