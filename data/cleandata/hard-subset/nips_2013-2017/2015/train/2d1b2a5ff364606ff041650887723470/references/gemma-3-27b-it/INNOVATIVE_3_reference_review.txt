This paper presents a surprising and significant result: the LASSO, when applied to measurements corrupted by a non-linear link function, behaves *as if* the measurements were linear, with an appropriate scaling and noise adjustment. This is a powerful and elegant finding, effectively expanding the applicability of well-understood linear LASSO analysis to a much broader class of models. The theoretical rigor appears strong, building upon recent advances in high-dimensional statistics and leveraging concepts like the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem. The connection to Brillinger’s earlier work is well-placed, and the extension to high-dimensional settings is a genuine contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core result – the equivalence of non-linear and scaled linear measurements for LASSO – is genuinely novel. This provides a new lens for understanding the behavior of the LASSO in non-linear settings.
* **Theoretical Depth:** The paper utilizes sophisticated theoretical tools and provides rigorous analysis, moving beyond mere observation to a precise characterization of the estimation error.
* **Generality:** The result applies to a broad class of link functions and regularizers, enhancing its practical relevance.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper expertly positions its contribution within the context of existing literature, drawing connections to Brillinger’s work, Plan & Vershynin, and recent advances in LASSO analysis.
* **Practical Implications:** The analysis opens the door to leveraging existing LASSO theory and algorithms for non-linear problems, potentially simplifying analysis and implementation. The section on q-bit compressive sensing is a nice illustration of this point.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** Assumption 1, while stated clearly, feels somewhat opaque and its verification might be challenging in some applications.  More concrete examples of how to check or approximate this assumption would be helpful.
* **Computational Complexity:** While Theorem 2.2 provides a precise error formula, actually *computing* the optimal α* could be computationally intensive, especially for complex regularizers. This isn’t a fatal flaw, but it’s a practical consideration.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The empirical validation (Figures 1 & 2) is somewhat limited. While the results support the theory, more extensive experimentation with different link functions, sparsity levels, and problem dimensions would strengthen the claims.
* **Presentation:** The paper is quite dense and theoretically focused.  While clarity is generally good, some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations and motivating examples. The jump from the general result to the specific sparse recovery case in section 2.2.3, for instance, is a bit abrupt.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core result is highly original and represents a significant advance in understanding the behavior of the LASSO in non-linear settings.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported.
* The paper's generality and potential for practical applications are substantial.
* The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall contribution. 

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of evaluating the error formula could limit practical applicability.
* The assumption requires careful examination in each specific setting.
* The empirical validation could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. While not perfect, the novelty and theoretical depth of the contribution are exceptional. The paper provides a genuinely new insight into a fundamental problem, and the potential impact on the field is significant. I am willing to overlook the minor weaknesses in light of the substantial advancements presented here. The authors have demonstrated a deep understanding of the relevant literature and have made a compelling case for the importance of their findings. This work sets the stage for future research exploring the application of LASSO-based methods to a wider range of non-linear problems.



**Score:** 9/10 (Strong Accept)
