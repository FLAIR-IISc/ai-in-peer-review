Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under single-index/nonlinear measurement models yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with i.i.d. Gaussian sensing vectors. It analyzes the Generalized LASSO with a broad class of convex regularizers and shows a striking equivalence: asymptotically, the LASSO’s estimation error under nonlinear measurements is identical to that under an appropriately “linearized” model ylin = A(µ x0) + σ z, where µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2] for γ ∼ N(0,1). Building on CGMT, the authors provide an exact scalar convex optimization that yields the asymptotic squared error for any λ, δ = m/n, regularizer f, and distributional model for x0 that satisfies a mild envelope assumption. They specialize the formulas to sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and sketch the low-rank (nuclear norm) case. As an application, they show that, for q-bit quantized compressive sensing, optimal quantizer design for LASSO reduces to minimizing σ^2/µ^2, and argue that Lloyd–Max yields stationary points of this objective.

Relation to prior work
- Brillinger (80s) analyzed LS under link mismatch for fixed n, m→∞, obtaining consistency up to a scalar µ. This paper generalizes to high-dimensional LASSO with m,n→∞ at fixed δ and gives exact asymptotic errors (beyond consistency), including δ<1 with structure.
- Plan & Vershynin derived non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO under nonlinear links, with key parameters µ,σ. This paper moves to regularized LASSO, replaces bounds with precise asymptotics, and explicitly shows the non-linear=linear equivalence in the LASSO error.
- Precise LASSO analyses for linear models (AMP- and CGMT-based) exist for specific losses/regularizers. Here, the authors extend the precision program to single-index nonlinearities and to general convex regularizers via a single scalar program (Theorem 2.2). For sparse recovery, they obtain a clean characterization with a critical λ and regimes that match known linear-model phase behavior.

Strengths
- Novel and bold conceptual contribution: a clean, exact “equivalence principle” mapping nonlinear measurement LASSO to a linear model with effective (µ,σ^2). This provides unifying intuition and actionable guidance.
- Technical depth: Theorem 2.2 reduces asymptotic error to a tractable 3-variable convex program parameterized by the Moreau-envelope-based F(·), enabling general regularizers and signal classes.
- Breadth: Specializations to sparse and group-sparse are concrete and practically relevant, with Theorem 2.3 giving an explicit system for κ, λcrit and clear regime behavior for δ<1 and δ>1.
- Practical payoff: The quantizer-design corollary (minimize σ^2/µ^2) is insightful and provides a simple recipe; the Lloyd–Max connection is a neat twist with potential engineering impact.
- Significance: The results will influence how practitioners justify LASSO under link misspecification, inform λ-tuning, and guide sensing/quantization design.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Gaussian design: The core equivalence relies on i.i.d. Gaussian A. It would help to discuss robustness to sub-Gaussian/rotationally invariant designs or provide a conjecture/outline for extensions.
- Assumption 1: The definition via pointwise convergence of the normalized Moreau envelope of f* is somewhat abstract. While the paper verifies it for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2, the low-rank case is sketched but not fully worked out; more explicit sufficient conditions or a self-contained lemma would improve accessibility.
- µ=0 cases: The paper notes issues for even link functions. A short discussion of what the theory predicts (e.g., impossibility, need for alternative methods) and whether a simple regularizer or modified objective can regain identifiability would be useful.
- Clarity/notation: 
  - Equation (2) uses the residual ℓ2 norm (not squared), usually called square-root LASSO, whereas the text refers to “`2-LASSO or square-root-LASSO.” Tighten the wording to avoid confusion with the standard squared-loss LASSO.
  - The scaling 1/√n in (9) is harmless but unconventional; a sentence explaining this normalization and its role in CGMT would help.
  - Some footnotes and reference formatting are rough; figures are referenced but not shown in this version.
- Empirics: The text alludes to simulations (Figures 1–2) showing excellent agreement. It would strengthen the paper to include (i) quantitative error vs. n to illustrate convergence rates; (ii) sensitivity to mis-specification of µ,σ; (iii) examples beyond sparse/group-sparse, if possible.

Questions for the authors
- Can you comment on extending Theorem 2.1 to sub-Gaussian or orthogonally invariant ensembles? Are there counterexamples where equivalence fails?
- For unknown g, how would you estimate µ and σ^2 from data? Is there a simple plug-in based on sample moments of y and A x̂ that preserves the asymptotic guarantees?
- In the low-rank setting, can you provide a concrete example where F(c1,c2,c3) is computed numerically (e.g., with random orthogonal factors and specified singular-value distribution)?
- Does the equivalence extend to other convex losses (e.g., Huber) or the squared-loss LASSO with different scaling, and would the scalar program change?
- Any insight on optimal λ as a function of δ, ρ, µ, σ (e.g., closed-form approximations) in the sparse case?

Pros for acceptance
- High conceptual novelty with a unifying equivalence principle for nonlinear measurements.
- Precise, general asymptotic error characterization via CGMT; extends prior linear-only precision results.
- Practical implications for quantizer design and for using LASSO under link uncertainty.
- Clear specializations; the sparse-case theorem is neat and likely to be reused.

Cons against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on Gaussian design; limited discussion of robustness.
- Assumption 1 and low-rank specialization could be made more explicit.
- Empirical section (in the current draft) is light and figures are not included here.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new concept and direction with strong theoretical backing and practical implications. Minor clarifications and a bit more empirical evidence would further strengthen it, but the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance.
