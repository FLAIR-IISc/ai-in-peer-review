### Summary of the Paper

This paper studies the problem of estimating a structured high-dimensional signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`, where `a_i` are measurement vectors and `g_i` are i.i.d. link functions. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, which is nominally designed for linear measurements, when applied to this non-linear setting.

The main contribution is a powerful and elegant result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow proportionally, and the measurement matrix `A` has i.i.d. Gaussian entries, the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is *exactly equivalent* to its performance on a corresponding linear model `y_lin = µ * A * x0 + σ * z`. Here, `z` is standard Gaussian noise, and the scaling factor `µ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are constants that depend only on the statistics of the link function `g`.

Building on this equivalence, the paper provides a precise, deterministic formula for the asymptotic mean-squared error `||x_hat - µ*x0||^2`. This formula is given as the solution to a low-dimensional convex optimization problem, whose parameters explicitly depend on the problem dimensions (`m/n`), the regularization parameter `λ`, and the link function characteristics (`µ`, `σ^2`), while implicitly capturing the signal structure and regularizer choice. The authors then specialize this general result to important practical cases, including sparse recovery (`l1`-norm), group-sparse recovery (`l1,2`-norm), and low-rank matrix recovery (nuclear norm), providing more explicit and insightful error characterizations. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their theory with an application to `q`-bit compressive sensing, showing that the practical problem of designing optimal quantizers can be reduced to a much simpler optimization of the ratio `σ^2/µ^2`.

This work connects to and significantly extends two main lines of research. First, it generalizes the classical result of Brillinger (1983) from the fixed-`n` regime and un-regularized least-squares to the modern high-dimensional, regularized setting. Second, it sharpens the recent non-asymptotic *upper bounds* of Plan and Vershynin (2015) for a constrained LASSO into *precise asymptotic predictions* for the more common regularized LASSO, confirming in a rigorous way the central role of the parameters `µ` and `σ^2`. The analysis leverages the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT), a framework that has been successfully used to obtain precise performance characterizations for LASSO in the linear measurement setting.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The main result is highly significant. The equivalence between the non-linear and a corresponding linear measurement model provides a deep and intuitive understanding of a complex problem. It elegantly demonstrates that, from the LASSO's perspective, the effect of a general non-linearity is asymptotically equivalent to simply scaling the signal and adding a specific amount of Gaussian noise. This is a powerful conceptual contribution that could influence how researchers and practitioners think about and analyze problems with non-linearities.

2.  **Technical Quality and Novelty:** The paper is technically strong and provides novel, precise results for a problem where previously only looser bounds were known. Deriving an exact asymptotic expression for the MSE is a substantial technical achievement. The specialization to sparse and group-sparse recovery (Theorems 2.3) adds concrete value, moving beyond the abstract optimization problem of Theorem 2.2 to provide more interpretable formulas. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide such a precise characterization for LASSO under general non-linear measurements.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the relevant literature, and summarizes the contributions effectively. The progression from the general theory (Theorems 2.1, 2.2) to specific instances and a practical application makes the paper's contributions clear and compelling.

4.  **Generality and Practical Relevance:** The framework is quite general, accommodating a wide class of non-linear link functions (known or unknown), convex regularizers, and signal structures. The application to `q`-bit quantization is an excellent example of the theory's practical relevance, demonstrating how the theoretical insight (equivalence to a linear model) can directly lead to a simplified and actionable approach for system design (optimizing quantizer thresholds).

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Gaussian Measurements:** The analysis relies critically on the assumption that the entries of the measurement matrix `A` are i.i.d. Gaussian. This is a standard assumption for the CGMT framework and related techniques (like AMP), but it is a significant limitation. It would strengthen the paper to explicitly acknowledge this limitation in the main text and briefly discuss the challenges or potential for extending the results to a broader class of matrices (e.g., sub-gaussian or rotationally invariant ensembles).

2.  **Asymptotic Nature:** The results are asymptotic in nature (`n, m -> infinity`). While the provided simulations show excellent agreement even for moderate dimensions, the theory provides no finite-sample guarantees. This is a characteristic of the analytical framework, not a flaw, but it is a limitation worth noting.

3.  **Incomplete Low-Rank Analysis:** The section on low-rank matrix recovery (2.2.4) is less developed than the other examples. It relies on an unstated assumption about the convergence of an empirical singular value distribution and defers the details. While the presented sketch seems plausible, this section feels less complete and rigorous compared to the thorough treatment of the sparse case.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Pro-acceptance:**

*   Presents a fundamental, elegant, and highly significant theoretical result ("non-linear = linear").
*   Provides the first precise asymptotic performance characterization for the LASSO with general non-linear measurements, a significant advance over existing upper bounds.
*   Technically strong, novel, and appears to be correct.
*   Clearly written, well-organized, and effectively connects theory to a practical application.
*   Likely to be highly influential and to spawn follow-up work in both theory and practice.

**Con-acceptance:**

*   The heavy reliance on the i.i.d. Gaussian matrix assumption limits direct applicability.
*   The analysis is purely asymptotic.
*   The treatment of the low-rank case is brief and less rigorous than the rest of the paper.

### Conclusion and Recommendation

This is an excellent theoretical paper that addresses a fundamental problem at the intersection of high-dimensional statistics, signal processing, and machine learning. The central result—that LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to a version with linear measurements—is both powerful and insightful. The paper is technically sound, novel, and very clearly presented. The strengths, particularly the significance and novelty of the core contribution, far outweigh the weaknesses, which are largely inherent to the powerful analytical framework being employed. This work significantly advances the state of the art in understanding regularized estimation methods and is an ideal fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
