

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary and Context
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a fundamental equivalence between the Generalized LASSO for non-linear measurements and the standard LASSO for linear measurements. The authors consider the problem of estimating a structured signal x₀ from measurements yᵢ = gᵢ(aᵢᵀx₀), where gᵢ are non-linear link functions. They prove that, asymptotically, the LASSO solution for non-linear measurements behaves exactly like the LASSO solution for linear measurements of the form y = µAx₀ + σz, where µ and σ² are defined in terms of the link function g. This generalizes Brillinger's early result [5] and extends the recent work of Plan and Vershynin [17] from constrained to regularized LASSO, while providing precise error characterization instead of loose upper bounds. The paper demonstrates the practical implications for q-bit compressive sensing, showing how to optimize quantization thresholds to minimize estimation error.

## Strengths

The paper's main strength is its elegant and profound theoretical insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements. This equivalence is not merely an approximation but an exact asymptotic result, which significantly simplifies the analysis of LASSO under non-linear measurements. The authors provide a precise error formula (Theorem 2.2) that characterizes the asymptotic behavior of the LASSO error in terms of three scalar optimization variables, which is computationally tractable.

The paper builds upon and extends several important lines of work at NIPS and beyond. It connects to the classic single-index model literature [5, 14, 15], the recent advances in precise analysis of LASSO for linear measurements [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 21], and the work of Plan and Vershynin on constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements [17]. The authors properly acknowledge these connections and clearly articulate how their work advances the state-of-the-art.

The paper's application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) is particularly compelling. By showing that the optimal quantization thresholds can be found by minimizing σ²/µ² (Theorem 2.1), they provide a concrete practical contribution that could guide the design of quantized sensing systems. Their connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm for this optimization problem is insightful and well-justified.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs (referenced to appendices) and carefully stated assumptions. The authors are honest about the limitations of their results, particularly the asymptotic nature of the analysis and the unit norm assumption for x₀.

## Weaknesses

The primary limitation is the asymptotic nature of the results (n→∞), which leaves open questions about finite-sample performance. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't provide guidance on how well their results might generalize to finite n, which is crucial for practical applications.

The assumption that x₀ has unit norm (‖x₀‖₂ = 1) is restrictive in some applications. While the authors note in Footnote 4 that this can be relaxed by redefining the link function, it would be helpful to explicitly discuss the practical implications of this assumption for specific applications.

The analysis assumes i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, which might not capture all practical measurement scenarios. While this is a common assumption in high-dimensional statistics, it would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss how their results might extend to other measurement ensembles.

The connection to practical applications beyond q-bit compressive sensing could be further developed. While the paper demonstrates the theoretical equivalence, it doesn't provide extensive empirical validation across multiple non-linear measurement scenarios.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in understanding the behavior of LASSO under non-linear measurements. The main result (Theorem 2.1) is elegant and powerful, providing a precise characterization of the asymptotic equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and builds upon and extends relevant prior work.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a theoretical foundation for using LASSO in non-linear settings, which are common in practice (e.g., 1-bit quantization, censored measurements). The insights could guide both theoretical research and practical system design for measurement systems with non-linearities.

While the asymptotic nature of the results and the Gaussian measurement assumption are limitations, these are common in high-dimensional statistical analysis and the authors properly acknowledge them. The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the literature on structured signal recovery.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of LASSO in non-linear measurement settings. The results are well-motivated, technically sound, and have clear practical implications. The paper is well-written and properly situates itself within the relevant literature. The main result is elegant, the proofs are rigorous, and the application to q-bit compressive sensing provides concrete practical value. While there are limitations to the asymptotic analysis, these do not diminish the paper's theoretical contribution.

This work addresses a fundamental question in high-dimensional statistics with important implications for both theory and practice. It represents a meaningful advancement over previous work and makes a strong contribution to the field. The paper meets all NIPS review criteria with high quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
