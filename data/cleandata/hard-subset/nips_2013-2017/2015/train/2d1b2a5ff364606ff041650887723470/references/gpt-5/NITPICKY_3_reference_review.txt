Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation from non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian design A and convex regularization f. It investigates the regularized square-root LASSO (minimize ||y − Ax||2 + λ f(x)) and claims a precise asymptotic characterization of the mean-squared error. The central message is a reduction: for i.i.d. Gaussian A, the asymptotic estimation error of the LASSO under non-linear measurements matches that under an equivalent linear model y = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2] with γ ~ N(0,1). The paper uses the CGMT framework to derive a scalar convex optimization (Theorem 2.2) whose unique optimizer α* yields the asymptotic error. Specializations for sparse (`1), group-sparse (`1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) cases are discussed, and an application to optimal q-bit quantization (via minimizing σ^2/μ^2; Lloyd–Max as a stationary-point method) is presented. The work positions itself as strengthening Plan–Vershynin’s constrained LASSO bounds by providing precise asymptotics for the regularized LASSO.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and impactful reduction: “non-linear single-index + Gaussian design” behaves like a linear model with effective gain μ and noise σ. This generalizes Brillinger (LS) and Plan–Vershynin (constrained LASSO) and aligns with intuition from single-index/sufficient dimension reduction.
- Use of CGMT to obtain an explicit scalar convex program for the asymptotic error is technically strong and, if correct, broadly useful. It appears to cover general convex regularizers via an abstract function F defined through the Moreau envelope of the conjugate.
- Specializations to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank provide concrete instances; for sparse, the fixed-point conditions (Theorem 2.3) look plausible and connect to known soft-thresholding risk formulas.
- The quantization application (minimizing σ^2/μ^2) provides a neat design principle and a bridge to Lloyd–Max.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
- Statement precision (potentially misleading/overstated):
  - Theorem 2.1 claims, “in the limit n→∞, with probability one, ||x̂ − μ x0||^2 = ||x̂_lin − μ x0||^2.” As written, this reads as almost-sure equality of the random errors, not equality of their almost-sure limits. It should be reformulated to assert that the limits coincide (and are deterministic), not that the finite-n errors are equal a.s.
  - The title “Non-linear = Linear” risks overclaiming; the reduction depends crucially on i.i.d. Gaussian A and asymptotics.
- Assumptions and notation are opaque in crucial places:
  - Assumption 1 uses a cumbersome and ambiguous expression “(1/n) e_{√n (f*)}^{(n), c3}(c1 h + c2 x0)” and mixes superscripts/subscripts in a way that is hard to parse. Please formalize the exact sequence of functions, the scaling (√n), and what “point-wise limit … exists with probability one” means (is it for each triple (c1,c2,c3)? uniform on compacts?).
  - In Section 2.1, the paper uses x0 both for the random unnormalized vector and its normalized version x0 := x0 / ||x0||2. This overloading is confusing; please use distinct symbols consistently throughout.
  - The assumption “μ and σ^2 are nonzero and bounded” is unclear: bounded in what sense (uniformly in n if g depends on n)? Is “nonzero” meant as “μ ≠ 0” and “σ^2 > 0”? Please separate identifiability (μ ≠ 0) from finite-variance (σ^2 < ∞) and discuss implications (e.g., even links where μ = 0).
- Scaling/normalization choices are nonstandard and insufficiently motivated:
  - A has N(0,1) entries, while many LASSO analyses use N(0,1/n) or scale the loss appropriately; you introduce a factor 1/√n in (9). Please justify this scaling, show equivalence to more standard normalizations, and ensure consistency across results.
  - Condition (b) in Section 2.1 uses n^(-1/2) f(x) and n^(-1/2) max ||s|| bounds; verify and explain these for the cited regularizers (e.g., nuclear norm with your √d scaling).
- Completeness/rigor of specializations:
  - Sparse case: Theorem 2.3’s fixed-point system (14)–(15) is plausible, but uniqueness claims (λcrit, κcrit, κ*(λ)) need conditions. Equation (15) is written as E[(η(...) · h)] — clarify whether this means E[η(...) h]. Define all expectations precisely and state the regularity on qX0 ensuring existence/uniqueness.
  - Group-sparse/low-rank: The definition of F and its computation are sketched at a very high level. For low-rank, the needed spectral distribution assumptions are only hinted; providing at least one fully worked example (e.g., spiked model) would strengthen the contribution.
- Experiments/empirics are insufficiently documented:
  - Figures are referenced but not included; no details on n, m, λ tuning, distributions, or error bars. Given the “precise” claims, rigorous numerical validation across regimes (δ < 1, δ > 1, different g, λ) is needed.
  - No discussion of practical selection of λ, despite emphasizing that the theory holds for all λ and the appearance of λcrit in the sparse case.
- Literature and presentation issues:
  - Related work is not fully up to date nor clearly contrasted (e.g., AMP-based precise analyses vs CGMT variants; more discussion on Yi et al. 2015 and other single-index estimation results).
  - Many references are incomplete or malformed (missing years/venues; inconsistent titles). Numerous typos and grammatical issues (“mesaurements,” “eventhough,” “redifining,” etc.), and awkward footnotes and numbering (e.g., footnote 5 inline).
  - The program in (10) mixes variables and the abstract F(c1,c2,c3) with scant intuition. A short derivation or interpretation would help.

Quality
- Promising theoretical contribution, but key statements are imprecisely worded, and core assumptions/notation are unclear. Appendices with proofs are referenced but not available here. Experimental support is minimal.

Clarity
- The paper needs substantial rewriting for precision and readability: consistent notation, clear assumptions, corrected theorem statements, and better exposition of F and Assumption 1.

Originality
- The reduction and precise asymptotics for regularized LASSO with general convex f under non-linear measurements (Gaussian design) appear novel and significant beyond Plan–Vershynin’s bounds and prior precise results for linear measurements.

Significance
- If cleaned up and fully substantiated, the results would be impactful for theory and practice (e.g., guidance for quantization design). Currently, the presentation gaps impede adoption.

Pros
- Conceptually elegant reduction with clear practical corollaries (μ, σ^2 govern performance).
- General framework (CGMT) potentially covering many regularizers.
- Concrete specializations and quantization design angle.

Cons
- Imprecise/possibly overstated Theorem 2.1.
- Opaque Assumption 1 and F; unclear notation and scaling.
- Insufficient experimental validation and missing appendices.
- Numerous editorial and referencing issues.

Questions for authors
- Please restate Theorem 2.1 precisely as equality of limits and specify conditions on g ensuring μ ≠ 0, σ^2 < ∞.
- Clarify Assumption 1 rigorously, with precise notation for the Moreau envelope/conjugate scaling and convergence mode.
- Provide full proofs (or at least detailed sketches) in the appendix and include them in the submission.
- Add comprehensive experiments with varied g, δ, λ (including λ selection), and compare to constrained LASSO/AMP predictions.
- For low-rank, give at least one explicit F(c1,c2,c3) computation.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The contribution is potentially strong, but the current manuscript has substantial clarity, rigor, and presentation issues that, taken together, prevent reliable assessment. With significant revision, this work could merit acceptance in a future cycle.

Confidence: High.
