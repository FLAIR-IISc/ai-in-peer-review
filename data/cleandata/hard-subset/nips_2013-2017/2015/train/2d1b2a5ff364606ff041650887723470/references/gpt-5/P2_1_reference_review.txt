Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies generalized LASSO with non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where A has i.i.d. Gaussian entries and f is a convex regularizer. Its main claim is an asymptotically exact equivalence: in high dimensions, the squared error of the generalized LASSO under these non-linear measurements equals the error one would obtain with an effective linear model y = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2] for γ ~ N(0,1). Building on this, the authors derive a precise characterization of the asymptotic MSE via a simple three-variable convex program whose value depends on δ = m/n, λ, the link through (μ,σ), and the structure/regularizer through a functional F defined via a Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate.

This work extends a line of results on (i) Brillinger’s classical consistency of least squares under link misspecification (n fixed, m → ∞), (ii) Plan and Vershynin’s non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO under single-index models that already highlight the roles of μ and σ, and (iii) precise error characterizations for LASSO with linear measurements via AMP and the CGMT (Bayati-Montanari; Stojnic; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.). The novelty here is an asymptotically exact mapping from non-linear to linear measurements together with a precise MSE characterization for generalized LASSO that holds for broad classes of convex regularizers, not merely ℓ1, and covers regimes δ < 1. Specializations to sparse and group-sparse cases yield concrete scalar fixed-point characterizations; a low-rank discussion outlines how to handle nuclear norm under assumptions on the singular value empirical distribution. The paper also leverages the equivalence to design optimal q-bit quantizers via minimizing σ^2/μ^2 and connects this to Lloyd–Max.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The results are carefully stated with clear assumptions (Gaussian A, independent gi, μ ≠ 0, σ^2 bounded; linear asymptotics; a regularizer normalization; and Assumption 1 on the pointwise convergence of a Moreau-envelope-based functional F). These are standard in precise LASSO analyses using CGMT. The equivalence theorem is plausible given Gaussian rotational invariance, and the derived convex scalar program matches prior linear-measurement results when g is linear. The sparse specialization reproduces known formulas when qX0 is Gaussian and extends them more generally. Numerical experiments (as described) corroborate the predictions.
- Caveats: Assumption 1 is somewhat opaque to non-experts; while the authors verify it for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2 and sketch it for nuclear norm, the low-rank section leaves details and practical evaluation of F to future work. The analysis relies crucially on Gaussian A; there is no robustness claim to sub-Gaussian or correlated designs. The results are asymptotic; no finite-sample deviation bounds are provided. The equivalence requires μ ≠ 0; while this is discussed (even links produce μ=0), guidance for μ unknown is limited.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized: motivation, setup, main equivalence theorem, precise error characterization, and specializations. The role of μ and σ is made explicit, and their operational meaning is nicely illustrated by the quantization example. However, Assumption 1 could be expanded with more intuition and a short list of regularizers for which it holds, with proofs or references. The low-rank section would benefit from a concrete worked example (e.g., spiked model) and explicit expression of F. It would also help to state clearly the probability spaces for “with probability one” and whether the same A is used in linear and non-linear problems (it appears so).

Originality:
- High. While the dependence on μ and σ was known qualitatively and via bounds (Plan–Vershynin), and precise LASSO error characterizations existed for linear measurements, this work unifies them by proving an asymptotically exact equivalence and giving precise MSE formulas for generalized LASSO under non-linear single-index measurements and general convex regularizers. The application to quantizer design via σ^2/μ^2 minimization and the LM connection is also a nice novel byproduct.

Significance:
- High. The result is conceptually clean and practically useful: it allows researchers to directly import a rich body of precise LASSO error results for linear models to a broad class of non-linear observation models by simply substituting (μ,σ). It provides insight into regularizer and λ tuning, sample complexity trade-offs (via δ), and measurement design (e.g., quantization thresholds). This is likely to influence work in one-bit and quantized compressed sensing, phase retrieval variants with preprocessing, and robust regression under link misspecification.

Pros:
- Clear, elegant equivalence principle with precise asymptotics.
- Broad coverage of convex regularizers; explicit sparse and group-sparse specializations.
- Simple convex scalar program to compute asymptotic MSE.
- Useful practical implication for quantizer design; LM connection.
- Solid grounding in and extension of CGMT-based precise analyses.

Cons:
- Strong Gaussian design assumption; no robustness guarantees to sub-Gaussian/non-isotropic A.
- Asymptotic only; lacking finite-sample error bars or rates of convergence.
- Assumption 1 is technical and, for nuclear norm, verification is incomplete.
- Limited empirical evaluation in the excerpt; more diverse simulations (non-Gaussian x0, different g, finite-n accuracy) would help.
- Discussion of μ estimation when g is unknown is minimal; in practice one may need plug-in estimators.

Suggestions and questions:
- Provide a brief intuitive explanation for Assumption 1 and a table listing regularizers for which it provably holds, with references or proof sketches (including nuclear norm under common low-rank priors).
- Discuss whether the equivalence extends to sub-Gaussian A or to Gaussian designs with covariance Σ (with appropriate whitening and modified μ,σ).
- Add experiments demonstrating robustness to mild model misspecification (e.g., sub-Gaussian A, slight dependence in gi).
- Include guidance on estimating μ and σ from data when g is unknown (e.g., moment-based estimators exploiting E[a_i y_i] and E[y_i^2]) and assess the effect on performance.
- Clarify the precise meaning of “with probability one” in Theorems 2.1–2.2 and whether A is shared between the non-linear and linearized problems.

Recommendation:
Strong accept. The paper makes a clean and impactful theoretical contribution with clear implications for practice. While some assumptions are idealized and certain technical parts could be clarified/extended, the core result is novel, technically solid, and significant.
