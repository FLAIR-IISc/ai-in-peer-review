

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by establishing a fundamental equivalence between LASSO with non-linear measurements and LASSO with appropriately scaled linear measurements. The authors demonstrate that for a wide class of non-linear link functions g, the asymptotic behavior of the Generalized LASSO under measurements y_i = g(a_i^T x_0) is identical to that of the LASSO under linear measurements y_i = μa_i^T x_0 + σz_i, where μ and σ^2 are defined in terms of the link function g (as in equation 3). This equivalence holds with high probability in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime (m, n → ∞ with m/n = δ).

The paper builds on the work of Plan and Vershynin [17], who provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements, but extends their results to provide precise error predictions for the regularized LASSO. The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 2.1, which establishes the equivalence, and Theorem 2.2, which provides a convex optimization framework for precisely predicting the error. The authors further specialize these results to important structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and demonstrate applications to q-bit compressive sensing.

**Strengths:**

1. **High Novelty:** The core insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements with specific scaling and noise parameters is fundamentally new and conceptually powerful. This represents a significant shift in how we should view LASSO with non-linear measurements, moving from ad-hoc analyses to a unified framework.

2. **Precise Characterization:** Unlike previous work that provided only loose upper bounds, the authors deliver precise asymptotic error predictions, which are valuable for both theoretical understanding and practical applications.

3. **Broad Applicability:** The results cover a wide range of structured signals (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and non-linear link functions, making the framework broadly applicable across compressed sensing, statistical learning, and signal processing.

4. **Practical Impact:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) demonstrates how the theoretical results can guide practical design decisions, such as optimal quantization thresholds, which is particularly valuable for hardware-constrained applications.

5. **Rigorous Analysis:** The proofs are technically sound and leverage the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework, which has been successfully applied to LASSO analysis in previous works.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Asymptotic Assumptions:** The results are strictly asymptotic (n → ∞), which may limit immediate practical utility for finite-dimensional problems. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and note that the asymptotic predictions often match finite-sample behavior well (as shown in Figures 1-2).

2. **Unit Norm Assumption:** The paper assumes x_0 has unit Euclidean norm, which excludes some cases (e.g., even link functions). While the authors note this can be addressed by redefining g, it's a minor technical limitation.

3. **Limited Experimental Validation:** While the theoretical predictions are supported by figures showing good agreement with empirical results, more extensive experiments across diverse problem instances would strengthen the practical relevance.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of LASSO under non-linear measurements. The core insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements is novel, elegant, and has broad implications for both theory and practice. While the work is theoretically focused, the application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates clear practical relevance.

The paper successfully addresses a fundamental question that has been open in the literature (as noted by the authors' references to Brillinger [5] and Plan and Vershynin [17]). Previous work provided only loose upper bounds or asymptotic results for specific cases, but this paper establishes a precise, general equivalence that unifies the analysis across different measurement models.

The novelty of the main insight (Theorem 2.1) is exceptional - it's not merely an incremental improvement but a conceptual shift in how we should view LASSO with non-linear measurements. The paper's focus on precise error prediction rather than just upper bounds represents a significant advance over previous work.

Given the reviewer's emphasis on novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. The theoretical contribution is substantial, the analysis is rigorous, and the practical implications are clear. The minor limitations (asymptotic regime, unit norm assumption) are well-understood and do not detract from the paper's fundamental contribution.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a significant conceptual advance that will likely influence future work on structured signal recovery with non-linear measurements. The equivalence result is both elegant and powerful, providing a unifying framework for understanding LASSO performance across different measurement models.
