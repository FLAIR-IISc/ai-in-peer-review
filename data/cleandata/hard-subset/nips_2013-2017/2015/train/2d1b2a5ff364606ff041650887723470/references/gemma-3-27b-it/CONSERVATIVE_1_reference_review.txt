This paper presents a theoretical result demonstrating the equivalence, in asymptotic performance, between the LASSO applied to non-linear measurements and its application to scaled, noisy linear measurements. While the underlying problem – single-index models – is well-established, and the connection to Brillinger’s earlier work is acknowledged, the authors claim to extend these results to high-dimensional settings and provide precise error characterizations. 

The core idea – characterizing non-linear measurements through a scaling factor and noise variance – is conceptually appealing and builds upon recent advances in understanding LASSO performance with linear measurements. The technical work appears rigorous, deriving precise expressions for the error based on the properties of the link function. The connection to existing work on constrained LASSO (Plan & Vershynin) and precise error analysis (Stojnic, Thrampoulidis et al.) is also clearly drawn. However, despite the technical sophistication, the paper feels largely theoretical and lacks a convincing demonstration of practical impact.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The mathematical derivations appear sound and build upon a strong foundation of existing literature in high-dimensional statistics and signal processing.
*   **Precise Results:** The authors obtain precise asymptotic error characterizations, a significant advancement over existing bounds.
*   **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively situates itself within the broader landscape of LASSO analysis and single-index models.
*   **Identified Key Parameters:** The emphasis on µ and σ² as characterizing the non-linearity is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** While the extension to high dimensions and precise error characterization are valuable, the core result – that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements – feels like a natural consequence of existing theory. The claim of substantial novelty feels overstated.
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. There is *no* experimental evaluation demonstrating the accuracy of the derived error formulas, even on simulated data. The figures (mentioned but not included in this submission) are crucial for validating the theoretical results and establishing practical relevance. Without them, the contribution remains abstract.
*   **Strong Assumptions:** The assumption of Gaussian measurement vectors and the unit norm constraint on x0, while common, limit the applicability of the results. While the authors mention generalizations, they are not thoroughly explored.
*   **Practical Impact Unclear:** It is not immediately obvious how these theoretical results translate into improved practical algorithms or insights for real-world applications. The discussion of q-bit compressive sensing feels tacked on and underdeveloped. Simply stating that the LM algorithm *can* be applied isn't sufficient; a demonstration of its efficacy is required.
*   **Overly Dense Notation:** While technical rigor is appreciated, the paper suffers from a density of notation that makes it difficult to follow the core arguments without careful study.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and mathematically rigorous analysis of the LASSO with non-linear measurements. However, its impact is limited by its purely theoretical nature and lack of empirical validation. The claim of significant novelty feels exaggerated, as the core result is a natural extension of existing theory. The assumptions are strong, and the practical implications are not clearly articulated. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a precise theoretical characterization of LASSO performance with non-linear measurements, which could be valuable to researchers in signal processing and statistics.
*   The rigorous mathematical analysis is a strength.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation severely weakens the paper’s claims.
*   The limited novelty and unclear practical impact do not justify acceptance to NIPS.
*   The strong assumptions limit the generalizability of the results.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject**. While the paper demonstrates technical competence, it falls short of the originality and significance required for acceptance to NIPS. The authors need to include substantial experimental validation and clarify the practical implications of their work before it can be considered a significant contribution. The absence of the figures makes it even more difficult to assess the value of this work.
