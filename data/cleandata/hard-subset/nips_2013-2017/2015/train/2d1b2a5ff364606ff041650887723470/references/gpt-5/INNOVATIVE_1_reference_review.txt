Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation from non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian measurement vectors and structured x0 estimated via generalized LASSO. The central claim is a precise “nonlinear = linear” equivalence: for a broad class of convex regularizers and link functions, the asymptotic mean-squared error (MSE) of the LASSO under non-linear measurements is exactly the same as the MSE under an equivalent scaled-and-noisy linear model y = µ A x0 + σ z, where µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µ γ)^2] for γ ∼ N(0,1). Building on CGMT-style analysis, the authors provide (i) a theorem formalizing this equivalence and (ii) an explicit scalar convex optimization that yields the asymptotic MSE for any λ, δ, f, and distribution of x0 encoded via a function F. They then specialize to sparse (including non-Gaussian coefficients), group-sparse, and low-rank settings, and discuss an application to quantized compressive sensing where the equivalence leads to a simple quantizer design criterion (minimize σ^2/µ^2), with a connection to Lloyd–Max.

This generalizes classical results of Brillinger (LS with fixed n, m→∞) to high-dimensional regimes and regularized estimators, and turns the non-asymptotic upper bounds of Plan & Vershynin (constrained LASSO) into precise asymptotics for the regularized LASSO. It also extends precise LASSO risk analyses previously available for linear measurements (AMP/CGMT-based works) to non-linear measurement channels.

Strengths
- Novel and bold conceptual contribution: a clean and exact asymptotic equivalence principle that reduces a wide class of non-linear measurement models to an effective linear observation model for the purpose of LASSO risk. This has strong unifying value and practical implications (e.g., tuning λ, choosing quantizers).
- Technical depth and generality: The scalar convex program (Eq. 10) provides a precise risk characterization across λ, δ, f, and structured priors, beyond earlier linear-only results. The sparse specialization (Theorem 2.3) improves prior assumptions (e.g., removes Gaussianity of nonzeros for `2-LASSO).
- Useful application: For q-bit quantization, the equivalence yields a simple design objective σ^2/µ^2 and a principled argument that Lloyd–Max fixed points are stationary points of this objective.
- Clear connections to and extensions of Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin; relates to CGMT/AMP lines of work.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Statement precision: Theorem 2.1 currently reads as an almost-sure equality of errors, not just of limits. Given the asymptotic framework, it should state equality of asymptotic MSE limits (or convergence of errors), not equality of the finite-n random errors. Please clarify.
- Assumptions and scope: The Gaussian A assumption is essential for CGMT; discussion of how far results extend to rotationally-invariant or sub-Gaussian ensembles is brief. Many practical designs use structured A (partial Fourier, subsampled Hadamard); even a conjectural extension or empirical evidence would strengthen the paper.
- µ ≠ 0 is assumed; important regimes like even link functions (e.g., sign without dithering, phase retrieval-style squaring) are excluded. The paper briefly notes alternatives, but the practical scope would benefit from a discussion of what can be salvaged when µ is small/zero (e.g., directional recovery, alternative algorithms).
- Assumption 1 is technical and its verification is deferred outside a few canonical cases; for low-rank, the section is more a blueprint than a full result (relies on singular value distribution convergence, leaves evaluation of F to future work). This feels incomplete for that setting.
- Empirical validation: The figures (not included here) reportedly show excellent matches, but the experimental section appears relatively light. A broader set of g’s (sign with/without noise, ReLU, multi-bit quantizers), λ sweeps, δ regimes, and mismatched/non-Gaussian A would help substantiate robustness.

Quality
The analysis appears technically sound and carefully connected to prior precise-risk literature. The convex scalar program is a valuable deliverable. The sparse specialization is well-worked and novel beyond [20]. The quantizer design angle is insightful. Some clarity issues (theorem phrasing, typos) and the partial low-rank treatment could be improved, but these are not fundamental.

Clarity
Overall well-organized, but dense. Suggestions:
- Fix Theorem 2.1 phrasing to emphasize equality of asymptotic errors.
- Clearly state all domains and uniqueness conditions for Eq. (10) and Theorem 2.3; briefly sketch why α* is unique and the program is convex in the relevant variables.
- Collect assumptions in one place (µ, σ^2 finite, µ ≠ 0, iid gi, Gaussian A) and discuss practical diagnostics for estimating µ, σ from data when g is unknown.
- Proof sketches for Theorems 2.1–2.2 in the main text would aid accessibility.

Originality
High. Asymptotically exact equivalence for LASSO under non-linear channels is, to my knowledge, new and impactful. The precise analysis beyond `1 and beyond linear measurements is a meaningful step.

Significance
High. This provides a unifying principle that many researchers and practitioners can build on: use linear-LASSO risk formulas with effective SNR determined by g. The quantizer design corollary is immediately useful.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you comment on extensions beyond Gaussian A (e.g., rotationally invariant, or universality evidence via simulation)?
2) Is there a data-driven way to estimate µ and σ^2 when g is unknown (method-of-moments from (y, Ax) proxies)? Any consistency guarantees?
3) How sensitive are the predictions when µ is small? Do you observe instability in practice?
4) For low-rank, can you provide at least one concrete distributional example (e.g., spiked model) where F can be computed and include a numerical validation?
5) Could you release code to solve Eq. (10) reliably and reproduce figures?

Minor comments/typos
- Multiple typos: “mesaurements”, “eventhough”, “redifining”, “gaussianity”, etc.
- Clarify whether ζ in (7) is used later; otherwise omit or relate to σ^2.
- Some references lack full details; please standardize.

Recommendation: Accept
This paper presents a bold and elegant equivalence principle with precise, general asymptotics and practical consequences. Despite some limitations (Gaussian A, µ ≠ 0, partial low-rank treatment), the novelty and potential impact are high, and the technical quality is strong. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to clarify statements and strengthen empirical breadth. Confidence: high.
