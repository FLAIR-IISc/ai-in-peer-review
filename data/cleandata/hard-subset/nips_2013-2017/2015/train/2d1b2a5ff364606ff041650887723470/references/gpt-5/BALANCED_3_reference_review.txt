Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under a single-index nonlinear measurement model yi = gi(a_i^T x0), with A having i.i.d. N(0,1) entries and x0 structured. The main result (Theorem 2.1) establishes an exact asymptotic equivalence between the generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements and a LASSO with linear, scaled-and-noisy measurements: ylin = µ A x0 + σ z, where µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µ γ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). Thus, up to an unavoidable scaling, the asymptotic mean-squared error (MSE) of the LASSO with nonlinear measurements equals that of a linear model with effective SNR determined by (µ, σ^2).

Building on this, Theorem 2.2 provides a precise asymptotic MSE formula via a convex scalar optimization depending on δ = m/n, λ, and a functional F encoding the structure via the regularizer f and the distribution of x0 (through the Moreau envelope of f*). The authors further specialize to (i) sparse signals with ℓ1 regularization, yielding fixed-point equations and a critical-λ transition (Theorem 2.3), (ii) group-sparse signals with ℓ1,2 regularization, and (iii) outline low-rank recovery with nuclear norm. As an application, they show how the equivalence reduces q-bit quantizer design for compressed sensing to minimizing σ^2/µ^2; they argue Lloyd–Max iterations yield stationary points for this objective.

This work connects and sharpens several lines of research: Brillinger’s classical consistency of least squares under link misspecification (fixed n, m→∞), Plan & Vershynin’s non-asymptotic bounds for constrained LASSO under nonlinear links (up to µ-scaling), and the recent precise analyses of LASSO under linear Gaussian designs (via AMP or CGMT). The paper leverages CGMT-style arguments to turn the non-asymptotic insights of Plan & Vershynin into exact asymptotics and unifies the treatment of many convex regularizers.

Strengths:
- Clear and compelling main message: for Gaussian designs, generalized LASSO “sees” nonlinear measurements as scaled noisy linear ones, with effective parameters determined by g. This is both intuitive and practically useful.
- Asymptotically exact, not just upper bounds; the scalar convex program makes the MSE prediction computationally tractable and amenable to parameter tuning.
- Broad applicability across convex regularizers; specialized results for sparse and group-sparse are clean and practically relevant. Recovering known results (e.g., for ℓ2-LASSO with Gaussian nonzeros) while extending to more general settings increases confidence.
- The quantization application is neat and shows genuine design implications; recasting quantizer optimization in terms of σ^2/µ^2 is insightful.
- Good contextualization with Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin; appropriate use of modern tools (CGMT).

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Scope of assumptions: i.i.d. Gaussian design, independence of gi, and linear asymptotics (n,m→∞ with fixed δ). The equivalence may fail for sub-Gaussian but non-Gaussian designs, correlated rows, or non-rotationally invariant matrices; the paper should more clearly state which parts rely critically on Gaussian rotational invariance.
- Assumption 1 (existence of the pointwise limit defining F) is abstract. While the paper checks it for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2, the low-rank case is only sketched. A clearer statement of sufficient conditions for general f and px0 (beyond examples) would improve rigor and usability.
- Theorem 2.1’s statement “with probability one, ‖x̂ − µx0‖^2 = ‖x̂_lin − µx0‖^2 in the limit n→∞” could be clarified: is this almost sure equality of the limits, or limit of the difference equals zero? Precision in the mode of convergence would help.
- Practicality when g is unknown: in many single-index settings, µ and σ^2 are unknown. The paper acknowledges the scaling ambiguity, but it would be valuable to discuss estimation of µ, σ^2 from data (e.g., method-of-moments using (A,y)) and the robustness of plug-in approaches.
- Experimental validation seems limited in the text (figures referenced but not shown here). More diverse nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU/censoring, saturating, logistic), a range of δ and λ, and finite-sample accuracy plots would strengthen the empirical case.
- Bibliography is incomplete; some citations lack full details.

Quality:
- The technical development appears sound and well aligned with prior precise LASSO analyses; the extension to nonlinear links is nontrivial and likely leverages rotational invariance and CGMT. The sparse specialization is carefully derived. However, the low-rank section is more of a roadmap than a finished result.

Clarity:
- Generally clear, with helpful pointers to related literature. The normalization conventions and assumptions are stated, but Assumption 1 and the convergence claims could be made more explicit. Some notational choices (e.g., reusing σ for both link-induced noise and signal variance) could be unified.

Originality:
- The nonlinear-to-linear equivalence for generalized LASSO with exact asymptotics appears novel and important. Prior works gave upper bounds or handled special cases; the present paper unifies and sharpens those results.

Significance:
- High for theory of high-dimensional estimation and practitioners who use LASSO under unknown nonlinear distortions (quantization, saturations, censoring). The parameter design/tuning implications and the quantizer design example are valuable.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, clean, and impactful theoretical result (exact asymptotics) with broad applicability.
- Bridges classical (Brillinger) and modern (CGMT/AMP) literatures; clarifies the role of g via (µ,σ^2).
- Useful specializations and a concrete design application.

Cons for acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on Gaussian designs; unclear robustness beyond that setting.
- Abstract Assumption 1; low-rank treatment incomplete.
- Limited discussion of how to handle unknown µ,σ^2 and finite-sample behavior.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the exact mode of convergence in Theorem 2.1 and Theorem 2.2; tighten statement language.
- Provide explicit sufficient conditions ensuring Assumption 1 for common f and px0; for the low-rank case, include a concrete worked-out F for a canonical random low-rank model.
- Add experiments across diverse nonlinearities (1-bit, ReLU/censoring, saturating, logistic), varying δ and λ, and show finite-sample convergence to the asymptotic predictions.
- Discuss estimation of µ and σ^2 from data when g is unknown; propose and test plug-in procedures.
- Comment on robustness to sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant designs; if possible, sketch whether extensions are feasible.
- Improve citation completeness and minor notational consistency.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper offers a substantial, elegant theoretical contribution with clear implications and aligns well with NeurIPS’s focus on foundational advances in ML/statistics.
