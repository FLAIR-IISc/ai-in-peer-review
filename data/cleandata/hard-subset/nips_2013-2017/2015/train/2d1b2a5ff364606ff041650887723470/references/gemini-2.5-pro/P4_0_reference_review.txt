### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a structured signal vector `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`, a setup known as the single-index model. The authors analyze the performance of the Generalized LASSO estimator, which is nominally designed for linear models, when applied directly to these non-linear observations. The central and striking contribution is a proof that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio `m/n`, the performance of the LASSO is asymptotically equivalent to its performance on a related linear model. Specifically, the non-linear measurement model is shown to behave identically to a linear model with additive Gaussian noise: `yi = µa_i^T x0 + σzi`. The scaling factor `µ = E[γg(γ)]` and effective noise variance `σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - µγ)^2]` (where `γ` is a standard normal random variable) depend only on the statistics of the (possibly unknown) link function `g`.

This work builds upon and significantly extends two distinct lines of research. First, it generalizes a classical result by Brillinger [5], who showed for unregularized least-squares that the estimate converges to `µx0` in a classical, fixed-`n` asymptotic setting. This paper extends this principle to the regularized, high-dimensional setting. Second, it sharpens recent non-asymptotic upper bounds by Plan and Vershynin [17] for a constrained LASSO variant, which first suggested the important role of the parameters `µ` and `σ`. The present work moves from loose upper bounds to precise, asymptotic error characterizations for the more common regularized LASSO formulation. Finally, the paper's methodology is rooted in the recent, powerful framework for precise asymptotic analysis of convex optimization problems, such as the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT), which has been used to characterize LASSO performance in the linear setting [2, 16, 19, 20]. The key novelty is the successful application and extension of this framework to the non-linear measurement case.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The main result is both elegant and powerful. It establishes a simple, intuitive, and rigorously proven equivalence principle: for the purposes of LASSO estimation, a wide class of non-linear measurement functions can be replaced by a simple scaling and an effective Gaussian noise term. This provides a deep insight into the behavior of a standard algorithm in a non-standard setting and dramatically simplifies the analysis of such systems. The application to optimal `q`-bit quantizer design demonstrates the practical utility of this simplification.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to provide an asymptotically exact performance characterization for the regularized LASSO with general non-linear measurements. By proving this "linear equivalence," the paper bridges the gap between the analysis of single-index models and the modern high-dimensional analysis of LASSO. The work also claims to provide new precise performance characterizations for the linear LASSO with regularizers beyond the `l1`-norm (e.g., group-LASSO, nuclear norm), which is a valuable contribution in its own right.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper appears to be technically sound. The theoretical claims are supported by a rigorous framework (CGMT), and the derived analytical predictions show excellent agreement with numerical simulations across various settings (sparse, group-sparse signals; different link functions), which lends strong credibility to the results. The problem is well-defined, and the theorems are stated with precision.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem, reviews the relevant literature, and summarizes the contributions. The main results (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) are presented first, followed by clear specializations to important practical cases. This structure makes the paper's core message easy to grasp.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Restrictive Assumptions:** The analysis relies on two key assumptions that limit its immediate generality. First is the use of an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix `A`. While this is a common assumption in the literature on precise asymptotic analysis, the behavior of the LASSO with more general sub-gaussian or structured random matrices remains an important open question. Second, the results are asymptotic (`n, m -> infinity`). While simulations show the predictions are accurate for moderately large `n`, any discussion on the non-asymptotic applicability or potential finite-sample deviations would be welcome.

2.  **Limitation to `µ ≠ 0`:** The analysis is valid only for link functions `g` where `µ = E[γg(γ)] ≠ 0`. This is acknowledged in a footnote but is a significant limitation as it excludes, for example, any even link function (`g(x) = |x|`) or other symmetric scenarios where the sign information is lost. A more prominent discussion of this limitation in the main body of the paper, perhaps outlining why this case is fundamentally different, would be beneficial.

3.  **Clarity of Assumption 1:** The general result in Theorem 2.2 hinges on "Assumption 1," which concerns the convergence of the Moreau envelope of the regularizer's conjugate function. While this assumption is shown to hold for the specific examples considered, a more intuitive explanation of its meaning and the class of regularizers for which it is expected to hold would improve the paper's self-containedness and accessibility.

### Justification for Rating

The paper presents a highly significant, novel, and technically strong result. The equivalence between the non-linear measurement model and an effective linear model for LASSO is a fundamental insight that has the potential for high impact, both in guiding theoretical analysis and in practical system design. The weaknesses, primarily related to the standard assumptions of the underlying analytical framework (Gaussian matrix, asymptotics), do not detract from the importance of the core contribution. The paper is well-written, places itself clearly within the literature, and validates its theory with compelling numerical evidence.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel, fundamental, and elegant equivalence principle for LASSO with non-linear measurements.
*   Extends the powerful framework of precise asymptotic analysis to a new and important problem domain.
*   The results are precise characterizations, a significant improvement over previous upper bounds.
*   The paper is very clearly written and the results are well-supported by simulations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis relies on a restrictive i.i.d. Gaussian matrix assumption.
*   The results are asymptotic in nature.
*   The `µ ≠ 0` condition excludes certain classes of non-linearities.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This is a top-tier theoretical paper that makes a substantial contribution to the field.
