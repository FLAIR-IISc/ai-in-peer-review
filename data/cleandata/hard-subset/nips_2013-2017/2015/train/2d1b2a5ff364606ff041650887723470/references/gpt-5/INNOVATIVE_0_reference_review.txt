Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation from non-linear single-index observations yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian design A and convex regularization f via the generalized LASSO. The central claim is an asymptotic “equivalence principle”: for LASSO, non-linear measurements behave exactly like linear measurements with an effective scaling µ = E[γg(γ)] and additive Gaussian noise variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ)−µγ)^2], with γ∼N(0,1). Theorem 2.1 formalizes this by showing the asymptotic MSE of LASSO with non-linear observations equals that of LASSO under the corresponding linearized model ylin = µAx0 + σz. Theorem 2.2 provides a precise error characterization via a three-scalar convex optimization depending on δ=m/n, λ, f (through a limit functional F defined via the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate), and (µ,σ^2). The authors then work out specializations for sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and sketch conditions for low-rank (nuclear norm), including an explicit sparse-case formula (Theorem 2.3) that recovers and generalizes prior AMP/CGMT results for linear measurements. They illustrate an application to q-bit quantized sensing: optimizing thresholds/levels reduces to minimizing σ^2/µ^2, with a Lloyd–Max procedure producing stationary points for this objective.

This work extends classic results of Brillinger on LS under link mismatch (fixed n, m→∞) to the high-dimensional regime, and sharpens the non-asymptotic bounds of Plan–Vershynin for constrained LASSO by giving asymptotically exact errors for the regularized LASSO. It also synthesizes and generalizes recent precise LASSO analyses under linear measurements (AMP, CGMT) by showing how to fold non-linearities into µ,σ^2.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clean and powerful equivalence principle that collapses a broad class of non-linear observation models into an effective linear-noise surrogate for the purpose of LASSO error analysis.
- Technical strength: asymptotically exact characterization (not just bounds), valid for a wide range of convex regularizers and all λ, framed as a low-dimensional convex program that is numerically tractable.
- Broad applicability: covers sparse, group-sparse, and sketches of low-rank; includes practical guidance (e.g., quantizer design via minimizing σ^2/µ^2).
- Clear connection and improvement over prior art: recovers Brillinger in high-dimensional LS, sharpens Plan–Vershynin, and extends precise linear LASSO error results to non-linear measurements.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumption 1 is abstract and somewhat opaque. The paper gives concrete verifications for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2, but the low-rank/nuclear-norm case is left as “future work,” despite being prominently advertised. Providing at least one fully worked-out low-rank example would strengthen the paper.
- Gaussian design only. The equivalence and CGMT-based analysis require i.i.d. Gaussian A. It is unclear how robust the results are to sub-Gaussian or structured designs commonly used in practice; even empirical evidence would help.
- µ≠0 requirement excludes important links (e.g., even functions like |·| or sign without dithering), where direction-only recovery is possible. The paper acknowledges this but does not discuss workarounds (e.g., dithering or alternative estimators) beyond a brief note.
- Experimental validation is mentioned but not detailed in the text provided. Finite-sample behavior, sensitivity to λ, and deviations from Gaussian A would be valuable.
- Relation to the GLM-AMP/GAMP literature could be strengthened. There is a rich body of work that characterizes estimation under non-linear links with Gaussian design via state evolution; positioning this result relative to those (e.g., showing consistency with GLM-AMP predictions for LASSO or discussing differences) would improve context.

Quality
The technical claims are ambitious but plausible, building on solid CGMT foundations and prior precise LASSO analyses. The sparse and group-sparse specializations are carefully derived; the nuclear-norm case needs more substance. The quantizer optimization angle (minimizing σ^2/µ^2 and LM connection) is insightful and practical.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with a strong narrative from prior work to main results to special cases and application. Some typos and minor inconsistencies (e.g., “mesaurements,” notational slips) should be cleaned. The statement of Theorem 2.1 should clarify it asserts equality of asymptotic errors (limits), not equality at finite n. A more accessible sufficient condition for Assumption 1 would improve readability.

Originality
High. While µ,σ^2 reductions appear in earlier works (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin bounds) and GLM-AMP intuitions, establishing an exact asymptotic equivalence for the LASSO and providing a unified convex-analytic recipe for precise error across general convex f is, to my knowledge, new.

Significance
High. This result gives a unifying lens on a large class of non-linear acquisition models, effectively allowing practitioners to reuse the extensive precise theory (and intuition) for LASSO under linear measurements by computing two scalar moments of the link. The quantizer design implication is particularly compelling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a fully worked-out low-rank (nuclear norm) example with an explicit F and numerical validation.
- Add experiments exploring: (i) finite-sample accuracy; (ii) robustness to sub-Gaussian/structured A; (iii) several links (ReLU, logistic, saturated quantizers).
- Expand discussion of µ≈0 cases and practical remedies (e.g., dithering) or alternative estimators.
- Strengthen connections to GLM-AMP/GAMP and related generalized linear estimation literature.
- Give user-facing guidance on λ selection based on the derived formulas (e.g., behavior of λcrit).

Arguments for acceptance
- Bold, general conceptual contribution (non-linear=linear equivalence) with precise, usable formulas.
- Extends and unifies multiple lines of prior work; opens new directions (e.g., principled quantizer design).
- Technically strong and likely to influence both theory and practice.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on Gaussian design; limited empirical validation of robustness.
- Abstract assumption and incomplete treatment for nuclear norm; some clarity issues.
- Limited experimental detail in the current form.

Recommendation
Accept. The core idea is novel and important, and the technical development is substantial. Even with some gaps (nuclear norm, broader experiments), this work sets a new direction and offers a unifying principle that the community can build on.

Confidence
Medium-high.
