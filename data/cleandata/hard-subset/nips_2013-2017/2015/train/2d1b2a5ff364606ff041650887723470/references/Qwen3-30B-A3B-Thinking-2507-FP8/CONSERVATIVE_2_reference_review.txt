

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper claims to establish an asymptotic equivalence between the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements (yi = gi(ai^T x0)) and the standard LASSO with appropriately scaled linear measurements (yi = μai^T x0 + σzi), where μ and σ are derived from the link function g. The authors build on the work of Plan and Vershynin (2015) who considered a constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements, and extend their results to the regularized LASSO setting. The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 2.1 which states that the error of the non-linear LASSO is asymptotically equal to the error of a linear LASSO with scaled measurements.

The paper makes a strong claim that their result "proves in an exact manner the role played by μ and σ2 to which [Plan and Vershynin's] result is only indicative." The authors provide precise error formulas for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank signal recovery, and apply their framework to q-bit compressive sensing to optimize quantization thresholds.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical framework is well-structured and the assumptions are clearly stated.
2. The paper provides a comprehensive literature review of related work, particularly highlighting connections to Brillinger (1980s) and Plan and Vershynin (2015).
3. The mathematical treatment is rigorous, with careful attention to the asymptotic regime and precise error analysis.
4. The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates potential practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Questionable novelty:** The core insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements is not as novel as claimed. The paper's main contribution appears to be extending Plan and Vershynin's constrained LASSO results to the regularized LASSO setting. However, the equivalence they establish (Theorem 2.1) is essentially a direct consequence of their earlier work (Plan and Vershynin, 2015) and the precise error analysis framework developed for linear measurements. The authors state their result "proves in an exact manner" the role of μ and σ2, but the key role of these parameters was already established in Plan and Vershynin's work. The paper would be significantly stronger if it clearly articulated how their result differs from and extends Plan and Vershynin's, rather than simply claiming it's "more precise."

2. **Lack of empirical validation:** The paper provides theoretical analysis but no empirical results to validate the equivalence claim. This is particularly concerning given the practical implications for q-bit compressive sensing. Without experimental results showing that the equivalence holds in finite-dimensional settings, the practical relevance of the theoretical results remains speculative. The authors claim "Figure 1 serves as an illustration" but the review does not include figures, making it impossible to assess their empirical validation.

3. **Overstated significance:** The paper claims to be "the first to derive asymptotically precise results on the performance of any LASSO-type program" for non-linear measurements. However, the authors cite Thrampoulidis et al. (2013) and others who have already provided precise error analysis for the linear case. Their contribution is to extend this analysis to non-linear measurements, but the novelty of this extension is not sufficiently justified.

4. **Technical assumptions:** The paper relies on strong asymptotic assumptions (m/n = δ ∈ (0,∞), n→∞) that may not be realistic for many practical applications. The paper does not discuss the finite-sample behavior of their method, which is crucial for practical implementation.

5. **Limited comparison with existing methods:** The paper does not compare their approach to alternative methods for non-linear measurements, such as those proposed in Yi et al. (2015) for non-linear regression, or the recent work on q-bit compressive sensing by Davenport et al. (2014). Without such comparison, it's difficult to assess whether their approach offers advantages over existing methods.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution, the novelty is overstated and the practical significance is not sufficiently demonstrated. The main result appears to be a straightforward extension of existing work rather than a significant new insight. The lack of empirical validation is particularly concerning for a paper making claims with potential practical implications.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More clearly articulating the precise novelty over Plan and Vershynin (2015)
2. Providing empirical validation of the equivalence claim in finite-dimensional settings
3. Comparing with alternative approaches for non-linear measurements
4. Discussing the practical implications and limitations of their asymptotic results

Given the skepticism toward unproven approaches and the emphasis on rigorous validation, I recommend rejection. The paper's main contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the lack of empirical validation for the central claim undermines its significance. While the theoretical framework is sound, the paper fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or practical relevance to warrant publication at NIPS.
