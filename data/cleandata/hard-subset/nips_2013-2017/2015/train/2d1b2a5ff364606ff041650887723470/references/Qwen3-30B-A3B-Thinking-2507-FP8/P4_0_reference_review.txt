

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary of Main Ideas
This paper addresses the problem of estimating a structured signal x₀ from non-linear measurements yᵢ = gᵢ(aᵢᵀx₀), where gᵢ are independent copies of a link function g. The authors prove that the Generalized LASSO (used for linear measurements) applied to non-linear measurements has an asymptotic error equivalent to that of a linear measurement model with specific scaling (μ) and noise (σ), where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ)-μγ)²] for γ ~ N(0,1). This extends previous work by Brillinger (1980s) who studied least squares for fixed dimension, and Plan & Vershynin (2015) who considered a constrained LASSO for non-linear measurements. The paper provides a precise error formula for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, generalizing recent advances in precise asymptotic analysis of the LASSO with linear measurements.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper establishes rigorous asymptotic results using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework. The main result (Theorem 2.1) is elegantly proven, showing that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements with appropriate noise. The error formula (Theorem 2.2) is precise and general, capturing the role of the link function g, signal structure, regularizer f, and regularization parameter λ.

2. **Novelty**: This is the first paper to derive asymptotically precise results for LASSO-type programs with non-linear measurements. While Plan & Vershynin (2015) considered a constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements, their results were loose upper bounds. This paper provides precise error expressions that generalize previous results for linear measurements, and demonstrates the applicability to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery.

3. **Significance**: The theoretical results have immediate practical implications, particularly for q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3). The paper shows how to optimize quantization thresholds and levels by minimizing the ratio σ²/μ², which is a significant contribution to a practical problem in signal processing. The results help practitioners understand when the LASSO can be effectively used with non-linear measurements.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient context for readers familiar with compressed sensing. The main contributions are clearly articulated in Section 1.1, and the connection to previous work is well-explained. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is particularly well-motivated.

## Weaknesses
1. **Assumption Limitations**: The unit norm assumption for x₀ (Footnote 4) is restrictive for some applications. While the authors note that this can be handled by redefining g, this limitation should be more explicitly discussed in the main text.

2. **Technical Depth of Proofs**: The proof of Theorem 2.1 is deferred to the appendix, which makes it harder for readers to verify the main result. While this is common practice, a brief sketch of the key steps would improve accessibility.

3. **Assumption 1**: The paper requires Assumption 1 to hold for the general result, which may not be immediately obvious to all readers. While the authors demonstrate that this assumption holds for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank signals, a more detailed discussion of its necessity and limitations would strengthen the paper.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- The paper provides a fundamental theoretical advance that extends precise asymptotic analysis from linear to non-linear measurements.
- The results are technically sound, with rigorous proofs and clear connections to previous work.
- The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates practical significance.
- The paper addresses an important question in signal processing: when can we use the LASSO with non-linear measurements?
- The precise error formula generalizes and improves upon previous results for linear measurements.

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- The unit norm assumption for x₀ might be seen as restrictive by some practitioners.
- The proof of the main theorem is deferred to the appendix, which may reduce accessibility.
- Assumption 1 requires additional verification for new signal models, though the paper demonstrates it holds for standard cases.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a significant contribution to the theory of compressed sensing and structured signal recovery. The main result (Theorem 2.1) is elegant and general, providing a precise characterization of the LASSO's behavior with non-linear measurements. The paper extends the state of the art by moving from loose upper bounds to precise error expressions, which is a substantial advancement. The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the practical relevance of the theory.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a novel contribution that advances the field. The results are significant for both theoretical understanding and practical applications in signal processing. While there are minor limitations (the unit norm assumption and deferred proof), these do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

I recommend acceptance to NIPS. The paper addresses a fundamental question with important theoretical and practical implications, and the results are rigorously established and clearly presented.
