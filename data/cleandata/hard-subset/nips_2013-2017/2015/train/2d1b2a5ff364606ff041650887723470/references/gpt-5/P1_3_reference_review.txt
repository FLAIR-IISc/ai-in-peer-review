Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under a general single-index (nonlinear) measurement model yi = gi(a_i^T x0), with Gaussian measurement vectors a_i and potentially unknown nonlinear link function g. The estimator is the generalized LASSO (square-root LASSO) with a convex regularizer f. The main contribution is an “equivalence principle”: for the purpose of asymptotic mean-squared error, using the LASSO on nonlinear measurements is equivalent to using the same LASSO on an effective linear model ylin = µ A x0 + σ z, where µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µ γ)^2] for γ ~ N(0,1). Building on CGMT-based techniques, the paper provides:
- Theorem 2.1: nonasymptotic sequences with n→∞, m/n→δ, with i.i.d. Gaussian A, have the limiting LASSO error under nonlinear g equal to that under the effective linear model with parameters (µ, σ^2).
- Theorem 2.2: a precise, asymptotically exact scalar convex optimization that yields the limiting MSE ||x̂ − µ x0||^2 for general convex f via a functional F depending on the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate of f and the distribution of x0.
- Specializations: explicit formulas for sparse recovery with ℓ1 (Theorem 2.3), block-sparse (group LASSO), and a roadmap for low-rank (nuclear norm).
- Application: q-bit quantized CS. The equivalence reduces quantizer design to minimizing σ^2/µ^2; the Lloyd–Max procedure is shown to find stationary points of this objective.

Relation to prior work
- Extends Brillinger’s classical LS result (consistency up to scaling under link misspecification) from fixed-n to high-dimensional asymptotics, and from LS to regularized LASSO, yielding precise MSE rather than bounds.
- Tightens and complements Plan & Vershynin’s nonasymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO under nonlinear measurements by providing exact asymptotics for the regularized LASSO and showing the precise role of µ and σ.
- Builds on precise LASSO error analyses for linear models via AMP and CGMT (e.g., Bayati-Montanari; Stojnic; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.), and generalizes them to nonlinear observations through the equivalence principle.
- Connects to one-bit CS and quantization literature by yielding a principled quantizer design criterion.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The core results are plausible and well-aligned with CGMT-based analyses. The reduction to (µ, σ^2) is elegant and consistent with intuition from score-function identities and prior linearization/mismatch results. The specialization to ℓ1 gives concrete fixed-point equations and identifies regimes (δ>1 vs δ<1 with λcrit). Simulations appear to match theory.
- Assumptions: A is i.i.d. Gaussian; nonlinearities only enter through (µ,σ^2); µ ≠ 0 is assumed (they note µ=0 cases are fundamentally unidentifiable for direction). Asymptotic regime m/n→δ. Assumption 1 (existence of pointwise limits of scaled Moreau envelopes) is technical but reasonable; the paper verifies it for common structures (sparse, group-sparse) and sketches low-rank conditions. It would help to better articulate sufficient conditions for Assumption 1 beyond examples.
- Completeness: Main proofs are deferred to appendices; derivations for sparse and group-sparse are provided. The low-rank case is outlined but leaves F’s numerical evaluation to future work.

Clarity
- The presentation is generally clear and well-organized: assumptions, main theorems, then special cases and application. The equivalence result (Theorem 2.1) could be phrased more precisely as “the limits of the errors coincide” rather than an equality of random quantities “in the limit.” Assumption 1 could be unpacked with explicit sufficient conditions (e.g., for separable f, bounded moments of x0, etc.). A short algorithmic note for solving the scalar convex program in (10) and for computing F in common cases would improve reproducibility. Minor typos and footnote references (e.g., µ≠0 discussion) should be cleaned.

Originality
- The equivalence principle and the precise asymptotic characterization for the regularized LASSO under nonlinear measurements appear novel. Prior works offered bounds (Plan–Vershynin) or focused on linear models (AMP/CGMT). Extending precise error formulas to general convex regularizers under link misspecification is an original and nontrivial contribution. The quantizer design corollary is also neat and actionable.

Significance
- Conceptual impact: Provides a unifying and practically useful reduction from nonlinear to linear with effective (µ, σ^2), clarifying when and how LASSO remains optimal (up to scaling).
- Practical impact: Offers exact guidance for tuning λ and for quantizer design; informs expectations of performance in single-index settings (including 1-bit CS).
- Breadth: Applies to multiple structures (sparse, group-sparse; low-rank in principle). This framework is likely to be used and extended by the community.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope: Restricted to i.i.d. Gaussian A; it would be valuable to discuss extensions to rotationally invariant or sub-Gaussian designs (CGMT can sometimes handle these).
- Identifiability and µ=0: Since µ may vanish for certain g (e.g., odd/even symmetries or unknown scaling), provide a short discussion on direction recovery and alternative estimators, or cite recent work handling µ=0 regimes.
- Finite-sample behavior: While asymptotically exact, some finite-n calibration or nonasymptotic deviation bounds would help practitioners.
- Estimating µ and σ in practice: The LASSO does not need µ,σ, but for interpretation and quantizer design they matter. Suggest practical estimators (e.g., moment-based from (A,y), or calibration with pilot data) and robustness to estimation error.
- Low-rank case: Provide at least one concrete instance (e.g., spiked model) with an explicit F, to demonstrate feasibility.

Minor comments
- Clarify Theorem 2.2 variables and domains (convexity, uniqueness of α*).
- Provide code or pseudo-code for computing F and solving (10).
- Improve figure captions with exact n, m, δ, ρ, λ, and number of trials.

Pros
- Strong, novel theoretical result with precise asymptotics.
- General across convex regularizers and structures.
- Clean equivalence principle with immediate practical implications.
- Solid validation and a compelling application to quantizer design.

Cons
- Gaussian A and asymptotic regime; limited discussion of robustness.
- Technical Assumption 1 could be better justified generally.
- Low-rank specialization remains partly abstract.
- Minor clarity issues and missing practical guidance on estimating (µ,σ).

Recommendation
Weak accept to accept. The paper offers a significant, well-founded advance with clear relevance to the NeurIPS community interested in high-dimensional inference, compressed sensing, and robust learning under model mismatch. Addressing the clarity and applicability points above would further strengthen the submission.
