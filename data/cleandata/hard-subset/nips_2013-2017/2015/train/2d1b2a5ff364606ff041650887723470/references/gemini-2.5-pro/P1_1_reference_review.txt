### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a structured signal `x₀` from non-linear measurements of the form `yᵢ = gᵢ(aᵢᵀx₀)`, a setup known as the single-index model. The authors investigate the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, which is nominally designed for linear measurements, when it is naively applied to this non-linear problem.

The main contribution is a powerful and elegant theoretical result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit (where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` go to infinity with a fixed ratio `δ = m/n`), the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimate is *exactly equivalent* to the error one would obtain if the measurements were from a corresponding linear model `yᵢ = μaᵢᵀx₀ + σzᵢ`. The scaling factor `μ` and effective noise variance `σ²` are determined solely by the properties of the link function `g`. Specifically, `μ = E[γg(γ)]` and `σ² = E[(g(γ) - μγ)²]` for a standard Gaussian variable `γ`.

This work builds upon and significantly extends several lines of research. It generalizes a classical result by Brillinger (1982) for unregularized least-squares from the fixed-`n`, large-`m` regime to the high-dimensional, regularized setting. It also makes precise the non-asymptotic but loose upper bounds recently provided by Plan and Vershynin (2015), confirming the central role of the parameters `μ` and `σ²`. Most importantly, it connects the analysis of non-linear models to the recent, sophisticated body of work on the precise asymptotic analysis of LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., from the Donoho, Montanari, Bayati, Hassibi, and Stojnic groups), which often relies on tools like Approximate Message Passing (AMP) or the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT). The paper provides a general error formula (Theorem 2.2) as the solution to a small scalar optimization problem and demonstrates its application to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems. An application to designing optimal quantizers for compressive sensing illustrates the practical utility of the derived formulas.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The central result—that LASSO with non-linear measurements is equivalent to one with linear measurements—is both profound and exceptionally clean. It provides a simple, intuitive, and actionable principle for understanding a complex problem. This finding has the potential to unify the analysis of a wide range of measurement models (quantized, censored, etc.) under the well-understood framework of noisy linear regression. Researchers and practitioners can now leverage the vast literature on linear LASSO to analyze and design systems with non-linearities.

2.  **Technical Quality and Precision:** The paper provides an asymptotically *exact* characterization of the estimation error, rather than just an upper bound. This level of precision is the current state-of-the-art in the theoretical analysis of high-dimensional estimators and represents a significant technical achievement. The theoretical framework is general, accommodating a wide class of regularizers, signal structures, and link functions.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the problem, a clear summary of the contributions, and a thorough discussion of the relevant literature. The main results are stated precisely and their implications are clearly explained through special cases and an application. The figures effectively demonstrate the remarkable accuracy of the theoretical predictions.

4.  **Originality and Novelty:** To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a precise asymptotic analysis of the LASSO for general non-linear measurements. While it builds on the CGMT framework developed for the linear case, its successful extension to the single-index model is a highly non-trivial and novel contribution. The connection established is new and fundamentally deepens our understanding of the LASSO algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on two strong, standard assumptions in this line of work: (i) an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix `A`, and (ii) a high-dimensional asymptotic limit. While these assumptions are necessary for the current analytical tools to work, they limit the direct applicability to finite-dimensional problems or problems with structured/non-Gaussian measurement ensembles. A discussion of these limitations and potential avenues for extension (e.g., universality arguments for non-Gaussian matrices) would be beneficial.

2.  **The `μ ≠ 0` Condition:** The theory requires the scaling factor `μ = E[γg(γ)]` to be non-zero. As noted in a footnote, this excludes certain link functions, such as even functions (e.g., `g(x) = |x|`). While the paper correctly points out this limitation, a more prominent discussion in the main text about the scope of this condition and what might be expected when `μ = 0` would be helpful for the reader.

3.  **Proof Details:** The proofs are deferred to the appendix. While this is standard for conference submissions, it makes it difficult to fully assess the technical novelty of the proof techniques themselves. Based on the problem structure and citations, the approach likely involves a clever application of the CGMT framework, but the specific innovations are not detailed in the main body.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a fundamental, clean, and surprising equivalence result that significantly advances the theory of high-dimensional statistics.
*   The results are precise (asymptotically exact), not just order-wise bounds, representing the highest standard of theoretical analysis in this area.
*   The paper is extremely well-written, well-motivated, and places itself perfectly within the existing literature.
*   The framework is general and its implications are clearly demonstrated for several important machine learning problems (sparse recovery, low-rank matrix recovery).
*   The work has high potential for impact, as it allows a large body of existing results for linear models to be immediately translated to non-linear settings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The reliance on the Gaussian matrix assumption and the asymptotic regime are significant theoretical limitations, though standard for this type of analysis.
*   The `μ ≠ 0` condition limits the class of applicable non-linearities.

### Conclusion and Recommendation

This is an excellent theoretical paper that presents a significant and elegant contribution to the literature on high-dimensional estimation. The strengths—the importance and simplicity of the main result, the technical precision, and the clarity of the presentation—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are largely inherent to the state-of-the-art analytical techniques in this field. This work successfully bridges the gap between the analysis of linear and non-linear measurement models for the LASSO, a result that is likely to be highly cited and built upon. It is a perfect fit for NIPS and I strongly recommend its acceptance.
