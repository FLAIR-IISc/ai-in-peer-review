**Summary**

This paper analyzes the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm for estimating a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. This setup, known as a single-index model, is common in statistics and econometrics. Although the LASSO objective function is designed for linear models, the authors show that when applied to this non-linear problem, its performance can be precisely characterized. The main contribution is a proof that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime (where `m, n -> ∞` with `m/n` fixed) and with Gaussian measurement matrices, the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimate is identical to that of a LASSO estimate derived from an equivalent linear model with scaled signal and additive Gaussian noise. The scaling factor `µ` and noise variance `σ^2` are shown to depend only on the link function `g`. This work extends a classical result by Brillinger on unregularized least-squares and makes precise the non-asymptotic bounds recently provided by Plan and Vershynin. The analysis leverages the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, which has been used to provide similar precise characterizations for LASSO with linear measurements.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Elegance and Precision:** The main result (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) is a strong and elegant theoretical statement. The discovery of a precise asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear measurement problem and a canonical linear one is a significant conceptual contribution. It replaces previous, looser bounds with an exact characterization, providing a much deeper understanding of the LASSO's behavior in this context.
2.  **Generality:** The framework presented is quite general, holding for a wide class of convex regularizers (l1, group-l1, nuclear norm) and non-linear link functions. This makes the results broadly applicable to many structured signal recovery problems.
3.  **Methodological Soundness:** The paper is built on a rigorous theoretical foundation (the CGMT framework) and the assumptions are clearly stated. Within the specified asymptotic and probabilistic model (i.i.d. Gaussian `A`), the claims appear technically sound and well-supported.
4.  **Useful Application:** The application to optimizing q-bit quantizers is a good illustration of the theory's utility. It demonstrates how the main equivalence result can transform a complex, implicit optimization problem over estimators into a simpler, explicit optimization of the `σ^2/µ^2` ratio.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Comparison with Established Methods:** The central weakness of the paper is its failure to position the analyzed approach within the broader literature on single-index models. The paper essentially analyzes the performance of a "naive" application of LASSO to a problem for which specialized estimators have been developed over decades (e.g., as cited in [4, 14, 15]). The authors prove that this naive approach is not catastrophic and can be precisely characterized, but they provide no evidence that it is a *good* approach compared to existing, established methods. A convincing paper would need to demonstrate, either theoretically or empirically, that the performance of the LASSO is competitive with, or superior to, bespoke algorithms for single-index model regression. Without this comparison, the significance of the result is limited to a theoretical curiosity.
2.  **Restrictive Assumptions:** The analysis relies heavily on strong, idealized assumptions, namely an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix `A` and a high-dimensional asymptotic limit. While standard in this line of theoretical work, it raises questions about practical relevance. The performance of LASSO can be sensitive to the properties of the design matrix, and it is unclear how these results would hold for more structured or heavy-tailed designs. The paper provides no discussion on the rate of convergence to the asymptotic limit or the robustness of the conclusions to deviations from the Gaussian model.
3.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments in Figures 1 and 2 serve only to validate that the derived asymptotic formulas match the simulated performance of the LASSO. This is a necessary sanity check for the theory but is not sufficient validation for a paper at a top-tier conference. The experiments do not compare against any alternative methods, nor do they explore the boundaries of the theoretical assumptions (e.g., finite-size effects or non-Gaussian matrices).

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel, precise, and elegant theoretical result that connects two important models.
*   The analysis is technically sound and builds upon a powerful and modern line of research in high-dimensional statistics.
*   The result is general and has the potential to simplify the analysis and design of systems involving non-linear sensing, as demonstrated by the quantization example.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The paper's primary claim—that LASSO is a viable estimator for non-linear measurements—is an unproven assertion in the absence of comparisons to established methods for such problems. The practical significance is therefore unclear.
*   The reliance on asymptotic analysis and i.i.d. Gaussian matrices limits the immediate applicability of the sharp results.
*   The empirical validation is weak, serving only to confirm the theory rather than demonstrating its competitiveness or practical utility in a broader context.

**Conclusion**

This paper is a technically solid piece of theoretical work that provides a precise and insightful characterization of the Generalized LASSO's performance on single-index models. The equivalence result is clean and powerful. However, the work is presented in a vacuum. By analyzing only the LASSO and not comparing it to any of the well-established methods designed for this exact problem setting, the paper fails to make a convincing case for the significance of its findings. While the theory is impressive, its contribution to the practice of machine learning or statistics remains unproven. For this reason, I am hesitant to recommend acceptance. The work would be much stronger if it included a thorough comparison against relevant baselines from the single-index model literature.
