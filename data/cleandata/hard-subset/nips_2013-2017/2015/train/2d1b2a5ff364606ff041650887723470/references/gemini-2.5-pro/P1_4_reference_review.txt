### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x_0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)`, where `g_i` are i.i.d. link functions and `a_i` are the rows of a Gaussian measurement matrix `A`. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, which is nominally designed for linear models, in this non-linear setting.

The main contribution is a powerful and elegant equivalence theorem. The paper shows that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime (`m, n -> infinity` with `m/n -> δ`), the mean-squared error (MSE) of the LASSO estimate is exactly the same as if the measurements had been generated by an equivalent linear model: `y_lin = µ * A * x_0 + σ * z`, where `z` is standard Gaussian noise. The scaling factor `µ` and effective noise level `σ` are constants determined solely by the statistics of the link function `g`.

This key result allows the authors to leverage the rich, recent literature on the precise asymptotic analysis of the LASSO for linear models (e.g., using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework) to provide exact, deterministic characterizations of the estimation error for the non-linear problem. The general error formula is given as the solution to a simple, low-dimensional convex optimization problem. The authors then specialize this general result to several important cases, including sparse recovery (l1-norm), group-sparse recovery (l1,2-norm), and low-rank matrix recovery (nuclear norm), providing new and precise error predictions. Finally, the practical utility of the theory is demonstrated through an application to designing optimal q-bit quantizers for compressive sensing, reducing the complex design problem to a simple optimization of the effective signal-to-noise ratio `µ^2/σ^2`.

This work builds upon and significantly extends two lines of research. It generalizes a classical result by Brillinger [5] (which treated the unregularized least-squares case for fixed `n`) to the modern high-dimensional, regularized setting. It also sharpens recent work by Plan and Vershynin [17] (which provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO) by providing asymptotically exact characterizations for the more common regularized LASSO, thus replacing loose bounds with precise formulas.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The central result establishing the equivalence between the non-linear and an effective linear model is a fundamental contribution. It provides a deep and satisfying insight into why and how a linear estimator like the LASSO can succeed with non-linear data. This moves the field from indicative upper bounds to precise, quantitative understanding. The result is highly original and elegantly connects two previously separate areas of analysis.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. Deriving precise asymptotic characterizations of estimator performance is a notoriously difficult task. The authors successfully apply sophisticated tools to a new problem domain to obtain clean, verifiable results. The theoretical predictions are convincingly validated by numerical simulations (Figures 1-2), which show a remarkable match between the formulas and empirical performance.

3.  **Generality and Scope:** The theoretical framework is impressively general. It applies to a wide class of (potentially random, unknown) link functions `g`, various signal structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank), and their corresponding convex regularizers `f`. The specialization of the main theorem to these important instances demonstrates the power and flexibility of the core result.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the relevant literature. The main results are stated cleanly and upfront, followed by detailed instantiations. The application to quantizer design is an excellent addition that makes the abstract theory tangible and demonstrates its practical implications.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on Gaussian Measurements:** The analysis relies heavily on the assumption that the measurement matrix `A` has i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a standard assumption in the line of work using tools like CGMT, but it is a significant limitation for practical applications where structured matrices (e.g., Fourier, Hadamard) are common. A discussion of the potential for extending these results to a broader class of matrices under a "universality" principle would strengthen the paper's claims of practical relevance.

2.  **Asymptotic Nature:** The results are asymptotic in nature, holding as the problem dimensions grow to infinity. While simulations suggest the predictions are accurate even for moderate dimensions, the theory provides no finite-sample guarantees. This is a characteristic of the entire research program, but it's a limitation worth acknowledging explicitly.

3.  **Exclusion of the µ=0 Case:** The analysis requires `µ = E[γg(γ)] ≠ 0`. As noted in Footnote 3, this excludes important link functions like even functions (e.g., `g(x) = |x|`). While the authors cite work addressing specific instances of this case, a brief discussion in the main text on the challenges posed by the `µ=0` regime and why their approach fails would provide a more complete picture.

### **Pro/Con Summary for Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides a fundamental, elegant, and highly significant theoretical result ("non-linear = linear").
*   Dramatically improves upon prior work by providing precise, asymptotically exact error characterizations instead of loose upper bounds.
*   The framework is general, powerful, and applicable to many important structured signal recovery problems.
*   The paper is technically excellent, clearly written, and the theory is well-supported by experiments.
*   The application to quantizer design highlights the practical value of the theoretical insights.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis is limited to the specific case of i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices.
*   The results are asymptotic and lack finite-sample guarantees.
*   The important case where the scaling factor `µ` is zero is not handled.

### **Conclusion**

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a major theoretical advancement in the understanding of high-dimensional estimation with non-linear data. The central equivalence theorem is a beautiful result that is both conceptually insightful and practically useful. The paper is a complete and polished piece of work that significantly pushes the state-of-the-art. It will be of great interest to researchers in machine learning, statistics, and signal processing. I strongly recommend acceptance.
