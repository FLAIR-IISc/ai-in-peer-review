

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper presents a bold and novel theoretical insight: the LASSO estimator with non-linear measurements can be precisely analyzed by treating the measurements as scaled and noisy linear measurements, where the scaling factor and noise variance are determined by the link function. The authors establish a precise equivalence between the asymptotic behavior of LASSO under non-linear measurements and the standard LASSO with linear measurements, providing a unified framework for analyzing structured signal recovery across measurement models.

The paper's main contribution is Theorem 2.1, which establishes that for non-linear measurements of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0), the error of the Generalized LASSO solution x̂ satisfies ||x̂ - μx0||² = ||x̂_lin - μx0||² in the limit of large dimensions, where x̂_lin is the solution to the LASSO with linear measurements y_lin = A(μx0) + σz. Here, μ and σ² are defined in terms of the link function g (as in Equation 3). This equivalence is remarkable because it shows that non-linear measurements can be treated as linear measurements with a known scaling and noise model, eliminating the need for specialized analysis of non-linear measurement models.

Theorem 2.2 further provides a precise error formula for the LASSO under non-linear measurements, which is expressed as the solution to a convex optimization problem involving three scalar variables. This is a significant advancement over previous work by Plan and Vershynin, who provided only loose upper bounds for the constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements. The authors also specialize their results to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems, demonstrating the broad applicability of their framework.

The paper's application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) is particularly compelling. By recognizing that optimal quantization thresholds correspond to minimizing σ²/μ², the authors provide a principled approach to designing quantizers for compressed sensing applications. This bridges a gap between theoretical analysis and practical implementation, demonstrating the paper's significance beyond pure theory.

In terms of novelty, this paper makes a significant conceptual leap. While previous work (e.g., Brillinger [5], Plan and Vershynin [17]) had established qualitative connections between non-linear and linear measurements, this paper provides the first precise asymptotic characterization of the error under non-linear measurements. The key insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements with known scaling parameters is both elegant and powerful, fundamentally changing how we approach LASSO with non-linear measurements.

The paper builds appropriately on prior work while making a clear distinction from previous results. The authors carefully situate their contribution within the literature on single-index models, structured signal recovery, and the generalized LASSO, while highlighting where their results extend and improve upon existing work.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical results supported by numerical illustrations (Figures 1-2). The assumptions are clearly stated, and the proofs (deferred to appendices) appear to follow standard approaches for high-dimensional asymptotic analysis. While the proofs are complex, the main results are presented in a clear and accessible manner.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a unified framework for analyzing LASSO under non-linear measurements across multiple domains (sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, low-rank matrix recovery), with practical implications for applications like q-bit compressive sensing. The equivalence result simplifies the analysis of non-linear measurement models, potentially opening up new research directions in signal processing, statistics, and machine learning.

The main limitation of the paper is that it focuses on the asymptotic regime, which may not directly apply to small-scale problems. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and emphasize the practical relevance of their asymptotic results, which have been shown to provide accurate predictions even in moderate dimensions (as demonstrated in their figures).

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual advances over methodological perfection, I find this paper exceptionally compelling. The insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements is both elegant and transformative for the field. The precise error prediction provided by Theorem 2.2 represents a significant theoretical advance beyond previous work, which had only established loose upper bounds.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a substantial contribution to the literature on structured signal recovery. It represents a significant step forward in understanding how to analyze and apply LASSO to non-linear measurement models, which are ubiquitous in real-world applications.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper to NIPS. It offers a novel conceptual framework that simplifies the analysis of LASSO with non-linear measurements and provides precise error predictions with broad applicability across multiple domains. The theoretical results are rigorous, the insights are bold, and the potential impact on both theory and practice is substantial.

Pro: 
- Novel conceptual insight (equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models)
- First precise error characterization for LASSO with non-linear measurements
- Significant theoretical advance over previous loose upper bounds
- Broad applicability across sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery
- Practical application to q-bit compressive sensing

Con: 
- Focus on asymptotic regime (though this is standard for such analyses)

This paper represents a significant contribution to the field, offering a bold new perspective on a long-standing problem in structured signal recovery. It exemplifies the kind of conceptual advance that NIPS values most highly.
