**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of structured signal recovery from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`, a setup known as the single-index model. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, which is nominally designed for linear measurements, in this non-linear setting. The central and striking result of the paper is an asymptotic equivalence principle: in the high-dimensional limit where the number of measurements `m` and the signal dimension `n` grow proportionally (`m/n -> δ`), the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is *exactly the same* as its performance with an equivalent linear model `y = µAx0 + σz`. The constants `µ` and `σ^2` are elegantly shown to be properties of the link function `g`, defined by `µ = E[γg(γ)]` and `σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - µγ)^2]` for a standard normal variable `γ`.

Building on this equivalence, the paper provides a precise, deterministic formula for the asymptotic mean-squared error `||x̂ - µx0||^2`, expressed as the solution to a small, convex scalar optimization problem. This general result is then specialized to important practical cases, including sparse (`l1`), group-sparse (`l1,2`), and low-rank (nuclear norm) recovery. Finally, the utility of the theory is demonstrated through an application to q-bit compressive sensing, where it is used to re-frame the difficult problem of choosing optimal quantization thresholds as a much simpler problem of minimizing the effective noise-to-signal ratio `σ^2/µ^2`.

**Relation to Prior Work**

The paper is exceptionally well-situated within the relevant literature. It correctly identifies its place as a powerful generalization of two distinct lines of research:

1.  **Classical Statistics:** The work extends the seminal result of Brillinger (1983), who showed a similar linearizing effect for unregularized least-squares in the classical regime (`n` fixed, `m` -> ∞). This paper brings that insight into the modern high-dimensional, regularized setting (`n, m` -> ∞), which is far more challenging and relevant to current machine learning and signal processing.

2.  **High-Dimensional Analysis:** The paper directly builds upon and significantly strengthens the recent work of Plan and Vershynin (2015). While Plan and Vershynin first tackled the high-dimensional, non-linear problem and identified the importance of `µ` and `σ`, their results were non-asymptotic upper bounds with unspecified constants. This paper makes a profound leap by moving from loose bounds to asymptotically *exact* characterizations, thereby proving definitively the equivalence that was only hinted at previously.

Methodologically, the paper leverages the powerful Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, placing it in the company of a recent, highly impactful series of papers (by authors such as Bayati, Montanari, Stojnic, Oymak, Hassibi, and Thrampoulidis) that have provided precise asymptotic analyses for the LASSO in the *linear* measurement case. This work represents a major extension of that powerful analytical machinery to the non-linear domain.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of very high technical quality. The theoretical claims are strong, non-trivial, and appear to be rigorously established using state-of-the-art analytical tools. The work is complete, presenting a general theory, applying it to several key instances, and demonstrating its practical utility. The claims are convincingly supported by numerical simulations (Figures 1-2) that show a remarkably accurate match between the theoretical predictions and empirical performance.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation and context. The main results (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) are stated clearly and their implications are discussed effectively. The core message—that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled and noisy linear ones—is a powerful and easily digestible concept. The authors do a great job of explaining the intuition behind their results without getting bogged down in the technical details of the proofs, which are appropriately deferred to the appendix.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The central contribution—the exact asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear and a corresponding linear model for the LASSO—is a novel and fundamental insight. It is not an incremental improvement over prior art but a conceptual leap that reframes our understanding of the problem. As the authors note, some of the specialized results derived from their framework appear to be new even for the standard linear measurement setting, further highlighting the novelty and power of their approach.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial.
*   **Theoretical Impact:** It provides a unifying and simplifying principle for a broad class of problems. It allows the vast and sophisticated body of knowledge on the performance of LASSO with linear measurements to be directly applied to the non-linear case. This is a powerful theoretical tool that will likely stimulate further research.
*   **Practical Impact:** The ability to predict estimation error with high precision is invaluable for practical system design. The application to quantizer design is a prime example. Instead of relying on expensive, brute-force simulations to optimize quantization parameters, designers can use the paper's much simpler formulation (`min σ^2/µ^2`) to guide their choices. This is directly applicable to areas like 1-bit compressive sensing and other resource-constrained sensing applications. The results are likely to be widely used and built upon by researchers and practitioners in signal processing, statistics, and machine learning.

The only minor point for improvement could be to provide a sentence or two of additional intuition in the main text regarding "Assumption 1," which may appear abstract to readers not deeply versed in the CGMT literature. However, this is a very minor issue in an otherwise outstanding paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** Establishes a novel and elegant equivalence principle that simplifies the analysis of a complex problem.
2.  **Technical Strength:** Provides the first asymptotically exact performance characterization for LASSO with non-linear measurements, a significant advance over previous upper bounds.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The framework is general, covering a wide class of link functions, signal structures, and convex regularizers.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** The theory leads to direct, actionable insights for practical problems, such as the design of optimal quantizers.
5.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
I can find no significant arguments against accepting this paper. It is a stellar example of high-quality theoretical work with clear practical implications.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept. This is a top-tier theory paper that makes a foundational contribution.
