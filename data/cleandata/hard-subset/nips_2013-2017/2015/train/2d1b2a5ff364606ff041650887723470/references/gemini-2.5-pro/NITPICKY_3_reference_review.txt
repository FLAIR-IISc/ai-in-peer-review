### Summary of the Paper

This paper studies the performance of the Generalized LASSO estimator for recovering a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(aT_i x0)`. The central contribution is a powerful and elegant theoretical result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime where the number of measurements `m` and the signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio `m/n`, the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimate is precisely equivalent to the error obtained from a corresponding linear model with additive Gaussian noise. Specifically, the non-linear measurements are shown to be asymptotically equivalent to linear measurements of the form `yi = µ*aT_i x0 + σ*zi`, where `zi` are i.i.d. standard normal, and the constants `µ` and `σ` depend only on the link function `g`.

This equivalence allows the authors to leverage recent advances in the precise asymptotic analysis of LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem) to provide exact characterizations of the estimation error for the non-linear case. The paper extends foundational work by Brillinger on unregularized least squares and improves upon recent non-asymptotic bounds by Plan and Vershynin by providing exact formulae instead of upper bounds. The theory is applied to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems, and its utility is demonstrated in an application to optimal quantizer design for compressive sensing.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The main result, establishing the equivalence between the non-linear and a specific linear measurement model, is a fundamental and impactful contribution. It simplifies a complex problem to a well-understood one, providing a powerful analytical tool.
2.  **Precise Characterization:** The paper moves beyond the upper bounds found in prior work (e.g., Plan and Vershynin [17]) to provide asymptotically exact expressions for the estimation error. This level of precision is crucial for a deep understanding of the estimator's behavior and for practical tasks like parameter tuning.
3.  **Generality:** The framework is presented for a general class of convex regularizers, signal structures, and link functions, making the results widely applicable. The authors claim that some of the derived error formulae for specific regularizers (group-lasso, nuclear norm) are novel even for the linear measurement case.
4.  **Clear Application:** The section on q-bit compressive sensing is a compelling demonstration of the theory's practical relevance. By simplifying the LASSO error analysis, it transforms a difficult optimization problem for quantizer design into a much more tractable one involving only `µ` and `σ`.

### Weaknesses

Despite the strong conceptual contribution, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws, ranging from cosmetic to technical, which collectively undermine the reader's confidence in the work's polish and rigor.

1.  **Lack of Careful Proofreading:** The manuscript is replete with typographical errors, formatting inconsistencies, and stylistic issues.
    *   Citations are improperly formatted (e.g., `[4,5,14,15]1`, `µ 6= 03`).
    *   The reference list contains significant formatting errors (e.g., multiple extraneous commas in several entries).
    *   There are numerous typos (e.g., "mesaurements," "redifining").
    *   Non-standard mathematical notation is used without definition (e.g., `.` for proportionality/approximation in Eq. (7), `6=` for `≠` in Footnote 4).

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The problem setup in Section 2.1 is confusingly worded. The description of how `x0` is generated and normalized (`x0 = x0/‖x0‖2,with x0 sampled...`) is ambiguous and uses duplicated notation.
    *   Key assumptions are not sufficiently justified in the main text. Assumption 1 is highly technical, and its verification for the low-rank recovery case is dismissed with "We leave the details and the problem of (numerically) evaluating F for future work." This is a significant omission, as it leaves a central claim of the paper (applicability to low-rank recovery) unsubstantiated.
    *   The main results (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) are stated, but the proofs are deferred entirely to the appendix. While standard, the main text would benefit from at least a high-level sketch of the proof technique to provide intuition.

3.  **Minor Technical Gaps:**
    *   The connection between the general error formula in Theorem 2.2 and the simplified version for sparse recovery in Theorem 2.3 is not explained. The sudden appearance of `λcrit` and `κcrit` without any intuitive link to the optimization problem in (10) makes the result feel disconnected.
    *   Footnote 3 contains a confusing statement: "different methodologies... have been recently proposed that do not require µ = 0." This seems to be a typo and should likely read "that can handle the case µ = 0."

### Detailed Comments

*   **Introduction, pg. 1:** The footnote markers (`1`, `3`, `4`) appear detached from the text they refer to, making the layout confusing. For example, the sentence ending "...well-studied, e.g. [4,5,14,15]1." is jarring.
*   **Eq. (9), pg. 4:** The use of `min` instead of `argmin` is unconventional for defining an estimator. Also, the `1/√n` normalization factor for the objective function is unusual and should be briefly justified or compared to more standard formulations (e.g., `1/2m ||...||^2`).
*   **Section 2.2.4 (Low-rank Matrix Recovery), pg. 6:** This section feels incomplete. The authors state that the results apply "If conditions are met such that the empirical distribution of the singular values... converges," but do not state what these conditions are or verify that they hold for a reasonable class of random matrices. Deferring this to "future work" is not acceptable for a result claimed in the present paper.
*   **Section 2.3.3 (Lloyd-Max Algorithm), pg. 7:** The claim that the Lloyd-Max algorithm converges to stationary points of the objective in (19) is interesting and non-obvious. As this is a key part of the application, a sketch of the argument should be included in the main text rather than relegated entirely to the appendix.

### Recommendation: Reject

The core theoretical idea of this paper is strong, novel, and significant. The established equivalence between non-linear and linear models for LASSO analysis represents a substantial advance. However, the current manuscript does not meet the high standards of NIPS. The work is undermined by a pervasive lack of care in its presentation, including numerous typos and formatting errors. More critically, the paper contains technical gaps, most notably the unsubstantiated claims regarding low-rank matrix recovery.

While the central result is likely correct and important, the execution is sloppy. A scientific paper must not only present correct ideas but also do so with clarity, precision, and rigor. In its current form, this paper fails on these latter criteria. I strongly encourage the authors to perform a thorough revision to address the formatting, clarity, and technical issues raised above and resubmit to a future conference. The underlying work is promising, but it requires significant polish before it is ready for publication.
