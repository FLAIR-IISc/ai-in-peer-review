

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper claims to establish an equivalence between solving the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements and solving it with appropriately scaled linear measurements. The authors show that for non-linear measurements of the form y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0), the solution to the Generalized LASSO is asymptotically equivalent to the solution obtained when measurements are treated as y_i = μ a_i^T x_0 + σ z_i, where μ and σ^2 are defined in terms of the link function g. The paper extends previous work by Plan and Vershynin (2015) on constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements to the regularized LASSO setting, providing precise error predictions rather than loose upper bounds.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements. The asymptotic analysis is rigorous, and the authors provide precise error formulas for several important cases (sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery). The theoretical framework builds upon established work in high-dimensional statistics, particularly the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework. The paper's results are well-motivated by the single-index model literature, and the theoretical results are presented with appropriate mathematical rigor.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the theoretical soundness, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings in terms of practical relevance and validation. The main claim—that non-linear measurements can be treated as linear measurements with scaling factor μ and noise variance σ^2—appears to be largely theoretical without clear practical utility. Crucially, the paper does not address the fundamental issue that μ and σ^2 are typically unknown when the link function g is unknown (which is the case the authors claim to handle). In practice, estimating μ and σ^2 from data would be challenging, and the authors provide no empirical evidence that their theoretical equivalence holds when these parameters must be estimated.

The paper's validation is limited to a single figure (Figure 1) that shows "good match" between theoretical predictions and empirical results under specific settings. However, the paper lacks comprehensive empirical validation against established baselines for non-linear measurement problems. For instance, it does not compare the performance of the Generalized LASSO with other methods specifically designed for non-linear measurements (e.g., methods for 1-bit quantized measurements). The authors claim their result is "the first to derive asymptotically precise results" for LASSO-type programs with non-linear measurements, but this claim seems questionable given the substantial literature on non-linear measurement problems.

The paper also makes strong assumptions that limit its practical applicability. The requirement that the measurement matrix has i.i.d. Gaussian entries is restrictive, as real-world measurement systems rarely satisfy this condition. The asymptotic regime (n → ∞, m/n = δ) is common in theoretical work but often does not reflect practical scenarios where n is moderate. The authors do not provide evidence that their theoretical results hold for finite n or for non-Gaussian measurement matrices.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound, but the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its practical claims. The paper does not adequately address the limitations of its approach or provide a clear path to practical implementation. The assumption that the link function g is unknown but μ and σ^2 can be computed from it is problematic, as these parameters would need to be estimated in practice.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical exposition. However, the practical significance of the theoretical results is not adequately explained. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the theoretical equivalence could be practically implemented and validated.

**Originality:** The paper builds upon existing work by Plan and Vershynin (2015) and other recent advances on LASSO analysis, but the main contribution appears to be extending their results to the regularized LASSO setting with precise error predictions. While the technical contributions are non-trivial, the practical novelty is limited, as the equivalence claim does not translate to a new algorithm or practical method for handling non-linear measurements.

**Significance:** The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of LASSO under non-linear measurements, but its significance is limited by the lack of practical utility. The theoretical equivalence does not provide a clear advantage over existing methods for non-linear measurement problems, and the paper fails to demonstrate how this equivalence could be practically exploited. The results are interesting from a theoretical perspective but do not advance the state-of-the-art in a way that would be immediately useful to practitioners.

**Overall Assessment:** While the paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to existing work on the LASSO with non-linear measurements, it lacks sufficient empirical validation and fails to demonstrate practical utility. The main claim of equivalence between non-linear and linear measurements is largely theoretical and does not translate to a practical method for handling non-linear measurements in real-world applications. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods for non-linear measurement problems, as well as empirical validation showing how the theoretical results could be practically applied.

The paper is technically sound but does not provide sufficient evidence that its theoretical results have practical significance. Given the NIPS emphasis on both theoretical rigor and practical relevance, I recommend rejection. The authors should either provide more comprehensive empirical validation or demonstrate a clear practical advantage of their approach over existing methods before this work could be considered for publication.
