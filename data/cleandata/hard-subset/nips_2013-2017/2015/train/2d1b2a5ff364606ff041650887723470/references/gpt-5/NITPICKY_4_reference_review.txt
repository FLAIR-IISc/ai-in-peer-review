Summary
This paper studies generalized LASSO with non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) under i.i.d. Gaussian designs. The core claim is an asymptotic equivalence: for convex regularizers and fixed λ, the error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements equals (in the n→∞ limit) the error of the same LASSO applied to an effective linear model with scaling µ = E[γ g(γ)] and noise variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2], γ~N(0,1). Theorems 2.1–2.2 formalize this and provide a precise error characterization via a scalar convex program (10) depending on δ=m/n, λ, µ, σ^2, and a problem-dependent function F capturing f and the distribution of x0. Specializations are given for sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and a sketch for low-rank (nuclear norm). An application to q-bit quantized CS reduces quantizer design to minimizing σ^2/µ^2, with a Lloyd–Max-based argument (Appendix C) for stationarity.

Relation to prior work
- Extends Brillinger’s classical LS result (fixed n, m→∞) to high-dimensional regimes and to regularized estimators, and recovers LS behavior when δ>1.
- Sharpens Plan & Vershynin’s non-asymptotic upper bounds by giving asymptotically exact formulas and making the role of µ and σ^2 precise.
- Builds on precise LASSO error analyses under linear measurements (AMP- and CGMT-based) and claims to generalize them to non-linear observations.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean reduction: a wide class of non-linear measurement models map to an effective linear model with two scalars (µ, σ^2).
- If correct, Theorem 2.2 offers a compact scalar optimization that can be evaluated numerically for many regularizers, extending precise error analysis beyond ℓ1 to group-sparse and (potentially) nuclear norm.
- The specialization to sparse recovery producing explicit fixed-point equations appears novel (beyond the Gaussian nonzero case).
- Practical insight: q-bit quantizer design via σ^2/µ^2 minimization is elegant and aligns with intuition.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and completeness
- Theorem 2.1 is stated as “with probability one, ‖x̂ − µx0‖^2 = ‖x̂lin − µx0‖^2 in the limit,” which reads like exact equality of the (random) errors rather than equality of the limits. Please clarify that the two limits coincide (a.s.) and precisely state the convergence mode.
- Assumption 1 is opaque and notationally confusing: “1/n e√n(f∗)(n),c3 (c1h+ c2x0)” is difficult to parse (mismatched parentheses and indices). Provide a precise, unambiguous definition. Why is c3 present if it disappears in the ℓ1 case?
- The convexity/uniqueness claims in (10) need justification. Why is there a unique α* for general f/x0? Under what conditions? Similarly, Theorem 2.3 asserts uniqueness of (λcrit, κcrit) with no conditions on qX0 beyond second moments.
- The non-linear→linear equivalence relies on Gaussian designs and orthogonal decomposition arguments; a clear proof sketch would aid credibility. Key independence/orthogonality steps with y depending on A via γ=a_i^T x0 need to be stated.
- The low-rank case (2.2.4) is left largely as a conjectural template (“we leave the details for future work”), including the limiting spectral distribution needed to compute F. This makes the “precise” claim incomplete for one of the headline structures.

Assumptions and scope
- Throughout you assume µ and σ^2 are nonzero and bounded. This excludes even link functions (µ=0) and some practical cases. The introduction discusses sign(a^T x) as a motivating example, but the model excludes even g unless dithering/noise is present. Be explicit early and consistently about the µ≠0 restriction and discuss what is and isn’t covered.
- The results are limited to i.i.d. Gaussian A. There is no discussion of extension to sub-Gaussian/rotationally invariant designs or robustness to mild deviations, which limits applicability.

Clarity and presentation
- Notation inconsistencies: you sometimes use ‖·‖2 to denote Euclidean norm and elsewhere ‖·‖22 for squared norms; in (9), it is unclear whether ‖y−Ax‖2 is a norm or a squared norm (but earlier you define the square-root LASSO). Please standardize.
- Redefining x0 := x0/‖x0‖2 with the same symbol is confusing.
- Several equations and footnotes are malformed: e.g., “5Such models…” appears inline in Theorem 2.1; (12)–(13) mix vector and scalar thresholds without careful indexing; (15) misses parentheses in the product with h.
- Key statements are followed by “we omit the proof for brevity,” including the comparison yielding (8) and the Lloyd–Max stationarity result in Appendix C. Given the strength of the claims, more detail is needed.
- Figures 1–2 are referenced repeatedly but not included; the empirical section is effectively absent. The quantizer design section has no experiments.

Related work
- While Plan & Vershynin and CGMT/AMP lines are cited, the reference list is very incomplete and poorly formatted (missing years/venues, truncated titles, inconsistent styles). Please fix and ensure coverage of closely related single-index estimation under unknown link and 1-bit CS literatures.

Minor issues and typos (non-exhaustive)
- Numerous typos: “mesaurements,” “eventhough,” “redifining,” “gaussianity,” “uknown,” “Henceforth,” “linear ones and both are,” “motivated by” sections with broken grammar. Please carefully proofread.
- Footnote numbering and placement are broken (e.g., “3,” “4,” “5” mid-text).
- Equation (4) vs (5) scaling: explain the transition and reconcile constants with your σ definition to avoid confusion.
- The reference list is missing bibliographic metadata across the board.

Significance
If the main equivalence and precise characterization are correct and well-presented, the contribution is significant for high-dimensional inference with non-linear measurements, unifying many models via µ,σ^2 and extending precise LASSO error analyses. However, the current submission is weakened by unclear statements, heavy/opaque assumptions, incomplete low-rank case, missing proofs for key claims, and lack of empirical validation.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Pros
- Novel and potentially important asymptotic equivalence and precise error characterization.
- Broad coverage of convex regularizers; clear specialization to sparse and group-sparse.
- Practical insight for quantizer design.

Cons
- Presentation/notation issues and many typos.
- Several key claims lack proof details; Theorem statements need clarification.
- Low-rank case incomplete; no experiments; missing figures.
- Reference list and formatting below NeurIPS standards.

I encourage the authors to (i) cleanly restate and prove Theorem 2.1–2.2 with a clear Assumption 1; (ii) add a robust experimental section (including the quantizer design); (iii) complete the low-rank case or remove it; and (iv) thoroughly fix notation, typos, and references. My confidence is medium-high.
