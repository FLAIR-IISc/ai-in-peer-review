## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a surprisingly clean and powerful result: under mild assumptions, the performance of the LASSO applied to measurements of the form `y = g(a^T x)` is asymptotically equivalent to the LASSO applied to scaled, noisy linear measurements `y' = μa^T x + σz`, where `μ` and `σ` are characterized by the link function `g`.  This is a significant generalization of prior work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, extending their results to high-dimensional settings, general regularizers, and providing *precise* error characterizations. The authors derive a tight characterization of the error, expressible as the solution to a convex program, and demonstrate its accuracy through simulations.  They further illustrate the implications for specific scenarios like q-bit compressive sensing, providing practical insights.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Result:** The core equivalence result is both elegant and impactful.  It dramatically simplifies the analysis of LASSO-based recovery in non-linear measurement settings.  
* **Generality:** The work handles a wide class of link functions `g`, regularizers `f`, and measurement dimensions (including the challenging `m < n` regime).  This broad applicability significantly enhances its value.
* **Precision:** Unlike many prior works that offer only upper bounds, this paper provides *precise* error characterizations. This level of detail is crucial for understanding the interplay between parameters and performance. The derived optimization problem provides a clear path for practical analysis.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper carefully positions its contribution relative to Brillinger's early work and the more recent results of Plan & Vershynin, highlighting improvements and extensions.
* **Practical Implications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the practical utility of the theoretical results and offers a concrete example of how the analysis can be used to optimize system design (e.g., finding optimal quantization thresholds).
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear notation and a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1 (Technicality):** The assumption regarding the limit of `n e√n(f∗)(n),c3 (c1h+ c2x0)` is somewhat technical and may be difficult to verify in practice. While the authors acknowledge this and provide discussion, a more detailed explanation of how this assumption manifests in common regularizers (beyond the `l1` case) would be beneficial. More examples illustrating scenarios where this holds or fails would be useful.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the derived error characterization is precise, solving the associated convex program in (10) for general `f` and `g` might be computationally demanding. The paper could benefit from discussing potential approximations or efficient solvers.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While Figures 1 and 2 provide some validation, the empirical evaluation feels a little limited given the theoretical strength of the results.  Exploring a wider range of link functions `g` and regularizers `f` in simulations would further strengthen the conclusions. Specifically, visualizing the error surface near the optimal `λ` would be valuable.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper primarily focuses on the *equivalence* result. A more detailed discussion of scenarios where the approximation may break down or where the non-linearities introduce qualitatively different behavior would be helpful.

**Originality:**

The result itself – establishing the precise equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements for LASSO – is highly original. While building on prior work, the authors have achieved a significant theoretical advance. The extension to general regularizers and the precise error characterization represent a substantial contribution.

**Significance:**

This paper has the potential to significantly impact the field of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. By simplifying the analysis of non-linear measurement models, it opens up new avenues for research and development. The insights gained from this work could lead to improved algorithms for a wide range of applications, including compressive sensing, medical imaging, and machine learning. The connection to q-bit compressive sensing is a particularly promising direction for future work.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The theoretical result is a significant advancement with broad applicability.
* The precise error characterization provides a level of detail not found in previous work.
* The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and rigorously analyzed.
* The potential impact on several fields is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical Assumption 1 might limit the applicability of the result in some scenarios.
* The empirical validation, while present, could be more extensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a very strong paper that presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field. The result is both elegant and impactful, and the authors have done an excellent job of presenting their work in a clear and rigorous manner. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the paper.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  I believe this paper is suitable for presentation at NIPS and has the potential to become a highly cited work in the field. I recommend the authors address the comments regarding Assumption 1 and consider expanding the empirical validation to further strengthen their conclusions.
