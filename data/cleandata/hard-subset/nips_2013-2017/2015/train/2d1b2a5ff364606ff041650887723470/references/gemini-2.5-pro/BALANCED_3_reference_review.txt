**Summary of the Paper**

This paper analyzes the performance of the Generalized LASSO estimator for recovering a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. This setup, known as the single-index model, is common in statistics and includes important special cases like 1-bit quantization. The authors study the problem in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime where the number of measurements `m` and the signal dimension `n` both tend to infinity with a fixed ratio `m/n`. The central and striking contribution of the paper is to show that the performance of the LASSO in this non-linear setting is asymptotically equivalent to its performance in a corresponding linear setting with measurements `yi = mu * a_i^T x0 + sigma * zi`, where `zi` are i.i.d. standard Gaussian noise. The scaling factor `mu` and effective noise variance `sigma^2` are determined solely by the statistical properties of the (possibly unknown) link function `g`.

Building on this equivalence, the paper leverages the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework to derive a precise, deterministic formula for the asymptotic mean squared error `||x_hat - mu*x0||^2`. This formula is given as the solution to a simple, low-dimensional convex optimization problem. The general result is then specialized to important practical cases, including sparse recovery (with `l1` regularization), group-sparse recovery (`l1,2` regularization), and low-rank matrix recovery (nuclear norm regularization). Finally, the authors demonstrate the practical utility of their theory with an application to designing optimal q-bit quantizers for compressive sensing, showing that the complex problem of minimizing estimation error can be reduced to a simpler problem of optimizing the `sigma^2/mu^2` ratio.

This work significantly extends two lines of research. First, it generalizes classical results by Brillinger on unregularized least-squares for single-index models from a fixed-`n` to a high-dimensional setting. Second, it sharpens recent non-asymptotic upper bounds by Plan and Vershynin for a constrained LASSO into exact asymptotic predictions for the more common regularized LASSO, providing a precise characterization of the phenomena they identified.

**Strengths**

1.  **Fundamental and Elegant Main Result:** The core finding—that LASSO with non-linear measurements behaves identically to LASSO with a specific, corresponding linear model—is a powerful and elegant result. It provides a deep and intuitive simplification of a complex problem, making the analysis of non-linear systems much more tractable.
2.  **Precise and Verifiable Predictions:** Unlike prior work which provided only upper bounds with unknown constants, this paper delivers exact asymptotic formulas for the estimation error. The remarkable accuracy of these formulas is convincingly demonstrated through numerical simulations (Figures 1 and 2), which show a near-perfect match between theory and practice.
3.  **Generality and Scope:** The theoretical framework is quite general, accommodating a wide class of convex regularizers, signal structures, and link functions. The specialization to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank models covers many of the most important applications of LASSO. The fact that some of these specialized results appear to be novel even for the linear measurements case (e.g., beyond `l1` regularization) further highlights the strength of the contribution.
4.  **Significance and Practical Implications:** The paper has high significance. Theoretically, it provides a unifying framework for understanding LASSO in a broad class of measurement models. Practically, the results are directly useful for system design and parameter tuning without requiring expensive Monte Carlo simulations. The application to optimal quantizer design is an excellent and compelling example of the theory's practical impact.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions**

1.  **Reliance on Gaussian Design:** The analysis relies on the assumption that the measurement matrix `A` has i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a common assumption in the CGMT literature but is a limitation for practical applications where other matrix designs are used. It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion on whether these results are expected to hold for a broader class of sub-Gaussian ensembles and what technical challenges stand in the way of such an extension.
2.  **Clarity of Assumption 1:** The technical Assumption 1 is central to the main theorems. While the paper demonstrates that it holds for several key examples, its abstract definition may be opaque to some readers. A more intuitive explanation of this condition would be helpful. For instance, what properties of the regularizer `f` and the signal distribution `p(x0)` ensure this assumption is met? Clarifying whether this is a mild technical condition or a more restrictive one would improve the paper's accessibility.
3.  **Lack of Proof Intuition:** The proofs are deferred to the appendix. The main text would benefit from a short, high-level proof sketch that provides intuition for the main equivalence result (Theorem 2.1). What is the key mechanism in the analysis that allows the non-linear function `g` to be cleanly separated into the effective gain `mu` and noise `sigma`? This would make the result feel less like a "black box" and more like an understandable phenomenon.
4.  **Incomplete Low-Rank Analysis:** The section on low-rank matrix recovery (2.2.4) is noticeably less developed than the others. After setting up the problem, the authors state that they "leave the details and the problem of (numerically) evaluating F for future work." This feels preliminary and weakens this part of the contribution. The paper would be stronger if it either provided a concrete numerical validation for this case (even a simple one) or removed the section to focus on the more thoroughly developed results.

**Overall Assessment and Decision**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the high-dimensional analysis of regularized estimation. The central equivalence result is both surprising and powerful, providing a new lens through which to understand a broad class of non-linear measurement problems. The theoretical claims are technically deep, general, and convincingly validated by numerical experiments. The paper is well-written and effectively situates its contributions within the relevant literature.

While the reliance on a Gaussian design is a limitation, this is standard for the analytical tools used and does not detract from the fundamental insight provided. The other minor weaknesses can be addressed through improved exposition. The strengths of the paper—its novelty, technical quality, and significance—are substantial and clearly meet the high standards of NeurIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel, fundamental result establishing an equivalence between non-linear and linear models for LASSO.
*   Provides precise, computable, and experimentally verified error characterizations, advancing beyond previous work that offered only loose bounds.
*   High theoretical significance, unifying and generalizing several lines of prior research.
*   Demonstrates clear practical relevance through a compelling application to optimal quantizer design.
*   The paper is very well-written and organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis is restricted to i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices.
*   The treatment of the low-rank matrix recovery case is incomplete.

The arguments for acceptance overwhelmingly outweigh the arguments against. This is a top-tier paper that will likely be influential.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
