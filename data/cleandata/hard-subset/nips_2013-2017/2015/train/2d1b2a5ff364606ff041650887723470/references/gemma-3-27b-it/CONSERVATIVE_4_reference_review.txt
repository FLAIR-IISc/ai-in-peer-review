This paper presents a theoretical result demonstrating the equivalence, in the asymptotic limit, of the Generalized LASSO applied to non-linear measurements and its application to scaled, noisy linear measurements. The core idea builds upon prior work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, extending their analyses to a more general, high-dimensional setting and providing precise error characterizations. The authors aim to provide a fundamental understanding of when and how the LASSO can be reliably used with non-linear measurement models.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the authors connect their work to a significant body of existing literature in statistics, econometrics, and signal processing. The presentation of the theoretical results (Theorems 2.1, 2.2, and 2.3) is concise and the derivation of specific cases such as sparse recovery and low-rank recovery add to the paper's completeness. The exploration of the application to q-bit compressive sensing and the proposed Lloyd-Max algorithm are interesting additions. 

However, my primary concern is the lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical results are presented rigorously, the paper relies almost entirely on asymptotic analysis. The authors mention figures validating the theoretical predictions, but these are not included in the submitted version (only referenced as “Figures 1-2”). Without seeing these figures, it’s difficult to assess the practical relevance of the results. The theoretical equivalence established is asymptotic; in real-world scenarios with finite data dimensions, the performance differences between the non-linear and equivalent linear models could be substantial.  It’s crucial to demonstrate through simulations (or even real data experiments) that the derived scaling factors and error bounds hold in practical settings. A strong argument could be made that the entire justification for extending the LASSO to non-linear measurements hinges on the utility of this asymptotic equivalence in practice, and without empirical support, it remains largely theoretical.

Another weakness is the reliance on strong assumptions. The assumption of Gaussian measurement vectors, while common, limits the applicability of the results. While the authors acknowledge this and provide citations to related work, a discussion of the robustness of the results to deviations from Gaussianity would be beneficial. Furthermore, the assumption of unit norm x0, while simplifying the presentation, feels somewhat restrictive. The authors claim to provide a straightforward generalization for non-unit norm vectors, but the burden is on them to demonstrate its practical usability. 

The claim of novelty isn't entirely convincing. While the generalization of Brillinger’s result and the precise error characterization are valuable contributions, the fundamental idea of relating non-linear measurements to linear ones has been explored before. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed comparison to existing methods for dealing with non-linear measurements, highlighting the specific advantages of the proposed approach (i.e., leveraging the existing LASSO framework) over alternatives.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis extending established results in the field.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.
*   Potential for broad impact on applications utilizing non-linear measurements.
*   Connections to a significant body of related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation – critical for assessing practical relevance.
*   Strong assumptions that limit the generality of the results.
*   Incremental novelty - builds upon existing ideas rather than presenting a radical departure.
*   Missing figures significantly hinder review.



**Overall Assessment:**

I am inclined to *reject* this paper in its current form. While the theoretical contributions are interesting and the analysis is sound, the lack of empirical validation is a major drawback. The paper feels incomplete without demonstration of the practical relevance of the asymptotic equivalence it establishes. The authors need to include simulations and, ideally, experiments on real data to support their claims and convince the community of the value of their work. Until then, it remains a largely theoretical exercise with limited practical impact. I would be open to reconsidering a revised version that addresses these concerns.
