

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper addresses a fundamental question in signal recovery: how does the performance of the Generalized LASSO change when measurements are non-linear (yi = gi(ai^T x0)) compared to the standard linear measurement setup? The authors establish a precise theoretical equivalence between the two scenarios, showing that the error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to the error of the LASSO with appropriately scaled linear measurements (yi = μ(ai^T x0) + σzi), where μ and σ depend on the non-linear link function g. This builds on seminal work by Brillinger (1980s) and Plan-Vershynin (2015), extending their results from least squares and constrained LASSO to the regularized LASSO with precise error characterization.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the precise asymptotic equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements. Theorem 2.1 provides an elegant and general result that connects non-linear measurement models to linear ones through the parameters μ and σ defined in (3). This equivalence simplifies the analysis of non-linear measurement models by reducing them to well-studied linear cases.

2. **Technical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous proofs for the main theorems (deferred to appendix), which is commendable for a theoretical contribution. The authors carefully address the technical challenges of extending previous results to the regularized LASSO setting.

3. **Novel Applications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) is particularly compelling. The authors show how their theoretical framework leads to a practical algorithm for optimizing quantization thresholds and levels, demonstrating the real-world relevance of their theoretical results.

4. **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper provides precise error formulas for multiple structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank), which extend and generalize previous results in the literature. The numerical experiments (Figures 1-2) validate the theoretical predictions across various scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Assumption Restrictiveness:** The paper assumes the measurement matrix A has i.i.d. Gaussian entries, which is a strong technical assumption that may limit the applicability of the results to non-Gaussian measurement scenarios. The authors should discuss how their results might extend to more general measurement ensembles.

2. **Unit Norm Signal Assumption:** The paper assumes ‖x0‖2 = 1 throughout, which is a significant restriction. While the authors note this is for presentation simplicity, it limits the applicability to real-world problems where signal norms are typically unknown. The authors should clarify how their results would change when this assumption is relaxed.

3. **Asymptotic Focus:** The analysis is strictly asymptotic (n→∞), which may not directly translate to finite-sample behavior. The authors should provide some discussion of how their results might inform practice in finite-sample settings, perhaps through numerical experiments with moderate n.

4. **Computational Considerations:** The paper does not address the computational complexity of evaluating the error formula in (10) or the practical implementation of the optimization problem. For a result with potential practical applications, a brief discussion of computational aspects would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes an important step toward understanding the behavior of structured signal recovery algorithms with non-linear measurements. The equivalence result is elegant and general, and the application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates clear practical relevance.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature. While the assumptions are somewhat restrictive, they are common in high-dimensional statistics and signal processing literature, and the results provide valuable theoretical insights that will inform future work in the field.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. The results are novel, well-supported, and have clear implications for both theory and practice. The minor weaknesses noted above are not sufficient to warrant rejection, and the paper would benefit from minor clarifications rather than major revisions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Briefly discuss how the results might extend to non-Gaussian measurement matrices (e.g., sub-Gaussian) to strengthen the practical relevance.

2. Address the unit norm assumption more thoroughly and explain how the results would change when the signal norm is unknown.

3. Add a short discussion of computational considerations for evaluating the error formula (10) and implementing the optimization in practice.

4. Consider including a brief comparison with alternative approaches for non-linear measurement models to highlight the advantages of the LASSO framework.

The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of structured signal recovery, and I believe it would be a strong addition to NIPS proceedings.
