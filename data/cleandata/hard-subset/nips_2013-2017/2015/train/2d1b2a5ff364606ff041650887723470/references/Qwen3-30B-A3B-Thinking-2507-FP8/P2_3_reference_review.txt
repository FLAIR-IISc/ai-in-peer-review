

# Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a precise asymptotic equivalence between the Generalized LASSO applied to non-linear measurements (yi = gi(ai^T x0)) and the same algorithm applied to appropriately scaled and noisy linear measurements (yi = μai^T x0 + σzi). The key insight is that the non-linear measurement model can be treated as linear with a scaling factor μ and noise variance σ², defined in terms of the link function g. The authors derive precise error formulas that capture the role of the link function, signal structure, regularizer, and regularization parameter. They validate their results with numerical experiments and apply them to q-bit compressive sensing to optimize quantization thresholds.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The main results (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) are well-supported by careful mathematical derivations and assumptions. The authors properly build on prior work by Brillinger [5] and Plan and Vershynin [17], extending their results to provide precise error predictions rather than loose upper bounds. The asymptotic analysis is sound, and the authors carefully address the limitations of their assumptions (e.g., i.i.d. Gaussian measurements, μ and σ non-zero and bounded).

The paper excels in its precise error characterization, which generalizes previous work on LASSO with linear measurements [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 21]. The authors clearly demonstrate how their result provides deeper insights than previous non-asymptotic bounds [17]. The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) is particularly compelling, showing how the theoretical result leads to a practical algorithm for optimizing quantization thresholds.

A minor concern is that the paper relies on Assumption 1 for the general result, which requires the existence of a point-wise limit of the Moreau envelope. While the authors show this holds for important cases (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank), they don't provide a more general characterization of when this assumption is satisfied.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and motivates the work by highlighting the gap between previous results and the need for precise error analysis. The authors effectively relate their contributions to prior work, particularly Plan and Vershynin [17] and recent advances in LASSO analysis.

The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The main theorems are stated precisely, and the paper provides clear explanations of the key parameters (μ and σ²). The figures (Figures 1-2) effectively validate the theoretical predictions with numerical experiments. The paper is well-structured, with each section building logically on the previous one.

## Originality
This paper makes a genuinely novel contribution to the field. While previous work [5, 17] had established qualitative connections between non-linear and linear measurements, this paper provides the first asymptotically precise error analysis for LASSO with non-linear measurements. The key insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled and noisy linear measurements with specific μ and σ² is both elegant and profound.

The precise error formula (Theorem 2.2) is original and generalizes previous results for linear measurements. The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) demonstrates the practical significance of their theoretical result and provides a novel algorithm for optimizing quantization thresholds, which is particularly valuable for hardware-constrained systems.

The paper distinguishes itself from related work by providing exact error predictions rather than loose bounds. As the authors note, "to the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first to derive asymptotically precise results on the performance of any LASSO-type program" for non-linear measurements.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. It fundamentally changes how we view the application of LASSO to non-linear measurement settings. The paper demonstrates that the LASSO can be effectively used for non-linear measurements without algorithmic modifications, as long as the scaling and noise parameters are properly accounted for. This has important implications for practitioners working with non-linear measurement systems.

The precise error formula provides a deeper theoretical understanding of how the LASSO behaves under non-linear measurements, which can inform better parameter selection and algorithm design. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is particularly impactful, as it provides a principled approach to optimizing quantization levels and thresholds in hardware-constrained systems.

The paper advances the state of the art in high-dimensional statistics and compressed sensing by bridging a gap between linear and non-linear measurement models. It provides a unifying framework that simplifies the analysis of LASSO in non-linear settings.

## Strengths
- Rigorous theoretical contribution with precise error characterization
- Clear connection between non-linear and linear measurement models
- Novel application to q-bit compressive sensing with practical implications
- Comprehensive validation through numerical experiments
- Builds upon and extends previous work in a meaningful way

## Weaknesses
- Reliance on Assumption 1 for the general result (though shown to hold for important cases)
- Restrictive assumption that μ ≠ 0 (excludes even link functions)
- Focus on asymptotic regime (n → ∞, m = δn), though this is standard for such analyses

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field with significant theoretical and practical implications. The results are well-supported, clearly presented, and represent a substantial advance over previous work. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and elegant solution, and the application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates clear practical value.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS as it meets all the criteria for a high-quality scientific contribution. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant to the field of high-dimensional statistics and compressed sensing.

The authors have made a valuable contribution that will inform both theoretical research and practical applications of LASSO in non-linear measurement settings. The precise error formula they derive provides a powerful tool for understanding and optimizing LASSO performance in a broad range of non-linear measurement scenarios.
