### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a^T_i x0)`, a setup known as the single-index model. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, which is nominally designed for linear measurements, in this non-linear setting.

The main contribution is a powerful and elegant asymptotic equivalence result. Under the assumption of an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix `A`, the paper demonstrates that the mean-squared error (MSE) of the LASSO estimator in the non-linear case is asymptotically identical to the MSE of the same estimator applied to a corresponding *linear* model: `y_lin = µ * A*x0 + σ * z`, where `z` is standard Gaussian noise. The scaling factor `µ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the properties of the (random) link function `g`. This result provides a precise, quantitative characterization of the estimator's performance via a low-dimensional, convex optimization problem.

This work builds upon and significantly advances two distinct lines of research:
1.  **Classical work on single-index models:** The paper extends a classical result by Brillinger (1982), who showed a similar equivalence for unregularized least squares in the classical regime where dimension `n` is fixed and samples `m` go to infinity. This paper modernizes Brillinger's finding to the high-dimensional, regularized setting where `m` and `n` grow proportionally.
2.  **Recent precise analysis of LASSO:** The work is inspired by Plan and Vershynin (2015), who first studied the regularized problem with non-linearities and identified the crucial roles of `µ` and `σ`. However, their work provided non-asymptotic upper bounds with unspecified constants for a constrained version of the LASSO. The present paper goes much further by providing asymptotically *exact* error characterizations for the more common penalized LASSO formulation. The methodology is rooted in the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, which has been successfully used to derive precise performance characterizations for LASSO in the linear setting (e.g., by Bayati & Montanari, Stojnic, Thrampoulidis et al.). This paper successfully applies this powerful machinery to the more complex non-linear measurement setting.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Elegance of the Main Result:** The central finding—that LASSO with non-linear measurements behaves exactly like LASSO with scaled, noisy linear measurements—is a profound and highly intuitive result. It simplifies the conceptual understanding of a complex problem and unifies the analysis of a broad class of non-linear measurement models under the well-developed theory for the linear LASSO. This is a major theoretical contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The paper employs a state-of-the-art analytical framework (CGMT) to derive its results. The outcome is not just an upper bound, but a precise asymptotic characterization of the MSE (Theorem 2.2). The derivation of explicit error formulas for specific cases, such as sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3), demonstrates a high level of technical command. These results are novel and, as the authors note, in some cases provide new insights even for the linear measurement setting with non-Gaussian signal priors.

3.  **Generality:** The framework is impressively general. It applies to a wide class of (potentially random and unknown) link functions `g`, convex regularizers `f`, and signal structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank). This breadth makes the findings widely applicable across different areas of signal processing, statistics, and machine learning.

4.  **Clear Practical Implications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing is an excellent demonstration of the paper's utility. By leveraging the main theoretical result, the complex problem of optimizing quantizer thresholds and levels to minimize reconstruction error is reduced to the much simpler and more tractable problem of minimizing the ratio `σ^2/µ^2`. This is a compelling example of how deep theory can lead to direct, practical algorithmic insights.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on Strong Assumptions:** The analysis hinges on several key assumptions standard in this line of theoretical work, but which limit direct applicability.
    *   The requirement of an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix `A` is crucial for the CGMT framework but is rarely met in practice. A discussion of the robustness of these results to deviations from Gaussianity would strengthen the paper.
    *   The analysis is purely asymptotic (`n, m -> ∞` with `m/n -> δ`). While the provided simulations show excellent agreement even for moderate `n`, the theory offers no guarantees on finite-sample performance or the rate of convergence to the asymptotic limit.
    *   The assumption of a random signal `x0` drawn from a known distribution is a technical convenience for the analysis, which may differ from the more common "fixed but unknown" signal model.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Conditions:** Assumption 1, which is central to the main theorems, is quite abstract. While the authors do a good job of showing it holds for the `l1`-norm, a more intuitive discussion of what this assumption entails or what kinds of regularizers might violate it would improve the paper's accessibility to a broader audience.

3.  **Title and Phrasing:** The title "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements **is Equivalent** to One With Linear Measurements" is a very strong claim. Given the asymptotic nature of the result, a more precise title would be "...**is Asymptotically Equivalent**...". This is a minor point but would improve accuracy.

### Detailed Comments

*   The connection made to Brillinger's work is excellent and provides valuable historical context.
*   The comparison to Plan and Vershynin's work is clear and effectively highlights the novelty and precision of the current paper's contributions (moving from loose bounds to exact characterizations).
*   The figures are well-executed and provide compelling evidence for the accuracy of the theoretical predictions, serving as a strong sanity check for the complex mathematical derivations.
*   The extension to group-sparse and low-rank models in Sections 2.2.3 and 2.2.4 is valuable, showcasing the versatility of the proposed framework.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a significant and elegant contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. The main result provides a fundamental insight into the behavior of a widely used algorithm (LASSO) under a challenging but practical measurement model. The work is technically deep, builds upon and significantly advances prior art, and has clear implications for both theory and practice. The weaknesses identified are largely inherent to the powerful theoretical framework employed and do not detract from the importance of the core contribution. This paper will likely be of great interest to researchers working on compressed sensing, statistical learning theory, and high-dimensional inference.
