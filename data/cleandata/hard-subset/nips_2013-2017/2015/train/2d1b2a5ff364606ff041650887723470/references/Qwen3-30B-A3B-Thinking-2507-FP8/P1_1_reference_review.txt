

# Peer Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing an equivalence between the Generalized LASSO applied to non-linear measurements and the same algorithm applied to appropriately scaled linear measurements. The authors consider the problem of estimating a structured signal x₀ ∈ ℝⁿ from measurements of the form yᵢ = gᵢ(aᵢᵀx₀), where gᵢ are independent copies of a link function g. They show that the estimation error of the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements asymptotically equals that of the same algorithm with linear measurements of the form y_lin = μA x₀ + σz, where μ and σ² are defined as μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ) - μγ)²] for γ ~ N(0, 1).

The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 2.1, which establishes this equivalence, and Theorem 2.2, which provides a precise error formula for the asymptotic behavior. The authors then specialize these results to important cases: sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3), group-sparse recovery, and low-rank matrix recovery. Finally, they apply their results to q-bit compressive sensing, showing how to optimize quantization thresholds to minimize estimation error.

This work builds on the important recent advances in the precise analysis of the Generalized LASSO with linear measurements [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 21] and extends them to the non-linear case. It also relates to the earlier work of Brillinger [5] and Plan and Vershynin [17], generalizing their results to the regularized LASSO setting with precise error characterization.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical results. The authors carefully establish the asymptotic behavior of the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements, providing precise error predictions rather than loose upper bounds. The proofs of the main theorems are deferred to the appendix, but the main results are well-motivated and supported by numerical experiments (Figures 1-2) that validate their theoretical predictions. The assumptions are reasonable and well-justified.

**Originality:** The equivalence between non-linear and linear measurements is a novel insight that significantly extends existing work. While Plan and Vershynin [17] considered a constrained version of the LASSO for non-linear measurements, this paper generalizes their result to the regularized LASSO and provides precise error expressions rather than loose upper bounds. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is particularly novel, demonstrating how the theory can guide the design of optimal quantization thresholds.

**Significance:** The results have important implications for practitioners working with non-linear measurements, such as in 1-bit quantization or censored data. The precise error predictions allow for better tuning of the regularizer parameter λ and provide theoretical guarantees for using LASSO-type methods in non-linear settings. The unified framework presented in the paper could be applied to various structured signal recovery problems beyond what's covered in the paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and relates it to existing literature. The main theorems are well-stated, and the special cases are clearly presented. The paper would benefit from slightly more intuitive explanations of the more complex parts of the proofs, but the core ideas are presented accessibly.

## Weaknesses

**Notational Consistency:** There are some minor notational inconsistencies throughout the paper (e.g., the definition of μ in equation (3) versus later in the paper). These could confuse readers and should be addressed.

**Assumption Limitations:** The paper assumes that the measurement matrix has i.i.d. Gaussian entries and that the link function g has known μ and σ². While these are reasonable assumptions for the theoretical analysis, it would be helpful to discuss how the results might extend to more general measurement models or how μ and σ² could be estimated from data when g is unknown.

**Practical Implementation:** The paper focuses on the theoretical equivalence but doesn't provide concrete guidance on how to implement the approach in practice. For example, while the q-bit compressive sensing application is promising, the paper doesn't discuss computational complexity or how the optimization in equation (19) would be implemented for large-scale problems.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of LASSO-type methods under non-linear measurements. The main result (Theorem 2.1) is elegant and general, providing a precise characterization of the error that extends existing work on the Generalized LASSO with linear measurements. The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The main weaknesses (notational inconsistencies and limited discussion of practical implementation) are relatively minor and could be addressed in a revision.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Provides a precise theoretical characterization of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements
- Establishes a novel equivalence between non-linear and linear measurements
- Extends and generalizes recent work by Plan and Vershynin [17]
- Includes important special cases (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and a practical application (q-bit compressive sensing)
- Well-supported by numerical experiments

**Con Acceptance:**
- Some notational inconsistencies that could confuse readers
- Limited discussion of practical implementation details
- Assumptions about known μ and σ² might not always hold in practice

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the theoretical understanding of structured signal recovery with non-linear measurements. The results have significant implications for both theory and practice, and the paper is well-written and technically sound. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the notational inconsistencies.
