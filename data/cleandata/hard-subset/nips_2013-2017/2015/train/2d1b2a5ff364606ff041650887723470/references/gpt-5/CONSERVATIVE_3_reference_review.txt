Summary
The paper studies generalized LASSO under non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0), with Gaussian sensing A and convex regularizers. Its central claim is an asymptotic equivalence: for a broad class of link functions g with µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2], the squared error of the LASSO estimator with non-linear measurements matches that of the same LASSO run on a linear model with scaled signal µx0 and additive Gaussian noise of variance σ^2. Theorem 2.2 provides a precise scalar convex program predicting the asymptotic error as n→∞ with m/n→δ, for any λ and convex f, under a technical Assumption 1. The paper then specializes to sparse (with `1), group-sparse (`1,2), and briefly to low-rank (nuclear norm), and discusses q-bit quantization design via minimizing σ^2/µ^2, connecting to Lloyd–Max.

Relation to prior work: Brillinger (1980s) analyzed LS under link violation in the classical n fixed, m→∞ regime. Plan–Vershynin analyzed constrained LASSO with upper bounds in high dimensions for non-linear models. Precise asymptotics for LASSO with linear measurements are known via AMP and CGMT (Bayati–Montanari, Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi, Thrampoulidis et al.). This paper extends the precise-error line to non-linear observations and general convex f.

Strengths
- Technically ambitious and mostly well-positioned: reduces non-linear LASSO analysis to established linear-noisy equivalents through µ, σ^2, which is clean and intuitive.
- Provides asymptotically exact error predictions via a simple three-variable convex program (Theorem 2.2). The sparse specialization (Theorem 2.3) is explicit and general (beyond Gaussian nonzeros), and recovers/extends known linear results.
- Unifies several structured settings (sparse, group-sparse; initial steps for low-rank).
- The quantization design consequence (optimize thresholds/levels by minimizing σ^2/µ^2) is simple and potentially practically useful; the Lloyd–Max connection is interesting.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope restricted to Gaussian design. The core equivalence and CGMT-based proofs fundamentally rely on i.i.d. Gaussian A. There is no discussion of robustness to sub-Gaussian or structured sensing (partial Fourier, coded diffraction, etc.), which limits practical significance.
- Assumption 1 is opaque and nontrivial to verify in general. While it is instantiated for `1 and `1,2, the nuclear-norm case is left largely as a sketch with strong rotational invariance assumptions and “details left for future work.” As a result, the advertised generality (“wide class of f and g”) is not fully substantiated.
- Theorem 2.1 statement seems stronger than necessary/standard. It claims that in the limit, with probability one, the two errors are equal. Typically one expects both sequences to converge to the same deterministic limit (or to coincide in limit values), not pathwise equality. The authors should clarify whether they prove equality of limits (lim sup/lim inf) rather than equality of random variables in the limit.
- Limited empirical validation. The figures (not provided here) are said to illustrate accuracy, but the manuscript lacks breadth of experiments:
  - No tests with non-Gaussian designs.
  - Few link functions beyond sign/ReLU/quantization.
  - No comparison against GLM-based estimators (e.g., logistic/probit regression for 1-bit), nor against GAMP/Generalized AMP predictions for GLMs, which is a prominent baseline in the non-linear measurement literature.
  - No finite-sample deviation analysis or rate to asymptopia; it would help quantify how large n must be for predictions to be accurate.
- Practical aspects under “unknown g” are underdeveloped. While the algorithm does not need g, performance depends on µ,σ. If g is unknown, how should one estimate µ and σ (or calibrate the scaling), and how sensitive is the error to misestimation?
- Clarity/presentation issues:
  - Assumption 1 could be accompanied by clear sufficient conditions for common f (including nuclear norm under realistic models).
  - Several typos and formatting glitches (footnotes, references incomplete).
  - The discussion of the λ phase transition (λcrit) is terse; guidance for choosing λ in practice is minimal.
  - The low-rank section is incomplete: the required spectral limit conditions and a concrete F(c1,c2,c3) evaluation are deferred.

Quality
The technical approach (CGMT) is sound and consistent with recent precise-asymptotic analyses. However, the generality claims rely on a technical assumption not fully unpacked, and the low-rank case is not fully delivered. Proofs are in the appendix; some central claims (Theorem 2.1 phrasing) need clarification.

Clarity
Generally well-structured but with dense notation and an abstract Assumption 1. The paper would benefit from:
- A precise, weaker statement of Theorem 2.1.
- A dedicated subsection giving verifiable sufficient conditions for Assumption 1 for popular regularizers.
- Clearer guidance on λ selection and its effect.

Originality
Extending precise error analysis to non-linear single-index observations for generalized LASSO appears novel. The reduction to µ,σ and the scalar optimization are meaningful contributions beyond prior upper bounds.

Significance
For the theory community, the result is valuable and likely to be cited. Practical significance is hampered by the Gaussian-design restriction and lack of empirical breadth/robustness studies. The quantization design corollary is appealing but needs experiments.

Pros
- Novel precise-asymptotic results connecting non-linear to linear-noisy LASSO.
- Covers general convex regularizers; explicit sparse and group-sparse specializations.
- Simple, actionable consequence for quantizer design.

Cons
- Restrictive Gaussian design; little discussion of robustness.
- Assumption 1 is abstract; low-rank case incomplete.
- Empirical evaluation limited; missing comparisons to GLM/GAMP baselines.
- Theorem 2.1 phrasing likely too strong; needs clarification.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Rationale: Strong theoretical potential but current form has gaps in generality (assumption clarity, low-rank), limited empirical support, and lacks comparisons to established non-linear methods (GLMs/GAMP). With clearer assumptions, expanded experiments (including non-Gaussian designs and GLM/GAMP baselines), and a corrected statement of Theorem 2.1, I would reconsider. Confidence: high.
