

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to non-linear measurement models of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where gi are independent copies of a link function g. The main contribution is establishing that, under appropriate asymptotic conditions (with A having i.i.d. Gaussian entries), the estimation error of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of the same algorithm applied to linear measurements of the form y = μAx0 + σz, where μ and σ are defined in terms of the link function g.

The paper builds on Plan and Vershynin's [17] work on constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements, but significantly advances the field by providing precise error expressions rather than loose upper bounds. The authors establish a general framework (Theorem 2.1) showing the equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models, then derive precise error formulas (Theorem 2.2) that characterize the performance for various structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank). The paper also demonstrates the practical application to q-bit compressive sensing, where the authors show how to optimize quantization thresholds to minimize estimation error.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing precise asymptotic error expressions for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. This extends Plan and Vershynin's work [17] from loose upper bounds to exact characterizations, which is a substantial advancement.

2. **Novelty**: To the best of my knowledge, this is the first paper to derive asymptotically precise results for LASSO-type programs with non-linear measurements, making it a clear contribution to the field.

3. **Applications**: The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) is particularly compelling, demonstrating how the theoretical results can guide practical design choices for quantization thresholds and levels.

4. **Rigor**: The paper is technically sound, with careful attention to the asymptotic regime, assumptions, and mathematical details. The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their results (e.g., the unit norm assumption for x0).

5. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem statement to main results to specific applications. The figures effectively demonstrate the accuracy of the theoretical predictions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Unit Norm Assumption**: The paper assumes ‖x0‖2 = 1 for simplicity (Footnote 4), which requires the authors to discuss the estimation of x0 only up to a constant of proportionality. While the authors note that this can be adjusted for non-unit norm signals, the assumption might limit applicability in some practical settings where the signal magnitude is important.

2. **Measurement Matrix Restriction**: The analysis assumes A has i.i.d. Gaussian entries, which is common in high-dimensional statistics but may not hold for all practical measurement systems. The paper would benefit from discussing how the results might extend to more general measurement matrices.

3. **Computational Considerations**: The paper focuses on theoretical performance but provides limited discussion about computational aspects. For example, how would one practically estimate μ and σ from data in a real-world application?

4. **Connection to Practice**: While the q-bit compressive sensing application is valuable, the paper could better bridge the gap between theory and practice by discussing implementation challenges or providing more concrete examples of how practitioners could apply these results.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a clear advancement over previous work. The main result (Theorem 2.1) is elegant and general, showing the precise equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models under the LASSO. The paper demonstrates significant novelty by providing exact error expressions rather than loose upper bounds, and the application to q-bit compressive sensing showcases practical relevance.

The paper meets all four criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission:
- **Quality**: The theoretical results are sound and well-justified.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized.
- **Originality**: The precise characterization of LASSO with non-linear measurements is novel.
- **Significance**: The results provide valuable theoretical insight for practitioners using LASSO in non-linear settings.

The main limitations are the unit norm assumption and the Gaussian measurement matrix restriction, but these are common in high-dimensional statistics and the authors appropriately acknowledge them. The paper represents a significant theoretical contribution that would be of interest to the NIPS community.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a clear theoretical contribution that extends previous work on the LASSO with non-linear measurements. The precise error expressions provide valuable insights for both researchers and practitioners, and the application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates practical relevance. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field.
