Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under a single-index model y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) with i.i.d. Gaussian design a_i, where g_i are i.i.d. copies of a possibly unknown nonlinearity g. The estimator is the generalized LASSO (square-root LASSO variant): minimize ||y − A x||_2 + λ f(x) for a convex regularizer f encoding structure (e.g., ℓ1, group ℓ1,2, nuclear norm). The core result is that, asymptotically, the estimation error of LASSO under nonlinear measurements is identical to that under an equivalent linear model y_lin = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2] with γ ∼ N(0,1). Thus the nonlinearity impacts performance only via (μ, σ^2). Building on Gordon’s comparison/CGMT framework, the paper further provides an exact limiting error characterization via a scalar convex program whose inputs are (δ, λ, μ, σ^2) and a functional F that encodes the regularizer f and signal distribution. Specializations are given for sparse recovery with ℓ1 (yielding explicit equations for the limit), group-sparse recovery with ℓ1,2, and a discussion for low-rank recovery under the nuclear norm. An application to q-bit quantized compressed sensing shows that the optimal quantizer for LASSO (in the asymptotic error sense) can be obtained by minimizing σ^2/μ^2, and that Lloyd–Max stationary points satisfy the optimality conditions for this criterion.

This extends classic results of Brillinger for least squares with nonlinearity (consistency up to a scale μ in fixed-n, m → ∞) to the proportional asymptotic regime m/n → δ and to regularized LASSO estimators. It sharpens and complements non-asymptotic bounds for constrained LASSO under nonlinear observations by Plan and Vershynin, which depend on Gaussian widths and only provide constants-hiding upper bounds. It also leverages and generalizes recent precise error analyses for LASSO under linear measurements using AMP and CGMT.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Assumptions: Gaussian design; i.i.d. link copies g with finite μ ≠ 0 and finite σ^2; proportional asymptotics with δ = m/n; mild normalization and boundedness conditions on f and its subgradients; a technical Assumption 1 ensuring existence of the limiting Moreau-envelope functional F(c1, c2, c3) (verified explicitly for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2, and discussed for nuclear norm).
- Results: Theorem 2.1 establishes that the asymptotic error of LASSO with nonlinear measurements equals that of the same LASSO under the equivalent linear model defined by (μ, σ^2). Theorem 2.2 provides a scalar convex optimization whose unique minimizer α* equals the limiting error ||x̂ − μ x0||_2. For ℓ1-regularization with sparse priors, Theorem 2.3 yields coupled equations defining the limiting error and a critical λcrit (with separate regimes when δ < 1), recovering known linear-measurement special cases and extending them to general non-Gaussian sparse priors.
- Proof methodology (sketched): CGMT/Gordon comparisons reduce the high-dimensional random optimization to an auxiliary scalar optimization; the nonlinearity enters only through (μ, σ^2) after conditioning and orthogonal decompositions. While full proofs are deferred, the structure and specializations align with prior CGMT-based analyses.
- Limitations: Gaussian design is essential; extension to sub-Gaussian or structured designs is not addressed. Assumption 1 is abstract; for nuclear norm the paper leaves computation of F for future work. The equality statement in Theorem 2.1 should clearly denote equality of limits (the current wording can be read as pathwise equality rather than equality of limiting values).

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, provides context, and clearly states the main equivalence and the exact error formula. Specializations to sparse and block-sparse models are concrete and include implementable equations. Some expository points can be improved:
- Clarify Theorem 2.1 as “the limits coincide almost surely” rather than implying equality of the finite-n errors.
- Expand Assumption 1 with more examples (beyond ℓ1 and ℓ1,2) and a brief intuitive explanation of why F exists and how to compute it for common f.
- Unify normalization/scaling (e.g., the 1/√n scaling in Eq. (9)) and clarify the relation to the square-root LASSO and to the 1/2||y − A x||_2^2 form.
- Fix minor typos and grammar, and include brief proof sketches to aid readability.
- For the low-rank case, include at least one concrete model (e.g., spiked plus Gaussian noise) where F can be computed using Marchenko–Pastur, with a numerical illustration.

Originality
The nonlinear-to-linear equivalence for the generalized LASSO with an exact asymptotic error formula appears novel. Prior work provided only upper bounds for nonlinear observations or precise formulas limited to linear measurements and specific regularizers. Extending precise analysis to general convex regularizers and to nonlinear single-index observations is a substantial advancement. The quantizer design insight via minimizing σ^2/μ^2 and the connection to Lloyd–Max stationary points is also original and practically useful.

Significance
The results provide a clear, actionable prescription: for Gaussian designs, analyzing LASSO under nonlinear measurements reduces to a linear-noise model with parameters (μ, σ^2). This unifies and simplifies performance predictions across a wide class of nonlinearities, informs parameter tuning (λ), and guides design choices (e.g., quantization thresholds). It will likely be used as a theoretical benchmark and design tool in high-dimensional inference with nonlinear sensors. Scope is limited to Gaussian designs and asymptotics, but within that scope the impact is high.

Pros
- Conceptually clean equivalence reducing nonlinear measurement analysis to linear with effective (μ, σ^2).
- Precise, asymptotically exact error characterization via a low-dimensional convex program.
- Coverage of several important structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank).
- Practical application to optimal quantizer design; interpretable criterion (σ^2/μ^2).
- Connections and improvements over Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin are clearly articulated.

Cons
- Requires i.i.d. Gaussian design; robustness to sub-Gaussian or correlated designs is not treated.
- Assumption 1 is abstract; the low-rank case lacks concrete F evaluations and experiments.
- Theorem 2.1 wording may be misleading; proofs are deferred and could benefit from summaries.
- Practical estimation of μ and σ when g is unknown is not discussed (though relevant in practice).

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Clarify Theorem 2.1 to explicitly state equality of limiting errors almost surely.
- Provide conditions ensuring uniqueness of α* and λcrit beyond the sparse case; discuss numerical solution stability.
- Add a short subsection on estimating μ and σ from data when g is unknown (e.g., method-of-moments via correlations between y and A x, or pilot estimates).
- For the nuclear-norm case, include at least one example where F is computed (e.g., spiked model) and a simulation.
- Discuss potential extensions to sub-Gaussian designs (what parts of the proof rely on rotational invariance?).
- Include finite-sample experiments comparing nonlinear and equivalent linear models across several g (sign, ReLU, saturated quantizers) to illustrate convergence and deviations.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a technically sound and novel equivalence with precise error characterizations, clear practical implications, and strong connections to existing literature. The suggested clarifications and additional examples would further strengthen the manuscript.
