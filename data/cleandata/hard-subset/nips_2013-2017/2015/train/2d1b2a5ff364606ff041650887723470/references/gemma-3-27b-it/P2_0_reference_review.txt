## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the behavior of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems involving non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(aTix0)`.  Building on prior work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, the authors demonstrate a surprising and powerful result: under certain conditions (Gaussian measurement matrices, and a reasonable setup for the signal and regularizer), the non-linear measurement problem is *asymptotically equivalent* to a scaled and noisy linear measurement problem.  This equivalence is quantified by identifying the scaling factor (µ) and noise variance (σ²) that characterize the effective linear model. They provide precise error bounds, generalizing existing results for linear measurements to the non-linear setting and offering novel insights into the role of the link function and regularizer.  The paper also includes a practical application to q-bit compressive sensing, suggesting an algorithm for optimal quantization levels.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Result:** The core result—the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements—is a strong theoretical contribution. It provides a valuable simplification for analyzing and designing algorithms for non-linear measurement systems.
* **Precise Asymptotic Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply establishing equivalence; it delivers precise asymptotic characterization of the estimation error, offering detailed insights into the impact of various parameters (λ, f, g, m/n).  This level of precision is a notable advancement.
* **Generality:** The results apply to a wide class of link functions `g` and regularizers `f`, making the findings broadly applicable.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper meticulously connects its work to the relevant literature in statistics, econometrics, and signal processing, including Brillinger's early work and more recent advancements in compressed sensing and the Generalized LASSO.
* **Practical Application:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing, including the proposed algorithm based on Lloyd-Max, demonstrates the potential practical impact of the theoretical results.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The mathematical notation is generally clear, and the paper is well-structured.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:**  The paper is highly technical and requires a strong background in high-dimensional statistics, optimization, and signal processing.  This density might limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* **Assumptions:** The assumptions (Gaussian measurement matrices, unit norm signals, specific conditions on the regularizer and signal distribution) are relatively strong. While these are common in the field, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions.
* **Lloyd-Max Algorithm Discussion:**  While the connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is intriguing, the discussion feels a bit hand-wavy. A more detailed justification for why LM converges to a stationary point of the relevant objective would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While Figure 1 illustrates the equivalence, a more comprehensive set of numerical experiments, perhaps exploring different link functions and regularizers, would further bolster the claims.  The q-bit application is a good start, but more could be done.
* **Footnote Density:** The frequent use of footnotes (while often helpful) can disrupt the flow of reading.  Some information might be better integrated into the main text.



**Review of Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by rigorous mathematical analysis. The asymptotic results are convincingly derived.  This is a complete and careful piece of work. (Score: Excellent)
* **Clarity:** The writing is generally clear, but the technical density makes it challenging for readers unfamiliar with the specific area. A more intuitive explanation of the core equivalence result might be helpful. (Score: Good)
* **Originality:**  The result demonstrating the equivalence of non-linear and scaled linear measurements is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field. The application to q-bit compressive sensing also adds to the originality. (Score: Excellent)
* **Significance:** The results have the potential to be highly significant.  Understanding that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear ones simplifies the analysis and algorithm design for a wide range of problems. This could lead to more efficient estimation methods and better theoretical understanding. (Score: Excellent)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*  The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result with potential for significant impact.
*  The analysis is rigorous and provides precise error bounds.
*  The results generalize existing work on the Generalized LASSO.
*  The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates practical relevance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*  The technical density may limit accessibility.
*  The strong assumptions require careful consideration.
*  More extensive empirical validation would strengthen the claims.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. While the technical density and strong assumptions are potential drawbacks, the originality, quality, and potential significance of the results outweigh these concerns. I **strongly recommend acceptance** to NIPS.  I suggest the authors consider incorporating more illustrative examples and discussing the limitations of their assumptions in the final version.  A more detailed justification of the Lloyd-Max algorithm connection would also be beneficial.
