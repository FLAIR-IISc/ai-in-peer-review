This paper investigates the performance of the LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements. The core contribution is demonstrating that, surprisingly, the LASSO behaves as if the measurements *were* linear, but scaled and corrupted by noise – with scaling and noise characteristics determined by the non-linearity. The authors derive precise asymptotic error bounds, generalizing previous results for linear measurements to the non-linear case. They also explore applications to q-bit compressive sensing, providing insights into optimal quantization strategies.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question about the applicability of a widely used algorithm (LASSO) in a non-standard setting (non-linear measurements). The problem setup is clearly defined, and the connection to existing work in single-index models, compressed sensing, and regularization is well-established through comprehensive citations. The core result – the equivalence to a scaled linear measurement problem – is quite striking and has potential to simplify analysis and algorithm design in various contexts. The extension to q-bit compressive sensing is a good illustration of the theoretical result, providing a concrete application and showcasing a practical algorithm (Lloyd-Max) for optimization. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The primary result demonstrating the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements is a significant contribution. It challenges the intuition that non-linearities would fundamentally alter the behavior of the LASSO and opens avenues for leveraging existing theory and algorithms.
* **Mathematical Rigor:** The paper presents a rigorous mathematical analysis, with precise asymptotic error bounds derived under reasonable assumptions. The assumptions are clearly stated and justified.
* **Generality:** The results are general, applying to a wide class of link functions `g` and regularizers `f`.  The extension to various structured signals (sparse, low-rank, group-sparse) demonstrates the broad applicability.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors do an excellent job of connecting their work to a rich literature spanning statistics, signal processing, and machine learning.  The discussion of related work is comprehensive and contextualizes the contributions effectively.
* **Practical Implications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing highlights potential practical benefits, suggesting how to optimize quantization strategies based on the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1:** Assumption 1, relating to the limiting behavior of the Fenchel conjugate, feels somewhat opaque and its verification for certain regularizers could be challenging. While the authors mention it holds for the l1 norm, more discussion on the conditions under which it is satisfied for other common regularizers (e.g., nuclear norm) would be beneficial.  A more intuitive explanation of this assumption would be valuable.
* **Complexity of Results:** While the theoretical results are precise, they involve complex expressions (e.g., the optimization problem in Theorem 2.2).  Simplifying these expressions or providing more guidance on how to practically compute them would enhance the usability of the results.  
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While Figure 1 validates the theoretical predictions, the empirical evaluation is limited. More extensive numerical experiments, perhaps with different types of non-linearities and structured signals, would further strengthen the findings. The application in Section 2.3 could benefit from numerical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the Lloyd-Max algorithm and the optimal quantization levels.
* **Clarity of Notation:** The paper uses a significant number of symbols, which can sometimes make it challenging to follow the derivations. While definitions are provided, a more consistent and streamlined notation could improve readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result with the potential to impact the field of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing.
* The analysis is rigorous and the results are well-supported by mathematical derivations.
* The paper builds upon a strong foundation of existing work and clearly articulates its contributions.
* The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the practical relevance of the results.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Assumption 1 is potentially restrictive and could limit the applicability of the results.
* The complexity of the theoretical results and limited empirical evaluation might hinder their adoption by practitioners.
* The density of notation can make the paper challenging to read and understand.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling theoretical contribution. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding the complexity of the results and the limited empirical evaluation, the novelty and significance of the findings outweigh these concerns. I believe the paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing more discussion on Assumption 1 and potentially adding more comprehensive numerical results.  Specifically, providing concrete examples of how to verify Assumption 1 for common regularizers would significantly strengthen the paper.
