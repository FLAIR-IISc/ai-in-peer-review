### Summary of the Paper

This paper analyzes the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to a non-linear measurement model, specifically the single-index model `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`. The main contribution is a theoretical result showing that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit, the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimate in this non-linear setting is precisely equivalent to the error obtained from an associated linear model with Gaussian noise: `y_i = µ * a_i^T x0 + σ * z_i`. The scaling factor `µ` and noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the properties of the link function `g`.

This equivalence allows the authors to leverage recent, sophisticated tools for the precise asymptotic analysis of LASSO (such as the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework) and apply them directly to the non-linear case. The paper provides explicit error formulas for various signal structures, including sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank signals, and demonstrates the theory's accuracy with simulations. This work extends the classical results of Brillinger to the high-dimensional, regularized setting and provides exact characterizations that improve upon recent non-asymptotic but looser bounds from Plan and Vershynin.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical derivation appears sound, building upon a rigorous and modern line of work in high-dimensional statistics. The central claim—that the non-linear problem is asymptotically equivalent to a linear one—is a powerful and elegant result. The claims are supported by a detailed theoretical framework (outlined in the main text and detailed in the appendix) and validated with numerical simulations that show a close match between the derived formulas and empirical performance.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do a good job of positioning their work within the existing literature, clearly articulating how it generalizes and refines previous results. The problem setup, assumptions, and main theorems are stated precisely. The use of examples for sparse and group-sparse recovery helps to ground the abstract formulas.

**Originality:** The work is original in its specific contribution. While the analysis of LASSO and single-index models are both established fields, the paper forges a precise, quantitative link between them in the high-dimensional setting. This moves beyond the indicative bounds of prior work to provide exact performance characterizations, which is a significant theoretical advance. Some of the specific error formulas for linear models (e.g., for non-Gaussian sparse signals) are also claimed to be novel in their own right.

**Significance:** The significance of the work is a point of concern. While the theoretical result is strong, its practical implications are not fully established. The primary weaknesses are:

1.  **Reliance on Idealized Assumptions:** The entire analysis hinges on the measurement matrix `A` having i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a common assumption in this theoretical literature because it makes the problem tractable via powerful tools like the CGMT, but it is a major departure from many practical scenarios. The paper offers no discussion or empirical evidence on how robust these precise predictions are to violations of the Gaussian assumption (e.g., for sub-gaussian or structured random matrices). This severely limits the direct applicability of the quantitative results.

2.  **Asymptotic Nature of Results:** The equivalence holds only in the asymptotic limit as `n, m -> infinity`. While the simulations are promising for moderately large `n`, the theory provides no finite-sample guarantees or rates of convergence. It is unclear how large `n` must be for the predictions to become reliable, which is a crucial question for practitioners.

3.  **Lack of Competitive Benchmarking:** The paper's core message is that a standard, "mismatched" algorithm (LASSO) performs predictably in a non-linear setting. However, to establish the significance of this finding, it is crucial to compare this performance against methods specifically designed for such problems. For instance, in the context of 1-bit compressed sensing (a special case of the model), numerous specialized algorithms exist. The paper does not compare LASSO's performance to any of these established baselines. It only validates that the theory correctly predicts LASSO's own performance. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to judge whether using vanilla LASSO is actually a good practical strategy, or merely a theoretically convenient one. The work characterizes the performance of a potentially suboptimal approach without arguing for its competitiveness.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   Presents a strong, elegant, and novel theoretical result establishing a precise asymptotic equivalence between LASSO performance on non-linear and an associated linear model.
*   Successfully extends a powerful line of theoretical analysis (the precise asymptotics of LASSO) to a broader and more challenging class of measurement models.
*   The derived formulas are explicit and provide clear insights into the interplay between the signal structure, the regularizer, and the non-linear link function.

### Arguments for Rejection

*   The analysis relies critically on strong, idealized assumptions (i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix) whose practical relevance is questionable and whose robustness is not investigated.
*   The results are purely asymptotic, with no finite-sample analysis, limiting their direct utility for practical, finite-dimensional problems.
*   The paper fails to benchmark the performance of the analyzed method (Generalized LASSO) against any competing algorithms designed for non-linear or single-index models. This is a significant omission, as it leaves the practical utility and competitiveness of the approach unevaluated.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a technically impressive and theoretically elegant piece of work. The core result is insightful and extends the frontier of precise performance characterization for regularized estimators. However, the paper's claims of significance are not fully substantiated due to its reliance on idealized assumptions and, most importantly, the complete absence of comparisons to established, specialized methods. While the analysis successfully characterizes *what* LASSO does on this problem, it does not make a convincing case that this is *what one should do*. The contribution is primarily of theoretical interest and may be better suited for a more specialized venue. For a broad conference like NeurIPS, the lack of demonstrated competitiveness against relevant baselines is a major weakness.
