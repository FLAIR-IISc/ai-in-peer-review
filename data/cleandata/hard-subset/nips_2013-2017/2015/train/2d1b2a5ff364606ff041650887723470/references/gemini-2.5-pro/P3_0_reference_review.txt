**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`. This setup, known as the single-index model, is classical but has received renewed attention in the context of high-dimensional statistics. The authors' main contribution is a powerful and elegant result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit, the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator (which is designed for linear models) on these non-linear measurements is *exactly equivalent* to its performance on an associated linear model `y_i = µ * a_i^T x0 + σ * z_i`. The effective signal scaling `µ` and noise level `σ` are determined solely by the statistical properties of the non-linear link function `g`. This equivalence allows the authors to leverage the sophisticated machinery of the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) to derive precise, explicit formulas for the asymptotic mean-squared error (MSE) for a wide range of regularizers (l1, group-l1, nuclear norm) and signal priors.

This work sits at the intersection of several important research lines. It provides a significant generalization of Brillinger's classical work [5] on single-index models from the fixed-`n`, `m` -> ∞ regime to the modern high-dimensional setting where `m/n` is constant. More recently, Plan and Vershynin [17] studied this problem and showed that a constrained LASSO could recover `µx0`, providing non-asymptotic error bounds. This paper dramatically sharpens that result by moving from loose upper bounds (with unspecified constants) for the constrained LASSO to asymptotically *exact* error characterizations for the more common regularized LASSO. The analytical framework is built upon the recent line of work using CGMT for precise LASSO analysis in the linear case [16, 18, 19, 20], extending these powerful tools to the non-linear measurement setting for the first time.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The central result—that LASSO effectively "linearizes" the problem—is a profound and simplifying insight. It unifies a wide variety of non-linear measurement problems (quantization, censoring, etc.) under a single theoretical umbrella. This provides a clear, intuitive understanding of why a simple, off-the-shelf algorithm like LASSO works surprisingly well in these settings.

2.  **Technical Depth and Precision:** The paper provides asymptotically exact predictions for the estimation error, not just upper bounds. The derivation via the CGMT framework represents a state-of-the-art analysis. Theorem 2.2 gives a general formula for the error as the solution to a low-dimensional scalar optimization problem, which is then specialized to provide even more explicit characterizations for important cases like sparse and group-sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3). The agreement between theory and simulation in the figures is remarkable and validates the analysis.

3.  **Generality:** The framework is quite general. It accommodates a wide class of (potentially unknown) link functions `g`, convex regularizers `f`, and structured signal priors. The extension of precise error analysis beyond standard l1-regularization to group-l1 and nuclear norms is a valuable contribution in its own right, even for the linear case.

4.  **Practical Implications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing is compelling. By showing that optimizing the quantizer design is equivalent to minimizing the `σ²/µ²` ratio, the paper transforms a complex, high-dimensional optimization problem into a simple, low-dimensional one. The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is an insightful practical takeaway.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Restrictive Assumptions:** The primary limitation is the reliance on i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices `A`. While standard in this line of theoretical work, it restricts the direct applicability of the results, as many real-world sensing matrices are structured (e.g., Fourier, Hadamard) or have heavy-tailed entries. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the potential for extending these results to a broader class of matrices, perhaps leveraging universality arguments, even if the proofs are left for future work.

2.  **Clarity on Assumption 1:** "Assumption 1" is a key technical condition required for the main theorems. While the authors show it holds for the examples considered, the assumption itself is quite abstract (convergence of the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate). A more intuitive discussion of what this assumption means, which regularizers it might cover beyond the examples, and importantly, if there are common regularizers for which it might *fail*, would improve the paper's clarity and completeness.

3.  **Comparison to Specialized Algorithms:** The paper convincingly shows that the *general* LASSO works well for these non-linear problems. However, for specific non-linearities like 1-bit measurements, there exists a large body of literature on specialized algorithms. A crucial missing piece is an experimental or theoretical comparison showing how this simple LASSO approach compares to state-of-the-art bespoke algorithms for, say, 1-bit or quantized compressed sensing. Is the performance comparable, or is there a significant price to pay for LASSO's generality?

4.  **The `µ=0` Case:** The analysis explicitly requires `µ ≠ 0`. This excludes important cases, such as even link functions `g(x) = g(-x)`. While the authors mention this in a footnote, this is a significant limitation of the framework that should be more prominently discussed in the main text.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   The core theoretical result ("non-linear = linear") is elegant, novel, and provides a fundamental insight into the behavior of LASSO.
    *   The paper makes a significant technical contribution by providing the first asymptotically exact performance analysis for LASSO with general non-linear measurements.
    *   The results are broad, covering many structures and regularizers, and have clear practical implications for problems like quantizer design.
    *   The work clearly advances the state of the art, moving beyond the bounds of Plan and Vershynin [17] to precise characterizations.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    *   The reliance on Gaussian sensing matrices limits the immediate practical scope.
    *   The paper lacks a comparison with specialized algorithms tailored to specific non-linearities, leaving its practical competitiveness unevaluated.

**Conclusion**

This is a high-quality, technically impressive paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the theory of high-dimensional estimation. The central result is both surprising and elegant, providing a powerful unifying framework for understanding a wide range of non-linear measurement problems. While the assumptions on the measurement matrix are strong, this is typical for this line of precise asymptotic analysis. The strengths, particularly the novelty and significance of the theoretical insight, far outweigh the weaknesses. This paper will likely be of great interest to researchers in high-dimensional statistics, signal processing, and machine learning theory. I strongly recommend acceptance.
