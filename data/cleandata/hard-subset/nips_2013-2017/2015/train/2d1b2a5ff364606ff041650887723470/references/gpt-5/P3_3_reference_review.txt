Summary and context
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation from non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with i.i.d. Gaussian sensing vectors and convex regularization. The main claim is that, when one uses the generalized LASSO (square-root LASSO) that ignores the nonlinearity, the asymptotic mean squared error behaves exactly as if the measurements were linear with an effective scale µ = E[γ g(γ)] and additive Gaussian noise with variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). This “nonlinear = linear” equivalence is formalized in Theorem 2.1. Theorem 2.2 then gives an exact asymptotic MSE formula via a three-scalar convex optimization that depends on δ = m/n, λ, the regularizer f through a function F involving a Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate, and the link g only through µ, σ^2. The paper specializes to sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and sketches a low-rank (nuclear norm) setting; for ℓ1 it derives a compact characterization (Theorem 2.3) with a critical λ and phase transition. As an application, the paper treats q-bit quantized CS and shows that the optimal choice of quantizer thresholds/levels for LASSO reduces to minimizing σ^2/µ^2, with Lloyd–Max iterations giving stationary points for that objective.

Relation to prior work
- Brillinger (1980s) analyzed least squares under single-index link in fixed n, m → ∞ and showed consistency towards µ x0 with variance governed by µ, σ^2. This paper extends to high-dimensional proportional asymptotics and to regularized LASSO.
- Plan–Vershynin (2015) gave non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO under nonlinear observations. This paper complements and sharpens their results by providing asymptotically exact risk for the penalized (more common) LASSO and showing the precise role of µ, σ^2.
- Precise LASSO error for linear measurements has been analyzed via AMP/state evolution and via CGMT (e.g., Bayati–Montanari; Thrampoulidis et al.; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi). This paper appears to leverage the CGMT blueprint to handle ignorance of the link and to generalize beyond ℓ1 to other convex regularizers through F.

Quality and technical soundness
- The equivalence result is crisp and compelling: the LASSO’s asymptotic error depends on the unknown link only via µ and σ^2, exactly matching the linear-noise surrogates. This formalizes a phenomenon hinted by Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin.
- The scalar variational formula (Theorem 2.2) is concrete and, for ℓ1, reduces to fixed-point equations matching known linear-measurement characterizations. The sparse case derivation (Theorem 2.3) is consistent with earlier precise analyses in the linear case and extends them to general non-Gaussian signal priors qX0.
- Assumption 1 (existence of F) encapsulates regularizer/signal structure. It is verified for ℓ1 and group ℓ1,2; the low-rank case is plausible but deferred (needs assumptions ensuring convergence of singular value empirical distributions). It would help to provide a concise set of sufficient conditions for Assumption 1 beyond the examples.
- The uniqueness of α* in (10) is stated; a brief justification (convexity/strict convexity in α under σ^2 > 0, properties of F) would strengthen the statement.
- The strong-sounding equality in Theorem 2.1 should be clarified as “the asymptotic limits of the errors coincide a.s.” rather than equality of random errors for each n.

Originality
- To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact risk characterization of penalized generalized LASSO under unknown nonlinear single-index measurements, and the first precise demonstration that the LASSO’s risk is invariant to the nonlinearity except through µ, σ^2. The extension to general convex regularizers via F is also novel.
- The quantization design angle (minimize σ^2/µ^2 and link to Lloyd–Max) is a neat and actionable corollary.

Significance
- The results provide a unifying and practical message: if one insists on using LASSO without modeling the link, one can still predict its performance exactly from µ, σ^2 and tune λ accordingly. This can guide practitioners in settings like 1-bit or q-bit sensing, censoring, and unknown link GLMs.
- The framework suggests immediate design rules (e.g., quantizer optimization), and clarifies the limits of “ignorant” LASSO versus link-aware methods (e.g., GLM/GAMP), setting a baseline.

Clarity
- Generally well organized with a clear narrative from Brillinger/Plan–Vershynin to precise asymptotics. The role of µ and σ^2 is consistently highlighted.
- Some notation could be tightened: the expression n e√n(f∗),c3(·) is hard to parse—please define the indexing and scaling of the Moreau envelope more cleanly. Ensure Theorem 2.1’s equality is stated as equality of limits. Minor typos (mesaurements, eventhough, redifining) should be corrected.
- The low-rank section is rather brief; a bit more detail on conditions ensuring the limit F exists (e.g., models with random orthogonal singular vectors) would improve readability.

Empirical validation
- The figures (not included here) reportedly match theory for sparse and group-sparse under several links. It would be valuable to:
  - Include finite-n convergence plots and sensitivity to λ mis-tuning.
  - Test non-Gaussian sensing (Rademacher, partial Fourier) to probe universality.
  - Compare against link-aware estimators (e.g., logistic/probit LASSO, GAMP) to contextualize performance gaps; your theory suggests ignorance only costs information via µ, σ^2, but link-aware methods can exploit more than those two moments.

Pros
- Conceptually clean equivalence result; reduces a broad nonlinear setting to a linear surrogate with computable parameters.
- Precise, nonasymptotic-in-λ error formula that generalizes prior linear results and covers multiple regularizers.
- Useful specialization to sparse case with explicit fixed-point characterization and phase-transition behavior.
- Practical application to quantizer design with a simple objective (σ^2/µ^2) and an algorithmic recommendation (Lloyd–Max).

Cons and questions
- Gaussian A is essential for CGMT; discuss (or empirically test) universality to sub-Gaussian or structured ensembles.
- Assumption 1 is abstract; provide sufficient conditions and examples beyond ℓ1 and group ℓ1,2, and flesh out the low-rank case.
- Estimation of µ and σ^2 from data when g is unknown is not addressed; simple moment estimators or plug-in schemes would increase practicality.
- Clarify uniqueness and convexity properties in (10); provide a short proof sketch or conditions.
- The µ = 0 case (even links) is excluded; a brief discussion of alternative estimators for direction recovery (e.g., sign measurements) would be useful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a subsection on estimating µ, σ^2 from data and assess the impact on the risk predictions.
- Provide more guidance on optimizing λ using the scalar program (e.g., compute λ that minimizes α* for given δ, ρ).
- Expand empirical evaluation to non-Gaussian A and to compare with GLM-aware baselines.
- Clarify Theorem 2.1’s statement, streamline notation around F and the Moreau envelope, and fix minor typos.

Overall assessment
This is a strong theoretical paper with a clean and impactful message, solidly grounded in contemporary high-dimensional analysis. With minor clarifications and an expanded empirical section probing robustness/universality and practical estimation of µ, σ^2, it would make a valuable contribution.
