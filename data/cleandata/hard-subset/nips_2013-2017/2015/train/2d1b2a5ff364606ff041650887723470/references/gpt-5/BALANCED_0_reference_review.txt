Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under nonlinear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0), with Gaussian design A and convex regularization f in the generalized LASSO x̂ = argminx ||y − Ax||2 + λ f(x). The central result (Theorem 2.1) shows an asymptotic equivalence: for a wide class of nonlinear link functions g (possibly random and unknown), the estimation error of the LASSO behaves as if the measurements were linear with an effective gain µ = E[γ g(γ)] and additive Gaussian noise of variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2], for γ ~ N(0,1). In particular, asymptotically ‖x̂ − µx0‖2 equals the error of the same LASSO run on ylin = µ A x0 + σ z. Building on this reduction, Theorem 2.2 provides an exact expression for the asymptotic error via a three-scalar convex optimization involving δ = m/n, λ, the pair (µ, σ^2), and a functional F that encodes the structure via f and the distribution of x0 (through a Moreau-envelope limit). The paper specializes to sparse LASSO (Theorem 2.3, with a piecewise characterization and a critical λcrit), block-sparse (group LASSO), and briefly outlines the low-rank (nuclear norm) case. An application to q-bit quantized compressed sensing shows how the analysis yields a clear design criterion: minimize σ^2/µ^2 over quantizer thresholds and representation levels, and relates this to Lloyd–Max iterations.

This work advances the constrained-LASSO bounds of Plan and Vershynin [17], which already identified the roles of µ and σ but only gave nonasymptotic upper bounds with unspecified constants. Here, under Gaussian designs and linear asymptotics m/n → δ, the authors obtain precise limits. It also connects to the growing literature on precise LASSO error analysis for linear measurements via AMP and CGMT (e.g., [2, 10, 16, 19, 20, 21]) and extends some of those results to non-ℓ1 regularizers and to the nonlinear-measurement setting.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: the nonlinear-to-linear reduction via (µ, σ^2) is clean, broadly applicable, and matches intuition from single-index models and earlier results (e.g., Brillinger [5], Plan–Vershynin [17]).
- Technical depth: an exact asymptotic characterization (Theorem 2.2) for generalized LASSO under nonlinear measurements, with a tractable scalar convex program; specializations include new results even for linear measurements with general convex f (via the functional F).
- Breadth: covers ℓ1, group ℓ1,2, and sketches nuclear norm; applies to random or unknown g; explicit application to quantized sensing leading to a practical quantizer design rule.
- Empirical validation: figures (not included here) reportedly show close agreement between theory and simulations across several link functions and distributions of x0.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope limited to Gaussian design: Rotational invariance is crucial in the analysis. The paper should discuss whether sub-Gaussian/isotropic designs or structured sensing (e.g., partial Fourier) might satisfy analogous results, even if only conjecturally or with additional assumptions.
- µ = 0 excluded: Important nonlinearities (e.g., even functions like sign with symmetric dithering or |·|) yield µ = 0, where the proposed equivalence does not cover direction recovery. The discussion acknowledges this but could be strengthened with suggestions (e.g., alternative losses, moment-based transformations, or algorithms tailored to µ = 0).
- Assumption 1 (limit F) is abstract: While verified for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2, the nuclear-norm case is left for “future work” regarding evaluation of F. It would help to either provide concrete classes of low-rank priors for which F can be computed (e.g., i.i.d. singular values with bounded moments) or a recipe to approximate F numerically from the prior.
- Statement precision: Theorem 2.1 is phrased as “‖x̂ − µx0‖2 = ‖x̂lin − µx0‖2 with probability one, in the limit,” which reads like pathwise equality rather than equality of deterministic limits. Please rephrase to: both errors converge almost surely to the same deterministic limit defined by (10).
- Practical guidance for λ: The precise error expression could be leveraged to suggest data-driven λ choices; a brief discussion or heuristic (e.g., minimizing the scalar program over λ) would increase practical impact.
- Unknown g: In applications with unknown g, µ and σ^2 are unknown. The paper could suggest simple consistent estimators (e.g., using sample moments of y and A x̂, or cross-moments E[y a_i^T x̂]/(‖x̂‖2^2)) and comment on whether plug-in estimates preserve the asymptotic guarantees.

Quality
- Technically sound and carefully connected to prior precise LASSO analyses. The main assumptions are clearly stated (Gaussian A, convex f with normalization, linear asymptotics, µ ≠ 0). Proof sketches use CGMT-style arguments (as inferred), which is appropriate. Some parts (nuclear norm) are less complete.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized, but dense in places. Suggestions:
  - Clarify convergence statements (a.s. limit vs equality of limits).
  - Collect assumptions (on g, µ, σ^2, independence across i) in one place.
  - Fix typos and notation (e.g., “mesaurements,” consistent use of ℓ2-LASSO vs square-root LASSO nomenclature).
  - Briefly explain the role and intuition of F(c1, c2, c3) after Assumption 1.

Originality
- Strong. To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact characterization for generalized LASSO under nonlinear single-index measurements, and it unifies a broad set of regularizers. It also yields new insights even for linear measurements via the general F framework.

Significance
- High. The reduction to an effective linear model is impactful for theory and practice (e.g., quantized sensing, saturating sensors). The precise formula can guide design choices (λ, quantizers) and deepen understanding of robustness of LASSO to link misspecification.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, general equivalence principle; exact asymptotics.
- Extends precise LASSO analysis beyond ℓ1 and into nonlinear observation models.
- Practical application to quantizer design with a novel criterion.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on Gaussian design; unclear generality beyond.
- Excludes µ = 0, leaving out notable cases.
- Abstract Assumption 1; incomplete treatment for nuclear norm.
- Limited discussion of estimating µ, σ when g is unknown.

Recommendation
- Accept (weak accept). The conceptual contribution and technical precision outweigh the limitations. Addressing the clarity issues and enriching the discussion on estimation of (µ, σ) and on non-Gaussian designs would further strengthen the paper.

Confidence: High.
