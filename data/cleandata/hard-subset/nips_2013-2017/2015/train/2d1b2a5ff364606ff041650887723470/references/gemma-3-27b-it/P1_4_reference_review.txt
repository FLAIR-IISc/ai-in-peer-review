## Peer Review for "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(aTi x0)`. Building upon recent work by Plan and Vershynin, the authors demonstrate a surprising and powerful result: under Gaussian measurement matrices and a specific asymptotic regime, the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of the LASSO with *scaled and noisy linear* measurements. This equivalence is quantified by a proportionality constant `µ` and a noise variance `σ2` that depend only on the link function `g`. The paper provides precise error formulas for both the non-linear and equivalent linear cases, generalizing previous results for linear measurements and offering new insights into the role of the link function, regularization, and measurement number.  Specific applications to sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery and q-bit compressive sensing are also considered.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges Brillinger’s early work on least squares with non-linear measurements, Plan & Vershynin's constrained LASSO analysis, and a wealth of recent advances in precise LASSO analysis (e.g., [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 20, 21]).  Crucially, it highlights how their work extends these results to the non-linear measurement setting, providing more general and precise characterizations. The connection to the single-index model and sufficient dimension reduction is also appropriately noted. The authors demonstrate a strong awareness of the current state-of-the-art in this area.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The main result – the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements – is a significant theoretical contribution.  It fundamentally alters our understanding of how LASSO behaves in non-linear settings.  This finding has the potential to simplify analysis and leverage existing theoretical tools developed for linear measurements.
* **Precision of Results:** The derivation of precise error formulas (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) is a major strength.  Moving beyond loose upper bounds, these formulas provide a detailed understanding of the factors influencing LASSO performance.
* **Generality:** The results hold for a wide class of link functions `g` and regularizers `f`, making them broadly applicable.  The asymptotic regime considered is also reasonably general.
* **Applications:** The paper demonstrates the practical implications of the theoretical results through concrete applications to sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery and q-bit compressive sensing, highlighting the utility of the developed framework.
* **Clear Mathematical Presentation:** The mathematical derivations and notation are generally clear and well-defined, facilitating comprehension.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technicality & Accessibility:** While mathematically sound, the paper is *highly* technical. The numerous assumptions (especially Assumption 1) and the complexity of the formulas might make it challenging for readers outside of a highly specialized area to fully grasp the results.  More intuitive explanations or illustrative examples could improve accessibility.
* **Assumption 1:** Assumption 1 regarding the limit of `n e√n(f∗)(n)` feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks sufficient justification. While the authors provide some discussion of its satisfaction in the sparse recovery case, a more thorough exploration of its validity for different regularizers `f` would strengthen the paper. 
* **Computational Complexity of Error Evaluation:** While Theorem 2.2 provides a precise error formula, actually *computing* the optimal `α*` can be challenging. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of practical methods for approximation or efficient computation would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While Figure 1 illustrates the accuracy of the prediction, the empirical validation remains somewhat limited. More extensive simulations, particularly for non-Gaussian signal distributions or more complex regularizers, would further bolster the claims.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The paper presents a highly original and significant theoretical result.
* The results are mathematically rigorous and well-supported.
* The generality of the results and the applications to various recovery problems are compelling.
* The paper builds upon and extends a rich body of existing literature.

**Con:**

* The technical complexity may limit the paper's accessibility.
* Assumption 1 requires further justification.
* The computational complexity of error evaluation poses a practical challenge.
* The empirical validation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of LASSO in the presence of non-linear measurements. While the technicality and a few minor weaknesses are present, the novelty, precision, and generality of the results outweigh these concerns. The paper is well-written and clearly demonstrates the authors' expertise in the field.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper contingent on the following minor revisions:

1. **Clarify Assumption 1:** Provide a more detailed justification for Assumption 1 and discuss its limitations.  Perhaps explore alternative conditions that could be used.
2. **Improve Accessibility:**  Consider adding more intuitive explanations or illustrative examples to make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.
3. **Discuss Computational Challenges:** Elaborate on practical methods for approximating or efficiently computing the optimal `α*` in Theorem 2.2.
4. **Expand Empirical Validation:** Include more extensive simulations with diverse settings to demonstrate the robustness of the theoretical results.
