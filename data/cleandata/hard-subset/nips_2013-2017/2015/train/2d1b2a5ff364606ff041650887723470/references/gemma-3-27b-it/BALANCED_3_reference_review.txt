This paper investigates the performance of the LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements. The core result demonstrates that, surprisingly, in a high-dimensional asymptotic regime, the LASSO behaves *as if* the measurements were linear, with an effective scaling and noise level determined by the properties of the non-linear link function. This is a significant and insightful result, generalizing prior work on linear measurements and providing a theoretical foundation for the practical use of LASSO in non-linear settings. The authors provide precise error formulas and demonstrate their accuracy through simulations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The equivalence result is highly novel and bridges a gap between classical linear regression and more complex non-linear models. This has significant implications for both theoretical understanding and practical applications where non-linear measurements are common (e.g., compressed sensing with quantized data).
* **Technical Rigor:** The paper employs a sophisticated mathematical analysis, building upon recent advances in high-dimensional statistics and random matrix theory (specifically, the CGMT framework). The theorems are stated precisely and appear well-motivated.
* **Generality:** The results are quite general, applying to a wide class of link functions *g* and regularizers *f*. The analysis isn't limited to specific structures like sparsity, but extends to group sparsity and low-rank recovery.
* **Practical Implications:** The work provides a clear understanding of how the non-linearity affects performance, quantifying it through the parameters µ and σ².  The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates a concrete benefit, allowing the optimization of quantization thresholds using the derived formulas.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The introduction clearly motivates the problem and relates it to existing literature. The organization is logical, with a clear progression from general results to specific applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** Assumption 1, regarding the limit of the Fenchel conjugate, seems somewhat opaque and its justification isn't fully convincing. While stated to hold in many cases, more discussion of conditions under which it holds *without* requiring significant computational effort would be valuable.  The connection to practical regularizers (beyond `1,2`) needs further elaboration.
* **Complexity:** The mathematical exposition is dense and requires a strong background in high-dimensional statistics and random matrix theory. While appropriate for NIPS, some readers might struggle with the numerous technical details.  More intuitive explanations of key concepts (like the CGMT framework) would enhance accessibility.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the figures validate the theory, the empirical evaluation is relatively limited. Expanding the numerical experiments to explore a wider range of non-linear functions *g* and regularizers *f* would strengthen the claims.  Specifically, exploring cases where the asymptotic assumptions are violated could be insightful.
* **q-bit Compressive Sensing Application:** The discussion of the q-bit compressive sensing application, while interesting, feels somewhat rushed.  A more detailed explanation of how the derived formulas can be used *in practice* to optimize quantization thresholds would be beneficial. A comparison to existing quantization schemes could highlight the advantage of this approach.
* **Notation:** The notation, particularly with the subscripts indicating the problem instance (e.g., x(n)0), can be cumbersome and occasionally confusing.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a truly novel and significant theoretical result that significantly advances our understanding of LASSO in non-linear measurement settings.
* The technical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated.
* The results have practical implications for a wide range of applications.
* The paper is well-organized and clearly written (despite some density).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Assumption 1 requires further justification and explanation.
* The mathematical complexity may limit the accessibility of the paper.
* The empirical evaluation is limited and could be strengthened.
* The application to q-bit compressive sensing could be more thoroughly developed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field. The core result is highly original and impactful. While there are some weaknesses, they are outweighed by the paper's strengths. The assumptions should be clarified, the presentation could be slightly more accessible, and the empirical evaluation could be expanded. However, these are relatively minor concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should focus on clarifying Assumption 1, providing more intuitive explanations of key technical concepts, and expanding the empirical evaluation.  A more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the q-bit compressive sensing application would also be beneficial.
