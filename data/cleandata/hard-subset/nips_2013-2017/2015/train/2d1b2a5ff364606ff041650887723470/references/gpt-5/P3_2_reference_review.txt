Summary and contributions:
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under single-index, nonlinear measurement models of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0), with Gaussian sensing vectors a_i and convex regularizers f. The main claim is an exact asymptotic equivalence: for LASSO-type estimators, nonlinear measurements behave as if they were linear with an effective gain μ = E[γ g(γ)] and an effective noise variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Concretely, the asymptotic squared error of generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements matches that of the same estimator applied to linear measurements y_lin = μ A x0 + σ z. Building on CGMT-style analysis, the paper provides:
- Theorem 2.1: Nonlinear = linear equivalence of the asymptotic error (with Gaussian A).
- Theorem 2.2: A precise, deterministic scalar convex program that yields the limiting MSE, explicitly showing the roles of δ = m/n, λ, μ, σ, and the regularizer via a functional F derived from the Moreau envelope of the conjugate f*.
- Specializations to sparse, group-sparse, and (partially) low-rank settings, including an explicit characterization for ℓ1-regularization (Theorem 2.3), capturing both δ > 1 and δ < 1 regimes and the λcrit phenomenon.
- An application to q-bit CS showing that minimizing the LASSO error is equivalent to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, and arguing that Lloyd–Max quantizer design yields stationary points for this objective.

Relation to prior work:
The work extends Brillinger’s classical fixed-n asymptotics for LS under link violation by handling high-dimensional scaling (m/n → δ) and general convex regularization. It sharpens Plan-Vershynin’s nonasymptotic bounds for constrained LASSO under nonlinear measurements by providing exact asymptotic formulas for the regularized LASSO, and makes precise the role of μ and σ^2 that appeared in those bounds. Compared to AMP-based precise risk analyses for linear measurements (e.g., Bayati-Montanari; Thrampoulidis et al.; Oymak et al.), the paper leverages CGMT to generalize beyond ℓ1 in a unified way and to the nonlinear-measurement setting.

Strengths:
- Clear and significant conceptual message: treat a broad class of nonlinearities as effective linear models with known μ and σ^2 for the purpose of LASSO risk.
- Technically strong: CGMT-based derivations are consistent with the literature and produce a tractable scalar optimization for the asymptotic MSE.
- Broad applicability across convex regularizers; concrete, new precise results for ℓ1 with non-Gaussian coefficient distributions; group-sparse extension; useful quantization design insight via σ^2/μ^2.
- The λcrit phase behavior in the compressed regime is characterized precisely.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Assumption 1 (existence of the pointwise limit of the scaled Moreau-envelope functional for f*) is abstract. While verified for ℓ1 and sketched for block ℓ1,2 and nuclear norms, the low-rank case is left essentially as “future work” with no experiments, which weakens the claimed breadth.
- Theorem 2.1 statement is phrased as equality of errors with probability one in the limit. It would be clearer to state that the limits of the errors coincide (i.e., equality of limiting values), not that the random errors themselves are equal.
- The analysis requires A to be i.i.d. Gaussian. Practical systems often use sub-Gaussian or structured (e.g., partial Fourier) sensing; the robustness of the equivalence to these settings is not addressed.
- The framework assumes μ ≠ 0 and σ^2 > 0; important cases with μ = 0 (e.g., even nonlinearities like square-law sensors) are excluded. Can the approach be extended to handle μ = 0 by considering higher-order statistics or different estimators?
- When g is unknown (a stated motivation), μ and σ^2 are unknown. The paper notes that only direction is identifiable in some cases but does not discuss data-driven estimation of μ, σ^2 or calibration procedures, which would increase practical impact.
- The quantizer design section is interesting but somewhat tangential and light empirically; showing that LM iterations decrease σ^2/μ^2 in practice, and comparing with alternative designs, would strengthen the claim.
- Finite-sample accuracy is not quantified; some guidance on how quickly the asymptotics set in would be useful.

Quality:
The theoretical development is sound and well-grounded in modern high-dimensional analysis. The specialization to ℓ1 is carefully worked out; the two-regime behavior with λcrit is insightful and matches known phenomena in linear settings. Some parts (low-rank) are underdeveloped, and several practical facets (unknown g, non-Gaussian A) are left open.

Clarity:
Overall clear, but notation is heavy. Assumption 1 could benefit from more intuition, examples, and a short, self-contained derivation of F for the main regularizers. Theorem 2.1 wording should be clarified as noted. A brief algorithmic note on how to numerically solve the scalar convex program (and how to compute F in common cases) would aid reproducibility.

Originality:
High. To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact characterization of LASSO under general single-index nonlinearities, with a clean and practically useful equivalence to a linear model identified by μ and σ^2. The precise ℓ1 results beyond Gaussian nonzero entries are new.

Significance:
High. The result provides a unifying and operational recipe: for LASSO-based recovery under unknown nonlinearities, compute μ, σ^2 and reuse linear-measurement risk formulas. It informs λ tuning, sample complexity, and quantizer design. Likely to influence both theory and practice in compressed sensing, quantized sensing, and robust regression.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify Theorem 2.1’s statement (equality of limits).
- Expand on Assumption 1 with a table of F(c1,c2,c3) for standard regularizers and guidance on numerical evaluation.
- Provide experiments for low-rank recovery and additional nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU, saturation with dithering).
- Discuss extensions beyond Gaussian A (orthogonally invariant ensembles) and the μ = 0 case.
- Comment on estimating μ and σ^2 from data when g is unknown.

Pros:
- Strong, elegant theoretical contribution with clear practical implications.
- Broadly applicable across regularizers; precise, non-asymptotic-free formulas.
- Bridges and extends multiple lines of prior work (Brillinger, Plan–Vershynin, CGMT/AMP).

Cons:
- Abstract assumption and incomplete low-rank treatment.
- Gaussian-sensing restriction; no discussion of μ = 0.
- Limited empirical validation in some sections; calibration aspects not addressed.

Recommendation: Accept (strong). Confidence: High.
