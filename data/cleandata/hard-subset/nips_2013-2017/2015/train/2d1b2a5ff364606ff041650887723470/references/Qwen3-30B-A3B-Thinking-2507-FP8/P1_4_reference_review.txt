

# Peer Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental question in high-dimensional statistics: how does the LASSO behave when measurements are non-linear (yi = gi(ai^T x0)) rather than linear (yi = ai^T x0 + noise)? The authors show that for large dimensions, the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to the LASSO with linear measurements that have been scaled and corrupted by noise. Specifically, they prove that the error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements converges to the same value as the error of the LASSO with linear measurements of the form yi = µ(ai^T x0) + σzi, where µ and σ are defined by the link function g as µ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ)-µγ)²] for γ ~ N(0,1). This work extends previous results by Brillinger [5] (who studied least squares with fixed dimension) and Plan and Vershynin [17] (who provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO).

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors use the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, which is a well-established tool for precise analysis of high-dimensional statistical problems. The proofs are complete (though deferred to the appendix) and the assumptions are clearly stated. The paper provides precise error formulas for specific structured signal cases (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank), which is a significant contribution beyond previous non-asymptotic bounds.

The numerical validation in Figures 1-2 effectively demonstrates the accuracy of the theoretical predictions across various settings. The paper also provides a practical application to q-bit compressive sensing, showing how their theoretical results can inform optimal quantization design.

However, the paper would be strengthened by more explicit discussion of the limitations of its assumptions (e.g., i.i.d. Gaussian measurements, unit-norm signals). The authors mention that their results can be extended to non-unit-norm signals, but a more thorough discussion of the robustness to violations of other key assumptions would be valuable.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly sets up the problem, motivation, and contributions. The technical sections flow logically from the general result to specific applications. The assumptions and theorems are precisely stated, and the paper provides sufficient detail for experts to follow the arguments.

The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The authors make good use of figures to illustrate their theoretical results, which is particularly helpful for readers who might not be familiar with the technical details. The paper is accessible to researchers in high-dimensional statistics and signal processing.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending the precise analysis of the LASSO from linear to non-linear measurements. While Plan and Vershynin [17] provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements, this work provides precise asymptotic characterizations for the regularized LASSO, which is more commonly used in practice.

The key insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled and noisy linear measurements is novel and elegant. The paper also extends the precise error analysis to structured signals beyond the standard sparse case (group-sparse, low-rank), which is a useful contribution. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is particularly compelling as it demonstrates the practical utility of the theoretical results.

The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work, particularly in the discussion of previous results by Brillinger [5] and Plan and Vershynin [17]. The authors also provide appropriate citations to relevant literature in the field.

## Significance

The results have both theoretical and practical significance. Theoretically, the paper provides a precise understanding of how non-linearities affect the LASSO, which is a fundamental question in high-dimensional statistics. Practically, the paper demonstrates how this understanding can be applied to improve q-bit compressive sensing by optimizing quantization thresholds and levels.

The paper advances the state of the art in the analysis of high-dimensional statistical problems by extending the precise error analysis from linear to non-linear measurements. This has the potential to influence both theoretical and applied research in signal processing, statistics, and machine learning.

## Strengths

1. Provides precise theoretical characterization of LASSO with non-linear measurements, extending previous work on linear measurements.
2. General result applies to a wide class of regularizers and link functions.
3. Includes specific applications to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery.
4. Demonstrates practical utility through application to q-bit compressive sensing.
5. Well-written and well-organized with clear statements of assumptions and theorems.

## Weaknesses

1. Lacks explicit discussion of the implications of relaxing the i.i.d. Gaussian measurement assumption.
2. Could provide more detailed discussion of how their approach relates to alternative methods for non-linear measurements (e.g., the methods mentioned in [24] for binary measurements).
3. Limited discussion of computational aspects of implementing the LASSO with non-linear measurements.
4. The paper mentions that for some special cases (e.g., binary measurements), different methodologies than LASSO have been proposed, but doesn't thoroughly discuss how their approach relates to these alternatives.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the LASSO in the context of non-linear measurements. The results are both theoretically significant and practically useful, as demonstrated by the application to q-bit compressive sensing. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS.

## Additional Comments

The paper is a nice extension of the authors' previous work on the LASSO with linear measurements. The connection between non-linear measurements and scaled linear measurements is elegant and has important implications for both theory and practice. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is particularly compelling and shows how theoretical insights can directly inform practical system design.

The authors should consider adding a brief discussion of the computational complexity of their approach, as this would help readers understand the practical feasibility of applying these results. They might also want to explicitly address how their results relate to other recent work on non-linear measurement systems (e.g., [24] for binary measurements).
