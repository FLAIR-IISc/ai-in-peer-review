Summary
The paper studies generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) under Gaussian designs. It shows that, asymptotically in high dimensions with m/n→δ, the squared-error of the generalized LASSO behaves as if the measurements were linear with an effective scaling and noise: ylin = µ A x0 + σ z, where µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2], γ∼N(0,1). Theorem 2.1 states an asymptotic “nonlinear = linear” equivalence for the estimation error, and Theorem 2.2 provides a precise characterization via a three-scalar convex program whose data encode the structure in x0 through a function F derived from the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate of the regularizer. The paper then specializes to sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) settings, deriving closed-form characterizations in the sparse case (Theorem 2.3), and discussing how to compute F for the others. As an application, the paper considers q-bit quantized sensing, reducing quantizer design to minimizing σ^2/µ^2 and arguing that Lloyd–Max yields stationary points of this objective.

Relation to prior work
This builds on classic single-index results (Brillinger) showing LS consistency up to a scaling under Gaussian designs and recent non-asymptotic bounds for constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements (Plan & Vershynin), where µ and σ already emerge. It also leverages precise LASSO error analyses under linear measurements via AMP and CGMT (Bayati–Montanari; Thrampoulidis–Oymak–Hassibi). The novelty here is an asymptotically exact equivalence between non-linear and corresponding linear measurements for generalized LASSO, plus precise error formulas applicable to a range of convex regularizers.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and useful reduction: establishes that (under Gaussian designs) the impact of the unknown link g on generalized LASSO is entirely captured by the two scalars µ, σ^2 for asymptotic MSE. This unifies and sharpens earlier upper bounds.
- Precise, computable predictions: Theorem 2.2 reduces asymptotic MSE to a tractable scalar convex optimization. The sparse specialization (Theorem 2.3) is explicit and appears novel beyond earlier results limited to ℓ2^2-LASSO or specific signal distributions.
- Broad scope of regularizers: The framework covers ℓ1, ℓ1,2, and nuclear norm (with caveats), aligning with established practice.
- Application to quantization: The reduction to σ^2/µ^2 is practical; the observation about Lloyd–Max stationarity for this objective is interesting.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Heavy reliance on Gaussian design: The results assume A has i.i.d. N(0,1) entries. There is no universality claim for sub-Gaussian or structured designs, which limits applicability given modern sensing systems and ML settings.
- Ambiguity/precision in statements: Theorem 2.1 is phrased as equality of errors “with probability one in the limit n→∞.” As written, it reads like exact equality of random errors, rather than equality of their limits. Please clarify that it is the limits of the errors that coincide (or strengthen the statement if true).
- Assumption 1 is abstract: The key limit defining F is nontrivial. The paper verifies it for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2, but the low-rank case is effectively deferred to future work (requires an assumption on the LSD of singular values and then “we leave the details”). Given this is one of the main advertised structures, the treatment feels incomplete.
- µ ≠ 0 restriction: Important cases with even links (e.g., certain saturations) are excluded. The paper notes alternatives exist for µ=0 but does not analyze robustness near µ≈0, which is practically relevant (ill-conditioning of effective SNR).
- Practicality of tuning and unknown g: The precise formulas depend on µ, σ and, for λ selection, on the distribution of the nonzeros (qX0). If g is unknown, how should µ, σ be estimated from data, and how sensitive are the predictions/tuning to estimation errors? This is not discussed.
- Finite-sample behavior: The work is purely asymptotic. No finite-n non-asymptotic deviation bounds or guidance on how large n must be for accuracy are provided. Given the robustness claims, some finite-n validation would strengthen the paper.
- Presentation and references: Numerous typos/formatting issues; several references are incomplete (missing years/venues). The exposition is dense; definitions (e.g., of Theorem 2.1’s probability statement) and Assumption 1 could be sharpened. Figures are referenced but not shown here; reproducibility details are absent.

Quality
Technically plausible and in line with CGMT-based analyses. The reduction to an equivalent linear model with (µ,σ^2) is consistent with earlier insights (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin). However, key details (proofs, low-rank case) are either deferred or only sketched. The claims would benefit from clearer statements and a more thorough treatment of assumptions (especially Assumption 1 and low-rank).

Clarity
Readable but quite terse and notation-heavy. The core ideas could be presented more cleanly (e.g., first state the exact sense of equivalence, then the optimization). Assumption 1 needs intuition. Improve typos and reference formatting.

Originality
Extends precise LASSO error analysis to non-linear measurements and general convex regularizers. The asymptotically exact equivalence result appears new and of interest.

Significance
Useful for theory-minded researchers working on high-dimensional M-estimation with non-linear measurements and for practitioners dealing with quantization/saturation under Gaussian designs. Practical impact is limited by Gaussian design and by the need to know/estimate µ and σ.

Questions for the authors
1) Clarify Theorem 2.1: is it equality of limits of the errors, or pathwise equality in the limit? Please restate precisely.
2) Universality: Do you expect the equivalence and error formula to hold for sub-Gaussian designs, or rotationally invariant designs? Any evidence?
3) µ, σ estimation: If g is unknown, how should µ and σ be estimated from data, and how does this affect the asymptotic error/tuning?
4) Finite-sample: Provide empirical evaluations showing convergence rates of the predictions across δ, ρ, and different g.
5) Low-rank: Specify concrete conditions on X0 and provide at least a worked example where F is computed and predictions validated.
6) Behavior when µ≈0: Discuss stability and whether small-µ regimes require modified estimators.

Pros
- Conceptually compelling equivalence and precise predictions
- General treatment of convex regularizers; explicit sparse specialization
- Bridges and sharpens prior work (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin; CGMT line)
- Practical quantization design insight

Cons
- Gaussian design only; no universality evidence
- Ambiguous theorem statement; abstract assumption; incomplete low-rank treatment
- No discussion of estimating µ,σ; no finite-sample theory
- Presentation and references need polishing

Recommendation: Weak Reject
The core idea is strong and, if the proofs are correct, potentially impactful. However, the current version overreaches in its claims relative to the presented rigor and completeness (especially for low-rank), lacks clarity in key statements, and is limited in scope (Gaussian designs) without universality evidence. With clearer statements, a fuller treatment of the low-rank case, empirical validation, and improved exposition, this could be a solid contribution.
