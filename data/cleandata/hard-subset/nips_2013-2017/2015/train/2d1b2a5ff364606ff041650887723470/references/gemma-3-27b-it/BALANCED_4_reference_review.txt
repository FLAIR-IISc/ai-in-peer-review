This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements. The core result demonstrates that, under certain conditions, the non-linear measurement problem is asymptotically equivalent to a scaled and noisy linear measurement problem, allowing the authors to leverage existing precise analysis tools from the linear case. This is a significant contribution, as it bridges the gap between well-understood linear models and more complex non-linear scenarios. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and the technical contributions appear sound.

**Summary of Contributions:** The authors demonstrate that the LASSO, when used with non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(aTi x0)`, behaves asymptotically as if the measurements were linear with a scaling factor `µ` and noise variance `σ2`, both dependent on the link function `g`. They derive precise error expressions, generalizing existing results from the linear case, and provide specific results for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery scenarios.  They also connect their work to q-bit compressive sensing, proposing an algorithm based on the Lloyd-Max method for optimizing quantization levels.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** Establishing the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements is a key result. It significantly expands the applicability of existing LASSO analysis techniques to a broader range of problems. The precise error characterization, particularly for various structured signals, is valuable.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper builds on a strong foundation of recent work in high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the asymptotic analysis seems well-justified. The connection to Brillinger’s earlier work is appropriately highlighted.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is generally very well-written and logically organized. The notation is consistent, and the key ideas are clearly explained. The inclusion of illustrative figures (mentioned but not included in the provided excerpt) would further enhance clarity.
* **Practical Implications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing is a nice example of how the theoretical results can be applied to a practical problem, and the proposed algorithm using the Lloyd-Max method is a tangible contribution.
* **Comprehensive Literature Review:**  The authors provide a thorough review of related work, placing their contribution in the context of existing research on the single-index model, compressed sensing, and regularization techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:**  The assumption of i.i.d. Gaussian measurement vectors is a standard one, but it limits the applicability of the results. While the authors mention the possibility of extending the work to other measurement matrices, the analysis presented is specifically tailored to the Gaussian case.  The assumption that x0 has unit Euclidean norm simplifies the presentation but might not hold in all practical applications.
* **Computational Complexity of Optimal Quantization:** While the connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is interesting, the computational complexity of finding the optimal quantization levels and thresholds could be significant, especially for high-dimensional problems.  Further discussion on the practical feasibility of this optimization would be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper mentions illustrative figures, but the excerpt doesn't include any experimental results beyond their mention.  Including more comprehensive empirical validation, demonstrating the accuracy of the theoretical predictions in realistic scenarios, would strengthen the paper.
* **Assumption 1:** The presentation of Assumption 1, while stated, lacks complete intuition as to *why* it is needed and *how* easily it is checked in practice. A more detailed discussion of its implications and verification would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution with potential for broad impact.
* The results are technically sound and well-motivated.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
* The work bridges a gap between linear and non-linear measurement problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumption of Gaussian measurement vectors limits the applicability of the results.
* The lack of extensive empirical validation weakens the evidence supporting the theoretical claims.
* Assumption 1 requires further clarification and discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The equivalence result is valuable, and the precise error analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements.  While the assumptions and limited empirical validation are weaknesses, the overall quality of the work is high. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* **Discuss extensions beyond Gaussian measurements:**  Briefly discuss the challenges and potential approaches for extending the results to other measurement matrices.
* **Provide more comprehensive empirical results:** Include more experimental validation to demonstrate the accuracy of the theoretical predictions in practical scenarios.
* **Elaborate on Assumption 1:**  Provide a more intuitive explanation of why Assumption 1 is necessary and how it can be verified in practice.
* **Discuss computational aspects:**  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of finding the optimal quantization levels and thresholds.
