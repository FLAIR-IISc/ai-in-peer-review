### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a structured signal vector `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. While the standard Generalized LASSO algorithm is designed for linear measurements, the authors investigate its performance in this non-linear setting. The main and striking contribution is a theorem establishing that, in a high-dimensional asymptotic regime (where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio `δ = m/n`), the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is *exactly equivalent* to its performance on a corresponding linear model with additive Gaussian noise.

Specifically, the non-linear model `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)` is shown to be asymptotically equivalent to a linear model `yi = µ * a_i^T x0 + σ * zi`, where `zi` are i.i.d. standard normal variables. The scaling factor `µ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the statistics of the link function `g`. Building on this equivalence, the paper leverages recent tools from random matrix theory (specifically, the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework) to derive a precise, deterministic formula for the asymptotic mean-squared error `||x_hat - µx0||^2`. This formula is given as the solution to a simple, low-dimensional convex optimization problem.

The authors demonstrate the generality of their framework by applying it to several common structured signal recovery problems, including sparse recovery (l1-norm), group-sparse recovery (l1,2-norm), and low-rank matrix recovery (nuclear norm). Finally, a compelling application to q-bit compressive sensing is presented, where the theoretical equivalence is used to transform the difficult problem of designing optimal quantization thresholds and levels into a much simpler problem of minimizing the ratio `σ^2/µ^2`.

This work builds upon and significantly sharpens previous results. It extends the classical work of Brillinger (1983) from the fixed-`n` to the high-dimensional setting and transforms the non-asymptotic, loose upper bounds of Plan and Vershynin (2015) into precise asymptotic characterizations.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper appears to be of high technical quality. The claims are substantial and supported by a rigorous theoretical framework that has been successfully applied to related problems in the literature. The derivation of a precise, closed-form (in terms of a scalar optimization) expression for the estimation error is a significant achievement. The claims are also validated empirically through simulations (Figs 1-2), which show an excellent match between the theoretical predictions and experimental results for different scenarios. The work is presented as a complete theoretical contribution with a clear application.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the problem, a clear summary of the contributions, and a thorough discussion of the relevant literature, effectively situating the work. The main results (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) are stated clearly and concisely. The authors do a good job of explaining the intuition behind their main equivalence result and demonstrating its power through specialization to various recovery problems and the quantizer design application.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original. While it uses an existing analytical framework (CGMT), its application to this problem is novel and leads to a powerful new insight. The central result—that LASSO with non-linear measurements behaves identically to LASSO with specific linear measurements—is a fundamental and non-obvious contribution. This equivalence provides a new and much simpler lens through which to analyze a wide class of non-linear estimation problems. This work goes far beyond previous results, which were either limited to the non-regularized case (Brillinger) or provided only non-tight upper bounds (Plan & Vershynin). The application to quantizer design is also an original and clever consequence of the main theory.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high.
1.  **Theoretical Impact:** It provides a unifying and simplifying principle for a broad class of problems involving structured signal recovery from single-index models. Researchers studying these models can now analyze them using the much more developed and simpler theory of the linear LASSO.
2.  **Practical Impact:** The ability to predict performance precisely allows for principled system design. The quantizer design application is a prime example: the theory converts a complex, simulation-driven design problem into a simple optimization of `σ^2/µ^2`, which can be solved efficiently. This could have a real impact on the design of quantized sensing systems.
3.  **Future Work:** The ideas presented are likely to inspire further research, such as investigating whether similar equivalence principles hold for other algorithms, other measurement ensembles, or different non-linear models.

**Weaknesses / Limitations:**
1.  **Asymptotic Regime:** The results are strictly asymptotic. While the simulations show good agreement for reasonably sized problems, the theory provides no finite-sample guarantees or information about the rate of convergence to the asymptotic limit.
2.  **Gaussian Measurements:** The analysis relies critically on the assumption that the measurement matrix `A` has i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a common assumption in this line of work but limits the direct applicability to other types of measurement designs. It would be valuable to discuss the potential for extending these results to sub-gaussian or other matrix ensembles.
3.  **Condition µ ≠ 0:** The analysis requires that the constant `µ = E[γg(γ)]` is non-zero. The authors acknowledge this limitation in a footnote, which excludes certain link functions (e.g., even functions). While this is a limitation of the LASSO-based approach itself rather than just the analysis, it is a key assumption that restricts the scope.

### Arguments for Acceptance (Pros)
*   **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper establishes a powerful and elegant equivalence principle between non-linear and linear measurement models for the LASSO.
*   **Precise Performance Characterization:** It provides exact asymptotic formulae for the estimation error, a significant improvement over existing loose bounds.
*   **High Generality:** The framework is applicable to a wide variety of structured signal models (via general convex regularizers) and non-linearities.
*   **Clear Practical Relevance:** The application to optimal quantizer design demonstrates the utility of the theory for solving practical engineering problems.
*   **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making a complex topic accessible.

### Arguments for Rejection (Cons)
*   The reliance on an asymptotic regime and i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices are significant limitations on the direct applicability of the theory, though these are standard in this research area.
*   The core analytical tools (CGMT) are not new, although their application here is highly novel and impactful.

### Conclusion and Recommendation
This is an excellent theoretical paper with clear practical implications. The core result is novel, significant, and elegant. It deepens our understanding of the LASSO algorithm and provides a powerful new tool for analyzing its performance in a broad class of non-linear settings. The weaknesses are primarily the standard limitations of the underlying analytical framework (asymptotic nature, Gaussian assumption) and do not detract from the importance of the contribution. The paper is a clear advance for the field and is very well suited for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
