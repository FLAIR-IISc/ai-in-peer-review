

# Peer Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary
This paper establishes a precise equivalence between LASSO estimation under non-linear measurements (y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)) and LASSO under appropriately scaled linear measurements (y_i = μa_i^T x_0 + σz_i), where μ and σ^2 are defined in terms of the link function g. The authors extend previous work by Plan and Vershynin [17], which provided loose upper bounds on the error, to deliver a precise asymptotic characterization of the LASSO error that applies to a wide range of structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank). This work generalizes Brillinger's classical result [5] to the high-dimensional structured signal setting.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper's core contribution—Theorem 2.1 showing the asymptotic equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models—is elegant and significant. It precisely characterizes how the LASSO treats non-linear measurements as scaled linear measurements with noise, which is a fundamental insight for practitioners and theorists alike.

2. **Technical Rigor**: The authors employ the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, a state-of-the-art technique for high-dimensional statistical analysis. Their proofs are technically sound and build upon recent advances in the analysis of structured signal recovery.

3. **Broad Applicability**: The paper successfully generalizes the equivalence to multiple structured recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) with detailed analysis for each, demonstrating the framework's versatility.

4. **Practical Implications**: The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) is particularly compelling. The authors derive a clear optimization criterion (minimizing σ^2/μ^2) for optimal quantization thresholds, which provides actionable guidance for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope for μ = 0**: The paper assumes μ ≠ 0 (Section 1.1), but many important non-linear measurements (e.g., g(x) = sign(x)) have μ = 0. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't provide a thorough analysis of the μ = 0 case, which is a significant gap given the practical importance of sign-based measurements.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The precise error formula in Theorem 2.2 involves a convex program that requires evaluating F(c1, c2, c3), which is challenging to compute for complex structures like low-rank matrices. The paper doesn't discuss efficient numerical methods for computing this quantity in practice.

3. **Empirical Validation**: While Figures 1-2 show reasonable agreement for sparse recovery, the empirical validation is limited to a few specific cases. More comprehensive experiments would strengthen the claims, particularly for the low-rank case which is only discussed theoretically.

4. **Comparison with Specialized Methods**: The paper doesn't compare the LASSO approach with alternative methods specifically designed for non-linear measurements (e.g., the binary measurement methods mentioned in Section 1.1). A comparison would clarify the practical advantages of their approach.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of LASSO estimation under non-linear measurements. The precise equivalence result is novel and fills an important gap in the literature, extending previous work by Plan and Vershynin [17] from loose upper bounds to exact asymptotic characterization.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a substantial contribution to the field of high-dimensional structured signal recovery. While the μ = 0 case is a notable limitation, this doesn't undermine the core theoretical contribution, which is both rigorous and broadly applicable to the many important non-linear measurement models with μ ≠ 0.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and advances the state of the art in structured signal recovery. The limitations noted are important but do not detract from the core contribution.

## Pro/Con Summary

*Pro:*
- Precise error characterization extends previous work from loose bounds to exact asymptotic analysis
- General framework applicable to multiple structured recovery problems
- Practical implications for q-bit compressive sensing with actionable optimization criterion
- Rigorous theoretical analysis using state-of-the-art CGMT framework

*Con:*
- Restriction to μ ≠ 0 case (though acknowledged by authors)
- Limited empirical validation for some cases (particularly low-rank)
- No comparison with specialized methods for non-linear measurements
- Computational complexity of error prediction not fully addressed

The paper represents a substantial advancement in the field that will be valuable to both theorists and practitioners working on structured signal recovery with non-linear measurements.
