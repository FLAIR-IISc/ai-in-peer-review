Summary
The paper studies generalized LASSO under non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian sensing matrix A. The main claim (Theorem 2.1) is that, asymptotically for m,n → ∞ with m/n → δ, the estimation error of the generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements matches exactly that of the same LASSO applied to an “equivalent” linear model with effective gain µ = E[γ g(γ)] and noise variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Theorem 2.2 provides a precise, convex, three-scalar optimization predicting the asymptotic squared error; specializations are given for sparse (Theorem 2.3), group-sparse, and sketched for low-rank (nuclear-norm) recovery. The paper also applies the equivalence to q-bit compressed sensing, arguing that optimizing quantizer thresholds and representation levels reduces to minimizing σ^2/µ^2 and proposing Lloyd–Max as a heuristic.

Relation to prior work: Brillinger (1982) established LS consistency up to a scale for fixed n, m→∞. Plan & Vershynin (2015) gave non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO with non-linear observations, revealing the same µ,σ dependence but with loose constants. Precise (asymptotically exact) LASSO error characterizations for linear models were developed via AMP/CGMT (Bayati–Montanari; Stojnic; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.). This paper claims to extend the precise characterization to non-linear measurements by reduction to an equivalent linear model.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and useful reduction: quantifies exactly how non-linearities affect LASSO through just two scalars (µ,σ^2) when A is Gaussian.
- Asymptotically exact, closed-form (optimization) prediction for the MSE covering general convex regularizers via a function F derived from the conjugate and Moreau envelope; specializations given for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2 norms.
- Extends Brillinger’s LS result to high-dimensional regime; improves Plan–Vershynin bounds to precise limits in the regularized setting.
- Practical implication: quantizer design for q-bit CS reduces to minimizing σ^2/µ^2; ties to Lloyd–Max are interesting.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical:
- Theorem 2.1 statement is imprecise: it reads as almost sure equality of the errors, whereas what is justified by the asymptotics is equality of the almost sure limits. Please clarify whether the claim is lim_n→∞ ||x̂−µx0||^2 = lim_n→∞ ||x̂_lin−µx0||^2 a.s., not equality of the finite-n quantities.
- Assumption 1 is opaque and appears ill-specified. The object 1/n e_{√n (f*)^(n), c3}(c1 h + c2 x0) is not clearly defined; the Moreau envelope e_{f,τ} was defined for a single function f, not for “√n (f*)^(n)” nor for a three-argument variant. Moreover, in the sparse example F(c1,c2,c3) does not depend on c3. This needs a careful, unambiguous definition, discussion of mildness, and verifiable conditions for common f and px0.
- Scaling/normalization choices are nonstandard and inconsistently motivated. The program (2) is called “`2-LASSO or square-root-LASSO,” but the square-root lasso is typically ||y−Ax||2/√m + λ||x||1; here it is ||y−Ax||2 (no square) + λ f(x) with a 1/√n factor in (9). Please reconcile terminology, justify the normalization, and keep consistent use of argmin vs min for solutions vs optimal values.
- The low-rank section stops short of giving a checkable F(c1,c2,τ): it assumes limiting singular-value distributions but “leaves the details and the problem of evaluating F for future work,” which weakens the completeness of this claimed generality.
- The quantizer design section asserts that Lloyd–Max converges to stationary points of σ^2/µ^2; the proof is deferred and not sketched. Given the practical importance, at least a summary argument or conditions is necessary.

Clarity:
- Numerous typos and formatting issues hinder readability: “mesaurements,” “gaussianity,” “eventhough,” “redifining,” “prism of linear measurements,” inconsistent use of ‖·‖2 vs ‖·‖22, footnotes embedded in running text, mismatched equation punctuation, and inconsistent use of argmin/min. Many long sentences would benefit from tightening.
- Figures 1–2 are referenced as central evidence but not included here; finite-sample behavior, parameter choices (λ tuning), and robustness are not described.
- Several notational ambiguities: random vs deterministic g (µ,σ defined “with respect to both γ and g”), the role of c3 in F, bold vs scalar norms, and the use of δ ∈ (1,∞) in one place vs δ ∈ (0,∞) elsewhere.

Originality:
- The “equivalence via µ,σ” is thematically known (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin), but establishing an asymptotically exact MSE characterization for regularized LASSO under general convex f appears novel and meaningful. However, some parts (e.g., sparse ℓ1 with Gaussian nonzeros) overlap with known precise analyses; the paper should be explicit about what is genuinely new versus previously covered.

Significance:
- If made rigorous and better presented, the result is impactful: it transfers a large body of precise LASSO theory from linear to non-linear regimes and guides quantizer design. That said, restricting to i.i.d. Gaussian A and µ≠0 limits scope; discussion of extensions to sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant ensembles would increase significance.

Detailed suggestions
- Restate Theorem 2.1 as equality of limits; specify the probability space and mode of convergence.
- Fix Assumption 1: unambiguously define the functional F via a proper epi-limit/LLN argument; explain why c3 is needed (or remove it) and provide sufficient conditions for common f (ℓ1, group ℓ1,2, nuclear).
- Provide a proof sketch for Theorem 2.2 (CGMT steps, reduction from non-linear to linear), and for the Lloyd–Max stationarity claim.
- Include finite-n experiments with multiple g (sign, ReLU, Tobit, quantizers), varying δ, λ, and mismatch in µ estimation; report confidence intervals and deviations from asymptotics.
- Clarify the treatment of µ unknown in practice; discuss estimating µ,σ from data and robustness to misspecification.
- Clean up terminology: square-root lasso vs least-squares LASSO; scaling by 1/√n; use argmin consistently.
- Improve the low-rank section with a concrete example (e.g., spiked model) and a practical recipe to compute F.
- Thoroughly edit references: complete bibliographic info, correct titles, years, and fix typos.

Pros
- Conceptually neat reduction; precise asymptotic characterization.
- Covers a broad class of convex regularizers; insightful sparse specialization.
- Practical quantizer-design implication.

Cons
- Technical assumptions/definitions unclear (Assumption 1); some theorem statements imprecise.
- Presentation has many typos and inconsistencies; missing figures and experimental details.
- Low-rank and quantizer sections underdeveloped; dependence on Gaussian A and µ≠0.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The core idea is strong and likely publishable after substantial revision for rigor and clarity, with strengthened technical statements, better-developed special cases, and solid empirical validation.

Confidence
High (familiar with CGMT/AMP literature and non-linear single-index models).
