Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under a single-index/nonlinear measurement model y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) with i.i.d. Gaussian sensing rows a_i, and proposes to use the generalized LASSO (l2-loss plus convex regularizer). The central result (Theorem 2.1) shows an asymptotic equivalence: for a broad class of link functions g, the mean-squared error of generalized LASSO with nonlinear observations is identical to that of the same estimator with an equivalent linear model y = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). Building on the CGMT framework, Theorem 2.2 gives a precise scalar optimization predicting the asymptotic error for general convex regularizers (via a function F encapsulating the regularizer and signal distribution). Specializations include sparse (Theorem 2.3), group-sparse, and a discussion for low-rank recovery. As an application, the paper studies q-bit quantized compressive sensing, showing that optimal quantizer design for LASSO can be reduced to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 and proposing Lloyd–Max as a practical method.

Relation to prior work
- Brillinger (80s) characterized LS with nonlinear link in fixed-d, m→∞, showing consistency up to a scaling μ; here this is extended to high-dimensional proportional regimes, and from LS to generalized LASSO.
- Plan & Vershynin provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO under nonlinear link; this work gives asymptotically exact risk for regularized LASSO and recovers their qualitative dependence on (μ,σ) precisely.
- Precise error analysis for LASSO with linear measurements via AMP/CGMT exists (Bayati–Montanari; Donoho–Maleki–Montanari; Stojnic; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.). This paper leverages CGMT to move from linear to nonlinear.
- The application to quantization connects to one-bit CS and quantizer design; mapping to linear equivalents aligns with the classical Bussgang decomposition intuition.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean and powerful “nonlinear = linear with effective (μ,σ)” equivalence for generalized LASSO under Gaussian designs, validating and sharpening folklore/Bussgang-type intuition.
- Technical depth: precise asymptotic error characterization via a low-dimensional convex program, applicable to a family of convex regularizers through F. The sparse specialization is clear and recovers known special cases while extending beyond them.
- Significance: Unifies and simplifies the analysis of mismatched LASSO under unknown links; offers practical guidance (e.g., λ tuning, quantizer design via minimizing σ^2/μ^2).
- Comparison to prior: goes beyond Plan–Vershynin’s bounds to exact asymptotics; extends Brillinger beyond LS; complements prior CGMT results by incorporating nonlinear links through (μ,σ).

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Scope of Assumption 1: The generality claim hinges on verifying the limit defining F. For l1 and group-lasso, this is explicit; for nuclear norm, the paper sketches conditions but leaves details and computation “for future work.” The manuscript would benefit from at least one fully worked low-rank example (with a concrete spectral distribution) to substantiate the general claim.
- Theorem 2.1 statement: It currently reads as equality of errors “with probability one in the limit,” which is ambiguous. Please restate as equality of the almost-sure limits of the errors, not equality of finite-n quantities.
- μ ≠ 0 requirement: The paper assumes μ and σ^2 are nonzero. Many practically relevant nonlinearities (e.g., odd symmetric links, sign) satisfy μ ≠ 0, but even links or some saturating nonlinearities can have μ = 0. It would be useful to more clearly delineate the boundary cases and discuss practical alternatives when μ = 0 (you briefly mention special methodologies; pointing to concrete strategies or limits of LASSO in that case would help).
- Monotonicity in σ^2/μ^2: The quantizer design section argues that minimizing σ^2/μ^2 minimizes the LASSO error after μ-rescaling. While this is true for many convex-regularized linear models (LASSO risk is nondecreasing in effective noise variance), making this monotonicity explicit from Theorem 2.2 (or adding a proposition/lemma under mild conditions) would strengthen the design claim.
- Experimental validation: The text refers to Figures 1–2 validating the theory. It would be important to show a broader set of experiments: multiple link functions (e.g., sign with/without dithering, ReLU, logistic/probit), different regularizers, and sensitivity to λ. For the quantization section, include empirical results comparing LM-derived thresholds versus standard uniform quantizers and report corresponding risk and σ^2/μ^2.
- Related work: Please discuss generalized AMP for nonlinear output channels (e.g., GAMP) and its relation to your results. GAMP provides performance predictions under mismatched priors/links; contrasting your LASSO-based, link-agnostic approach with GAMP’s model-based approach would position the contribution more clearly.
- Notational/typo issues: A number of minor typos/grammar and footnote references are confusing (e.g., “do not require μ = 0” likely means “do not require μ ≠ 0”). Equation numbering and the role of β in (10) could use more intuition. Clarify normalization of A (variance-1 entries) and scaling in (2) vs (9).

Quality
- The theory appears sound and consistent with known counterparts in the linear case; the derivations are deferred to the appendix but the main statements are plausible and well-motivated. The sparse specialization is carefully developed. More complete treatment of the low-rank case and empirical validation would further solidify the contribution.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable for an expert audience, but the paper relies on a dense assumption (Assumption 1) and advanced tools (CGMT). Adding intuition for the derivation of (10), a brief sketch of the CGMT mapping, and more guidance on verifying Assumption 1 would improve accessibility.

Originality
- High. The asymptotically exact equivalence to a linear model for generalized LASSO under nonlinear links is, to my knowledge, new, and the precise error characterization generalizes several prior results.

Significance
- High. The results will be used by researchers analyzing mismatched estimators, and practitioners dealing with quantization, saturation, and unknown links in high dimensions. The mapping reduces nonlinear problems to well-understood linear ones, enabling immediate transfer of existing LASSO theory.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and impactful equivalence result; precise risk formulas.
- Unifies and sharpens previous bounds; broad applicability to convex regularizers.
- Practical design insight for quantization; clear path to λ tuning.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on i.i.d. Gaussian designs; robustness to sub-Gaussian or structured matrices is not discussed.
- Assumption 1 limits practical generality unless more examples are worked out; low-rank case incomplete.
- Experimental section appears limited; more comprehensive validation desirable.

Questions for the authors
- Can your equivalence extend to sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant designs? What fails without exact Gaussianity?
- Is there a simple sufficient condition ensuring the uniqueness of α* in (10)?
- Can you provide at least one concrete low-rank example with a tractable F and numerical evaluation?
- Can you formalize the monotonic dependence of the risk on σ^2/μ^2 to justify the quantizer design objective?

Overall recommendation
- Accept (leaning). The paper makes a clear, technically nontrivial, and broadly useful contribution. Strengthening clarity around Assumption 1, expanding experiments, and minor fixes would further improve the paper.
