## NIPS Review: LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements

**Summary:**

This paper establishes a surprising and powerful connection between the Generalized LASSO applied to non-linear measurements and its performance relative to a corresponding linear measurement model. The authors demonstrate that, under Gaussian measurements and a unit-norm signal assumption, the error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of the LASSO applied to scaled and noisy linear measurements. This equivalence is quantified by two parameters, µ and σ², characterizing the link function.  The authors build upon prior work on constrained LASSO (Plan & Vershynin, 2015) and precise LASSO error analysis (e.g., Stojnic, 2013; Thrampoulidis et al., 2013, 2016) to derive precise error formulas for non-linear measurements, generalizing Brillinger’s earlier work (1980s). They also showcase specific applications to sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, and q-bit compressive sensing, demonstrating the practical implications of their theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Theoretical Insight:** The central result – the equivalence of non-linear and scaled linear measurements for LASSO – is a significant theoretical advance. It provides a new lens through which to analyze estimation problems with non-linearities, leveraging the well-developed theory for linear models.
* **Generality:** The results are remarkably general, holding for a wide class of link functions *g* and convex regularizers *f*, and for various signal structures.  The asymptotic regime considered is also broad (m/n -> δ ∈ (0, ∞)).
* **Precision:** The paper goes beyond loose bounds to provide precise error formulas, including a characterization of the error via a convex optimization problem. This level of precision is crucial for understanding the interplay between the link function, the regularizer, and the measurement regime.
* **Practical Applications:** The demonstrated applications to sparse recovery, group sparsity, and q-bit compressive sensing highlight the practical relevance of the work. The application to q-bit sensing is particularly compelling, offering a framework for optimizing quantization levels.
* **Strong Connections to Existing Literature:** The paper is well-grounded in the existing literature, properly citing and building upon relevant work in statistics, signal processing, and machine learning.
* **Clear Presentation (Generally):** The introduction clearly motivates the problem and summarizes the contributions. The theoretical results are presented rigorously.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Unit Norm Assumption:**  The assumption of a unit-norm signal (x0) simplifies the analysis but limits the direct applicability of the results. While the authors acknowledge this and mention how to generalize (Footnote 4, Section 1.1), a more detailed discussion of the impact of this assumption and a more complete generalization would strengthen the paper.
* **Complexity of Theorem 2.2:** While powerful, Theorem 2.2 and the associated optimization problem (10) could be intimidating for readers unfamiliar with convex optimization. Providing more intuition about the terms in the optimization problem and potentially simplifying it for specific cases would enhance accessibility.
* **Lloyd-Max Algorithm Application:** The discussion of the Lloyd-Max algorithm in Section 2.3.3 feels somewhat tacked on. While the connection is interesting, the justification for its use and the detailed analysis of its convergence properties could be expanded.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the paper emphasizes theoretical results, some additional empirical validation beyond Figures 1 and 2, perhaps demonstrating the optimized q-bit quantization levels in a realistic scenario, could bolster the impact of the work.
* **Notational Density:** The paper is dense with notation, which, while mathematically precise, can hinder readability.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Excellent. The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by rigorous analysis, and the authors demonstrate careful consideration of the limitations of their results.
* **Clarity:** Good. The paper is generally well-written, but the complexity of some of the results and the density of the notation make it challenging at times.
* **Originality:** Excellent. The core result is highly original and provides a novel perspective on a classic problem.
* **Significance:** Excellent. The results are likely to have a significant impact on the field, providing a theoretical foundation for analyzing estimation problems with non-linear measurements and guiding the design of algorithms for these problems.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance with broad implications.
* The results are rigorously derived and supported by strong theoretical analysis.
* The work addresses a challenging problem in a novel and impactful way.
* The applications to sparse recovery, q-bit sensing, and group sparsity demonstrate the practical relevance of the findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The unit norm assumption is a limitation.
* The complexity of some of the theoretical results might hinder accessibility.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** This is a highly original and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the theory of sparse recovery and estimation with non-linear measurements. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh its limitations.  I believe it is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.  I would encourage the authors to consider addressing the weaknesses related to the unit norm assumption and the complexity of Theorem 2.2 in a revised version.
