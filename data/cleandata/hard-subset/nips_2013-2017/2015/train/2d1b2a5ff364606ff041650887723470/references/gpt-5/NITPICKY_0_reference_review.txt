Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under a single-index (nonlinear measurement) model yi = gi(a_i^T x0), with A Gaussian and x0 structured. It analyzes the generalized square-root LASSO (a.k.a. ℓ2-LASSO): minimize ‖y − Ax‖2 + λ f(x) for convex f encoding structure. The main claim (Theorem 2.1) is an asymptotic “Non-linear = Linear” equivalence: for Gaussian A and broad f, the error of the LASSO with nonlinear measurements behaves exactly as if the data were linear with an effective gain µ = E[γ g(γ)] and noise variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2], γ∼N(0,1). Theorem 2.2 provides a precise scalar optimization formula for the asymptotic squared error, which depends on δ = m/n, λ, the link via µ,σ^2, and the pair (f, pX0) through a functional F derived from the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate f*. The paper specializes this to sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and sketches low-rank (nuclear norm) cases. An application to q-bit quantized sensing is given: minimizing the LASSO error reduces to minimizing σ^2/µ^2 over quantizer thresholds/levels, and the Lloyd–Max iterations are argued to produce stationary points of this objective.

Relation to prior work: extends Brillinger’s classical fixed-n LS analysis to high-dimensional LASSO; strengthens Plan–Vershynin’s non-asymptotic bounds for constrained LASSO to precise asymptotics for regularized LASSO; builds on precise LASSO analyses via AMP/CGMT for linear models.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and impactful equivalence: reducing nonlinear single-index observations to an “effective” linear model characterized by µ, σ^2, with no knowledge of g required by the estimator.
- Precise asymptotic error characterization via a low-dimensional convex program; handles general convex regularizers (beyond ℓ1) through F, and works for all λ.
- Concrete specialization to sparse and group-sparse settings; recovers and generalizes known AMP/CGMT results for linear models; identifies regime changes via λcrit in the compressed setting.
- Practical implication for quantized sensing: the design criterion σ^2/µ^2 is simple and insightful; connecting Lloyd–Max to this criterion is a nice observation.

Weaknesses and detailed issues
Technical correctness/clarity
- Theorem 2.1 statement is imprecise and arguably incorrect as written: “in the limit of n→∞, with probability one, ‖x̂ − µx0‖2 = ‖x̂lin − µx0‖2.” This looks like asserting equality of random limits rather than stating that the two errors converge to the same deterministic limit or that their difference tends to zero. Please restate as limn→∞ (‖x̂ − µx0‖2 − ‖x̂lin − µx0‖2) = 0 w.p.1, or that both converge to the same α*.
- Assumption 1 is opaque and has notational glitches. You define F(c1,c2,c3) as the pointwise limit of (1/n) e_{√n (f*)^{(n)}, c3}(c1 h + c2 x0). Later, in the sparse example, c3 disappears and F(c1,c2,c3) reduces to (1/2) E[η^2(c1 h + c2 X0; 1)], with no explanation of the fate of c3 and the √n scaling. Equation (12) seems to silently set c3=1 via the conjugate of ℓ1 (indicator of ℓ∞-ball), but the scaling path (√n times f*, envelope index c3, etc.) needs to be explicitly derived and consistent. The notation “n e√n(f∗)(n),c3(·)” is garbled.
- Program (10): the convexity and uniqueness of α* are asserted but not justified in the main text. Some terms have unusual dimensions (e.g., µ^2 τ^2/α) and there is a mix of max/min over (β,τ,α) with F evaluated at (β/λ, µτ/(λα), τ/(λα)). Please check signs, convexity in α, and consistency of arguments of F with Assumption 1. A compact derivation sketch would help.
- Equation (9) scales the objective by 1/√n. This is consistent with square-root LASSO, but earlier (2) uses ‖y − Ax‖2 + λ f(x) without the 1/√n; ensure scaling consistency across statements and with the limiting program (10). The terminology is occasionally inconsistent (“ℓ2-LASSO” vs “square-root-LASSO”).
- µ and σ^2: you assume both nonzero and bounded; earlier you discuss µ=0 cases only briefly. Clarify what happens when µ=0 (even g), and whether your equivalence reduces to a trivial model with zero gain. When σ^2 depends on g randomness, the assumptions should include finite fourth moments for CGMT + LLN steps.
- Low-rank section is incomplete: you posit conditions under which the empirical singular value distribution of c1 H + c2 X0 converges and then defer evaluation of F. As written, there is no precise error prediction for this important case.
- Group-sparse section: “it is not hard to show” should be accompanied by a statement/proof (even in the appendix) mapping Assumption 1 to F with vector soft-thresholding, including the block normalization constants.
- Quantization section: while the reduction to minimizing σ^2/µ^2 is neat, the assertion that Lloyd–Max converges to stationary points of this ratio requires a proof; referring to Appendix C is fine, but the main text should clarify the objective mismatch (MSE quantizer vs σ^2/µ^2) and any conditions (e.g., symmetry).
- Some references to figures (Fig. 1–2) are critical to validate accuracy but are not described in detail (no experimental settings, SNR, distributions, λ selection). Reproducibility is limited.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous typographical and grammatical errors: “mesaurements,” “eventhough,” “redifining,” inconsistent capitalization (“gaussian”), missing or extra spaces and punctuation. Footnotes interwoven into the main text break flow and are mislabeled. Please proofread carefully.
- Notation inconsistencies: In Sec. 2.1, “We let x0 ∈ Rn… We assume that x0 = x0/‖x0‖2, with x0 sampled from px0.” This shadows x0 by x0 and is confusing. Use distinct symbols (e.g., draw x̃0 ∼ px0, then set x0 = x̃0/‖x̃0‖2).
- Several equations have minor issues: (5) should specify convergence (in probability or almost surely). In (7), asymptotic notation “.” and “&” are used without explicitly stating constants. In (8) you claim an exact bound; specify assumptions and whether it follows from your Theorem 2.2 or additional lemmas.
- References are incomplete (missing years, venues, page numbers) and inconsistent in style. Some crucial prior works on one-bit compressed sensing (e.g., Plan & Vershynin 2013/2014 papers) and generalized linear models with unknown link in high dimensions could be better situated.

Significance and originality
The central equivalence and precise asymptotics for regularized LASSO with nonlinear single-index measurements are important and, to my knowledge, novel in this generality. The work leverages modern CGMT/AMP theory and unifies several strands (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin; precise LASSO). If presented cleanly with complete proofs and polished exposition, this would likely be impactful.

Recommendation: Reject (encourage resubmission)
While the core idea and results are strong, the paper in its current form suffers from numerous clarity/notation/formatting problems, an imprecise statement of the main equivalence (Theorem 2.1), an opaque key assumption (Assumption 1) with inconsistent instantiations, and incomplete treatment for low-rank and experimental validation. These are not mere cosmetic issues; they hinder verification and adoption. A thorough rewrite, tightening of statements, complete and consistent notation, rigorous connection between Assumption 1 and concrete F for major regularizers, and stronger empirical demonstrations would substantially strengthen the submission.

Questions for the authors
- Restate Theorem 2.1 precisely (limit sense) and quantify the rate at which the difference vanishes, if possible.
- Provide a clear, self-contained derivation of Assumption 1 for ℓ1, ℓ1,2, and nuclear norm, including the role of c3 and scaling.
- Clarify convexity/uniqueness in (10); provide a short proof sketch.
- Extend or concretize the low-rank case with a computable F under standard random matrix assumptions.
- Discuss robustness to non-Gaussian A (sub-Gaussian, rotationally invariant) and to µ≈0.
- Add experimental details and code to reproduce figures; include diverse g beyond quantization (e.g., ReLU, saturating, sign with noise).
