

# Peer Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary and Context
This paper addresses a fundamental question in signal processing: how does the Generalized LASSO behave when applied to non-linear measurement models of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0)? The authors establish a remarkable equivalence showing that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime (m,n → ∞ with m/n = δ), the LASSO solution with non-linear measurements behaves exactly as if it were applied to a scaled and noisy linear measurement model with parameters μ and σ² defined by the link function g. This generalizes earlier results by Brillinger [5] and Plan and Vershynin [17], providing a precise characterization rather than loose upper bounds.

The paper makes several key contributions: (1) a general equivalence theorem (Theorem 2.1) showing non-linear and linear measurement regimes yield identical asymptotic errors; (2) a precise error formula (Theorem 2.2) that explicitly captures the role of the link function, signal structure, and regularizer; and (3) specialized results for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery, with an application to q-bit compressive sensing.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Depth and Precision**: The paper provides a significant advancement over previous work. While Plan and Vershynin [17] established non-asymptotic upper bounds on error, this work delivers a precise asymptotic characterization using the CGMT framework. The equivalence result (Theorem 2.1) is elegant and general, showing that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements with known noise parameters.

2. **Comprehensive Special Cases**: The paper demonstrates the applicability of their framework to multiple structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) with novel error formulas. The sparse recovery result (Theorem 2.3) generalizes prior work by Oymak et al. [16] and Thrampoulidis et al. [20] to non-Gaussian signal distributions.

3. **Practical Application**: The q-bit compressive sensing application (Section 2.3) provides a concrete example where the theory directly informs design choices. The authors show how to optimize quantization thresholds by minimizing σ²/μ², demonstrating the practical value of their theoretical results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Strong Assumptions Limiting Applicability**: The paper assumes i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, unit-norm signals, and i.i.d. link functions across measurements. These assumptions exclude important cases (e.g., deterministic measurement matrices, signals with non-unit norm, or structured non-linearities). While the authors acknowledge these limitations, they don't provide clear pathways to relax them.

2. **Lack of Comparison with Alternative Approaches**: The paper doesn't discuss how their results compare with recent work on non-linear measurement systems, such as the "non-linear LASSO" approach by Yi et al. [24] for binary measurements. This is a significant gap given the paper's focus on non-linear measurements.

3. **Computational Complexity Not Addressed**: While Theorem 2.2 provides a precise error formula, it's a three-dimensional convex optimization problem. The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of solving this optimization or how it compares to the original LASSO problem. For practitioners, this could be a significant barrier.

4. **Missing Discussion of Unknown Link Functions**: The paper states that the link function g might be unknown, but doesn't address how their framework applies in this case. Since the key parameters μ and σ² depend on g, the authors should clarify how these would be estimated when g is unknown.

## Critical Evaluation

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of the Generalized LASSO in non-linear measurement settings. The core insight—that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements with known noise parameters—is elegant and powerful, with clear implications for both theory and practice.

However, the paper's impact is somewhat limited by its restrictive assumptions. While the Gaussian measurement assumption is common in high-dimensional analysis, the unit-norm signal constraint (Footnote 4) is particularly restrictive and excludes important cases like the sign-link function where the norm is inherently ambiguous. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide a clear way to extend their framework to handle non-unit-norm signals.

The comparison with Plan and Vershynin [17] is well-handled, but the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their precise error formula improves upon the upper bounds in [17]. While they note that their result in (8) improves upon (7), they don't explicitly quantify the improvement.

The q-bit compressive sensing application is compelling, but the paper doesn't address the practical implementation challenges of estimating μ and σ² from data when g is unknown. This is a critical gap for real-world applications.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. The results are technically sound, well-justified, and extend the recent body of work on precise analysis of the LASSO. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a strong case for the equivalence between non-linear and appropriately scaled linear measurements.

While the assumptions limit the immediate applicability of the results, the framework they establish provides a powerful new lens through which to analyze non-linear measurement systems. The precise error formula and its application to q-bit compressive sensing are particularly valuable contributions.

**Recommendation**: Accept. This paper represents a significant theoretical advance with clear implications for both the theory and practice of compressed sensing and structured signal recovery. The results are novel, well-supported, and provide a new perspective on a classic problem in signal processing. The minor limitations noted above are common in high-dimensional asymptotic analysis and do not detract from the paper's core contribution.

The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how their framework might be extended to handle unknown link functions, but this is a minor point given the paper's focus on the asymptotic regime where such extensions would be challenging.
