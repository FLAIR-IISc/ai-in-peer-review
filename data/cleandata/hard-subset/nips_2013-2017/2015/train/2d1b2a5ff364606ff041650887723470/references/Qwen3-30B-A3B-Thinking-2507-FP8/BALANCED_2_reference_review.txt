

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary
This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to non-linear measurement models of the form y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0). The authors establish a precise asymptotic characterization of the estimation error, showing that under certain conditions, the LASSO with non-linear measurements behaves asymptotically like the LASSO with linear measurements that have been scaled and corrupted by noise. This extends previous work by Plan and Vershynin (2015) who provided only upper bounds for the constrained LASSO. The paper makes significant contributions by generalizing to regularized LASSO (not just constrained), providing precise error expressions rather than loose upper bounds, and applying the theory to multiple structured signal recovery scenarios (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank).

## Strengths
1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper's main result (Theorem 2.1) is elegant and general, showing that the error of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that with appropriately scaled linear measurements. This provides a powerful theoretical tool for analyzing non-linear measurement systems.

2. **Precision**: The paper significantly advances the field by moving from loose upper bounds (as in Plan and Vershynin 2015) to precise asymptotic error expressions. Theorem 2.2 provides a convex optimization program that exactly characterizes the asymptotic error, which is a substantial theoretical advance.

3. **Broad Applicability**: The framework applies to multiple signal structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) through the choice of regularizer, with specific results for each case (Theorems 2.3-2.4). This demonstrates the generality of the approach.

4. **Practical Application**: The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) is well-motivated and shows how the theoretical results can guide practical design (e.g., optimal quantization thresholds), making the theoretical contribution directly applicable.

## Weaknesses
1. **Strong Assumptions**: The results rely on the measurement matrix having i.i.d. Gaussian entries and the asymptotic regime (n → ∞). While common in high-dimensional statistics, these assumptions limit the immediate applicability to practical settings where measurements may not be Gaussian and problem sizes may be moderate.

2. **Limited Numerical Validation**: The paper includes Figures 1-2, but they cover only specific cases (Gaussian and Bernoulli signals for sparse recovery). More comprehensive numerical experiments across diverse link functions, signal structures, and measurement regimes would strengthen the claims.

3. **Handling of Special Cases**: The paper assumes x_0 has unit norm, which excludes some important cases (e.g., even link functions like g(x) = sign(x) where the norm information is lost). While the paper mentions this limitation, a more thorough discussion of how to handle such cases would be valuable.

4. **Connection to Practical Implementation**: The paper doesn't discuss how the precise error characterization can be used for practical parameter tuning in non-asymptotic settings, which would be helpful for practitioners.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs (deferred to appendix) and carefully considered assumptions. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the mathematical analysis. The paper demonstrates honesty in acknowledging limitations (e.g., the unit norm assumption, asymptotic regime).

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a good balance between technical detail and intuitive explanations. The main contributions are well-summarized in Section 1.1. However, the dense mathematical notation in Theorems 2.1-2.2 may pose challenges for readers unfamiliar with convex optimization and asymptotic analysis.

## Originality
This work significantly extends Plan and Vershynin (2015), which provided only upper bounds for the constrained LASSO. The precision of the error characterization and the extension to the regularized LASSO with a wide range of regularizers (beyond L1) represent substantial novelty. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first paper to derive asymptotically precise results for LASSO-type programs under non-linear measurements.

## Significance
The results provide a unifying theoretical framework for analyzing LASSO performance with non-linear measurements, which is common in many applications (quantized measurements, 1-bit sensing, etc.). The equivalence result simplifies the analysis of non-linear measurement systems by reducing them to linear ones with scaled noise. The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates practical significance.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of LASSO performance in non-linear settings. While the assumptions limit immediate practical applicability, the precise characterization provides a valuable foundation for future work. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the literature on high-dimensional structured signal recovery.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Include more diverse numerical examples to validate the theory across different link functions and signal structures.
2. Discuss how to handle cases where the unit norm assumption doesn't hold (e.g., even link functions).
3. Consider extending the analysis to non-Gaussian measurement matrices (e.g., sub-Gaussian), which would broaden the practical applicability.
4. Add a brief discussion of how the precise error characterization could inform parameter tuning in finite-sample settings.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the field. The results are novel, well-supported, and have clear significance for both theory and practice.
