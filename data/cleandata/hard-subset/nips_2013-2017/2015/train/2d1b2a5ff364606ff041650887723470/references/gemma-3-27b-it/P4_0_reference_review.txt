This paper investigates the performance of the LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, under certain conditions (Gaussian measurement matrices, unit norm signals), the non-linear measurement problem is asymptotically equivalent to a scaled and noisy linear measurement problem. This allows the authors to leverage existing precise analysis of the LASSO with linear measurements to characterize the performance of the LASSO in the non-linear setting. The paper provides precise error formulas and explores specific applications to sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, and quantized compressive sensing.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon recent advances in both the analysis of the LASSO and the study of single-index models. The core idea of relating non-linear measurements to scaled linear measurements is insightful and provides a powerful tool for analysis. The mathematical treatment appears rigorous, and the authors carefully state their assumptions. The extension of existing results on linear measurements to the non-linear case is a significant contribution. The detailed analysis of specific applications (sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery, as well as quantized sensing) adds practical relevance. The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm for optimal quantization is a nice touch, showing a clear application of the theoretical results. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. Firstly, the notation is dense and can be difficult to follow, particularly for readers not intimately familiar with the literature. While the authors attempt to define terms, the sheer number of symbols makes it challenging to grasp the overall flow. Secondly, the assumption of Gaussian measurement matrices and unit norm signals, while common, restricts the applicability of the results. A discussion of the robustness of the results to deviations from these assumptions would be beneficial. Thirdly, the presentation sometimes feels overly terse. For example, the derivation of the error formulas for specific applications could be more detailed, and the reasoning behind certain steps could be clarified. Although the authors cite relevant work, it’s occasionally difficult to place the contribution within the broader context of signal processing and statistics. Further clarifying how this work differs from and builds on existing results in those fields would enhance its impact. The paper also leans heavily on asymptotic analysis. While this allows for precise results, it would be valuable to discuss the finite-sample behavior of the proposed method, even if only qualitatively.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The equivalence result between non-linear and scaled linear measurements is a novel and significant contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical analysis appears rigorous, and the derivation of error formulas is well-motivated.
*   **Significance:** The results have the potential to advance our understanding of the LASSO in non-linear settings and to inform the design of practical algorithms for estimation and signal recovery.
*   **Clarity:** While dense, the paper is logically structured and presents a clear argument.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The notation is overly dense and can hinder comprehension.
*   **Scope:** The strong assumptions (Gaussian matrices, unit norm signals) limit the practical applicability of the results.
*   **Completeness:** A more detailed discussion of finite-sample behavior and a more thorough comparison with related work would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the theory of sparse recovery and estimation. The novelty of the equivalence result and the technical soundness of the analysis outweigh the weaknesses related to clarity and scope. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically focusing on improving the readability of the notation and providing a more detailed discussion of the assumptions and their impact on the results.
