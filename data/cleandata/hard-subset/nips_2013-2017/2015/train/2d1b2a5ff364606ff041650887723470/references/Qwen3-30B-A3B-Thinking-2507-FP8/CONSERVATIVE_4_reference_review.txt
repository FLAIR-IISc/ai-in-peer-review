

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper investigates the behavior of the Generalized LASSO when applied to non-linear measurement models of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0). The authors claim to show that, asymptotically, the Generalized LASSO with such non-linear measurements is equivalent to the LASSO with appropriately scaled and noisy linear measurements. The main contribution is a precise error prediction formula that depends on the link function g through parameters µ and σ², and that holds for a wide class of regularizers and signal structures.

The paper builds on previous work by Plan and Vershynin [17], who studied a constrained version of the LASSO with non-linear measurements. The authors extend this to the regularized Generalized LASSO and provide precise error predictions rather than the loose upper bounds in [17]. They also connect their results to the classical work of Brillinger [5] on least squares with non-linear measurements.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides precise error formulas for various signal structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank).
2. The paper clearly differentiates its results from previous work by providing asymptotically precise expressions rather than loose upper bounds.
3. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is novel and demonstrates a practical use case for the theoretical results.
4. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with careful attention to mathematical details.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The title is misleading. The "equivalence" is only asymptotic under specific conditions (Gaussian measurements, large n/m ratio), not a general equivalence. This could lead to misinterpretation by practitioners.

2. The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation. While Figures 1-2 show good agreement between theory and simulation for specific cases, there is no extensive comparison against established baselines or real-world datasets. The paper claims to provide "precise error predictions" but only validates these in limited synthetic settings.

3. The practical significance is not well-established. The asymptotic results require large n and m, which may not be realistic in many applications. The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on when this equivalence would be practically useful or how to estimate µ and σ² in real applications where the link function g is unknown.

4. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from Plan and Vershynin [17] beyond the precision of the error bounds. The constrained LASSO analysis in [17] already identified the role of µ and σ², and the authors' main contribution is to provide precise formulas rather than just upper bounds.

5. The paper makes the strong claim that "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements" without adequately qualifying the limitations of this equivalence. This is particularly problematic since the LASSO is often used in non-asymptotic settings with limited measurements.

**Comparison to previous work:**
The paper builds on Plan and Vershynin [17] and Brillinger [5], but the key novelty is the precise error characterization rather than the basic equivalence. Previous work on the LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 21]) has already established precise error formulas for linear cases, so the main contribution here is extending this to non-linear measurements through the asymptotic equivalence.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to understanding the behavior of the LASSO with non-linear measurements. However, the lack of extensive empirical validation against established baselines and the misleading title significantly weaken the paper. The asymptotic results, while theoretically sound, may not be immediately applicable to real-world problems where the asymptotic regime doesn't hold.

I would recommend rejection for the following reasons:
- The title and main claim are misleading without proper qualification of the asymptotic nature of the equivalence
- Insufficient empirical validation beyond a few specific synthetic cases
- Lack of clear practical guidance for when and how to apply these results
- The theoretical contribution, while sound, is incremental compared to existing work on LASSO with linear measurements

The paper would be significantly stronger with:
1. A more accurate title that specifies the asymptotic nature of the equivalence
2. More extensive empirical validation, including comparisons to established methods for non-linear measurement models
3. A clearer discussion of the practical limitations and when the asymptotic results would actually be applicable
4. Better differentiation from Plan and Vershynin [17] and other related work

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution is too incremental to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS, especially given the field's preference for methodological rigor with strong empirical validation. The theoretical results are sound but lack the practical impact needed for this venue.
