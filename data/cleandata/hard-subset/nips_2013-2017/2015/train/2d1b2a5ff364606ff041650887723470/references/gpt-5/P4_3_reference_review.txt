Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies generalized LASSO under single-index (non-linear) measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0), with A Gaussian and x0 structured. It establishes an asymptotic equivalence: in high dimensions, the estimation error of the generalized LASSO with non-linear observations matches that of the same LASSO run on an effective linear model with scaled signal and additive Gaussian noise, where the scale and noise variance are µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2] for γ ∼ N(0,1). Theorem 2.1 formalizes this “nonlinear = linear” equivalence. Theorem 2.2 provides a precise, asymptotically exact characterization of the squared error via a three-variable convex optimization whose data dependence enters through a limit functional F involving the Moreau envelope of the conjugate regularizer. The paper specializes these results to sparse (Theorem 2.3), group-sparse, and low-rank settings, deriving explicit characterizations (e.g., coupled equations for the sparse case) and discusses an application to q-bit compressive sensing, where choosing quantization thresholds/levels reduces to minimizing σ^2/µ^2. It also connects to design via Lloyd–Max, arguing its fixed points are stationary for the σ^2/µ^2 objective.

This extends: (i) Brillinger’s classical consistency of least squares under non-linear links (fixed n, m → ∞) by giving finite-δ asymptotics and moving beyond LS to regularized LASSO; (ii) Plan–Vershynin’s non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO under unknown links, by giving asymptotically exact error expressions for regularized LASSO; and (iii) recent precise analyses of LASSO with linear measurements (AMP/CGMT-based) by showing they transfer to the non-linear setting through µ, σ and by giving new precise results for general convex regularizers.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling equivalence principle: reduces non-linear measurement analysis to an effective linear model with explicit µ, σ; conceptually simple and practically actionable.
- Technically solid framework leveraging CGMT to obtain asymptotically exact errors for generalized LASSO across regularizers; specialization recovers or extends known linear-measurement results and yields new ones.
- Broad applicability across structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank), and across link functions, including quantization.
- Useful design implication for quantized sensing: performance ordering governed by σ^2/µ^2 and compatibility with Lloyd–Max design.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on an abstract Assumption 1 for the limit functional F; verification is demonstrated for ℓ1 and block norms but not fully worked out for nuclear norm (left to future work).
- Gaussian i.i.d. measurement assumption is restrictive; no sub-Gaussian or structured sensing extension is provided.
- Theorem 2.1’s statement could be tightened for clarity (equality of limits vs. almost-sure equality for finite n).
- Cases with µ = 0 (e.g., even link functions) are excluded; the practical issue of estimating µ, σ when g is unknown is not addressed empirically or theoretically.
- Experimental validation is described but appears limited in breadth (based on the text); finite-sample behavior and robustness to model misspecification are not discussed.
- References are incomplete/inconsistent in formatting.

Quality
- The results appear technically sound and grounded in established tools (CGMT). The sparse-case derivation (Theorem 2.3) yields explicit fixed-point equations akin to state evolution and aligns with prior precise analyses. The assumptions are stated (Gaussian A; µ, σ bounded; normalization of x0; regularizer growth bounds). The limiting optimization is convex with a unique solution, enabling unambiguous predictions.
- However, Assumption 1 is an important technical condition; for low-rank, the paper sketches rather than fully demonstrates the conditions under which F exists (e.g., spectral distribution convergence), and computational recipes for F are deferred. The application section argues Lloyd–Max optimality for σ^2/µ^2 via stationary conditions; providing a short proof sketch in the main text would increase confidence.

Clarity
- The narrative is generally clear, with a good contextualization relative to Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin, and explicit expressions for µ, σ. Notation is heavy and sometimes reused (e.g., x0 vs. its normalized version), and the role of Assumption 1 could be better motivated. Theorem 2.1’s phrasing (“with probability one, ‖x̂ − µx0‖^2 = ‖x̂_lin − µx0‖^2 in the limit”) should be clarified to mean equality of almost-sure limits. Several typos and reference formatting issues are present.

Originality
- High. To the best of current knowledge, this is the first asymptotically precise characterization of generalized LASSO error under broad non-linear single-index measurements, for general convex regularizers, and it cleanly unifies non-linear and linear analyses. The quantization design perspective via σ^2/µ^2 is also a neat implication.

Significance
- High theoretical significance: provides a reusable principle and concrete formulas that can guide both analysis and design (e.g., quantizer design, λ tuning) for a wide class of non-linear observation models. The results can be directly applied by replacing g with µ, σ in existing linear LASSO formulas. Practical significance is promising but would benefit from more empirical corroboration and guidance on estimating µ, σ when g is unknown.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify Assumption 1: provide sufficient conditions for F to exist for nuclear norm (e.g., random orthogonal factors with i.i.d. singular values) and a practical method to compute F numerically.
- State Theorem 2.1 precisely as equality of almost-sure limits, and specify whether the equivalence extends to optimal cost and other risk functionals.
- Discuss estimation of µ, σ from data when g is unknown, and sensitivity of the predictions to estimation error.
- Comment on extensions to sub-Gaussian A and structured sensing (e.g., partial Fourier).
- Expand experiments across different g (e.g., sign-with-noise, ReLU, Tobit) and report finite-sample deviations.
- Provide guidance for λ selection based on the derived formulas (e.g., characterizing λcrit in sparse case as a function of (δ, ρ, µ, σ)).

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution with clear, generalizable principle.
- Precise, asymptotically exact results; extends and unifies multiple lines of work.
- Useful specialization to important structures and to quantized sensing design.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on Gaussian A and an abstract assumption; partial treatment for low-rank.
- Limited empirical validation and no treatment of unknown g (µ, σ estimation).
- Minor clarity and presentation issues (notation, theorem statements, references).
