Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation in single-index models y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0), with Gaussian measurement matrix A and structured x0 estimated via the generalized LASSO: min_x ||y − A x||_2 + λ f(x). The central claim is an exact asymptotic equivalence: for a broad class of convex regularizers f and link functions g (with µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µ γ)^2]), the asymptotic mean-squared error of the LASSO under non-linear observations equals that under an appropriately scaled noisy linear model y_lin = µ A x0 + σ z. Theorem 2.2 then provides a precise characterization of the limit error as the solution of a three-variable convex optimization. The paper specializes the result to sparse (with explicit fixed-point characterization and a λcrit phase), group-sparse, and low-rank settings, and presents an application to q-bit quantized CS where the equivalence reduces quantizer design to minimizing σ^2/µ^2. The analysis is in the linear-asymptotic regime m/n → δ.

This work builds on: (i) Brillinger’s classical single-index result for least squares (fixed n, m→∞), here extended to high-dimensional regimes and to regularized estimators; (ii) Plan & Vershynin’s non-asymptotic bounds for constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements, here sharpened to precise asymptotics for the regularized LASSO; and (iii) precise LASSO analyses under linear measurements via AMP and, more generally, the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT). The paper cites and connects to Bayati–Montanari (AMP risk), Stojnic/Oymak/Thrampoulidis/Hassibi (CGMT-based upper bounds and precise characterizations), and related sparse/group-sparse/nuclear norm literature.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The equivalence principle is plausible under Gaussian designs due to rotational invariance and Stein-type identities, and CGMT is an appropriate tool for precise asymptotics. The general theorem is supported by a clear reduction (Theorem 2.1) and a scalar convex program (Theorem 2.2). Specializations to ℓ1 and group-ℓ1 yield concrete formulas via soft-thresholding operators. Some pieces (low-rank) are only sketched, with conditions for F(c1,c2,c3) left to future work.
- Rigor/assumptions: The Gaussian design and i.i.d. links are standard but restrictive. Assumption 1 (existence of a limiting functional F involving the Moreau envelope of f*) is reasonable for common f (ℓ1, ℓ1,2), but its verification and scope deserve more discussion, especially for nuclear norm. The requirement µ ≠ 0 is essential; the manuscript acknowledges cases where identifiability is only up to direction.
- Clarity of statements: Theorem 2.1 is phrased as “in the limit … ‖x̂ − µ x0‖2 = ‖x̂_lin − µ x0‖2” without explicit lim notation; this should be clarified as equality of almost-sure deterministic limits, not pathwise equality of random quantities. Notation around the Moreau envelope of f* (e√n(f∗)(n),c3) is hard to parse and could be cleaned up. A few typos and minor grammatical issues appear.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized: from model to main equivalence, to precise error characterization, to structured special cases and an application. However, some notational density (Assumption 1, definition of F, and the role of c3) and the ambiguous phrasing of Theorem 2.1 can be improved. The low-rank subsection is brief and would benefit from a concrete example or a numerical illustration. Figures are referenced but not visible in the provided text; assuming they corroborate the claims, they strengthen clarity.

Originality
To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact characterization of generalized LASSO performance under non-linear single-index observations, unifying disparate settings and elevating prior upper bounds (Plan–Vershynin) to precise limits. The reduction to a scaled noisy linear model with parameters (µ,σ^2) encapsulating g is elegant and novel in the LASSO context. The application to quantizer design via the σ^2/µ^2 criterion and connection to Lloyd–Max is also a nice twist.

Significance
The equivalence principle is conceptually impactful: it says that for Gaussian designs, non-linearities in the link function only matter through two scalar moments (µ,σ^2) for the purpose of LASSO error, enabling immediate transfer of the rich linear-measurement theory. This should influence both theoretical analyses and practical design (e.g., quantization, dithering). The precise formulas for sparse and group-sparse regimes (including the λcrit phenomenon) are useful. The low-rank case is promising but currently underdeveloped in the manuscript.

Strengths
- Clean, general equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement LASSO errors under Gaussian designs.
- Precise asymptotic characterization via a tractable scalar convex program; specializes to familiar soft-thresholding formulas.
- Extends and sharpens prior results (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin; CGMT-based analyses).
- Practical application to quantizer design; interpretable criterion (σ^2/µ^2).

Weaknesses
- Strong Gaussian-design assumption; no discussion of extensions to sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant ensembles.
- Assumption 1 is technical; verification for nuclear norm and other f is only sketched.
- The low-rank section lacks explicit formulas or experiments.
- Theorem 2.1 wording is ambiguous; some notation/typos reduce readability.
- No discussion of estimating µ,σ from data when g is unknown; scaling and calibration in practice could be addressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 2.1 as equality of almost-sure deterministic limits; explicitly state the convergence mode for both errors.
- Expand the discussion of Assumption 1: give a checklist for common f (ℓ1, ℓ1,2, nuclear norm) and x0 models, and a brief proof sketch for the nuclear-norm case or at least a concrete example with simulations.
- Add experiments for the low-rank setting to validate predictions.
- Discuss robustness to non-Gaussian A (e.g., sub-Gaussian, rotationally invariant), and whether the µ–σ reduction persists; include at least empirical evidence.
- Provide guidance on estimating µ,σ from data when g is unknown, and on practical scaling of x̂.
- Improve notation around the Moreau envelope and clean typos.

Pro and con arguments
Pros:
- Novel, unifying theoretical contribution with precise asymptotics.
- Broad applicability across structured settings.
- Conceptual simplicity (µ–σ reduction) with practical implications.
- Builds on and advances established NeurIPS-relevant lines (AMP/CGMT, structured recovery).

Cons:
- Restricted to Gaussian designs; unclear generality beyond.
- Technical assumption (Assumption 1) not fully unpacked for all regularizers.
- Low-rank case underdeveloped; some clarity issues in statements/notation.

Recommendation: Accept
This is a strong theoretical paper offering a clear and useful equivalence principle and precise error characterizations for LASSO under non-linear single-index measurements. While some clarity and scope extensions would further strengthen it, the contributions are substantial and of clear interest to the NeurIPS community. Confidence: high.
