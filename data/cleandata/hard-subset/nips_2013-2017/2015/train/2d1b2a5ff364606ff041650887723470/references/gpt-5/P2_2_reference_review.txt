Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under a non-linear single-index model y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian measurement vectors and structured x0 estimated via a generalized LASSO with convex regularizer f. The central claim is a precise equivalence: for Gaussian A, the asymptotic error of the generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements equals that under a linear model with an effective scaling µ = E[γ g(γ)] and additive Gaussian noise with variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2]. Theorem 2.1 formalizes this equivalence, and Theorem 2.2 provides an exact single-letter (scalar convex program) characterization of the asymptotic squared error, covering a wide class of convex f and random link functions g. The paper then specializes the formula to sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and sketches the low-rank (nuclear norm) case. An application to q-bit quantized compressed sensing is included, showing that minimizing σ^2/µ^2 yields optimal thresholds/levels and that Lloyd–Max fixed points are stationary for this objective.

This work generalizes and sharpens older results: Brillinger’s LS consistency for fixed n and m→∞, Plan & Vershynin’s non-asymptotic constrained LASSO bounds under non-linear links, and precise LASSO error analyses under linear models via AMP/CGMT (Bayati & Montanari; Stojnic; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.). To my knowledge, it is the first asymptotically exact characterization of generalized LASSO error with non-linear measurements and unknown link, for general convex regularizers.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The results are rigorous and consistent with the CGMT-based line of precise analyses. The equivalence via (µ, σ^2) is well-motivated by prior heuristic/upper-bound results and made exact here. The general result relies on a clear (though technical) Assumption 1 involving the limit of the Moreau envelope of f*; the paper verifies this for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2 and outlines nuclear norm conditions. The sparse specialization (Theorem 2.3) yields explicit equations with good empirical validation.
- Scope and assumptions: The Gaussian design assumption is central (as in CGMT/Gordon arguments). The link requires finite µ and σ^2 and µ ≠ 0; even g (e.g., sign without dither) is excluded for direction-only estimation unless one redefines the target. The analysis is asymptotic (m,n→∞, m/n→δ), with convincing simulations but no finite-sample guarantees.
- Evaluation: Simulations for sparse and group-sparse settings show close match to theory; the quantized CS application is compelling and connects theory to design (σ^2/µ^2). It would be helpful to include more low-rank experiments or a concrete evaluation of F for a standard low-rank ensemble.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear and well-structured, with a useful literature discussion and intuition. There are some typos and minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., “mesaurements”, occasionally ambiguous normalization choices). The statement of Theorem 2.1 could be clarified: it currently reads as an equality “with probability one, in the limit,” which might be misread as pathwise equality; please state explicitly that the two asymptotic limits of the errors coincide almost surely.
- Assumption 1 is technical; it would help to give a short table or paragraph listing common regularizers and signal priors for which F(c1,c2,c3) is explicit (ℓ1, ℓ1,2, nuclear), and one worked-out example for the nuclear norm to guide practitioners.
- The role of λ and λcrit is insightful; practical guidance on choosing λ (e.g., from the fixed-point equations) would strengthen the paper.

Originality
- High. The precise equivalence between non-linear and effective linear measurements for generalized LASSO, and the scalar convex formula for the error under non-linear links, are new. The extension beyond ℓ1 to general convex f is notable. The use of σ^2/µ^2 to guide quantizer design, with a link to Lloyd–Max, is also original and practically relevant.

Significance
- High. The results consolidate an important message for practitioners: for Gaussian designs, one can safely use the standard generalized LASSO under unknown link violations and interpret performance via an effective SNR characterized by (µ, σ^2). This applies to 1-bit/low-bit quantization, censoring, saturating sensors, etc. The precise formulas enable principled tuning and design (e.g., thresholds, λ), and will likely influence subsequent theoretical and applied work.

Pros
- Conceptually clean and strong main result (non-linear ≈ linear with (µ,σ^2)).
- Precise, asymptotically exact error characterization via a tractable scalar convex program.
- Covers general convex regularizers; explicit specialization for sparse and group-sparse.
- Practical application to quantizer design; clear design surrogate σ^2/µ^2 and LM connection.
- Careful contextualization with prior literature; simulations match theory.

Cons
- Restricted to Gaussian measurement matrices; discussion of extensions to sub-Gaussian/rotationally invariant designs would be valuable.
- Assumption 1 is abstract; more concrete verification for nuclear norm and other structured priors would improve accessibility.
- Excludes µ=0 links; brief discussion of alternative estimators or target (direction-only) would round out the scope.
- Asymptotic focus; limited finite-sample guidance and limited empirical coverage for low-rank.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 2.1’s statement (limits vs. pathwise equality) and explicitly state dependence on µ≠0.
- Add a subsection giving explicit F for nuclear norm under a standard low-rank model (e.g., random orthogonal factors with specified singular value distribution), plus a small simulation.
- Provide a short discussion and experiments on robustness to non-Gaussian designs (e.g., sub-Gaussian) if feasible, or state clearly why CGMT requires Gaussianity.
- Offer practical guidance for λ selection based on the fixed-point equations (e.g., how to estimate µ,σ from data or use cross-validation informed by theory).
- Proofread for typos and standardize terminology (“ℓ2-LASSO” vs. “square-root LASSO”).

Overall assessment
This is a technically solid, original, and significant contribution that advances our theoretical understanding of LASSO under link function misspecification and provides actionable insights for quantized/compressed sensing. Despite some limitations (Gaussian designs, technical assumptions), I believe it meets NeurIPS standards on quality, clarity (with minor edits), originality, and significance. I lean toward acceptance.
