

# Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary
This paper investigates the behavior of the Generalized LASSO when applied to non-linear measurement models of the form y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0), where g_i are non-linear link functions. The authors establish a precise equivalence between the LASSO with non-linear measurements and a linear measurement model with appropriately scaled and noisy measurements. Specifically, they prove that in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime (m, n → ∞ with m/n = δ fixed), the estimation error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements equals that of the LASSO with linear measurements y_lin = A(μx_0) + σz, where μ and σ² are defined in terms of the link function g. The paper then derives precise error formulas for various structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and applies the results to q-bit compressive sensing.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and makes a significant theoretical contribution. The authors build upon recent advances in the precise analysis of the LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., [2, 10, 16, 19, 20]) and extend them to the non-linear measurement setting. The main theoretical result (Theorem 2.1) is well-justified and proven using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT), a powerful framework for high-dimensional analysis. The assumptions are reasonable for the high-dimensional asymptotic regime they study, and the experimental results (Figures 1-2) validate the theoretical predictions. The paper carefully addresses the limitations of the asymptotic regime and connects their results to previous work, particularly the non-asymptotic bounds of Plan and Vershynin [17].

## Clarity
The paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly presents its contributions. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and motivation, and the theorems are precisely stated. The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) is particularly well-motivated and clearly explained. The notation is consistent throughout, though the technical details of the proofs (deferred to the appendix) might be challenging for some readers. The paper would benefit from slightly more intuitive explanations of the key concepts (e.g., the role of the function F) to aid readers less familiar with the CGMT framework.

## Originality
This paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by extending the precise analysis of the LASSO to the non-linear measurement setting. While Plan and Vershynin [17] provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements, this paper establishes a precise equivalence to a linear measurement model and derives precise error formulas that hold for all values of λ. The specific application to q-bit compressive sensing is also novel and demonstrates the practical utility of the theory. The paper clearly differentiates its contribution from prior work, particularly the earlier results of Brillinger [5] and Plan and Vershynin [17].

## Significance
The results have significant implications for practical applications where measurements are non-linear (e.g., quantized measurements, 1-bit sensing). The precise error prediction provides theoretical guarantees that can guide the choice of regularization parameters and measurement design. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is particularly noteworthy, as it shows how to optimally design quantization thresholds and levels based on the theoretical framework. The paper advances the state of the art by providing the first precise characterization of the LASSO for non-linear measurements, which was previously unknown.

## Strengths
- Precise theoretical contribution that extends recent advances on LASSO analysis to non-linear measurements
- Clear and rigorous proof of the equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models
- Broad applicability to various structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank)
- Practical application to q-bit compressive sensing with a concrete optimization algorithm
- Well-motivated and well-executed experimental validation

## Weaknesses
- The asymptotic regime (m, n → ∞ with m/n = δ fixed) limits direct applicability to finite-dimensional problems
- The technical presentation of Theorem 2.2 and its special cases is dense and might be challenging for some readers
- The paper doesn't thoroughly compare with other methods for handling non-linear measurements (e.g., [24] for binary measurements)
- The assumption that the measurement matrix has i.i.d. Gaussian entries is restrictive for some practical applications

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of the LASSO with non-linear measurements. The results are technically sound, well-justified, and have clear practical implications. While the asymptotic regime limits direct applicability to finite-dimensional problems, the precise error formulas provide valuable theoretical insights that can guide future work in this area. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a novel contribution that advances the state of the art.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It addresses an important problem, provides a significant theoretical advance, and has clear practical implications. The results are likely to be of interest to a broad audience in machine learning, statistics, and signal processing.

## Arguments for Acceptance:
1. First precise characterization of the LASSO for non-linear measurements, extending recent advances on linear measurements
2. Clear theoretical equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models
3. Precise error formulas for multiple structured signal recovery problems
4. Practical application to q-bit compressive sensing with a concrete optimization algorithm
5. Well-executed experimental validation of theoretical predictions

## Arguments against Acceptance:
1. Asymptotic regime limits direct applicability to finite-dimensional problems
2. Technical presentation might be challenging for some readers
3. Limited comparison with alternative methods for non-linear measurements

Overall, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and it makes a valuable contribution to the field.
