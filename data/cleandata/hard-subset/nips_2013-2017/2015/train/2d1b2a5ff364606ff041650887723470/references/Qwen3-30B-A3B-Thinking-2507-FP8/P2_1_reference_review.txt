

# Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary
This paper investigates the application of the Generalized LASSO to non-linear measurement models of the form $y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)$, where $g_i$ are independent copies of a link function $g$. The authors demonstrate that, under certain asymptotic conditions, the LASSO solution for non-linear measurements behaves identically to the LASSO solution for appropriately scaled linear measurements $y_i = \mu a_i^T x_0 + \sigma z_i$, where $\mu = \mathbb{E}[\gamma g(\gamma)]$ and $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[(g(\gamma)-\mu\gamma)^2]$ for $\gamma \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. This extends previous work by Plan and Vershynin (2015), which established a looser upper bound on the estimation error for constrained LASSO under non-linear measurements. The paper provides precise error predictions that apply to a wide range of structured signals (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and regularizers.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical proofs. The authors build upon recent advances in analyzing the LASSO with linear measurements using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework. The main results (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) are well-supported by mathematical derivations, and the authors carefully establish the conditions under which their results hold. The experimental validation in Figures 1-2 demonstrates good agreement between theoretical predictions and empirical results across different signal structures and link functions. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the requirement that $\|x_0\|_2 = 1$ (which excludes certain cases like even link functions), and discusses how to handle the more general case where $\|x_0\|_2 \neq 1$. The theoretical contribution is complete and not merely a position paper.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem and relates it to prior work. The formal setup (Section 2.1) precisely defines the assumptions and asymptotic regime. The main theorems are stated with sufficient detail, and the specialized cases (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank recovery) are presented in a logical progression. The paper avoids unnecessary jargon and provides clear explanations of technical concepts. The figures (particularly Figure 1) effectively illustrate the accuracy of the theoretical predictions. The only minor issue is that some readers unfamiliar with the CGMT framework might find the technical proofs challenging, but this is expected for a theoretical contribution at NIPS.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant original contribution by extending Plan and Vershynin's work to provide a precise characterization of LASSO performance with non-linear measurements. While Plan and Vershynin (2015) established a loose upper bound on the error, this paper provides a precise error formula that matches the error of the equivalent linear measurement model. The key insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled and noisy linear measurements (with scaling factor $\mu$ and noise variance $\sigma^2$) is novel and powerful. The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) demonstrates the practical significance of this theoretical result, showing how to optimize quantization thresholds and levels. The paper adequately references related work and clearly distinguishes its contribution from previous literature.

## Significance
This work has substantial significance for both theoretical and applied machine learning. The precise error formula provides practitioners with a theoretical foundation for using LASSO in non-linear measurement settings without needing to know the link function $g$. This is particularly valuable in applications where the measurement process is inherently non-linear (e.g., 1-bit quantization, censored data). The paper's results extend to various structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank), making it broadly applicable. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is especially compelling, as it demonstrates how the theory can guide the design of optimal quantization schemes. The paper advances the state of the art by moving from loose upper bounds to precise error predictions, which is a significant step forward in understanding LASSO performance under non-linear measurements.

## Strengths and Weaknesses
**Strengths:**
1. Theoretical contribution is significant, precise, and well-supported.
2. Clear connection between non-linear and linear measurement models.
3. Broad applicability across structured signal recovery problems.
4. Strong experimental validation of theoretical predictions.
5. Practical application to q-bit compressive sensing.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The assumption $\|x_0\|_2 = 1$ excludes some cases (e.g., even link functions), though the paper acknowledges this limitation.
2. The paper focuses on the asymptotic regime; it would be interesting to see how the results translate to finite-sample settings.
3. The technical depth of the proofs may be challenging for some readers, though this is expected for a theoretical contribution.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong, original contribution to the understanding of LASSO performance under non-linear measurements. The theoretical results are rigorous, the experimental validation is convincing, and the practical application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates real-world relevance. The paper advances the state of the art by providing precise error predictions where previous work only offered loose upper bounds. It is a high-quality contribution that would be of significant interest to the NIPS community.

I recommend **acceptance** for NIPS. The paper meets all the key criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission, with particular strengths in originality and significance. The theoretical contribution is substantial and well-executed, and the results have clear implications for both theoretical understanding and practical applications of the LASSO in non-linear measurement settings.
