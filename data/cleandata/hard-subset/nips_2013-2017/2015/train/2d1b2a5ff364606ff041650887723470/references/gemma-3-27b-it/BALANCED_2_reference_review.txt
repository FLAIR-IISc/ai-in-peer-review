This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements. The central result demonstrates that, under certain conditions, the non-linear measurement problem can be effectively treated as a scaled and noisy linear measurement problem, characterized by a proportionality constant µ and noise variance σ². The authors provide a precise characterization of the estimation error, extending recent advances in the analysis of LASSO with linear measurements, and illustrating its application to q-bit compressive sensing.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon a rich literature in sparse recovery, single-index models, and recent theoretical advances in LASSO analysis. The core idea – relating non-linear measurements back to an equivalent linear model – is conceptually appealing and potentially very useful.  The authors carefully outline the assumptions necessary for their results, and make connections to existing work in areas like Brillinger’s early work on least squares with non-linear measurements, and more recent constrained LASSO analysis by Plan and Vershynin. The detailed exploration of q-bit compressive sensing as a specific application showcases the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds upon solid theoretical foundations, clearly stating assumptions and providing rigorous proofs (deferred to the appendix).
* **Precise Error Characterization:**  The main result, providing a precise error formula for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, is a significant contribution.  This goes beyond previous upper bounds and offers a more accurate understanding of the algorithm's behavior.
* **Connection to Linear Measurements:**  The demonstration that non-linear measurements can be effectively reduced to a linear equivalent is a key insight, allowing the leverage of existing results from linear LASSO analysis.
* **Practical Application:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the relevance of the theoretical findings and offers a concrete example of how the results can be used.
* **Comprehensive Literature Review:** The paper includes a very thorough and well-organized literature review, placing the work within the broader context of related research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** The paper is quite mathematically dense, which may make it challenging for readers not already familiar with the relevant theoretical tools.  While the authors do a good job of outlining the assumptions, the proofs likely require significant expertise to fully appreciate.
* **Assumption 1:** Assumption 1, regarding the limit of a specific function related to the regularizer, feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks clear intuition. The authors mention it is met in many specific cases, but a more in-depth discussion of its implications and limitations would be helpful. It would be beneficial to provide examples where this assumption *doesn’t* hold and discuss the consequences.
* **Limited Numerical Validation:** While Figures 1 and 2 illustrate the accuracy of the theoretical predictions, the numerical validation is somewhat limited. Expanding the numerical experiments to explore a wider range of scenarios and parameters (e.g., different sparsity levels, different link functions) would strengthen the paper.
* **Lloyd-Max Algorithm Discussion:** The discussion of the Lloyd-Max algorithm for optimal quantization feels a bit tacked on.  While the connection is interesting, it could benefit from more detail, including a discussion of the algorithm's convergence properties and potential limitations in this specific context.  Is it guaranteed to find the global optimum?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound analysis of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements.
* The precise error characterization is a significant contribution to the field.
* The connection to equivalent linear measurements provides a valuable insight and simplifies the analysis.
* The paper is well-written (albeit dense) and includes a comprehensive literature review.
* The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the practical relevance of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical density may limit the paper’s accessibility to a broader audience.
* Assumption 1 requires further clarification and justification.
* The numerical validation is somewhat limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and technically rigorous paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of LASSO algorithms with non-linear measurements. The precise error characterization and the connection to equivalent linear measurements are particularly noteworthy. While the technical density and the need for further clarification of Assumption 1 are weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more intuitive explanation of Assumption 1 and discuss its limitations with concrete examples.
2.  Expand the numerical validation to explore a wider range of scenarios and parameters.
3.  Provide more detail regarding the convergence properties and limitations of the Lloyd-Max algorithm in the context of q-bit compressive sensing.
4. Consider adding a paragraph discussing the potential computational complexity of evaluating the error formula in practice.
