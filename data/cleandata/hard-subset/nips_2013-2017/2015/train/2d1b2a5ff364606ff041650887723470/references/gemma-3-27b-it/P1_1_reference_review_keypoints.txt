- Main result: Shows asymptotic equivalence between Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements and LASSO with appropriately scaled/noisy linear measurements under Gaussian sensing and unit-norm signal.
- Key parameters: Equivalence quantified by two link-function parameters µ and σ² that determine the effective linear model.
- Theoretical foundation: Builds on Plan & Vershynin and precise LASSO error analyses to provide exact asymptotic error formulas.
- Generality: Results hold for a wide class of link functions, convex regularizers, signal structures, and for m/n → δ ∈ (0,∞).
- Precision: Provides precise error characterization via a convex optimization formulation rather than loose bounds.
- Applications: Demonstrates practical implications for sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, and q-bit compressive sensing, including quantizer design.
- Literature connection: Well-grounded and properly cites prior work, generalizing earlier results like Brillinger’s.
- Presentation: Introduction and main contributions are clearly motivated and results are rigorous.
- Unit-norm limitation: Reliance on a unit-norm signal assumption simplifies analysis but limits direct applicability; generalization discussion is brief.
- Theorem complexity: Theorem 2.2 and optimization problem (10) are powerful but may be intimidating and need more intuition or simplification.
- Lloyd-Max section: Discussion of Lloyd-Max algorithm feels tacked on and lacks deeper justification and convergence analysis.
- Empirical evaluation: Limited experiments (only Figs.1–2); more empirical validation, especially for optimized q-bit quantization in realistic settings, would strengthen the paper.
- Notation density: Heavy notation reduces readability despite mathematical precision.
- Quality assessment: Technically sound, rigorous, and complete work.
- Clarity assessment: Generally well-written but complex results and dense notation make parts challenging.
- Originality assessment: Highly original core result offering a new perspective on non-linear measurements.
- Significance assessment: Likely high impact for theory and algorithm design in estimation with non-linear measurements.
- Arguments for acceptance: Significant theoretical advance, rigorous derivations, and relevant practical applications.
- Arguments against acceptance: Unit-norm assumption, theoretical complexity hindering accessibility, and limited empirical support.
- Recommendation: Strong Accept, with suggestions to address unit-norm generalization and improve accessibility of main theorem.
