## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant theoretical result demonstrating a surprising equivalence between the LASSO applied to non-linear measurements and a scaled, noisy linear measurement model. The analysis is rigorous, the results are presented clearly, and the implications for understanding the behavior of the LASSO in practical scenarios are considerable. While the theoretical nature might limit immediate practical impact, the paper’s contribution to foundational knowledge in high-dimensional statistics and signal processing is strong. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary of Contributions and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles a fundamental question: can we still trust the LASSO when applied to non-linear measurement models? Existing work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin demonstrated consistency results for least squares and constrained LASSO, respectively, but often relied on specific assumptions (e.g., large *m*, specific constraints). This work extends these results to the *generalized* LASSO, providing a *precise* characterization of the estimation error in a high-dimensional setting (*m/n* -> constant) for a broad class of non-linear link functions. 

The core contribution is showing that, asymptotically, the LASSO behaves as if the non-linear measurements were scaled and noisy linear measurements, with scaling factor *µ* and noise variance *σ²* defined by the link function *g*. This allows leveraging the well-developed theoretical understanding of the LASSO with linear measurements to analyze its performance in non-linear scenarios.  The authors build upon recent advances in precise error analysis of the LASSO (e.g., [2, 10, 16, 20]) and extend them to the non-linear case. The paper also provides a compelling connection to the single-index model and sufficient dimension reduction literature.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Result:** The equivalence theorem is a significant and surprising result. It provides a valuable theoretical foundation for using the LASSO in contexts with non-linear measurements.
* **Generality:** The results hold for a broad class of link functions *g*, including 1-bit quantization and censored Tobit models.  The assumption of Gaussian measurement vectors is relatively standard.
* **Precision:**  The paper derives *precise* asymptotic error expressions, going beyond previous upper bounds. This is crucial for understanding the behavior of the LASSO and optimizing its performance.
* **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The notation is clear, and the key ideas are presented in a straightforward manner.
* **Thorough Related Work Discussion:** The authors provide a comprehensive review of relevant literature, carefully positioning their contribution within the existing body of work. The comparison with Plan & Vershynin [17] is particularly insightful.
* **Illustrative Examples:** The inclusion of sparse recovery and low-rank recovery examples demonstrates the applicability of the results in important practical scenarios. The discussion of q-bit compressive sensing with the Lloyd-Max algorithm shows a potential for practical impact.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Asymptotic Setting:** The results are asymptotic, which limits their direct applicability in situations with small *n* or *m*. While this is typical for theoretical analysis, it would be valuable to discuss the practical implications of this limitation.
* **Unit Norm Assumption:** The assumption that *x₀* has unit Euclidean norm simplifies the analysis but might not hold in all applications. While the authors acknowledge this and mention a straightforward generalization, explicitly outlining the modifications needed for the general case would enhance the paper.
* **Complexity of F:**  Calculating *F* (defined in Assumption 1) can be challenging in practice for arbitrary regularizers.  The paper acknowledges this, but further discussion on approximations or practical methods for estimating *F* would be helpful.
* **Lloyd-Max Algorithm:** The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm in the q-bit compressive sensing example feels somewhat tacked on. While interesting, it could be further developed or presented as a future direction.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Assumption 1:** The statement of Assumption 1 and the justification for its plausibility are a bit dense.  Providing more concrete examples of regularizers that satisfy this assumption would be beneficial.
* **Theorem 2.3:**  Could the conditions for the sparse recovery case (Theorem 2.3) be related to sufficient conditions for the distribution of *X₀* to ensure that the empirical distribution converges to a limiting distribution as *d* -> infinity?
* **Computational Cost:** How does the computational complexity of calculating the error bounds scale with *n* and *m*?
* **Practical Implications:** Beyond the theoretical result, what are the immediate practical implications for choosing link functions or regularizers? Does the analysis suggest any guidelines for practitioners?




**5. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and significant theoretical result with broad implications.
* Rigorous analysis and clear presentation.
* Thorough discussion of related work.
* Strong connection to existing literature on LASSO and high-dimensional statistics.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Primarily theoretical; limited immediate practical application.
* Asymptotic setting may limit applicability in real-world scenarios.




**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and the analysis is rigorous. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above (specifically, discussing the limitations of the asymptotic setting and elaborating on the generalizability of the unit norm assumption) would further strengthen the paper.  The clarity and thoroughness of the presentation make it a valuable contribution to the field.
