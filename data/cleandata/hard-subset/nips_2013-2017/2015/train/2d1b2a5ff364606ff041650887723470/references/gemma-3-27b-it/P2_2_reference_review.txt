## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

**Summary:**

This paper establishes a surprising and powerful equivalence: the Generalized LASSO applied to measurements of the form `yi = gi(aTi x0)` behaves, asymptotically, identically to a Generalized LASSO applied to scaled and noisy *linear* measurements `yi = µaTi x0 + σzi`. This holds for a wide class of link functions `g`, structured signals `x0` with appropriate regularizers `f`, and in high-dimensional regimes. The key insight is that the non-linearity is effectively captured by scaling and adding noise, quantified by parameters µ and σ derived from the link function. The authors provide a precise error formula, generalizing existing results for linear measurements, and illustrate its accuracy through examples including sparse recovery and q-bit compressive sensing.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** This is a highly original result. Establishing this equivalence unlocks the possibility of applying the well-developed theory and algorithms for linear LASSO to a much broader class of non-linear measurement models. This significantly expands the applicability of the LASSO framework.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, building upon existing work on the LASSO and extending it to the non-linear setting. The authors carefully state their assumptions and derive a precise error formula.
* **Generality:** The result is surprisingly general, holding for a wide class of link functions `g` and regularizers `f`. The analysis doesn't require knowledge of `g`, making it applicable to scenarios where the measurement process is partially unknown.
* **Practical Implications:** The paper demonstrates a practical application to q-bit compressive sensing, showing how the derived results can be used to optimize quantization levels and thresholds.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions of notation and a logical flow of arguments. The connection to existing literature is well-established.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:**  The assumption of Gaussian measurement vectors `A` and the unit norm constraint on `x0` simplify the analysis. While the authors acknowledge the potential to relax the norm constraint, the impact of non-Gaussian `A` isn’t fully discussed. It would be helpful to comment on the robustness of the results to deviations from these assumptions.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors provide a precise error formula, explicitly computing it (especially for complex regularizers) could be challenging.  The discussion of the Lloyd-Max algorithm is promising, but a more detailed analysis of its computational cost and convergence properties would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the figures show good agreement with the theoretical predictions, the empirical evaluation is somewhat limited.  More extensive simulations with a variety of link functions, signal structures, and noise levels would strengthen the claims.
* **Assumption 1:** The detailed requirement of Assumption 1 (regarding the limit of a specific expression) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more intuitive explanation of why this condition is necessary would be helpful.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper presents a technically sound and significant result. The analysis is rigorous, and the claims are well-supported. The assumption-heavy nature prevents a perfect score.
* **Clarity (8/10):** The paper is generally clear, but some sections (particularly concerning Assumption 1) could benefit from further clarification. 
* **Originality (10/10):** The result is highly original and represents a significant advance in the field.
* **Significance (9/10):** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in signal processing and machine learning, and the results are likely to be influential and have practical applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and mathematically rigorous result with significant theoretical and practical implications.
* The generality of the result is impressive, extending the applicability of the LASSO framework to a wider range of measurement models.
* The connection to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the potential for real-world impact.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on specific assumptions (Gaussian `A`, unit norm `x0`) might limit the practical applicability of the results.
* The computational complexity of evaluating the error formula and the need for further analysis of the Lloyd-Max algorithm.
* Relatively limited empirical evaluation.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept**. This is a highly valuable contribution to the field and deserves to be published at NIPS. The technical rigor, originality, and potential impact of the work are exceptional. While the authors could further strengthen the paper by addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above, the core result is compelling and represents a significant advance in our understanding of the LASSO and its applications to non-linear measurement models. I believe it will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
