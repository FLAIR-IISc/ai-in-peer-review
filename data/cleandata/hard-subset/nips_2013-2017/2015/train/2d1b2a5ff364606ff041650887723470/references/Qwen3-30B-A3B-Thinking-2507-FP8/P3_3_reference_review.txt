

# Peer Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a precise asymptotic equivalence between LASSO with non-linear measurements (y_i = g(a_i^T x_0)) and LASSO with appropriately scaled linear measurements (y_i = μa_i^T x_0 + σz_i). The authors build upon the seminal work of Brillinger (1980s) on least squares with non-linear measurements and the recent constrained LASSO analysis by Plan and Vershynin (2015). While Plan and Vershynin provided loose upper bounds, this paper delivers precise error predictions for the Generalized LASSO (including regularization) using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework.

The key insight is that the non-linear link function g can be fully characterized by two parameters μ and σ^2 (defined in terms of g and a standard Gaussian), which transform the non-linear problem into an equivalent linear one. The paper demonstrates this through rigorous asymptotic analysis (n, m → ∞ with m/n = δ fixed) and provides explicit error formulas for various structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper's main result (Theorem 2.1) is elegant and profound. It establishes a precise equivalence that has been missing in the literature, reducing the analysis of non-linear measurements to the well-studied linear case. This is a substantial theoretical advance over Plan and Vershynin's non-asymptotic upper bounds.

2. **Precision and Generality**: The paper provides precise error formulas (Theorem 2.2) rather than loose upper bounds, which is a significant improvement over prior work. The framework applies to a wide class of regularizers (including group-LASSO and nuclear norm) and link functions.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) demonstrates the practical utility of the theory. The authors show how to optimize quantization thresholds by minimizing σ^2/μ^2, which is a concrete and valuable contribution.

4. **Rigorous Analysis**: The proofs leverage the CGMT framework, which has been successfully applied to linear measurement problems, and the authors carefully verify the conditions required for their asymptotic analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Asymptotic Nature**: The results are asymptotic (n, m → ∞ with m/n = δ fixed), which may limit their immediate applicability to finite-sample problems. While asymptotic analysis is standard for such problems, the paper would benefit from discussing the finite-sample behavior and convergence rates.

2. **Assumption on x_0 Norm**: The normalization ‖x_0‖_2 = 1 (Footnote 4) is restrictive. While the authors note that the results can be generalized to ‖x_0‖_2 ≠ 1, the paper would be stronger if this was incorporated into the main results.

3. **Limited Numerical Validation**: While Figures 1-2 show good agreement between theory and simulation, the paper lacks comprehensive numerical experiments across diverse scenarios. More extensive comparisons (e.g., varying δ, different link functions) would strengthen the claims.

4. **Computational Complexity**: The error formula involves solving a three-variable convex program (equation 10). While theoretically elegant, the paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of solving this program for specific cases, which might be a barrier to practical application.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of LASSO with non-linear measurements. The precise equivalence result is both elegant and practically useful, with clear implications for applications like quantized compressive sensing. The paper builds well upon previous work (notably Plan and Vershynin) and provides a rigorous framework for analyzing these problems.

The main limitations are the asymptotic nature of the results and the restrictive normalization assumption. However, these are common in this line of work, and the paper's contributions are substantial enough to overcome these limitations for a NeurIPS audience.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that is well-motivated, rigorously proven, and has clear practical implications. It advances the state-of-the-art in structured signal recovery by providing precise error analysis for non-linear measurement models. The paper is well-written and clearly connects to prior work while making a novel contribution.

The paper addresses a fundamental question in signal processing and statistics with significant implications for real-world applications involving non-linear measurements. The precise error formulas will be valuable to researchers and practitioners working on compressed sensing, quantized measurements, and structured signal recovery.

## Pro/Con Summary

**Pros**:
- Elegant theoretical result establishing precise equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models
- Extends Plan and Vershynin's work to provide precise error formulas rather than loose upper bounds
- Provides concrete application to q-bit compressive sensing with optimization guidelines
- Rigorous analysis using CGMT framework

**Cons**:
- Results are asymptotic (n, m → ∞)
- Restrictive normalization ‖x_0‖_2 = 1
- Limited numerical validation across diverse scenarios
- No discussion of computational cost of the error formula

This paper represents a significant advance in the theoretical understanding of LASSO with non-linear measurements, with implications for both theory and practice. It is well-suited for NeurIPS as it combines strong theoretical contributions with practical applications.
