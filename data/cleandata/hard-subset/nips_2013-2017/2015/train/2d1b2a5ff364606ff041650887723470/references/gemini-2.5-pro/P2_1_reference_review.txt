### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`, a setup known as the single-index model. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, an algorithm designed for linear measurements, when it is applied directly to this non-linear problem.

The main contribution is a powerful and elegant theoretical result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit (where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio `m/n`), the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is *exactly equivalent* to its performance on a corresponding linear model with additive Gaussian noise. Specifically, the non-linear model `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)` is shown to be equivalent to a linear model `y_i = µ * a_i^T x0 + σ * z_i`, where `z_i` is standard Gaussian noise. The scaling factor `µ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the properties of the (possibly random) link function `g`.

Building on this equivalence, the paper leverages the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) to derive a precise, deterministic formula for the asymptotic mean-squared error `||x̂ - µx0||^2`. This formula accurately captures the interplay between the problem parameters: the measurement ratio `m/n`, the signal structure, the choice of convex regularizer, and the regularization parameter `λ`. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their framework by specializing the general formula to important cases like sparse (`l1`), group-sparse (`l1,2`), and low-rank (nuclear norm) recovery. Finally, they showcase a practical application by using their theory to optimize the thresholds and levels for q-bit quantization.

The work builds upon and significantly extends several lines of research. It generalizes a classical result by Brillinger from the fixed-`n`, `m -> inf` regime to the modern high-dimensional setting. It also refines recent work by Plan and Vershynin, which provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a related problem, by delivering asymptotically *exact* error characterizations. Lastly, it extends the powerful framework for precise LASSO analysis, previously developed for linear measurements, to a broad class of non-linear observation models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The central claim—the equivalence between the non-linear and a specific linear model—is a deep and non-trivial result. The theoretical analysis is grounded in the state-of-the-art CGMT framework, which is the appropriate tool for deriving such precise asymptotic results. The claims are well-supported by the derived theorems, which provide explicit, computable expressions for the estimation error. The authors are careful in laying out their assumptions (Gaussian measurement matrix, asymptotic regime, etc.). The inclusion of several concrete examples (sparse, group-sparse) demonstrates a thorough understanding and application of the main theoretical result. The numerical simulations mentioned in the figure captions serve as excellent validation, showing a close match between the theoretical predictions and empirical performance.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction is particularly effective at motivating the problem, clearly summarizing the main contributions, and positioning the work within the context of relevant literature. The distinction between this work and prior results by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin is made explicit and easy to understand. The mathematical notation is standard and consistent. The main results (Theorems 2.1, 2.2, 2.3) are stated clearly. While the proofs are deferred to the appendix (as is standard), the main body of the paper successfully conveys the core ideas and their implications.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the idea that non-linearities can sometimes be approximated by additive noise is not entirely new, this paper is the first to establish a *precise asymptotic equivalence* for the LASSO estimator in the high-dimensional setting. Moving from the loose upper bounds of Plan and Vershynin to an exact characterization is a significant intellectual leap. This result effectively unifies the analysis of a wide range of non-linear measurement models under the well-developed umbrella of the linear LASSO theory. The application to optimizing quantizers based on minimizing the `σ^2/µ^2` ratio is also a novel and clever consequence of the main theorem.

**Significance:** The results are of high significance for both the machine learning theory and signal processing communities. The primary contribution is a conceptual simplification: a large class of seemingly complex non-linear problems can be analyzed using the tools of a much simpler, canonical linear problem. This insight is likely to be highly influential and can spur further research. Researchers can now take many existing precise results for the linear LASSO and immediately generalize them to single-index models. The framework provides a concrete recipe for predicting performance, which can guide the design of practical systems, as compellingly demonstrated by the q-bit compressive sensing application. The paper substantially advances the state of the art in the theoretical understanding of high-dimensional estimation under model mismatch.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

*   **Weaknesses/Limitations:** The primary limitation, common to this line of theoretical work, is the reliance on the i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix `A`. While this assumption is crucial for the CGMT machinery, real-world measurement matrices often have more structure. It would strengthen the paper to explicitly acknowledge this limitation and briefly discuss potential avenues for extending the results to other matrix ensembles.
*   **The `F` function:** The general error formula in Theorem 2.2 depends on a function `F` derived from the Moreau envelope of the regularizer's conjugate. While the authors admirably work this out for standard norms, this function remains abstract. A brief discussion on the properties or potential difficulty of computing `F` for other structured regularizers (e.g., those based on submodular functions) would be beneficial.
*   **Clarity on Novelty in the Linear Case:** The paper mentions that some of its specialized results are novel even for the linear measurement case (e.g., beyond `l1`-regularization with Gaussian signal priors). This is an interesting secondary contribution that could be made slightly more prominent, perhaps with a dedicated remark, to ensure it is not overlooked.

### Summary of Arguments for Acceptance

**Pros:**
1.  **Fundamental Result:** Establishes a clean, powerful, and surprising equivalence between LASSO performance on non-linear and linear measurement models.
2.  **Technical Strength:** Leverages sophisticated, state-of-the-art proof techniques (CGMT) to deliver precise, non-asymptotic results that go far beyond existing upper bounds.
3.  **High Significance:** The conceptual simplification provided by the theory has major implications, allowing a vast body of existing work on the linear LASSO to be extended to non-linear models.
4.  **Excellent Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, motivated, and contextualized within the literature.

**Cons:**
1.  **Gaussian Assumption:** The analysis is restricted to i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, limiting direct practical applicability.

Overall, this is an outstanding theoretical paper. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. The core result is elegant, significant, and represents a major step forward in our understanding of high-dimensional inference. It is a perfect fit for NIPS and is likely to become a key reference in this area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
