Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies generalized LASSO under single-index, non-linear measurements of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where A has i.i.d. Gaussian rows and gi are i.i.d. copies of a (possibly random) link function g. The main result establishes that, asymptotically in the proportional regime m/n → δ, the estimation error of the generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is identical to the error attained when using linear measurements ylin = µ A x0 + σ z, where the two scalars µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µ γ)^2], with γ ∼ N(0,1), capture the effect of the nonlinearity. This “nonlinear = linear” equivalence holds for a broad class of convex regularizers and link functions with µ ≠ 0 and finite σ^2.

Building on this equivalence, the paper derives an asymptotically exact characterization of the squared error via a scalar convex optimization (three variables) whose parameters depend explicitly on δ, λ, µ, σ^2, and on the regularizer/structure through a functional F that is the limit of a Moreau-envelope expression involving the Fenchel conjugate of the regularizer. The paper then specializes the general result to:
- Sparse recovery with ℓ1 regularization, yielding equations for the limiting error and identifying a critical λ regime when δ < 1.
- Group-sparse recovery with ℓ1,2 regularization via block soft-thresholding.
- Low-rank matrix recovery with nuclear norm (sketches conditions under which the F functional is computable).
- q-bit quantized sensing, where the equivalence reduces quantizer design to minimizing σ^2/µ^2; the authors connect this to the Lloyd–Max algorithm and show it finds stationary points for the new objective.

This work extends several lines of research. It generalizes Brillinger’s classical asymptotic LS result (fixed n, m → ∞) to high-dimensional proportional regimes and from LS to regularized estimators. It sharpens Plan and Vershynin’s non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO with single-index models by providing tight asymptotic predictions for the regularized LASSO and demonstrating the exact role of µ and σ^2. It leverages precise LASSO error analyses for linear measurements (AMP-based and CGMT-based results by Bayati–Montanari; Donoho–Maleki–Montanari; Stojnic; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.) and extends them to non-linear observations. Related to the broader literature, generalized AMP for nonlinear output channels is also relevant for performance prediction with arbitrary output non-linearities; while algorithmically different from LASSO, it provides a useful point of comparison not discussed here.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual message: in high dimensions, generalized LASSO with non-linear single-index measurements behaves as with linear measurements with effective gain µ and noise σ^2. This yields immediate interpretability and practical guidance (e.g., quantizer design).
- Technical rigor: the results are precise (not order-wise bounds) and apply to general convex regularizers under transparent asymptotic assumptions; the CGMT-based analysis appears sound.
- Breadth: covers sparse, group-sparse, and sketches low-rank. The sparse specialization recovers known special cases and extends them to broader priors.
- Practical implications: the q-bit sensing section translates theory into a concrete design rule (minimize σ^2/µ^2) and connects to a standard algorithm (Lloyd–Max).
- Empirical validation: simulations reported align with the theoretical predictions across multiple regimes.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Gaussian design: the i.i.d. Gaussian assumption on A is central to the CGMT analysis and to the equivalence; extension to subgaussian, rotationally invariant, or correlated designs is not addressed.
- Asymptotic nature: results are asymptotic; finite-sample accuracy and rates are not quantified.
- Identifiability dependence on µ: the framework assumes µ ≠ 0; cases with µ = 0 (e.g., certain even link functions or unknown scaling) are excluded or require different methods.
- Technical Assumption 1: the definition of F requires existence of a pointwise limit of a Moreau-envelope of the conjugate; while verified for standard regularizers (ℓ1, ℓ1,2) and sketched for nuclear norm, the generality and minimal sufficient conditions could be clarified further.
- Low-rank section is incomplete: it outlines conditions but defers evaluation of F and experiments.
- Empirical breadth: experiments focus on a few settings; broader tests (e.g., different g, λ tuning, group/nuclear norm) and comparisons to alternative methods (e.g., constrained LASSO, GAMP-based predictions) would strengthen the case.
- Practical estimation of µ and σ^2: for unknown g, the paper does not discuss how to estimate µ and σ^2 from data to leverage the design insights (though the LASSO itself does not use them).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The main theorems are technically sound within stated assumptions. The reduction to a scalar convex program is compelling. Proof details are deferred but consistent with CGMT methodology used in prior precise analyses.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and readable, but contains typos and some dense notation. Assumption 1 and the role of F could be further elucidated with additional examples. The distinction between equality of limiting errors vs. finite-n behavior should be made explicit.
- Originality: The “nonlinear = linear” equivalence for generalized LASSO with precise asymptotic error is novel and meaningful. The quantizer-design corollary is a useful application. The work extends precise-error analysis beyond linear measurements.
- Significance: Results provide a unifying perspective on LASSO under model nonlinearity and practical guidance for system design. The impact is strong within the Gaussian-design setting, and motivates future generalizations.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, general, and precise theoretical contribution.
- Clear and actionable insight (µ, σ^2) with a practical application (quantization design).
- Extends and unifies multiple strands of literature.

Cons for acceptance
- Restricted to Gaussian designs and asymptotic regime.
- Technical assumption on F and incomplete low-rank development.
- Limited empirical coverage and lack of finite-sample guidance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss robustness to non-Gaussian designs (subgaussian, rotationally invariant), and whether the equivalence persists.
- Provide more detail and examples clarifying Assumption 1 and computation of F for nuclear norm.
- Add experiments for group-sparse and low-rank, varying λ, and compare to constrained LASSO and GAMP-based predictions.
- Address data-driven estimation of µ and σ^2 and sensitivity to misspecification.
- Improve notation consistency and fix typographical issues; provide code for computing the scalar optimization.

Overall recommendation
The paper offers a technically solid and conceptually elegant contribution with clear significance for understanding and designing LASSO under non-linear measurements. Subject to the stated limitations, it merits acceptance.
