Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under a single-index measurement model y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian design A and structured x0 estimated via the generalized LASSO. The main claim is an asymptotically exact equivalence: for convex regularizers and λ > 0, the LASSO error with non-linear measurements behaves exactly as if the observations were linear with an unknown gain and additive Gaussian noise determined by the link via µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µγ)^2]. Theorem 2.1 formalizes this “nonlinear = linear” principle by stating an almost-sure equality of the asymptotic errors. Theorem 2.2 gives a precise scalar optimization for the limiting squared error as a function of δ = m/n, λ, regularizer f, and the link through (µ, σ^2), under a technical Assumption 1. The paper specializes to sparse and group-sparse cases (giving equations involving soft-thresholding), sketches an extension to low-rank with nuclear norm, and applies the result to design q-bit quantizers via minimizing σ^2/µ^2, arguing that Lloyd–Max iterations find stationary points of the relevant objective.

Relation to prior work
The work extends classic results of Brillinger (consistency of least squares under single-index link) and builds on Plan–Vershynin who provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO with link-induced parameters µ, σ. It leverages the recent CGMT/AMP literature to derive precise asymptotic risks for generalized LASSO with linear measurements, now transferred to non-linear settings via the µ, σ reduction. The specialization to l1 and group-lasso matches strands of precise risk analyses in [Bayati–Montanari, Thrampoulidis et al.], while the equivalence principle is conceptually close to other work on estimation under unknown monotone/nonlinear transforms.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling conceptual message: under Gaussian design, only the first two Stein-type moments (µ, σ^2) of g matter for the LASSO’s asymptotic error; all non-linearities with µ ≠ 0 reduce to an effective linear model.
- Precise asymptotic characterization (scalar optimization) covering arbitrary convex regularizers, all λ, and a wide class of x0 models, aligning with and extending established CGMT-based analyses.
- Concrete specializations for sparse and group-sparse recovery with equations that practitioners can, in principle, solve numerically; identification of a “critical λ” regime is consistent with prior theory.
- Application to quantizer design that turns a difficult end-to-end criterion into minimizing σ^2/µ^2, with a connection to Lloyd–Max iterations.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope restricted to Gaussian A. While standard in CGMT analyses, the practical impact hinges on robustness to sub-Gaussian or mildly dependent designs; neither theory nor experiments explore this.
- Assumption 1 (existence of a limit F for a scaled Moreau envelope of f∗) is nontransparent. The paper verifies it for a few cases, but the breadth of “wide class of functions f and g” remains unclear. For the low-rank case, verification and the computation of F are deferred, leaving that contribution incomplete.
- The “Non-linear = Linear” theorem is stated very strongly. As written, it reads like exact equality of errors in the limit; please clarify that it is equality of almost-sure limits (or convergence in probability) and specify the mode of convergence. A more careful statement and proof sketch would help.
- The exclusion of µ = 0 links (e.g., sign) is a substantial limitation given the active literature in 1-bit CS. The paper mentions this but does not offer workarounds (e.g., modified objectives or dithering) or discuss how the theory could be extended.
- Empirical validation is thin in the current writeup (figures referred to, but breadth unclear). No results are shown for the low-rank case, limited coverage for group-lasso, and no sensitivity to link choices, λ tuning, or finite-sample deviations. The quantitative tightness of the asymptotics for moderate n is not demonstrated.
- Practicality: knowing µ and σ is assumed; but in many applications g is unknown. A discussion and experiments on estimating (µ, σ) from data (and robustness of the risk to estimation error) are needed to support the “use as-is without g” motivation.
- Clarity and presentation: several notational choices (normalization by 1/√n in the objective; Assumption 1 definitions; use of envelopes/conjugates) could be streamlined. A high-level proof roadmap (e.g., how CGMT reduces the problem and where µ, σ enter) would aid readability. References are incomplete/inconsistently formatted.

Quality
The technical direction is sound and in line with established CGMT/AMP methodology, but key details are deferred. The strongest claims would benefit from clearer statements and more complete proofs or at least a precise proof sketch. The low-rank section is not carried through.

Clarity
Moderately clear but dense. Assumption 1 and Theorem 2.2 deserve more intuition. Improve notation consistency, define all operators once, and ensure figures and experimental setups are fully described. References need fixing.

Originality
The equivalence principle is a natural and incremental extension of Plan–Vershynin’s insight and the CGMT precise-risk line. The main novelty is to show an exact asymptotic equality of risks and provide a general scalar optimization for the non-linear case via reduction to linear with (µ, σ). The sparse/group-sparse specializations are useful but largely expected. The quantizer design angle is interesting but limited.

Significance
For the theory community working on precise high-dimensional risk, the result is valuable as a unifying statement and may stimulate further extensions (non-Gaussian designs, µ = 0, estimation of µ, σ). Practical impact is currently limited by assumptions and lack of empirical breadth.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 2.1’s statement (mode of convergence, precise conditions) and give a proof outline.
- Make Assumption 1 more transparent; provide a checklist for common f (l1, group-l1, nuclear norm) and x0 models. For low-rank, include at least one fully worked example (e.g., spiked model) with an explicit F and experiments.
- Provide finite-sample experiments across links (e.g., ReLU, saturated sign with/without noise), multiple δ, λ, and compare to constrained LASSO and link-aware estimators. Assess robustness to sub-Gaussian designs.
- Discuss and demonstrate estimation of µ, σ from data; quantify the impact of estimation error on the predicted risk.
- Expand the quantization example with end-to-end recovery comparisons against standard LM levels and existing 1-bit/q-bit CS methods.
- Clean up notation and references; provide code to compute α* from (10).

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper has a strong and plausible theoretical message but, in its current form, relies on heavy assumptions, leaves notable cases incomplete, and provides limited empirical support. With clarified statements/proofs, a completed low-rank treatment, and more thorough experiments (including robustness and µ,σ estimation), I would be inclined to support acceptance. Confidence: high.
