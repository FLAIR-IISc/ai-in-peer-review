- Main result: In high dimensions with Gaussian measurement matrices, Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to LASSO on scaled, noisy linear measurements determined by constants µ and σ² from the link function g.
- Contribution: Provides a precise asymptotic error expression extending prior work to the non-linear measurement setting.
- Theoretical strength: Offers a rigorous, well-motivated asymptotic analysis grounded in modern high-dimensional theory.
- Novelty: The equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements is a new, insightful result enabling use of linear-model theory for non-linear problems.
- Generality: Results apply to a wide class of link functions g and regularizers f, not limited to specific sparsity models.
- Precise analysis: Delivers quantitative error formulas rather than only consistency or bounds.
- Applications: Demonstrates relevance to sparse recovery and q-bit compressive sensing, including a connection to Lloyd–Max quantization.
- Writing: Paper is generally well-written, organized, and uses consistent notation.
- Key assumption: Requires i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices (strong but standard); unit-norm x0 assumed for simplicity.
- Technical caveat: Assumption 1 is somewhat restrictive and its justification for sparse recovery is not fully detailed.
- Empirical limitation: Limited simulations/experiments; more extensive validation across λ, m/n, and different g would strengthen claims.
- Practical issue: Computing optimal parameters (e.g., for q-bit sensing) may be challenging in practice.
- Quality assessment: Technically sound with complete, well-supported mathematical analysis.
- Clarity assessment: Generally clear, though parts (notably Assumption 1) need more explanation.
- Originality assessment: Highly original contribution combining existing techniques to analyze the non-linear case.
- Significance assessment: Likely influential for high-dimensional statistics and signal processing; opens new research directions.
- Pro arguments for acceptance: Significant theoretical breakthrough, rigorous analysis, broad applicability, practical potential.
- Con arguments against acceptance: Strong measurement-matrix assumptions, limited empirical validation, technical complexity, restrictive Assumption 1.
- Recommendation: Strong Accept.
