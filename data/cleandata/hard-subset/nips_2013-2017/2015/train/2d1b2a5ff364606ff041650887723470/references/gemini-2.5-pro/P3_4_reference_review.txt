### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x₀` from non-linear measurements of the form `yᵢ = gᵢ(aᵢᵀx₀)`, a setup known as the single-index model. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO, an algorithm designed for linear models, when applied directly to this non-linear problem. The central and powerful contribution is showing that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit with Gaussian measurement matrices, the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimator is exactly equivalent to its performance on a corresponding *linear* model with scaled signal and additive Gaussian noise: `yᵢ = µaᵢᵀx₀ + σzᵢ`. The constants `µ` and `σ²` are determined solely by the statistics of the link function `g`.

This equivalence allows the authors to leverage the sophisticated and powerful Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, previously used for precise analysis of the LASSO in linear settings, to derive exact asymptotic characterizations of the estimation error for the non-linear case. The paper provides a general error formula (Theorem 2.2) and specializes it to important cases like sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery. Finally, it demonstrates the utility of this result by showing how it simplifies the problem of designing optimal quantizers for compressive sensing into a much more tractable optimization of the `σ²/µ²` ratio.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The main result—that the LASSO treats these non-linear measurements as scaled, noisy linear ones—is elegant, insightful, and powerful. It provides a unifying principle that connects a broad class of seemingly complex non-linear problems to a well-understood linear counterpart. This is a substantial conceptual leap beyond prior work (e.g., Plan and Vershynin [17]), which provided non-asymptotic upper bounds with loose constants. This paper replaces those bounds with exact asymptotic characterizations, thereby proving the precise nature of the relationship hinted at in earlier work.

2.  **Technical Depth and Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. It builds upon the state-of-the-art CGMT framework, which is the correct and modern tool for this type of analysis. The resulting error predictions (Theorems 2.2 and 2.3) are not just bounds but precise formulas, which is the gold standard for this line of research. The provided figures demonstrate an excellent match between the theoretical predictions and empirical simulations, validating the asymptotic analysis even for moderately sized problems.

3.  **Generality and Scope:** The framework presented is remarkably general. It applies to a wide class of convex regularizers beyond the standard `ℓ₁`-norm (including group LASSO and nuclear norm), various signal priors (e.g., Bernoulli-Gaussian, Bernoulli-Rademacher), and a broad family of link functions `g`. This makes the results widely applicable across different domains where structured signal recovery from non-linearities is relevant. The derivations for group-sparse and low-rank recovery, even under linear measurements, appear novel and are valuable in their own right.

4.  **Practical Implications:** The application to optimal quantizer design is an excellent showcase of the paper's practical significance. It transforms a computationally intractable optimization problem (tuning quantizer thresholds by running the full LASSO) into a simple, low-dimensional optimization of the `σ²/µ²` ratio. This is a concrete and useful outcome of the theoretical analysis.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Reliance on Gaussian Measurements:** The analysis heavily relies on the assumption that the measurement matrix `A` has i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a critical ingredient for the CGMT framework to apply. While this is a standard assumption in the field, it is a significant limitation. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on the potential for extending these results to more general sub-Gaussian ensembles. Even if a full proof is out of scope, a discussion of the technical challenges and potential roadblocks would add valuable context.

2.  **Clarity of the Core Mechanism:** While the main result is stated clearly, the paper could benefit from providing more intuition as to *why* this "non-linear = linear" equivalence holds. The proofs are deferred to the appendix, but a short, high-level paragraph in the main text explaining the role of Gaussianity and high-dimensionality in effectively "linearizing" the problem from the perspective of the LASSO objective would greatly improve readability and impact for a broader audience.

3.  **The `µ=0` Case:** The paper explicitly assumes `µ ≠ 0`, which excludes important link functions, such as even functions (e.g., `g(x) = |x|`). This is a necessary condition for the approach to work, as `µ` represents the effective "signal" component. The authors mention this in a footnote, but this limitation is significant enough to warrant a more prominent discussion in the main body of the paper. Acknowledging this as a key open direction for future research would be appropriate.

4.  **Asymptotic Nature:** The results are strictly asymptotic. The simulations show remarkable agreement, but a brief discussion of the pre-asymptotic regime or potential finite-sample deviations would be welcome. For instance, are there known bounds on the rate of convergence to these asymptotic formulas?

### Recommendation

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental and significant contribution to the theory of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. The core result is both surprising and powerful, providing a deep and precise understanding of how a standard linear estimation tool behaves in a non-linear setting. The technical analysis is state-of-the-art, and the results are general and practically relevant. The weaknesses are primarily related to the assumptions common in this line of work and do not detract from the importance of the contribution.

**I strongly recommend acceptance.** This paper will be of great interest to the NIPS community working on statistical learning theory, high-dimensional inference, and signal processing.
