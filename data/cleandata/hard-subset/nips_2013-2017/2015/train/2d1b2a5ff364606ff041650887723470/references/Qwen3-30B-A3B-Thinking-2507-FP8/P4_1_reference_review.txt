

# Review

This paper investigates the relationship between LASSO estimation with non-linear measurements and LASSO with linear measurements. The authors consider a single-index model where measurements take the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), with non-linear link functions gi. They establish that, under Gaussian measurement matrices and in the high-dimensional limit, the performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of the Generalized LASSO with appropriately scaled linear measurements (with scaling factor μ and noise variance σ^2 defined in terms of the link function g). This generalizes previous work by Brillinger (1980s) who studied least squares in fixed-dimensional settings, and Plan and Vershynin (2015) who provided loose upper bounds for constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements.

The main contributions are Theorems 2.1 (showing the equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement cases) and 2.2 (providing a precise error formula for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements). The authors demonstrate how this framework applies to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank signal recovery, and apply their results to q-bit compressive sensing to optimize quantization thresholds.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with well-defined assumptions and clear theorems. The authors carefully establish the equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement cases, and the proofs (though deferred to appendices) appear sound. The paper properly references relevant prior work, including key papers by Plan and Vershynin.

2. **Novelty:** The paper makes a significant contribution by extending previous work on LASSO with non-linear measurements from loose upper bounds to precise error predictions. The key insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled and noisy linear measurements is elegant and novel. The paper provides novel error formulas for structured recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) that were previously unknown.

3. **Significance:** The results have important implications for understanding when and how LASSO can be used with non-linear measurements. The precise error predictions can guide practitioners in choosing regularization parameters. The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates practical relevance by providing a framework for optimizing quantization thresholds.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The main result (Theorem 2.1) is clearly stated and explained, and the authors provide specific examples to illustrate their general results. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Assumptions:** The paper assumes i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, which is a strong assumption that may limit applicability to some real-world scenarios. The paper also assumes the link function g (or at least its moments μ and σ^2) is known, which may not hold in all applications.

2. **Scope:** The paper focuses on the Generalized LASSO with the squared error loss. It does not address other loss functions that might be more appropriate for certain non-linear measurement models, which could limit the practical applicability of the results.

3. **Numerical Validation:** While the paper includes numerical experiments that validate the theoretical results, they could be more comprehensive, including more diverse signal models or measurement conditions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides the first precise asymptotic analysis of LASSO with non-linear measurements.
- Establishes a fundamental equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement setups.
- Derives novel error formulas for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery.
- Demonstrates practical application to q-bit compressive sensing.
- Builds upon and significantly extends previous work by Plan and Vershynin.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Relies on strong assumptions (i.i.d. Gaussian measurements, known link function moments).
- Focuses on a specific loss function (squared error) without addressing other loss functions.
- Numerical validation could be more comprehensive.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of LASSO with non-linear measurements. The results are technically sound, novel, and significant for both theoretical understanding and practical applications in signal processing. While the assumptions are strong, they are common in high-dimensional statistical analysis and the authors clearly state their limitations. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing precise error predictions rather than loose upper bounds, which is a substantial improvement over previous work.

This paper is well-suited for NeurIPS as it addresses a fundamental question in high-dimensional statistical learning with clear theoretical contributions and practical implications. The work is technically rigorous, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the literature on structured signal recovery.
