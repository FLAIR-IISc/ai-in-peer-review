This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements. The core contribution is demonstrating that, asymptotically, the non-linear measurement problem behaves as if it were a scaled and noisy linear measurement problem. This is quantified by defining scaling and noise parameters µ and σ² based on the link function g. The authors provide precise error bounds, generalizing previous work on linear measurements and extending results by Plan & Vershynin. They also explore applications to q-bit compressive sensing, offering insights into optimal quantization strategies.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The main result – the equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurements in the asymptotic regime – is a significant theoretical advance.  This connection allows leveraging existing, sophisticated analysis tools developed for linear measurements in a non-linear setting.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper appears mathematically rigorous, building on a strong foundation of existing results in high-dimensional statistics and compressed sensing (e.g., [2, 16, 17, 19, 20]). The proofs, while not presented in full, are referenced to appendices, suggesting a solid grounding.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and positions the work within the existing literature. The notation is standard, and the concepts are explained reasonably well.
*   **Significance:**  The result has the potential to be impactful, particularly in areas where non-linear measurements are common (e.g., signal processing with quantized data, medical imaging).  The application to q-bit compressive sensing is a concrete example of potential practical relevance. The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is interesting.
*   **Thorough Literature Review:** The related work section is extensive and covers relevant papers from statistics, econometrics, and signal processing. The authors demonstrate awareness of previous contributions and accurately position their work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The reliance on Gaussian measurement matrices and the assumption of unit norm signals (although addressed with a footnote regarding generalization) limit the immediate applicability of the results.  It would be valuable to discuss the sensitivity of the results to deviations from these assumptions. Assumption 1 is stated without substantial intuition.
*   **Computational Complexity of Application:** While the paper identifies a connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm for q-bit compressive sensing, it doesn't delve into the practical challenges of implementing this algorithm, particularly for large q. A discussion of the computational cost would be helpful.
*   **Proof Sketches:**  Referring to appendices for proofs is standard, but a slightly more detailed sketch of the key proof ideas within the main body of the paper could improve understanding for readers not specializing in the specific technical tools used (e.g., CGMT).
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical results. While Figures 1 and 2 show the accuracy of the predicted error, the examples are limited. More extensive numerical experiments demonstrating the performance of the approach in various scenarios would strengthen the claims.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result that significantly advances our understanding of LASSO-type algorithms in the context of non-linear measurements.
*   The connection to existing work on linear measurements is cleverly exploited, allowing for the transfer of powerful analytical tools.
*   The application to q-bit compressive sensing is a promising example of practical relevance.
*   The extensive and well-organized literature review demonstrates a thorough understanding of the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumptions regarding Gaussian matrices and unit norm signals may limit the immediate applicability of the results.
*   The lack of extensive empirical validation leaves some questions about the practical performance of the approach.
*   The detailed proofs are relegated to appendices, making it difficult for readers to fully assess the technical soundness of the claims without significant effort.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. The limitations are primarily related to the assumptions made and the lack of extensive empirical validation. However, the theoretical contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, including a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to deviations from the assumptions and perhaps a slightly more detailed sketch of the proof ideas in the main body of the paper.
