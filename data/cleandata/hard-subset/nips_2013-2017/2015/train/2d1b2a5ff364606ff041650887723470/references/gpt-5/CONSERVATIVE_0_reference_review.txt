Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under a single-index (nonlinear) measurement model yi = gi(a_i^T x0), with Gaussian sensing matrix A, structured x0, and convex regularization. The main claim is an asymptotically exact “equivalence principle”: when using the (square-root) Generalized LASSO minx ||y−Ax||2 + λ f(x), the estimation error matches that of the same estimator under a linear Gaussian model with an effective scale and noise, y_lin = µ A x0 + σ z, where µ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µ γ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). Theorem 2.1 formalizes this equivalence; Theorem 2.2 gives a scalar convex optimization that yields the asymptotic risk; specialized results are provided for sparse (Theorem 2.3), group-sparse, and sketched for low-rank recovery. A quantized sensing application uses the equivalence to advocate choosing quantizer thresholds/levels to minimize σ^2/µ^2, with an LM-type algorithm justified as finding stationary points of that objective.

Relation to prior work: Brillinger (early LS consistency under nonlinear links with fixed n, m→∞) and Plan–Vershynin (non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO under nonlinear links) established that the µ,σ defined above govern performance up to scaling. Recent precise asymptotics for LASSO under linear measurements have been developed via AMP and CGMT. This paper leverages CGMT-style arguments (and existing precise analyses under linear models) to extend precise error characterizations to nonlinear links and a range of convex regularizers.

Strengths
- Technically ambitious and (assuming proofs check) strong: converting nonlinear observation models to an “effective” linear model with exact asymptotic risk is a clear and useful message. It unifies and sharpens earlier upper bounds (Plan–Vershynin) and connects cleanly to Brillinger’s classical result.
- Generality across convex regularizers is attractive; the sparse and group-sparse specializations are explicit, and Theorem 2.3 for `2-LASSO with general sparse priors appears novel and precise.
- The scalar convex program in Theorem 2.2 is practical to evaluate and makes the roles of δ, λ, f, and g (via µ,σ) explicit.
- The quantized CS application is natural, and reducing design to minimizing σ^2/µ^2 is intuitively appealing.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Gaussian design only; no universality. All main theorems require i.i.d. N(0,1) rows. There is no theoretical universality nor empirical evidence for sub-Gaussian or structured sensing matrices, which limits practical impact.
- Opacity and strength of Assumption 1. The key technical assumption is stated abstractly in terms of pointwise limits of normalized Moreau envelopes of f*; while examples are worked out for `1 and `1,2, the low-rank case is left “for future work,” and even for sparsity the assumption places nontrivial regularity on p(x0). The paper would benefit from crisp sufficient conditions ensuring Assumption 1 for common models.
- Identifiability and calibration. Much of the discussion assesses ||x̂−µ x0|| and sometimes rescales by µ−1. In practice µ is unknown when g is unknown. There is no proposed data-driven estimator for µ (and σ) nor an analysis of how estimation error in µ affects the LASSO risk or tuning. This weakens the operational message.
- Limited empirical validation. The manuscript refers to figures validating predictions, but experiments seem narrow (Gaussian A only, a few priors), and there is no comparison to competing methods designed for nonlinear observations (e.g., one-bit CS methods, GLMs, or spectral initializers). Also missing are sensitivity studies to misspecified λ and to finite-n deviations.
- Clarity and presentation issues. There are several typos and inconsistencies (“mesaurements,” terminology around “`2-LASSO” vs square-root LASSO, scaling in (9) with 1/√n), Theorem 2.1’s statement reads as equality of random errors rather than equality of limits, which should be clarified. The low-rank section explicitly defers details. Some notation (Assumption 1, F) could be made more concrete earlier.
- Scope of nonlinearity. The theory requires µ ≠ 0 and bounded σ^2; common links like symmetric one-bit without dithering can yield µ=0, which the paper acknowledges but does not address beyond remarks. Practical guidance for such regimes is absent.

Quality
The results appear technically solid and in line with the CGMT literature, but the reliance on a strong, somewhat opaque assumption and the omission of several details (low-rank case, empirical breadth, µ estimation) make the current version feel incomplete. The quantizer design section is interesting but proves only stationarity of LM for the σ^2/µ^2 objective and provides limited empirical evidence.

Clarity
Generally well organized, but the manuscript would benefit from:
- Clearer statement of Theorem 2.1 as equality of limits, not sample-wise equality.
- A standalone, user-friendly recipe: given f, p(x0), δ, λ, g, how to compute the risk (including how to evaluate F), with worked examples.
- A brief discussion on how to estimate µ,σ from data, and how to choose λ in practice using the theory.

Originality
The “nonlinear = linear” precise equivalence for LASSO appears new and conceptually clean, extending precise LASSO analyses to nonlinear links. The sparse specialization beyond Gaussian priors is also new. Much of the technical machinery is based on established CGMT tools.

Significance
For the high-dimensional inference/theory community, the result is meaningful: it sharpens our understanding of LASSO robustness to link misspecification and provides computable risk formulae. However, the heavy Gaussian assumption, lack of universality, and limited practical calibration guidance temper broader impact.

Pros
- Clear, general equivalence principle with precise asymptotics.
- Covers multiple convex regularizers; new explicit sparse results.
- Bridges classical Brillinger/Plan–Vershynin with modern precise LASSO analysis.
- Natural application to quantized sensing.

Cons
- Gaussian design only; no universality or non-Gaussian experiments.
- Abstract Assumption 1; incomplete low-rank treatment.
- No procedure to estimate µ and σ; practical calibration/tuning under unknown g is not addressed.
- Limited empirical breadth and baselines; clarity issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add universality evidence (theory or experiments) beyond Gaussian A.
- Provide practical estimators for µ,σ and discuss impact on risk/tuning; possibly include a joint estimator.
- Expand experiments: different links (e.g., logistic, ReLU-like), non-Gaussian designs, comparisons to GLM-based methods and one-bit CS algorithms.
- Clarify Assumption 1 with concrete sufficient conditions and complete the low-rank case.
- Clean up presentation and theorem statements.

Recommendation: Borderline reject
While the core idea is strong and likely correct, the paper in its current form feels incomplete on both theory (assumption clarity, low-rank) and practice (calibration, universality, experiments). With these addressed, it could be a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
