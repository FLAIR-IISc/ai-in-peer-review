This paper investigates the equivalence of LASSO solutions with nonlinear measurements to those obtained with scaled and noisy linear measurements. The core result, Theorem 2.1, asserts that under Gaussian measurements, the estimation error for the generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements matches that of a corresponding linear model with adjusted parameters. The paper further provides precise error formulas (Theorem 2.2) and applies these results to specific cases like sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, and q-bit compressive sensing.

The paper's strength lies in its rigorous analysis and its attempt to provide asymptotically exact error bounds – a valuable contribution given the existing literature focused on upper bounds or approximate results. The connection established between nonlinear and linear measurements is insightful and potentially simplifies the analysis of complex estimation problems. The application to q-bit compressive sensing, with a proposed algorithm based on the Lloyd-Max principle, is also a noteworthy contribution.

However, the paper is riddled with minor but pervasive issues that, collectively, detract from its overall quality. The writing style is dense and often lacks sufficient explanation. For instance, the introduction jumps quickly into technical details without adequately motivating the problem or highlighting the significance of the results for a broader audience. The notation is often inconsistent, and key definitions are buried within paragraphs rather than explicitly stated.  Several statements lack sufficient justification.

Specifically:

* **Assumption 1:** The justification for Assumption 1, crucial for Theorem 2.2, feels incomplete. The explanation of how it holds for sparse recovery is rushed and lacks sufficient detail. The statement about the convergence of the empirical distribution in the low-rank case is vague and requires elaboration.
* **Theorem 2.3 (Sparse Recovery):** The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm feels tacked on. While interesting, the discussion lacks detail regarding its practical implementation and potential limitations.  The claim that the LM algorithm *converges* to stationary points without demonstrating convergence is problematic.
* **Mathematical Rigor:** While the authors claim "precise" error formulas, the level of mathematical rigor is uneven. The proofs are deferred to the appendix, which is standard, but the statements themselves lack clarity in certain places, making it difficult to assess their correctness.
* **Formatting and Typos:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and typos throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, missing periods, grammatical errors). While seemingly trivial, they detract from the paper’s professionalism. The notation is often difficult to follow.
* **Related Work:** The related work section is extensive but somewhat disorganized. While comprehensive, it often feels like a laundry list of papers rather than a focused discussion of relevant contributions.  The comparisons to Plan and Vershynin [17] are superficial.
* **Justification of Asymptotic Regime:** The choice of the asymptotic regime (m/n = δ) is stated without sufficient justification. It's unclear why this particular scaling is chosen and how it affects the generality of the results.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of the results. For example, the Gaussian assumption on the measurement matrix is strong, and the paper doesn't address the robustness of the results to deviations from this assumption.
* **Equation (1):** The notation `gi(x)` is confusing; it’s not immediately clear if this represents the i-th component of a vector-valued function or a single function evaluated at x.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novelty: The connection between nonlinear and linear measurements is an interesting and potentially useful result.
* Technical Soundness (potentially): The core theoretical result (Theorem 2.1) appears plausible.
* Significance (potentially): If the results are robust, they could simplify the analysis of nonlinear estimation problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Clarity: The paper is difficult to read and understand.
* Rigor: The mathematical rigor is uneven and some claims lack sufficient justification.
* Presentation: Numerous formatting inconsistencies and typos detract from the paper’s professionalism.
* Completeness: The discussion of limitations and practical considerations is insufficient.
* Pervasive minor flaws: While individually small, the cumulative effect of these flaws is substantial.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential significance of the results, I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a novel idea and attempts a rigorous analysis, the numerous flaws in clarity, presentation, and mathematical rigor significantly outweigh its strengths. The paper requires substantial revision to address these issues before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS.  I believe addressing these issues would likely require a significant rewrite and a more thorough review of the mathematical arguments. The level of polish expected for NIPS is not currently met.
