### **Summary**

This paper studies the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimating a structured signal `x_0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)`. The central and striking contribution is to show that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit (where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio `δ = m/n`), the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is *exactly equivalent* to its performance on an effective linear model. This equivalent linear model has measurements `y_lin = µ * A * x_0 + σ * z`, where `z` is standard Gaussian noise. The scaling factor `µ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the statistics of the link function `g`.

Building on this equivalence, the authors leverage recent advances in the precise analysis of the LASSO (namely, the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework) to derive an exact, deterministic formula for the asymptotic mean-squared error `||x_hat - µ*x_0||_2^2`. This formula is given as the solution to a simple scalar optimization problem that captures the influence of all key parameters: `δ`, the regularization parameter `λ`, the signal structure, the choice of regularizer, and the non-linearity `g` (via `µ` and `σ^2`). The general result is then specialized to important cases like sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery. Finally, the practical utility of the theory is demonstrated in an application to q-bit compressive sensing, where it simplifies the problem of designing optimal quantization thresholds to a tractable optimization of the ratio `σ^2/µ^2`.

This work significantly extends the classical results of Brillinger on un-regularized least squares in the fixed-`n` regime and provides a precise, asymptotic characterization that sharpens the non-asymptotic upper bounds recently developed by Plan and Vershynin. It bridges the gap between the rich literature on the precise analysis of LASSO for linear models and the more complex setting of non-linear observations.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Fundamental and Elegant Result:** The main finding—that a complex, non-linear measurement model behaves identically to a simple, noisy linear one in the high-dimensional limit—is a profound and elegant conceptual contribution. It provides a powerful simplification and a unifying lens through which to view a wide array of problems, including 1-bit sensing, censored data, and general quantized measurements.

2.  **Technical Strength and Precision:** The paper is technically very strong. It moves beyond the loose upper bounds common in prior work for this problem and provides asymptotically exact predictions for the estimation error. The validation of these precise theoretical formulas against Monte Carlo simulations (Figures 1 and 2) is impressive and provides strong evidence for the correctness of the analysis.

3.  **Generality and Significance:** The framework is remarkably general, accommodating a wide class of convex regularizers and signal structures (sparsity, group sparsity, low rank) as well as a broad family of non-linear link functions. This generality makes the results widely applicable. The significance is high, as it allows the extensive body of research on the precise performance of LASSO in the linear Gaussian case to be directly translated to many non-linear settings by simply calculating the corresponding `µ` and `σ`.

4.  **Clear Practical Implications:** The application to optimal quantizer design in Section 2.3 is a compelling demonstration of the theory's practical value. It shows how a complex design problem involving the behavior of a LASSO estimator can be reduced to a much simpler, low-dimensional optimization problem over the parameters of the link function. This is an excellent example of theory guiding practical system design.

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the contributions within the context of prior art. The main results are stated cleanly, followed by insightful specializations and a concrete application, making the paper easy to follow despite its technical depth.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is of very high quality, and the following points are minor suggestions for further improvement rather than significant flaws.

1.  **Discussion of Assumptions:** The analysis relies on an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix `A`. This is a standard assumption in the line of work using tools like the CGMT. However, it would be beneficial to add a brief discussion on the potential for extending these results. For instance, is the Gaussian assumption critical to the equivalence result, or might it hold more broadly for other random matrix ensembles (e.g., sub-gaussian)? A sentence or two clarifying the scope would be helpful.

2.  **Intuition for the Error Formula:** The general error formula in Theorem 2.2 is presented as the solution to a minimax optimization problem involving the function `F`. While `F` is computed for specific cases, its general role could be demystified slightly. A brief intuitive explanation (e.g., relating it to the error of an associated scalar denoising problem, which is common in AMP-based analyses) could help readers build a deeper understanding of the formula's structure.

3.  **Proof Sketch in Main Body:** The proofs are appropriately deferred to the appendix. However, including a very short (1-2 paragraph) high-level proof sketch in the main paper would enhance its accessibility. This sketch could outline the main steps, such as applying the CGMT to the LASSO objective, which transforms the vector optimization problem into an equivalent scalar one, and then showing how the non-linearity `g` affects this scalar problem only through `µ` and `σ`.

### **Decision**

This is an outstanding paper that makes a fundamental, novel, and significant contribution to the theory of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. The results are technically deep yet yield a conceptually simple and powerful principle. The paper is well-executed, clearly written, and demonstrates the practical utility of its theory. It significantly advances the state of the art in understanding regularized estimation with non-linear data.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Establishes a novel and fundamental equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models for the LASSO.
*   Provides the first asymptotically exact performance characterization for the Generalized LASSO with general non-linearities.
*   Results are technically strong, general, and convincingly verified with simulations.
*   Has clear and significant implications for both theory and practice, as demonstrated by the quantizer design application.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The reviewer finds no significant arguments against acceptance. The reliance on the Gaussian matrix assumption is a limitation, but it is standard for this type of precise analysis and does not detract from the paper's core contribution.

**Recommendation: Strong Accept.**
