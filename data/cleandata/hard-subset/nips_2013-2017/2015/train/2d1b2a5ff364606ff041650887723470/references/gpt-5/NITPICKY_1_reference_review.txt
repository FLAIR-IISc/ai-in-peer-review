Summary
This paper studies generalized LASSO with non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where A has i.i.d. Gaussian entries and gi are i.i.d. copies of a possibly random link g. The main claim is an exact asymptotic equivalence: for a broad class of convex regularizers f and under a linear n,m→∞ regime with m/n→δ, the mean-squared error of the generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is the same as that of the same estimator applied to an effective linear model with scaled signal and Gaussian noise: ylin = A(µ x0) + σ z, where µ = E[γ g(γ)], σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − µ γ)^2], γ∼N(0,1). Using CGMT-type arguments, the paper asserts (i) an asymptotic “nonlinear = linear” equivalence theorem (Theorem 2.1) and (ii) a precise scalar convex program (Theorem 2.2) predicting lim ||x̂ − µ x0||^2. It specializes to sparse and group-sparse regularization (deriving fixed-point characterizations beyond the Gaussian-signal case) and sketches an extension to low-rank with nuclear norm. As an application, it proposes quantizer design for q-bit compressive sensing by minimizing σ^2/µ^2, with a Lloyd–Max-type algorithm argued to converge to stationary points of that objective.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and timely question: performance of LASSO under unknown/nonlinear link functions with Gaussian design.
- Conceptually clean equivalence principle (reducing to a matched linear model) that sharpens Plan–Vershynin’s non-asymptotic bounds into asymptotically exact predictions.
- Uses modern CGMT methodology to obtain a three-scalar convex program; provides explicit fixed-point characterizations in sparse and group-sparse cases (beyond Gaussian coefficients).
- Practical angle: connects theory to quantizer design for q-bit compressed sensing and derives closed-form µ, τ^2 for quantizers.
- If correct and complete, would be a significant contribution likely to be used for analysis and design of LASSO-type estimators under nonlinearities.

Weaknesses and detailed issues
Technical assumptions and completeness
- Assumption 1 (existence of a pointwise limit of the normalized Moreau envelope of f* evaluated at c1 h + c2 x0) is nonstandard and pivotal. It is verified for ℓ1 and block-ℓ1,2 but explicitly left unverified for the nuclear norm case, for which the paper says “we leave the details … for future work.” As stated, Theorems 2.1–2.2 hinge on Assumption 1, so the scope for low-rank recovery is unclear.
- Theorem 2.1 is stated as “in the limit of n→∞, with probability one, ||x̂−µx0||^2 = ||x̂lin−µx0||^2.” This phrasing is odd: the equality should be between limits (or limit points) of the errors, not between the random errors themselves. Please restate clearly (e.g., lim_n ||x̂−µx0||^2 = lim_n ||x̂lin−µx0||^2) and specify whether uniqueness of the limit is guaranteed.
- Uniqueness/selection of minimizers is not discussed. For δ<1, the objective may not be strictly convex; CGMT results often deliver concentration of the optimal cost and norm but require care to handle potentially non-unique minimizers. Please state conditions ensuring uniqueness (or argue that all minimizers have the same asymptotic error).
- The reduction to optimizing σ^2/µ^2 for quantizer design implicitly assumes the LASSO error is monotone in the effective noise variance for fixed µ (and in SNR generally), uniformly over λ and f. This monotonicity is not established; Theorem 2.2 shows dependence on µ, σ and F jointly. Please justify rigorously when minimizing σ^2/µ^2 indeed minimizes the predicted error.

Modeling and normalization
- The paper uses the square-root LASSO objective (||y−Ax||_2 + λ f(x)) but calls it “ℓ2-LASSO” and contrasts it with the squared loss variant. The terminology is confusing and nonstandard. Also, in (9) the objective is scaled by 1/√n (unusual; typically 1/√m or no scaling). Please standardize the naming and explain/justify the normalization choices consistently across theorems.
- The treatment of x0 is confusing: Section 2.1 says “x0 = x0 / ||x0||2, with x0 sampled from px0,” then “Thus, x0 is deterministically of unit norm,” but also “x0 ∼ p_x0.” Please fix notation: use distinct symbols for the random unnormalized draw and its unit-norm version, and avoid reusing x0 for both.
- The assumption “µ and σ^2 are nonzero and bounded” excludes µ=0 cases (e.g., even links). This is mentioned in passing; however, the contribution is limited to µ≠0. Please emphasize this limitation early and discuss how the theory might extend (or not) to µ=0.

Rigor and clarity of statements
- Theorem 2.2’s scalar program (10) is hard to parse: missing parentheses/spaces make terms ambiguous (e.g., “+ µ2τ 2α − αλ 2 τ F(…)”). Please rewrite cleanly, define all variables and domains, and ensure dimensions are consistent.
- In (9) you write “x̂(n) := min_x …” but it should be “argmin.”
- In the sparse case, the construction qX0 with second moment 1/ρ to enforce ||x0||=1 on average is nonstandard and deserves explanation; otherwise the normalization appears ad hoc.

Experimental/empirical support
- The paper refers to Figures 1–2 validating predictions but the excerpt shows no experimental details (settings, confidence intervals, number of trials). For NeurIPS, quantitative validation (including for the quantizer design algorithm vs. baselines) is important.
- The quantization section’s Lloyd–Max claim (Appendix C) is non-trivial; a numerical study comparing the proposed design against standard LM (MSE criterion) and against 1-bit sign with optimized threshold would strengthen the application.

Formatting and referencing (numerous issues)
- Many typos and grammatical errors: “mesaurements,” “eventhough,” “redifining,” “gaussian” vs “Gaussian,” “is in principle is possible,” etc. Equations and footnotes sometimes break (e.g., “.3 An Application…”, “5Such models…” inline). Inconsistent use of “6=” for “≠.”
- Several equations/typesetting glitches: e.g., “n e√n(f∗)(n),c3(…)” is unreadable. Please check all math displays.
- References are incomplete and not in NeurIPS format (missing years, venues, page numbers; titles truncated; inconsistent capitalization). This must be fixed.
- Prior work coverage: the connection to Plan & Vershynin is appropriate, but related developments via AMP state evolution for square-root LASSO and for general denoisers could be discussed; also clarify how your results relate to Bayati–Montanari (risk of LASSO), and more recent CGMT-based precise analyses (e.g., the conditions under which your Theorem 2.2 recovers those).

Pros
- Clear and potentially impactful equivalence principle; sharp asymptotics via CGMT.
- Novel precise predictions for generalized sparse priors and group LASSO; practical quantizer-design angle.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on a technical assumption with incomplete verification (notably for nuclear norm).
- Clarity/notation issues and several imprecise statements (Theorem 2.1 phrasing, argmin/min).
- Numerous typos/formatting/reference problems.
- Limited empirical validation and unproven monotonicity used in the quantization design rationale.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The conceptual contribution is strong, but the paper in its current form has too many technical, clarity, and presentation issues for NeurIPS. With a careful rewrite (fixing notation, statements, proofs/assumptions), full verification for the nuclear-norm case (or a clearly delimited scope), and stronger empirical validation (including the quantizer design), I would be inclined to support acceptance.
