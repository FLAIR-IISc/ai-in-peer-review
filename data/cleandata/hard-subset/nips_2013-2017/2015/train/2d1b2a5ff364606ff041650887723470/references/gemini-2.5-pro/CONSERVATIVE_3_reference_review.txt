### Summary of the Paper

This paper analyzes the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO algorithm when applied to the problem of structured signal recovery from non-linear measurements. The measurement model, known as a single-index model, takes the form `y_i = g(a_i^T x_0)`, where `g` is a potentially unknown and non-linear link function. The main contribution is a theoretical result showing that, in a high-dimensional asymptotic regime with i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, the mean-squared error (MSE) of the LASSO estimator is precisely equivalent to the MSE of the same estimator on a corresponding linear model with additive Gaussian noise. The parameters of this equivalent linear model—a scaling factor `µ` and a noise variance `σ^2`—are shown to depend only on the statistical properties of the link function `g`. The paper leverages this equivalence to provide exact, deterministic formulas for the asymptotic MSE for a general class of convex regularizers. These general results are then specialized to important cases like sparse (`l1`), group-sparse (`l1,2`), and low-rank (nuclear norm) recovery. The work positions itself as an extension of classical results by Brillinger to the high-dimensional, regularized setting, and as a sharpening of recent non-asymptotic bounds by Plan and Vershynin into exact predictions.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical derivations, which build upon the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, appear sound and represent a non-trivial extension of existing analysis for linear models. The assumptions (Gaussian measurements, specific asymptotic limit, etc.) are clearly stated. The main claim—that LASSO on a non-linear model behaves identically to LASSO on an equivalent linear one—is a powerful and elegant result. The accompanying simulations in Figures 1 and 2 effectively verify the accuracy of the derived theoretical formulas, showing a close match between theory and empirical performance.

However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its evaluation and the scope of its claims. While the theory is validated, the practical utility of using the standard LASSO for this problem is not established. The analysis is performed in isolation, without comparison to any alternative methods. There is a significant body of work on estimation in single-index models and on specific problems like 1-bit compressed sensing, which have developed specialized algorithms. The paper makes no attempt to benchmark the LASSO against these established, problem-specific baselines. Consequently, while we learn that LASSO's performance is *predictable* in this setting, we are given no evidence that its performance is *competitive*. This omission is critical, as it is unclear whether applying a simple, off-the-shelf linear model solver is a methodologically sound choice compared to estimators designed to handle the non-linearity explicitly. Furthermore, the analysis is strictly asymptotic and relies on i.i.d. Gaussian matrices, which are strong idealizations that may not hold in practice.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent motivation and situates the work within the relevant literature. The problem setup and main results (Theorems 2.1, 2.2, and 2.3) are stated with precision. The flow from the general theorem to specific applications in sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery is logical and helps the reader appreciate the breadth of the result.

**Originality:** The paper extends a well-established line of research on the precise analysis of LASSO-type estimators. The core analytical tools (CGMT) are not new, having been used extensively to analyze the linear measurement case. The originality lies in successfully applying this machinery to the non-linear measurement setting and rigorously establishing the equivalence to a linear model. This bridges the gap between the classical work of Brillinger and the modern high-dimensional analysis of LASSO, and provides a more precise characterization than the bounds in recent related work. The result is novel and significant from a theoretical standpoint.

**Significance:** The theoretical significance of the paper is high. It provides a definitive and precise characterization of a standard algorithm in a complex setting, offering a deep understanding of how LASSO processes non-linearities. The result that the complex, non-linear function `g` can be summarized by just two scalar parameters, `µ` and `σ`, is a fundamental insight. The application to optimizing quantizer design is a compelling example of the theory's potential utility.

However, the practical significance is less clear due to the lack of empirical comparison with baseline methods. The paper provides a powerful analytical tool but stops short of demonstrating that the analyzed approach is preferable to existing, specialized techniques. For a practitioner facing a problem with non-linear measurements, this paper provides a formula to predict how LASSO will perform, but it does not provide the guidance needed to choose LASSO over other methods. The endorsement of this approach is therefore premature without more thorough empirical validation against relevant competitors.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a strong, non-trivial, and elegant theoretical result that precisely characterizes the asymptotic performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements.
2.  The paper is well-written, clear, and rigorously establishes the connection to an equivalent linear model, which is a fundamental insight.
3.  The framework is general and applicable to a wide range of structures and regularizers, as demonstrated through several important examples.
4.  The work successfully extends a powerful line of theoretical analysis to a new and important problem domain.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The paper critically lacks any comparison to established baseline methods designed for single-index models or specific non-linear problems (e.g., 1-bit compressed sensing). It validates the theory but fails to demonstrate its practical competitiveness.
2.  The analysis relies on strong, idealized assumptions (asymptotic regime, i.i.d. Gaussian matrices) that may limit the direct relevance of the precise formulas in practical, finite-dimensional settings with structured data.
3.  The practical impact is muted by the absence of evidence showing that applying a standard linear method (LASSO) is a sound approach for these non-linear problems compared to specialized algorithms.

In conclusion, this is a very strong piece of theoretical work that offers a deep and precise analysis. However, its claims of utility are not sufficiently substantiated through comparison with established, relevant methods. The lack of such validation makes it difficult to endorse the use of this approach in practice, despite its theoretical appeal.
