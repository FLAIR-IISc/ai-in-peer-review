Summary
The paper studies generalized LASSO estimators for structured signal recovery under non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where A has i.i.d. N(0,1) entries and g is a (possibly random and unknown) link. Building on Brillinger’s classical result for least squares and on non-asymptotic bounds of Plan and Vershynin, the main contribution is an asymptotically exact characterization of the error of generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. The central theorem shows an equivalence: in the high-dimensional Gaussian design regime, the generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements behaves as if the measurements were linear with effective gain μ = E[γ g(γ)] and noise variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). The paper provides (i) a precise scalar convex optimization (in α,β,τ) whose optimum α* gives lim ‖x̂ − μx0‖2, (ii) specializations to sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) settings, including explicit equations for the sparse case, and (iii) an application to q-bit quantized compressed sensing, showing that minimizing the LASSO estimation error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 and that Lloyd–Max quantizers yield stationary points of this objective. The analysis leverages the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) and extends prior precise-error results for linear measurements to the non-linear setting.

Relation to prior work
- Brillinger (1980s) gave consistency up to a scale under non-linear link for fixed n, m → ∞; this paper covers m/n → δ with general convex regularization and yields exact asymptotic error.
- Plan and Vershynin provided non-asymptotic upper bounds and highlighted μ and σ; this work replaces loose bounds with precise asymptotics and treats the regularized LASSO rather than the constrained version.
- For linear measurements, precise LASSO error characterizations via AMP and CGMT (e.g., Bayati–Montanari; Thrampoulidis–Oymak–Hassibi; Stojnic) are generalized here to the non-linear observation model with broad classes of regularizers.

Quality
The technical development appears careful and sound within the stated assumptions:
- Gaussian i.i.d. design, linear asymptotics m/n → δ, regularizers satisfying normalization and subdifferential bounds, and a technical Assumption 1 ensuring a well-defined limiting functional F via the Moreau envelope of the conjugate regularizer.
- The equivalence to an effective linear model with parameters (μ,σ^2) is rigorously established, and the resulting scalar convex program is well-posed with unique α*.
- Specializations are worked out in detail for sparse and group-sparse settings; the low-rank section outlines the pathway and conditions but leaves evaluation of F for future work.
- Empirical results (figures referenced) match theory across different links and structures, including phase transitions with λcrit in the compressed regime.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The role of μ and σ^2 is made explicit, and the scalar program offers interpretable dependence on δ, λ, f, and the structure distribution. Assumption 1 is abstract; providing concrete verification for common regularizers (ℓ1, group ℓ1,2, nuclear norm) earlier in the main text would improve readability. A brief intuitive sketch connecting CGMT steps to the scalar program would help non-experts. The discussion of the μ=0 case is brief; a short subsection outlining behavior and alternatives would be useful.

Originality
The work appears to be the first to give asymptotically exact error characterizations for generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements, upgrading earlier upper bounds to precise formulas and extending precise analyses from the linear to the non-linear single-index setting. The quantizer-design consequence (Lloyd–Max optimality for minimizing σ^2/μ^2) is a neat and practically relevant byproduct.

Significance
The results are significant both theoretically and practically. They provide:
- A unifying principle: generalized LASSO treats a large class of non-linearities as linear with effective (μ,σ^2).
- Precise guidance for tuning λ and for understanding sample complexity/phase transitions under non-linear sensing.
- A pathway for measurement system design (e.g., quantizer thresholds/levels) via σ^2/μ^2 optimization.
Limitations include the Gaussian design assumption and asymptotic nature; extensions to rotationally invariant or sub-Gaussian designs and finite-sample deviation guarantees would broaden impact.

Strengths
- Clear, general equivalence result reducing non-linear to effective linear measurements.
- Precise scalar characterization with unique solution; explicit sparse-case formulas.
- Coverage of multiple structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank).
- Practical insight into quantizer design; connection to Lloyd–Max.
- Consistent and convincing simulations.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Gaussian i.i.d. design: discuss robustness to sub-Gaussian/rotationally invariant A; even a proposition or empirical study would strengthen conclusions.
- Assumption 1: move verification examples (ℓ1, ℓ1,2, nuclear norm) to the main text with a short proof sketch; clarify conditions under which spectral distributions for low-rank satisfy the needed convergence.
- μ and σ^2 estimation: when g is unknown, include a simple consistent estimator for μ, σ^2 from data and a remark on plug-in performance (e.g., method-of-moments using sample covariances of y and Ax).
- Low-rank case: add at least one concrete numerical example with a specified singular-value distribution to demonstrate evaluation of F and compare to experiments.
- Finite-sample behavior: include a brief section with non-asymptotic concentration around the asymptotic prediction (via CGMT tails) or empirical calibration across n,m.
- Clarify the μ=0 regime: summarize applicable alternatives or modifications (e.g., normalized estimators, different objectives) and relate to [24].

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong theoretical contribution that closes a gap between non-asymptotic bounds and precise asymptotics for non-linear measurement models.
- Broad applicability across structures and links, with actionable design consequences.
- Technically solid and aligned with NIPS interest in high-dimensional inference and convex methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on Gaussian design and asymptotics; limited discussion of robustness.
- Low-rank specialization is less developed.
- Practical issues (estimation of μ,σ^2; finite-sample guarantees) are not addressed in depth.

Overall recommendation
A solid and timely contribution with precise and broadly useful results. With minor additions on robustness, practical estimation of μ,σ^2, and a more concrete low-rank example, the paper would be even stronger.
