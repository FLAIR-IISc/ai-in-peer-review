This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO (GLASSO) when applied to estimating a structured signal from nonlinear measurements. The authors demonstrate that, surprisingly, the GLASSO performs asymptotically as well with nonlinear measurements as it would with appropriately scaled and noisy linear measurements. They derive precise expressions for the estimation error, a significant contribution as previous results were either less precise or focused on constrained versions of the problem. A particularly interesting consequence is the identification of the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error, which turns out to be the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The core result – equivalence of nonlinear and appropriately scaled linear measurements for GLASSO estimation – is novel and conceptually insightful. It bridges work in nonlinear statistics with the increasingly popular GLASSO framework. The theoretical results significantly advance the state-of-the-art by providing precise error characterizations, rather than bounds, in a high-dimensional setting. The application to q-bit compressive sensing, identifying the optimal Lloyd-Max quantizer, highlights the practical implications.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper appears technically rigorous. The derivation of the asymptotic error expression, while likely complex, seems carefully constructed. The authors build upon existing work by Brillinger, Plan & Vershynin, and recent advances in LASSO analysis (e.g., CGMT framework), appropriately citing relevant literature. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the mathematical notation is consistent.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, and the exposition is generally clear. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the contributions. The notation, while dense at times, is consistently used.  The flow from general results to specific applications (sparse recovery, group sparse recovery, low-rank recovery, q-bit CS) is logical.
* **Completeness:** The paper feels complete in its theoretical development. It doesn't just present a result but also explores its implications and connections to existing work. The inclusion of proofs (deferred to the appendix) is standard and expected. The discussion of related literature is comprehensive.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1:**  Assumption 1, relating to the limit of a complex expression involving the Fenchel conjugate, is stated without sufficient intuition or explanation of *why* it’s reasonable to expect it to hold for many common regularizers.  A more detailed justification or a discussion of specific classes of regularizers that demonstrably satisfy this assumption would be beneficial. It feels like a crucial, but opaque, technical condition.
* **Computational Complexity of Theorem 2.3:** While Theorem 2.3 provides a nice specialization for sparse recovery, the discussion of solving for λcrit and κcrit isn’t fully developed. A discussion of the computational challenges involved in solving the equations or suggestion of practical algorithms beyond simply mentioning the Lloyd-Max algorithm would add value.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:**  While Figure 1 is mentioned, it’s not clearly presented within the main body of the paper. A more robust empirical validation of the theoretical results would significantly strengthen the paper. Simulations showing the accuracy of the error predictions across a range of parameters (sparsity levels, noise levels, dimension ratios) would be compelling.
* **Connection to Existing Nonlinear Estimation Literature:** While the authors acknowledge the single-index model literature, the connection could be made more explicit. The paper focuses on the GLASSO framework, but a brief discussion of how the results compare to other nonlinear estimation techniques (e.g., kernel methods, neural networks) could broaden the perspective.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result with clear implications for signal processing and statistical estimation.
* The technical analysis is sound and builds upon a strong foundation of existing work.
* The paper is well-written and organized, making it accessible to researchers in the field.
* The potential impact of the work is high, particularly in applications involving nonlinear measurements (e.g., compressive sensing, quantized data).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Assumption 1 is a potentially strong assumption that lacks sufficient justification.
* The lack of extensive empirical validation weakens the confidence in the practical relevance of the results.
* The computational aspects of applying the results (e.g., finding optimal quantization levels) are not fully addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The novelty and technical soundness of the results outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance**, but encourage the authors to address the concerns regarding Assumption 1 and to provide more empirical validation of their results.  Adding a section illustrating the practical implementation or computational challenges of the proposed method would also strengthen the paper.
