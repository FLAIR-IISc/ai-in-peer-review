This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimating a structured signal from nonlinear measurements. The authors demonstrate, under fairly general conditions, that the estimation error is asymptotically equivalent to that of a linear measurement model with appropriately scaled measurements and noise. This is a significant result, as it justifies the continued use of LASSO-type algorithms even when the underlying measurement process is nonlinear. The paper builds upon previous work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, providing more precise error bounds and a deeper understanding of the role played by the nonlinear link function. A particularly interesting consequence is the identification of the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error.

**Summary of Contributions:** The core contribution is establishing a precise asymptotic equivalence between the estimation error with nonlinear measurements and a corresponding linear model. This is supported by deriving explicit expressions for the error, which depend on the characteristics of the nonlinear function. The authors also demonstrate that the Lloyd-Max quantizer is optimal for minimizing estimation error in certain scenarios. The paper offers a rigorous theoretical analysis with connections to existing literature and provides insightful implications for practical applications like compressive sensing with quantized measurements.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for its results, building upon existing work and extending it with precise asymptotic analysis. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the proofs appear to be well-structured (although detailed verification is beyond the scope of this review).
*   **Novelty:** The derivation of explicit error expressions for the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements is a novel and significant contribution. This allows for a deeper understanding of the impact of nonlinearities on estimation performance.
*   **Practical Implications:** The identification of the Lloyd-Max quantizer as optimal for minimizing estimation error has clear practical implications for compressive sensing and other applications involving quantized measurements.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and provides a clear overview of the related work.
*   **Connections to existing work:** The authors clearly position their work with respect to Brillinger [5] and Plan & Vershynin [17] and highlight the improvements and extensions made in this paper.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumption 1:** The assumption regarding the limit of `n e√n(f∗)(n),c3 (c1h+ c2x0)` is quite strong and may not hold for all relevant scenarios. The paper acknowledges this but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of its implications and potential limitations.  A more intuitive explanation of why this assumption is needed could be helpful.
*   **Computational Complexity of Optimal Quantizer:** While identifying the Lloyd-Max quantizer is theoretically significant, the computational cost of finding the optimal quantizer parameters may be prohibitive in some applications. This point isn't thoroughly discussed.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The figures provided (Figures 1 and 2) offer some validation of the theoretical results but appear somewhat limited in scope. More extensive simulations with varying parameters and different types of signals could strengthen the empirical evidence.
*   **Notation:** The notation can be dense in places, and it requires careful attention to follow all the definitions and relationships. More judicious use of subscripts and superscripts could improve readability.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the performance of LASSO-type algorithms with nonlinear measurements.
*   The results have clear practical implications for applications like compressive sensing and quantized data analysis.
*   The paper is well-written and organized, demonstrating a clear understanding of the relevant literature.
*   The novelty and rigor of the analysis are commendable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumption regarding the limit in Assumption 1 may limit the generality of the results.
*   The lack of extensive empirical validation weakens the confidence in the practical relevance of the findings.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and merits acceptance. The theoretical results are novel and significant, and the practical implications are promising. While the strong assumption and limited empirical validation are weaknesses, they do not overshadow the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the implications and limitations of Assumption 1.
2.  Expand the empirical validation with more comprehensive simulations.
3.  Consider briefly discussing the computational cost associated with finding the optimal Lloyd-Max quantizer and potential strategies for mitigating this cost.
