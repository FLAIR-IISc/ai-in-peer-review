Summary
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under single-index (non-linear) measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0), where A has i.i.d. N(0,1) rows and x0 is structured. The estimator is the (regularized) generalized LASSO min_x ||y − Ax||_2 + λ f(x) with a convex structure-promoting f. The main claim is an asymptotically exact equivalence: for any fixed λ, the LASSO error with non-linear measurements is the same as the error with an effective linear model y = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Beyond this equivalence (Theorem 2.1), the paper provides a precise scalar optimization formula (Theorem 2.2) for the asymptotic squared error lim ||x̂ − μ x0||^2, expressed via a Moreau-envelope functional F that depends on f and the distribution of x0. Specializations are given for sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and a discussion for low-rank (nuclear norm). The framework recovers and generalizes Brillinger’s classic LS result and significantly sharpens recent bounds by Plan & Vershynin for constrained LASSO. As an application, the paper shows that for q-bit quantized measurements, minimizing the LASSO error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 over quantizer parameters, and argues that Lloyd–Max quantization yields stationary points for this objective.

Relation to prior work
- Brillinger (1980s) established consistency of LS up to scale under link misspecification with fixed n, m→∞; this work extends to high-dimensional regimes and to regularized LASSO.
- Plan & Vershynin provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO under single-index models; here those bounds are replaced by precise asymptotics, and the regularized (penalized) LASSO is treated.
- Recent precise analyses of LASSO under linear measurements (AMP-based and CGMT-based) are extended to the non-linear measurement setting by a reduction to an equivalent linear model.
- The specialization to ℓ1 aligns with and generalizes prior precise results for linear noise (e.g., Bayati–Montanari; Thrampoulidis et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: the non-linear-to-linear equivalence with explicit μ, σ is simple and powerful, with broad implications for design (e.g., quantization).
- Technical depth: a general scalar optimization characterization (Theorem 2.2) covering a wide class of convex regularizers and distributions for x0, not only ℓ1.
- Concrete specializations: closed-form characterization for sparse and group-sparse cases; connection to Brillinger’s limit and improved expressions in compressed regimes (δ<1).
- Application to quantized sensing is insightful; the reduction to σ^2/μ^2 is elegant and practically useful, with a principled rationale for Lloyd–Max.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumption 1 (existence of F from the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate) is crucial yet opaque. While it is verified for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2 with common x0 models, the scope and minimal conditions could be clarified, especially beyond i.i.d. coordinates (e.g., structured correlated x0 or deterministic x0).
- Gaussian A is essential for the CGMT-based machinery; it would be valuable to discuss robustness to subGaussian/isotropic designs or to provide counterexamples/limitations.
- Theorem 2.1 statement suggests almost sure equality of errors; strictly, it should assert equality of asymptotic limits (or convergence of the difference to zero). Please clarify the mode of convergence.
- Treatment of μ=0 (e.g., even link functions) is excluded. While acknowledged, it would help to delineate what part of Theorem 2.2 survives (e.g., directional consistency) and whether a modified estimator could be analyzed.
- Low-rank case: conditions ensuring existence and characterization of F via singular-value distributions are only sketched; no numeric validation is provided. Given the significance of matrix problems, a worked example would strengthen the paper.
- Notational glitches and minor typos hinder readability in places (see below).

Quality
The claims are well-supported: the reduction to an effective linear model is proved, the scalar variational formula is consistent with known special cases, and numerical experiments (for sparse and group-sparse) reportedly match predictions. The link to Brillinger and sharpening of Plan–Vershynin are convincing. More extensive empirical validation for diverse g (e.g., rectified, saturating, probit/logit) would further bolster generality.

Clarity
Overall clear but dense. The introduction and related work are well-situated. The definition and role of F and Assumption 1 would benefit from more intuition (e.g., brief derivations for ℓ1, nuclear norm). Some notational inconsistencies (x0 vs x̄0, Ax0 vs Ax) should be fixed.

Originality
High. The precise asymptotic equivalence for generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements and the general error formula appear new and fill a gap between earlier upper bounds and exact linear-model analyses.

Significance
High. The result informs practice (e.g., quantizer/measurement design), provides theoretical unification, and enables precise performance prediction across structured settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 2.1’s convergence statement and assumptions on g (e.g., finite second moments, independence across i, randomness vs deterministic g).
- Expand on Assumption 1: provide sufficient conditions and examples beyond i.i.d. coordinates; discuss deterministic x0 regimes.
- Add experiments for nontrivial link functions (e.g., Tobit/censoring, ReLU, saturating quantizers) and for the low-rank case.
- Discuss estimation of μ, σ when g is unknown: simple moment-based estimators from (y, A) and their consistency in high dimensions.
- Comment on extensions to subGaussian A and to the square-loss LASSO variant 1/2||y−Ax||_2^2 + λ f(x).
- For the quantization application, include numerical optimization details and comparisons between Lloyd–Max and alternative designs under the σ^2/μ^2 criterion.

Pros
- Elegant and broadly applicable equivalence principle; precise asymptotics.
- General framework covering multiple regularizers; matches and extends prior precise results.
- Practical design insight for quantized sensing.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on Gaussian designs; limited discussion of robustness.
- Opaque technical assumption (Assumption 1) and incomplete low-rank development.
- Some clarity/notation issues; limited empirical breadth for non-linear g.

Minor comments and typos
- Abstract/Section 1: “x̂ := argminx ‖y − Ax0‖2 + λf(x)” should be Ax, not Ax0.
- Use consistent notation for Euclidean norm vs its square (||·|| vs ||·||_2^2).
- The normalization and reuse of x0 for both random and normalized vectors is confusing; consider distinct symbols.
- Theorem 2.1: specify “lim_{n→∞} ||x̂−μx0||^2 = lim_{n→∞} ||x̂_lin−μx0||^2” (or convergence in probability/a.s. of the difference to zero).
- Check footnotes numbering and references (e.g., [11, Sec. 2.2]).

Overall recommendation: accept. The work is timely, technically solid, and impactful, with clear advances over prior art. Addressing clarity and breadth of experiments would further improve it.
