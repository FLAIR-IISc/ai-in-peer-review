This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional, structured signal from non-linear measurements. Specifically, it analyzes the performance of the Generalized LASSO estimator when applied to measurements of the form `y_i = g(a_i^T x_0)`, where `g` is a potentially unknown non-linear link function. The central and surprising result is that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime, the performance of this model-mismatched estimator is precisely equivalent to the performance of the same estimator on an effective linear model `y_i = μa_i^T x_0 + σz_i`. The parameters `μ` and `σ` of this equivalent model depend only on the link function `g`.

This work builds upon and significantly extends a line of research initiated by Brillinger for unregularized least-squares in a classical, low-dimensional setting, and more recently advanced by Plan and Vershynin, who provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO formulation. The key contribution here is the move from performance bounds to an asymptotically exact characterization of the mean-squared error. The paper provides a general formula for this error as the solution to a simple scalar optimization problem and demonstrates its application to important special cases, including sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank signal recovery. Finally, the utility of this precise characterization is demonstrated in an application to optimal quantizer design for compressed sensing.

**Strengths:**
*   The primary strength of the paper is its main theoretical result: the establishment of an exact asymptotic equivalence between the LASSO with non-linear measurements and one with a corresponding linear model. This is a fundamental and powerful insight that simplifies the analysis of a complex problem, connecting it to a rich body of literature on the precise analysis of LASSO in the linear case.
*   The work is technically deep and provides precise, verifiable predictions for the estimation error. The provided simulation results (Figures 1 and 2) show a remarkable agreement between the theoretical predictions and empirical performance, validating the soundness of the asymptotic analysis.
*   The framework presented is general and applicable to a wide range of convex regularizers and non-linear link functions, making the results broadly relevant. The specific analyses for `l1`, group-`l1,2`, and nuclear norm regularization cover the most common use-cases for structured signal recovery.
*   The application to optimal q-bit quantizer design is compelling. It shows how the theoretical result can be translated into a practical design principle, reducing a complex optimization over the estimator's performance to a much simpler problem of optimizing the ratio `σ^2/μ^2`. The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is particularly insightful.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is of high quality, but could be strengthened further with a few clarifications. These are suggestions for minor revisions and do not detract from the core contribution.
*   The technical derivation relies on Assumption 1. While this assumption is shown to hold for several important regularizers, a more intuitive discussion of its role and scope would be beneficial. Clarifying what this assumption entails and whether there are common structured-sparsity regularizers for which it might be difficult to verify would help the reader better understand the applicability of the main theorems.
*   The section on low-rank matrix recovery (2.2.4) is slightly less detailed than the preceding sections. It posits that the results apply if the empirical singular value distribution of a certain random matrix converges. While this is a standard assumption in the field, explicitly citing the relevant results from random matrix theory that support this convergence for common low-rank matrix models would make the claims in this section more self-contained and rigorous.
*   The analysis requires that the parameter `μ` be non-zero, as noted in Footnote 3. This excludes certain link functions, such as even functions (e.g., `g(x) = |x|`). It would be helpful to include a brief discussion in the main text on the significance of this limitation. For instance, does the LASSO estimator fail catastrophically when `μ=0`, or does the problem require a different analytical approach? This would help to better frame the contribution of the present work.

**Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The technical quality of the paper appears to be very high. The claims are substantial and are supported by what seems to be a rigorous mathematical analysis (deferring proofs to the appendix is standard). The experimental validation is excellent and convincingly demonstrates the accuracy of the theoretical formulae.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly articulates the problem setting, its relation to prior art, and the specific contributions. The main ideas are communicated effectively, making a complex technical topic accessible.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it operates within an established line of research, the main result—moving from upper bounds to an exact asymptotic characterization for the regularized LASSO with non-linearities—is a novel and significant leap forward. It provides a definitive answer to the question of how and why the LASSO works in this setting.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. They offer a fundamental theoretical understanding of a widely used class of estimators in a challenging but practical setting. Researchers and practitioners working on problems involving quantization, saturation, or other sensor non-linearities are likely to use these ideas. The precise performance characterization can serve as a benchmark for new algorithms and can guide system design, as elegantly demonstrated by the quantizer design application. The work meaningfully advances the state of the art in the high-dimensional analysis of statistical estimators.
