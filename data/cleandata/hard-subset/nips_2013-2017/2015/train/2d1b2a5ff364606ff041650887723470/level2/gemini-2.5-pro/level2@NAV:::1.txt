This paper studies the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal from non-linear measurements using the Generalized LASSO estimator. The main contribution is a powerful and elegant theoretical result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit, the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is precisely equivalent to its performance with an associated linear model corrupted by Gaussian noise. The paper provides an explicit characterization of the mean-squared error as the solution to a simple, low-dimensional convex program. This work builds upon and significantly extends prior results. While Brillinger established a similar principle for unregularized least-squares in a classical, fixed-dimension setting, and Plan & Vershynin provided non-asymptotic error bounds for a constrained LASSO, this paper delivers the first asymptotically exact error characterization for the widely used regularized LASSO in the high-dimensional regime.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Impact:** The central equivalence theorem is a highly significant contribution. It provides a simple, intuitive, and powerful tool for understanding and analyzing a complex problem. It allows researchers to leverage the vast body of work on LASSO with linear measurements to understand settings with non-linearities, which are common in practice (e.g., quantization, saturation). The application to optimal quantizer design, which surprisingly connects the LASSO estimation error to the classical Lloyd-Max quantizer, is a compelling demonstration of the result's utility.
*   **Quality and Originality:** The paper is technically sound and the theoretical claims are rigorously established. The derivation of a precise, deterministic formula for the estimation error is a novel and substantial advance over existing upper bounds. The theoretical predictions are convincingly validated by numerical simulations that show a perfect match.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and does an excellent job of situating the contributions within the context of related literature. The core ideas are communicated effectively.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The analysis relies on standard but strong assumptions for this line of work, namely the asymptotic regime (`m, n -> ∞` with a fixed ratio) and an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix. The practical applicability of the results for finite dimensions or other matrix ensembles is not established.
*   While the error formula is applied successfully to several common structures (sparse, group-sparse), its computation for more general structures depends on evaluating a limit (`F`) that may not be straightforward.

Overall, this is an outstanding theoretical paper. The strengths far outweigh the minor limitations, which are inherent to this style of analysis. The central result is both fundamental and practical, making a significant and original contribution to the field.
