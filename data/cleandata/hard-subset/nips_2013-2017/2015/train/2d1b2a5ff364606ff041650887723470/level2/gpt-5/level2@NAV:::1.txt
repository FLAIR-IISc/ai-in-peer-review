Summary: The paper studies generalized LASSO under single-index, non-linear measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) with i.i.d. Gaussian design A and structured x0. Building on Brillinger (LS under link violations) and Plan & Vershynin (constrained LASSO with bounds), the authors prove an asymptotically exact equivalence: generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements behaves as if the data were linear with an effective gain μ = E[γ g(γ)] and noise variance σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2]. Using the CGMT framework, they provide a precise high-dimensional error formula via a scalar convex program, and specialize it to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank settings, recovering and extending prior precise analyses known for linear models (AMP/CGMT lines of work at NIPS). A notable application is q-bit compressive sensing: minimizing LASSO estimation error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, for which they argue Lloyd–Max quantization yields stationary points.

Quality: The technical approach is sound and timely. The extension from bounds to precise asymptotics for non-linear measurements, with general convex regularizers, is significant. Assumption 1 (limit of Moreau-envelope expressions) is reasonable in standard models but should be better motivated. Proofs are deferred yet consistent with prior CGMT analyses. Experiments (as described) align with theory, though broader validation would help.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear, but has typos and some notational inconsistencies (e.g., normalization of x0, role of μ, σ when g is unknown). The meaning of the “non-linear = linear” equality should be emphasized as an error-level equivalence in the high-dimensional limit.

Originality: High. To my knowledge, this is the first precise characterization of generalized LASSO under non-linear single-index measurements across a broad class of regularizers.

Significance: High. The results inform estimator choice, λ tuning, and quantizer design in practical systems with nonlinear sensing/quantization.

Limitations and suggestions:
- The Gaussian i.i.d. design is restrictive; discuss extensions to sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant ensembles.
- Address the μ = 0 case more fully (e.g., dithering or alternative estimators).
- Provide guidance on estimating μ and σ from data when g is unknown (method-of-moments/calibration), and study robustness to misspecification.
- Include finite-sample experiments with non-Gaussian designs and real datasets.
- Make F(c1,c2,c3) explicit for nuclear norm under standard spectral models; add a short proof sketch for the Lloyd–Max stationary-point claim.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution with precise predictions.
- Broad applicability to structured estimation and quantizer design.
- Clear connections and improvements over prior NIPS work (AMP/CGMT, Plan–Vershynin).

Cons against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on Gaussian design and asymptotics.
- Some assumptions opaque; limited empirical breadth.
- Clarity/notation issues and minor typos.
