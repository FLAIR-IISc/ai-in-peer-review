- Problem: Estimating a high-dimensional structured signal x0 from non-linear measurements y_i = g(a_i^T x0) with i.i.d. Gaussian sensing vectors.

- Main contribution: Equivalence principle showing asymptotic LASSO error equals that for linear model y_i = μ a_i^T x0 + σ z_i with μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ)-μγ)^2].

- Analysis: Uses CGMT to derive exact asymptotic formulas for MSE ||x̂ - μx0||^2 as a function of m/n, λ, μ, σ^2, and signal/regularizer structure.

- Specializations: Provides concrete results for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery cases.

- Application: Reduces optimal q-bit quantizer design to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 and links solution to the Lloyd–Max quantizer.

- Impact: Extends precise asymptotic LASSO analysis from linear to non-linear measurement settings, bridging prior non-asymptotic and fixed-dimension work.

- Significance: Simple, powerful message that many non-linearities reduce to two scalar parameters under Gaussian sensing, with broad potential applications.

- Technical quality: Rigorous derivations leveraging CGMT and strong empirical validation via simulations.

- Originality: First precise asymptotic error characterizations for regularized LASSO with general non-linear measurements and a novel connection to quantizer design.

- Clarity: Well-written and well-structured presentation, progressing logically from theory to applications.

- Limitation: Analysis assumes i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, limiting applicability to structured or non-Gaussian ensembles.

- Accessibility: Key Assumption 1 invokes advanced convex-analysis concepts (Fenchel conjugate, Moreau envelope) and could use more intuition for non-expert readers.

- Incomplete part: Low-rank matrix recovery section is less rigorous, relying on unproven singular-value convergence and deferring details.

- Suggested fixes: Cite random matrix theory results to justify low-rank claims and add a concluding discussion on limitations and future directions (e.g., structured matrices, universality).

- Overall verdict: Strong, influential paper with minor weaknesses; recommended for acceptance.
