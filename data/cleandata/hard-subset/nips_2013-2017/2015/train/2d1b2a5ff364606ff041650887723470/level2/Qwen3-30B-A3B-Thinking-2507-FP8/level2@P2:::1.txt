

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to non-linear measurement models of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), where the link function g is arbitrary. The authors establish that, asymptotically as m,n → ∞ with m/n = δ, the estimation error of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is equivalent to that of a linear measurement model with appropriately scaled and noisy measurements. Specifically, they prove that ‖x̂ - μx0‖2 is asymptotically equal to the error of the LASSO with measurements yi = μai^T x0 + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)2] for γ ~ N(0,1). This result generalizes and strengthens previous work by Plan and Vershynin (2015), who only provided non-asymptotic upper bounds.

The paper's main contribution is Theorem 2.2, which provides a precise asymptotic expression for the estimation error as the solution to a convex optimization program. The authors validate their results with numerical experiments in Figures 1-2, showing excellent agreement between predicted and actual errors across various measurement models and regularizers. They also apply their results to derive optimal quantization thresholds for q-bit compressive sensing, showing that the optimal quantizer corresponds to the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

The paper is technically sound and builds on the CGMT framework, which has been successfully applied in recent NIPS papers for precise error analysis of LASSO-type estimators. The authors clearly situate their work in relation to prior literature, particularly the work of Brillinger (1982) and Plan and Vershynin (2015), and correctly identify how their results extend previous results by providing precise error expressions rather than loose upper bounds. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical results to applications.

The main strengths of the paper are:
1. The precise characterization of estimation error for non-linear measurements, which fills an important gap in the literature
2. The elegant equivalence result that connects non-linear measurements to scaled linear measurements
3. The practical application to optimal quantizer design, which demonstrates the significance of the theoretical results
4. The thorough treatment of multiple structured signal models (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank)

A few minor weaknesses:
1. The paper assumes μ ≠ 0 throughout, which excludes important cases like binary measurements where μ = 0 (e.g., g(x) = sign(x)). While the authors note this limitation, they don't discuss how to handle these cases, which could be important for practical applications.
2. The asymptotic regime (m,n → ∞ with m/n = δ) is standard for this line of work, but the paper doesn't provide concrete bounds on the rate of convergence to the asymptotic behavior, which would be helpful for practitioners.
3. The connection between the Lloyd-Max algorithm and the optimal quantizer design is mentioned but not fully developed in the main text, requiring readers to consult Appendix C for details.

The significance of the paper is high, as it provides a theoretical foundation for using the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, which is common in applications like 1-bit quantization. The equivalence result simplifies the analysis of non-linear measurement models and provides practical insights for designing optimal quantizers. The precise error expressions could also help with hyperparameter tuning in real-world applications.

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS as it makes a significant theoretical contribution to a fundamental problem in high-dimensional statistics. The results are clearly stated, the proofs are rigorous (though deferred to the appendix), and the connection to prior work is well-explained. The authors have made a compelling case for the novelty and significance of their results.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding a brief discussion of the μ = 0 case (e.g., binary measurements) and how the results might need to be adapted
2. Providing a more concrete discussion of the rate of convergence to the asymptotic behavior
3. Expanding the main text to include a more detailed explanation of the connection between the Lloyd-Max algorithm and optimal quantization, rather than deferring this to the appendix

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. The precise error characterization and the practical implications for quantizer design make this paper significant for both theoretical and applied researchers. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.
