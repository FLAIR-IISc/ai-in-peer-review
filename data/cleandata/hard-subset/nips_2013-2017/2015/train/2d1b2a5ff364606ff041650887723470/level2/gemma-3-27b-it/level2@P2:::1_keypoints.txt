- Core contribution: GLASSO with nonlinear measurements performs asymptotically as well as with appropriately scaled noisy linear measurements, with precise error formulas.  
- Practical implication: Identifies the Lloyd–Max quantizer as the optimal q-bit quantizer for minimizing estimation error.  
- Novelty & significance: Bridges nonlinear statistics and GLASSO, providing precise high-dimensional error characterizations rather than loose bounds.  
- Technical soundness: Derivations appear rigorous, build on prior work (Brillinger, Plan & Vershynin, CGMT), with clear assumptions and consistent notation.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-organized, motivated, and flows logically from general results to specific applications.  
- Completeness: Theoretical development is thorough, includes proofs and comprehensive related-work discussion.  
- Weakness: Assumption 1 is presented without sufficient intuition or examples of regularizers that satisfy it.  
- Weakness: Theorem 2.3’s computation of λcrit and κcrit lacks discussion of numerical methods and computational challenges.  
- Weakness: Empirical validation is limited (Figure 1 not clearly presented); more simulations across sparsity, noise, and dimension regimes are needed.  
- Weakness: Connections to broader nonlinear estimation literature (e.g., kernel methods, neural nets) are underdeveloped.  
- Argument for acceptance: Novel, significant theoretical result with clear signal-processing and statistical implications.  
- Argument for acceptance: Sound technical analysis and accessible exposition increase impact and readability.  
- Argument against acceptance: Critical Assumption 1 may be strong and insufficiently justified.  
- Argument against acceptance: Insufficient empirical evidence weakens practical confidence.  
- Argument against acceptance: Practical/computational aspects (e.g., finding optimal quantization) are not fully addressed.  
- Overall assessment: Strong paper recommended for acceptance, contingent on addressing Assumption 1, adding empirical validation, and discussing implementation/computational issues.
