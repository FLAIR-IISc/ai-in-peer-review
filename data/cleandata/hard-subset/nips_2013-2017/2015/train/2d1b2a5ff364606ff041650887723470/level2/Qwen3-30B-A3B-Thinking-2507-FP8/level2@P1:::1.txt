

This paper investigates the behavior of the Generalized LASSO estimator when applied to non-linear measurement models of the form y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0), where g_i are independent copies of a link function g. The authors establish a remarkable equivalence between the estimation performance of the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements and a corresponding linear measurement model with appropriately scaled measurements and noise. Specifically, they show that under the assumption of Gaussian measurement matrix entries, the estimation error ‖x̂ - μx_0‖_2^2 for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to the error in a linear measurement model with y_lin = A(μx_0) + σz, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2] with γ ~ N(0,1).

The main contribution is Theorem 2.2, which provides a precise asymptotic expression for the estimation error as the solution to a convex optimization program. This extends previous work by Brillinger (who considered the unregularized case) and Plan & Vershynin (who provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained version of the LASSO). The authors demonstrate that their result holds for various structured signal models (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and provide explicit error formulas for each case.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. The theoretical contribution is significant and well-motivated, extending previous results to provide precise asymptotic error expressions rather than loose upper bounds.
2. The paper establishes a meaningful connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer for optimal quantization in q-bit compressive sensing, providing a concrete application of the theory.
3. The results are thoroughly validated through numerical experiments (Figures 1-2), showing excellent agreement between theoretical predictions and empirical results.
4. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough discussion of related work and careful specification of assumptions.

The main weaknesses are:
1. The paper assumes ‖x_0‖_2 = 1 throughout, which simplifies the presentation but might be restrictive for practical applications where the signal norm is unknown. While the authors mention in Section 1.1 that the results can be generalized to cases where ‖x_0‖_2 ≠ 1, a brief discussion of the practical implications of this assumption would strengthen the paper.
2. The paper refers to "Assumption 1" as a requirement for the general result, but it's not entirely clear how restrictive this assumption is beyond the specific cases they analyze (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank). Providing more intuition about when this assumption holds would be helpful for readers.
3. While the paper mentions that some of the error formulae are novel even for linear measurements, it would be valuable to explicitly state which specific formulae differ from existing results in the linear measurement literature (e.g., [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 21]).

The significance of this work is substantial, as it provides a theoretical foundation for using LASSO-type estimators in settings with non-linear measurements, which is common in many practical applications. The precise error characterization will be valuable for practitioners designing systems with non-linear measurement devices, as it provides a principled way to understand and optimize the trade-offs between measurement non-linearities and estimation performance.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and careful attention to the asymptotic regime. The results are a meaningful contribution to the literature on structured signal recovery, extending our understanding of LASSO performance beyond the standard linear measurement model. The connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates a concrete application of the theory with practical implications for quantizer design.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field. The results are well-motivated, technically sound, and have clear practical implications. The paper is clearly written and makes a compelling case for its novelty and significance. The minor weaknesses noted above are easily addressable and do not detract from the overall quality of the work.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents an important extension of previous work on LASSO with non-linear measurements and provides precise asymptotic error expressions that will be valuable for both theoretical understanding and practical applications. The paper's contribution to the literature is substantial, and the results are likely to be widely cited and built upon in future work.
