This paper addresses the problem of estimating a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`, where `g` is a potentially unknown and random non-linear function. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, `x̂ = argmin ||y - Ax||_2 + λf(x)`, which is ostensibly designed for linear models.

The main contribution is a precise, asymptotic characterization of the estimation error. The central and most elegant result is that the performance of the LASSO in this non-linear setting is asymptotically identical to its performance on an equivalent linear model with measurements `y_i = μ(a_i^T x0) + σz_i`. Here, `z_i` are i.i.d. standard Gaussian noise, and the effective signal scaling `μ` and noise level `σ` are determined solely by the non-linear function `g` via `μ = E[γg(γ)]` and `σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2]`, where `γ` is a standard normal random variable.

This work builds upon and significantly strengthens two lines of prior research. It extends the classical result of Brillinger, which showed a similar phenomenon for unregularized least squares in a fixed-dimension setting, to the modern high-dimensional, regularized setting. It also refines the recent work of Plan and Vershynin, which provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO variant, by replacing those bounds (which contain unknown constants) with exact asymptotic expressions for the more common penalized LASSO formulation. The authors provide a general formula for the asymptotic mean-squared error (Theorem 2.2) and specialize it to important cases like sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank signal recovery. As an application, they show how their theory can be used to optimize the design of q-bit quantizers, connecting the problem to the classical Lloyd-Max algorithm.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are strong and appear to be sound. The main equivalence result is powerful and provides deep insight. A particular strength is the experimental validation; the plots in Figures 1 and 2 show a strikingly accurate match between the derived asymptotic formulae and the results of finite-dimensional simulations, lending strong credence to the analysis. The authors are careful to state their assumptions (e.g., i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix, specific asymptotic regime) clearly.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem, framing the contributions, and situating the work within the context of relevant literature. The main results are stated clearly and upfront in Section 2. The specialization to different recovery scenarios (sparse, group-sparse) makes the general theory concrete and accessible.

One area for potential improvement would be to provide more intuition behind the proof of the main theorems in the body of the paper. The proofs are deferred to the appendix, which is standard, but a short, high-level sketch of how the non-linearity is handled to produce the equivalent linear model would be very helpful for the reader. For instance, explaining which step in the analysis (presumably based on the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem) leads to the emergence of the `μ` and `σ` parameters would strengthen the paper's narrative.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original. While it leverages a powerful analytical framework (CGMT) that has been recently developed for the analysis of LASSO with linear measurements, its application to the single-index model with general non-linearities is novel and impactful. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide precise, asymptotically exact error characterizations for LASSO in this setting. The established equivalence principle is a fundamentally new insight that simplifies a complex problem in a surprising and elegant way.

**Significance:** The results are very significant and likely to be influential. The problem of dealing with non-linearities in measurement systems is of great practical and theoretical importance. The paper's main finding—that one can analyze this complex system by simply studying an equivalent linear one—is a powerful conceptual tool. It makes the performance of LASSO under non-linearities predictable and understandable. Researchers and practitioners can now use existing results for the linear LASSO to predict performance and tune parameters for a much wider class of problems. The application to optimal quantizer design is a compelling demonstration of the practical utility of these theoretical insights. This work significantly advances our understanding of regularized estimation in high dimensions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a fundamental and elegant equivalence principle, showing that LASSO with non-linear measurements behaves like LASSO with a specific, equivalent linear model.
2.  Delivers the first asymptotically exact performance characterization for the Generalized LASSO in this setting, moving far beyond previous upper bounds.
3.  The theoretical predictions are impressively validated by numerical simulations.
4.  The results have direct practical implications, as demonstrated by the novel application to optimal quantizer design.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The analysis relies on the standard but restrictive assumption of i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices. A brief discussion on the challenges or possibilities of extending this to more general matrix ensembles would be welcome.
2.  The section on low-rank matrix recovery is slightly less developed than the other examples, leaving some details to future work.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, significant, and elegant theoretical result with clear practical implications. The strengths substantially outweigh the minor weaknesses. It represents a major contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing.
