

This paper makes an important contribution by extending precise asymptotic characterization of the l2-lasso (square-root lasso) to single-index models with non-linear measurements, while relaxing the Gaussian prior assumption on the signal x0. The authors establish that non-linear measurements yi = gi(ai^T x0) can be asymptotically treated as scaled and noisy linear measurements yi = μai^T x0 + σzi, where μ and σ are determined by the link function g. This extends the work of Plan and Vershynin (2015) from loose upper bounds to precise error predictions, and provides explicit expressions for the estimation error that hold for all λ and for various structured signals (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank). The results are theoretically significant as they represent the first-known precise asymptotic analysis in this context.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with thorough treatment of the theoretical foundations. The authors effectively connect their work to previous NIPS contributions, particularly building upon the recent advances in precise LASSO analysis using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework.

However, the main-text clarity requires improvement. The crucial equivalence between single-index models and linear models with scaled signals and non-standard noise (Brillinger 1982) is only presented in Appendix A, yet the paper references this equivalence in the main text on line 140 without proper justification. This should be moved to the main text to strengthen the argument.

Additionally, the practical relevance section needs elaboration. Since the l2-lasso's oracle regularization parameter does not depend on noise variance, the authors should address whether their results support preferring l2-lasso over l2^2-lasso in practice. This is particularly relevant given the paper's focus on practical applications like q-bit compressive sensing.

The work demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous proofs and extensive experimental validation. The significance is high as the results provide a unified theoretical framework for understanding LASSO performance under non-linear measurements, with direct implications for designing optimal quantizers (as demonstrated in the q-bit application). The originality is clear through the precise characterization of error in the non-linear measurement regime, which extends previous work significantly. Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in high-dimensional statistics.
