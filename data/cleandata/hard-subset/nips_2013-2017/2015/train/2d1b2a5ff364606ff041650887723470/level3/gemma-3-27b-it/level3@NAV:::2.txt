## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper investigates the Generalized LASSO for estimating signals from non-linear measurements, building upon the work of Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin. The core contribution lies in providing *precise* asymptotic characterizations of the estimation error, extending previous results that were either less accurate or limited to specific settings. The authors demonstrate that the non-linear measurement problem can be effectively treated as a scaled and noisy linear problem, providing a valuable theoretical framework. This has a practical consequence, demonstrated with q-bit compressive sensing, where it justifies the use of the Lloyd-Max quantizer for optimal performance.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the presentation is logical. The authors appropriately connect their work to existing literature in statistics, econometrics, and signal processing. The inclusion of numerical results (Figures 1 & 2) is a strength, supporting the theoretical findings. The discussion of the connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer is a nice application of the theoretical result, highlighting its practical relevance. 

One weakness is that the crucial equivalence between the single-index model and a linear model with scaled noise (Brillinger 1982) is relegated to Appendix A. While technically correct, mentioning this equivalence in the main text—specifically around line 140—would significantly improve the paper's flow and justification for key arguments. This would make the reasoning more accessible to readers less familiar with the single-index model literature.

Regarding practical relevance, while the paper shows the asymptotic equivalence to a linear model, it does *not* directly address whether the l2-lasso (square-root lasso) is preferable to the l2^2-lasso in practice, given that the oracle regularization parameter is independent of noise variance. Addressing this point, perhaps with a brief discussion or simulation, would further strengthen the paper's impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** Provides precise asymptotic error characterization, filling a gap in the literature.
*   **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is easy to follow and well-organized.
*   **Practical Implications:** The result has demonstrable relevance, exemplified by the application to q-bit compressive sensing and justification for Lloyd-Max quantization.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The mathematical analysis appears rigorous and builds upon established foundations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Minor Clarity Issue:** The crucial equivalence to a linear model should be highlighted in the main text.
*   **Limited Practical Discussion:** Could benefit from a discussion regarding the practical preference of l2-lasso over l2^2-lasso.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The minor weaknesses are easily addressable, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper is a valuable addition to the literature on sparse recovery and non-linear estimation.
