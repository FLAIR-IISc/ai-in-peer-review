## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to measurements corrupted by non-linear link functions. The core contribution lies in extending precise asymptotic characterizations of the l2-lasso (square-root lasso) to single-index models, relaxing the typical Gaussian prior assumption on the signal. The authors demonstrate, building on the work of Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, that the non-linear measurement problem can be effectively recast as a linear one with scaled signals and non-standard noise, allowing for a precise analysis of the estimation error. 

The work is well-written and logically structured. The authors clearly motivate the problem, connect it to existing literature in statistics, econometrics, and signal processing, and present their results with mathematical rigor. The inclusion of illustrative figures (mentioned in the text) is a positive aspect, and the discussion of practical implications, such as optimal quantization, is valuable. The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work, particularly the Brillinger’s work on single-index models and the more recent contributions of Plan and Vershynin on constrained LASSO. It also appropriately references relevant literature on sparse recovery and compressed sensing.

However, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the equivalence between the single-index model and a scaled linear model with non-standard noise. Currently, this crucial connection is relegated to Appendix A, and a brief mention in the main text (as suggested by the supervisor) would significantly enhance clarity, particularly for readers less familiar with the Brillinger (1982) result. Specifically, referencing this equivalence around line 140 would justify the subsequent blockquote.

Regarding practical relevance, the authors correctly highlight that the l2-lasso’s oracle regularization parameter is independent of the noise variance. However, the paper doesn’t fully address whether their theoretical results *support* preferring the l2-lasso over the l2^2-lasso in practice. A more direct comparison, or at least a discussion of scenarios where the derived insights would favor one over the other, would strengthen the paper’s impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong theoretical contribution:** Provides precise asymptotic characterizations for a practically relevant problem.
*   **Well-written and clear:** The paper is easy to follow and logically organized.
*   **Novelty:** Extends existing results to a more general setting and provides new insights into the behavior of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements.
*   **Significance:** The results have potential applications in areas such as compressive sensing with quantized measurements and non-linear regression.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity issue:** The crucial equivalence between single-index and scaled linear models is not adequately highlighted in the main text.
*   **Limited practical guidance:** The paper doesn't fully leverage the theoretical results to provide concrete recommendations for practical algorithm selection (l2-lasso vs. l2^2-lasso).



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issue and expand on the practical implications of the results. The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and is of high quality.
