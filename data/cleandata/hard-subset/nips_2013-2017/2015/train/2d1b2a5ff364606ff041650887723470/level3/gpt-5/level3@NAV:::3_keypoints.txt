- Paper analyzes generalized Lasso under non-linear measurements yi = g_i(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian design and proves an asymptotic equivalence to a linear model with effective parameters μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2].  
- Derives precise asymptotic squared-error formulas for penalized generalized Lasso with broad convex regularizers, covering Lasso, group Lasso, and nuclear norm.  
- Empirically validates theoretical predictions across sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank settings and presents a quantizer-design application showing optimizing σ^2/μ^2 recovers a Lloyd–Max-type solution.  
- Technically solid and carefully executed; results sharpen prior bounds and unify several special cases.  
- Paper is generally well written but unbalanced: core ideas are clear while essential technical details (e.g., the role of F in Theorem 2.2) are pushed to appendices.  
- Terminology/notation fixes requested: call generalized Lasso a convex program/decoder (not an “algorithm”), change the “proximal function” label since it is the Moreau envelope, and streamline notation to link to AMP/CGMT literature.  
- Formalization request: Assumption 1 should be presented with a variational-analysis interpretation (e.g., epi-convergence/Moreau envelope limits).  
- Minor edits: fix Figure 1 caption formatting ($n=768$), correct typo on p.5 line 247, and explicitly comment on equivalence between penalized and constrained formulations via a parameter–radius mapping.  
- Originality: high — extends the Plan–Vershynin program from bounds to explicit asymptotic formulas, including new cases like group and nuclear-norm regularization.  
- Significance: high — the equivalence principle and closed-form performance characterization have substantial theoretical and practical impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: precise novel theory, broad applicability, strong empirical validation, and impactful quantizer-design application.  
- Arguments against acceptance: results restricted to Gaussian designs and μ ≠ 0, Assumption 1 needs clearer formalization, and presentation imbalances due to space constraints.
