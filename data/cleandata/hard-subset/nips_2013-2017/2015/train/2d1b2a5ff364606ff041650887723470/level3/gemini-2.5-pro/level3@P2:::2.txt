This paper studies the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`, where `g` is a potentially unknown non-linear link function. This setup, known as the single-index model, includes important special cases like 1-bit compressed sensing. The authors analyze the performance of the Generalized `l2`-LASSO (or square-root LASSO) estimator, which naively treats the measurements as if they were linear.

The main contribution is a precise, asymptotic characterization of the estimator's mean-squared error (MSE). Building on the classical work of Brillinger and more recent results by Plan and Vershynin, the authors rigorously prove that in the high-dimensional limit, the performance of the `l2`-LASSO with non-linear measurements is equivalent to its performance on a corresponding linear model with additive Gaussian noise: `y_i = mu * a_i^T x0 + sigma * z_i`. The scaling factor `mu` and effective noise variance `sigma^2` are determined solely by the link function `g`. The paper provides an explicit formula for the asymptotic MSE as the solution to a simple, three-dimensional convex optimization problem. This result is a significant extension of recent work on the precise analysis of LASSO, which had been limited to linear measurement models. The analysis is general, applying to a wide class of convex regularizers (l1, group-l1, nuclear norm) and relaxing the common assumption of a Gaussian prior on the signal `x0`.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper establishes a powerful and elegant equivalence result. Proving that the simple LASSO algorithm, when applied to a complex non-linear measurement model, behaves exactly like it would in a specific, well-understood linear model is a fundamental contribution. This provides deep insight into why such a "mismatched" estimator works so well and precisely quantifies its performance. The results have direct implications, for instance, in designing optimal quantizers for compressed sensing, as demonstrated in Section 2.3.

2.  **Originality:** To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide asymptotically *precise* error characterizations for LASSO-type methods in the single-index model setting. It moves beyond the upper bounds provided by Plan and Vershynin to exact expressions. Furthermore, it generalizes recent precise analyses of the `l2`-LASSO (e.g., from Bayati & Montanari, Oymak et al.) from the standard linear model to this much broader class of non-linear problems, and for a more general class of signal priors.

3.  **Quality:** The technical claims are strong and well-supported. The theoretical analysis, based on extending powerful tools like the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT), appears to be sound. The derived error formulas are validated convincingly with numerical simulations across different settings (sparse, group-sparse, different signal distributions), showing excellent agreement between theory and practice.

**Weaknesses/Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally very well-written. However, the central claim—that the non-linear problem is equivalent to a linear one—is presented as a main result in the introduction (lines 137-143). The authors rightly connect this to the classical work of Brillinger, but the details of this connection are deferred entirely to the proof in the appendix. To improve the self-containedness and intuitive flow of the main text, it would be beneficial to include a short, high-level paragraph in the introduction or related work section explaining *why* this equivalence emerges in the high-dimensional Gaussian setting. This would help the reader grasp the core mechanism behind the main result without needing to delve into the appendix.

2.  **Discussion of Practical Relevance:** The authors focus their analysis on the `l2`-LASSO (`argmin ||y-Ax||_2 + lambda*f(x)`), as opposed to the more common `l2^2`-LASSO (`argmin ||y-Ax||_2^2 + ...`). A key advantage of the `l2`-LASSO in the standard linear model is that the optimal choice of the regularization parameter `lambda` is independent of the noise variance. The results of this paper show that the non-linearity `g` induces an effective noise variance `sigma^2`. This implies that the `l2`-LASSO is particularly well-suited for the single-index model, as one can tune `lambda` without knowledge of the link function `g` (and thus `sigma^2`). This is a powerful practical argument for preferring the `l2`-LASSO over the `l2^2`-LASSO in this context. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this point, as it highlights a key practical consequence of the theoretical findings and provides valuable guidance for practitioners.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a fundamental and precise characterization of LASSO in the important single-index model.
*   The equivalence to a linear model is an elegant and insightful result.
*   The work is technically strong, original, and significantly advances the state of the art in the theoretical understanding of high-dimensional regularized estimation.
*   The results are general, covering various structures and signal priors.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper misses an opportunity to discuss the practical implications of its findings for the choice between `l2` and `l2^2`-LASSO formulations.
*   The clarity of the core argument could be slightly improved by bringing some of the intuition from the appendix into the main text.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of high-dimensional statistics. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be easily addressed in a final version. I strongly recommend acceptance.
