This paper studies the problem of estimating a structured signal `x0` from measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`, where `gi` is a potentially unknown and non-linear function. This setup, known as the single-index model, is a generalization of the standard linear measurement model. The authors analyze the performance of the generalized square-root LASSO, an estimator designed for linear models, when it is naively applied to these non-linear measurements.

The main contribution is a powerful and elegant asymptotic equivalence result. In the high-dimensional limit where the number of measurements `m` and the signal dimension `n` grow large with a fixed ratio `m/n`, the paper demonstrates that the estimation error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is precisely the same as that of a LASSO applied to an effective linear model `y_i = μ * a_i^T x0 + σ * z_i`. The scaling factor `μ` and effective noise level `σ` are determined solely by the first and second moments of the non-linear function `g` with respect to a standard Gaussian input. This result sharpens and generalizes classical work by Brillinger (which considered the unregularized, fixed-`n` case) and more recent non-asymptotic bounds by Plan and Vershynin. Building on this equivalence, the paper provides an exact, deterministic formula for the asymptotic mean-squared error, expressed as the solution to a small-scale convex optimization problem.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The central result establishing an exact asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear and a corresponding linear model is highly significant. It provides a clear and profound explanation for why a simple, seemingly misspecified estimator works so well. This moves beyond existing upper bounds to provide a precise characterization that deepens the theoretical understanding of high-dimensional estimation under model mismatch.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper's theoretical contributions are strong. Deriving a precise, deterministic expression for the asymptotic error (Theorem 2.2) is a challenging technical achievement. The results are general, applying to a wide class of convex regularizers (sparsity, group sparsity, low-rank) and non-linear link functions. The predictions are impressively accurate, as demonstrated in the figures.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-motivated. The main ideas are communicated effectively, and the relationship to prior work is clearly explained. The application of the theory to the design of optimal quantizers for compressive sensing is a compelling example of the work's practical relevance and power.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Presentation and Typos:** The primary weakness of the paper is its presentation. The manuscript is marred by numerous typographical errors. While the core concepts are understandable, the typos are distracting and are especially prevalent in the appendices and proofs, which undermines confidence in the technical details and requires very careful reading to verify. The paper would benefit greatly from a thorough proofreading and copy-editing pass.
*   **Related Work:** While the authors position their work well within the high-dimensional statistics literature, my own familiarity with the extensive literature on single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction is limited. It is possible that related equivalence results may exist in that domain, and the authors should ensure they have thoroughly situated their contribution within that broader context.

**Recommendation:**

This is a very strong theoretical paper that makes a fundamental contribution to our understanding of regularized estimation with non-linear measurements. The results are novel, important, and technically impressive. The main weakness is the lack of polish in the writing, particularly the high number of typos. However, this is correctable and does not detract from the core value of the contribution.

Therefore, I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper presents a complete and significant piece of research that will be of great interest to the NIPS community working on high-dimensional statistics and theory. My positive evaluation of the paper's core contribution is firm, and I believe it is a clear accept, contingent on the authors carefully addressing the typographical errors in the final version.
