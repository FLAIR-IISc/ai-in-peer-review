This paper investigates the Generalized LASSO for estimating signals from non-linear measurements, specifically focusing on the case where the measurement matrix has i.i.d. Gaussian entries. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, asymptotically, the performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is equivalent to that of a LASSO applied to scaled and noisy linear measurements. The authors derive precise expressions for the estimation error, extending previous work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin and providing a more complete and accurate understanding of the estimation performance. The results are validated empirically across various settings including sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, and low-rank matrix recovery, and a compelling application to q-bit compressive sensing with a connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer design is presented.

The work builds on a strong foundation of existing literature. The authors appropriately position their contribution within the context of single-index models, generalized LASSO formulations, and recent advances in analyzing the LASSO’s performance with linear measurements (citing works by Donoho, Stojnic, Thrampoulidis, among others).  The paper acknowledges the earlier work of Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin and clearly articulates how their results refine and generalize these prior findings. The connection to the broader field of compressive sensing, particularly the application to q-bit quantization, demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

The paper’s strengths are numerous. The technical approach appears sound, with rigorous mathematical derivations leading to the key asymptotic error formulas. The explicit expressions for the estimation error are a significant advancement over existing bounds and provide valuable insights into the roles of the link function, signal structure, regularizer choice, and regularization parameter. The detailed empirical validation across different scenarios is convincing and supports the theoretical findings. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is particularly noteworthy, as it illustrates a practical impact and suggests a concrete avenue for improving quantization strategies.  The authors also demonstrate a good understanding of related concepts like the Moreau envelope and Fenchel conjugate, though some notation could be clarified.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. While well-written overall, the level of detail is sometimes limited, presumably due to space constraints. This necessitates familiarity with the underlying mathematical framework.  The presentation can feel somewhat dense in certain sections, potentially hindering accessibility for readers less versed in high-dimensional statistics. The assumption required for Theorem 2.2, denoted Assumption 1, could benefit from a more formal interpretation and justification, perhaps referencing results from variational analysis.  Additionally, the repeated reference to the "Generalized LASSO as an algorithm" is slightly imprecise; it’s more accurately described as a convex program or decoder. Minor issues like the formatting of the caption in Figure 1 ($n=768$) and a typo on page 5 (line 247) should be corrected. The use of "proximal function" to describe the Moreau envelope could be clarified with a change in notation, as "prox" commonly refers to the proximal operator.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The derivation of precise asymptotic error formulas for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical analysis appears rigorous and well-supported.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the potential for real-world impact.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results convincingly support the theoretical findings.
*   **Clear Connection to Existing Work:**  The paper builds upon a solid foundation and clearly articulates its contribution in relation to prior research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Density of Presentation:** The technical details can be challenging, potentially limiting accessibility.
*   **Minor Notational Issues:** The use of “proximal function” and the formatting of figure captions require clarification.
*   **Slight Imprecision in Terminology:** Referring to the Generalized LASSO as an "algorithm" is less accurate than describing it as a convex program.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The derivation of precise error formulas for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is a valuable addition to the field. The empirical validation and the insightful application to q-bit compressive sensing further strengthen the paper's impact. While there are some minor areas for improvement regarding clarity and precision, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the noted comments.
