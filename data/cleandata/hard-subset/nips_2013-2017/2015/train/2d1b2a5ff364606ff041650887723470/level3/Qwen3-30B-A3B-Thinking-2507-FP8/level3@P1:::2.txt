

This paper addresses the problem of estimating structured signals from non-linear measurements using the Generalized LASSO framework. The authors extend previous work by Plan and Vershynin (2015) on the constrained LASSO with non-linear measurements to the regularized Generalized LASSO (which is more commonly used in practice), providing precise asymptotic characterizations of the estimation error. The key insight is that the estimation performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to one with linear measurements of the form yi = μa_i^T x0 + σz_i, where μ and σ are determined by the non-linear link function g. This result generalizes Brillinger's classical work from the early 80s.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides precise asymptotic error expressions for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements that hold for all values of the regularization parameter λ. Second, it extends previous results on the precise analysis of the LASSO with linear measurements to the non-linear case, which appears to be novel. Third, it applies these results to provide a theoretical foundation for optimal quantization in q-bit compressive sensing, showing that the optimal quantizer is the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

The quality of the paper is excellent. The authors provide rigorous proofs using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework, and the results are well-supported by numerical experiments. The paper builds upon and extends the recent line of work on precise error analysis of the LASSO, particularly the contributions by Thrampoulidis, Oymak, and Hassibi (2015) and others. The authors carefully address the relationship between their work and previous results, including the important work by Plan and Vershynin (2015) on constrained LASSO.

The clarity of the paper is good, but there is a minor issue with the presentation. The key equivalence between single-index models and linear models with scaled unknown signal and non-standard noise (which is crucial for the paper's main claim) is only presented in Appendix A. This equivalence, which is a generalization of Brillinger's work, should be mentioned earlier in the main text to justify the blockquote on line 140, which states "The estimation performance of the Generalized LASSO with measurements of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0) is asymptotically the same as if the measurements were rather of the form yi = μa_i^T x0 + σz_i." This would improve the flow and justification of the main result.

The originality of the work is strong. While Plan and Vershynin (2015) provided upper bounds on the estimation error for the constrained Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, this paper provides precise error expressions that hold for all values of λ and for a wide class of regularizers f and link functions g. The paper also extends the precise error analysis to non-linear measurements, which appears to be the first such result in the literature. The authors carefully situate their work within the existing literature, noting the connection to Brillinger's classical work and the more recent work by Plan and Vershynin.

The significance of the paper is high. The result provides a deeper understanding of the behavior of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, which is important for applications like 1-bit quantized measurements. The application to q-bit compressive sensing showing that the optimal quantizer is the Lloyd-Max quantizer has clear practical implications. The theoretical results also provide a foundation for better understanding how to choose regularization parameters and quantization thresholds in practice.

One minor concern relates to practical relevance. The paper focuses on the l2-LASSO (square-root LASSO), but does not address whether its results support preferring this over the l2^2-LASSO in practice. The authors note that "the l2-LASSO's oracle regularization parameter does not depend on noise variance," which is a key advantage, but they don't explicitly discuss how their results might inform the choice between these two variants of the LASSO in practice. A brief discussion on this point would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

The paper is well-written and the mathematical presentation is clear. The experiments in Figures 1-2 demonstrate the accuracy of the theoretical predictions across different settings (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank recovery), which adds to the credibility of the results.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on structured signal recovery by providing precise asymptotic characterizations for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. The results extend previous work in a meaningful way, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. The minor issues with presentation and the lack of discussion about practical implications of the l2-LASSO versus l2^2-LASSO are easily addressable in a revision. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and makes a valuable contribution to the field.
