Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under single-index (non-linear) measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where A has i.i.d. N(0,1) entries and x0 is structured (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and unknown. The estimator is the Generalized (ℓ2) LASSO: x̂ = argminx ||y − Ax||2 + λ f(x), with f a convex regularizer.

The main theoretical result is a precise, asymptotically exact characterization of the estimation error of the ℓ2-LASSO under non-linear measurements. The core theorem shows a Nonlinear = Linear reduction: asymptotically, the ℓ2-LASSO behaves as if the measurements were linear with an effective scaling μ = E[γ g(γ)] and an effective noise variance σ2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)2], γ ∼ N(0,1). Consequently, precise risk formulas known for linear models apply verbatim after replacing the SNR with μ and σ. The paper then provides a general scalar convex program (in three variables) that yields the exact asymptotic squared error; for important classes (sparse, group-sparse), this specializes to simpler characterizations (e.g., equations (14)–(15) for ℓ1). The work extends Brillinger’s classical result (LS with fixed n, m → ∞) to the high-dimensional regime (m,n → ∞, m/n → δ), and substantially strengthens the non-asymptotic upper bounds of Plan & Vershynin by yielding exact limits. It also relaxes common distributional assumptions on x0, allowing general priors with bounded moments (beyond Gaussian nonzeros), and covers several convex regularizers (ℓ1, ℓ1,2, nuclear norm).

An attractive application is quantizer design for q-bit compressive sensing: because performance depends only on μ and σ, the optimal design reduces to minimizing σ2/μ2 over thresholds and levels; the paper shows Lloyd–Max stationary points solve this surrogate, providing a principled link between signal recovery risk and classical quantization.

Quality
- The analysis is technically strong. The derivation of the exact error via a scalar convex program is consistent with recent CGMT/AMP-based precise analyses in the linear case, and simulations support the predictions across link functions and regularizers.
- Assumption 1 (existence of the limit F from the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate) is abstract but reasonable, and the paper verifies it for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2. For nuclear norm, the argument is sketched; a more detailed treatment or numerical recipe to compute F for common low-rank models would strengthen the contribution.
- The Nonlinear = Linear result requires μ ≠ 0; the paper clearly discusses this and its implications (direction recovery vs scale). Extensions to subgaussian designs or correlated rows of A are not covered.

Clarity
- The paper is well written and generally well organized. The contributions, model, and consequences are clearly stated, and the empirical figures are informative.
- Important suggestion: the striking Nonlinear = Linear equivalence (currently stated in Appendix A) should be brought into the main text as a named theorem early (and tied explicitly to Brillinger, 1982). This will better justify the statement around line 140 and orient readers before diving into the general program (10).
- Minor: collate the assumptions (on g, μ, σ, x0 normalization) in one place; clarify when g is deterministic vs random and how expectations are taken.

Originality
- To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact error analysis of LASSO-type estimators under general single-index measurements, going beyond the constrained LASSO bounds of Plan & Vershynin and the fixed-n LS result of Brillinger.
- The generality over regularizers and priors on x0 (beyond Gaussian nonzeros) is a clear advance over prior precise analyses of the ℓ2-LASSO.

Significance
- The reduction provides a unifying principle: for a wide class of nonlinearities, one can analyze and tune ℓ2-LASSO using linear-model formulas with effective (μ, σ). This is practically valuable and theoretically clarifying.
- The quantizer-design implication (Lloyd–Max optimality for minimizing σ2/μ2) is neat and actionable.
- Limitations include reliance on i.i.d. Gaussian A and μ ≠ 0, but within this scope the results are likely to influence follow-on work and practice.

Pros
- Conceptually clean Nonlinear = Linear equivalence with precise risk formulas.
- Covers multiple structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and relaxes Gaussian prior assumptions on x0.
- Useful design insight for quantized compressive sensing (Lloyd–Max connection).
- Strong alignment with and extension of recent precise-analysis literature; simulations validate theory.

Cons and questions
- The key equivalence theorem is relegated to the appendix; elevate it to the main text and explicitly connect to Brillinger (1982).
- Practical guidance on choosing λ is limited. Given that ℓ2-LASSO’s oracle tuning is insensitive to σ (unlike ℓ22-LASSO), do your results support preferring ℓ2-LASSO in practice under unknown g? Please discuss and, if possible, add experiments comparing ℓ2 vs ℓ22 across nonlinearities and unknown σ.
- Assumption 1 and the function F are nontrivial for nuclear norm; more detail or examples would help.
- Scope is limited to Gaussian A. Comment on potential extensions to subgaussian or structured designs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Promote the Nonlinear = Linear theorem to Section 2, with a short, intuitive proof sketch and explicit statement of conditions (μ ≠ 0, bounded σ2).
- Add a section on tuning λ: (i) how to compute λcrit and the optimal κ numerically for a given (μ, σ, ρ, δ); (ii) whether the mapping suggests data-driven tuning (e.g., cross-validation based on linear surrogate) and whether ℓ2 should be preferred to ℓ22 when σ (hence g) is unknown.
- For the low-rank case, include a concrete example (e.g., spiked model) with computed F and a plot validating the prediction.
- In the quantization section, report an experiment optimizing (t, ℓ) via Lloyd–Max for several q and compare the resulting recovery error to non-optimized baselines.

Minor comments
- Clarify expectations in (3) are over both γ and any randomness in g.
- Note explicitly that when g is even (μ = 0), only direction recovery is possible with the proposed approach.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely paper that makes a clear, novel, and significant contribution to the precise analysis of high-dimensional estimators under nonlinear measurements. With the suggested clarifications—especially moving the core equivalence into the main text and expanding the practical guidance on λ and ℓ2 vs ℓ22—this would be a valuable addition to NIPS.
