This paper analyzes the performance of the generalized square-root LASSO when estimating a structured signal from measurements transformed by a non-linear, and potentially unknown, function. The central contribution is a precise asymptotic characterization of the estimation error in a high-dimensional setting with Gaussian measurements. The key finding is that the LASSO's performance in this non-linear scenario is asymptotically equivalent to its performance in a standard linear model with a specific signal scaling factor and additive noise variance, both of which are determined by the non-linear function. This work extends the foundational results of Brillinger and the more recent bounds by Plan and Vershynin, transitioning from performance bounds to exact asymptotic formulas, which aligns with the state-of-the-art in LASSO analysis.

The primary strength of this work is its significant and elegant theoretical result. Establishing an equivalence between the complex non-linear problem and a well-understood linear one is a powerful contribution that simplifies analysis and provides deep insight. This finding allows the rich body of research on the linear LASSO to be directly applied to this more general setting. The authors effectively illustrate the importance of their result through an application to optimal quantizer design, showing that their framework recovers the classical Lloyd-Max quantizer. The originality and significance of this precise characterization represent a substantial advance in the field.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. The most significant issue is the large number of typographical errors, particularly within the proofs presented in the appendix. For a theoretical contribution, such errors raise concerns about the overall carefulness of the work and make it difficult for readers to verify the correctness of the derivations. This lack of polish detracts from the paper's clarity and overall quality. As a point of transparency, I should also state that I am not an expert in the literature on single-index models or sufficient dimension reduction, and thus may have missed some relevant prior work from those fields.

In conclusion, the paper presents a strong, novel, and important theoretical result that significantly advances our understanding of regularized estimation with non-linear measurements. Despite my concerns regarding the numerous typos, the core contribution is of high quality and significance. My recommendation is for acceptance, conditional on a thorough proofreading and correction of the manuscript. This assessment is firm, as the value of the main result is clear, but the presentation must be improved to meet the standards of the conference.
