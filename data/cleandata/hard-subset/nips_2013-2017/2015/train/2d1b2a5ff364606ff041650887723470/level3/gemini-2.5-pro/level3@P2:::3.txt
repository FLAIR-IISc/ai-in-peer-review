This paper studies the problem of structured signal recovery using the Generalized LASSO when the measurements are subjected to a potentially unknown non-linear transformation. The main contribution is a precise, asymptotic characterization of the mean-squared error (MSE). The authors demonstrate that, for a Gaussian measurement matrix, the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements of the form y_i = g(a_i^T x_0) is asymptotically equivalent to its performance with linear measurements of the form y_i = μ a_i^T x_0 + σ z_i. The constants μ and σ are derived from the link function g and a standard Gaussian variable. This work builds upon and significantly strengthens the foundational results of Brillinger and the more recent upper bounds provided by Plan and Vershynin. By leveraging techniques for precise asymptotic analysis, the paper derives explicit formulas for the MSE that depend on the problem parameters (m, n), the regularizer, and the signal structure. These theoretical predictions are validated through experiments for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems. A compelling application to optimal quantizer design in compressive sensing is also presented, connecting the problem to the classical Lloyd-Max quantizer.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and significant theoretical contribution. While prior work by Plan and Vershynin suggested a connection between the non-linear and a corresponding linear model, their results were non-asymptotic upper bounds with unspecified constants. This paper provides the first asymptotically *exact* characterization of the estimation error. The equivalence result is elegant and powerful, as it maps a complex, seemingly intractable problem onto a well-understood linear one, for which a mature body of analytical tools exists.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is a technically solid and complete piece of work. The analysis appears rigorous, leading to precise, computable formulas for the estimation error. The framework is general, applicable to a wide class of convex regularizers (l1, group l1, nuclear norm) and signal structures. The theoretical claims are well-supported by both the derivations and the numerical experiments.

3.  **Clarity and Impact of Application:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing is particularly insightful and demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical findings. The authors show that the problem of designing an optimal quantizer to minimize the final reconstruction error can be simplified to minimizing the noise-to-signal ratio σ²/μ². This provides a clear, actionable design principle and interestingly connects the problem to the Lloyd-Max algorithm. This application significantly strengthens the paper and broadens its potential impact.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The numerical simulations are well-executed and provide compelling evidence for the theory. The plots clearly show that the empirical MSE matches the theoretical predictions with high accuracy across different settings, confirming both the non-linear to linear equivalence and the correctness of the derived error formulas.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** While generally well-written, the paper is quite dense, likely due to conference page limits. This makes some of the technical sections difficult to follow without deep familiarity with the subject. For instance, Assumption 1, which is central to the main theorem, is stated formally but could benefit from a more intuitive or formal interpretation, perhaps through the lens of variational analysis, to make it more accessible. The presentation could be improved by further streamlining the main narrative and deferring more of the heavy technical machinery to the appendix.

2.  **Minor Terminological Imprecision:** The paper occasionally refers to the Generalized LASSO as an "algorithm." It would be more precise to call it an "estimator," "decoder," or "convex program," as the LASSO itself is an optimization problem, not a specific procedure for solving it.

### Detailed Comments for the Authors

-   In the introduction, you briefly mention the distinction between the sqrt-LASSO and the standard `l2^2`-penalized LASSO. You state that your results can be accustomed to the latter. While space is tight, a brief remark clarifying that these formulations are equivalent under a proper mapping of the regularization parameter λ would be helpful for readers.
-   Assumption 1 is crucial but presented in a rather technical manner. Providing more intuition or connecting it to established concepts in variational analysis could strengthen its foundation and improve readability.
-   The term "proximal function" is sometimes used colloquially for related concepts. While you correctly define `e_{f,τ}(v)` as the Moreau envelope, please ensure this terminology is used consistently to avoid confusion with the proximal operator, which is distinct.
-   There appears to be a minor formatting issue in the caption of Figure 1, where 'n=768' should ideally be rendered in math mode as $n=768$.
-   A minor typo was noted on page 5. Please double-check the manuscript for small errors.

### Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
*   Provides the first asymptotically exact error characterization for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements.
*   The established equivalence to a noisy linear model is an elegant and powerful theoretical result.
*   The results are general, applying to various structures and regularizers.
*   The application to optimal quantizer design is insightful and demonstrates practical utility.
*   Strong empirical results convincingly validate the theory.

**Con:**
*   The presentation is dense, which may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
*   Some of the core assumptions could be explained more intuitively.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that makes a substantial and original contribution to the understanding of structured signal recovery under model mismatch. The strengths, particularly the novelty and significance of the theoretical results, far outweigh the minor weaknesses in presentation. The paper advances the state of the art and is likely to be influential for researchers working on high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. I strongly recommend acceptance.
