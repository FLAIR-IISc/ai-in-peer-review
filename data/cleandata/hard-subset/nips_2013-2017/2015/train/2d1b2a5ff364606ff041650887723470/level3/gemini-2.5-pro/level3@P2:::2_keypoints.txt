- Problem: Estimating a high-dimensional structured signal x0 from non-linear single-index measurements y_i = g(a_i^T x0).  
- Estimator studied: Generalized l2-LASSO (square-root LASSO) that treats non-linear measurements as if they were linear.  
- Main contribution: Precise asymptotic characterization of the estimator's MSE in the high-dimensional limit.  
- Equivalence result: Shows the l2-LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to a linear model y = μ A x0 + σ z.  
- μ and σ: The scaling factor μ and effective noise variance σ^2 are determined solely by the link function g.  
- Explicit formula: Provides the asymptotic MSE as the solution of a simple three-dimensional convex optimization problem.  
- Generality: Extends precise LASSO analyses to the single-index model, covering many convex regularizers (ℓ1, group-ℓ1, nuclear norm) and relaxing Gaussian prior assumptions.  
- Technical approach: Rigorous analysis leveraging and extending tools like the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT), building on Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin.  
- Empirical validation: Numerical simulations across sparse, group-sparse, and varied priors show excellent agreement with theory.  
- Strength — significance: Fundamental and elegant equivalence result with direct implications (e.g., optimal quantizer design).  
- Strength — originality: First work providing asymptotically precise error characterizations for LASSO in the single-index setting.  
- Strength — quality: Strong, well-supported technical analysis and validated predictions.  
- Weakness: Core intuition for why the equivalence holds is deferred to the appendix; a short high-level explanation should be added to the main text.  
- Weakness: Lacks discussion of practical implications comparing l2-LASSO to l2^2-LASSO, notably that λ tuning for l2-LASSO is independent of the unknown link-induced noise.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Fundamental contribution, elegant and insightful equivalence, technical strength, originality, and broad applicability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Missed opportunity to discuss practical l2 vs l2^2 implications and could improve clarity by surfacing appendix intuition.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept — strengths substantially outweigh minor, easily addressable weaknesses.
