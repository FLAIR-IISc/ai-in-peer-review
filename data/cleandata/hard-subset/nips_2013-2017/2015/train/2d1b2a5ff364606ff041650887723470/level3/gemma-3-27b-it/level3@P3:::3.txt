This paper investigates the recovery of structured signals from non-linear measurements using the Generalized Lasso. The key contribution is demonstrating that, under Gaussian design, non-linear measurements can be effectively treated as noisy linear measurements, and providing precise asymptotic error formulas. This builds on prior work by Brillinger, Plan & Vershynin, and significantly extends their results by deriving explicit error expressions. The work is of high quality, well-written, and presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of high-dimensional statistics and compressive sensing.

**Summary:**

The authors tackle the problem of estimating an unknown signal from measurements corrupted by non-linear link functions. They demonstrate that, surprisingly, solving the Generalized Lasso (a regularized least squares problem) yields good estimates even with these non-linearities, behaving asymptotically as if the measurements *were* linear with appropriately scaled noise.  They rigorously derive precise formulas for the estimation error, dependent on the link function's characteristics, the regularization parameter, and the signal structure. A compelling application is presented in the context of q-bit compressive sensing, demonstrating a connection to the optimal Lloyd-Max quantizer.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is built upon solid mathematical foundations, leveraging recent advances in random matrix theory and convex optimization. The proofs appear rigorous, and the asymptotic analysis is thorough.
* **Novelty:**  The derivation of explicit error formulas for the Generalized Lasso with non-linear measurements is a significant advancement. Existing work provided bounds or asymptotic behavior but lacked the precision offered here.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing is insightful and demonstrates the practical implications of the theoretical results.  The connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer offers a potential design principle for efficient quantizers.
* **Clear Exposition:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors clearly explain the motivation, methodology, and results.
* **Comprehensive Literature Review:**  The related work section is extensive and demonstrates a good understanding of the relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Uneven Detail:** While generally clear, the paper occasionally sacrifices detail for brevity, likely due to space constraints. Some derivations could benefit from further elaboration.
* **Notation:** The use of "prox" to label the Moreau envelope is confusing, as "prox" conventionally denotes the proximal operator. Changing this notation would improve clarity.
* **Assumption 1:**  Assumption 1, regarding the behavior of the regularizer, lacks a sufficiently formal interpretation using variational analysis.  A more precise statement would strengthen the theoretical foundation.
* **Terminology:** Referring to the Generalized Lasso as an "algorithm" is misleading. It is more accurate to describe it as a convex program or decoder.



**Specific Comments:**

* Figure 1 caption: "n=768" should be rendered as $n=768$.
* Page 5, line 247: There is a typo at "When ...".
* The discussion of the relationship between regularized and penalized formulations could be more explicit, perhaps with a brief equation highlighting the parameter mapping.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the behavior of the Generalized Lasso with non-linear measurements.
* The derived error formulas are novel and provide valuable insights into the performance of the estimator.
* The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the practical relevance of the results.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and findings.
* The experimental results validate the theoretical predictions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper could benefit from more detailed derivations in certain sections.
* The notation and terminology could be slightly improved for clarity.
* Assumption 1 would be strengthened with a more formal interpretation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical results are novel and significant, the application is insightful, and the paper is generally well-written. Despite a few minor weaknesses, the strengths far outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the notation, terminology, and Assumption 1. The authors should also consider adding a little more detail to certain derivations if space allows.
