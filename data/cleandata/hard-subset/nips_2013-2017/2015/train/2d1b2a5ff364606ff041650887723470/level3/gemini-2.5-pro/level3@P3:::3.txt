This paper studies the problem of estimating a structured signal from non-linear measurements using the generalized Lasso. The measurement model considered is `yi = gi(a_i^T x_0)`, where `x_0` is the structured signal (e.g., sparse or low-rank), `A` is a measurement matrix with i.i.d. Gaussian entries, and `g` is a potentially unknown and random non-linear link function. The central contribution is to show that, in a high-dimensional asymptotic regime, the performance of the generalized Lasso is precisely equivalent to its performance on a related *linear* measurement model `y_lin = μ a_i^T x_0 + σ z_i`, where `z_i` are i.i.d. standard normal variables. The scaling factor `μ` and effective noise level `σ` are determined solely by the moments of the link function `g`. This work builds upon and significantly sharpens prior results. It extends the classical work of Brillinger to the modern high-dimensional setting where both signal dimension and the number of measurements grow large, and it provides exact asymptotic error formulas where more recent work by Plan & Vershynin provided only non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained variant of the problem.

**Strengths:**
*   **Quality and Significance:** This is a solid, well-executed, and technically deep piece of work. The main result, establishing an exact asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear measurement problem and an effective noisy linear one, is both elegant and powerful. It provides a fundamental insight into why a simple linear decoder works so well for a non-linear problem.
*   **Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is the derivation of novel and explicit formulas for the asymptotic mean squared error. To the best of my knowledge, these are the first such precise characterizations for the generalized Lasso with non-linear measurements. These formulas precisely capture the interplay between the signal structure, the regularizer, the link function, and the problem dimensions, moving far beyond the order-wise bounds common in related literature.
*   **Applications and Experiments:** The theoretical results are convincingly validated through numerical experiments across several important special cases, including the standard Lasso, group Lasso, and nuclear norm regularization. The application to designing optimal q-bit quantizers in compressed sensing is particularly insightful. It demonstrates the practical utility of the precise error formulas by reducing a complex decoder design problem to a much simpler optimization of the `σ²/μ²` ratio, and it uncovers an interesting connection to the classic Lloyd–Max quantizer. This serves as an excellent example of how sharp theory can guide practical system design.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is generally well-written, but the presentation is somewhat unbalanced, likely due to severe space constraints that limit the inclusion of important details in the main text. The following points are offered as constructive feedback for improving the manuscript.

*   **Terminology:** The generalized Lasso is a convex optimization problem, not a procedural method. It would be more precise to refer to it as a "convex program" or "decoder" rather than an "algorithm" throughout the text.
*   **Notation:** The function labeled as the "proximal function" in Assumption 1 is, in fact, the Moreau envelope. Since "prox" is the standard notation for the proximal operator, using `e_f,τ` or a similar established notation for the Moreau envelope would avoid potential confusion for readers familiar with convex optimization literature.
*   **Assumption 1:** While the assumption is shown to hold for key examples, it could be given a more formal interpretation. Grounding it in the language of variational analysis or explaining its connection to the convergence of certain random processes could make the condition more intuitive and its scope clearer.
*   **Formulation Equivalence:** The authors correctly state that their analysis of the square-root-Lasso formulation can be adapted to the more common quadratically penalized Lasso. As this is an important point for practitioners, it might be worth briefly commenting on how the parameters of the equivalent linear model would change under this alternative formulation.
*   **Minor Corrections:**
    *   The caption for Figure 1 should render mathematical variables correctly, e.g., 'n=768' should be `$n=768$`.
    *   There appears to be a typo on page 5, line 247, starting with "When ...".

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a very strong contribution to the theoretical understanding of high-dimensional structured signal recovery. The results are novel, significant, and technically sound. The discovery of an exact asymptotic equivalence between non-linear and noisy-linear observation models for the Lasso is a fundamental insight. The paper's strengths, particularly the precision of its theoretical results and the insightful application to quantizer design, far outweigh its minor presentational weaknesses, which can be easily addressed. I strongly recommend acceptance.
