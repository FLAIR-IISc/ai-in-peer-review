This paper studies the problem of estimating a structured signal x0 from non-linear measurements of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0). The authors propose using the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, which is designed for linear models, and analyze its performance in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime where the number of measurements (m) and signal dimension (n) grow to infinity with a fixed ratio. The central contribution is an exact asymptotic characterization of the mean-squared error. The main result (Theorem 2.1) establishes that the estimation error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is identical to that of a LASSO applied to an equivalent linear model yi = µa_i^T x0 + σzi, where µ and σ are constants determined by the link function g. This work builds directly upon the classical results of Brillinger for unregularized least squares in the fixed-n setting and the more recent non-asymptotic bounds for the constrained LASSO by Plan and Vershynin. The key advance here is the derivation of precise error formulas, rather than upper bounds, for the regularized LASSO, extending recent analytical frameworks like the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) to the non-linear measurement setting.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The main result is clean, insightful, and significant. Establishing that, asymptotically, the LASSO "sees" a non-linear problem as an equivalent, scaled linear problem with additive Gaussian noise provides a powerful and intuitive understanding of its behavior. Deriving precise, computable formulas for the asymptotic error (Theorem 2.2) is a substantial technical contribution that goes well beyond the upper bounds found in prior work. This allows for a much finer-grained analysis of the estimator's performance and its dependence on various problem parameters.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper appears to be technically sound. The analysis leverages and extends a sophisticated framework for the precise analysis of convex optimization programs in high dimensions. The specific characterizations for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery are valuable and demonstrate the generality of the main theorem. The application to finding the optimal q-bit quantizer by minimizing σ²/µ² is a compelling example of the practical utility of the derived theory.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out, the assumptions are stated precisely, and the main theorems are presented in an accessible manner before delving into specific applications. The connections to prior art by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin are well-explained, helping to contextualize the contributions.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Omission of Relevant Literature:** The paper's claim to be the first to derive precise results of this nature seems to overlook some highly relevant recent work. In particular, the work of Yi et al. (2015, "Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform," arXiv:1505.03257) also studies high-dimensional estimation under non-linear transforms and establishes a similar equivalence between the non-linear and an effective linear model. Crucially, their work provides guarantees for sparse recovery even when µ=0, a case this paper explicitly excludes. Acknowledging and thoroughly discussing the relationship to this work is essential for accurately positioning the paper's contribution. Furthermore, while the single-index model is mentioned, the paper could benefit from a broader discussion of the extensive literature in statistics and econometrics on this topic and on sufficient dimension reduction, which has long studied the problem of estimating a signal's direction under an unknown link function.

2.  **Reliance on Gaussian Measurements:** The analysis relies heavily on the assumption that the entries of the measurement matrix A are i.i.d. Gaussian. This is a common assumption in this line of theoretical work, but it is a significant limitation. Could the authors comment on the necessity of this assumption for their proof technique? Are there fundamental obstacles to extending this "linear equivalence" principle to sub-gaussian or other matrix ensembles? A discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Dependence on µ and σ:** The results show that the difficulty of the problem, as seen by the LASSO, is determined by the effective signal-to-noise ratio, governed by µ and σ. Is this dependence a fundamental property of the estimation problem itself, or is it an artifact of using a mismatched estimator (the LASSO)? In other words, could an estimator that explicitly accounts for the non-linearity (if it were known) overcome the performance degradation when µ is small? This is a key conceptual point that warrants discussion.

4.  **Guidance on Regularizer Selection:** The paper provides a powerful tool for *analyzing* the performance of the LASSO with a given regularizer `f(x)`. However, it provides little guidance on the practical problem of *choosing* `f(x)`. For example, the analysis of the constrained LASSO relates the performance to the Gaussian width of the set K, but how should one translate a constraint set K into an appropriate regularizer `f(x)` for the penalized version? Some discussion on this point would make the work more accessible to practitioners.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper presents a strong, elegant, and theoretically significant result. The "linear equivalence" theorem is intuitive and powerful. The derivation of precise asymptotic error formulas is a notable technical achievement that advances the state of the art in the analysis of high-dimensional estimators. The paper is very clearly written.

*   **Against Acceptance:** The primary concern is the failure to cite and discuss highly relevant and contemporaneous work by Yi et al., which addresses a similar problem and tempers the claims of originality, particularly regarding the µ=0 case. The strong reliance on Gaussian measurements without discussion of this limitation is another weakness.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with interesting and well-presented results. The strengths are significant. However, the issues with missing citations are serious and should be addressed in a revision to properly contextualize the contribution within the field.
