Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation from single-index (nonlinear) measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) using the generalized LASSO with convex regularization. The main result (Theorem 2.1) shows an exact asymptotic equivalence: for i.i.d. Gaussian measurement vectors, the error of generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements matches that of the same estimator under a linear model with effective parameters μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). Theorem 2.2 provides a precise scalar optimization that yields the asymptotic squared error; specialized corollaries cover sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank models, and the analysis further leads to an optimal quantizer design observation (Lloyd–Max is stationary for the relevant criterion).

This work generalizes Brillinger’s classical fixed-dimension result (least squares under unknown link) to high-dimensional, structured settings and strengthens Plan–Vershynin’s bounds for the constrained LASSO by (i) treating the regularized form and (ii) providing exact asymptotics rather than loose upper bounds. The use of CGMT connects to prior precise LASSO analyses under linear models.

Quality
The results are technically strong and carefully argued. The CGMT-based derivations and specialization to common regularizers are convincing, and the simulations track theory closely. Assumption 1 is made explicit and verified for important cases (`1, group `1,2, nuclear norm), and the role of δ, λ, and the structure is clear in the limiting programs. The quantizer design application is neat and practically motivated.

That said, several points merit clarification:
- Necessity of Gaussian measurement vectors: The proof technique (CGMT) and the equivalence in Theorem 2.1 use Gaussian rotational invariance and the Hermite/projection decomposition that yields μ and σ. Please discuss whether the result extends to rotationally invariant ensembles (e.g., A with i.i.d. N(0,1/m) columns scaled, or orthogonally invariant matrices), to i.i.d. sub-Gaussian designs via universality, or to general isotropic designs. If Gaussianity is essential, identify precisely which steps fail and whether weaker conditions (e.g., exact rotational invariance, Lindeberg-type universality) would suffice. Some empirical results beyond Gaussian would strengthen the case.
- Dependence on μ and σ: The appearance of μ and σ aligns with the orthogonal projection of g(γ) onto span{γ} and its orthogonal complement. Are these dependences information-theoretically necessary for generalized LASSO (i.e., no other features of g can influence the asymptotic error under Gaussian designs)? A short argument or reference explaining why only these two “link-difficulty” parameters can enter (under Gaussian A) would help. It would also be helpful to discuss the μ = 0 case explicitly: when μ = 0, is generalized LASSO fundamentally unable to recover direction, and are there modifications (reweighting, spectral initialization) that could help?
- Choice of regularizer given a set K: The paper analyzes convex regularizers but does not guide how to choose f from a known candidate set K containing x0. Practical recommendations would be valuable: e.g., choose f as the gauge/atomic norm whose unit ball approximates K, so that the descent cone/statistical dimension matches K’s geometry (cf. Chandrasekaran et al., 2012). Some discussion on the tradeoff between using the true constraint set K (constrained LASSO) versus a surrogate convex regularizer f (regularized LASSO), and how this affects the function F and hence the error, would make the results more actionable.
- Tuning of λ: The asymptotic formula characterizes error for any λ and identifies λcrit. It would be useful to translate this into a practical tuning rule (e.g., estimating μ, σ from data and then selecting λ that minimizes the limiting error), and to comment on sensitivity to mis-specification of μ, σ when g is unknown.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The summary of contributions and the connections to Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin are clear. Suggestions to further improve readability:
- Provide intuition for Assumption 1 and the function F, and collect explicit F for the main regularizers in one place.
- When introducing Theorem 2.3, briefly explain the two regimes around λcrit and how they relate to known phase transitions.
- In Section 2.3, state clearly the scope of the Lloyd–Max optimality claim (stationary point for σ^2/μ^2) and any conditions ensuring global optimality or near-optimality.

Originality
The exact asymptotic equivalence between nonlinear and linear measurements for generalized LASSO, and the precise error characterization via CGMT, are novel and timely. The quantizer-design angle adds an interesting design implication. This goes beyond prior upper bounds (Plan–Vershynin) and complements AMP-based precise analyses for linear models. However, related results on equivalence in high dimensions under unknown nonlinear transforms (see Yi et al., 2015) should be discussed.

Significance
The results provide sharp insight into when generalized LASSO “ignores” nonlinearity with only a rescaling and added noise penalty determined by μ and σ. This is both conceptually clarifying and practically useful (e.g., quantizer design, link-agnostic recovery). Assuming Gaussian designs, the work will likely influence subsequent theoretical and algorithmic research. Exploring universality beyond Gaussian would further enhance impact.

Requested citations and comparisons
- Single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction: Please cite additional foundational SDR works such as Sliced Inverse Regression (Li, 1991) and related methods (e.g., Cook and Weisberg). The current list includes Li–Duan (1989) and Ichimura (1993) but omits SIR and follow-ups.
- Yi, Wang, Caramanis, Liu (2015): Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform. This work treats optimal linear estimators under unknown link, including sparse recovery even when μ = 0, and establishes equivalence between linear and nonlinear high-dimensional estimation. A detailed comparison—assumptions, estimators (regularized vs spectral/linear), treatment of μ = 0, and precision of results—would strengthen the positioning.

Pros
- Technically solid, with precise asymptotics for a widely used estimator.
- Clean equivalence principle tying nonlinear to linear models via μ and σ.
- Broad coverage of structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and clear specialization.
- Insightful application to quantizer design with concrete implications.

Cons
- Strong reliance on Gaussian designs; universality not addressed.
- Limited guidance on selecting the regularizer f from a given set K and on practical tuning of λ.
- Minimal discussion of μ = 0 regime and alternatives.
- Missing or limited citations to SDR literature and Yi et al. (2015) comparison.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper is well written and provides a compelling, precise theoretical contribution with practical implications. Addressing the Gaussian-design scope, adding guidance on regularizer/tuning selection, and incorporating the missing citations and comparisons would further strengthen the submission.
