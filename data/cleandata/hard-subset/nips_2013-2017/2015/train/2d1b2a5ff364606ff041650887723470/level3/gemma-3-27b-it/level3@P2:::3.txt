This paper studies the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements and a Gaussian design matrix. The core contribution is demonstrating that non-linear observations can be effectively treated as noisy linear ones, and deriving precise asymptotic error formulas that extend and refine previous work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin. The analysis builds upon recent advances in high-dimensional statistics and random matrix theory, providing a rigorous theoretical foundation for the use of the LASSO in non-linear measurement settings. The authors validate their results empirically across several important special cases (Lasso, group Lasso, nuclear norm regularization) and offer an insightful application to q-bit compressive sensing, demonstrating a connection to the well-known Lloyd-Max quantizer design.

The paper is a solid, well-done piece of work. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the results are presented clearly. The authors skillfully connect their work to existing literature, accurately placing their contributions within the broader context of signal recovery and estimation. The treatment of the q-bit compressive sensing problem is particularly noteworthy, offering a practical demonstration of the theoretical results. 

The clarity of the paper is generally strong, however, it occasionally feels somewhat unbalanced. The authors acknowledge space constraints limiting the inclusion of all details, which is understandable. However, in some instances, a slightly more detailed explanation of the assumptions or technical steps would enhance readability. For example, Assumption 1 could benefit from a more formal interpretation using concepts from variational analysis. While the notation is mostly clear, the use of "proximal function" is potentially misleading; it refers to the Moreau envelope, and a change in terminology would avoid confusion, as "prox" conventionally denotes the proximal operator. The authors consistently refer to the Generalized LASSO as an “algorithm,” which is somewhat imprecise; it would be more accurate to describe it as a convex program or a decoder. 

The originality of the work lies in the derivation of novel, explicit expressions for the estimation error. These expressions extend prior work by providing asymptotically precise characterizations, rather than merely upper bounds, and accurately capture the influence of the link function, signal structure, regularizer, and regularization parameter. This precise analysis allows for a deeper understanding of the behavior of the LASSO in non-linear settings and provides a foundation for optimizing its performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a mathematically sound analysis with clear derivations and rigorous proofs.
*   **Novelty:** The precise error formulas represent a significant advance over existing results.
*   **Generality:** The results are applicable to a wide range of regularization techniques and non-linear measurement models.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates the practical utility of the theoretical findings.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature and highlights its contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity (Minor):** Some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations of assumptions and technical steps.
*   **Terminology:** The use of "algorithm" to describe the LASSO is slightly imprecise.
*   **Assumption 1:** Could benefit from a more formal interpretation.
*   **Formatting:** Minor formatting issues such as the figure 1 caption ($n=768$) and a typo on page 5, line 247 ("When...").



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of LASSO-type estimators in non-linear measurement settings.
*   The results are novel, rigorously proven, and have the potential to impact a variety of applications.
*   The empirical validation confirms the theoretical findings and demonstrates the practical relevance of the work.
*   The connection to q-bit compressive sensing and the Lloyd-Max quantizer design is particularly insightful.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   While the clarity is generally good, some sections could be more accessible to readers not intimately familiar with the field.
*   The minor issues with terminology and formatting, although not critical, detract slightly from the overall polish of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. Despite a few minor weaknesses in clarity and presentation, the technical novelty, theoretical rigor, and practical relevance of the work are substantial. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of signal processing and machine learning and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS. I believe that the insights provided by this work will be of great interest to the NIPS community.

**Rating:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)
