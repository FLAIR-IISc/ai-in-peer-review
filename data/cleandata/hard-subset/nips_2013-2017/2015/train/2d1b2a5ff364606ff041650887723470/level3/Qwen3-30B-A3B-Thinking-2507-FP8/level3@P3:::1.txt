

# Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper studies the Generalized LASSO for estimating structured signals from non-linear measurements of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), where gi are independent copies of a random link function g. The authors establish that, under certain asymptotic conditions, the estimation performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that with appropriately scaled and noisy linear measurements. Specifically, they show that the estimation error ‖x̂ - μx0‖2 is asymptotically equivalent to the error of the Generalized LASSO with linear measurements yi = μai^T x0 + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2] for γ ~ N(0,1).

This work builds on earlier contributions by Brillinger (1980s), who studied the least-squares estimator in the fixed-dimensional regime, and Plan and Vershynin (2013), who provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained version of the Generalized LASSO. The paper's main contribution is deriving precise asymptotic characterizations of the estimation error, which improves upon previous non-tight upper bounds by providing exact expressions that capture the role of the link function g, the signal structure, the regularizer f, and the regularization parameter λ.

The paper makes significant progress in the literature on single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction, which have been studied extensively in statistics (e.g., [4,5,14,15]). While the paper acknowledges this literature, it appears to be the first to provide precise asymptotic results for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements in the high-dimensional setting, extending the recent precise analysis of LASSO with linear measurements [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 21].

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Quality**: The paper establishes technically sound asymptotic guarantees with precise characterizations of the estimation error. The proofs are complex but well-structured, and the results provide a deep understanding of how the Generalized LASSO behaves with non-linear measurements.

2. **Originality**: The paper extends previous work on LASSO with linear measurements to non-linear measurements with precise asymptotic characterizations where prior work only provided upper bounds. The results are novel and address an important gap in the literature.

3. **Significance**: The findings have practical implications for applications involving non-linear measurements, such as quantized sensing. The paper shows that the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error is the celebrated Lloyd-Max quantizer, which is a significant practical insight.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-written, with clear organization and a good balance of intuition and technical detail. The main results are presented in a logically structured manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Typos in Proofs**: The paper contains numerous typos, particularly in the proofs, which detract from the overall quality and may raise concerns about carefulness. These should be corrected before publication.

2. **Literature Coverage**: As a reviewer unfamiliar with the single-index model literature, I may have missed relevant prior work. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with the extensive literature on sufficient dimension reduction.

3. **Assumption Specificity**: While the assumptions in the asymptotic regime are reasonable, the paper could better explain the practical implications of these assumptions for real-world applications.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper provides the first precise asymptotic analysis for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, filling a significant gap in the literature.
- The results are technically sound and provide deep theoretical insights with practical implications.
- The paper is well-written and well-organized, making the complex material accessible.

*Con:*
- The numerous typos in the proofs may indicate carelessness that should be addressed.
- The connection to the single-index model literature could be more thoroughly explored to better position the work within the field.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the understanding of the Generalized LASSO in the presence of non-linear measurements. The results are technically sophisticated, provide precise characterizations where previous work only offered upper bounds, and have clear practical implications. While the paper contains some typos that should be corrected, these are minor issues that do not undermine the significance of the results.

The author responses and other reviews did not change my opinion. I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field, and the technical quality is strong despite the minor issues with typos. The work is well-positioned to advance the understanding of LASSO-type methods in non-linear measurement settings and has the potential to influence both theoretical and applied research in compressed sensing and high-dimensional statistics.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to correct the typos, particularly in the proofs. The paper is of high quality, makes a clear contribution to the field, and would be valuable to the NIPS community.
