This paper investigates the asymptotic estimation error for linear estimation with arbitrary nonlinear link functions using the generalized LASSO. The core contribution is demonstrating that, surprisingly, the asymptotic error is identical for linear and nonlinear link functions under specific conditions, extending previous work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin to the case where µ ≠ 0. The authors provide a precise theoretical analysis, deriving explicit expressions for the estimation error and exploring implications for quantized measurements.

The work builds upon a substantial body of literature. It correctly positions itself within the context of single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction, acknowledging the existing work on linear/subspace estimation under unknown link functions. The authors also appropriately reference recent advancements in the precise analysis of the LASSO with linear measurements, citing key papers by Stojnic, Thrampoulidis et al. However, a critical omission is the work by Yi et al. (2015) on optimal linear estimation under unknown link functions. This paper directly addresses sparse recovery even when µ = 0 and, importantly, establishes the equivalence between linear and nonlinear high-dimensional estimation, providing a closely related and highly relevant result. Including this citation would significantly strengthen the related work section.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty & Theoretical Rigor:** The main result – the equivalence of asymptotic errors for linear and nonlinear links – is a significant and potentially surprising finding. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical justification for this claim.
*   **Precise Error Analysis:** Deriving explicit expressions for the estimation error represents a substantial advancement in understanding the behavior of the generalized LASSO in non-linear settings.
*   **Practical Implications:** The application to quantized measurements and the analysis of the Lloyd-Max algorithm demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical results.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the notation is consistent.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively builds on previous research, particularly the work of Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Gaussian Assumption:** The assumption of Gaussian measurement vectors is a significant limitation. The reviewer’s question regarding the necessity of this assumption is valid. While the authors likely leveraged Gaussian tools in their analysis, a discussion of potential extensions to other measurement matrix distributions would be beneficial.  Why does the result *require* Gaussianity?
*   **Regularizer Selection:** The paper discusses convex regularizers but does not address the practical issue of *choosing* the regularizer based on the set K containing x0. A more detailed discussion of this selection problem, perhaps including guidance on how to determine appropriate regularization parameters, would increase the paper's practical value.
*   **Dependence on µ and σ:** The reviewer correctly points out the dependence of the theorem on µ and σ, which characterize link difficulty. The authors should address whether these dependencies are truly necessary, or if the result could be further generalized to reduce sensitivity to these parameters. Are there cases where the error is independent of these parameters?
*   **Missing Citation (Yi et al., 2015):** As noted earlier, omitting Yi et al.’s work is a significant oversight, given its close connection to the paper’s central theme.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While Figures 1 and 2 illustrate the accuracy of the prediction, more extensive numerical experiments with diverse link functions and signal structures would further strengthen the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result with potentially significant implications for signal estimation under nonlinear measurements.
*   The rigorous analysis and precise error bounds provide valuable insights into the behavior of the generalized LASSO.
*   The application to quantized measurements demonstrates the practical relevance of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong Gaussian assumption limits the applicability of the results.
*   The lack of discussion regarding regularizer selection and the dependence on µ and σ reduce the paper’s practical guidance.
*   The omission of Yi et al. (2015) weakens the related work section.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a worthwhile contribution to the field. While the limitations regarding the Gaussian assumption and practical considerations should be addressed, the theoretical novelty and rigor of the analysis are compelling. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the reviewer's concerns, particularly the inclusion of the Yi et al. (2015) citation, and providing a discussion on the Gaussian assumption and the implications of relaxing it. I would also suggest addressing the question of how to best choose the regularizer parameter in practice.
