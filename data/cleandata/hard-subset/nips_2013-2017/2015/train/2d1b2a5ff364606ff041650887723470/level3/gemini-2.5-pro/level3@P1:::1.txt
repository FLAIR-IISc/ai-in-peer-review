This paper studies the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal x0 from non-linear measurements of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where A is a matrix with i.i.d. Gaussian entries. The authors analyze the performance of the Generalized LASSO (specifically, the square-root LASSO formulation), a method ostensibly designed for linear models, when applied directly to this non-linear problem. The main contribution is a precise, asymptotic characterization of the mean squared error. The central and elegant result is that, in the high-dimensional limit, the performance of the LASSO with these non-linear measurements is equivalent to its performance on an associated linear problem with measurements y_i = μa_i^T x0 + σz_i, where z_i are i.i.d. standard normal, and the constants μ and σ depend only on the statistics of the non-linear function g.

This work builds upon a rich history. The connection between single-index models and linear regression with Gaussian designs was first established in a classical work by Brillinger for the unregularized, fixed-dimension (n) and large-sample (m) regime. More recently, Plan and Vershynin extended this line of thinking to the high-dimensional, regularized setting, providing non-asymptotic upper bounds on the estimation error that highlighted the critical role of the same μ and σ parameters. This paper significantly strengthens these results by moving from loose upper bounds to asymptotically exact predictions of the error, leveraging techniques from the line of work on the precise analysis of LASSO, such as those based on the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT). The derived formulas precisely capture the interplay between the problem dimensions (m, n), the signal structure (via the regularizer f), the choice of λ, and the non-linearity g (via μ and σ).

**Quality:** The paper presents a high-quality, technically deep piece of work. The theoretical claims are significant and appear to be sound, providing a precise formula for the asymptotic mean squared error. These theoretical predictions are convincingly validated through numerical simulations in Figures 1 and 2 for various signal structures (sparse, group-sparse) and distributions. The work is self-contained and complete. One weakness is the presence of numerous typographical errors throughout the manuscript, particularly in the appendices containing the proofs. While these do not appear to invalidate the main results, they detract from the overall polish and require a careful proofreading pass.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating the contribution within the relevant literature (Brillinger, Plan & Vershynin, precise LASSO analysis), and summarizing the main results. The core message—that the non-linear problem behaves identically to a specific linear one—is communicated effectively and is a powerful, easily understood takeaway. The specialization of the general error formula to specific cases like sparse and group-sparse recovery is helpful for readers familiar with those applications. The main weakness in clarity, as noted above, is the density of typos.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the idea that non-linear measurements can be treated as linear ones in the presence of Gaussianity has been explored before, this paper is the first to provide an asymptotically *exact* performance characterization for the regularized LASSO in the high-dimensional regime. It turns the intuition suggested by prior work into a precise, quantitative statement. The derivation of these precise error formulas for a general class of regularizers and non-linearities is a novel contribution that extends the state-of-the-art in the analysis of LASSO-type estimators. The reviewer is not an expert in the broader single-index model or sufficient dimension reduction literature, so it is possible that some related work has been missed. However, within the context of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing, the contribution is clearly novel and significant.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential for both theoreticians and practitioners.
1.  For theory, it provides a powerful analytical tool, effectively reducing the complex analysis of a non-linear estimation problem to the more tractable analysis of a linear one. This can enable further theoretical developments for problems involving non-linearities.
2.  For practice, it provides strong theoretical justification for a very simple and widely-used method (the LASSO) in settings where it might not seem directly applicable, such as quantized or saturated measurements.
3.  The application to optimal q-bit quantizer design is a compelling example of the utility of the results. By showing that optimizing the quantizer to minimize the LASSO's estimation error is equivalent to minimizing the simple ratio σ²/μ², the authors connect a complex design problem to the well-known Lloyd-Max algorithm. This is a beautiful and practical consequence of the main theorem.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel, precise, and surprisingly clean theoretical result for an important and practical problem.
*   The main finding (asymptotic equivalence to a linear model) provides a powerful conceptual simplification and deepens our understanding of the LASSO.
*   Significantly strengthens and extends important prior work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, moving from bounds to exact characterizations.
*   Demonstrates the practical utility of the theory with a compelling application to optimal quantizer design.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis is asymptotic in nature, and while simulations show good agreement, the finite-sample performance is not characterized.
*   The manuscript requires careful proofreading to correct numerous typos.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a substantial contribution that provides fundamental new insights into the behavior of the LASSO with non-linear measurements.
