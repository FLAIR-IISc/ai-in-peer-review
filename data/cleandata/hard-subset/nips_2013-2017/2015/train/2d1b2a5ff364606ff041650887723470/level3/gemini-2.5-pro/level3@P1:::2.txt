This paper presents a precise asymptotic analysis of the Generalized LASSO (specifically, the square-root LASSO) for estimating a structured signal from non-linear measurements. The measurement model, often called a single-index model, assumes observations of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where g is a potentially unknown and random non-linear link function. The main contribution is to demonstrate that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit (where m, n -> infinity with m/n fixed), the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimate is equivalent to the error of the same algorithm applied to a corresponding linear model with scaled signal and additive Gaussian noise. Specifically, the non-linear problem behaves identically to a linear problem with measurements yi = μa_i^T x0 + σz_i, where the scaling factor μ and effective noise standard deviation σ are determined solely by the link function g. This work significantly extends the classical results of Brillinger and the more recent non-asymptotic bounds of Plan and Vershynin by providing asymptotically exact error characterizations. The authors leverage this equivalence to derive precise, deterministic formulas for the estimation error (Theorem 2.2), which depend on the problem dimensions, the regularizer, the signal structure, and the parameters μ and σ. The framework is applied to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems, and an interesting application to optimal quantizer design is presented.

**Strengths (Pros):**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's main result is a powerful and elegant extension of the precise asymptotic analysis of LASSO-type estimators. While prior work provided non-asymptotic bounds, this paper delivers the first asymptotically *exact* characterization of the square-root LASSO's performance in the single-index model setting. This provides a much deeper and more quantitative understanding of how these simple estimators handle non-linearities.

2.  **Generality and Rigor:** The theoretical framework is quite general. It accommodates a wide class of convex regularizers and, importantly, relaxes the common assumption of a Gaussian prior on the non-zero entries of the signal x0 (as seen in the specific analysis for sparse recovery in Theorem 2.3). The result that the complex, non-linear problem rigorously simplifies to a canonical linear one in the limit is a clean and impactful finding.

3.  **Clarity of Writing:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent context, clearly positioning the work relative to Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin. The summary of contributions is effective, and the main theorems are stated with precision. The simulation results in Figures 1 and 2 provide compelling validation for the theoretical predictions.

**Weaknesses (Cons):**

1.  **Clarity of the Central Argument:** The main claim of the paper, as stated in Theorem 2.1 and alluded to in the introduction (e.g., lines 139-145), is that the non-linear measurement problem is asymptotically equivalent to a specific linear one. This is a profound statement and the cornerstone of the subsequent results. However, the justification for this equivalence is entirely deferred to the appendix. For the main text to be more self-contained and for the reader to better appreciate the technical novelty, it would be beneficial to include a brief, high-level sketch or intuition of this proof argument in the main text, perhaps preceding the statement of Theorem 2.1.

2.  **Missed Discussion on Practical Implications:** The paper establishes a powerful result for the square-root (`l2`) LASSO. A well-known practical advantage of this formulation over the more standard squared-error (`l2^2`) LASSO is that its oracle regularization parameter (λ) is independent of the noise variance. The paper's main result beautifully maps the non-linear distortion `g` into an effective noise variance `σ^2` for an equivalent linear system. This provides a very strong practical argument for preferring the square-root LASSO in this setting, as one does not need to know or estimate the properties of `g` (and thus `σ^2`) to properly tune λ. This is a significant practical insight that strengthens the paper's impact. A discussion of this point should be included, as it would be of great interest to practitioners.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper appears to be technically sound. The claims are supported by rigorous theoretical analysis, leading to precise, non-trivial predictions. The work is complete, moving from a general theorem to specific instances and applications. The simulations strongly corroborate the theoretical findings.

*   **Clarity:** Good. As mentioned, the paper is well-written and structured. The primary suggestion for improvement is to bring some high-level intuition for the proof of the central "non-linear = linear" equivalence from the appendix into the main text to improve its flow and self-containedness.

*   **Originality:** High. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide asymptotically precise error formulas for a regularized estimator in the single-index model setting. It builds upon a strong foundation of prior work but makes a significant leap from loose bounds to exact characterizations. The extension beyond Gaussian signal priors in the sparse case is also novel and valuable.

*   **Significance:** High. The results are important for both the machine learning theory community and for practitioners. They provide a precise understanding of a widely used heuristic (applying linear models to non-linear data) and justify its effectiveness. The application to optimal quantizer design, showing that minimizing the LASSO error is equivalent to a Lloyd-Max objective, is a beautiful and practical consequence of the theory. The significance would be further amplified by including the suggested discussion on the practical choice between `l2` and `l2^2` LASSO formulations.
