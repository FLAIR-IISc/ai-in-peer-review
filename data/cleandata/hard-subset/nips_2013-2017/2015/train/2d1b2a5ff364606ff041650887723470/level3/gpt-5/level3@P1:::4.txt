This paper studies high-dimensional estimation with nonlinear single-index measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) when x0 has known structure captured by a convex regularizer f, and shows that the generalized LASSO behaves, asymptotically, exactly as if the measurements were linear with an effective gain and noise computed from the link g. The main result (Theorem 2.1) establishes an asymptotic equivalence of errors between the generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements and the same LASSO fed with linear measurements y_lin = A(μ x0) + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). Theorem 2.2 provides an asymptotically exact scalar convex program for the squared error, and specialized results treat sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank settings, recovering and extending precise LASSO error characterizations known for linear models. An application to quantized compressed sensing shows that the optimal quantizer for minimizing LASSO error is given by stationary points of Lloyd–Max, with a simple objective in terms of σ^2/μ^2.

Relation to prior work: The paper substantially strengthens Brillinger’s classical result for LS under link misspecification (fixed n, m → ∞) and the high-dimensional upper bounds of Plan–Vershynin for constrained LASSO by moving to precise asymptotics and covering regularized LASSO with general convex f and arbitrary λ. Compared to precise LASSO analyses for linear models (e.g., via AMP or CGMT), the work shows that the entire effect of nonlinearity enters through μ and σ^2, enabling one to reuse linear-model results. The quantized CS connection and the Lloyd–Max consequence are novel and practically relevant. There is related concurrent work on optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transforms (Yi et al.), including μ = 0 regimes; the present paper focuses on generalized LASSO and exact error characterizations and would benefit from a deeper comparison.

Quality: The claims are precise, technically substantial, and, based on the presentation, appear to rely on modern sharp Gaussian comparison methods (CGMT) and careful handling of convex regularizers via the function F that encodes structure. The specialization to sparse and group-sparse yields clean fixed-point characterizations that match simulations, and the mapping to quantizer design is compelling. A few points would improve rigor and readability:
- Theorem 2.1 states equality of errors “with probability one” in the limit; please clarify whether the statement is equality of the limits of the errors, or almost-sure convergence of the difference to zero.
- Assumption 1 (existence of the pointwise limit defining F) is central. The paper verifies it for several f, but a brief discussion of sufficient conditions (e.g., via law of large numbers/empirical process arguments) would help readers apply the theorem beyond the examples.
- The requirement μ ≠ 0 and σ^2 > 0 excludes important regimes (e.g., even links, symmetric quantizers without dithering). Please delineate precisely what fails when μ = 0 and whether the analysis can accommodate the μ = 0 direction-recovery setting.

Clarity: The paper is well written and well organized. The linkage among Brillinger → Plan–Vershynin → precise CGMT-based analysis is clear. Suggestions:
- Provide an intuitive explanation for why only μ and σ^2 of g matter (e.g., Hermite expansion under Gaussian A and rotational invariance), and whether higher-order components can matter for non-Gaussian A.
- In Section 2.2, add a short recipe for computing F for common f (ℓ1, group, nuclear) and a table of the resulting error fixed-point equations. In the low-rank case, a brief example with a concrete spectral distribution would be helpful.
- For the quantizer design section, include a small experiment showing that Lloyd–Max choices indeed minimize the predicted σ^2/μ^2 and reduce empirical LASSO error.

Originality: The asymptotically exact equivalence between nonlinear and linear measurements for generalized LASSO under general convex regularizers, with explicit scalar optimizations for the error, appears new. The reduction immediately transfers a large body of precise linear-model results and yields new insights (e.g., the quantizer-design objective). This goes beyond prior upper bounds and beyond existing precise results restricted to linear measurements.

Significance: The results are important both theoretically and practically. They offer a unifying view of nonlinear single-index measurements through two scalars (μ, σ) and provide tools to tune λ and f. The quantization application is timely and actionable. The breadth across structures (sparse, block-sparse, low-rank) makes the contribution relevant to a wide audience.

Questions and weaknesses:
- Gaussian A assumption: The results rely on i.i.d. standard normal rows. Is rotational invariance essential? Can the equivalence extend to i.i.d. sub-Gaussian designs (via Lindeberg replacement) or to rotationally invariant ensembles? What breaks under anisotropy (AΣ1/2) and can μ, σ be replaced by design-adjusted quantities?
- Dependence on μ and σ^2: Is it necessary and sufficient that g enters only through μ and σ^2? Under Gaussian A, a Hermite expansion suggests higher-order terms average out; please make this argument explicit and state whether any additional regularity on g is needed (e.g., L2 integrability).
- Choice of regularizer and λ: The theory suggests selecting f to align with the descent cone at μx0 and tuning λ to optimize the scalar program. Can the authors offer guidance, e.g., characterizing λcrit beyond sparsity, or providing heuristics based on F for choosing between group vs. elementwise sparsity?
- μ = 0 regime: Recent work (e.g., Yi et al.) develops estimators that recover x0 (up to scale) even when μ = 0 (e.g., even links, certain 1-bit models). The paper mentions μ ≠ 0 as an assumption; a more thorough comparison would clarify complementarities and limitations.
- Related work: The paper cites single-index and SDR literature (Brillinger, Li & Duan, Ichimura), but a brief connection to sliced inverse regression and other SDR techniques would situate the contribution for readers outside CS/LASSO. The relation to Yi et al. could be expanded beyond a passing mention.

Pros:
- Strong, precise theoretical results that generalize multiple prior lines of work.
- Simple and powerful equivalence principle parameterized by μ and σ^2.
- Coverage of several important structures and explicit fixed-point characterizations.
- Practical implication for quantizer design with simple objective.
- Clear writing and convincing experiments.

Cons:
- Reliance on Gaussian designs; robustness to broader ensembles is unclear.
- Limited discussion of μ = 0 regimes and comparison to recent alternatives.
- Assumption 1 and the function F could be better operationalized for practitioners.
- Low-rank section lacks a worked-out example and empirical validation.

Overall, this is a well-executed and interesting paper with solid theoretical contributions and practical insights. Addressing the questions on design assumptions, μ = 0, and strengthening the comparisons and applications would further increase its impact.
