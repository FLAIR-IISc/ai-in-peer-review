Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation under a single-index (nonlinear link) model yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with i.i.d. Gaussian measurement vectors a_i and an unknown but structured signal x0. The estimator is the generalized square-root LASSO min_x ||y − Ax||2 + λ f(x) with a convex structure-inducing regularizer f. The main claim is an asymptotically precise equivalence: for a broad class of link functions g, as m, n → ∞ with m/n → δ, the error of the generalized LASSO with nonlinear observations equals that of the same estimator applied to an equivalent linear model y_lin = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Building on this, the paper derives an exact scalar optimization predicting the asymptotic MSE and specializes it to sparse, group-sparse, and (partially) low-rank settings. As a consequence, for quantized compressed sensing, optimal quantizer design for LASSO-based recovery reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, and Lloyd–Max emerges as a stationary-point method for this design objective.

This work extends and sharpens classical results of Brillinger on least squares with link violations (fixed n, m → ∞) and recent non-asymptotic error bounds for constrained LASSO by Plan & Vershynin (single-index model with Gaussian A). It also leverages and generalizes the precise error characterizations of LASSO with linear measurements via AMP and the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) (e.g., Bayati & Montanari; Stojnic; Thrampoulidis et al.).

Strengths
- Clear, compelling main message: in high dimensions the generalized LASSO “doesn’t see” the nonlinearity except through (μ, σ^2). This is useful both conceptually and practically.
- Technically solid framework (CGMT) yielding asymptotically exact expressions, not just bounds, for general convex f and all λ.
- Concrete specializations: explicit scalar equations for sparse recovery; extension to group-sparse and a roadmap for nuclear norm.
- Practical implication: principled quantizer design reducing to minimizing σ^2/μ^2; insightful connection to Lloyd–Max.
- Well written overall, with helpful positioning relative to Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin, and informative figures validating predictions.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Gaussian i.i.d. measurement assumption is central. Robustness to sub-Gaussian, rotationally invariant, or structured A (e.g., partial Fourier) is not addressed; this limits applicability.
- The key Assumption 1 (existence of the limit of scaled Moreau envelope of f*) is abstract; while verified for ℓ1 and group ℓ1, the low-rank case is left at a high level and requires additional spectral assumptions. More guidance/examples would help practitioners.
- The equivalence requires μ ≠ 0 and σ^2 finite; even or degenerate links (μ = 0) are excluded. It would be helpful to more clearly delineate which common nonlinearities are excluded and discuss alternative estimators when μ = 0.
- The analysis is asymptotic; finite-sample accuracy is only illustrated empirically. Some discussion of rates or concentration around the asymptotic prediction would strengthen the practical message.
- Numerous typos and minor notational inconsistencies (especially in the proofs) distract from the presentation.

Quality
The technical development appears careful and builds on a mature line of CGMT-based analyses. The equivalence theorem (nonlinear = linear) and the scalar optimization for the asymptotic MSE are plausible and consistent with prior precise analyses in the linear case. Specializations to sparse and group-sparse cases match known results where available and extend them otherwise. While I did not line-by-line verify all proofs, the approach is standard and credible. Empirical results align well with the theory.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and accessible to readers familiar with high-dimensional convex statistics. The role of μ and σ^2 is explained clearly, and the figures are informative. However, there are many typographical errors (e.g., “mesaurements,” “redifining,” missing subscripts/superscripts, occasional symbol overload) and some overly terse statements of technical assumptions (Assumption 1, low-rank specialization). A careful proofreading and a short appendix clarifying when Assumption 1 holds for common f would improve readability.

Originality
The main contributions—an exact asymptotic error characterization for generalized square-root LASSO under nonlinear link and the equivalence to a suitably scaled noisy linear model—are, to my knowledge, new. They significantly strengthen prior non-asymptotic bounds and extend precise LASSO error analyses beyond the linear observation model and beyond ℓ1 to other convex regularizers. The quantizer design corollary is neat and, while related to classical Lloyd–Max, is original in its objective.

Significance
The results are likely to be useful to researchers working on high-dimensional M-estimation, compressed sensing with nonlinearities (quantization, saturation, censoring), and practitioners interested in robust sensing with simple convex estimators. The reduction to (μ, σ^2) gives a unifying lens and a practical design rule. While the Gaussian A assumption narrows scope, the conceptual insight is impactful.

Questions and suggestions
- Robustness: How sensitive is the equivalence to deviations from Gaussian A (e.g., sub-Gaussian rows, orthogonally invariant ensembles, or mildly dependent rows)? Any heuristic or empirical evidence?
- Estimating μ, σ^2: In practice, g is often unknown. Can μ and σ^2 be estimated from (A, y) (e.g., via moments of y and Ax, or Stein’s identities) and plugged into risk predictions or rescaling?
- Finite-sample behavior: Can you provide confidence bands or deviations around the asymptotic MSE, at least for sparse ℓ1?
- Low-rank case: Please give a concrete example where the spectral limit exists (e.g., spiked model) and show a numerical evaluation of F and the resulting error curve.
- Literature: I am less familiar with the single-index/SDR literature; please ensure connections to Ichimura (SLS), Li & Duan (link violation), and recent semiparametric work are complete and highlight differences (structured high-dimensional regime, convex regularization, precise error).

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, clean main theorem with broad conceptual value.
- Technically solid, extends precise error analyses to a new and important setting.
- Useful practical corollary for quantizer design.
- Clear empirical validation.

Cons for acceptance
- Restricted to Gaussian measurement matrices and μ ≠ 0; limited robustness discussion.
- Abstract assumption and partial low-rank treatment.
- Many typos and minor clarity issues in the proofs.

Author response and other reviews did not change my assessment.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a substantive theoretical advance with practical implications and is appropriate for NIPS. A revision addressing clarity/typos and adding a brief robustness/low-rank discussion would further strengthen it.
