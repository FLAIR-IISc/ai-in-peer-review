This paper studies the performance of the Generalized LASSO (specifically, the square-root LASSO) for estimating a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. The measurement vectors `a_i` are assumed to have i.i.d. Gaussian entries, and the non-linear link function `g` can be random and unknown. The main contribution is a precise asymptotic characterization of the estimation error in the high-dimensional regime where the number of measurements `m` and the signal dimension `n` grow to infinity with a fixed ratio.

The central and surprising result is that the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimate in this non-linear setting is asymptotically identical to the error of a LASSO estimate obtained from an equivalent *linear* model with additive Gaussian noise: `y_i = μa_i^T x0 + σz_i`. The effective signal scaling `μ` and noise level `σ` are determined solely by the first and second moments of the link function `g` with respect to a standard Gaussian input. This work significantly extends the classical results of Brillinger, which established a similar connection for unregularized least-squares in a low-dimensional setting (`m -> ∞`, `n` fixed), and the more recent work of Plan and Vershynin, which provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained LASSO formulation. By providing asymptotically exact error formulas, this paper brings the analysis of LASSO with non-linear measurements to the same level of precision recently achieved for linear models via frameworks like Approximate Message Passing (AMP) and the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT). It should be noted that the reviewer is not an expert in the broader single-index model or sufficient dimension reduction literature, so there may be other relevant prior work not discussed here.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The main result establishing the asymptotic equivalence between the non-linear measurement model and a corresponding linear one is both novel and highly significant. It provides a powerful and simplifying insight into a complex problem. This result allows the vast and growing body of literature on the precise analysis of LASSO in the linear case to be directly applied to the non-linear setting. The paper demonstrates this by providing precise, and in some cases new, error characterizations for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper presents a strong, technically sound theoretical analysis. The derivations lead to precise, deterministic formulas for the asymptotic mean-squared error, which are expressed as the solution to a low-dimensional convex optimization problem. These theoretical predictions are shown to be remarkably accurate via numerical simulations.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the contributions within the context of related work. The main theorems are stated clearly, and their implications are discussed effectively. The application to finding the optimal quantizer (the Lloyd-Max quantizer) for compressive sensing is a compelling example of the theory's utility.

### Weaknesses

*   **Clarity and Quality of Presentation:** The primary weakness of the paper is the presence of numerous typographical errors, particularly in the appendices containing the proofs. While the main results in the body of the paper appear correct and the overall argument is convincing, the typos in the technical sections detract from the paper's quality and make it difficult for an expert to verify the derivations without significant effort. This needs to be carefully addressed before publication.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a fundamental and elegant result that simplifies the understanding of a complex, non-linear estimation problem.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Analysis:** The work provides the first asymptotically precise performance characterization for the regularized LASSO with non-linear measurements, advancing the state-of-the-art in this area.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The framework is general, applying to a wide class of convex regularizers and non-linearities. The demonstrated application to optimal quantization highlights its practical relevance.
4.  **Well-Written and Motivated:** Despite the typos, the paper is easy to follow and effectively communicates the importance of its results.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

1.  **Numerous Typos:** The density of typos, especially in the proofs, is a significant concern that impacts the paper's perceived polish and makes verification difficult. This is the main point against acceptance in its current form.

In summary, this is a very strong paper with a significant and novel theoretical contribution. The results are important and likely to be influential. The main drawback is the lack of careful proofreading, which is a correctable issue. I strongly recommend acceptance, with the condition that the authors perform a thorough revision to correct the typographical errors throughout the manuscript, particularly in the appendices.
