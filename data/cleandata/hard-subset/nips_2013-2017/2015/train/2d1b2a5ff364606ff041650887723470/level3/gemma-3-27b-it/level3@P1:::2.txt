This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO for estimating signals from nonlinear measurements. The authors demonstrate, under fairly general conditions, that the estimation error with nonlinear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of a linear model with scaled measurements and additive Gaussian noise. This extends previous work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, providing explicit and precise expressions for the error, holding for high-dimensional settings (m, n growing large) and a wide class of regularizers.  A particularly interesting consequence is the identification of the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error, showing it’s the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

The work builds upon a rich history of research in areas like single-index models (Brillinger, Ichimura, Li & Duan), compressed sensing (Donoho & Johnstone, Candès & Tao, Plan & Vershynin), and regularized regression (Tibshirani, Yuan & Lin). The paper appropriately cites relevant literature and positions itself within this context.  The connection drawn to Brillinger’s earlier work (1982) is particularly valuable, demonstrating the evolution of understanding in this area.  The authors also clearly relate their results to recent advances in precise analysis of the LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., Oymak et al., Thrampoulidis et al.). 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty & Originality:**  The precise asymptotic error analysis for the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements is a significant contribution. Deriving explicit expressions for the error and demonstrating its equivalence to a scaled linear model is a substantial advancement. The extension to different regularizers (beyond the `1-norm) is also noteworthy.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper appears mathematically rigorous. The derivations and proofs (referenced in the appendix) seem well-structured, and the assumptions are clearly stated.  The use of the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework and connection to recent work in high-dimensional statistics strengthens the theoretical foundation.
*   **Significance:** The results have potential implications for various applications where nonlinear measurements are common, such as signal processing, machine learning, and econometrics. The identification of the optimal quantizer is a particularly valuable practical insight.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the key ideas are explained in a clear and concise manner. The motivation for the work is well-established in the introduction.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Main-text Clarity:** While the equivalence between the single-index model and the scaled linear model is a core result, its initial presentation in the main text is somewhat understated. The reference to the detailed explanation being relegated to Appendix A (line 140) feels insufficient; a brief summary within the main flow would improve readability and understanding.
*   **Practical Implications & Comparison:** The paper demonstrates that the estimation performance is *asymptotically the same* as a scaled linear model. However, it doesn't thoroughly explore the practical implications of this equivalence. Since the optimal regularization parameter for the l2-LASSO does *not* depend on the noise variance, the authors should discuss whether their results support a preference for using the l2-LASSO over alternatives like the l2^2-LASSO in practice.  A deeper dive into the consequences for parameter tuning would enhance the paper’s practical relevance.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The optimization problem presented in Theorem 2.2, while theoretically insightful, may be computationally challenging to solve in practice. While the mention of the Lloyd-Max algorithm for quantization is helpful, a brief discussion of the computational complexity of finding the optimal parameters in general settings would be valuable.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advancement in understanding the performance of the LASSO with nonlinear measurements.
*   The results are mathematically rigorous and build upon a strong foundation of existing research.
*   The findings have potential practical implications for various applications.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the equivalence to a scaled linear model limits the paper's direct impact.
*   The computational complexity of solving the optimization problem in Theorem 2.2 may be a barrier to real-world application.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. While there are minor areas for improvement, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and potential impact of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the points raised regarding practical implications and computational complexity. Specifically, clarifying the connection between the theoretical equivalence and practical algorithm choices, and briefly discussing the computational burden of applying the results, would substantially strengthen the paper.
