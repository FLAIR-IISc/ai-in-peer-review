### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x₀` from measurements that have passed through a non-linear and potentially random link function, i.e., `yᵢ = gᵢ(aᵢᵀx₀)`. The authors analyze the performance of the Generalized LASSO estimator, which is formally designed for a linear model but is applied here directly to the non-linear measurements. The central contribution is a precise, asymptotic characterization of the estimator's mean-squared error. The main result (Theorem 2.1) establishes that in the high-dimensional limit with i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, the estimation error of the LASSO with non-linear measurements is identical to that of a LASSO problem with linear measurements of the form `yᵢ = µaᵢᵀx₀ + σzᵢ`, where `zᵢ` is standard Gaussian noise. The constants `µ` and `σ` depend only on the statistical properties of the link function `g`. This extends previous work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin by replacing non-asymptotic upper bounds with exact asymptotic formulas. The paper provides a general framework for calculating this error (Theorem 2.2) and applies it to specific cases of sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank signal recovery. Finally, it presents an interesting application to optimal `q`-bit quantizer design, showing that minimizing the estimation error is equivalent to minimizing the ratio `σ²/µ²`.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Quality and Significance:** The paper provides a strong theoretical result that is both elegant and significant. The equivalence between the non-linear measurement problem and an effective linear one offers a clear and intuitive understanding of a complex phenomenon. Moving from the upper bounds of prior work to precise, asymptotically exact error characterizations is a substantial technical advance that provides a much deeper understanding of the LASSO's performance in this setting.
2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The main theorems are stated precisely, and their implications are discussed effectively through special cases and a practical application. The experimental results in Figures 1 and 2 provide compelling validation for the theoretical predictions.
3.  **Originality:** While building on the foundations laid by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, the work is original in its derivation of exact asymptotic error formulas for the regularized LASSO in the non-linear setting. The application of these results to quantizer design, connecting it to the Lloyd-Max algorithm, is also a novel contribution.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

The paper presents valuable results, but several points warrant clarification and discussion to strengthen the manuscript.

1.  **On the Gaussian Measurement Assumption:** The analysis throughout the paper assumes that the entries of the measurement matrix `A` are i.i.d. Gaussian. Could the authors elaborate on the necessity of this assumption? Is this a technical limitation of the proof technique (e.g., required for the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem), or is there a more fundamental reason? A discussion on the potential for extending these results to broader classes of random matrices, such as sub-gaussian ensembles, would be very valuable for understanding the generality of the findings.
2.  **On the Choice of Regularizer:** The paper frames the problem using a general convex regularizer `f(x)`. In many practical settings, prior knowledge about the signal `x₀` is expressed as membership in a convex set `K`. The manuscript could be improved by including a brief discussion on the practical matter of how one should choose the regularizer `f(x)` based on a given constraint set `K`.
3.  **On the Dependence on `µ` and `σ`:** A key takeaway is that the complex, non-linear function `g` impacts the asymptotic error only through the two scalars `µ` and `σ`. This is a powerful simplification. Could the authors provide more intuition as to why higher-order moments or other characteristics of `g` vanish in the high-dimensional limit? This would help the reader build a more fundamental understanding of the result.
4.  **Omission of Relevant Literature:** The paper's positioning could be improved by acknowledging and discussing its relationship to other relevant work.
    *   The problem falls under the umbrella of single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction, which have a long history in statistics. While some seminal works are cited, acknowledging this broader context would be appropriate.
    *   More importantly, the paper appears to have missed the recent and closely related work of Yi et al. ("Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform," arXiv:1505.03257, 2015). That paper also establishes an equivalence between linear and non-linear estimation in high dimensions for structured signals. Notably, their framework handles the `µ=0` case, which this paper explicitly excludes but is important for certain link functions (e.g., even functions). A thorough comparison with the results of Yi et al. is essential for assessing the precise novelty and contribution of this work.

### **Summary of Evaluation**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a high-quality, technically sound, and significant theoretical result. It provides the first precise asymptotic error characterization for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, which is a clear advance over existing literature. The paper is well-written and the theory is supported by convincing numerical simulations.
*   **Arguments against acceptance:** The main concern is the omission of highly relevant prior work, particularly Yi et al. (2015), which addresses a very similar problem. This makes it difficult to fully assess the paper's originality and contribution without a direct comparison. The limitations of the analysis (Gaussian matrices, `μ ≠ 0`) could also be discussed more thoroughly.

Overall, this is a very interesting paper with a strong core contribution. However, the authors must address the relationship between their work and the missing citations to properly contextualize their results. I would be willing to increase my rating if this is clarified satisfactorily in the author response.
