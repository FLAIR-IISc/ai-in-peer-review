This paper studies high-dimensional estimation under a single-index/nonlinear measurement model yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where A has iid standard Gaussian rows, x0 is structured, and g may be unknown and random. The authors analyze the generalized square-root LASSO (minimize ||y − Ax||2 + λ f(x) with convex f) and prove a striking equivalence: asymptotically, the estimation error of LASSO under nonlinear measurements is identical to that under an effective linear model yi = μ a_i^T x0 + σ z_i, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2] for γ ∼ N(0,1). They further provide a precise scalar convex program whose unique optimizer gives the asymptotic squared-error, and they specialize the formula to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank settings. An application to q-bit quantization shows that minimizing the LASSO error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, leading to the Lloyd–Max quantizer as optimal.

Relation to prior work: This extends classical results of Brillinger on LS with link-misspecification (consistency up to proportionality when m → ∞ and n fixed) and recent nonasymptotic upper bounds by Plan and Vershynin for constrained LASSO under nonlinear links. It connects to precise error analyses for LASSO with linear measurements via AMP and the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT), including works by Bayati–Montanari, Donoho–Maleki–Montanari, Stojnic, and Thrampoulidis et al. Relative to sufficient dimension reduction and single-index literature (e.g., Ichimura; Li & Duan), the contribution is to the high-dimensional, structured, convex-regularized setting with precise asymptotics under Gaussian designs. The quantization application relates to 1-bit and q-bit compressed sensing and clarifies optimal quantizer design for LASSO-based recovery.

Quality: The main theorems are technically strong and well-motivated. The equivalence result (nonlinear-to-linear with parameters μ, σ derived from g) is intuitive and the proof framework (CGMT-like arguments) appears sound. The precise scalar program yields concrete, computable predictions; the specializations to ℓ1, group-ℓ1,2, and nuclear-norm are useful, and the sparse case matches and extends prior precise analyses in the linear case. The assumptions are clearly delineated: iid Gaussian A; independent link instances; finite moments and μ ≠ 0; linear m,n asymptotics; and a technical regularity (Assumption 1) that reduces to explicit, verifiable forms for common regularizers. Simulations corroborate the theory across regimes (δ < 1 and δ > 1). Limitations are that (i) the Gaussian design is essential to the proof and scope; robustness to sub-Gaussian or structured A is not addressed; (ii) the μ = 0 case (e.g., odd/even links or extreme quantizers) is excluded; (iii) finite-sample deviations and guidance on tuning λ beyond asymptotics are limited; and (iv) the application to quantization would benefit from comparison to estimators tailored to 1-bit/q-bit CS.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful positioning versus Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin and with concrete examples. The presentation of the scalar optimization and specialized forms is accessible. However, there are numerous typos and minor inconsistencies, especially in the proofs and notation (e.g., occasional mismatches between f, f*, and their Moreau envelopes; normalization factors; swapping of linear/“square-root” LASSO terminology). A careful proofreading pass is needed. Providing more intuition around Assumption 1, and moving some definitions (Fenchel conjugates, envelopes) upfront would improve readability.

Originality: To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact characterization of generalized LASSO error under unknown nonlinear link functions, unifying many nonlinearities via (μ,σ). The equivalence theorem and the precise scalar program go beyond earlier upper bounds and extend precise linear-measurement analyses to a broader setting and wider class of regularizers. The Lloyd–Max consequence for optimal quantization tailored to LASSO error is a neat and practically relevant insight.

Significance: The results sharpen our understanding of LASSO’s behavior under nonlinear measurement systems, a common situation in sensing and learning with saturation, quantization, or unknown device nonlinearities. The ability to port precise linear results to the nonlinear case via (μ,σ) should influence both theory and practice (e.g., error prediction, λ tuning, and quantizer design). The Gaussian design restriction tempers generality, but within this scope the impact is high.

Pros for acceptance:
- Precise, novel asymptotic theory connecting nonlinear to effective linear models for generalized LASSO.
- Clear scalar characterization usable across regularizers; insightful specializations (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank).
- Practical implication for quantizer design (Lloyd–Max optimality for minimizing LASSO error).
- Careful positioning with prior work; convincing experiments.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong reliance on iid Gaussian designs; limited discussion of robustness to sub-Gaussian/structured matrices.
- Excludes μ = 0 cases; limited guidance when the proportionality constant vanishes.
- Finite-sample guidance and λ selection discussion could be expanded.
- Many typos and minor notational inconsistencies, particularly in appendices.

Suggestions and questions:
- Discuss extensions (or barriers) to sub-Gaussian designs, partial orthogonality, or structured sensing (e.g., Fourier).
- Clarify practical computation of F(c1,c2,c3) for nuclear-norm regularization; include more numerical examples for low-rank.
- Provide a short section on estimating μ and σ from data when g is unknown, and how estimation error affects predictions.
- Elaborate on λ tuning recommendations informed by the scalar program; include sensitivity plots.
- Compare against specialized methods for quantized/1-bit CS (e.g., BIHT, logistic/probit-Lasso), both in theory and empirics.
- Add a brief discussion of the μ = 0 regime and whether alternative objectives (e.g., hinge/logistic losses) avoid the limitation.
- Proofread carefully; fix typos in definitions (conjugates/envelopes), normalization constants, and theorem statements.

Author responses and other reviews did not change my overall assessment. While I am less familiar with the single-index/sufficient-dimension-reduction literature and may have missed related citations, the core high-dimensional convex-regularized contribution appears solid, original, and significant within the NeurIPS scope. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on a careful editorial pass and strengthened discussion of assumptions and robustness.
