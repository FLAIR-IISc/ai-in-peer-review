

This paper investigates the problem of estimating structured signals from non-linear measurements using the Generalized LASSO. The authors extend classical results by Brillinger (1981) and Plan & Vershynin (2013) to provide precise asymptotic characterizations of the estimation error when measurements are transformed by an unknown nonlinear link function g. Their main contribution (Theorem 2.1) demonstrates that for Gaussian measurement matrices, the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements yi = gi(ai^T x0) is asymptotically equivalent to the same algorithm applied to linear measurements yi = μai^T x0 + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ)-μγ)²] for γ ~ N(0,1). This elegant equivalence allows the authors to leverage existing precise error analysis for LASSO with linear measurements to characterize performance in non-linear scenarios.

The paper's primary strength lies in its precise asymptotic error characterization (Theorem 2.2), which provides a deterministic convex optimization problem to compute the limiting error. This is the first known precise result in this context, significantly advancing the understanding of Generalized LASSO performance with non-linear measurements. The authors demonstrate the framework's applicability to multiple structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) with specific examples and validation through numerical experiments. The practical implication that the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error is the Lloyd-Max quantizer is particularly compelling, with clear applications in q-bit compressive sensing.

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and effective figures illustrating the theoretical results. The technical content is strong, with rigorous proofs (despite some typos) that carefully address the asymptotic regime where m/n = δ ∈ (0,∞) and both grow large. The authors properly relate their work to previous contributions, particularly the recent advances in precise LASSO analysis using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework.

The main weaknesses are the numerous typos throughout the manuscript, particularly in the proofs (e.g., inconsistent use of subscripts, incorrect indices), which could distract readers attempting to verify the theoretical arguments. Additionally, the reviewer, while familiar with LASSO theory, is less familiar with the single-index model literature in statistics, and it's possible that some relevant prior work may have been missed.

The paper demonstrates high quality through its rigorous theoretical analysis, precise characterization of the error, and careful validation through multiple examples. The significance is substantial, as the results provide a unifying framework for understanding LASSO performance in non-linear measurement scenarios, which are common in practical applications like sensor networks, medical imaging, and communication systems. The connection to optimal quantizer design represents a concrete practical contribution.

The paper is highly original in its precise characterization of LASSO performance with non-linear measurements, extending previous work by Plan & Vershynin with tighter asymptotic guarantees. While the single-index model has been studied in statistics, the precise characterization of LASSO performance under such models appears to be novel, especially for structured signals.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper provides the first precise asymptotic characterization of Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, which is both theoretically significant and practically relevant. The connection to optimal quantizer design (Lloyd-Max) is particularly compelling. The paper is well-structured, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field.

Con arguments for rejection: The numerous typos in the proofs could undermine confidence in the theoretical claims. While the paper is well-written overall, these errors require correction.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of structured signal recovery with non-linear measurements. The precise error characterization is novel and valuable, and the practical implications for measurement system design are compelling. The paper's quality and significance warrant acceptance, though the authors should carefully proofread the manuscript to correct the typos, particularly in the proofs. Given its theoretical depth, clear presentation, and practical relevance, this paper is well-suited for NIPS and represents a meaningful advance in the field.
