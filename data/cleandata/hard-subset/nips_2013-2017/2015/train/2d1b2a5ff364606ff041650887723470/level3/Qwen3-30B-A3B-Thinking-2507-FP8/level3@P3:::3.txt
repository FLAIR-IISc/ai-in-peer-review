

**Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the analysis of structured signal recovery with non-linear measurements. The authors extend the foundational work of Brillinger (1980s) and Plan & Vershynin (2013) by providing precise asymptotic error formulas for the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurement models. Their key insight establishes that for Gaussian measurement matrices, the Generalized LASSO with measurements yi = gi(ai^T x0) behaves asymptotically like the Generalized LASSO with linear measurements yi = μ(ai^T x0) + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ)-μγ)²] with γ ~ N(0,1).

The main technical contribution is Theorem 2.2, which characterizes the asymptotic error ‖x̂ - μx0‖² as the solution to a convex program involving the Moreau envelope of the regularizer. This extends recent precise analysis of the LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., Donoho, Maleki, Montanari; Thrampoulidis et al.) to the non-linear measurement setting. The authors validate their results through extensive experiments across special cases (Lasso, group Lasso, nuclear norm) and demonstrate an insightful application to q-bit compressive sensing, where they prove the optimal quantizer is the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

The paper's quality is excellent - the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the derivations are well-justified, and the empirical validation is thorough. The clarity is generally good, though the paper would benefit from a more balanced presentation of details given space constraints. The originality is strong - the explicit error expressions represent a significant extension of prior work, and the connection between non-linear measurements and scaled linear measurements is novel.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a fundamental understanding of how non-linear measurements affect structured signal recovery, with implications for applications where measurement nonlinearities are present or designed (e.g., quantized measurements). The application to q-bit CS quantizer design is particularly compelling, showing how theoretical insights directly inform practical design.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses that could be addressed in revision: The paper refers to the Generalized LASSO as an "algorithm" rather than a convex program or decoder; the "proximal function" should be clarified as the Moreau envelope; Assumption 1 could be given a more formal interpretation using variational analysis; and Figure 1 caption should render "n=768" as $n=768$. There's also a typo on page 5, line 247, and the distinction between "regularized" and "penalized" formulations should be clarified (they are equivalent with appropriate parameter mapping).

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community, building upon recent advances in precise LASSO analysis while extending to a more general measurement model. The results are technically sound, well-supported by theory and experiments, and provide valuable insights for researchers in high-dimensional statistics and signal processing.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Novel precise error formulas extending Plan & Vershynin's work; fundamental insight that non-linear measurements can be treated as scaled linear measurements; insightful application to q-bit CS quantizer design; thorough empirical validation.

Con arguments for acceptance: Minor formatting issues; some unbalanced presentation of details; the "regularized vs penalized" distinction could be clarified.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. It provides precise theoretical insights with practical implications, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the formatting and terminology issues noted above.
