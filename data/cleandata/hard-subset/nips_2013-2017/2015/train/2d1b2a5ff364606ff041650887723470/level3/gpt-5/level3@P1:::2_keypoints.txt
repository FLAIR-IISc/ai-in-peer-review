- Main contribution: asymptotically exact error characterization for l2-LASSO under non-linear single-index measurements, showing equivalence to a linear model with effective signal μx0 and noise variance σ^2 (Theorems 2.1–2.2).  
- Technical result: precise scalar optimization predicting asymptotic squared error, proved via CGMT.  
- Specializations: explicit formulas for sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2) and low-rank (nuclear norm) regularizers; detailed sparse-case result (Theorem 2.3) matches simulations.  
- Quantization application: minimizing asymptotic LASSO error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 over quantizer parameters, yielding Lloyd–Max optimality.  
- Relation to prior work: unifies and strengthens Brillinger (single-index) and Plan–Vershynin (non-asymptotic LASSO) and extends AMP/CGMT precise LASSO analyses to non-linear measurements and broader priors.  
- Assumptions: results require Gaussian design A, proportional asymptotics (m/n→δ), and an abstract Assumption 1 on p(x0) and f.  
- Quality: rigorous, careful analysis with clearly stated theorems and explicit assumptions; simulations corroborate theory.  
- Presentation weaknesses: Assumption 1 is abstract and needs concrete sufficient conditions/examples; practical guidance on tuning λ and choosing loss (ℓ2 vs ℓ2^2) is limited.  
- Clarity issues: the central non-linear=linear equivalence is placed in the appendix and should appear in the main text; reconcile ℓ2-LASSO vs square-root LASSO early; streamline x0 normalization and give a recipe for computing F(c1,c2,c3).  
- Originality: first asymptotically exact error analysis for LASSO-type estimators under general single-index non-linear measurements and broad convex regularizers; Lloyd–Max consequence is novel.  
- Significance: provides an interpretable bridge from non-linear to linear models, enabling transfer of LASSO theory and practical performance prediction/design.  
- Practical questions to address: (i) empirical/theoretical guidance on preferring ℓ2-LASSO vs ℓ2^2-LASSO when g (hence σ) is unknown; (ii) whether μ and σ can be reliably estimated from (A,y); (iii) concrete sufficient conditions for Assumption 1 on common f and p(x0).  
- Pros for acceptance: strong novel theory, broad regularizer/ prior coverage, clear advancement over prior work, useful quantization result, simulation support.  
- Cons/weaknesses: reliance on Gaussian A (no universality discussion), central equivalence relegated to appendix, limited practical λ/loss guidance, abstract Assumption 1, limited empirical exploration of robustness and μ=0 cases.  
- Minor suggestions: explicitly note identifiability issues when μ=0; give a concrete low-rank example for empirical singular value assumptions and how to compute F numerically; clarify Lloyd–Max optimization landscape and initialization/sensitivity.
