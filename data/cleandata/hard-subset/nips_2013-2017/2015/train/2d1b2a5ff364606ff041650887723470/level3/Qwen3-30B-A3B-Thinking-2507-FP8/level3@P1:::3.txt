

This paper investigates the Generalized LASSO for non-linear measurements of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), where gi are independent copies of a link function g. The authors establish that, under Gaussian measurement matrices and in the asymptotic regime, the estimation error of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to that of the same algorithm applied to linear measurements of the form yi = μ(ai^T x0) + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2] for γ ~ N(0,1). This significantly extends prior work by Brillinger (who considered the non-regularized case) and Plan & Vershynin (who provided loose upper bounds for the regularized case).

The main contribution is a precise asymptotic error formula for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, expressed through a convex optimization program involving the Fenchel conjugate and Moreau envelope of the regularizer. The authors derive explicit expressions for specific cases (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank signals) and demonstrate their accuracy through simulations. A particularly insightful application is to q-bit compressive sensing, where they show that the optimal quantizer is the celebrated Lloyd-Max quantizer.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs building on recent advances in the analysis of the Generalized LASSO for linear measurements (e.g., the CGMT framework). The theoretical results are novel, providing the first known precise results for this problem. The application to q-bit CS quantizer design is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates how theoretical insights can directly inform practical design choices.

Strengths include the theoretical novelty, broad applicability across various regularizers and link functions, and the practical significance of the quantizer design application. The empirical validation across multiple settings (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) confirms the theoretical predictions. The paper carefully addresses the limitations of the assumptions and provides appropriate context for their results.

The paper has minor weaknesses in presentation clarity. The main results (Theorem 2.2) are somewhat dense and could be made more accessible to a broader audience. The discussion of Assumption 1 would benefit from a more formal interpretation using variational analysis. The paper incorrectly refers to the Generalized LASSO as an "algorithm" rather than a convex program, and the "proximal function" is actually the Moreau envelope (not the proximal operator), requiring notation correction. There are minor formatting issues (Figure 1 caption should use proper LaTeX formatting for "n=768") and a typo on page 5.

Quality is excellent: the theoretical results are well-supported by rigorous analysis and empirical validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their assumptions and provide appropriate context for their results. The paper represents a complete and well-executed piece of work.

Clarity is generally good but could be improved. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of the main theoretical results and their implications. The application to quantizer design is particularly well-motivated and insightful.

Originality is strong: this is the first known precise characterization of Generalized LASSO performance with non-linear measurements. It extends Plan & Vershynin's work by providing precise error expressions rather than loose upper bounds, and establishes a clean equivalence between non-linear and scaled noisy linear measurements.

Significance is high: the results provide a deeper understanding of how the Generalized LASSO behaves under non-linear measurements, which is relevant to many practical applications (e.g., 1-bit CS, quantized measurements). The theoretical justification for the Lloyd-Max quantizer provides a concrete example of how theoretical insights can directly inform practical design choices in compressive sensing systems.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by bridging a gap between linear and non-linear measurement models. It represents a substantial step forward in understanding the behavior of the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements, which has important implications for both theoretical and applied signal processing. The results are well-supported, the analysis is rigorous, and the application to quantizer design is particularly compelling. The minor issues with presentation and notation are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's substantial contributions. I recommend acceptance.
