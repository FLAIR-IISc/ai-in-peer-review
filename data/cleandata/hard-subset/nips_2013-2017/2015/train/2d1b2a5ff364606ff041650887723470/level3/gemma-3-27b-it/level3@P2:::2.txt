This paper presents a significant contribution to the understanding of the Generalized LASSO in the context of non-linear measurements. The authors establish a precise asymptotic equivalence between the performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements and a linear model with appropriately scaled signals and noise. This builds upon prior work by Brillinger (1982) and Plan & Vershynin (2015), providing significantly tighter and more general results, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The paper is well-written and the technical arguments appear sound.

The core idea is to demonstrate that, despite the non-linearity introduced by the measurement function *g*, the Generalized LASSO behaves asymptotically as if the measurements were linear, scaled by a factor μ (defined as E[γg(γ)] where γ is standard normal) and corrupted by noise with variance σ² (defined as E[(g(γ) − μγ)²]). This is a powerful result as it allows leveraging existing analysis techniques developed for linear models to understand the performance of the LASSO with non-linear measurements. The authors rigorously derive precise expressions for the estimation error, considering various structured signals (sparse, low-rank, etc.) and different regularizers. They also explore an application to q-bit compressive sensing, demonstrating how their results can be used to optimize quantization thresholds and levels, identifying the Lloyd-Max algorithm as a suitable optimization method.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous mathematical analysis, building upon existing literature while significantly extending its scope and precision. The proofs appear carefully constructed, and the assumptions are clearly stated.
*   **Novelty and Originality:** The precise asymptotic characterization of the error in the non-linear measurement setting is a novel contribution. Extending the results of Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin to high-dimensional settings and providing explicit error expressions is a substantial advance.
*   **Generality:** The analysis is quite general, applicable to a wide class of link functions *g* and regularization functions *f*. The discussion of sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery demonstrates the broad applicability of the results.
*   **Practical Implications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing highlights the practical relevance of the work, providing insights into optimal quantization design. The connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is a valuable finding.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the authors provide sufficient context for the reader to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Impact of µ and σ²:** While the paper rigorously defines µ and σ², it would be valuable to discuss how these parameters manifest in practice. Specifically, given that the optimal regularization parameter of the l2-lasso doesn’t depend on the noise variance, should practitioners prefer it over the l2²-lasso? A more detailed exploration of this point would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.
*   **Equivalence Location:** The crucial equivalence between single-index models and scaled linear models (drawing from Brillinger 1982) is relegated to Appendix A. Explicitly mentioning this connection within the main text, particularly near line 140, would enhance the flow and justification of the subsequent analysis.
*   **Assumption 1 – Potential Complexity:** While stated clearly, Assumption 1 regarding the limit of the Fenchel conjugate may require significant effort to verify in some practical scenarios. Further discussion on the types of functions *f* that satisfy this assumption would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in signal processing and statistics – estimation with non-linear measurements.
*   The theoretical results are rigorous, novel, and significantly advance the state-of-the-art.
*   The paper is well-written and presents the technical details clearly.
*   The potential applications, such as q-bit compressive sensing, demonstrate the practical relevance of the work.
*   The analysis provides a deeper understanding of the behavior of the LASSO in non-linear settings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical implications could be further emphasized, particularly concerning the choice between l2-lasso and l2²-lasso.
*   The somewhat hidden location of the key equivalence result (Brillinger 1982) impacts the flow of the main text.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The analysis is rigorous, the results are novel, and the potential applications are promising. While there are minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with a confidence score of 8/10. Addressing the minor points raised above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
