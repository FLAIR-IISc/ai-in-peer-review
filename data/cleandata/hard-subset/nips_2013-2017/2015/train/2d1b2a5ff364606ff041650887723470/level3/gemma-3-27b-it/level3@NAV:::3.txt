## Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

This paper investigates the Generalized Lasso for signal estimation when measurements are taken through a non-linear link function. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, asymptotically, these non-linear measurements can be treated as noisy linear measurements, and deriving precise asymptotic error formulas building upon recent work by Plan & Vershynin. This is a significant step forward, offering a practical understanding of how regularization performs in non-linear measurement settings. The authors build upon Brillinger’s earlier work and provide explicit error characterizations, going beyond previously known bounds.

The paper is technically sound and the derivations appear rigorous. The theoretical results are supported by empirical validation across several important special cases – Lasso, group Lasso, and nuclear norm regularization – demonstrating the broad applicability of their findings. The application to q-bit compressive sensing quantizer design is particularly insightful, linking the analysis to the well-known Lloyd-Max quantizer, offering a novel perspective on optimal quantization strategies in this context. The authors clearly articulate the assumptions and limitations of their analysis. 

However, the paper suffers from a slight imbalance in presentation. While the theoretical results are detailed, the motivation and intuitive explanation for some of the technical choices could be strengthened. The authors acknowledge space constraints, but expanding upon the intuition behind Assumption 1, potentially using variational analysis as suggested, would enhance clarity. There are several minor issues: the figure caption formatting (n=768 should be $n=768$), a typo on page 5, line 247, and the potentially misleading labeling of the “proximal function” (better termed a Moreau envelope).  Additionally, repeatedly referring to the Generalized Lasso as an “algorithm” is inaccurate; it’s more appropriately described as a convex program or a decoder.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and explicit error formulas for a relevant problem in signal processing.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and empirical validation.
*   Insightful application to q-bit CS quantizer design.
*   Builds upon and extends existing work in a meaningful way.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Slightly unbalanced presentation; some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanation.
*   Minor technical issues (caption formatting, typo, nomenclature).



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the minor issues would further improve its clarity and impact. I recommend acceptance, pending minor revisions.
