This paper investigates the asymptotic estimation error when using the Generalized LASSO for signal recovery from measurements corrupted by nonlinear link functions. The authors extend previous work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin to a more general setting, providing explicit expressions for the estimation error and demonstrating that, asymptotically, the performance is equivalent to that of a linear measurement model with appropriately scaled noise. The results are a significant contribution to understanding the robustness of LASSO-type algorithms to nonlinearities in the measurement process.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents its ideas. The motivation is well-explained, connecting the work to both classical statistics and more recent advances in high-dimensional estimation. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the authors carefully state their assumptions. The inclusion of illustrative figures strengthens the presentation and provides intuitive validation of the theoretical results. The organization of the paper is logical, building from the problem setup and related work to the main theoretical contributions and their applications. The authors also delve into practical implications, such as the optimal design of quantizers, making the work relevant beyond purely theoretical considerations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The paper establishes a precise equivalence between nonlinear and scaled linear measurement models in the context of the Generalized LASSO, a result not previously known. This provides valuable insights into the behavior of these algorithms under realistic conditions.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of the asymptotic error expression is mathematically sound, building upon established techniques in high-dimensional statistics.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the concepts and derivations. The notation is consistent and well-defined.
* **Practical Implications:** The application to q-bit compressive sensing and the discussion of the Lloyd-Max algorithm demonstrate the potential impact of the results on practical applications.
* **Generality:** The results are shown to hold for a wide class of regularizers and link functions, enhancing the applicability of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Gaussian Assumption:** The paper relies heavily on the assumption of i.i.d. Gaussian measurement vectors. While this is a common assumption in high-dimensional statistics, the authors do not sufficiently justify why this is necessary.  It’s unclear whether the results extend to other random matrix ensembles. A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to deviations from Gaussianity would be valuable.
* **Regularizer Selection:** The paper focuses on analyzing the performance of the LASSO *given* a regularizer. However, it does not address the problem of *selecting* the appropriate regularizer based on the characteristics of the signal (e.g., the set K containing x0). This is a critical practical issue, and some discussion of this aspect would enhance the paper.
* **Necessity of µ and σ:** The authors demonstrate the role of µ and σ in characterizing the difficulty of the estimation problem. However, the paper doesn’t rigorously prove the *necessity* of these parameters. While intuitively plausible, a stronger argument or supporting evidence would strengthen the claim.
* **Missing Citations:** The literature review, while comprehensive, omits several relevant works. Specifically, the authors should cite recent advances in single-index models and sufficient dimension reduction, which extensively study linear/subspace estimation under unknown link functions (e.g., Li, 1991). Furthermore, the work of Yi et al. (2015) on optimal linear estimation under unknown link functions, which addresses sparse recovery even when µ = 0 and demonstrates the equivalence between linear and nonlinear high-dimensional estimation, is a critical omission.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and mathematically rigorous result with significant theoretical implications.
* The clarity of the presentation and the well-organized structure make the paper easily accessible to a broad audience.
* The practical applications discussed demonstrate the potential impact of the work beyond purely theoretical contributions.
* The results extend existing work in the field and offer valuable insights into the robustness of LASSO-type algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong reliance on the Gaussian assumption without sufficient justification limits the generality of the results.
* The lack of discussion on regularizer selection and the necessity of µ and σ slightly diminish the practical relevance of the paper.
* The omission of relevant citations weakens the context and completeness of the literature review.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of high-dimensional estimation. The novel results, rigorous analysis, and clear presentation outweigh the limitations. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the Gaussian assumption, adding a discussion on regularizer selection, elaborating on the necessity of µ and σ, and incorporating the missing citations.  A stronger discussion around the limitations of the Gaussian assumption – perhaps outlining potential avenues for extension to other matrix ensembles – would significantly strengthen the paper.
