This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized Lasso for structured signal recovery when the measurements are subjected to a non-linear transformation, a setting common in applications like quantized compressed sensing. Building upon foundational work by Brillinger and more recent high-dimensional analysis by Plan & Vershynin, the authors make a significant theoretical contribution. They demonstrate that, under an i.i.d. Gaussian design matrix, the non-linear measurement problem is asymptotically equivalent to a standard noisy linear measurement problem. The key advance over prior work is the derivation of precise, explicit asymptotic formulas for the mean-squared error, replacing the non-asymptotic upper bounds previously available.

The paper’s primary strength is its high technical quality and originality. The derivation of exact asymptotic error characterizations is a substantial theoretical achievement that provides a much deeper understanding of the estimator's behavior. This framework is shown to be broadly applicable, with empirical validations convincingly demonstrated for several important special cases, including the standard Lasso, group Lasso, and nuclear norm regularization. The results accurately predict the performance in simulations. Furthermore, the application of this theory to the design of q-bit quantizers for compressed sensing is particularly insightful, elegantly connecting the problem of optimal quantizer design to the classic Lloyd–Max quantizer. This is a novel link that highlights the practical significance of the theoretical findings.

The main weakness of the paper is its clarity, which appears somewhat compromised by space constraints, making the presentation feel unbalanced. While generally well-written, the omission of certain details could hinder a reader's ability to fully reproduce the results. A number of minor corrections and clarifications would strengthen the manuscript for the final version:
*   The term "proximal function" should be clarified as the Moreau envelope to avoid confusion with the more common proximal operator.
*   The Generalized Lasso is a convex program or decoder, not an "algorithm."
*   Assumption 1 would benefit from a more formal interpretation using variational analysis.
*   Minor fixes: A typo exists on page 5 (line 247), and the caption for Figure 1 should use mathematical formatting (e.g., $n=768$).

Overall, this is a solid and well-executed piece of work. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the weaknesses are minor and addressable. The paper advances the state of the art in the precise analysis of high-dimensional estimators and should be of great interest to the community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
