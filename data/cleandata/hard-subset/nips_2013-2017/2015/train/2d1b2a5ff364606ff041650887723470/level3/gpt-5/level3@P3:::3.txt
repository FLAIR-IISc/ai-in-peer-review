Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the generalized Lasso under non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian design. Building on classical results by Brillinger and, crucially, on the non-asymptotic theory of Plan & Vershynin for constrained programs, the authors prove that, asymptotically in high dimensions with Gaussian A, non-linear observations can be treated as scaled and noisy linear ones: yi ≈ μ a_i^T x0 + σ zi, with μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). They then derive precise asymptotic error predictions for the penalized generalized Lasso across a wide class of convex regularizers via a scalar convex program (Theorem 2.2). Specializations cover Lasso, group Lasso, and nuclear-norm regularization; for sparse vectors, a closed-form characterization recovers and extends AMP/CGMT-era precise analyses for linear measurements and goes beyond in generality. An insightful application shows that optimal q-bit CS quantizer design for generalized Lasso amounts to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, and that stationary points coincide with outputs of the Lloyd–Max algorithm, thereby connecting estimation-optimal and MSE-optimal quantization.

Strengths
- Technically solid extension of precise Lasso error analysis to non-linear single-index observations, with clear identification of effective linear parameters (μ, σ^2).
- Generality across convex regularizers (ℓ1, ℓ1,2, nuclear norm), with concrete specializations and interpretable scalar characterizations.
- Strong empirical validation matching theory, including special cases and regimes.
- Practical and elegant application to q-bit quantization; the Lloyd–Max connection is particularly insightful and likely to influence practice.
- Clear positioning with respect to Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin; clarifies the role of μ and σ^2 precisely rather than up to constants.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Reliance on Gaussian design; it is unclear how far the results extend to sub-Gaussian or structured designs.
- The key Assumption 1 is stated informally and could be made more transparent via standard tools in variational analysis (e.g., epi-convergence/Γ-limits of Moreau envelopes or law-of-large-numbers arguments for separable convex functionals).
- Some imbalance in exposition: proof ideas and intuition for the CGMT-based route (if used) are brief, while several technical definitions (e.g., F) are abstract for non-experts.
- Minor notational overload; occasional inconsistencies (e.g., σ vs. σ^2) can momentarily confuse.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and, to my reading, careful. The equivalence (Theorem 2.1) and the precise scalar optimization (Theorem 2.2) are plausible and well-supported by experiments. Specializations (sparse/group/low-rank) are consistent with known linear-measurement results and extend them to the non-linear setting. The quantizer design section is a nice addition with convincing reasoning and numerics. This is a complete, well-executed piece of work.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, though somewhat unbalanced: the main text compresses several technical steps (likely due to space constraints) and leaves important definitions (Assumption 1 and F) at a high level. Figures are informative and match theory. A few minor notational and editorial issues should be fixed (listed below).

Originality
High. While Plan–Vershynin established non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained estimators with non-linear measurements, this work provides novel explicit asymptotic error expressions for penalized generalized Lasso and shows exact equivalence to a corresponding noisy linear model. The quantizer design link to Lloyd–Max under the σ^2/μ^2 criterion is a fresh and useful insight.

Significance
Significant. Precise asymptotics are valuable for understanding/tuning regularization and for principled quantizer design. The generality across regularizers makes the results broadly relevant to sparse and low-rank recovery from non-linear measurements.

Experiments
The experiments convincingly:
- Validate the asymptotic equivalence to the effective noisy-linear model across scenarios (Lasso, group Lasso, nuclear norm).
- Match theoretical error curves over λ and δ.
- Demonstrate the quantizer-design insight; the Lloyd–Max solution aligns with minimizing σ^2/μ^2 and improves estimation performance.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Terminology: Please avoid calling the generalized Lasso an “algorithm.” It is a convex program/estimator/decoder.
- Penalized vs constrained: The regularized (penalized) and constrained formulations are equivalent under standard Lagrange multiplier mappings (under mild regularity). Since you compare to the constrained form of Plan–Vershynin, add a brief discussion of the mapping and its implications for error predictions.
- Assumption 1: Provide a more formal interpretation via variational analysis. For separable or unitarily invariant regularizers, one can justify the existence of F(c1,c2,c3) by epi-convergence/LLN for the normalized Moreau envelope of f* (Rockafellar–Wets). This would demystify F and clarify scope.
- “Proximal function”: The labeled proximal function is the Moreau envelope with index τ. Since “prox” usually denotes the proximal operator, please rename to “Moreau envelope” e_{f,τ} and reserve prox_{τ f} for the operator.
- Notation consistency: In the abstract and early sections, ensure σ vs σ^2 are used consistently as variance vs standard deviation.
- Scope: You explicitly exclude μ=0 cases. It would help to add a brief remark on possible workarounds (e.g., dithering, alternative decoders) and whether your framework can be adapted.
- Figure 1: In the caption, render n as math; “n=768” should be written as $n=768$.
- Typo: Page 5, line 247 (“When ...”): there is a typo/grammatical error; please correct.
- Minor: Clarify early that “regularized LASSO” here refers to the ℓ2 data-fit with convex penalty (sometimes termed L2-LASSO) to avoid confusion with the square-root Lasso.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, novel theory with precise, interpretable predictions beyond prior bounds.
- Broad applicability across popular convex regularizers.
- Careful empirical validation and a compelling, practically relevant application to quantizer design.
- Well-aligned with NeurIPS interests in high-dimensional statistics, convex optimization, and compressed sensing.

Arguments against acceptance
- Restricted to Gaussian measurement matrices; extension to broader ensembles is not addressed.
- Exposition of Assumption 1 and some notation could be clearer for a general audience.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a solid and well-executed contribution that advances our understanding of generalized Lasso under non-linear measurements, with both theoretical depth and practical insights (notably, the Lloyd–Max connection). The suggested clarifications are minor and easily addressed.
