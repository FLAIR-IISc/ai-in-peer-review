This paper studies generalized LASSO estimation from nonlinear single-index measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) with Gaussian design. The main theoretical contribution is an exact asymptotic equivalence: for arbitrary convex regularizers f and fixed regularization parameter λ, the estimation error of the generalized LASSO with nonlinear observations is identical (in the high-dimensional limit with m/n→δ) to that of the same program fed with linear measurements y = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2] for γ ~ N(0,1). Beyond this invariance result, the paper derives a precise scalar optimization formula for the asymptotic squared error as a function of δ, λ, f, and the signal/regularizer geometry encapsulated via a Moreau-envelope-based functional F. The authors specialize these results to sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) recovery, recovering and extending prior precise LASSO analyses in the linear case and offering new formulas for general f and nonlinear links. An application to q-bit compressive sensing shows that minimizing the resulting error is equivalent to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 over quantizer thresholds and levels, which connects to and justifies the Lloyd–Max procedure.

Relation to prior work: Brillinger’s classical result established consistency up to a scale factor μ for least squares with Gaussian regressors under link misspecification in the low-dimensional regime. Plan and Vershynin later obtained non-asymptotic upper bounds for constrained generalized LASSO under nonlinear observations, highlighting the roles of μ and σ but with unspecified constants. The present paper advances both lines by: (i) proving an asymptotic exact equivalence between nonlinear and corresponding linear models for the penalized generalized LASSO; (ii) providing an exact error formula that depends on μ and σ only through the effective linearization. The technical approach and specializations connect to precise LASSO error analyses via AMP and the CGMT (e.g., Bayati–Montanari; Stojnic; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.), and extend them to broader regularizers and nonlinear links.

Quality: The results appear technically sound and are carefully scoped. The asymptotic equivalence theorem is crisp and impactful, and the scalar convex optimization (Theorem 2.2) is practical to evaluate. Specializations (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) are convincing, and the sparse case recovers known linear-measurement results while extending to non-Gaussian sparse priors. Simulations track theory closely, including the two-regime behavior in the compressed regime and the quantizer design application. Assumptions are standard for this line of work (i.i.d. Gaussian A, independent links, finite μ,σ). The main caveats are: (i) the Gaussian design restriction (important for the precise CGMT-style analysis), (ii) the requirement μ ≠ 0, which excludes even link functions or some degenerate cases, and (iii) the somewhat abstract Assumption 1 on the variational limit F, which could be made more concrete and easier to verify.

Clarity: Generally well-written and well-organized, with a helpful positioning relative to Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin and clear statements of the main theorems. That said, the exposition is somewhat unbalanced: the general theorem section is dense, and some technical assumptions and mappings (e.g., constrained vs penalized formulations) are treated too briefly. Providing more guidance on computing F (beyond the ℓ1/ℓ1,2 cases) and a short “recipe” for using Theorem 2.2 in practice would improve readability. Minor notational choices (calling a Moreau envelope a proximal function) and a few typos detract slightly.

Originality: High. The asymptotic equivalence between nonlinear and linear measurements for the penalized generalized LASSO, together with exact error characterizations for general convex regularizers, appears new. The quantizer design corollary that Lloyd–Max stationary points minimize σ^2/μ^2 for estimation error is an insightful and practically relevant connection.

Significance: High. These results unify and simplify the analysis and design of LASSO-type decoders under nonlinear sensing, with concrete implications for tuning λ, predicting performance, and designing quantizers. The framework should be useful to researchers and practitioners working on generalized linear inverse problems and quantized sensing. While reliance on Gaussian A limits immediate applicability to some engineered systems, the insights are likely to inform broader classes (e.g., sub-Gaussian designs).

Specific comments and suggestions:
- Terminology: Avoid calling the generalized LASSO an “algorithm”; refer to it as a convex program, estimator, or decoder.
- Penalized vs constrained: Clarify the mapping between the penalized (regularized) and constrained formulations and when the results extend to the constrained form (and vice versa).
- Assumption 1: Provide a more formal variational interpretation (e.g., via epi-convergence / convex-analytic limits) and explicit sufficient conditions that cover the main examples, including low-rank models, to help readers verify F.
- Notation: The object referred to as a “proximal function” is the Moreau envelope with index τ; reserve “prox” for the proximal operator and rename the envelope accordingly.
- Quantizer design: Emphasize that Lloyd–Max yields stationary points for σ^2/μ^2; discuss conditions for local/global optimality and sensitivity to λ and δ.
- Edge cases: Briefly discuss behavior when μ is near zero or zero, and how one might adapt estimation (e.g., alternative decoders) in those cases.
- Experimental breadth: If space permits, add experiments with mildly non-Gaussian/sub-Gaussian designs to probe robustness, and provide code or parameter settings for reproducibility.
- Minor edits:
  - Figure 1 caption: format n=768 as a mathematical quantity consistently.
  - Typo on p.5, line 247 (around “When …”).
  - A few instances of punctuation/spacing around equations could be cleaned up.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution (exact equivalence and precise error).
- Broad applicability across convex regularizers; clean specializations.
- Clear practical implication for quantizer design (Lloyd–Max connection).
- Solid empirical validation aligned with theory.

Cons for acceptance:
- Gaussian design assumption limits generality; discussion of extensions is brief.
- Abstract Assumption 1 could be more accessible and checkable.
- Exposition could better guide practitioners in computing F beyond the canonical cases.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a significant, well-supported contribution that advances understanding of LASSO-type estimation under nonlinear measurements and offers actionable insights for practice.
