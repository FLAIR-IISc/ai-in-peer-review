This paper investigates the asymptotic estimation error when employing the Generalized LASSO for signal recovery from measurements corrupted by nonlinear link functions. The work extends the results of Plan and Vershynin by considering scenarios where the proportionality constant µ (relating the nonlinear to a linear measurement) is non-zero. The central claim, formalized in Theorem 2.1, is that the asymptotic error is identical whether the measurements are truly linear or derived through a nonlinear link function, provided appropriate scaling. Subsequent theorems provide precise expressions for this error, depending on the regularization parameter and properties of the link function. A specific application to q-bit compressive sensing is also presented, leading to the interesting conclusion that the optimal quantizer is the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

The paper builds upon a well-established body of work in compressive sensing and statistical estimation. It correctly identifies Brillinger’s early work on least squares estimation with nonlinearities and situates itself within the more recent focus on precise error analysis, referencing contributions from Donoho, Johnstone, Stojnic, and others. The connection to the Generalized LASSO, introduced by Tibshirani, is clearly explained, and the work appropriately frames its contribution as extending these existing results to the nonlinear measurement setting. The authors provide a good overview of related techniques, such as the convex Gaussian min-max theorem and Approximate Message Passing. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its technical soundness and clarity. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the exposition is generally well-organized. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the existing literature and carefully position their contributions within this context. The application to q-bit compressive sensing provides a concrete illustration of the theoretical results and showcases potential practical implications. The theorems are clearly stated and the derivation seems robust. The inclusion of numerical validation (Figures 1 and 2) is a positive aspect, as it supports the theoretical findings.

However, there are several weaknesses. The strong assumption of Gaussian measurement vectors is not sufficiently justified. While common in the field, the authors do not explore the sensitivity of their results to deviations from this assumption, or whether similar conclusions can be drawn under more general measurement matrix distributions. A discussion of the limitations imposed by this assumption would be beneficial.  Furthermore, the paper doesn’t delve into how to *choose* the appropriate regularizer, *f*, given the structure of the underlying signal *x₀*. While various convex regularizers are mentioned, a more detailed discussion of how to select *f* based on properties of the solution set *K* (containing *x₀*) would enhance the practical utility of the results. The role of the constants μ and σ in characterizing the ‘difficulty’ of the link function is clearly stated, but the paper doesn't thoroughly investigate whether the dependence on these parameters is strictly necessary or if their influence could be minimized through clever algorithm design.

Crucially, the paper omits relevant citations. While the authors acknowledge the single-index model literature, they neglect significant work in sufficient dimension reduction (SDR) which directly addresses linear/subspace estimation under unknown link functions. Specifically, work by Li and Duan (2008) and Garnham and Prendergast (2015) is relevant. Additionally, the recent work by Yi, Wang, Caramanis, and Liu (2015) should be cited. This work tackles sparse recovery specifically when μ = 0, demonstrating the equivalence between linear and nonlinear high-dimensional estimation, providing context to the current results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents novel and theoretically sound results extending existing work on the Generalized LASSO to the challenging setting of nonlinear measurements.
*   The derivations are rigorous, and the exposition is clear and well-organized.
*   The application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates potential practical impact.
*   The precise error characterizations are a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The strong assumption of Gaussian measurement vectors is not adequately justified or explored.
*   The paper lacks a discussion of optimal regularizer selection based on the solution set K.
*   Important related work in single-index models, sufficient dimension reduction and Yi et al. (2015) is missing from the bibliography.
*   The necessity of the dependence on the parameters µ and σ remains unclear.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a solid contribution to the field, presenting interesting and technically sound results. However, the lack of discussion around the Gaussian assumption and the omission of key related work weaken the overall impact. Addressing these points, particularly including the missing citations, would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on addressing the cited weaknesses.
