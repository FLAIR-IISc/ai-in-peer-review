This paper studies the problem of estimating a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. The authors analyze the performance of the Generalized LASSO estimator, which naively treats the measurements as if they were linear. The main contribution is a precise asymptotic characterization of the estimation error. Building on the work of Brillinger and, more recently, Plan and Vershynin, this paper extends previous results by providing an exact asymptotic formula for the error of the regularized LASSO, rather than just upper bounds for a constrained version. The central result, Theorem 2.1, claims that in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit with i.i.d. Gaussian measurement vectors, the estimation error `||x̂ - µx0||^2` is identical to the error that would be obtained if the measurements were from a corresponding linear model `yi = µa_i^T x0 + σzi`. The parameters `µ` and `σ` depend only on the statistics of the link function `g`. The paper then provides an explicit, though complex, formula for this asymptotic error (Theorem 2.2) and illustrates its accuracy on several structured signal recovery problems, such as sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Precise Asymptotic Analysis:** The primary strength of the paper is moving beyond the upper bounds found in prior work (e.g., Plan & Vershynin) to a precise, asymptotic characterization of the estimation error. The provided formulas (Theorems 2.2 and 2.3) are explicit and shown via simulation to be highly accurate. This represents a significant technical advance in the theoretical understanding of LASSO-type estimators in this setting.

2.  **Elegant Main Result:** The conclusion that the non-linear measurement model is asymptotically equivalent to a specific linear model is both elegant and powerful. It provides a clear intuition for why a simple, model-mismatched estimator like the LASSO can be effective and precisely quantifies the "price" of the non-linearity through the effective scaling `µ` and noise level `σ`.

3.  **Broad Scope:** The analysis is presented for a general class of convex regularizers, and its consequences are worked out for several important special cases, including `l1`, group `l1,2`, and nuclear norm regularization. The application to optimizing q-bit quantizers is a nice, concrete example of the theory's potential utility.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the main results are presented in a logical progression from the general to the specific.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper's positioning relative to existing literature could be significantly improved.
    *   The problem of estimating a signal's direction `x0` under an unknown non-linear link function is the canonical setup for **single-index models (SIM)** and, more broadly, **sufficient dimension reduction (SDR)**. While the paper briefly mentions SIM, it fails to engage with this vast body of literature, which has studied this problem for decades.
    *   More critically, the paper omits a discussion of the highly relevant recent work by Yi et al. (2015), "Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform" (Ref [24] in the paper). That work also establishes an equivalence between linear and non-linear high-dimensional estimation. Crucially, their results hold even when `µ = E[γg(γ)] = 0`, a case this paper explicitly excludes but which is critical for even link functions (e.g., `g(x) = |x|`). Their analysis also covers sparse recovery and demonstrates that other methods can succeed where the LASSO fails. This omission significantly impacts the assessment of the paper's originality and contribution, as a key part of the central claim has been independently and more generally established.

2.  **Necessity of the Gaussian Assumption:** Theorem 2.1 relies on the assumption that the measurement vectors `a_i` have i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a very strong assumption that is central to many of the proof techniques in this line of work (e.g., CGMT). However, the paper does not discuss why this assumption is necessary or whether the results might extend to broader matrix ensembles, such as sub-Gaussian designs. Is this a fundamental requirement for the "linearization" phenomenon to occur, or is it an artifact of the proof technique? A discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Dependence on µ and σ:** The theorem's error characterization depends on `µ` and `σ`, which are presented as characterizing the difficulty of the link function. While this is true for the LASSO estimator, the authors should discuss whether this dependence is fundamental to the problem itself. As the work by Yi et al. shows, for certain structures and `µ=0`, optimal recovery is still possible with different estimators. This suggests that the LASSO's performance, as characterized by `µ`, is not necessarily indicative of the fundamental statistical limits of the problem. Acknowledging this would provide a more complete picture.

4.  **Guidance on Regularizer Selection:** The theoretical framework assumes a properly chosen convex regularizer `f(x)` that reflects the structure of the unknown signal `x0` (which is assumed to lie in or near a set `K`). However, the paper offers no guidance on how one should choose `f(x)` based on knowledge of `K`. This is a crucial step in applying the method in practice and its omission makes the results less accessible for practitioners.

**Summary of evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and its claims appear to be well-supported by the theoretical analysis. The core results are complete and detailed.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** The work provides a novel and precise error characterization, which is an original contribution. However, its originality is diminished by the failure to discuss and contrast with highly relevant prior work (especially Yi et al.), which established a similar and more general equivalence result.
*   **Significance:** The results are fairly interesting and contribute to a deeper theoretical understanding of a popular heuristic (applying LASSO to non-linear data). The clean nature of the main result could be influential for theorists. However, its practical significance is limited by the strong Gaussian assumption and the lack of guidance on applying the framework.

Overall, this is a well-executed theoretical paper with a clean and interesting result. However, its contribution is somewhat overstated due to the significant omission of closely related literature. Addressing the weaknesses above, particularly by thoroughly discussing the relationship to Yi et al. and the limitations of the Gaussian assumption, would substantially improve the manuscript.
