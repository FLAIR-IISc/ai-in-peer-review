This paper addresses the problem of estimating a structured signal x0 from non-linear measurements of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where A is a matrix with i.i.d. Gaussian entries and g is a (possibly unknown) link function. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, which naively treats the measurements as if they were linear. The main contribution is a precise, asymptotic characterization of the mean-squared error. Building on the classical work of Brillinger and more recent results by Plan and Vershynin, the paper demonstrates that, in the high-dimensional limit, the performance of the Generalized LASSO on these non-linear measurements is asymptotically identical to its performance on an equivalent linear model with measurements yi = μa_i^T x0 + σzi. Here, μ and σ are constants that depend only on the link function g, and z is standard Gaussian noise.

The authors provide an explicit, albeit complex, formula for the asymptotic mean-squared error, ‖x̂ - μx0‖², as the solution to a scalar convex optimization problem derived using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework. This general result is then specialized to several important cases, including sparse recovery with the ℓ1-norm (LASSO), group-sparse recovery with the ℓ1,2-norm (Group LASSO), and low-rank matrix recovery with the nuclear norm. A particularly compelling application of the theory is presented for the design of optimal quantizers in q-bit compressive sensing, where the analysis reveals a connection to the classic Lloyd-Max quantizer.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper's main theoretical result is highly significant. While Plan and Vershynin established the connection between non-linear measurements and an effective linear model, they provided non-asymptotic upper bounds with unknown constants. This work provides a precise, asymptotic characterization of the estimation error. Deriving explicit, computable error formulas is a substantial step forward and deepens the understanding of LASSO-type estimators in this setting. This originality brings a new level of precision to a problem of growing importance.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work appears to be a solid and technically sound contribution. The claims are well-supported by a rigorous theoretical framework and corroborated by numerical simulations. The experiments across various settings (sparse, group-sparse) confirm the accuracy of the theoretical predictions and compellingly demonstrate the equivalence to the corresponding noisy linear model.

3.  **Insightful Application:** The application to optimal quantizer design for compressive sensing is a major strength. It showcases the practical power of the derived theory. By reducing the complex problem of designing a quantizer to minimize the final reconstruction error to a simple optimization of the ratio σ²/μ², the authors provide a clear and actionable design principle. The connection drawn to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is elegant and insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** While the paper is generally well-written, the presentation feels somewhat unbalanced due to space constraints. The main results are stated clearly, but the assumptions and the derivation framework are condensed, with most technical details relegated to the appendix. For instance, Assumption 1 is central to the main theorem, but its general interpretation could be clarified. A more formal discussion, perhaps using concepts from variational analysis, would help delineate the scope of its applicability beyond the specific examples provided.

2.  **Completeness:** As is common in conference papers, the reliance on the appendix for proofs makes a full, self-contained assessment of the work challenging from the main text alone. The discussion on low-rank matrix recovery feels less developed than the other sections, leaving the problem of evaluating the function F for future work.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The term "Generalized LASSO algorithm" is used throughout the paper. It is more accurate to refer to this as a convex program, estimator, or decoder, as "algorithm" typically refers to the specific optimization procedure used to solve the program (e.g., ISTA, FISTA, ADMM).
*   The function `e_{f,τ}(v)` is referred to as the "proximal function." This is non-standard terminology. This function is the Moreau envelope (or Moreau-Yosida regularization) of `f` with index `τ`. The term "proximal operator" usually refers to the minimizer of that expression, `prox_{τf}(v)`. Using standard terminology would improve clarity.
*   The equivalence between the regularized formulation in (2) and a penalized formulation (e.g., min ½‖y−Ax‖² + λ̃f(x)) is well-known. A brief comment noting that the results can be mapped to that setting would be helpful for completeness.
*   Assumption 1 is a key technical condition. Providing more intuition or a more formal interpretation of what this assumption means for the pair of signal structure (px0) and regularizer (f) would strengthen the paper.
*   There appears to be a typo on page 5, line 247: "When measurements are linear, our Theorem 2.3 generalizes this assumption." It seems it should refer to the result in [20] rather than an assumption.
*   In the caption of Figure 1, numbers like 'n=768' should be rendered in math mode as `$n=768$` for better typesetting.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of high-dimensional estimation. It provides a precise and powerful framework for analyzing the performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. The theoretical results are rigorous, well-validated, and lead to insightful practical applications. The minor weaknesses in presentation do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work.
