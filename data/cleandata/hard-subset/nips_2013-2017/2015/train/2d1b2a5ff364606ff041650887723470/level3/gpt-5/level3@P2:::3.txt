Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies generalized LASSO estimators under non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0), with A having i.i.d. N(0,1) entries and f a convex (typically non-smooth) regularizer promoting structure (e.g., ℓ1, group-ℓ1,2, nuclear norm). The central result (Theorem 2.1) shows an asymptotic equivalence: the estimation error of the generalized LASSO with non-linear observations is identical to that of the same estimator under a linear model with effective scale and noise parameters (μ, σ^2) defined by μ = E[γ g(γ)], σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Theorem 2.2 then provides an asymptotically exact formula for the error as the solution of a simple three-scalar convex program, with a function F encapsulating the structure and regularizer through the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate. Specializations include sparse (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) models, with a refined characterization for sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3). An application to q-bit compressive sensing shows the asymptotic error is minimized by choosing quantizers that minimize σ^2/μ^2; the paper connects this to Lloyd–Max and shows LM stationary points solve the derived design objective.

This work significantly extends Brillinger’s classical consistency result for least squares with non-linear responses (fixed n, m → ∞) and the non-asymptotic constrained-LASSO bounds of Plan and Vershynin, by: (i) moving to a high-dimensional proportional regime, (ii) treating regularized (penalized) LASSO with general convex f, and (iii) deriving precise asymptotic error expressions. It builds on recent precise analyses of LASSO with linear measurements via AMP and CGMT, generalizing those insights to the non-linear single-index setting.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: a clean and powerful equivalence principle reducing non-linear to linear measurements with (μ, σ^2).
- Technical contribution: asymptotically exact error formula via a low-dimensional convex program, applicable across regularizers. The sparse-case refinement (Theorem 2.3) is insightful and matches simulations.
- Breadth: covers sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank; the F functional cleanly abstracts structure.
- Practical impact: the quantizer design application is elegant; connecting the optimal design to Lloyd–Max through σ^2/μ^2 is both surprising and useful.
- Empirical validation: figures demonstrate close agreement between theory and simulations across regimes.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Gaussian i.i.d. design is assumed throughout. While standard in precise asymptotics, discussion of robustness to sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant designs (or where CGMT still applies) would strengthen practical relevance.
- Assumption 1 could be formalized more rigorously using tools from variational analysis (e.g., epi-convergence/Mosco convergence of Moreau envelopes, conditions ensuring LLN for separable f or orthogonally invariant X0). Concrete sufficient conditions (beyond examples) would aid readers.
- The paper uses “proximal function” to refer to the Moreau envelope; better to avoid confusion and call it the Moreau envelope, reserving “prox” for the proximal operator.
- The work alternates between “algorithm” and “estimator/convex program.” Please refer to generalized LASSO as an estimator/program/decoder rather than an algorithm.
- The paper contrasts constrained vs penalized forms but could explicitly discuss their equivalence (under appropriate mapping of λ ↔ constraint radius) to relate more directly to Plan–Vershynin and to clarify where results transfer.
- The asymptotics assume μ ≠ 0; a dedicated discussion of edge cases (e.g., even g, or designs where μ = 0) and pointers to alternative estimators would help delineate scope. Also, in practice, g may be unknown; adding a short note on consistent estimation of μ, σ from data (plug-in) and the effect of estimation error on the error formula would be valuable.
- Space constraints push many proofs to the appendix; a brief proof sketch for Theorem 2.1 (how CGMT yields equivalence) would improve readability.

Quality:
The results appear technically sound, with a clear lineage to CGMT-based precise asymptotics. Theorems 2.1–2.3 are well-motivated, and the specialization to common structures is credible. Experiments substantiate claims. The quantizer design insight is carefully derived (σ^2/μ^2 criterion) and convincingly linked to Lloyd–Max.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. However, a few notational and terminology issues should be cleaned up (Moreau envelope naming; “algorithm” vs “estimator”). Assumption 1 deserves a more formal statement. Minor presentation issues (see “Minor comments and typos”) detract slightly but are easily fixable.

Originality:
High. The precise asymptotic equivalence and error characterization for generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, especially across general convex regularizers, is new. The quantizer design angle and Lloyd–Max connection in this estimation context are novel and interesting.

Significance:
High. The equivalence principle provides a unifying lens for a broad class of non-linear observation models, immediately enabling practitioners to reuse precise linear-measurement LASSO theory. The results should influence both theory (extensions to broader designs) and practice (estimator selection, quantizer design).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, general, and clean theoretical contributions with precise, testable predictions.
- Bridges important threads (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin; CGMT/AMP precise asymptotics).
- Practical design insight (quantization) with broad potential impact.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Restricted to Gaussian designs and asymptotic regime; limited discussion of robustness.
- Some technical assumptions (Assumption 1) need clearer, more formal treatment.

Questions for the authors:
- Can you provide sufficient conditions under which Assumption 1 holds (e.g., separable f with iid coordinates, orthogonally invariant low-rank models) using epi-convergence?
- How robust are the predictions to non-Gaussian (but sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant) designs?
- If g is unknown, can μ, σ be estimated from data reliably, and how would that affect the asymptotic error?
- Do you have guidance for selecting λ in practice using the derived formulas (e.g., numerically solving (10) for α* to tune λ)?

Minor comments and typos:
- Please avoid calling the Moreau envelope a “proximal function”; use “Moreau envelope” and reserve “prox” for the proximal operator.
- Use “estimator/convex program/decoder” instead of “algorithm” when referring to generalized LASSO.
- Clarify that penalized and constrained formulations are equivalent under appropriate λ-to-radius mapping, and specify where your results transfer directly to the constrained form.
- Figure 1 caption: typeset n=768 as n = 768.
- Typo on p.5, line 247 (“When ...”): please fix.
- Notation: the paper refers to the ℓ2-LASSO or square-root LASSO; ensure consistent definition (square-root LASSO uses the ℓ2 norm of residual, not squared loss).
- Consider adding a short proof sketch for Theorem 2.1 and a flowchart for specializing Theorem 2.2 (how to compute F for common f).

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper delivers a substantial, broadly applicable theoretical advance with convincing validation and a neat practical application. The weaknesses are mainly scope and exposition, and can be addressed in revision.
