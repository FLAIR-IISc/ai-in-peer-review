This paper studies the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements and Gaussian design. The authors show that non-linear observations can be treated as noisy linear ones and derive precise asymptotic error formulas building on the work of Plan and Vershynin. The paper provides a solid, well-done piece of work that makes significant contributions to the field of signal processing and machine learning.

The paper is well-written, but somewhat unbalanced, with some sections feeling a bit rushed due to space constraints. However, the authors do an excellent job of providing a clear and concise introduction to the problem and the relevant literature. The technical sections are also well-organized and easy to follow.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of precise asymptotic error formulas for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. This is a significant achievement, as previous work in this area has been limited to upper bounds or loose approximations. The authors' use of the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework is particularly noteworthy, as it allows them to derive tight bounds on the estimation error.

The paper also provides a number of insightful applications of the Generalized LASSO, including the design of optimal quantizers for compressive sensing. The authors show that the popular Lloyd-Max algorithm can be used to find the optimal quantization levels and thresholds, and that the resulting quantizer achieves a lower error than other methods.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to balance theoretical rigor with practical applications. The authors provide a number of simulations and examples that illustrate the effectiveness of their approach, and they also discuss the implications of their results for real-world problems.

If I were to offer any criticisms, it would be that the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the Generalized LASSO. While the authors do an excellent job of highlighting the strengths of their approach, they could also provide more guidance on when and how to use the Generalized LASSO in practice.

Additionally, there are a few minor errors and typos throughout the paper. For example, the caption for Figure 1 should render 'n=768' as $n=768$. There is also a typo on page 5, line 247. Finally, the labeled "proximal function" is actually the Moreau envelope of index τ, and the notation should be changed to reflect this.

Overall, however, this is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to the field of signal processing and machine learning. The authors' work on the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is particularly noteworthy, and their applications to compressive sensing and quantizer design are insightful and practical.
