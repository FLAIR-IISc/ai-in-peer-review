Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation in single-index models y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) with unknown (potentially random) nonlinearity g and structured signals x0, using the generalized LASSO x̂ = argmin_x ||y − A x||_2 + λ f(x). The central result (Theorem 2.1) shows that, under i.i.d. Gaussian measurement vectors, the asymptotic squared error of the generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements equals that of the same estimator applied to a linear model with an effective gain and noise: y_lin = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). Thus, in the large-system limit, the nonlinearity is “absorbed” into two scalars that depend only on g. Building on the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT), the authors further provide a precise scalar optimization (Theorem 2.2) that yields the asymptotic error for general convex regularizers f under proportional growth m/n → δ. They specialize the formula to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery (e.g., Theorem 2.3 for ℓ1 with general sparse priors) and give an application to q-bit compressive sensing, showing that designing thresholds/levels to minimize σ^2/μ^2 aligns with the Lloyd–Max quantizer.

This work extends Brillinger’s classical fixed-p asymptotics for least squares under link violation to high-dimensional regularized estimators, and significantly strengthens the nonasymptotic results of Plan and Vershynin (constrained LASSO under unknown g) by providing asymptotically exact characterizations for the regularized LASSO. It also connects to a large body of precise LASSO risk analysis for linear models via AMP/CGMT (e.g., Bayati–Montanari; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: the equivalence “nonlinear measurements ≡ linear with (μ, σ)” is simple and powerful, and is made precise for generalized LASSO.
- Technical contribution: an exact asymptotic risk characterization (three-scalar convex program) that applies to a broad class of convex regularizers and links; specialization to sparse/group-sparse/low-rank cases is useful.
- Practical insight: for quantized sensing, optimizing σ^2/μ^2 explains why Lloyd–Max is optimal for minimizing LASSO error.
- Well written, with informative figures that match predictions.

Weaknesses and questions
- Gaussian design assumption: The current theory requires i.i.d. N(0,1) rows. This is natural for CGMT and for the Hermite/Stein calculations that make only μ and σ matter. However, practitioners often use sub-Gaussian, Rademacher, partial orthogonal (e.g., subsampled Fourier), or correlated designs. Which parts of the proof critically require Gaussianity (rotational invariance vs. exact CGMT comparison)? Can the equivalence and/or the error formula extend to rotationally invariant ensembles, general sub-Gaussian designs, or designs with known covariance (after whitening)? Some discussion or experiments would help delineate the scope.
- Dependence on μ and σ: The results suggest that only μ and σ^2 of g affect the asymptotic error. Is this dependence information-theoretically necessary in the Gaussian-design setting (e.g., via Hermite expansions showing that only the first two coefficients influence the LASSO risk), or an artifact of the proof technique? A short discussion (or a pointer) would be very helpful.
- μ = 0 regime: The theory assumes μ ≠ 0, ruling out symmetric links such as noiseless 1-bit sign measurements. There is recent work addressing sparse recovery even when μ = 0 and also establishing linear–nonlinear equivalences under unknown links. In particular, please cite and compare with:
  - Yi, Wang, Caramanis, Liu (2015): “Optimal linear estimation under unknown nonlinear transform,” which develops optimal linear estimators and addresses regimes where μ = 0, providing complementary insights and equivalences.
  Beyond Yi et al., it would be useful to discuss how your approach could be adapted (if at all) when μ = 0 (e.g., higher-order terms in Hermite expansion, dithering).
- Choice of regularizer f vs. a set K: The paper analyzes general convex f but does not guide the practitioner on selecting f for a given structural set K or descent cone geometry. A brief discussion connecting your precise error to geometric complexity measures (Gaussian width/statistical dimension of descent cones) would be valuable (e.g., tie-ins with Chandrasekaran et al., and phase-transition literature).
- Assumption 1 and low-rank case: The nuclear-norm case is left at a high level. Since Assumption 1 and the function F(c1,c2,c3) are central, it would strengthen the paper to provide concrete conditions ensuring the required spectral measure convergence and to demonstrate a numerical evaluation of F for a canonical low-rank model.
- Unknown g: In many applications g is unknown. While your methodology does not require knowing g to run the LASSO, interpreting x̂ up to the scale μ and quantizer design depend on μ and σ. Can μ and σ be estimated consistently from data (e.g., via method of moments using (y,a_i), or a pilot LASSO)? A short section on estimation/calibration would enhance practical impact.
- Related literature: The single-index and sufficient-dimension-reduction literatures are broader than the current citations. Consider adding and positioning with classical SDR/single-index works (e.g., Li’s Sliced Inverse Regression (1991), SAVE, MAVE; Ichimura 1993; Härdle–Hall–Ichimura), and recent “link-agnostic” high-dimensional methods.

Quality
The technical development appears solid and leverages state-of-the-art CGMT techniques. The specialization to ℓ1 with general sparse priors (Theorem 2.3) and the λ_crit phenomenon are clearly presented and empirically validated. The low-rank section is less complete.

Clarity
Overall clear and well organized. Suggestions: clarify early that “generalized LASSO” uses ||y−Ax||_2 (not squared), and explain why this choice is made; state and interpret Assumption 1 when first introduced; provide intuition for why only μ and σ matter (e.g., Hermite expansion). Minor typos: in the abstract and Section 1 the LASSO objective occasionally reads ‖y − Ax0‖ instead of ‖y − Ax‖.

Originality
To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact risk characterization for generalized LASSO under nonlinear single-index measurements. The equivalence to a linear model with (μ,σ) is known at a qualitative level (Brillinger; Plan–Vershynin), but the paper gives a precise, quantitative, and broadly applicable theory.

Significance
High: precise error formulas underpin principled tuning and design (e.g., quantizer optimization) and should influence both theory and practice in high-dimensional inference with nonlinearities.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution with precise, usable formulas.
- Clear unification: nonlinearity reduces to (μ,σ) under Gaussian designs.
- Practical consequence for quantizer design; convincing experiments.

Cons for acceptance
- Restricted to Gaussian designs; limited discussion of robustness.
- μ=0 regime excluded; comparison to closely related recent work (e.g., Yi et al.) is missing.
- Low-rank case not fully developed; Assumption 1 could be better motivated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand discussion on extensions beyond Gaussian designs and on the necessity of μ,σ dependence.
- Add comparisons and citations to SDR/single-index literature and Yi et al. (2015).
- Provide guidance on estimating μ,σ from data when g is unknown.
- Flesh out the low-rank example with concrete F and experiments.
