This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to measurements corrupted by a nonlinear link function. The authors demonstrate that, asymptotically, the performance of the Generalized LASSO with these nonlinear measurements is equivalent to that of a linear measurement model with appropriately scaled noise and measurements. This is a significant result, providing a theoretical justification for using the Generalized LASSO even in scenarios where the measurement process is inherently nonlinear. The paper builds on prior work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, strengthening their results by providing explicit and precise expressions for the estimation error. A particularly interesting consequence of the work is the identification of the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error, which corresponds to the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical scenario where measurement devices often exhibit nonlinearities. The theoretical contributions are substantial, delivering precise error bounds and insightful connections between nonlinear and linear measurement models. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, although, as the authors acknowledge, numerous typos exist, particularly within the proofs, which could hinder reproducibility.  The methodology is sound, leveraging asymptotic analysis and carefully defining the assumptions under which the results hold. The connection to compressive sensing, particularly q-bit compressive sensing, adds to the practical relevance of the findings.  

A key strength of this work is the level of precision achieved in characterizing the error. Previous results often relied on upper bounds or lacked explicit expressions, whereas this paper provides a concrete formula for the estimation error, linking it to the properties of the link function (µ and σ2). The identification of the Lloyd-Max quantizer as optimal is also a valuable contribution, providing practical guidance for measurement design.  The paper effectively bridges the gap between theoretical analysis and practical applications, offering insights that are likely to be useful for researchers and practitioners in areas such as signal processing and machine learning.

However, the reviewer admits unfamiliarity with the single-index model and sufficient dimension reduction literature. While the authors reference relevant work in these areas, a more thorough engagement with this existing body of knowledge might reveal further connections and potential improvements. It’s possible that the core ideas presented here have been explored in this related literature, and a deeper understanding of that context would strengthen the originality claim.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant theoretical contribution:** The paper delivers precise and insightful results regarding the performance of the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements.
*   **Practical relevance:** The findings have implications for compressive sensing and other applications where nonlinear measurements are common.
*   **Clear and generally well-written presentation:** The core ideas are clearly explained, despite the presence of typos.
*   **Strong mathematical foundation:** The analysis is rigorous and builds upon established results in the field.
*   **Novelty:** The precise error characterization and the identification of the optimal quantizer represent novel contributions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Typographical errors:** The numerous typos, particularly in the proofs, detract from the overall quality of the presentation and may hinder reproducibility.
*   **Potential overlap with existing literature:** The reviewer's unfamiliarity with the single-index model literature raises the possibility of overlap with prior work.
*   **Strong assumptions:** The results rely on specific assumptions about the measurement matrix (Gaussian entries) and the signal structure, which may limit their applicability in some scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the presence of typos and the potential for overlap with existing work in related fields, this paper represents a significant contribution to the understanding of the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements. The precise error characterization and the identification of the optimal quantizer are valuable results that are likely to stimulate further research. The theoretical soundness of the analysis, combined with the practical relevance of the findings, makes this paper a strong candidate for acceptance. Addressing the typos and clarifying the novelty relative to the single-index model literature would further strengthen the manuscript.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions (addressing the typos).  The authors should also consider briefly discussing the connection to the single-index model literature and demonstrating how their work extends or complements existing results in that area.
