Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation from nonlinear scalar measurements of the form yi = gi(a_i^T x0), with A having i.i.d. Gaussian rows and g potentially unknown and random. It proposes to use the generalized square-root LASSO (i.e., minx ||y − Ax||2 + λ f(x) with convex f promoting structure) and derives asymptotically exact error characterizations. The central result shows that, asymptotically, the generalized LASSO with nonlinear observations behaves exactly as if the observations were linear with an effective scale and noise: yi ≈ μ a_i^T x0 + σ z_i, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], γ ∼ N(0,1). This yields a precise, three-scalar optimization formula for the asymptotic error (Theorem 2.2). The paper also specializes the result to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank settings, and uses the characterization to design optimal q-bit quantizers, showing that Lloyd–Max rules emerge as optimal for minimizing the LASSO error.

These results substantially strengthen earlier work. Brillinger’s classical analysis addressed least squares with nonlinear link in the low-dimensional regime and showed consistency up to a scale μ. Plan and Vershynin established nonasymptotic upper bounds for constrained LASSO under nonlinear observations with Gaussian designs, identifying μ and σ as key. The present work moves to precise asymptotics for the regularized (square-root) LASSO, provides exact error formulas (not just bounds), and treats a broad class of convex regularizers, connecting to and extending precise LASSO analyses for linear models via AMP and CGMT. The paper also relates to the extensive single-index model and sufficient dimension reduction literatures (e.g., Ichimura; Li & Duan), where g is unknown and one recovers x0 up to scale; here, the focus is instead on the performance of a generic convex estimator under Gaussian designs.

Strengths:
- Clear, compelling main message: generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to a particular linear model with effective μ, σ, yielding an exact performance characterization.
- Technically strong: uses modern precise analysis tools (CGMT framework) to derive a three-scalar convex program whose optimizer gives the asymptotic error; specialization to important structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) is provided.
- Novel consequences: quantizer design via minimizing σ^2/μ^2 connects convex recovery performance to Lloyd–Max rules; this is interesting and practically relevant.
- Well written overall, with good positioning relative to Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin, and useful comparisons to precise LASSO literature.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Scope restricted to i.i.d. Gaussian designs and independent links across measurements. Robustness to sub-Gaussian, anisotropic, or correlated designs is not addressed; extensions (e.g., via whitening or rotational invariance arguments) would broaden impact.
- The equivalence hinges on μ ≠ 0; even links (e.g., sign-symmetric) and some other cases are excluded. The manuscript mentions this, but a more detailed discussion of practical diagnostics or remedies (e.g., dithering, response transformations) would be helpful.
- Assumption 1 (existence of the limit F via a Moreau envelope of f*) is technical and may be opaque to many readers. More intuition and additional worked examples (beyond l1 and l1,2) would improve accessibility. The low-rank case is set up but not fully developed; providing a concrete evaluation of F and an experiment would strengthen the paper.
- Empirical validation could be expanded to include comparisons against estimators tailored to single-index models (e.g., SLS/W-SLS, SDR methods) and other nonlinear compressed sensing baselines (logistic/probit regression, specialized 1-bit CS algorithms).
- Numerous typos and small inconsistencies, especially in the proofs (e.g., “mesaurements,” “redifining,” “eventhough,” spacing and punctuation), occasionally impede readability. The nomenclature around “generalized LASSO,” “l2-LASSO,” and “square-root LASSO” could be clarified early to avoid confusion.

Quality:
The technical development appears sound and carefully executed. The CGMT-based derivations and the specialization to structured cases are convincing. The quantizer-design application is a nice, nontrivial corollary. Proofs are dense and contain many typos; careful proofreading is required.

Clarity:
The exposition is generally good, with intuitive statements (nonlinear=linear) and clear highlighting of μ and σ. However, Assumption 1 deserves more intuition; the role and computation of F should be illustrated with more examples, including a fully worked nuclear-norm case. Improve notation and terminology consistency, and fix typos.

Originality:
High. Asymptotically precise characterization of generalized square-root LASSO under nonlinear observations (with general convex f) appears new, and the exact equivalence to a linear model with effective parameters is crisp and impactful. The application to optimal quantization is also novel in this context.

Significance:
High. The results sharpen our understanding of convex estimators under model misspecification/nonlinear links, unify several settings (including quantized and censored measurements), and give actionable guidance (e.g., quantizer design). Likely to be influential for both theory and practice under Gaussian designs.

Pro acceptance:
- Precise, insightful theory with clean equivalence and broad applicability across regularizers.
- Extends and unifies multiple lines of work; likely to be a reference for nonlinear measurement models.
- Practical takeaway on quantizer design.

Con acceptance:
- Limited to Gaussian designs and μ ≠ 0; robustness and diagnostics not fully explored.
- Technical assumption (Assumption 1) under-explained; low-rank case underdeveloped.
- Typos and minor clarity issues.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a short subsection giving intuition for Assumption 1 and a recipe to compute F for popular f (nuclear norm example worked out).
- Report experiments for low-rank recovery and include baselines from single-index/SDR methods.
- Discuss extensions to sub-Gaussian or anisotropic designs; even a conjecture and preliminary evidence would help.
- Provide practical guidance when μ is small or unknown (estimation of μ, use of dithering, or alternative estimators).
- Thorough proofreading to fix typos and nomenclature.
