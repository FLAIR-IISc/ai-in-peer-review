This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. The core idea – demonstrating asymptotic equivalence between non-linear measurements and scaled, noisy linear measurements – is well-motivated by the existing work of Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, and rigorously extended to provide precise error characterizations. The authors successfully build upon recent advances in analyzing the LASSO with linear measurements, applying and generalizing those frameworks to the non-linear case.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the presentation is logical, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The inclusion of illustrative figures further enhances understanding. The problem setting – estimating structured signals from non-linear measurements – is relevant to a variety of applications, including compressive sensing with quantized data.

However, one minor clarity issue exists. The crucial equivalence between the single-index model and a linear model with scaled signals and non-standard noise (as originally established by Brillinger, 1982) is only detailed in Appendix A. This equivalence is fundamental to justifying the blockquote on line 140, which directly references Brillinger's work. Explicitly mentioning this equivalence in the main text, even briefly, would strengthen the reader’s understanding of the justification. A sentence like, "As shown in Appendix A, the single-index model can be recast as a linear model with scaled signals and non-standard noise, providing the basis for applying linear estimation techniques," would be sufficient.

More substantially, while the paper provides a powerful theoretical result, it could benefit from discussing its practical implications for algorithm selection. The l2-lasso (square-root lasso) is highlighted as having an oracle regularization parameter independent of the noise variance. Given this property, a key practical question arises: do the results presented here support preferring the l2-lasso over the l2^2-lasso in practice, particularly when dealing with non-linear measurements? Addressing this point – perhaps through a brief discussion or simulation – would significantly increase the paper's impact and appeal to practitioners. It's worth noting that while the theoretical analysis focuses on asymptotic behavior, practical scenarios often involve finite sample sizes where the noise variance dependence might be more significant. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** Rigorous mathematical analysis and derivation of precise error bounds.
*   **Novel Contribution:** Extends existing theory to non-linear measurements and provides new insights into the role of the link function.
*   **Excellent Clarity:** Well-written, organized, and easy to understand.
*   **Relevance:** Addresses a practically important problem with applications in various fields.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:**  Clearly situates the work within the context of related literature (Brillinger, Plan & Vershynin, and recent LASSO analysis).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity Issue:** Lack of explicit mention of the Brillinger equivalence in the main text.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Limited discussion of practical implications and comparison with alternative algorithms (e.g., l2^2-lasso).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in the field of high-dimensional estimation.
*   The results are rigorously derived and provide valuable insights into the behavior of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements.
*   The writing is exceptionally clear and the paper is well-organized.
*   The work builds upon and extends existing knowledge in a meaningful way.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of discussion regarding practical implications limits the paper’s immediate impact.
*   The minor clarity issue regarding the Brillinger equivalence could slightly hinder understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The minor weaknesses identified are easily addressable and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors briefly address the practical relevance of their results and clarify the connection to Brillinger’s work in the main text. The paper is a valuable addition to the literature on sparse recovery and compressive sensing.
