This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`, known as the single-index model. Instead of designing a new estimator tailored to the non-linearity, the authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO (specifically, the square-root or l2-LASSO), which naively assumes a linear model. The main contribution is a powerful and elegant result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit, the mean squared error of the l2-LASSO on this non-linear problem is *precisely* the same as its error on an equivalent linear problem with measurements `y_i = μa_i^T x0 + σz_i`. The scaling factor `μ` and effective noise variance `σ^2` are determined solely by the link function `g`.

This work builds upon and significantly extends two main lines of research. First, it generalizes the classical result of Brillinger (1982), which showed a similar equivalence for unregularized least-squares in the classical, fixed-dimension (`n`) setting. Second, it makes precise the recent non-asymptotic upper bounds of Plan and Vershynin (2015) for constrained LASSO, replacing loose constants with exact characterizations. The analysis leverages the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, which has recently been used to obtain precise error characterizations for LASSO in the linear measurements setting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper's main result is highly significant. It provides a definitive and surprisingly simple answer to the question of how a standard linear estimator behaves under the model mismatch of non-linear measurements. The equivalence to a linear model with parameters `μ` and `σ` is a powerful insight that simplifies the analysis of a complex problem. This is a novel and fundamental contribution to the understanding of high-dimensional estimation. The work also extends prior precise analyses of the l2-LASSO (e.g., [20]) by relaxing the Gaussianity assumption on the signal's non-zero entries to more general distributions, which is an important theoretical advance in its own right.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The proofs, based on the CGMT framework, are rigorous and lead to precise, verifiable formulas for the asymptotic mean squared error. The theoretical predictions are shown to be remarkably accurate through simulations across various settings (sparse, group-sparse) and signal distributions (Gaussian, Bernoulli), which strongly supports the validity of the claims. The application to optimal quantizer design is a compelling demonstration of the theory's utility, transforming a difficult problem into a tractable optimization of the `σ^2/μ^2` ratio.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to the existing literature. The main theorems are stated clearly, and the assumptions are laid out explicitly. The figures provide excellent visual confirmation of the main theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity in Motivation:** The central claim in the introduction (around line 140) that "the LASSO treats non-linear measurements exactly as if they were scaled and noisy linear measurements" is a key motivation. However, the justification for even considering this possibility, rooted in Brillinger's classical work on the unregularized case, is deferred to the appendix. Mentioning Brillinger's result in the introduction would provide stronger context and a more gradual build-up for the reader, better motivating why one might expect such an equivalence to hold before it is formally proven later in the paper.

*   **Discussion of Practical Relevance (l2 vs l2^2-LASSO):** The paper focuses on the l2-LASSO (square-root LASSO), which is known to possess the practical advantage that its "oracle" regularization parameter `λ` is independent of the noise variance. The main result shows that the non-linear measurement model is equivalent to a linear one with an effective noise level `σ`. This connection is profound and has important practical implications that are not discussed. Specifically, for the standard l2^2-LASSO (`min ||y-Ax||_2^2 + λf(x)`), the optimal choice of `λ` depends directly on the noise variance. In the single-index model setting, the effective noise variance `σ^2` depends on the link function `g`, which may be unknown. Therefore, the authors' results implicitly provide a strong argument for preferring the l2-LASSO over the l2^2-LASSO in this setting, as it is robust to the unknown effective noise level. A discussion of this point would significantly strengthen the paper's practical impact and provide valuable guidance to practitioners.

*   **Assumptions on the Signal:** The analysis relies on a random matrix theory framework where the signal `x0` is drawn from a probability distribution, and the results hold in expectation or with high probability over this draw. While standard in this line of work, it is worth acknowledging that this is a different setting from work that provides guarantees for deterministic, arbitrary signals.

**Summary:**

This is an excellent paper presenting a fundamental and surprisingly clean result. The theoretical contribution is significant, providing a precise, asymptotic characterization of the l2-LASSO's performance for single-index models and extending previous analyses to more general signal priors. The technical quality is high, and the claims are well-supported. While the paper could be improved by slightly reorganizing the motivation in the introduction and by adding a discussion on the practical implications concerning the choice between l2 and l2^2-LASSO, these are minor points. The work is a substantial advance in our understanding of high-dimensional regression under model mismatch and is very likely to inspire follow-up research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
