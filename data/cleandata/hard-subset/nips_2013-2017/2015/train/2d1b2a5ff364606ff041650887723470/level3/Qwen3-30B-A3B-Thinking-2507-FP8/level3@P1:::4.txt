

This paper addresses a fundamental question in high-dimensional estimation: how well can the Generalized LASSO recover structured signals from non-linear measurements? The authors extend the work of Plan and Vershynin by providing precise asymptotic error expressions for the Generalized LASSO when measurements follow yi = gi(ai^T x0), where gi are independent copies of a non-linear link function g. Their main result (Theorem 2.1) shows that the asymptotic estimation error under non-linear measurements is identical to that of a linear measurement model with scaled observations yi = μai^T x0 + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ)-μγ)²] for γ ~ N(0,1). This generalizes Brillinger's classical results to the regularized case with structured signals.

The paper's primary strength lies in its precise error characterization, which goes beyond the upper bounds provided by Plan and Vershynin. The authors derive explicit expressions for the asymptotic error that capture the roles of the link function g, the signal structure, and the regularizer choice. Their application to q-bit compressive sensing (Section 2.3) is particularly compelling, showing that the optimal quantizer is the Lloyd-Max quantizer - a significant practical contribution that demonstrates the paper's applicability.

The paper is well-written with clear organization, making the complex technical content accessible. The figures (Figures 1-2) effectively validate the theoretical predictions across various signal structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank). The authors also provide a thorough comparison with recent work on linear measurement models (e.g., using CGMT framework), positioning their contribution within the broader literature.

However, the paper has several limitations. The Gaussian assumption for the measurement matrix A is not justified beyond convenience; the authors should address why this assumption is necessary for their results. The paper discusses convex regularizers generally but doesn't provide guidance on how to select the regularizer based on the structure of x0 (i.e., how to choose K in the constrained LASSO). The dependence on μ and σ is central to the results, yet the authors don't thoroughly analyze whether these characterizations are necessary or if they could be simplified under certain conditions.

Critically, the paper misses important citations to relevant literature. The authors should reference the extensive work on single-index models (e.g., Li & Duan, 1985; Ichimura, 1993) that studies linear estimation under unknown link functions. More significantly, they overlook recent work by Yi et al. (2015) which addresses sparse recovery with non-linear measurements when μ = 0 and establishes equivalence between linear and nonlinear high-dimensional estimation - a direct precursor to their work that should be acknowledged.

Regarding quality, the technical content is sound with precise asymptotic analysis, though the Gaussian measurement assumption requires justification. The paper excels in clarity with well-organized presentation and effective figures. Originality is strong in the precise error characterization for non-linear measurements, though the core equivalence result builds directly on Plan and Vershynin. Significance is demonstrated through the practical application to optimal quantizer design and theoretical insights into the behavior of the Generalized LASSO.

The main weaknesses are the missing citations to relevant literature (especially Yi et al.) and the unaddressed justification for the Gaussian measurement assumption. While the paper presents interesting and useful results, the missing connections to existing work on single-index models and the unresolved question about the necessity of the Gaussian assumption slightly diminish the contribution.

Pro acceptance: Precise error characterization for non-linear measurements, practical application to optimal quantization, strong technical content, well-written.

Con against acceptance: Missing citations to relevant literature (Yi et al., single-index model work), unaddressed justification for Gaussian measurement assumption, lack of guidance on regularizer selection based on signal structure.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. The theoretical results are novel, well-supported, and have practical implications. With minor revisions addressing the missing citations and justifying the Gaussian assumption, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
