

This paper investigates the asymptotic estimation error of the Generalized LASSO when applied to non-linear measurement models of the form $y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)$, extending the work of Plan & Vershynin. The main contribution is Theorem 2.1, which establishes that the asymptotic error of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is identical to that of a linear measurement model $y_i = \mu a_i^T x_0 + \sigma z_i$, where $\mu = \mathbb{E}[\gamma g(\gamma)]$ and $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[(g(\gamma)-\mu\gamma)^2]$ for $\gamma \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. The authors derive precise error expressions for various structured signal recovery problems (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank), providing a significant extension to previous work on linear measurements.

The paper is well-written and presents interesting theoretical results. The technical quality is strong, with rigorous asymptotic analysis using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem framework. The main strength lies in providing the first precise characterization of Generalized LASSO performance under non-linear measurements, which has important implications for applications like 1-bit quantization.

However, several concerns warrant attention. First, the Gaussian measurement assumption in Theorem 2.1 is not justified, and the necessity of this assumption for the main result is unclear. Second, the paper fails to address how to select the regularizer $f$ based on the structure set $K$ containing $x_0$, which is crucial for practical implementation. Third, the dependence on $\mu$ and $\sigma$ characterizing the link function difficulty is not adequately discussed—these parameters are essential to the results but their necessity isn't proven.

The paper also misses important related work. The authors should cite the single-index model literature (e.g., Li & Duan, 1985; Semiparametric Least Squares) and particularly Yi et al.'s recent work (2015) on optimal linear estimation under unknown link functions, which addresses sparse recovery when $\mu=0$ and shows equivalence between linear and nonlinear estimation in high dimensions.

While the results are theoretically significant and the paper is well-executed, these omissions and unresolved questions about the assumptions and practical implementation somewhat limit the impact of the contribution. The paper would benefit from addressing these issues to strengthen its theoretical foundation and practical relevance.
