This paper studies high-dimensional estimation from non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) using the generalized LASSO (l2-LASSO/square-root LASSO). The main result is an asymptotically exact error characterization showing that, with Gaussian design A, the l2-LASSO under non-linear measurements behaves as if it were solving a linear regression problem with effective signal μx0 and additive Gaussian noise of variance σ^2, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2] with γ ∼ N(0,1). Building on the CGMT line of work, the authors provide: (i) a rigorous non-linear=linear equivalence (Theorem 2.1) and (ii) a precise scalar optimization predicting the asymptotic squared error (Theorem 2.2), applicable to a broad class of convex regularizers and signal priors p(x0). They further specialize these predictions to sparse (l1), group-sparse (l1,2), and low-rank (nuclear norm) settings, and derive explicit equations for the sparse case (Theorem 2.3). As an application, they show that, for q-bit quantized measurements, minimizing the asymptotic LASSO error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 over quantizer thresholds/levels, which leads to Lloyd–Max optimality.

Relation to prior work: The paper unifies and strengthens Brillinger’s classical single-index regression (consistency up to scale for fixed n, m → ∞) and Plan–Vershynin’s non-asymptotic constrained LASSO bounds, by providing exact asymptotic error formulas for the regularized l2-LASSO in the proportional regime (m/n → δ). It also extends precise LASSO analyses for linear models (e.g., Bayati–Montanari, Donoho–Maleki–Montanari via AMP; Stojnic and Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi via CGMT) to non-linear measurements, and relaxes the common Gaussian prior assumption on x0 by working with general p(x0) satisfying mild moment conditions.

Quality: The analysis is careful and technically strong. Theorems 2.1–2.2 are clearly stated with explicit assumptions (Gaussian A; linear asymptotics; Assumption 1 on p(x0), f). The sparse specialization (Theorem 2.3) recovers known linear-model results as special cases and matches simulations. The quantization application is compelling and the σ^2/μ^2 reduction is both intuitive and useful. Two areas where the presentation can be strengthened: (i) Assumption 1 is abstract; providing concrete sufficient conditions and examples (beyond sparse/group-sparse/low-rank sketches) would improve readability and reproducibility. (ii) Practical implications for tuning λ and model choice (l2 vs l2^2 loss) deserve a more explicit discussion (see “Practical relevance” below).

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and well organized. However, the central non-linear=linear equivalence that justifies much of the intuition appears to be relegated to Appendix A. Given its importance and the blockquote around line 140, this equivalence should be stated and briefly justified in the main text (even if proofs remain in the appendix). It would also help to: (a) clearly reconcile the terminology l2-LASSO vs square-root LASSO early on, (b) streamline notation around the normalization of x0, and (c) provide a short “recipe” for computing F(c1,c2,c3) for common regularizers.

Originality: To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically exact error analysis of LASSO-type estimators under general single-index non-linear measurements, covering broad convex regularizers and relaxing Gaussianity of the signal prior. The quantizer-optimality consequence (Lloyd–Max via σ^2/μ^2) is a nice, non-obvious byproduct.

Significance: The results give a precise and interpretable bridge from non-linear to linear models, enabling immediate transfer of a large body of LASSO theory and practice. This will likely influence both methodological work (e.g., design under quantization/censoring) and practice (performance prediction and parameter selection).

Practical relevance and requested clarifications:
- l2-LASSO vs l2^2-LASSO: A key practical distinction is that the l2-LASSO’s oracle λ is pivotal w.r.t. the noise variance, whereas l2^2-LASSO tuning depends on σ. Since Theorem 2.1 reduces non-linear measurements to an equivalent linear model with parameters (μ,σ), please discuss whether your results empirically/theoretically support preferring l2-LASSO over l2^2-LASSO when g (hence σ) is unknown or hard to estimate. Small-scale experiments comparing both losses under misspecified σ would be informative.
- Estimating μ,σ: When g is unknown, can μ and σ be estimated (e.g., from moments of (A,y) or via a pilot) well enough for the linear-equivalence predictions to remain accurate? Even a brief discussion of identifiability and robustness would help practitioners.
- Assumption 1: Provide concrete sufficient conditions and a short catalog for common f (l1, group, nuclear) and typical p(x0) to help readers verify applicability.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution with precise, interpretable results.
- Broad coverage of regularizers and priors; extension beyond sparse case.
- Clear connection to and advancement over Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin.
- Useful application to quantizer design with an actionable takeaway (Lloyd–Max).
- Simulations corroborate theory.

Cons / weaknesses:
- Gaussian A assumption; no discussion of universality beyond Gaussian designs.
- Central equivalence result appears only in the appendix; should be elevated to the main text.
- Practical guidance on λ selection and choice of loss (l2 vs l2^2) is limited.
- Assumption 1 is abstract; needs more concrete sufficient conditions and examples.
- Limited empirical exploration (e.g., unknown g, μ=0 cases, robustness).

Minor comments and suggestions:
- Explicitly note early that only the direction of x0 is identifiable when μ=0 or g is unspecified; point to any strategies for these regimes.
- In the low-rank section, add a concrete example where the empirical singular value distribution assumption holds, and a short note on numerically evaluating F.
- In the quantization section, clarify the optimization landscape and initialization for Lloyd–Max when minimizing σ^2/μ^2, and report sensitivity to local minima.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution that advances precise high-dimensional theory for non-linear measurements and has clear implications for practice. With the suggested clarifications and a brief practical discussion of loss/λ choices, it would make a strong NIPS paper.
