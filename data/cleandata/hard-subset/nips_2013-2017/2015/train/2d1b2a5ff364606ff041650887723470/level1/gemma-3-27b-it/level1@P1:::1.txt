This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to estimating a structured signal from nonlinear measurements. The core result demonstrates that, asymptotically, the performance of the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements is equivalent to that of a linear measurement model with scaled and noisy observations, where the scaling and noise parameters are explicitly characterized in terms of the nonlinear link function. This is a significant strengthening of previous work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin. The authors provide precise expressions for the estimation error and explore applications to quantized measurements, deriving the optimality of the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

**Summary of Contributions:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the Generalized LASSO with nonlinear measurements, offering a surprising and useful equivalence to a linear model. The derived error expressions are novel and accurate, contributing to a deeper understanding of the algorithm's behavior in this context. The application to quantized measurements is a practical and insightful result.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds on established results from statistics and signal processing and provides a mathematically rigorous analysis. The asymptotic analysis appears sound, and the derivations are well-motivated.
*   **Novelty & Significance:** The main result – the equivalence to a linear model – is both surprising and highly valuable. It simplifies the analysis of nonlinear measurements and leverages existing knowledge from the linear case. The precise error characterization is a significant advance.
*   **Practical Application:** The application to quantized measurements is a compelling example, demonstrating the practical relevance of the theoretical findings. The identification of the Lloyd-Max quantizer as optimal is a notable contribution.
*   **Clear Presentation (Mostly):** The paper is generally well-organized, and the key results are clearly stated. The notation is standard, and the authors make an effort to connect their work to existing literature.
*   **Thorough Literature Review:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of related work, citing relevant papers from statistics, signal processing, and machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Density:** While the presentation is generally clear, the paper is quite technical and may be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with high-dimensional statistics and optimization. The numerous theorems and equations can be overwhelming.
*   **Assumptions:** The results rely on several assumptions, including i.i.d. Gaussian entries in the measurement matrix and a specific normalization of the signal. The robustness of the results to deviations from these assumptions is not fully explored.  The assumption of unit norm for x0, while acknowledged as simplifying, feels somewhat restrictive without further discussion of generalization.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors identify the Lloyd-Max algorithm for optimal quantization, the computational cost of finding the optimal parameters in practice is not discussed.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The figures provide some validation, but the empirical results seem limited in scope. More extensive simulations, perhaps exploring different link functions and signal structures, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Clarity in Some Sections:** The transition between theorems and proofs could be smoother.  Some of the notation within the proofs is dense and requires careful following.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While building upon existing results, the authors significantly extend the theory and provide a novel and insightful perspective on the problem of estimation with nonlinear measurements. The precise error analysis and the connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer are particularly original contributions.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-written, demonstrating a strong understanding of the relevant literature and techniques. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, but the technical density and the number of equations can make it challenging to follow. A more gentle introduction to the key concepts and a more intuitive explanation of the results would improve clarity.

**Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to impact research in a variety of areas, including compressed sensing, signal processing, and machine learning. The equivalence to a linear model simplifies the analysis and opens up new avenues for research.  The optimality result for Lloyd-Max quantization is directly applicable to practical system design.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant theoretical results.
*   Strong technical foundation and rigorous analysis.
*   Practical application to quantized measurements with a useful optimality result.
*   Well-written and thoroughly researched.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Technical density may limit accessibility.
*   Limited empirical validation.
*   Assumptions could be further investigated for robustness.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper. Despite some minor weaknesses, the novelty, significance, and technical quality of the work are outstanding. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is likely to have a lasting impact. The authors have addressed a challenging problem and provided a surprisingly elegant and insightful solution. A request for minor revisions to improve clarity and add more extensive empirical validation would be appropriate.
