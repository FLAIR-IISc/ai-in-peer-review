- Paper studies recovery of structured signals from non-linear measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) using the Generalized LASSO under Gaussian designs and large n,m.  
- Main theoretical insight: asymptotic error of Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements matches that for linear measurements y_i = μ(a_i^T x0) + σ z_i with μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ)-μγ)^2].  
- Theorem 2.1 establishes the error equivalence between non-linear and scaled-noisy linear measurements.  
- Theorem 2.2 gives an explicit convex program to compute the asymptotic error for a broad class of regularizers and link functions.  
- Theorem 2.3 and extensions cover sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery settings.  
- Practical application: q-bit compressive sensing analysis showing the Lloyd–Max quantizer is optimal for minimizing LASSO error.  
- Significant advancement over prior work (Brillinger; Plan & Vershynin) by providing precise asymptotic error expressions instead of upper bounds.  
- Results are general, applying to many regularizers (including ℓ1, ℓ1,2, nuclear norm) and link functions.  
- Numerical validation is thorough (Figures 1–2) and supports the theoretical predictions.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with careful discussion of limitations.  
- Strength: provides a novel, precise characterization that fills an important theoretical gap.  
- Strength: practical implications for designing measurement systems with non-linearities and for parameter tuning.  
- Weakness: comparison with Plan & Vershynin could better emphasize the distinct roles of μ and σ^2.  
- Weakness: assumption that x0 has unit norm excludes some cases (e.g., even link functions).  
- Weakness: main proofs are deferred to the appendix, making technical assessment harder without reading supplementary material.  
- Weakness: could more clearly argue practical importance of precise error formulas beyond theoretical interest.  
- Overall: technically sound, novel, and significant with minor limitations; suitable for NIPS given its relevance to high-dimensional statistics and ML.  
- Pros: precise asymptotic expressions, broad applicability, rigorous theory with experiments; Cons: restrictive assumptions, deferred proofs, and limited contrast with prior bounds.
