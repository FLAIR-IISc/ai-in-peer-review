### Paper Review

**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a structured signal `x0` (e.g., sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) from measurements `y` obtained through a potentially unknown, non-linear link function `g`, such that `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0)`. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized-LASSO estimator, which naively fits a linear model `y ≈ Ax` while using a convex regularizer `f(x)` to promote the known structure of `x0`.

The central contribution is a precise, asymptotic characterization of the mean-squared error of this estimator. The main result (Theorem 2.1) is a powerful and elegant equivalence: in the high-dimensional asymptotic regime where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow proportionally, the estimation error `||x̂ - μx0||^2` for the non-linear problem is exactly the same as the error for an equivalent linear problem with measurements `y_lin = A(μx0) + σz`. The scaling factor `μ = E[γg(γ)]` and effective noise variance `σ^2 = E[(g(γ) - μγ)^2]` (where `γ` is a standard normal random variable) depend only on the statistics of the link function `g`.

Building on this equivalence, the paper provides a precise formula for the asymptotic error (Theorem 2.2) as the solution to a small, deterministic, scalar optimization problem. This general result is then specialized to important cases like sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery. Finally, the theory is applied to the practical problem of designing optimal q-bit quantizers for compressive sensing, showing that error minimization is equivalent to minimizing the ratio `σ^2/μ^2`, a problem for which the Lloyd-Max algorithm is shown to be effective.

This work builds upon a rich body of literature. It extends the classical result of Brillinger on unregularized least-squares (fixed `n`, `m` -> ∞) to the modern high-dimensional, regularized setting. It also significantly sharpens recent non-asymptotic bounds by Plan and Vershynin, replacing their upper bounds with exact characterizations. Furthermore, it contributes to the line of research on the precise analysis of LASSO-type estimators (e.g., using AMP or CGMT frameworks), extending these powerful tools from the linear measurement case to a broad class of non-linear models.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of exceptional technical quality. The theoretical claims are strong and appear to be rigorously proven (though proofs are in the appendix). The core equivalence result is profound and provides a deep understanding of why a simple, model-mismatched estimator works so well. The work is very complete; it presents a general theory and then meticulously applies it to several of the most important structured signal models in the field. The claims are strongly supported by numerical simulations (Figures 1 and 2), which show a perfect match between the theoretical predictions and empirical performance, lending high confidence to the analysis. The authors are careful to state their assumptions (i.i.d. Gaussian matrix, asymptotic regime) clearly.

**Clarity:** The paper is a model of clarity. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem, summarizing the main contributions, and positioning the work within the existing literature. The flow is logical, moving from the general theory to specific instantiations and a practical application. The theorems are stated precisely and cleanly. The notation is consistent and the writing is clear and concise. This level of clarity makes a technically dense paper highly accessible.

**Originality:** The work is highly original and innovative. The primary contribution—the exact asymptotic equivalence between the LASSO's performance on a non-linear model and a corresponding linear one—is a novel and fundamental insight. While prior work hinted at the roles of `μ` and `σ`, this paper is the first to establish this precise, quantitative relationship for the regularized LASSO. This moves the field from loose performance bounds to exact predictions. The specialization of the results to group-sparse and low-rank models is also valuable, and the authors claim these results are new even for the standard linear measurement setting, further enhancing the paper's originality. The application to optimal quantizer design is a clever and non-obvious consequence of the main theory, demonstrating its practical reach.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is very high.
*   **Theoretical Impact:** This work provides a powerful analytical tool and a fundamental simplification for a broad class of problems. The idea that, under Gaussian measurements, many non-linear distortions can be distilled into an effective signal attenuation and additive Gaussian noise is an elegant and impactful concept. It is likely to influence future theoretical work on high-dimensional inference with non-ideal measurements.
*   **Practical Impact:** The results have immediate relevance. They give practitioners a tool to accurately predict the performance of the widely-used LASSO algorithm in settings with known nonlinearities like sensor saturation or quantization, without resorting to expensive Monte Carlo simulations. The finding that the optimal quantizer for this setup is the well-known Lloyd-Max quantizer is a clean and actionable result. The fact that the LASSO procedure itself does not require knowledge of the link function `g` is a major practical advantage that this paper rigorously justifies and characterizes.

**Weaknesses:** The weaknesses are minor and do not detract significantly from the contribution.
1.  The analysis relies on the standard but strong assumptions of an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix `A` and a high-dimensional asymptotic limit. While common in this literature, a brief discussion on the robustness of these results to structured or non-Gaussian matrices, or how they might apply in finite `(m, n)` regimes, would be beneficial.
2.  The section on low-rank matrix recovery feels slightly less complete than the others, as it defers some details and relies on an extra assumption about the convergence of singular value distributions.
3.  While understandable due to space constraints, the main text offers little intuition for *why* the equivalence theorem holds. A brief, high-level sketch of the proof technique in the main body could have enhanced the reader's understanding.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Provides a fundamental, novel, and elegant theoretical result establishing an equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models for the LASSO.
*   **Pro:** Advances the state-of-the-art from loose performance bounds to precise, verifiable asymptotic characterizations.
*   **Pro:** Technically very strong, clearly written, and well-supported by convincing simulations.
*   **Pro:** High potential for both theoretical and practical impact, with direct application to areas like quantized compressive sensing.
*   **Pro:** The analysis covers a broad and important class of regularizers (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con (minor):** Relies on strong, though standard, assumptions (i.i.d. Gaussian `A`, asymptotics) that may limit direct applicability in some practical scenarios.
*   **Con (minor):** The low-rank recovery section is slightly underdeveloped compared to the rest of the paper.

In summary, this is an outstanding paper that makes a significant and lasting contribution to the theory of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing. The strengths overwhelmingly outweigh the minor weaknesses. It is an ideal fit for NIPS and is likely to be widely cited and built upon.
