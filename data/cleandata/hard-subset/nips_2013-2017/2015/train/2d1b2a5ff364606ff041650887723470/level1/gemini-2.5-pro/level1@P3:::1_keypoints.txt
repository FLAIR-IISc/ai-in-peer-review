- Main contribution: Proves that in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit, LASSO on non-linear measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0) has the same MSE as on an equivalent linear model y_i = mu a_i^T x_0 + sigma z_i, with mu and sigma determined by g.  
- Builds on prior work: Generalizes Brillinger’s unregularized LS result and sharpens Plan & Vershynin’s non-asymptotic bounds into exact asymptotic formulas.  
- Methodology: Uses the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) to derive asymptotically exact error expressions for a wide class of convex regularizers.  
- Theoretical results: Theorem 2.2 gives a general formula for error; Theorem 2.3 provides an explicit characterization for sparse recovery.  
- Empirical validation: Simulations (Figures 1–2) show excellent agreement with theoretical predictions.  
- Generality: Analysis covers various signal structures (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and corresponding convex penalties (L1, group-LASSO, nuclear norm).  
- Practical application: Reduces optimal q-bit quantizer design for compressive sensing to minimizing sigma^2/mu^2, linking to the Lloyd–Max algorithm.  
- Strength — conceptual impact: Provides a deep, intuitive explanation for why model-mismatched LASSO can perform well on non-linear measurements.  
- Strength — technical precision: Moves from upper bounds to asymptotically exact characterizations using sophisticated CGMT derivations.  
- Strength — clarity: Well-written and well-organized presentation, with main results stated upfront for accessibility.  
- Weakness — Gaussian design: Analysis assumes i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, limiting direct applicability to structured measurement ensembles.  
- Weakness — asymptotic nature: Results are strictly asymptotic with no finite-sample rates or guidance on how large n must be for accuracy.  
- Weakness — opacity of Assumption 1: Assumption 1 is central but may be opaque to readers unfamiliar with CGMT; needs more intuition.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept — significant, elegant, and technically rigorous contribution with clear practical implications despite Gaussian/asymptotic limitations.
