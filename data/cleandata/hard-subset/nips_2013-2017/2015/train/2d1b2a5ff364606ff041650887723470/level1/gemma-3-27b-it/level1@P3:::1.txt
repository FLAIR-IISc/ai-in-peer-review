This paper investigates the performance of the Generalized LASSO (GLASSO) when applied to estimation problems with non-linear measurements, specifically demonstrating that, asymptotically, the non-linear measurement problem is equivalent to a linear one with appropriately scaled measurements and noise. The results build on earlier work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, and significantly strengthen them by providing *precise* expressions for the estimation error. The paper is well-motivated, tackles an important problem, and the results are presented with significant mathematical rigor.

**Summary of Contributions:** The primary contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that the GLASSO’s performance with non-linear measurements is asymptotically identical to that of a linear measurement model with scaled measurements and additive Gaussian noise.  This is quantified through explicit expressions for the squared error, a result previously unknown. A consequential application is identifying the optimal quantizer (Lloyd-Max) for minimizing the estimation error in the context of 1-bit compressive sensing. The paper also provides a thorough discussion of related work and positions its contributions clearly within the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears very strong, utilizing tools from high-dimensional statistics and random matrix theory. The proofs are likely to be complex, but the paper clearly outlines the key assumptions and provides a roadmap for understanding the derivations.
*   **Novelty & Originality:** The precise error characterization for non-linear measurements is a significant advancement. While the equivalence to a linear model was hinted at in previous work, this paper provides a quantifiable, asymptotic result. The application to optimal quantization is also a valuable contribution.
*   **Significance:**  The result has implications for a wide range of applications where measurements are inherently non-linear (e.g., 1-bit sensing, signal processing with device non-linearities).  The identification of the Lloyd-Max quantizer is a practical outcome with direct relevance to compressive sensing.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is standard, and the authors carefully define key terms. The connection to existing work is clearly explained. The inclusion of illustrative examples (Figures 1 & 2) is beneficial.
*   **Thorough Literature Review:** The authors provide an extensive and well-organized review of related work, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field and properly citing relevant papers (including NIPS proceedings).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The paper relies on several assumptions, including i.i.d. Gaussian entries in the measurement matrix A and a specific form of the asymptotic regime. While these assumptions are common in the field, the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions is not thoroughly discussed.  Specifically, the authors mention the complexity of generalizing beyond Gaussian matrices, but don't offer any intuition about expected behavior for other matrix distributions.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the derived error expressions are precise, evaluating them in practice could be computationally challenging, particularly for complex regularizers. The paper briefly mentions numerical evaluation but does not provide any practical guidance.
*   **Generality of Assumption 1:**  The assumption regarding the limit of `n e√n(f∗)(n)` (Assumption 1) seems quite strong. While justified for certain common regularizers (like `1`-norm), its verification for other, more complex regularizers might be non-trivial and could limit the applicability of the results. More discussion about the types of regularizers for which this assumption holds, or potential relaxations of the assumption, would be valuable.
* **Presentation of Theorem 2.3**: The jump from Theorem 2.2 to 2.3 is abrupt. A more detailed explanation linking the two would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result with significant implications for the field of compressive sensing and related areas.
*   The analysis is rigorous and builds on a strong foundation of existing work.
*   The identification of the optimal quantizer is a practical and valuable contribution.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumptions might limit the applicability of the results in certain scenarios.
*   The computational complexity of evaluating the derived error expressions could be a barrier to practical implementation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the theory of non-linear estimation. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised about the assumptions, computational complexity, and clarifying the jump between theorems. Further discussion on the robustness of the results to deviations from the idealized assumptions would also be beneficial.
