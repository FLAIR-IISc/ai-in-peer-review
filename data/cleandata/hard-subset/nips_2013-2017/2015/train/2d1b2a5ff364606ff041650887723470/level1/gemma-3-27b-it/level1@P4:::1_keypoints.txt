- Main contribution: Shows asymptotic equivalence between Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements and a scaled, noisy linear LASSO, giving explicit error formulas and optimal quantizer identification.
- Motivation/context: Tackles practical estimation under non-linearities and builds on Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin, extending results to high-dimensional settings.
- Novelty: Provides asymptotically precise error expressions where prior work gave only bounds or consistency.
- Technical soundness: Mathematical analysis appears rigorous, leveraging high-dimensional statistics and convex optimization; the µ scaling and σ² noise link is well-established.
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, organized, with consistent notation and clear theorems and contribution summary.
- Significance: Results impact signal processing, ML, econometrics; optimal quantizer result is practically valuable.
- Thoroughness: Considers multiple scenarios (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and includes q-bit compressive sensing application.
- Assumptions concern: Assumption 1 (limiting behavior of the Fenchel conjugate) is strong and may be hard to verify in practice; needs more discussion on feasibility.
- Computational complexity: Calculating the precise error expressions can be computationally challenging for complex regularizers or high dimensions; more on efficient methods is needed.
- Empirical validation: Limited experiments (Figures 1–2); more extensive simulations across parameters and non-linearities would strengthen the work.
- Literature connections: While extensive, connections to robust estimation and outlier detection under non-linear measurements could be better integrated.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, significant theoretical contribution with rigorous analysis, clear presentation, and practical relevance.
- Arguments against acceptance: Strong assumptions, computational barriers, and limited empirical validation may limit applicability.
- Overall assessment: Strong paper worthy of acceptance pending clearer discussion of Assumption 1’s practicality and expanded empirical validation.
