This paper investigates the estimation performance of the Generalized LASSO when applied to measurements corrupted by non-linear link functions. The core result demonstrates that, asymptotically, the performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is equivalent to that of a linear measurement model with scaled and noisy observations. The authors derive explicit expressions for the estimation error and highlight a consequence: the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error is the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical problem where measurement devices often exhibit non-linearities. The connection to Brillinger’s earlier work and the more recent contributions of Plan and Vershynin are clearly articulated. The problem setting is also relevant to fields like signal processing and machine learning where compressive sensing and sparse recovery are widely used. The mathematical exposition is generally rigorous, and the authors make an effort to carefully state their assumptions and provide conditions for the theorems to hold. The extension to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery scenarios is valuable, demonstrating the generality of the results. The application to q-bit compressive sensing and the connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm add further practical relevance.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the notation is dense and often requires careful tracking. While mathematically precise, this makes the paper less accessible. The numerous references, while demonstrating awareness of related work, can feel overwhelming and disrupt the flow of reading. Second, while the theoretical results are strong, the numerical validation is limited. Figures 1 and 2 offer some evidence of the theory’s accuracy, but more comprehensive numerical experiments would significantly strengthen the paper’s claims. A deeper exploration of the parameter regimes where the asymptotic approximations hold would be beneficial. Third, while the authors claim this is the "first precise analysis" in this context, the distinction between "precise" and "tight" bounds isn’t fully clarified. Often in high-dimensional statistics, bounds are considered precise if they reveal the scaling with problem parameters, even if constants are unknown. Here, the level of precision achieved relative to existing work isn't always clearly delineated. Fourth, the discussion of the Assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1) feels somewhat abrupt; a more intuitive explanation of these conditions and their implications would be helpful. Finally, while the application to q-bit quantization is interesting, it feels a bit disconnected from the main theoretical results and could benefit from a more in-depth discussion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The result establishing equivalence between non-linear and scaled linear measurement models is a significant theoretical contribution.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed mathematical analysis with explicit error expressions.
*   **Generality:** The results extend to various regularization techniques (sparse, low-rank, etc.) and measurement scenarios (q-bit quantization).
*   **Potential Impact:** The findings could inform the design of better measurement systems and signal recovery algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Accessibility:** The dense notation and numerous references make the paper challenging to read.
*   **Limited Numerical Validation:** The numerical results are relatively sparse and could be expanded to strengthen the claims.
*   **Clarity of Precision:** The term "precise" needs better contextualization relative to existing work.
*   **Assumption Clarity:** The assumptions made could be explained with more intuition.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field. While it has some weaknesses in terms of accessibility and numerical validation, the theoretical results are novel, rigorous, and potentially impactful. I would rate it as a **weak accept**. Addressing the comments regarding clarity and providing more comprehensive numerical experiments would significantly strengthen the paper. I encourage the authors to revise and resubmit.
