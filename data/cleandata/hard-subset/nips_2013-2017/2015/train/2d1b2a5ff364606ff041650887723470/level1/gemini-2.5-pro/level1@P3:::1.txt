This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x_0` from non-linear measurements of the form `y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)`. Instead of designing a new estimator tailored to the non-linearity `g`, the authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO, which naively assumes a linear model. The main contribution is a powerful and elegant theoretical result: in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit (where `m, n -> infinity` with `m/n` fixed), the mean-squared error of the LASSO estimator is *exactly the same* as if it were applied to an equivalent linear model `y_i = mu * a_i^T x_0 + sigma * z_i`. The effective signal scaling `mu` and noise variance `sigma^2` are determined solely by the properties of the non-linear function `g` averaged over a standard Gaussian variable.

This work builds directly upon and significantly strengthens two lines of research. First, it generalizes a classical result by Brillinger, which established this equivalence for unregularized least-squares in the fixed-`n`, large-`m` regime. Second, it provides a precise asymptotic characterization that refines the non-asymptotic upper bounds recently developed by Plan and Vershynin for a constrained LASSO formulation. By leveraging the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, previously used for the precise analysis of LASSO with linear measurements, the authors derive exact formulas for the estimation error that hold for a wide class of convex regularizers (L1, group-LASSO, nuclear norm) and signal structures. Finally, the paper demonstrates the practical utility of this theoretical insight by applying it to the problem of optimal quantizer design for compressive sensing, showing that the complex problem of minimizing estimation error simplifies to minimizing the effective noise-to-signal ratio `sigma^2/mu^2`.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** The central result establishing the equivalence between the non-linear measurement model and an effective linear one is a significant conceptual contribution. It provides a deep and intuitive understanding of why a simple, model-mismatched estimator like the LASSO can be surprisingly effective. This equivalence simplifies a complex problem into a well-understood one, allowing a rich body of existing theory for linear models to be applied directly.

2.  **Technical Sophistication and Precision:** The paper moves beyond the upper bounds of prior work to provide asymptotically exact characterizations of the estimation error. The derivations, based on the CGMT framework, are technically impressive. Theorem 2.2 provides a general formula, and Theorem 2.3 offers a more explicit and insightful characterization for the sparse recovery case. The simulation results in Figures 1 and 2 show a remarkable match with the theoretical predictions, validating the analysis.

3.  **Generality and Scope:** The theoretical framework is presented in a general form that encompasses various signal structures (e.g., sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and their corresponding convex regularizers. This makes the results broadly applicable across many areas of high-dimensional statistics and signal processing.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction clearly situates the work in the context of existing literature, highlighting what is known and what new contributions are being made. The main results are stated upfront in Section 2 before delving into specific applications, which makes the paper easy to follow.

5.  **Practical Relevance:** The application to optimal q-bit quantizer design is compelling. It shows that the abstract theoretical result has direct, practical consequences. The simplification of the design objective to minimizing `sigma^2/mu^2` and the connection to the Lloyd-Max algorithm is a valuable insight for practitioners in compressive sensing.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on Gaussian Measurements:** The analysis is contingent on the measurement matrix `A` having i.i.d. Gaussian entries. This is a common assumption in this line of research as it is required by the CGMT, but it limits the direct applicability of the results to practical scenarios where structured matrices (e.g., subsampled Fourier or Hadamard) are used for fast computations. It would be valuable for the authors to comment on the potential for extending these results to other matrix ensembles, even if it is left as future work.

2.  **Asymptotic Regime:** The results are strictly asymptotic. While the simulations are convincing for the chosen dimensions, the theory does not provide finite-sample guarantees or a characterization of the rate of convergence to the asymptotic limit. A brief discussion on how large `n` must be for the theory to be a good approximation would be helpful.

3.  **Clarity of Assumption 1:** Assumption 1 is central to the main theorems but may be opaque to readers not deeply familiar with the CGMT literature. While the authors do a good job of showing it holds for standard regularizers, a sentence or two providing more intuition on what this assumption means (e.g., that the Moreau envelope of the dual regularizer has a well-behaved average) could enhance the paper's accessibility to a broader NIPS audience.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel, fundamental, and elegant result that unifies two important areas: non-linear statistical models and the precise analysis of high-dimensional estimators. The contribution is significant and provides a powerful new tool for analysis. The theoretical claims are strong, technically sound, and well-supported by experiments. The work is likely to have a high impact and inspire follow-up research.
*   **Con:** The main limitation is the assumption of i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrices, which is standard in this specific theoretical area but restricts immediate practical transfer. The results are also asymptotic in nature.

**Conclusion:**

This is an outstanding paper that makes a significant and lasting contribution to the theory of high-dimensional estimation. The equivalence principle it establishes is both powerful and intuitive. The technical analysis is rigorous and leads to precise, verifiable predictions that advance the state of the art. Despite the limitations inherent in the asymptotic, Gaussian-design setting, the insights are fundamental and the practical implications, particularly for quantization, are clear. The paper is well-written, impactful, and of high quality. It represents a clear and strong accept for NIPS.
