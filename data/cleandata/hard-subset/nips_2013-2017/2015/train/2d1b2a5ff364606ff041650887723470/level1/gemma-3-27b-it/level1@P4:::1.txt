This paper investigates the Generalized LASSO for estimating a structured signal from non-linear measurements. The authors demonstrate that, asymptotically, the performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is equivalent to that of a LASSO with scaled and noisy linear measurements, providing explicit expressions for the estimation error. A key consequence is the identification of the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical problem of estimation under non-linearities, and builds upon existing work by Brillinger and Plan & Vershynin. The authors clearly state their contributions, which include extending previous results to a high-dimensional setting and providing precise error expressions. The introduction provides sufficient background and contextualizes the problem effectively, referencing relevant literature. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the theorems are clearly stated. The inclusion of specific applications, such as q-bit compressive sensing, enhances the practical relevance of the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The derivation of explicit and asymptotically precise expressions for the estimation error with non-linear measurements is a significant contribution. Existing work provided bounds or consistency results, but lacked the precision achieved here.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical analysis appears solid, leveraging concepts from high-dimensional statistics and convex optimization. The connection to linear measurements via the scaling factor µ and noise variance σ² is well-established.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the arguments are presented in a logical flow. The summary of contributions is helpful.
*   **Significance:** The results have potential impact in areas where non-linear measurements are common, such as signal processing, machine learning, and econometrics. The identified optimal quantizer is a particularly valuable practical outcome.
*   **Thoroughness:** The authors consider several relevant scenarios, including sparse recovery, group-sparse recovery, and low-rank matrix recovery. The discussion of q-bit compressive sensing is a nice demonstration of the applicability of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** Assumption 1 regarding the limiting behavior of the Fenchel conjugate appears quite strong and potentially difficult to verify in practice. While the authors discuss its implications, more discussion on its practical feasibility would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the paper derives precise error expressions, calculating these expressions can be computationally challenging, especially for complex regularizers or high-dimensional problems. The authors acknowledge this, but further discussion of efficient numerical methods would be valuable.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While Figures 1 and 2 provide some validation, the empirical results are limited. More extensive simulations with varying parameters and different types of non-linearities would strengthen the paper.
*   **Connections to Existing Literature:**  The literature review is extensive, but some relevant connections could be strengthened. For example, the paper could discuss its relationship to work on robust estimation and outlier detection in the context of non-linear measurements.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical contribution to the field of high-dimensional estimation.
*   The mathematical analysis is rigorous and the results are clearly stated.
*   The results have potential practical impact in various applications.
*   The paper is well-written and organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumptions, particularly Assumption 1, may limit the applicability of the results.
*   The computational complexity of evaluating the error expressions could be a barrier to practical use.
*   The empirical validation is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. While the assumptions and computational complexity are potential concerns, the novelty and rigor of the analysis outweigh these weaknesses.  I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS, with the caveat that the authors may consider adding a more detailed discussion of Assumption 1’s practical implications and potentially including more extensive empirical validation in a revised version.
