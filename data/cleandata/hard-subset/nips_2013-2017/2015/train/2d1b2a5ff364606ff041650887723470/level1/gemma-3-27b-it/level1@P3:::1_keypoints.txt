- Main result: Asymptotically equates GLASSO on non-linear measurements to a linear model with scaled measurements and additive Gaussian noise, giving precise squared-error expressions.  
- Builds on prior work (Brillinger; Plan & Vershynin) and significantly strengthens it by providing exact error characterizations.  
- Practical application: Identifies the Lloyd–Max quantizer as optimal for minimizing estimation error in 1-bit compressive sensing.  
- Technical soundness: Rigorous high-dimensional statistical and random matrix theory analysis with clearly stated assumptions and proof roadmap.  
- Novelty: First precise asymptotic error characterization for GLASSO under non-linear measurements.  
- Significance: Results impact many non-linear measurement settings (e.g., 1-bit sensing, device non-linearities) and have practical implications.  
- Clarity: Well-written, organized presentation with standard notation and helpful illustrative figures.  
- Literature review: Thorough, well-organized discussion and proper positioning within existing work.  
- Assumptions concern: Relies on i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix and a specific asymptotic regime, with limited discussion on robustness to violations.  
- Computational concern: Evaluating the explicit error expressions may be computationally challenging for complex regularizers and lacks practical guidance.  
- Assumption 1 worry: The requirement on the limit involving n e√n(f∗)(n) is strong and may be hard to verify beyond common regularizers like the l1-norm.  
- Presentation gap: Transition from Theorem 2.2 to Theorem 2.3 is abrupt and needs a clearer linking explanation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, rigorous, significant theoretical contribution with practical quantizer identification and strong exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Strong assumptions limiting applicability and potential practical evaluation barriers due to computational complexity.  
- Overall recommendation: High-quality paper; accept with minor revisions to address assumptions, computational guidance, and clarifying theorem linkage.
