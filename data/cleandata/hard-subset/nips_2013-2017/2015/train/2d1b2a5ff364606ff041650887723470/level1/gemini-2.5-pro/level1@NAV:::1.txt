This paper studies the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal from non-linear measurements using the Generalized LASSO. The central and powerful result is that, in the high-dimensional asymptotic limit, the performance of the LASSO under a non-linear link function `g` is precisely equivalent to its performance under an effective linear model `y = μAx + σz`. The scaling factor `μ` and effective noise level `σ` are determined solely by moments of the function `g` with respect to a standard Gaussian variable. This work significantly strengthens foundational results by Brillinger (for unregularized least-squares) and more recent non-asymptotic bounds by Plan and Vershynin, by providing the first asymptotically exact characterizations of the mean-squared error for the regularized LASSO in this setting. The analysis extends the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, previously used for precise analysis in linear models, to this more general non-linear case.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its significant theoretical contribution. The equivalence result is both elegant and insightful, explaining why a seemingly naive application of the LASSO to non-linear data is so effective. The derivation of precise, explicit error formulas (Theorems 2.2 and 2.3) represents a major advance over existing upper bounds, providing a far deeper understanding of the interplay between the signal structure, the regularizer, the number of measurements, and the non-linearity. The paper is very clearly written, well-organized, and does an excellent job of situating its contributions within the relevant literature. The application to optimal quantizer design is compelling, demonstrating that the theoretical insights can be used to solve practical engineering problems by simplifying a complex optimization over the LASSO solution to a simple optimization over `σ²/μ²`.

The main limitation is inherent to the analytical approach: the results are asymptotic (`n → ∞`, `m/n → δ`). While the provided experiments show remarkable agreement with the theory even for moderate dimensions, the guarantees are not finite-sample. A minor suggestion for improvement would be to provide more intuition on the scope of "Assumption 1", which is critical for the main theorems, perhaps by discussing scenarios where it might not hold.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Significance:** Establishes a fundamental equivalence between LASSO performance on non-linear and linear models, a result of broad interest.
*   **Originality:** Provides the first asymptotically precise error characterizations for LASSO with non-linear measurements, advancing the state-of-the-art.
*   **Quality:** The work appears technically sound and builds upon a powerful modern analysis framework. Claims are well-supported by theory and validated empirically.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis is purely asymptotic, which is a standard but important caveat.

This is an excellent theoretical paper that resolves a key question in high-dimensional statistics. Its combination of elegance, technical depth, and practical relevance makes it a strong candidate for acceptance.
