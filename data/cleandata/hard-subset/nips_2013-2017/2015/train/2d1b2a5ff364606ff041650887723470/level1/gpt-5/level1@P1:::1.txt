Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies estimation from single-index, non-linear measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0) using the generalized LASSO with an arbitrary convex regularizer: x̂ = argminx ||y − Ax||2 + λ f(x). The main claim is an exact asymptotic equivalence: when A has i.i.d. N(0,1) entries and m,n → ∞ with m/n → δ, the estimation error of generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements is identical to that under a linear model with an effective gain and noise: ylin = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). This generalizes classical results by Brillinger (LS under single-index nonlinearity in fixed n, m → ∞) and non-asymptotic bounds by Plan & Vershynin for a constrained LASSO, by providing asymptotically precise error expressions for the regularized LASSO.

Using the CGMT framework, the authors derive a deterministic scalar optimization (Eq. (10)) whose unique optimizer α* gives the limit of ||x̂ − μ x0||. They instantiate the framework for sparse recovery (ℓ1), group-sparse (ℓ1,2), and briefly for low-rank (nuclear norm), yielding explicit fixed-point conditions (Theorem 2.3) for ℓ1. A noteworthy application is to q-bit quantized compressive sensing: minimizing the LASSO error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 over quantizer thresholds and levels, and the Lloyd–Max (LM) algorithm is shown to produce stationary points for this objective.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: a clean reduction from non-linear to “equivalent” linear measurements captures the role of the nonlinearity entirely via (μ, σ^2). This generalizes and sharpens Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin, and aligns with intuition from single-index models.
- Technical depth: the CGMT-based derivation of a precise error formula for general convex f is nontrivial and timely. The scalar convex program (Eq. (10)) is usable and reveals how δ, λ, g(·), and f(·) jointly determine performance.
- Breadth: treatment covers broad classes of structure (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank), and the ℓ1 specialization yields new precise results beyond previous linear-only analyses.
- Practical insight: the quantizer-design consequence (optimize σ^2/μ^2) provides actionable guidance; connecting LM to stationary points of this criterion is useful.
- Empirical validation: figures show excellent agreement between nonlinear and equivalent linear errors and match the theoretical predictions.

Weaknesses and concerns:
Quality/technical soundness:
- Theorem 2.1 wording suggests that ||x̂ − μx0|| equals ||x̂lin − μx0|| “with probability one” in the limit. As written, it may be read as eventual sample-wise equality rather than equality of the limits of the errors. Please restate precisely as: both errors converge almost surely to the same deterministic limit.
- Assumption 1 (existence of a pointwise limit of the normalized Moreau envelope of f*) is abstract. The paper verifies it for ℓ1 and sketches it for ℓ1,2; for low-rank the needed spectral distribution assumptions are deferred. It would strengthen the work to formalize sufficient conditions for low-rank (e.g., random orthogonal singular vectors with bounded-moment spectrum) and provide a worked-out F(c1,c2,c3).
- Gaussian A is essential for CGMT. The paper does not discuss universality. Many applications (e.g., sub-Gaussian, partial Fourier) would benefit from comments or evidence on robustness.
- The μ ≠ 0 requirement excludes important cases (even g or certain saturations). While the authors acknowledge this and cite alternatives, it would help to delineate precisely which nonlinearities violate μ ≠ 0 and how performance degrades as μ → 0.
- The LM claim: earlier text states “the optimal quantizer is Lloyd–Max,” but later only shows LM yields stationary points of σ^2/μ^2. This is weaker than global optimality; the claim should be toned down or conditions for global optimality provided.

Clarity:
- The role and interpretation of the auxiliary variables (α, β, τ) in Eq. (10) are not explained, making the formula feel opaque. Brief intuition (e.g., β as correlation, τ as effective thresholding parameter) would help readers.
- Typos and minor issues: several misspellings (“mesaurements,” “redifining,” “eventhough”), an odd “μ 6= 03,” and some footnotes interrupt flow. Please proofread.
- Practical guidance on λ: Theorem 2.3 identifies λcrit and regimes, but explicit recommendations for tuning λ (e.g., minimizing (10) over λ or cross-validation proxies) would increase impact.

Originality:
- High. To my knowledge, this is the first asymptotically precise error analysis for generalized LASSO under nonlinear single-index observations across general convex regularizers. The linear-precision extension to group-sparse and nuclear-norm also appears novel.

Significance:
- High. The reduction enables immediate transport of a large body of linear LASSO theory to non-linear sensing. The quantizer-design insight is valuable for 1-bit/q-bit CS and hardware-constrained systems. The results will likely influence both theory and practice of mismatched convex recovery.

Related work:
- Consider citing generalized approximate message passing (GAMP) for nonlinear output channels (e.g., Rangan, ISIT 2011; subsequent rigorous SE analyses). While GAMP targets matched estimators, it offers precise asymptotics for nonlinear measurements and provides a useful contrast to convex LASSO under mismatch.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify Theorem 2.1 convergence statement.
- Provide sufficient conditions guaranteeing Assumption 1 for nuclear-norm and include at least one computed F(c1,c2,c3) example.
- Discuss universality beyond Gaussian A (empirically or conjecturally).
- Refine the LM/quantizer claim and, if possible, add experiments comparing LM-designed quantizers against alternatives under the σ^2/μ^2 criterion.
- Add guidance for λ selection based on (10), and report sensitivity to λ.
- Expand experiments to include other nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU/Tobit) and different sparsity distributions.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Elegant and powerful equivalence result; technically rigorous with CGMT.
- First precise asymptotics for LASSO under nonlinear measurements; broad applicability.
- Clear practical consequence for quantizer design.
- Strong empirical support; advances state-of-the-art understanding.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on Gaussian A; universality not addressed.
- Assumption 1 is abstract; low-rank case not fully worked out.
- Some claims (LM optimality) overstated; clarity could be improved.
- No finite-sample guarantees; entirely asymptotic.

Overall, this is a strong and impactful contribution that merits presentation at NIPS after clarifications and minor tightening.
