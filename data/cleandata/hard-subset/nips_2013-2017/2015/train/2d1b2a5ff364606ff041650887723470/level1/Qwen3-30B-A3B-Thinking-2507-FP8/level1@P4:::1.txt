

This paper investigates the estimation of structured signals from non-linear measurements using the Generalized LASSO. The authors consider measurements of the form $y_i = g_i(a_i^T x_0)$, where $g_i$ are non-linear link functions, and establish that under asymptotic conditions (Gaussian measurement matrix, large $n$ and $m$), the estimation performance of the Generalized LASSO is asymptotically equivalent to that of a linear measurement model with scaled and noisy measurements. The key insight is that the error $\|x̂ - \mu x_0\|^2$ for non-linear measurements matches the error for linear measurements $y_i = \mu(a_i^T x_0) + \sigma z_i$, where $\mu = \mathbb{E}[\gamma g(\gamma)]$ and $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[(g(\gamma) - \mu\gamma)^2]$ for $\gamma \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

The paper builds on the work of Brillinger (1980s) who showed consistency of least squares for non-linear measurements, and Plan and Vershynin (2015) who provided upper bounds for the constrained Generalized LASSO. The authors significantly extend these results by providing precise asymptotic error expressions for the regularized Generalized LASSO. The main result (Theorem 2.1) shows the error equivalence between non-linear and linear measurements. Theorem 2.2 provides an explicit convex program to compute the asymptotic error, which applies to a wide class of regularizers (beyond $\ell_1$-norm) and link functions. The paper applies these results to sparse recovery (Theorem 2.3), group-sparse recovery, and low-rank matrix recovery, and demonstrates a practical application to q-bit compressive sensing where they show the optimal quantizer is the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides precise asymptotic error expressions that are a significant improvement over the upper bounds in Plan and Vershynin (2015). This precise characterization is novel and fills an important gap in the literature.
2. The results are general, applying to a wide range of regularizers (including $\ell_{1,2}$-norm and nuclear norm) and link functions.
3. The application to q-bit compressive sensing is compelling, demonstrating that the optimal quantizer for minimizing LASSO error is the Lloyd-Max quantizer, a well-known result in signal quantization theory.
4. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with thorough numerical validation across multiple settings (Figures 1-2).
5. The authors carefully address limitations (e.g., the unit norm assumption for $x_0$) and provide context for their results relative to prior work.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could better contrast its precise error expressions with Plan and Vershynin's upper bounds, particularly regarding the exact role of $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$.
2. The assumption that $x_0$ has unit norm excludes certain cases (e.g., even link functions), though the authors acknowledge this limitation.
3. The proofs of the main theorems are deferred to the appendix, which makes it difficult to fully assess the technical details without consulting the appendix.
4. The paper could more clearly articulate why precise error expressions are practically important beyond theoretical interest.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical development and good experimental validation. The results extend previous work by providing precise asymptotic error expressions rather than just upper bounds, which is a substantial theoretical advance. The novelty is significant, as precise characterization of the Generalized LASSO error for non-linear measurements was previously unknown.

The significance is high. The results have practical implications for designing measurement systems with non-linearities (e.g., 1-bit quantization), and the demonstration that the Lloyd-Max quantizer is optimal for LASSO-based recovery is valuable for signal processing applications. The precise error expressions could also aid in parameter tuning for practitioners.

The paper is within the scope of NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in high-dimensional statistics with applications to machine learning and signal processing.

Pro: Precise error expressions for Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements (extending Plan and Vershynin's work); Practical application to q-bit compressive sensing showing Lloyd-Max is optimal; Rigorous theoretical development with numerical validation.

Con: Some restrictive assumptions (unit norm signal); Proof details deferred to appendix; Could better contrast with prior work on precise error expressions.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The main strengths are the precise error expressions, the generality of the results, and the compelling application to q-bit compressive sensing. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall significance of the contribution. The paper represents a notable advance in understanding the behavior of the Generalized LASSO under non-linear measurements.
