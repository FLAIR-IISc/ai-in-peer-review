

**Review of "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"**

This paper studies the Generalized LASSO for non-linear measurements, where measurements take the form $y_i = g_i(\mathbf{a}_i^T \mathbf{x}_0)$ with potentially non-linear link functions $g_i$. The authors establish that, under the assumption of i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix entries, the estimation performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to the same algorithm applied to linear measurements of the form $y_i = \mu(\mathbf{a}_i^T \mathbf{x}_0) + \sigma z_i$, where $\mu = \mathbb{E}[\gamma g(\gamma)]$ and $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[(g(\gamma) - \mu\gamma)^2]$ for $\gamma \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. The authors derive precise asymptotic expressions for the estimation error, significantly improving upon previous work by Plan and Vershynin which provided only upper bounds. The paper also demonstrates that the optimal quantizer for minimizing estimation error is the Lloyd-Max quantizer.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors extend the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework to derive precise error predictions, which is a substantial improvement over previous work that provided only upper bounds. The proofs are well-structured and supported by numerical experiments (Figures 1-2) that validate the theoretical predictions. The paper carefully addresses both strengths and limitations of the results, including the necessary assumptions on the measurement matrix and link function. The analysis covers multiple structured signal models (sparse, group-sparse, low-rank) and provides a comprehensive treatment of the problem.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent context for the problem, clearly relating it to previous work by Brillinger (1980s) and Plan and Vershynin (2014). The main results (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) are stated with precision, and the paper includes helpful illustrative examples and figures that clarify the theoretical contributions. The notation is consistent throughout, and the manuscript provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results. The paper effectively distinguishes between the classical results and the novel contributions of this work.

**Originality**: This paper makes a significant contribution to the field. While building on Brillinger's classical work and Plan and Vershynin's recent results, the authors provide a substantial generalization to the high-dimensional setting with precise error predictions. The key insight that the estimation performance of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically equivalent to linear measurements with scaled noise is novel and insightful. The result that the Lloyd-Max quantizer is optimal for minimizing estimation error is particularly noteworthy, as it connects theoretical analysis to practical design. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first paper to derive asymptotically precise results for the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, which represents a significant advance over previous work.

**Significance**: The results have clear practical implications for applications involving non-linear measurements, particularly in quantized signal processing. The precise error predictions provide a deeper understanding of how the Generalized LASSO behaves with non-linear measurements, and the connection to linear measurements simplifies the analysis of non-linear measurement models. The finding about the optimal quantizer has direct relevance to the design of quantization schemes in applications like 1-bit compressive sensing. The paper's results also contribute to a better understanding of the role of the regularizer, the number of measurements, and the regularizer parameter $\lambda$ in the Generalized LASSO.

**Strengths**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis that significantly extends previous work, with precise error predictions rather than just upper bounds. The connection between non-linear and linear measurements is elegant and insightful. The application to q-bit compressive sensing and the demonstration that the Lloyd-Max quantizer is optimal is particularly valuable. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproduction.

**Weaknesses**: The paper relies on the Gaussian measurement matrix assumption, which is common in high-dimensional statistics but limits applicability to other measurement models. The technical assumptions (particularly Assumption 1) may restrict the generality of the framework to specific regularizers (L1, group-L1, nuclear norm), though the authors demonstrate that these hold for common cases. The paper focuses primarily on theoretical results without extensive discussion of practical implementation details for real-world applications.

**Recommendation**: I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements. The precise error predictions are a substantial improvement over previous work, and the connection to linear measurements with scaled noise provides valuable insights. The results have clear significance for practical applications involving non-linear measurements, particularly in quantization design. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a novel contribution that advances the state of the art in high-dimensional statistics and compressed sensing.

**Arguments for acceptance**: 
- Provides precise error predictions for Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements (a major improvement over previous upper bounds)
- Establishes a clear theoretical connection between non-linear and linear measurements
- Demonstrates that Lloyd-Max quantizer is optimal for minimizing estimation error
- Well-structured, rigorous proofs with numerical validation
- Clear significance for practical applications in quantized signal processing

**Arguments against acceptance**: 
- Relies on Gaussian measurement matrix assumption (common in this field)
- Requires technical assumptions that may limit generality to specific regularizers
- Limited discussion of practical implementation details (though this is expected for a theoretical contribution)

This paper represents a substantial advance in the theoretical understanding of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements, with clear implications for both theory and practice. It meets all the NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
