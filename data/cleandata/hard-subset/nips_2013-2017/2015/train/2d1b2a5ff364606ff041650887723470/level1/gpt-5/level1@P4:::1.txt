Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation from non-linear single-index measurements yi = gi(a_i^T x0), where A has i.i.d. Gaussian rows and x0 is structured (e.g., sparse, group-sparse, low-rank). It analyzes the Generalized LASSO with an L2 data-fit and convex regularizer f, used without knowledge of g. The central result (Theorem 2.1) establishes an asymptotic equivalence: for Gaussian A, the estimation error of LASSO with non-linear measurements is the same as that of LASSO with linear measurements ylin = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2] for γ ~ N(0,1). Theorem 2.2 provides a precise characterization of the asymptotic squared error via a three-scalar convex min-max program (10), in terms of δ = m/n, λ, μ, σ and a functional F capturing f and the signal model. The paper further specializes to sparse and group-sparse cases and sketches a path for low-rank recovery, deriving an explicit characterization for the sparse case (Theorem 2.3) that matches prior AMP/CGMT-based precise results in linear settings and extends them beyond ℓ1 with Gaussian nonzeros. As an application, the paper shows that, under this equivalence, designing quantizers to minimize LASSO error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, for which the Lloyd–Max algorithm yields stationary points.

The work extends Brillinger’s classical result (consistency of LS up to scaling under non-linear single-index with fixed n, m→∞) to high-dimensional LASSO with general convex regularizers and derives precise error predictions. It refines upper bounds from Plan and Vershynin (constrained LASSO with sub-Gaussian designs) to asymptotically exact formulas, and leverages the CGMT framework that has yielded precise analyses for LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., Bayati–Montanari, Stojnic, Thrampoulidis–Oymak–Hassibi). The novelty lies in the precise equivalence to a scaled-noisy linear model and the resulting exact error characterization for non-linear measurements.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Theorems are clearly stated with assumptions: Gaussian A, single-index model with i.i.d. copies of g, bounded and nonzero μ, σ, linear asymptotics m/n→δ, and a regularity Assumption 1 enabling a limiting functional F based on Moreau envelopes of the conjugate regularizer.
- The equivalence (Theorem 2.1) is plausible given Gaussian rotational invariance and matches intuitions from prior non-asymptotic bounds. The precise error program (10) follows the CGMT paradigm and reduces to scalar optimization, consistent with prior precise analyses in linear settings.
- The sparse specialization (Theorem 2.3) yields explicit fixed-point equations involving soft-thresholding η, covering non-Gaussian nonzero distributions. This aligns with known results for ℓ2- and ℓ2^2-LASSO in linear models and appears technically correct.
- The quantization application correctly reduces design to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, and the Lloyd–Max connection is well motivated.
- Limitations: the requirement σ^2>0 excludes noiseless linear g(x)=αx (though this should be approachable by continuity); the equivalence critically relies on Gaussian A; Assumption 1 is somewhat abstract and checking it may be nontrivial beyond the provided examples; the low-rank case defers concrete evaluation of F.

Clarity
- Overall well organized: introduction, assumptions, main results, specializations, and application. The roles of μ and σ are emphasized and connected to prior work.
- Notation is heavy but standard for CGMT-based analyses; the definition of F via Moreau envelopes is compact but may be opaque to some readers. The paper mitigates this by working out F explicitly for ℓ1 and ℓ1,2 norms.
- Minor issues: a few typos (e.g., in the abstract the objective has Ax0 instead of Ax; “mesaurements”; inconsistent capitalization for Gaussian). It would help to clarify in Theorem 2.1 that it is equality of asymptotic limits, not equality for finite n; to discuss the σ^2=0 case; and to add guidance on verifying Assumption 1 for low-rank models.

Originality
- The non-linear-to-linear asymptotic equivalence for Generalized LASSO with precise error characterization appears new. Prior works gave non-asymptotic bounds (Plan–Vershynin) or precise analyses only for linear measurements. The extension to general convex regularizers (via F) beyond ℓ1 and to group-sparse is novel; the sparse result beyond Gaussian nonzeros is also new compared to some AMP-based precise results.

Significance
- The results provide actionable, exact predictions of LASSO error under broad non-linear measurement models, guiding choice of λ and sample complexity. The reduction to a scaled linear model has practical implications for system design (e.g., quantization), as illustrated by the Lloyd–Max application. Despite Gaussian A and asymptotic limitations, the insights are likely to influence both theory and practice in compressed sensing and high-dimensional statistics.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, general theoretical contribution: precise asymptotic error for non-linear measurements with LASSO.
- Unifying equivalence principle (non-linear → scaled noisy linear) with clear dependence on μ and σ.
- Coverage of multiple structures and regularizers; explicit sparse-case characterization.
- Practical design consequence for quantization (Lloyd–Max) and guidance for parameter tuning.
- Empirical validations match theory.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on i.i.d. Gaussian design; extension to sub-Gaussian or structured designs is not addressed.
- Assumption 1 is abstract; verification for low-rank models is deferred, making that part incomplete.
- Excludes σ^2=0 and μ=0 cases; practical handling of unknown μ, σ (estimation from data) is not explored.
- Some notational/typographical issues and minor clarity gaps (equality-in-the-limit phrasing).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 2.1 as equality of asymptotic limits and discuss the σ^2=0 case (e.g., by taking limits).
- Provide guidance or sufficient conditions ensuring Assumption 1 for common low-rank models; include a worked example evaluating F numerically.
- Discuss robustness to non-Gaussian sub-Gaussian designs and whether the equivalence might extend or fail.
- Add a brief section on estimating μ and σ from data when g is unknown, and the impact of plug-in estimation on error.
- Correct typos and ensure consistent notation; clarify the use of “Generalized LASSO” vs “square-root LASSO.”

Overall, the paper offers a technically strong, original, and significant contribution with clear implications. The main limitations are reasonable for a first treatment and can be addressed in future work.
