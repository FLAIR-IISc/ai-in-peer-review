### Summary

This paper analyzes the performance of the Generalized LASSO estimator for recovering a structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`. The core contribution is to demonstrate that, in a high-dimensional asymptotic regime where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow large with a fixed ratio `δ = m/n`, the performance of the LASSO is precisely equivalent to its performance on an effective linear model `y_i = μa_i^T x0 + σz_i`. Here, `z_i` are i.i.d. standard Gaussian noise, and the constants `μ` and `σ` depend only on the statistics of the non-linear link function `g`.

The work builds upon and significantly extends two main lines of research. First, it generalizes the classical result of Brillinger, which established a similar equivalence for unregularized least squares in a fixed-dimension (`n`) setting. This paper extends the analysis to the modern high-dimensional, regularized setting where `m` and `n` can be of the same order. Second, it sharpens the recent work of Plan and Vershynin, which provided non-asymptotic upper bounds for a constrained version of the LASSO. In contrast, this paper provides asymptotically exact characterizations of the mean-squared error for the more common regularized LASSO formulation. The analysis leverages the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, which has been recently used to obtain precise performance characterizations for LASSO with linear measurements.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality and Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and rigorous piece of work. The main claims are supported by formal theorems (Theorems 2.1, 2.2, 2.3) whose assumptions are clearly stated. The authors rely on the established CGMT framework but make a non-trivial extension to handle the non-linear measurement model. The theoretical predictions are validated with numerical simulations across various settings (different signal priors, different regularizers), which show an excellent match with the derived formulas, reinforcing the correctness of the asymptotic analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the problem, situates the work within the relevant literature, and precisely summarizes the contributions. The main results are presented in a structured manner, starting with a general theorem and then specializing it to concrete, important examples such as sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery. The modeling assumptions are explicitly listed. The writing makes a complex theoretical result accessible and its implications easy to grasp.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—the asymptotically exact equivalence between the non-linear measurement problem and a corresponding linear one for the Generalized LASSO—is highly original. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to provide precise error characterizations for LASSO-type estimators under such a general non-linear model. While it uses existing analytical tools, their application to this problem is novel and yields significant new insights. The paper also claims that some of the specialized results (e.g., for sparse recovery with non-Gaussian priors under `l2`-LASSO) are new even in the context of linear measurements. The application to quantizer design, which demonstrates that the Lloyd-Max quantizer is optimal for minimizing the final LASSO estimation error, is a novel and practical consequence of the main theorem.

**Significance:** The results are of high significance for both theory and practice.
1.  **Theoretical Impact:** The paper provides a fundamental and unifying insight into the behavior of regularized estimators under model mismatch. The equivalence result simplifies the analysis of a complex non-linear problem into a tractable linear one, which is a powerful conceptual and analytical tool. It advances the state-of-the-art in the precise characterization of high-dimensional estimators.
2.  **Practical Impact:** Non-linearities and quantization are ubiquitous in real-world data acquisition. This work shows that a standard, off-the-shelf algorithm (LASSO) is not only robust to these effects but its performance can be accurately predicted. This allows for principled system design. The specific application to q-bit compressive sensing demonstrates how the theory can be used to optimize system parameters (quantization thresholds and levels) by solving a simple, explicit optimization problem rather than relying on costly end-to-end simulations. Researchers and practitioners are likely to build upon these results.

**Weaknesses:** The work's primary limitations stem from its underlying assumptions, which are standard for this line of theoretical analysis but are worth noting.
1.  **Asymptotic Regime:** The results are strictly valid in the limit where `m, n → ∞`. While simulations suggest the predictions are accurate for moderately large dimensions, the theory provides no finite-sample guarantees.
2.  **Gaussian Design:** The analysis relies critically on the assumption that the measurement matrix `A` has i.i.d. Gaussian entries. Extending these precise characterizations to more general matrix ensembles remains a major open challenge in the field.
3.  **Condition on `μ`:** The analysis requires `μ = E[γg(γ)] ≠ 0`. This excludes certain link functions, such as even functions (e.g., `g(x)=|x|`), where the signal direction information is lost. The paper acknowledges this limitation.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel, fundamental, and significant theoretical result: an asymptotically exact equivalence between LASSO with non-linear measurements and an effective linear model.
*   Provides the first precise error characterizations for the Generalized LASSO in this setting, substantially improving upon prior work which offered only upper bounds or was limited to the unregularized case.
*   The paper is technically strong, exceptionally clear, and the theory is convincingly validated by experiments.
*   The results have direct practical implications, for example, in the principled design of quantized sensing systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis is restricted to an asymptotic regime and assumes an i.i.d. Gaussian measurement matrix, limiting direct applicability where these assumptions do not hold. However, these are standard assumptions in this research area and do not detract from the paper's core contribution to the field's understanding.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It addresses an important problem with a novel and powerful approach, delivering results that are significant for both the machine learning theory and signal processing communities.
