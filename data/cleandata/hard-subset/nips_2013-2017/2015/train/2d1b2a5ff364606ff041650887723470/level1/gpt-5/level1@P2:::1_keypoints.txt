- Studies high-dimensional estimation from nonlinear single-index measurements using generalized LASSO and claims exact asymptotic equivalence to a linear model y_lin = μ A x0 + σ z with μ = E[γ g(γ)], σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2].

- Main contributions: general equivalence theorem (Theorem 2.1), single-letter scalar characterization of asymptotic squared error (Theorem 2.2), and specializations to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery.

- Shows quantizer-design reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 and that Lloyd–Max yields stationary points for end-to-end LASSO error.

- Extends classical Brillinger consistency and Plan–Vershynin nonasymptotic bounds to precise high-dimensional asymptotics with regularization and general convex f via Moreau-envelope.

- Reduction to an equivalent linear model is technically precise and elegant; Assumption 1 is standard and verified for l1 and group l1,2, with a clear recipe for others.

- Derivation of a scalar convex program whose optimizer gives exact asymptotic error aligns with prior CGMT analyses and yields explicit sparse fixed-point equations and a critical λ.

- Quantizer-design corollary is nontrivial and practically useful; empirical simulations reportedly show excellent agreement with theory.

- Limitations: analysis assumes Gaussian design, excludes some links via μ ≠ 0, low-rank section lacks full details, and no finite-sample/nonasymptotic bounds are provided.

- Paper is dense but well organized; main ideas and role of μ,σ are clear; sparse case aids intuition but notation/typos and lack of a main-text proof sketch for Theorem 2.1 reduce accessibility.

- Originality: the equivalence theorem for generalized LASSO under nonlinear measurements and the unifying Moreau-envelope treatment for convex regularizers are novel contributions.

- Scientific impact: provides a simple principle—ignore nonlinearity and characterize performance by two moments—which validates common practice and enables principled design choices.

- Practical relevance: sparse specialization gives actionable fixed-point formulas; quantizer result links estimator error directly to quantizer parameters for practical optimization.

- Breadth: focused on Gaussian A but likely adaptable to rotationally invariant ensembles, suggesting avenues for future work.

- Strengths: clear nonlinear→linear equivalence, precise scalar optimization for asymptotic error, detailed specializations, practical quantizer criterion, and advancement over prior AMP/CGMT literature.

- Weaknesses: Gaussian-design restriction, μ ≠ 0 requirement, incomplete low-rank analysis, absence of finite-sample guarantees, and need for more examples verifying technical assumptions.

- Questions for authors: does equivalence extend to sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant designs (any empirical evidence)?; provide concrete low-rank conditions and a worked example?; can μ and σ be estimated from data for unknown g?; robustness to mild model misspecification?

- Suggestions: include a brief proof sketch of Theorem 2.1 in the main text, a table of F for common f, an experiment with non-Gaussian design, and an expanded low-rank numerical example with validation plots.

- Recommendation: strong accept — technically solid, original, significant, and provides a simple, actionable principle with precise error characterizations across convex regularizers.
