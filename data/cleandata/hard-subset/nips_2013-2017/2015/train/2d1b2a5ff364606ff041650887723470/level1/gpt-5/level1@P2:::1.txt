Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional estimation from nonlinear single-index measurements y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) using the generalized LASSO with a convex regularizer f. Its central claim is an asymptotically exact equivalence: for i.i.d. Gaussian design A, the estimation error of the generalized LASSO with nonlinear outputs is identical to that of the same LASSO applied to an equivalent linear model with scaled signal and additive Gaussian noise, y_lin = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μγ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Building on the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT), the authors provide (i) a general equivalence theorem (Theorem 2.1), (ii) a precise, single-letter characterization of the asymptotic squared error as the solution of a 3-scalar convex optimization (Theorem 2.2), and (iii) specializations to sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery. An application shows that quantizer design for q-bit compressed sensing reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2, implying that Lloyd–Max is locally optimal for the end-to-end LASSO error.

This extends Brillinger’s classical LS result (consistency up to a scale for fixed n, m → ∞) to high-dimensional regularized estimators and extends Plan–Vershynin’s nonasymptotic upper bounds (for constrained LASSO) to asymptotically exact error formulas with regularization. It also subsumes and complements recent precise analyses of LASSO for linear models (AMP-based and CGMT-based works) by incorporating nonlinear links and by covering general convex f via a unifying Moreau-envelope formulation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reduction to a linear equivalent model is precise and elegant. Assumption 1 (existence of a pointwise limit for a scaled Moreau envelope of f* under Gaussian perturbations) is standard and is verified for important cases (ℓ1, group ℓ1,2), with a clear recipe for others. The derivation of a scalar convex program whose optimizer α* gives the exact asymptotic error is consistent with prior CGMT analyses in the linear case, lending credibility. The sparse specialization yields explicit fixed-point equations and identifies a critical λ. The quantizer-design corollary (minimize σ^2/μ^2) is nontrivial and practically useful; showing that Lloyd–Max yields stationary points of the new objective is a nice twist.
- Empirical support: The paper reports simulations (not reproduced here) showing excellent agreement between theory and practice for various links and regularizers. While finite-sample concentration is not theoretically quantified, the CGMT methodology typically concentrates well, and the figures cited suggest robustness.
- Limitations: Gaussian design is assumed throughout; sub-Gaussian or structured designs (e.g., partial Fourier) are not analyzed. The requirement μ ≠ 0 excludes some links (e.g., even functions); the authors note this and discuss workarounds in special cases. The low-rank section outlines how to compute F but defers details; more explicit conditions/examples would strengthen that part. The results are asymptotic in n,m→∞ with m/n→δ; no finite-sample nonasymptotic bounds are provided.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well organized. The main ideas are clearly stated, the role of μ and σ is highlighted, and the general template via F(c1,c2,c3) makes the scope explicit. The sparse case is worked out in detail, aiding intuition. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., occasional typos, footnotes breaking flow), and a short proof sketch for Theorem 2.1 in the main text would help readers unfamiliar with CGMT. More guidance on verifying Assumption 1 beyond the listed examples would also improve accessibility.

Originality
The equivalence theorem for generalized LASSO under nonlinear measurements and the precise asymptotic error characterization appear new. The unifying treatment for multiple convex regularizers and the quantizer-design implication are original contributions that go beyond previous LS-only or upper-bound-only results.

Significance
- Scientific impact: The work provides a sharp and surprisingly simple principle: in high dimensions with Gaussian designs, one can ignore the nonlinear link and run a standard LASSO, with performance captured by two moments of g. This both validates widespread practice (e.g., 1-bit CS, quantized regression) and enables principled design (choose g to minimize σ^2/μ^2).
- Practical relevance: The sparse specialization offers actionable formulas (fixed-point equations) and clarifies the effect of λ, δ, sparsity, and g. The quantizer result connects estimation error directly to quantizer parameters, suggesting practical optimization procedures.
- Breadth: While limited to Gaussian A, the template is likely adaptable to other rotationally invariant ensembles; this could stimulate further work.

Strengths
- Clear, powerful equivalence (nonlinear → linear with μ,σ).
- Precise scalar optimization for asymptotic error; covers general convex f.
- Detailed sparse and group-sparse specializations; new insights on λ regimes.
- Useful application to quantizer design with a concrete criterion (σ^2/μ^2).
- Good alignment with and advancement over prior NIPS literature (AMP/CGMT, Plan–Vershynin).

Weaknesses
- Gaussian-design restriction; no results for sub-Gaussian or structured designs.
- Assumption μ ≠ 0 excludes some links; limited discussion of remedies.
- Low-rank section is less complete; evaluation of F left for future work.
- No finite-sample guarantees or rates of convergence to the asymptotic formula.
- Some technical assumptions (Assumption 1) could use more verification examples and guidance.

Questions for the authors
- Do you conjecture the equivalence (Theorem 2.1) extends to sub-Gaussian or rotationally invariant designs? Any empirical evidence?
- Can you provide concrete conditions for low-rank models (e.g., spiked ensembles) under which F can be computed numerically, and a worked example?
- For unknown g, can μ and σ be estimated from data to enable data-driven design using your theory (e.g., via moment estimation or Stein’s identity)?
- How sensitive are the predictions to mild model misspecification (e.g., mild correlation in rows of A or dependence in g_i)?

Suggestions
- Add a brief proof sketch of Theorem 2.1 in the main text and a table of F for common f.
- Include an experiment with a non-Gaussian design to illustrate robustness or failure modes.
- Expand the low-rank example with a numerical evaluation of F and validation plots.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper is technically solid, original, and significant. It delivers a simple, actionable principle with precise error characterizations across a broad class of convex regularizers, and it meaningfully advances our understanding of high-dimensional estimation under nonlinear measurements.
