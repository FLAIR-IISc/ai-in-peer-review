**Paper Review**

**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper addresses the problem of estimating a high-dimensional structured signal `x0` from non-linear measurements of the form `yi = gi(a_i^T x0)`, where `A` is a random Gaussian matrix and `g` is a non-linear link function. The authors analyze the performance of the standard Generalized LASSO estimator, which naively minimizes `||y - Ax||_2 + lambda * f(x)` despite the model mismatch.

The central and most striking result of the paper is an asymptotic equivalence: in the high-dimensional limit where the number of measurements `m` and signal dimension `n` grow proportionally, the estimation error `||x_hat - mu * x0||_2^2` is shown to be *exactly* the same as the error obtained from an equivalent linear model with measurements `y_lin = mu * A * x0 + sigma * z`, where `z` is standard Gaussian noise. The scaling factor `mu` and effective noise variance `sigma^2` are determined solely by the properties of the link function `g`.

Building on this equivalence, the paper provides a precise, deterministic characterization of the asymptotic mean-squared error (MSE) as the solution to a simple, low-dimensional convex optimization problem. This general formula is then specialized to important cases like sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery. Finally, the authors demonstrate the utility of their theory by applying it to the problem of optimal quantizer design for compressive sensing, showing that minimizing the LASSO estimation error is equivalent to minimizing the ratio `sigma^2 / mu^2`, a problem for which the Lloyd-Max algorithm is effective.

This work connects to and significantly extends several important lines of research. It generalizes a classical result by Brillinger for unregularized least-squares in the fixed-`n` regime to the modern high-dimensional, regularized setting. It also sharpens recent non-asymptotic bounds from Plan and Vershynin by providing asymptotically exact characterizations. Most notably, it extends the powerful Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT) framework, previously used for the precise analysis of LASSO with linear measurements, to this more complex non-linear setting.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The main equivalence result is both elegant and highly significant. It provides a clean, intuitive, and powerful reduction of a complicated non-linear estimation problem to a well-understood linear one. This allows the vast body of recent, precise results on the performance of LASSO in the linear case to be directly transferred to settings with non-linearities, such as quantized, censored, or saturated measurements. This is a fundamental contribution that could have a substantial impact on both theory and practice.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The paper appears to be technically sound, building upon the rigorous CGMT framework. The theoretical claims are stated precisely and are backed by compelling numerical simulations (Figures 1 and 2) that show a near-perfect match between the derived asymptotic formulas and the actual performance of the LASSO in finite dimensions. This provides strong validation for the theory.

3.  **Originality:** While the problem has been studied before, the approach of deriving an asymptotically *exact* characterization for the regularized LASSO is novel. The connection established between the non-linear and an equivalent linear problem is a key original insight that is much stronger than previous upper bounds. The application to optimal quantizer design is also a clever and original consequence of the main theory.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating the work within the existing literature, and clearly summarizing the main contributions. The results are stated clearly in Section 2, and the specializations to different recovery problems help to make the general theory more concrete.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Intuition behind the Equivalence:** The main results (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) are powerful, but the paper provides limited intuition as to *why* such a simple equivalence holds. While proofs are understandably deferred to an appendix, the main body would benefit from a short, high-level paragraph sketching the key mechanism. This likely involves properties of high-dimensional Gaussian projections that effectively linearize the problem, and exposing this intuition would make the result less "magical" and more accessible to a broader NIPS audience.

2.  **Clarity of Assumption 1:** Assumption 1 is critical for the main theorems but is stated in a rather technical manner involving the Moreau envelope of the Fenchel conjugate. While the authors do a good job of verifying it for common regularizers, a more intuitive explanation of what this assumption entails for a generic signal structure and regularizer `f` would be beneficial.

3.  **Limitations:** The analysis relies on the condition `mu != 0`, which excludes certain non-linearities, notably even functions like `g(x) = |x|`. While the authors correctly mention this in a footnote, a slightly more prominent discussion of this limitation in the main text would be helpful for the reader to understand the scope of the results.

4.  **Low-Rank Case:** The treatment of the low-rank matrix recovery case in Section 2.2.4 feels less complete than the sections on sparse and group-sparse recovery. It relies on an additional assumption about the convergence of singular value distributions and defers a detailed treatment. While perhaps necessary due to space, this makes this particular contribution feel somewhat preliminary.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

*   **Pro:** The paper presents a fundamental, elegant, and impactful result that provides a precise understanding of the LASSO with non-linear measurements. The theory is novel, technically strong, and well-verified. It unifies and extends several important areas of research and is likely to be highly influential.
*   **Con:** The technical machinery may be challenging for some readers, and some of the key assumptions and results could be explained more intuitively. The treatment of the low-rank case is not as fully developed as the other examples.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and fundamental contribution to the understanding of high-dimensional regularized estimation. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to presentation and completeness in one specific case. The central result is powerful, insightful, and provides a new tool for analyzing a wide range of problems. I strongly recommend acceptance.
