Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies high-dimensional estimation under the single-index model y_i = g_i(a_i^T x0) with i.i.d. Gaussian sensing rows a_i and a possibly random, unknown nonlinearity g. The proposed estimator ignores the nonlinearity and solves the generalized LASSO min_x ||y − Ax||_2 + λ f(x), where f promotes structure (e.g., sparsity, group sparsity, low rank). The main result shows that, asymptotically, the estimation error of this generalized LASSO under nonlinear measurements is identical to that under a linear model with effective gain and noise: y = μ A x0 + σ z, where μ = E[γ g(γ)] and σ^2 = E[(g(γ) − μ γ)^2], γ ~ N(0,1). Beyond this equivalence, the paper provides precise asymptotic error formulas via a three-parameter convex program (Theorem 2.2) using the Convex Gaussian Min-max Theorem (CGMT), and specializes them to sparse (with general nonzero distributions), group-sparse, and low-rank settings. An application to q-bit quantization argues that minimizing the LASSO estimation error reduces to minimizing σ^2/μ^2 over thresholds/levels, leading to Lloyd–Max quantizers as stationary points.

This extends classical results of Brillinger (nonlinear link, least squares, fixed n, m→∞) and the non-asymptotic analysis of Plan–Vershynin (constrained LASSO with subgaussian A and upper bounds with hidden constants). It also builds on recent precise asymptotics for generalized LASSO with linear measurements (AMP and CGMT based analyses by Bayati–Montanari; Stojnic; Oymak–Thrampoulidis–Hassibi; Thrampoulidis et al.), providing, to my knowledge, the first asymptotically exact characterization for LASSO-type estimators with nonlinear observations.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a clean and surprising equivalence—nonlinear measurements are “seen” by the generalized LASSO as linear with parameters (μ, σ^2) computed from g.
- Technical depth: precise error prediction through a low-dimensional convex program (Theorem 2.2), with careful specialization to sparsity (Theorem 2.3) including non-Gaussian coefficient distributions, and to group sparse and low-rank models.
- Practical implications: reduction of quantizer design for compressed sensing to minimizing σ^2/μ^2; link to Lloyd–Max quantizers is elegant and useful.
- Broad regularizers: framework accommodates convex f beyond ℓ1 (group norms, nuclear norm), at least under Assumption 1.
- Good positioning within literature: relates to Brillinger, Plan–Vershynin, and precise LASSO analyses, clarifying and strengthening their insights.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Gaussianity of A: Unlike Plan–Vershynin, which allows subgaussian designs, the CGMT requires i.i.d. Gaussian. This limits generality; the paper should discuss whether rotational invariance suffices or whether extensions are possible.
- Theorem 2.1 statement: It reads as if the errors are almost surely equal in the limit. Presumably, the two errors converge to the same deterministic limit; please restate precisely as equality of limits, not pathwise equality.
- Assumption 1 (limit of the Moreau envelope of f*): This is central but opaque. The paper gives verifications for ℓ1 and group norms and a sketch for nuclear norm, but a more explicit set of sufficient conditions would help. The low-rank example defers the actual evaluation of F(c1,c2,c3), which weakens that specialization.
- Requirement μ ≠ 0: The analysis excludes some cases (e.g., even g). While sign(.) has μ > 0, other practically relevant nonlinearities may not. Please discuss whether direction recovery is still possible when μ=0 and whether a modified estimator (e.g., correlation maximization) can be handled within your framework.
- Finite-sample behavior: The results are asymptotic; figures illustrate accuracy but lack thorough finite-n diagnostics across g, δ, and λ. Guidance on how large n,m must be for good approximation would enhance practical relevance.
- Choice and tuning of λ: The precise formulas can, in principle, guide λ. The paper would benefit from explicit prescriptions or examples of using Theorem 2.3 (and its λcrit) to tune λ in practice for nonlinear measurements.
- Independence and randomness in g: It is assumed that the g_i are i.i.d. copies. Many applications have a fixed deterministic nonlinearity plus noise. Clarify whether the expectations for μ, σ^2 only use randomness of γ (hence deterministic g is covered), and what role randomness in g plays in the proofs.
- Notational clarity: Overloading of x0/x̄0, the normalization ‖x0‖2=1, and the variant of “square-root LASSO” used could be clarified early. Some footnotes interrupt flow.

Quality
The paper appears technically sound and careful, leveraging the CGMT rigorously. The equivalence and precise error characterizations are well grounded and connect smoothly to known linear results. The quantization application is a compelling demonstration.

Clarity
Generally well written and well organized, with helpful specialization sections. However, Theorem 2.1 phrasing, Assumption 1 exposition, and the low-rank specialization need sharpening. More explicit statements of the conditions under which F exists and examples beyond sketches would improve clarity.

Originality
High. While inspired by Brillinger and Plan–Vershynin, the asymptotically exact equivalence and precise error predictions for generalized LASSO under nonlinear measurements are new and significant.

Significance
High. The work unifies understanding of LASSO under nonlinearities, provides actionable design criteria (e.g., for quantizers), and extends precise-analysis techniques to a broader class of observation models. It is likely to be influential for theory and practice in compressed sensing and high-dimensional statistics.

Specific suggestions
- Restate Theorem 2.1 as equality of deterministic limits and add a brief proof sketch.
- Provide sufficient conditions ensuring Assumption 1 for nuclear norm (or an appendix with a worked-out F for a canonical low-rank model).
- Discuss robustness to non-Gaussian A (e.g., rotationally invariant ensembles) and whether the conclusions persist empirically.
- Add a subsection on λ selection using the fixed-point equations (e.g., solving (14–15) to target minimal error).
- Expand experiments: include multiple nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU+censoring, logistic), vary δ and λ, and report finite-sample gaps to asymptotic predictions.
- In the quantization section, clarify global vs local optimality of Lloyd–Max for the σ^2/μ^2 objective and provide numerical comparisons across q.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, elegant equivalence principle with strong theoretical guarantees.
- First precise asymptotics for generalized LASSO with nonlinear observations.
- Broad applicability across structures and useful design consequences.
- Solid connections to and advancements over prior work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Dependence on Gaussian designs; limited discussion of robustness.
- Assumption 1 is not fully transparent; low-rank specialization incomplete.
- Asymptotic nature with limited finite-sample guidance; some clarity issues.

Overall, this is a strong and impactful contribution; addressing the noted clarifications would further strengthen it.
