- Main contribution: Shows that Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements yi = g(ai^T x0) is asymptotically equivalent (under Gaussian measurements) to LASSO with linear measurements yi = μ(ai^T x0)+σzi, and provides precise asymptotic error formulas.  
- Extension of prior work: Sharpens and extends Brillinger (1981) and Plan & Vershynin (2015) by giving exact error expressions rather than loose upper bounds.  
- Technical quality: Uses rigorous CGMT-based asymptotic analysis with sound proofs (deferred to appendix) and novel precise error formulae (Theorem 2.2).  
- Originality: Key insight that non-linear measurements reduce to a scaled linear model with noise is original and powerful.  
- Significance: Results have immediate practical impact, notably for q-bit quantization, and inform regularization and trade-offs in non-linear measurement settings.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, well-organized, and uses effective figures to illustrate theoretical results and the non-linear→linear connection.  
- Application to structured recovery: Provides precise error expressions for sparse, group-sparse, and low-rank recovery problems.  
- Quantization result: Applies theory to q-bit quantization and shows the Lloyd–Max quantizer is optimal in this framework.  
- Assumptions limitation: Results rely on strong assumptions (Gaussian measurement matrices, unit-norm x0, specific conditions on f), limiting applicability to non-Gaussian or arbitrary-norm settings.  
- Finite-sample concern: Paper does not sufficiently address how asymptotic results translate to finite sample regimes; experiments are limited in scope.  
- Practical-application gap: q-bit quantization section could be strengthened with more concrete real-world quantizer design guidance.  
- Citation/precedence clarity: Unclear whether Plan & Vershynin (2015) was a preprint at submission time; paper should clarify citation/context.  
- Arguments for acceptance: First precise asymptotic analysis for Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements; sharpens prior results; provides unified framework and concrete quantization application.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Strong assumptions restrict scope; limited finite-sample discussion; some technical details hidden in appendix may reduce accessibility.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept — substantial theoretical contribution with significant implications despite the noted limitations.
