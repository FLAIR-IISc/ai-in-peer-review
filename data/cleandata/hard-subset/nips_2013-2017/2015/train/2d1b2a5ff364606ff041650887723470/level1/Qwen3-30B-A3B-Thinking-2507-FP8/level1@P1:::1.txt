

# Peer Review: "LASSO with Non-linear Measurements is Equivalent to One With Linear Measurements"

## Summary
This paper addresses the fundamental question of whether the Generalized LASSO (a standard tool for structured signal recovery from linear measurements) can be effectively applied to non-linear measurement models of the form yi = gi(ai^T x0), where gi are nonlinear link functions. The authors extend the classical work of Brillinger (for unregularized least squares) and Plan and Vershynin (for constrained regularized LASSO) by establishing a precise asymptotic equivalence between the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements and a linear measurement model with scaled and noisy observations. Specifically, they show that when the measurement matrix has i.i.d. Gaussian entries and the signal dimension n grows large, the estimation error of the Generalized LASSO with non-linear measurements is asymptotically identical to that of the same algorithm with linear measurements of the form yi = μai^T x0 + σzi, where μ = E[γg(γ)] and σ² = E[(g(γ) - μγ)²] for γ ~ N(0,1). This work builds on recent advances in precise analysis of the LASSO with linear measurements (e.g., [2, 10, 16, 18, 19, 21]) and extends them to the non-linear setting, which to the best of our knowledge has not been previously analyzed with such precision.

## Strengths
1. **Quality**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical results supported by well-motivated theorems. The main result (Theorem 2.1) establishes a precise asymptotic equivalence between non-linear and linear measurement models, significantly strengthening previous work by Plan and Vershynin [17] which provided only upper bounds. The authors carefully consider the conditions under which their results hold, and the proofs (deferred to the appendix) appear technically sound. The derivation of the precise error formula (Theorem 2.2) is particularly impressive, as it provides an explicit characterization of the estimation error that depends on the structure of x0, the choice of regularizer f, and the properties of the link function g.

2. **Originality**: This work represents a significant theoretical advance in the understanding of the LASSO under non-linear measurements. While Plan and Vershynin [17] showed that the LASSO can be used with non-linear measurements (with a constant of proportionality), this paper provides the first precise asymptotic characterization of the estimation error in this context. The connection to the Lloyd-Max quantizer (Section 2.3) is a particularly elegant application that demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

3. **Significance**: The results have important implications for practitioners using LASSO-type estimators in scenarios with non-linear measurements (e.g., quantized measurements, censored observations). The precise error formula (Theorem 2.2) provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the behavior of the LASSO under non-linear measurements, which could guide practitioners in choosing appropriate regularization parameters and understanding the fundamental limits of estimation in such settings. The connection to optimal quantization (Lloyd-Max) is a particularly valuable contribution to the signal processing community.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization and precise mathematical statements. The authors effectively motivate the problem, build upon previous work, and clearly state their contributions. The technical details are presented in a logical progression, and the paper provides sufficient context for readers familiar with LASSO theory but not necessarily with the specific tools used (e.g., CGMT).

## Weaknesses
1. The paper assumes the measurement matrix has i.i.d. Gaussian entries, which is a strong technical assumption that may limit the applicability of the results to other measurement models (e.g., Fourier or compressed sensing matrices). While this is common in theoretical analysis, the authors might benefit from briefly discussing how their results might extend to other measurement ensembles.

2. The asymptotic regime (m, n → ∞ with m/n = δ) is well-justified for theoretical analysis, but the paper could provide more discussion on how the asymptotic results translate to practical finite-dimensional settings. The numerical results in Figure 1 are helpful, but more comprehensive experiments across different signal structures and link functions would strengthen the practical relevance.

3. The paper does not address the case when μ = 0 (e.g., when g is an even function), which would require a different analysis. While the authors note this limitation in Section 1.1, a more detailed discussion of the implications for specific applications (e.g., 1-bit quantization with g(x) = sign(x)) would be valuable.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of LASSO-type estimators under non-linear measurements. The precise error characterization is novel, well-supported, and has clear practical implications. While the Gaussian measurement assumption is standard in this line of work, the authors could strengthen their case by briefly discussing potential extensions to other measurement models. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and represents a substantial advance over previous work on this topic.

**Recommendation**: Accept. This paper is a strong contribution to the NIPS community, advancing our theoretical understanding of the LASSO in a non-trivial setting with clear practical implications. The results are of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers working on structured signal recovery and compressed sensing.
