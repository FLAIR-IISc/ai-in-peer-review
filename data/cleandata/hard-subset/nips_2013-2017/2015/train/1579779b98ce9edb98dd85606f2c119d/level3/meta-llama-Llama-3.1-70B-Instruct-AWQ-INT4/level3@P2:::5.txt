This paper proposes a novel algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions by optimizing an upper bound on empirical loss, regularized with L2 and optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, which enables the formulation of a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss.

The problem of learning decision trees is framed as a global optimization problem, where the goal is to find a joint configuration of oblique splits and leaf parameters that minimize the empirical loss on the training dataset. The authors show that this problem is related to structured prediction with latent variables and propose a novel formulation of the decision tree learning problem. They introduce a binary latent decision variable for each split node in the tree and use these latent variables to formulate the tree's empirical loss.

The proposed algorithm uses SGD to optimize the upper bound on the empirical loss. The authors also propose a variant of SGD, called Stable SGD (SSGD), which changes the assignment of data points to leaves more conservatively than SGD. The algorithm is evaluated on several benchmark datasets, and the results show that the non-greedy trees achieve better test performance across tree depths and are less susceptible to overfitting.

The paper is well-written, and the problem formulation is elegant. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is a significant contribution, and the proposed algorithm is well-motivated. However, the empirical results are not entirely convincing, as the global optimization only significantly outperforms greedy trees on a few datasets and is more computationally intensive.

One of the major strengths of the paper is its clarity and organization. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, and the paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The writing is clear, and the authors provide sufficient background information for readers who may not be familiar with the topic.

However, there are some minor issues and typos that need to be fixed. For example, the description of Stable SGD is unclear, and the authors need to provide more details on how data points are assigned to leaves and a pseudo-code for the procedure. Additionally, the authors need to clarify Figure 2, stating whether the results use SGD or stable SGD and defining what an "active" leaf means.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution by establishing a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables. The proposed algorithm is also novel and well-motivated. However, the empirical results are not entirely convincing, and the authors need to provide more evidence to support the claim that global optimization outperforms greedy methods.
