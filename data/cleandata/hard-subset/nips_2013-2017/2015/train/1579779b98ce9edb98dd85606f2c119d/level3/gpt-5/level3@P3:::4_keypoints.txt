- Paper reformulates hard oblique decision-tree training as latent structured prediction over internal-node sign vectors and derives a differentiable upper bound for gradient-based optimization.  
- Training proceeds with SGD and loss-augmented inference; exact and fast (Hamming-ball radius-1) algorithms are provided, with O(d^2 p) per-step complexity for depth d and feature dim p.  
- Method jointly updates internal-node weight vectors and leaf parameters, with per-node L2 constraints to tighten the bound and regularize.  
- Empirical results show consistent single-tree accuracy improvements over greedy baselines and OC1-style oblique trees, initialized from a greedy CO2 procedure.  
- Novel connection to prior work: global tree optimization hardness, soft-split models (HME), and latent-structured SVM formulations.  
- Strength: clear and principled reformulation enabling SGD on hard splits.  
- Strength: efficient exact and approximate loss-augmented inference that scales to deeper trees.  
- Strength: insightful analysis of bound dependence on W scale and role of per-node norm constraints; SSGD variant discussed.  
- Strength: Hamming-ball surrogate yields sparse updates (typically changing a single internal node per SGD step), which suggests adaptive capacity control.  
- Strength: empirical single-tree accuracy gains across multiple datasets.  
- Weakness: experiments don’t convincingly beat strong simple heuristics or ensembles; method evaluated only on single trees despite mentions of forests.  
- Weakness: insufficient baseline detail—unclear pruning, hyperparameter tuning, and whether baselines had comparable pruning or lookahead.  
- Weakness: dynamics of sparse single-node updates are not analyzed (do updates add nodes or revise earlier splits?).  
- Weakness: ambiguity at zero weights and tie-breaking (sgn(0) default) is problematic—requires principled handling (allow internal-node outputs, explicit pruning rules).  
- Weakness: interpretability concerns—oblique splits with L2 constraints are dense; L1/group-lasso regularization should be explored and compared.  
- Weakness: inference procedures (Sections 6.2/6.4) need clearer pseudocode, explicit f(g) definition, and diagrams of Hamming-ball node changes.  
- Weakness: fast surrogate is looser—need empirical quantification of gap between full and fast surrogates and ablations of accuracy vs speed.  
- Weakness: sensitivity to initialization (greedy vs random vs zero) and inactive-leaf dynamics are not evaluated; results rely on greedy CO2 init.  
- Quality: technically sound with correct reformulation, valid upper bounds, and complexity analysis, but empirical evaluation lacks key baselines and ablations.  
- Clarity: generally well written but algorithmic core and zero-weight/pruning behaviors need explicit statements.  
- Originality: high—novel casting of hard-split tree learning as latent structured prediction with efficient loss-augmented inference.  
- Significance: promising for global tree optimization, but broader impact requires stronger empirical validation, interpretability-focused regularization, and forest experiments.  
- Requested: define behavior for wi = 0 and ties; consider internal-node outputs/pruning formalization.  
- Requested ablations: full vs fast inference, effect of regularization parameter ν, SSGD vs SGD, and initialization variants.  
- Requested experiments: L1 or elastic-net per-node regularization to trade sparsity vs accuracy; stronger baselines (multiple greedy trees, lookahead, cost-complexity pruning).  
- Recommendation: borderline accept if authors address clarifications and add stronger baselines/ablations; otherwise borderline reject.  
- Post-rebuttal note: zero-weight initialization remains critical—analyze zero-to-nonzero growth and whether method tends to add nodes versus revise existing ones.
