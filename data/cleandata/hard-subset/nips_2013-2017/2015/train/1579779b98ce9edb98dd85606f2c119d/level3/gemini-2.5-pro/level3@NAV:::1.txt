This paper presents a novel framework for the non-greedy, global optimization of decision trees with a fixed structure. The core contribution is to reframe the problem of finding optimal oblique splits as a structured prediction task with latent variables. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective that can be optimized efficiently using stochastic gradient descent. This work provides an elegant and more scalable alternative to classic greedy induction algorithms like CART and ID3, which optimize one node at a time, and previous attempts at global optimization that were often computationally intractable.

The paper’s strengths are significant. The formulation is elegant, technically sound, and clearly written, making a difficult problem accessible. Linking decision tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables is a highly original and insightful contribution that is likely to spur further research in this area. The development of a "fast loss-augmented inference" procedure to make the optimization practical for deeper trees is a valuable technical achievement. The work is a complete and high-quality piece of research that advances our understanding of how decision trees can be trained.

However, the primary weakness lies in the experimental results. While the proposed method consistently outperforms greedy baselines, the margin of improvement is somewhat modest across the benchmark datasets. This raises questions about the practical value of the additional complexity compared to simpler, well-established methods. A more critical point is the need for a deeper analysis of overfitting. The global optimization could potentially find complex decision boundaries that fit the training data too closely, and a more rigorous evaluation is needed to confirm that the proposed regularization scheme effectively prevents this.

To potentially improve performance, the authors could consider a hybrid approach. After performing the global optimization, a final local refinement step could be applied, such as dropping and re-optimizing the last split and its corresponding leaf nodes using a standard greedy criterion. This might capture useful local structure that the global objective overlooks.

In conclusion, despite the underwhelming experimental gains, the novelty and elegance of the formulation make this a strong contribution that merits publication. The paper tackles a fundamental, challenging problem in machine learning with a principled and well-executed approach that will likely serve as a foundation for future work.
