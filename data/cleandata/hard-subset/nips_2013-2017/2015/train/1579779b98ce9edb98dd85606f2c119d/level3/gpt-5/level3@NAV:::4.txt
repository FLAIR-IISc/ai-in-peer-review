This paper proposes a global optimization framework for decision trees by casting tree induction as latent structured prediction: sign vectors of internal nodes act as latent variables that select leaves, and the empirical loss is upper-bounded by a differentiable (though non-convex) surrogate optimized by SGD. A key algorithmic component is solving successive loss-augmented inference problems; the authors provide an exact O(mp) routine and a fast O(d^2p) variant using a Hamming-ball (radius-1) restriction. The work connects to latent SSVMs and structured prediction (e.g., Yu & Joachims), and contrasts with OC1, CO2, HME, and global tree optimization (Bennett). Experiments on several multiclass datasets show accuracy gains over greedy baselines.

Quality: The reformulation and the bounding argument are sound and clearly derived; the fast loss-augmented inference is elegant and leads to efficient SGD updates. An intriguing property is the sparsity of gradient steps under the radius-1 bound—each update appears to touch only one node’s weight—beyond mere computational savings. However, practical concerns remain: (i) behavior under zero-weight initialization is under-specified (the implied “take right branch” default is brittle); (ii) interpretability is at odds with L2-regularized oblique splits likely yielding dense, non-interpretable features; and (iii) experimental baselines and pruning strategies for greedy methods are insufficiently detailed, limiting conclusions about real-world usefulness.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written, but the descriptions of inference and loss-augmented inference (Sections 6.2/6.4) could be summarized more explicitly with a compact pseudo-code and a small running example to clarify path-bit updates and why only one node changes under the Hamming-ball bound. The title mentions forests, yet forests are neither developed nor evaluated.

Originality: Casting global DT optimization as latent structured prediction with efficient loss-augmented inference is novel and interesting, especially the sparse, per-step update property suggesting a pathway to adaptively growing an infinitely deep tree from zero weights.

Significance: The approach is promising and could stimulate follow-up work (e.g., adaptive-depth growth, alternative regularizers), but current experiments do not yet establish clear practical gains across realistic baselines.

Suggestions:
- Specify zero-weight node semantics; allow node outputs and define principled pruning/splitting.
- Evaluate L1 or elastic-net regularization to balance sparsity and accuracy, and report interpretability metrics.
- Strengthen baselines: detail pruning, and add simple global strategies (e.g., multiple greedy trees on random subsets).
- Either address forests experimentally or adjust the title.
- Analyze whether the method prefers adding new nodes versus revising existing ones.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clever, principled reformulation; efficient algorithms; intriguing sparse updates.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Ambiguities in edge cases; interpretability concerns; limited and underspecified experimental baselines; title–content mismatch.
