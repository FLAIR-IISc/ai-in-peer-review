

This paper presents a novel non-greedy approach for optimizing decision trees by formulating the global optimization problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a key connection between decision tree learning and latent structured prediction, deriving a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient stochastic gradient descent (SGD) optimization. The method achieves O(d²p) computational complexity per gradient step (where d is tree depth and p is feature dimension), a significant improvement over the naive O(2^dp) complexity. This work builds on prior non-greedy tree optimization attempts (Bennett, 2001; Murthy & Salzburg, 1994) while addressing their limitations: Bennett's method was restricted to binary classification with high complexity, while soft-split approaches like Hierarchical Mixture of Experts sacrificed computational efficiency. The paper's formulation extends to general loss functions and regularization, providing a principled way to control overfitting.

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The theoretical derivation of the upper bound (Equation 7) is rigorous, with a clear proof sketch and supporting propositions. The algorithmic development (Section 6) is particularly strong, with the fast loss-augmented inference technique (Equation 13) elegantly solving the computational bottleneck. The writing is exceptionally clear, with well-structured sections that guide the reader through the problem formulation, theoretical insights, and algorithmic implementation. The experiments on SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST datasets demonstrate consistent, though modest, improvements over greedy baselines (including OC1, axis-aligned trees, and CO2). The method shows better generalization (less overfitting) as tree depth increases, with the regularization parameter ν implicitly controlling pruning.

Regarding originality, the core insight—linking decision trees to structured prediction with latent variables—is genuinely novel. While some connections exist between decision trees and structured prediction (e.g., Yu & Joachims, 2009), the authors' specific formulation for global tree optimization with efficient inference represents a significant advancement over previous work. The paper properly contextualizes this contribution against NIPS proceedings (e.g., CO2 forest [Norouzi et al., 2015] is cited as a greedy variant).

The significance of this work is moderate. While the method enables training deep trees efficiently, the empirical gains over greedy approaches are marginal (typically 0.5-2% accuracy improvement). The paper's most compelling contribution is the elegant theoretical framework, which will likely inspire future work on non-greedy tree methods. However, the practical impact is limited by the modest performance gains and lack of clear advantage over well-established greedy methods in real-world applications. The authors' suggestion that "non-greedy trees achieve the best test performance across tree depths" (Fig. 2) is accurate but understates the small magnitude of improvements.

Key weaknesses include:
1. Experimental results are underwhelming: The 1-2% accuracy gains on standard benchmarks (especially MNIST) don't strongly justify the method's complexity. The comparison with CO2 (a greedy variant) is insightful but doesn't demonstrate transformative gains.
2. Overfitting analysis is insufficient: While the paper notes that regularization helps generalization, it doesn't provide concrete evidence (e.g., validation curves, learning curves) showing the non-greedy method avoids overfitting better than greedy alternatives.
3. The "non-greedy" advantage is not fully exploited: The suggestion to drop and re-optimize the last split/leaf node (as noted in key points) could yield meaningful improvements but isn't explored. This is a missed opportunity to demonstrate practical value.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a meaningful problem. However, the modest empirical gains and lack of compelling evidence for significant practical advantage limit its impact. The authors should strengthen the evaluation by:
- Adding experiments on larger datasets to demonstrate scalability
- Providing more detailed analysis of overfitting behavior
- Testing the suggested post-optimization refinement (dropping last split)

Pro: Elegant formulation linking decision trees to structured prediction; clear theoretical development; efficient algorithm with solid complexity analysis; well-written and well-organized.
Con: Marginal empirical gains over strong greedy baselines; insufficient overfitting analysis; practical significance limited by small performance improvements.

This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theory of decision tree learning. While the experimental results are somewhat disappointing, the method's elegance and theoretical grounding warrant publication. The authors should address the overfitting analysis and explore the suggested post-optimization step in future work. The paper will likely stimulate further research into non-greedy tree methods, making it a valuable addition to NIPS 2015. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the experimental evaluation.
