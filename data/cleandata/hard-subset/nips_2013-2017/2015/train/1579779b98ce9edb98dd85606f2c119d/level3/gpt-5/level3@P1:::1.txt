This paper proposes a non-greedy training method for oblique decision trees that jointly optimizes all split functions and leaf parameters under a global objective. The key idea is to recast tree routing decisions as latent binary variables and to upper-bound the empirical loss with a convex–concave surrogate inspired by structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive two bounds: a full loss-augmented inference objective and a fast variant that restricts to a Hamming ball around the current routing, enabling per-example optimization in O(d^2 p), where d is the depth and p is the input dimension. Training proceeds via (projected) stochastic gradient descent with row-wise norm constraints on W, and a “Stable SGD” variant that stabilizes leaf assignments. Empirically, on several LibSVM multiclass datasets the non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) and the greedy CO2 variant.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects decision tree learning to structured prediction (Taskar et al.; Tsochantaridis et al.; Yu & Joachims) through a latent-variable view of routing decisions and loss-augmented inference. It improves over earlier global or margin-based tree methods (Bennett; Bennett & Blue; Bennett et al.) by scaling to deeper trees and multi-class losses, and contrasts with probabilistic soft-split alternatives (Jordan & Jacobs) that sacrifice tree-time inference cost. It is complementary to online/streaming forests (Mondrian forests) and to standard greedy CART/ID3 and OC1 oblique splits. The CO2 work (Norouzi et al.) is a special case of the bound applied greedily; this paper’s joint optimization is the main conceptual step forward.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: a clean latent-variable formulation with a practical upper bound and principled regularization via per-node norm constraints.
- Algorithmic contribution: efficient loss-augmented inference restricted to a Hamming-1 neighborhood yields O(d^2 p) per-example complexity, making deep trees feasible.
- Practicality: SGD with sparse updates and projection; SSGD to avoid inactive leaves; initialization strategy via standard greedy trees is sensible.
- Empirical evidence: consistent improvements over several greedy baselines across tree depths; analysis of runtime for the fast bound.

Weaknesses:
- Experimental scope and gains: improvements, while consistent, are modest on the chosen datasets, and stronger modern baselines (e.g., boosted trees, extremely randomized trees) are not included.
- Fixed structure and initialization dependence: the method assumes a fixed full binary structure and relies on greedy initialization; sensitivity to initialization is not analyzed.
- Overfitting analysis is limited: the claim of reduced overfitting is plausible via norm constraints, but evidence is primarily qualitative; no calibration or generalization analysis (e.g., bounds or stability) is provided.
- Surrogate tightness trade-offs: the fast (Hamming-1) bound improves scalability but may limit global coordination across distant parts of the tree; an ablation quantifying the effect on accuracy is brief.

Quality:
- The technical development is sound, with a correct latent-variable reformulation, derivation of the upper bounds, and a clear projected SGD procedure. The justification for row-wise norm constraints is well-motivated and aligns with margin intuition. One missing piece is a deeper analysis of surrogate tightness vs. optimization dynamics (full vs. Hamming-1) and how ν mediates that trade-off beyond empirical tuning.

Clarity:
- The paper is elegant and clearly written. The navigation function f and the loss-augmented inference are explained well, and the computational complexity is transparent. Minor suggestions: provide a small running example of g/h along a depth-3 tree, and summarize SSGD in pseudo-code for completeness.

Originality:
- The latent-structured view of tree routing with a convex–concave upper bound and efficient Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference is novel in the context of decision trees. It goes beyond prior margin-at-node and greedy upper-bound work (including CO2).

Significance:
- Decision trees remain widely used; a scalable, non-greedy training procedure is impactful. Even if single-tree gains are moderate, the formulation could spur follow-on work (e.g., sparse W, kernels, end-to-end learned structures, ensembles). The method is likely to be adopted by researchers and may influence practical training for oblique trees.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Strengthen baselines: include gradient boosting (e.g., oblique boosted trees if available) and extremely randomized trees; report ensembles to show single-tree vs. ensemble effects.
- Overfitting evaluation: report train/val/test curves across ν and depth; plot number of active leaves vs. accuracy; assess calibration (e.g., ECE) to understand the effect on leaf distributions.
- Ablations: quantify the accuracy gap between full and Hamming-1 loss-augmented inference; report the impact of SSGD vs. SGD; sensitivity to initialization (axis-aligned vs. random).
- Practical post-processing: after global optimization, drop the last split (deepest level), collapse the corresponding leaves, and re-optimize that local split and leaves via standard greedy/maximum gain. This can recover local refinements the global surrogate might miss and may boost test accuracy.
- Report wall-clock and memory for deeper trees and larger datasets; clarify how Θ is regularized (or add label-smoothing/priors).
- Discuss learning the tree structure (e.g., grow/prune during optimization) as future work.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled, and efficient non-greedy training framework for decision trees.
- Solid connections to structured prediction with latent variables.
- Demonstrated improvements over relevant greedy baselines.
- Likely to stimulate further research and practical variants.

Cons against acceptance:
- Empirical gains are not overwhelming and lack comparison to the strongest modern tree baselines.
- Limited analysis of overfitting/generalization beyond ν tuning.
- Dependence on fixed structure and initialization, with little sensitivity analysis.

Overall assessment: A well-executed, clearly presented contribution with a neat theoretical–algorithmic bridge and practical training improvements. Despite somewhat modest empirical gains, the ideas are likely to be influential. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening the empirical section and the overfitting analysis.
