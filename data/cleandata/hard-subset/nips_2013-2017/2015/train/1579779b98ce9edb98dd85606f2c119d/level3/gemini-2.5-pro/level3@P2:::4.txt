This paper presents a novel method for the non-greedy, global optimization of decision trees. The core contribution is to reframe the problem of learning optimal split functions as a latent variable structured prediction problem. The authors associate a binary latent vector with the sequence of split decisions for a given input, which determines the path to a leaf node. The original, discontinuous empirical loss function is intractable to optimize directly. To address this, the authors derive a continuous, piecewise-smooth (convex-concave) upper bound on the loss, inspired by latent structured SVMs. This surrogate objective is then optimized using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). A key technical result is the development of an efficient algorithm for the loss-augmented inference step required by SGD, reducing its complexity from exponential to quadratic in the tree depth, which makes the approach practical for deep trees. This work connects decision tree induction with the well-established framework of structured prediction, moving beyond the standard greedy, node-by-node training procedures common in the literature (e.g., CART, ID3).

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of global decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is highly novel and elegant. It provides a principled optimization framework for a classic and notoriously difficult problem that is known to be NP-complete. This connection opens up several promising avenues for future work, such as applying the kernel trick or exploring different forms of regularization, as the authors note in their conclusion.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the surrogate objective and, critically, the development of the "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm are solid contributions. Reducing the complexity of the gradient update step from exponential (`O(2^d*p)`) to polynomial (`O(d^2*p)`) is what makes this theoretical framework a viable algorithm.

3.  **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate that the proposed non-greedy trees consistently outperform several greedy baselines, including axis-aligned and oblique trees (OC1), across multiple datasets. The method also appears less prone to overfitting, likely due to the explicit norm regularization on the split parameters, which provides a principled way to control model complexity.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened.
    *   The paper lacks sufficient detail on the baseline implementations. For greedy methods, it is standard practice to apply post-pruning to control complexity and prevent overfitting. It is unclear if the baselines were properly pruned. The regularization in the proposed method acts as a form of complexity control, and a fair comparison requires that baselines also have their complexity appropriately controlled.
    *   The comparison could benefit from including other simple, global optimization strategies as baselines. For instance, training multiple greedy trees with different random initializations or on random data subsets and selecting the best one on a validation set could serve as a stronger and more realistic baseline.

2.  **Practical Implications and Discussion:** The paper misses a discussion on several important practical aspects.
    *   One of the primary reasons for using decision trees is their interpretability. The L2 regularization used in the proposed method is likely to produce dense, oblique splits (i.e., linear combinations of all features), rendering the resulting tree difficult to interpret. The paper should discuss this trade-off. An experiment with L1 regularization to encourage sparse, more interpretable splits would have been a valuable addition.
    *   The title, "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees," is appropriate, but the abstract mentions "randomized forests," and the introduction cites forest-based methods. However, the paper never evaluates or extends the proposed method to forests. This is misleading and should be corrected.

3.  **Clarity and Unexplored Theoretical Aspects:**
    *   The description of the inference algorithms in Section 6 could be made clearer. While the core idea is present, a more explicit summary or pseudocode would improve readability and reproducibility.
    *   The paper does not clearly specify the behavior of a split node when its weight vector is zero. This is a critical edge case. A random initialization might prevent this from happening in practice, but the possibility of starting from zero weights and adaptively "growing" them is an interesting theoretical direction that this ambiguity prevents from being explored.
    *   The SGD update has a sparse structure (only `g_hat` and `h_hat` contribute to the gradient of `W`). This implies that at each step, very few rows of `W` (potentially only one for the fast inference variant) are updated. This is a powerful property beyond mere computational efficiency and suggests a connection to greedy methods that revise one node at a time. This connection is not explored or discussed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper introduces a clever and technically impressive method for globally optimizing decision trees by reformulating the problem within the latent structured prediction framework. The development of an efficient optimization algorithm is a significant achievement. However, the practical usefulness of the approach is not yet fully convincing due to weaknesses in the experimental validation and a lack of discussion on key practical issues like interpretability. While the core idea is excellent, the paper would be much stronger with a more thorough experimental comparison and a deeper discussion of the properties and trade-offs of the proposed method.
