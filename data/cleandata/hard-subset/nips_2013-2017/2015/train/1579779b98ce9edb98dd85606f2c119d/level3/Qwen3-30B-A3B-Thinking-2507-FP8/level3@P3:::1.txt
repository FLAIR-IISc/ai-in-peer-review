

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper presents a novel approach to decision tree learning by formulating global optimization as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The key insight is that the decision tree navigation can be viewed as a latent variable problem, where the optimal path through the tree is treated as a latent variable. The authors then derive an efficient algorithm with O(d²p) complexity per gradient update (where d is tree depth and p is feature dimension), making deep tree learning feasible. This represents a significant improvement over previous non-greedy approaches that had exponential complexity.

The work builds on prior efforts in non-greedy tree learning (Bennett [1]) and structured prediction (Yu et al. [25]), but makes important contributions in both theoretical formulation and computational efficiency. Unlike soft-split approaches (Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13]), the method preserves the computational advantages of decision trees while enabling global optimization. The paper also distinguishes itself from the recent CO2 method [18] by optimizing the entire tree structure jointly rather than greedily adding nodes.

**Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making a complex technical contribution accessible. The theoretical foundation is sound, with well-justified approximations and complexity analysis. The algorithmic implementation is elegant, with the fast loss-augmented inference technique being particularly clever. The authors thoroughly address the limitations of their approach, including the non-convex nature of the optimization and the trade-offs in regularization.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the experimental results are somewhat disappointing. The paper demonstrates modest improvements over greedy baselines (typically 1-3% accuracy gains), and the gains are not consistent across datasets. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of the overfitting behavior of the non-greedy trees, especially since the authors acknowledge the importance of regularization in preventing overfitting. The paper mentions that the method may produce trees that are less susceptible to overfitting, but doesn't provide a rigorous comparison of overfitting metrics (e.g., training vs. test error gaps) across different methods.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The experimental design is appropriate for the problem, but the results don't demonstrate a dramatic improvement over existing methods. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, including the modest empirical gains.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear, with well-structured arguments and precise mathematical formulations. The algorithm descriptions are particularly well-explained, making the approach reproducible for experts.

**Originality:** The connection between decision trees and structured prediction with latent variables is novel, as is the efficient optimization technique. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly the CO2 method [18] and Bennett's earlier work [1].

**Significance:** While the method is technically interesting and addresses a genuine limitation of standard decision tree learning, the practical significance is somewhat limited by the modest empirical gains. The approach could inspire further research on global optimization of tree-based models, particularly for deep tree structures, but the immediate impact on practice may be limited.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Elegant theoretical framework connecting decision trees to structured prediction
- Novel and efficient optimization algorithm with O(d²p) complexity
- Clear writing and well-organized presentation
- Addresses a fundamental limitation of standard decision tree learning
- Potential to spur further research on global optimization of tree structures

*Con:*
- Experimental results show only modest improvements over greedy baselines
- Insufficient analysis of overfitting behavior despite the authors' acknowledgment of its importance
- The method requires careful tuning of the regularization parameter ν, which may be a practical barrier
- The approach is limited to fixed tree structures, not addressing the important problem of optimal tree size

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and represents a significant theoretical contribution to decision tree learning. While the experimental results are somewhat underwhelming, the method's elegance and potential to inspire future research justify its publication. I recommend acceptance, with the following suggestion: After global optimization, the authors could consider dropping and re-optimizing the last split and its leaf node using standard methods to potentially improve results. This would address the observation that the method may not fully exploit the tree's capacity, as noted in the paper's discussion of inactive leaves.

This work merits publication at NIPS as it makes a clear theoretical contribution with potential to influence future research on tree-based models, even if the immediate empirical gains are modest. The authors have successfully demonstrated a principled approach to non-greedy tree learning that is both theoretically grounded and computationally feasible.
