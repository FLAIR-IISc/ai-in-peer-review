- Proposes jointly optimizing split functions and leaf parameters for decision trees, moving beyond greedy induction.
- Establishes a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables.
- Derives a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss enabling stochastic gradient descent training.
- Core idea is original and addresses a long-standing challenge in decision-tree learning.
- Paper is well-written with clear motivation and a good relation to prior work (Breiman, Quinlan, Taskar, Yu & Joachims, CO2).
- Theoretical justification for the proposed upper bound is unconvincing; the argument around line 216f fails on a simple counterexample.
- Proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement is incomplete and lacks essential details.
- Experimental results show improvements over greedy baselines but the evaluation is incomplete.
- Missing a crucial baseline: greedy training of non-axis-aligned (oblique) splits without special initialization.
- Pre-specifying tree depth is a limitation; experiments should vary depth and analyze its impact.
- Some leaves receive no data points, indicating potential issues with the objective or initialization.
- The Stable SGD (SSGD) heuristic seems ad-hoc and would benefit from stronger theoretical motivation (e.g., EM or CCCP connections).
- The benefit of the efficient loss-augmented inference is questionable for depths ≤16; brute-force may be feasible and Hamming-ball radius=1 restriction needs justification.
- Terminology is unclear: clarify what "leaves" refers to in the inference discussion (internal vs. terminal nodes).
- Minor stylistic suggestion: use ‘max’ instead of ‘argmax’ in the loss bound expression for consistency.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental limitation, shows promising empirical gains, and provides a significant structured-prediction connection.
- Arguments for rejection: shaky theoretical foundations for the upper bound, incomplete experimental baselines, and unclear efficiency claims.
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution but hampered by theoretical flaws and incomplete experiments; reviewer leans toward reject but may reconsider if authors fix theoretical issues and add experiments (including runtime comparison to brute-force for small depths).
