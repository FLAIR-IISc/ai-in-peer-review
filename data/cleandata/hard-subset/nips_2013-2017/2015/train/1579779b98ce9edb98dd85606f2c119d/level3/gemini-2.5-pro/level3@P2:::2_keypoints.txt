- Proposes a novel, principled framework for non-greedy decision tree training by jointly optimizing oblique splits and leaf parameters via a structured prediction surrogate loss.  
- Establishes a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss and uses SGD to optimize it, connecting tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables.  
- Key technical contribution: an algorithm reducing loss-augmented inference from exponential to quadratic in tree depth, enabling training of deeper trees.  
- Empirical results show improvements over greedy axis-aligned trees and the CO2 oblique-tree baseline on multi-class benchmarks.  
- Strength: tackles an important, long-standing problem with an elegant, original formulation and clear writing.  
- Strength: the latent-binary-variable formulation for routing is insightful and supports the surrogate derivation.  
- Strength: method has potential impact if technically sound and robust, enabling more compact, optimal trees.  
- Major concern: validity of the central upper bound (Eq. 7) is unclear and the provided proof (Prop. 1) appears incomplete, undermining theoretical soundness.  
- Major concern: optimization instability (inactive leaf nodes) and the proposed Stable SGD heuristic seem ad-hoc and lack principled justification; alternatives like CCCP or EM-style methods were not considered.  
- Major concern: the fast loss-augmented inference (Hamming-ball radius 1) lacks analysis of its accuracy trade-off and necessity; experiments do not show its impact on much deeper trees where brute-force is intractable.  
- Experimental issues: missing a standard greedy oblique-split baseline (not initialized from axis-aligned trees), making it hard to isolate benefits of global optimization.  
- Experimental issue: requirement to pre-specify tree depth is a practical limitation compared to greedy methods that grow trees dynamically; sensitivity to depth is not analyzed.  
- Minor suggestions: clarify phrasing before Eq. (7) to refer to the value of the maximization, and define terms like “off-the-path bits” more precisely.  
- Summary judgment: paper is highly original with promising results but has serious concerns about the theoretical bound, ad-hoc optimization fixes, and incomplete experimental validation.  
- Reviewer stance: leaning toward rejection but willing to reconsider if the authors provide rigorous proofs for the bound and stronger empirical justification for methodological choices.
