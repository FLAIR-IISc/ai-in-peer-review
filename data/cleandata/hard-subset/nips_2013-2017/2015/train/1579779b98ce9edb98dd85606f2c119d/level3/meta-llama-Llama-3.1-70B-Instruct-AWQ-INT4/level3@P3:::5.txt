This paper proposes a novel algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions by optimizing an upper bound on empirical loss, regularized with L2 and optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, which enables the formulation of a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. This bound is optimized using SGD to find a locally optimal configuration of the split functions.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is elegant and provides a new perspective on decision tree learning. The use of SGD for optimization enables effective training with large datasets.

However, the empirical results are not entirely convincing. The authors compare their non-greedy decision tree algorithm with several greedy baselines, including conventional axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, and random oblique trees. While the non-greedy trees achieve better test performance across tree depths on multiple datasets, the improvement is not significant on all datasets. Furthermore, the computational complexity of the non-greedy algorithm is higher than that of the greedy baselines.

The authors also propose a variant of SGD, called Stable SGD (SSGD), which changes the assignment of data points to leaves more conservatively than SGD. However, the description of SSGD is unclear, and it would be helpful to provide more details on how data points are assigned to leaves and a pseudo-code for the procedure.

The paper could also benefit from more detailed explanations of certain aspects of the algorithm. For example, the authors mention that the number of latent decision variables grows exponentially with the tree depth d, but they do not provide a clear explanation of how this affects the computational complexity of the algorithm. Additionally, the authors mention that the norm of W is regularized, but they do not provide a clear explanation of why this is necessary or how it affects the performance of the algorithm.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, some of the notation and terminology could be clarified. For example, the authors use the term "active" leaf, but they do not provide a clear definition of what this means. Additionally, the authors mention that the loss term on the LHS of equation (7) is invariant to the scale of W, but they do not provide a clear explanation of why this is the case.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is also novel and provides a new perspective on decision tree learning. However, the use of SGD for optimization is not new, and the authors could provide more context on how their approach differs from previous work in this area.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in machine learning, namely the optimization of decision trees. The authors provide a clear explanation of why greedy algorithms are limited and propose a novel approach that addresses these limitations. However, the empirical results are not entirely convincing, and the authors could provide more context on how their approach could be used in practice.
