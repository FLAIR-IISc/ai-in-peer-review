

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by jointly optimizing all tree parameters (split functions and leaf posteriors) through a global objective function. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. This bound enables efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent with complexity O(d²p) for depth d and dimension p. The key novelty is the joint optimization framework, which they claim outperforms greedy approaches.

The paper's main strengths include a clear connection to structured prediction (a well-established framework at NIPS), a technically sound derivation of the upper bound (with a clean optimization procedure), and the demonstration that non-greedy trees achieve better generalization than greedy baselines across multiple datasets. The problem of joint optimization of decision trees is indeed important and has been largely unexplored in the literature beyond some limited prior work (e.g., Bennett [1]). The authors make a valuable contribution by providing a tractable optimization framework for this challenging problem.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that affect its quality and significance. First, there is a potential flaw in the theoretical foundation: the claimed upper bound (Equation 7) appears to be incorrect based on the authors' own example (l.216f). The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement is incomplete, which undermines the theoretical guarantees of the method. This is a serious concern as the entire approach rests on the validity of this bound.

Second, the paper suffers from weak experimental evaluation. The authors claim their method outperforms greedy baselines, but they fail to compare with a critical baseline: greedy training of non-axis-aligned splits without special initialization. This would help determine whether the performance gains are due to the non-greedy optimization or simply the use of oblique splits. Additionally, the paper does not sufficiently explore the effect of tree depth (a key hyperparameter), as the depth must be pre-specified without thorough analysis of how depth impacts performance.

Third, the implementation of loss-augmented inference has questionable necessity. The authors claim their fast inference method (O(d²p)) is necessary for deep trees, but the experiments only go up to depth 18, where brute-force inference (O(2^d p)) would still be feasible for most datasets. The benefit of restricting the search to a Hamming ball (radius 1) is not clearly demonstrated across different tree depths.

Fourth, the paper identifies an issue where some leaves receive no data points during training, indicating potential problems with the optimization process. The authors introduce a "stable SGD" heuristic to address this, but this is poorly motivated and lacks theoretical justification. It would be more principled to use established methods like EM or CCCP for dealing with latent variables.

The paper also contains several minor issues: inconsistent terminology around "leaves" in the inference context, missing citation to related work on latent variable structured prediction (e.g., Yu & Joachims [25]), and a suggestion to replace "argmax" with "max" in the loss bound expression.

While the problem of joint decision tree optimization is important and original, the theoretical flaws, weak experimental evaluation, and questionable implementation details significantly reduce the paper's significance. The method shows promise but requires stronger theoretical foundations and more thorough empirical validation to be considered a strong contribution to NIPS.

The authors should address the theoretical concerns, add missing baselines, provide more thorough analysis of tree depth effects, and better motivate their heuristic for handling inactive leaves. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution is limited despite its promising direction. The key strength of the paper lies in the problem formulation, but the technical execution and validation are insufficient to support the claimed advantages over existing methods. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous theoretical analysis and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation to demonstrate the true value of the proposed approach.
