

This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach to decision tree optimization by framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, deriving a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently using stochastic gradient descent. Their key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) through a novel fast loss-augmented inference technique, making deep tree learning feasible. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over greedy baselines on multiple classification benchmarks, though the magnitude of improvement is generally modest.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for their approach, with a valid proof of the upper bound (equation 7) and thorough complexity analysis. The connection to structured prediction is well-motivated and properly contextualized against prior work. The computational complexity reduction is particularly impressive - the Hamming ball formulation (equation 13) and resulting O(d²p) inference algorithm represent a significant technical advance over previous non-greedy approaches, which were limited to shallow trees due to exponential complexity. The experimental results are well-conducted, with careful hyperparameter tuning and comparison against multiple relevant baselines including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and random oblique trees. The paper appropriately acknowledges the modest nature of the performance gains (as noted in the key points), and the regularization parameter ν provides a principled way to control overfitting.

The paper is well-written and clear. The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction is explained with sufficient detail, and the algorithmic description (Algorithm 1) is precise and reproducible. The complexity analysis is thorough, and the supplementary material provides additional mathematical details. The authors effectively address the key challenge of handling the exponential number of latent variables through their novel inference approach. The presentation of results (Figures 2-4) clearly demonstrates the computational advantages of their method and the consistent (though modest) performance improvements.

The originality of the work is strong. While non-greedy tree optimization has been attempted before (Bennett, 1997), this work represents a significant advance by generalizing to any loss function while maintaining computational feasibility for deep trees. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is novel and well-executed, and the fast loss-augmented inference technique is a major technical contribution. The paper properly cites relevant prior work, including NIPS papers on structured prediction (e.g., Yu and Joachims, 2009; Tsochantaridis et al., 2005), and clearly distinguishes their approach from previous efforts. The extension to arbitrary loss functions and the computational efficiency represent meaningful advances beyond the existing literature.

The significance of the work is moderate. While the method provides a principled alternative to greedy tree induction with clear computational advantages, the performance improvements over greedy baselines are generally small (as noted in the key points). The paper demonstrates that non-greedy optimization leads to better generalization at various tree depths, which is valuable, but the absolute improvements may not be sufficient to warrant widespread adoption in practice without further evidence of substantial gains on larger or more challenging datasets. The computational efficiency does make the approach applicable to large-scale problems, which is valuable, but the paper could better articulate how this method would be used in practice versus existing methods like random forests or gradient boosting. The connection to structured prediction opens potential for future extensions, such as applying the kernel trick or sparsity-inducing regularization, which adds to the significance.

Strengths: (1) Novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables; (2) Rigorous theoretical foundation with valid upper bound and complexity analysis; (3) Significant computational advance enabling deep tree learning (O(d²p) vs O(2^dp)); (4) Consistent improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets; (5) Clear, well-written presentation with reproducible algorithms.

Weaknesses: (1) Modest absolute improvements in test accuracy (as noted in key points), limiting practical significance; (2) Limited discussion of why improvements are modest and how the method might be combined with other techniques for larger gains; (3) Insufficient discussion of practical deployment considerations (e.g., how this would be implemented in existing ML pipelines); (4) The regularization parameter ν, while well-handled, could be better justified for different problem domains.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a theoretically sound, computationally feasible approach to non-greedy decision tree optimization. While the performance gains are modest, the method represents a meaningful advance over previous non-greedy approaches and provides a principled framework for future work. The technical contributions are substantial and well-executed, making this a solid NIPS paper that would be of interest to the broader machine learning community. The work is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel theoretical approach and practical computational improvements.
