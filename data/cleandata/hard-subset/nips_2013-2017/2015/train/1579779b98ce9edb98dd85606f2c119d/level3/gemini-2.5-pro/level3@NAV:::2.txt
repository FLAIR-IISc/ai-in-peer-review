This paper addresses the long-standing problem of greedy, node-by-node training in decision trees. The authors propose a novel algorithm for jointly optimizing all decision-tree parameters—both the split functions and the leaf posteriors—within a single, global optimization framework. The key technical contribution is a reformulation of the decision tree learning problem as structured prediction with latent variables, which allows the authors to derive a tractable convex-concave upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss. This approach is a significant departure from classic greedy induction methods like CART and ID3 and aims to be more scalable than previous non-greedy methods.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its originality and significance. Investigating a principled, non-greedy optimization for decision trees is an important research direction, and the connection drawn to structured prediction with latent variables is novel and insightful. The paper is generally well-written, with a clean derivation of the proposed surrogate objective.

However, the work has several notable shortcomings that temper its contribution. The most critical issue is a potential flaw in the theoretical reasoning. The argument justifying the empirical loss bound (around l.216f) is confusing, and an example can be constructed where the claimed upper bound does not appear to hold. Furthermore, the proof for Proposition 1 in the supplement seems incomplete. These issues cast doubt on the technical soundness of the core formulation.

The experimental evaluation and presentation also require improvement. The necessity of the efficient loss-augmented inference is not clearly demonstrated; for the depths tested (≤16), a brute-force approach may be feasible, making the impact of the Hamming ball restriction unclear. The experiments are missing a crucial baseline: a comparison against a standard greedy method for training non-axis-aligned splits. Additionally, the pre-specification of tree depth is a major limitation that should be discussed and evaluated more thoroughly. The phenomenon of some leaves receiving no data points during training is concerning and may indicate issues with the objective or initialization. The proposed fixed-assignment heuristic (SSGD) feels ad-hoc and would benefit from better motivation, perhaps by drawing connections to EM or CCCP.

In summary, while this paper introduces a valuable and original approach, its significance is undermined by potential theoretical flaws and an incomplete experimental evaluation. I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can provide a clear rebuttal addressing the soundness of the upper bound and include the requested baseline comparisons and runtime analysis.
