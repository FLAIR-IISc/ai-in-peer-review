This paper proposes an efficient non-greedy optimization method for decision trees, which jointly trains all decision-tree parameters via a single optimization with an upper-bound approximation for tractability. The authors establish a link between the decision tree optimization problem and the problem of structured prediction with latent variables, and propose a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss.

The paper is well-written overall, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method. The derivation is clean, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of their algorithm. However, the experiments and their presentation need improvement. The authors only provide a limited number of experiments on a few datasets, and the presentation of the results is not clear.

One of the main contributions of this work is the proposal of a novel formulation of the decision tree learning problem that associates a binary latent decision variable with each split node in the tree. This formulation enables the use of structured prediction techniques to optimize the decision tree parameters. The authors also propose a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm that reduces the computational complexity of the optimization problem.

However, there are some potential flaws in the theoretical reasoning. The argument around line 216 is confusing, and an example shows that the claimed upper bound may not hold. Additionally, the proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement is incomplete.

The proposed solution is valuable, but it has some notable shortcomings that affect its significance. Some leaves receive no data points, which may indicate issues with the objective or initialization. The fixed-assignment heuristic seems suboptimal and could be better motivated. The necessity and impact of the efficient loss-augmented inference are also questioned.

To improve the paper, the authors should provide more thorough experiments and a clearer presentation of the results. They should also address the potential flaws in the theoretical reasoning and provide a more complete proof of Proposition 1. Additionally, they should investigate the issues with the objective or initialization and provide a better motivation for the fixed-assignment heuristic.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and efficient non-greedy optimization method for decision trees.
* The authors establish a link between the decision tree optimization problem and the problem of structured prediction with latent variables.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The experiments and their presentation need improvement.
* There are potential flaws in the theoretical reasoning.
* The proposed solution has some notable shortcomings that affect its significance.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with the condition that the authors address the issues mentioned above and provide a revised version that improves the experiments and the theoretical reasoning.
