This paper proposes a novel, non-greedy algorithm for optimizing decision trees with linear split functions. The core idea is to formulate the problem as a structured prediction task with latent variables, enabling a global optimization of both split functions and leaf parameters via stochastic gradient descent (SGD) on a convex-concave upper bound of the empirical loss. This work builds on decades of research in decision tree induction [21, 5, 4], addressing the inherent limitations of greedy approaches like ID3 and CART. Recent advances have focused on improving objective functions within the greedy framework [20, 12], but this paper aims for a fundamentally different approach: joint optimization. The connection to structured prediction, drawing inspiration from works like [25, 23, 24], is a particularly elegant contribution.

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretical contributions. The formulation is well-motivated, and the reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is significant, allowing for the training of deeper trees. The introduction of a regularized surrogate objective provides a principled approach to avoid overfitting, a common issue with complex tree structures [4, 2]. The authors also identify a crucial issue regarding inactive leaves and propose a "Stable SGD" (SSGD) algorithm to address it, although the description of this algorithm remains somewhat opaque. 

However, the paper's primary weakness is the limited and often inconclusive empirical evaluation. While the authors demonstrate improvements over greedy baselines on *some* datasets, the gains are not substantial or consistent. The computational cost of the non-greedy approach is also higher, making the practical benefits questionable. Figure 2 is particularly unclear – it needs to explicitly state whether the results shown are obtained with standard SGD or the proposed SSGD and clearly define what is meant by an "active" leaf. The experimental setup could also benefit from a more thorough comparison against stronger baselines, such as ensemble methods (e.g., Random Forests [4]) optimized with more advanced techniques.

Several aspects require clarification and correction. Algorithm 1, line 10, should detail how the leaf parameters (Θ) are projected onto the simplex in practice, emphasizing that this enforces the constraint that each row sums to one.  The phrasing in line 407-408 concerning features is confusing and should be revised for clarity. The repeated use of “argmax” should be replaced with “max” in Equations (7)–(9) for consistency.  Figure 1 is cited in the text but never actually presented.  Several sentences are grammatically incorrect or contain typos (lines 141, 238, 301, 404) and the acronym FSGD is introduced on line 360 but not defined.  The supplementary material also requires correction: on line 29, the sign notation should be “=” rather than “∈”, and line 30 should use Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)) instead of sgn(Wx) inside the loss function, aligning with the paper's notation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound formulation connecting decision tree learning with structured prediction.
*   Reduction in computational complexity enables training of deeper trees.
*   Principled regularization scheme to mitigate overfitting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Empirical results are not compelling and do not consistently demonstrate significant advantages over greedy approaches.
*   The description of the SSGD algorithm is unclear and lacks sufficient detail.
*   Several minor errors and inconsistencies in the presentation (typos, undefined acronyms, unreferenced figure).



**Overall:**

The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework for non-greedy decision tree learning. However, the limited and somewhat inconclusive experimental results, coupled with the need for clarification and correction of several minor issues, make it difficult to strongly recommend acceptance at this time. While the problem formulation is elegant, the experimental evidence does not convincingly support the claim that global optimization provides a substantial advantage over established greedy methods. A major revision addressing the experimental weaknesses and clarifying the details of the SSGD algorithm would significantly strengthen the paper.
