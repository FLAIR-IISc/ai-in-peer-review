

# Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

This paper proposes a novel approach to decision tree learning by framing the search for optimal fixed-depth decision trees as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized using stochastic gradient descent. Their key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from exponential (O(2^dp)) to polynomial (O(d^2p)) by reformulating the loss-augmented inference problem within a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current tree path.

The work relates to prior efforts on non-greedy tree optimization, particularly Bennett's multi-linear programming approach [1], which was limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss and high computational complexity. The paper also distinguishes itself from soft-split approaches like Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13] that sacrifice computational efficiency for smooth optimization. While the authors acknowledge CO2 [18] as a greedy variant of their method, they demonstrate that their non-greedy approach consistently outperforms CO2 across multiple datasets.

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound contributions with thorough theoretical analysis. The proof of the convex-concave upper bound is well-motivated, and the complexity analysis is rigorous. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements in test accuracy over greedy baselines (including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and random oblique trees), though the absolute gains are modest (typically 1-3% across datasets). The authors carefully address limitations, including the need for regularization tuning and the small magnitude of improvements. However, the paper could provide more detailed analysis on how the regularization parameter ν interacts with tree depth and dataset characteristics.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The connection between decision trees and structured prediction is clearly articulated, and the algorithmic details are presented with a clear pseudocode (Algorithm 1). The authors effectively use Figures 1-4 to illustrate key concepts and experimental results. The supplementary material provides sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The core insight—linking decision tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables—is genuinely novel. While some connections between decision trees and structured prediction have been explored (e.g., [23, 24, 25]), this paper provides a concrete, efficient formulation that enables non-greedy optimization. The reformulation reducing complexity from exponential to polynomial is particularly significant. The authors properly contextualize their work against prior art, clearly distinguishing their contribution from Bennett's [1] and CO2 [18].

**Significance:** The work addresses a longstanding challenge in decision tree learning: the inability to jointly optimize all split functions under a global objective. While the improvements in test accuracy are modest, they are consistent across multiple datasets and demonstrate that non-greedy optimization can improve generalization. The framework's generality (applicable to any loss function) and potential for extension (e.g., sparsity-inducing regularization, kernel methods) enhance its significance. However, the paper doesn't fully address whether the computational overhead of their method is justified by the modest gains in accuracy.

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction
- Significant computational complexity reduction (exponential to polynomial)
- Clear, well-organized presentation with thorough empirical validation
- Provides a principled regularization approach for tree learning

**Weaknesses:**
- Improvements over greedy methods are small (typically 1-3%)
- Limited to fixed-depth trees without addressing how depth affects performance
- Doesn't compare to more recent tree-based methods (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM)
- Computational complexity analysis focuses on per-iteration cost without addressing convergence rates

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a significant technical contribution by enabling efficient non-greedy optimization of decision trees, which has been a longstanding challenge in the field. The theoretical insights are sound, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines. While the absolute gains are modest, the work represents a meaningful advancement in understanding and optimizing decision trees. The paper's clarity and thoroughness make it suitable for NIPS, and the framework provides a solid foundation for future work on tree-based methods. The authors' careful acknowledgment of limitations and small improvements demonstrates scientific rigor.

The paper advances the state of the art in decision tree learning by providing a principled, efficient framework for non-greedy optimization. While the improvements may not be revolutionary, they are demonstrable and consistent across multiple datasets, making this a valuable contribution to the field.
