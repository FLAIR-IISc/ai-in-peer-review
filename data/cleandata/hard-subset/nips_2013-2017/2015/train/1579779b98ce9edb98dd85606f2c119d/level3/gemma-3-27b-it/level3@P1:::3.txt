## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper presents a novel approach to decision tree induction, framing the problem as a structured prediction task with latent variables. The authors argue that standard greedy algorithms for tree building are suboptimal and propose a non-greedy method based on optimizing a convex-concave upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key contribution lies in relating decision tree optimization to structured prediction and developing an efficient gradient computation (O(d), where d is the tree depth) enabling training of deep trees.  Experimental results on several benchmark datasets demonstrate that the proposed non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines, albeit with modest gains.

The paper builds upon a long history of research in decision trees and forests [4, 5, 7, 10].  The limitations of the greedy approach, highlighted in the introduction, are well-known, and previous work has attempted to address these limitations [1, 2, 3, 15]. The authors accurately position their work within this context, noting that prior non-greedy methods often suffer from computational complexity [11] or are limited in scope [1, 15].  The connection to structured prediction [23, 24, 25] is a clever idea, and the formulation with latent variables provides a principled way to formulate a non-greedy objective. Related work on soft splits [13] and Mondrian Processes [15] are also appropriately acknowledged, with the authors differentiating their work based on computational efficiency and applicability to general loss functions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a significant contribution. This allows for a global optimization approach rather than the traditional greedy node-by-node construction.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors demonstrate a reduction in computational complexity to O(d) for gradient computation, which is crucial for training deep trees.  This is a notable improvement over potentially exponential complexities of naive approaches. The development of fast loss-augmented inference further enhances efficiency.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms greedy baselines on the tested datasets, providing evidence of the benefits of non-greedy optimization.  The performance improvements, while not dramatic, are statistically significant in some cases.
* **Theoretical Justification:**  The use of a convex-concave upper bound and the analysis of its properties provide a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed approach.  The discussion of regularization and its effect on the bound is insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and the technical details are presented clearly, making the approach accessible. The inclusion of Alg 1 outlining the SGD procedure is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Modest Gains:** The performance improvements over greedy baselines are often small, raising the question of whether the increased computational complexity is justified in all cases. A more thorough analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the regularization parameter ν, and the method of selecting ν through validation introduces a potential for overfitting to the validation set.  A more robust hyperparameter tuning strategy may be needed.
* **Limited Comparison:** While several baselines are included, a comparison to more recent and sophisticated methods for building decision trees and forests, such as those leveraging gradient boosting or deep learning approaches, would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Initialization Dependence:** The approach relies on initializing the trees with a greedy method (CO2). The impact of different initialization strategies on the final performance is not explored.
* **Practical Considerations:** The practical challenges of applying this method to very high-dimensional datasets, or datasets with a large number of features, are not fully addressed. The O(dp) complexity could become prohibitive in such cases.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to decision tree learning.
* The proposed method demonstrably outperforms greedy baselines.
* The computational efficiency improvements are significant and enable training of deeper trees.
* The connection to structured prediction provides a valuable new perspective on the problem.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The performance improvements are modest and may not justify the increased complexity in all applications.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and dependence on initialization could limit the practicality of the method.
* The evaluation could be more comprehensive, including comparisons to more recent state-of-the-art methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed non-greedy approach is theoretically well-motivated, computationally efficient, and empirically effective. While the performance improvements are not overwhelming, the novelty of the formulation and the potential for future extensions justify acceptance.  I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider adding a discussion of the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency, exploring more robust hyperparameter tuning strategies, and expanding the comparison to include more recent state-of-the-art methods.
