## Review of “Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees”

This paper presents a novel approach to optimizing decision trees by reformulating the problem as a latent structured prediction task. The core idea – utilizing sign vectors as latent variables and approximating the non-differentiable loss with a differentiable upper bound – is clever and addresses a fundamental limitation of traditional, greedy tree induction methods. The authors demonstrate a theoretical reduction in computational complexity to O(d), where d is tree depth, enabling feasible training of deeper trees via SGD. 

The work builds upon established ideas in structured prediction (e.g., [25, 23]) and connects them to a classic machine learning problem, offering a fresh perspective. The introduction of the surrogate objective and the development of efficient algorithms for loss-augmented inference are significant contributions. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, effectively motivating the problem and outlining the proposed solution. 

However, despite the ingenuity of the approach, several concerns limit its immediate impact. The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail regarding the baselines, particularly the pruning strategies employed for the naive/greedy method. Comparing against simpler global strategies, like multiple greedy trees trained on random subsets of the data, would strengthen the claim of superiority. The regularization aspect (L2) raises concerns about interpretability, a key advantage of standard decision trees, and the authors need to thoroughly investigate the L1/L2 tradeoff. 

Furthermore, the assumption about zero-weight nodes – implicitly taking the right branch – is problematic. The paper should explicitly define pruning/splitting behavior at these nodes. A crucial point, highlighted in the post-rebuttal, regarding zero-weight initialization and the potential for interesting growth dynamics from zero needs further investigation. The title mentions "forests," yet the paper doesn’t address or evaluate their application, creating a disconnect. Clarity could be improved in sections 6.2 and 6.4 regarding inference details.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel and efficient reformulation of decision tree optimization, addressing a long-standing limitation of greedy methods. Potential for building infinitely deep trees adaptively.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Insufficient experimental validation, particularly regarding baseline comparisons and regularization effects. Practical concerns regarding interpretability and zero-weight node handling. The forest aspect in the title is unsupported.



**Overall:** This is a theoretically sound and intriguing paper. While the proposed method holds promise, the lack of convincing experimental results and lingering practical concerns prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the raised concerns and provide a more compelling empirical demonstration of the method’s benefits.
