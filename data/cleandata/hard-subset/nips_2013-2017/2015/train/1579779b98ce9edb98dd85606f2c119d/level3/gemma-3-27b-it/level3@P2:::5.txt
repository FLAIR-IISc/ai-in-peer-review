This paper proposes a novel approach to learning decision trees by formulating the optimization problem as a structured prediction task with latent variables. The authors present a global optimization framework, aiming to overcome the limitations of traditional greedy algorithms. They derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and utilize stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for efficient training, introducing a “Stable SGD” variant to improve convergence. While the problem formulation is elegant and the connection to structured prediction is insightful, the empirical results are not entirely convincing, and several aspects of the method require further clarification.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of decision tree learning, acknowledging the prevalence of greedy algorithms (ID3, CART) and discussing previous attempts at global optimization (Bennett [1], Mondrian Processes [15]). It also highlights relevant work on structured prediction [23, 24, 25] and hierarchical models [13]. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive and effectively demonstrates the authors’ awareness of existing approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Formulation:** The core strength of this work lies in its elegant reformulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem. This allows the authors to leverage techniques from that field and develop a principled approach to global optimization.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The development of a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm, achieving O(d<sup>2</sup>p) complexity, is a significant contribution, enabling the training of deeper trees.
*   **Regularization:** The inclusion of L2 regularization, motivated by the properties of the upper bound, offers a mechanism to control overfitting.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Gains:** The experimental results presented do not consistently demonstrate substantial improvements over greedy baselines. While the proposed method outperforms greedy algorithms on some datasets (e.g., Connect4), the gains are often marginal, and in some cases, the performance is comparable or even worse. The computational overhead of global optimization is therefore questionable.
*   **Unclear Stable SGD:** The description of the Stable SGD (SSGD) algorithm is vague. It lacks sufficient detail regarding how data point assignments to leaves are managed and updated. A clear pseudo-code implementation is necessary to fully evaluate the effectiveness of this variant.
*   **Figure 2 Ambiguity:** The figure comparing the performance of different methods doesn’t clarify whether results are obtained using SGD or Stable SGD. The definition of an "active" leaf also remains unclear.
*   **Implementation Details:** Line 10 of Algorithm 1, updating Θ, requires further clarification. How is Θ projected onto the simplex to ensure that each row represents a valid probability distribution (i.e., sums to one)? This detail is essential for a complete understanding of the optimization process.
*   **Minor Issues:** Several minor issues detract from the paper's polish, including confusing phrasing (line 407–408), inconsistent use of “argmax” vs. “max” in equations (7)–(9), the absence of a reference to Figure 1, and typographical errors/unclear sentences (lines 141, 238, 301, 404) and the undefined acronym FSGD (line 360). Corrections are also needed in the supplementary material (line 29 and line 30).

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a well-defined mathematical formulation and a clear description of the optimization algorithm. However, the lack of strong empirical evidence and the ambiguities surrounding Stable SGD raise concerns about the practical impact of the proposed method.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the areas mentioned above (Stable SGD, Algorithm 1, minor edits) require improvement to enhance clarity. The notation is mostly consistent, but could benefit from more careful attention to detail.

**Originality:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem is a novel contribution. However, the core ideas build upon existing work in both decision trees and structured prediction, so the overall originality is moderate.

**Significance:** While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the limited empirical gains cast doubt on the overall significance of this work. If the proposed method can be refined to consistently outperform greedy algorithms with comparable computational cost, it would represent a significant advance in the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Elegant and theoretically sound formulation.
*   Potential for future improvements and extensions.
*   Connection to established fields of structured prediction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Modest empirical gains compared to greedy baselines.
*   Lack of clarity regarding Stable SGD.
*   Implementation details requiring further explanation.
*   Several minor errors and inconsistencies.



**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting and theoretically well-motivated approach to learning decision trees. However, the empirical results do not strongly support the claims of significant improvement over existing methods. The ambiguities surrounding Stable SGD and the need for clarification in several aspects of the implementation further weaken the paper. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses and provide more compelling empirical evidence. Further clarification on the benefits of the non-greedy approach, alongside a more detailed explanation of the Stable SGD algorithm and thorough revision of the technical details, could strengthen the paper for future consideration.
