- Proposes novel joint optimization of both split functions and leaf posteriors, moving beyond greedy tree construction.
- Connects decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables and formulates a convex-concave upper bound for tractable optimization.
- Theoretical derivation is clean and well-presented, showing strong optimization understanding.
- Introduces fast loss-augmented inference to address computational complexity of joint optimization.
- Paper is generally clearly written and easy to follow.

- Empirical loss bound is questionable (example suggests the claimed upper bound may not always hold).
- Proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement is incomplete, undermining confidence in the core theoretical claims.
- Some leaves receive no data points, indicating possible issues with the objective function or initialization procedure.
- Fixed-assignment heuristic for empty leaves is pragmatic but lacks principled motivation; alternatives like EM or CCCP are suggested.
- The necessity of efficient loss-augmented inference is unclear given the paper restricts depth to 16; brute-force may be feasible.
- Terminology is ambiguous: unclear whether “leaves” refers strictly to terminal nodes or includes intermediate nodes in inference.
- Missing a crucial baseline: greedy training of non-axis-aligned splits without special initialization.
- Pre-specifying tree depth is a significant limitation and the paper lacks experiments varying depth.
- Minor clarity fix: replace "argmax" with "max" in the loss bound expression.

- Experiments show some gains but lack thoroughness: need broader datasets, hyperparameter sweeps, more runtime comparisons, and statistical significance testing.

- Arguments for acceptance: genuinely novel approach, solid theoretical grounding (despite flaws), and some empirical improvements.
- Arguments against acceptance: doubts about theoretical correctness, objective/initialization problems, unclear benefit of efficient inference, missing baselines, and insufficient experimental validation.

- Overall: interesting and potentially impactful idea but currently flawed in theory and experiments; paper is below acceptance threshold unless authors provide rigorous proofs, address initialization/objective issues, add missing baselines, and expand empirical evaluation.
