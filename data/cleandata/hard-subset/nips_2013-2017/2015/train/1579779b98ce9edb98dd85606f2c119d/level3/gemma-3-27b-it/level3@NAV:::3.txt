## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a novel approach to training decision trees by framing the optimization of split functions as a structured prediction problem. The core idea is to leverage techniques from structured prediction – specifically, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss – to enable joint optimization of splits across all levels of the tree. This contrasts sharply with the traditional, greedy approaches like ID3 and CART, which optimize splits node-by-node. The authors establish a link between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, and demonstrate a reduction in computational complexity to O(d), where d is the tree depth, allowing for efficient training of deeper trees using stochastic gradient descent.

The work builds upon a substantial body of literature in decision tree learning, acknowledging the limitations of greedy methods and referencing previous attempts at global optimization (e.g., Bennett [1], Murthy & Salzburg [17]). It correctly positions itself within the context of more recent advancements in structured prediction (e.g., Taskar et al. [25]) and draws parallels to related approaches like Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13] and CO2 [18]. The paper effectively articulates the benefits of a non-greedy approach, particularly in avoiding suboptimal trees and potentially improving generalization performance.

A key strength of this work is the theoretical analysis demonstrating the computational efficiency of the proposed method, reducing the complexity to O(d). The experimental results on benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) demonstrate that the non-greedy trees generally outperform greedy baselines, though the improvements are often marginal. The ablation study examining the impact of the regularization parameter ν is also valuable. 

However, the gains in accuracy are relatively small. While statistically significant, the improvements over strong greedy baselines might not be substantial enough to warrant a complete shift in methodology for all applications. The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the conditions under which non-greedy optimization is *most* likely to be beneficial, and potentially exploring scenarios where greedy methods are sufficient. While the paper addresses limitations in the conclusion, a more thorough discussion of the computational trade-offs and the practical implications of increased training time would be helpful. The reliance on initializing the method with a greedy tree also feels somewhat limiting.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem.
*   Theoretical guarantees on computational efficiency (O(d)).
*   Empirical results demonstrating improved accuracy over greedy baselines.
*   Clear presentation and thorough related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Improvements in accuracy are modest.
*   The benefits of the approach aren’t fully contextualized.
*   Reliance on greedy initialization.



Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field. It's a well-written and theoretically sound work that proposes a promising alternative to traditional decision tree learning. While the practical impact may be limited by the modest accuracy gains, the novelty of the approach and the theoretical insights justify acceptance. I recommend accepting the paper, with minor revisions to further discuss the conditions for benefit and the practical implications of the method.
