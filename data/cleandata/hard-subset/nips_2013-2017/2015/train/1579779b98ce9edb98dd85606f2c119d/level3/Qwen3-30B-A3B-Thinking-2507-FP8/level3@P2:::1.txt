

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy learning of decision trees by optimizing split functions at all levels of the tree jointly with leaf parameters, rather than using the traditional greedy approach. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized using stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is showing how to reduce the computational complexity of gradient updates from O(2^dp) to O(d²p), making training deep trees feasible. This work builds on structured prediction literature (e.g., Yu & Joachims, ICML 2009) while addressing a long-standing challenge in decision tree learning.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors successfully establish the connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, and provide a clear proof of the upper bound. The complexity analysis is thorough, and the authors demonstrate how to efficiently compute gradients with O(d²p) complexity. The experimental results consistently show improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST), with non-greedy trees achieving better generalization performance and being less susceptible to overfitting. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, including the need for careful tuning of the regularization parameter ν and the potential for inactive leaves during training. The paper is well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The connection to structured prediction is explained with sufficient detail to be accessible to NIPS readers, and the mathematical formulations are precise and well-motivated. The authors effectively use figures (e.g., Fig. 1 showing the tree navigation function) to illustrate key concepts. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are presented in a logical manner. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution to the field by presenting a novel framework for non-greedy decision tree learning. While previous work has explored global tree optimization (Bennett, 1998), this paper provides a substantially more efficient approach that can handle deep trees with O(d²p) complexity, making it applicable to large-scale problems. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is particularly innovative and provides a principled way to formulate the optimization problem. Compared to prior NIPS papers on decision trees (e.g., Criminisi & Shotton, NIPS 2011), this work offers a fundamentally different approach that addresses the core limitation of greedy tree induction.

**Significance:** The proposed method has significant potential to advance the state of the art in decision tree learning. By providing a principled way to optimize decision trees globally, the approach could lead to better-performing models for classification tasks. The computational efficiency of the method makes it applicable to large-scale problems, which is increasingly important in modern machine learning. The framework is general enough to be applied to various loss functions and could be extended to other tree-based models. The work is likely to spur further research in non-greedy tree learning and structured prediction.

**Strengths:**
- Elegant theoretical connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction
- Significant computational efficiency improvement over previous non-greedy approaches
- Clear experimental results demonstrating consistent improvements over greedy baselines
- Well-written and well-organized paper that makes a substantial contribution to the field

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental results, while consistent, show modest improvements over greedy baselines (e.g., 1-3% accuracy gains on most datasets), which might limit immediate practical impact
- The paper does not thoroughly investigate whether the global optimization increases overfitting (despite noting that the regularization parameter controls generalization)
- The authors could have included more discussion about how the method compares to recent work on decision forests (e.g., Lakshminarayanan et al., NIPS 2014)

**Suggested improvements:**
1. The authors could explore the suggestion to drop and re-optimize the last split and its leaf node using standard methods after global optimization, as this might yield additional improvements.
2. A more thorough investigation of the overfitting behavior, particularly for deeper trees, would strengthen the experimental section.
3. The paper could benefit from a comparison to recent non-greedy methods like Mondrian forests (Lakshminarayanan et al., NIPS 2014) that handle online learning.

**Overall evaluation:**
This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The theoretical insights are sound, the technical approach is novel and well-executed, and the experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines. While the experimental gains are somewhat modest, the method is elegant, theoretically grounded, and opens up new possibilities for non-greedy tree learning. The paper is clearly written and well-suited for publication at NIPS.

The paper merits acceptance, as it advances the state of the art in decision tree learning with a novel and well-executed approach. It is likely to be influential in the field, inspiring further research on non-greedy optimization of tree-based models.
