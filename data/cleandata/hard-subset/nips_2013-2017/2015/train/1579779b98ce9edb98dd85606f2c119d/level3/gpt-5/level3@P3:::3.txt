Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a non-greedy training method for oblique decision trees by casting the selection of split decisions as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. Each internal node’s binary decision is treated as a latent bit h, and the full sequence of split decisions selects a leaf whose parameters produce the prediction. Building on latent structural SVMs, the authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized by (projected) stochastic gradient descent. A key algorithmic contribution is a “fast loss-augmented inference” scheme that restricts the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current split decisions, reducing per-example inference from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p). The method regularizes each row of the split matrix W with a per-row norm constraint, improving generalization and preventing the bound from degenerating. A “Stable SGD” variant holds leaf assignments fixed for several updates to avoid leaf inactivity. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets show that single non-greedy trees achieve higher test accuracy than typical greedy baselines (CART axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) and the closely related CO2 method; gains are generally modest but consistent across depths.

The work is well situated relative to decades of greedy tree induction (ID3, CART), prior global/oblique methods (OC1; MILP/global optimization by Bennett; margin-based trees), soft/probabilistic trees (HME), and the authors’ own CO2. The structured prediction link and surrogate bound connect to max-margin Markov networks and latent structural SVMs.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The latent-variable formulation sgn(Wx) = argmax_h h^T Wx is standard; the upper bounds (global and Hamming-ball variants) are correct and intuitively tightness-controlled by the scale of W. The projection enforcing per-row norm constraints is straightforward. The fast loss-augmented inference argument (at most d+1 affected leaves) is plausible and aligns with tree-path structure.
- Empirical support: Results on multiple datasets and across depths show consistent, if small, test improvements over strong greedy baselines and CO2. Complexity measurements corroborate scalability of the fast inference variant. However, statistical significance is not reported, and ablations are limited (there is some analysis of ν and SSGD, but fewer controlled studies of initialization and radius choice).
- Completeness and honesty: The paper is clear that the improvements are modest and focuses on single-tree analysis to isolate the effect of non-greedy training.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally clear and well-organized. The navigation function f and the role of the Hamming-ball restriction are explained cleanly, with helpful intuition about path bits vs. off-path bits. Pseudocode for SGD is provided. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement, which is reasonable. A brief illustrative example of loss-augmented inference on a tiny tree could further aid intuition.

Originality
The mapping of oblique decision-tree training to latent structured prediction with a jointly optimized global objective is novel in the decision-tree literature, and the convex–concave upper bound with a radius-1 loss-augmented inference trick seems new and practically important. The work is differentiated from OC1 (greedy local search), MILP (intractable beyond small trees), and HME (soft splits with high inference cost), and goes beyond the authors’ prior CO2 by optimizing all nodes jointly.

Significance
Methodologically, the paper contributes a principled, scalable framework that others can build on (e.g., alternative losses, kernels, sparsity). Practically, single-tree accuracy gains over greedy training are small but consistent; given that ensembles dominate in practice, the immediate impact on state-of-the-art end-to-end accuracy is moderate. Nonetheless, improving single-tree training is relevant for interpretability, latency, and as a building block for forests/boosting.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation linking tree learning to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient algorithmic innovation (Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference) enabling O(d^2 p) training.
- Per-row norm regularization provides a tunable bias–variance tradeoff and reduces overfitting.
- Consistent improvements over several strong greedy baselines and CO2 across depths.
- Practical training details (initialization, SSGD) and complexity measurements are provided.

Cons
- Accuracy gains are generally small; no comparison to strong ensemble baselines (random forests, gradient boosting), limiting practical significance.
- No statistical significance tests; robustness across random seeds/splits is unclear.
- Dependence on initialization (greedy/CO2) is strong; sensitivity analysis is limited.
- Theoretical guarantees are limited to an upper bound; no convergence or generalization bounds are provided.
- Hamming-ball radius 1 is a heuristic; impact of larger radii or adaptive strategies is unexplored.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please report variance over multiple random initializations/splits and provide statistical tests to quantify the (often small) improvements.
- Compare single non-greedy trees to pruned greedy trees tuned by validation, and to soft decision trees/HME trained by EM, to contextualize accuracy vs. inference cost.
- Provide comparisons (or at least discussion) against standard ensembles (RF, GBDT) on accuracy vs. training/inference time to better position the contribution.
- Analyze sensitivity to initialization (axis-aligned vs. OC1 vs. random), ν, learning rate, and the SSGD schedule. An ablation showing how much each component (regularization, fast inference, SSGD) contributes would be valuable.
- Consider exploring sparse oblique splits via L1 or group-sparse constraints to improve interpretability and speed; discuss feasibility within the current projection framework.
- Can the Hamming radius be increased adaptively (e.g., early training larger radius, later smaller) to balance exploration and efficiency?
- Release code and hyperparameter settings to aid reproducibility.

Verdict
This is a solid, well-executed paper with a principled optimization framework and careful algorithmic design. While empirical gains on single trees are modest, the approach is original and potentially influential for future tree/forest learning methods. I lean toward acceptance, provided the authors strengthen the empirical analysis (robustness, statistical significance) and better contextualize practical impact against ensemble baselines.
