- Proposes a non-greedy training method for oblique decision trees by casting split decisions as latent binary vectors and optimizing a global surrogate inspired by latent structural SVM.  
- Introduces fast loss-augmented inference restricted to a Hamming ball (radius 1) reducing per-example complexity to O(d^2 p) and enabling SGD optimization.  
- Uses per-row norm constraints on W and a Stable SGD (SSGD) variant to control overfitting and prevent inactive leaves.  
- Supports general loss functions and joint optimization of split and leaf parameters.  
- Empirical evaluation on SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST shows consistent but often modest accuracy gains over greedy oblique baselines and CO2.  
- Positions work relative to prior methods: greedy induction (ID3/CART), intractable global optimizations, soft-split HMEs, and CO2; claims novel global hard-split latent-structured formulation.  
- Strength: clear conceptual novelty linking hard-split trees to latent structured prediction with a principled surrogate.  
- Strength: algorithmic efficiency via Hamming-ball inference and per-example gradient scaling with depth rather than number of leaves.  
- Strength: generality across losses and oblique split learning, with explicit regularization and stability mechanisms.  
- Strength: empirical validation and ablation analyses on the role of the regularizer ν and runtime.  
- Weakness: absolute improvements over strong greedy oblique baselines and CO2 are frequently small, limiting practical impact for single trees.  
- Weakness: lacks comparisons to standard ensembles (random forests, boosting), weakening practical significance for typical applications.  
- Weakness: surrogate is non-convex in W and Θ; optimization behavior, sensitivity to initialization, and SSGD are not deeply analyzed.  
- Weakness: scalability concerns remain for high-dimensional data and deep trees; wall-clock and memory costs versus strong greedy implementations are not fully detailed.  
- Weakness: no generalization bounds or margin-based theoretical guarantees provided.  
- Quality: technically sound derivations and complexity analysis, but empirical ablations (initialization, SSGD effects, depth/p scaling) could be stronger.  
- Clarity: well organized and readable; would benefit from concrete pseudocode for fast inference and more on SSGD trade-offs.  
- Originality: the latent-structured hard-split formulation with efficient surrogate appears novel compared to prior soft-split and global methods.  
- Significance: moderate — principled and efficient advance, but broader impact would increase with ensemble integration and larger empirical scope.  
- Suggestions: evaluate non-greedy trees as base learners in ensembles, ablate initialization and SSGD, report wall-clock/memory comparisons, explore sparsity regularization, and release code/splits.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel principled formulation, efficient algorithm, clear presentation, and consistent empirical gains.  
- Cons for acceptance: modest gains, limited optimization/initialization analysis, and heuristic SSGD without theory.  
- Recommendation: weak accept — technically solid with clear potential, stronger empirical work (especially ensembles) would make the contribution more compelling.
