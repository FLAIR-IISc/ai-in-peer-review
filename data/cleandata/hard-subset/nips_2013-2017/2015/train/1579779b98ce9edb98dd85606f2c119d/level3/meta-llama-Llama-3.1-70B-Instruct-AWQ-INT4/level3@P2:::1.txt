This paper presents a novel approach to decision tree optimization, deviating from the traditional greedy methods that optimize the split functions one node at a time. The proposed method formulates the decision tree learning as a global optimization problem, where the split functions at all levels of the tree are optimized jointly with the leaf parameters. This non-greedy approach is made possible by establishing a link between the decision tree optimization problem and the problem of structured prediction with latent variables.

The paper provides a clear and concise overview of the related work, highlighting the limitations of previous methods and the contributions of the proposed approach. The authors demonstrate that their method can handle any general loss function while maintaining the O(dp) complexity of inference imparted by the tree structure. The surrogate objective provides a natural way to regularize the joint optimization of tree parameters to discourage overfitting.

The experimental results show that the proposed non-greedy decision trees outperform greedy decision tree baselines on several classification benchmarks. However, the results are not overwhelmingly superior, and the authors acknowledge that the method may not always produce better results. The suggestion to drop and re-optimize the last split and its leaf node using standard methods after global optimization is a potential way to improve the results.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of their approach, including the problem formulation, the upper bound on empirical loss, and the optimization algorithm. The use of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and the introduction of a regularizer on the norm of W are well-justified. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the results.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper meets the criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The writing is clear and concise, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The approach is novel and contributes to the field of decision tree optimization. The results are significant, and the paper has the potential to spur further research in this area.

However, there are some concerns regarding the potential for overfitting. The authors acknowledge that the method may be prone to overfitting, but they do not provide a thorough evaluation of this issue. It is essential to assess whether the global optimization increases overfitting and to explore methods to mitigate this problem.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel approach to decision tree optimization, and the results are promising. While there are some concerns regarding overfitting, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of their approach. The paper meets the criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance, making it a strong candidate for publication.
