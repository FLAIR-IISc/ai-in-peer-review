Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training algorithm for decision trees that jointly optimizes all internal split functions and leaf parameters under a single global objective. The key idea is to cast the split decisions along a path as latent binary variables and to relate tree training to structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss that is differentiable almost everywhere and amenable to stochastic optimization. A naive loss-augmented inference step would be exponential in tree depth; the paper shows how to reduce this to O(d^2 p) per example (d = depth, p = feature dimension) by restricting to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current split decisions. An SGD/SSGD procedure with per-node norm constraints on the split weights enables scalable training. On several LibSVM classification benchmarks (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST), the resulting oblique trees outperform several greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1 oblique, random oblique, and CO2), with gains that are consistent but generally modest. The method appears less prone to overfitting than greedy training and can exploit deeper trees.

Relation to prior work
- Connects global decision tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables (e.g., max-margin Markov networks and latent structural SVM). The bound resembles loss-augmented inference common in structured SVMs, and the per-row norm constraints parallel margin regularization in SVMs.
- Improves on earlier global or lookahead tree-training approaches that are either NP-hard or computationally infeasible beyond shallow trees. Compared to Bennett’s multi-linear programming and margin-based trees, this work scales via SGD and a carefully designed surrogate.
- Distinct from Hierarchical Mixture of Experts, which uses soft splits and sacrifices tree-time inference; here, hard splits and O(dp) inference are preserved.
- Builds on CO2 (a depth-1 continuous optimization of oblique splits) by optimizing all nodes jointly under a global objective.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a principled latent-structured formulation leading to a global surrogate objective.
- Efficient training: fast loss-augmented inference reduces per-step complexity to O(d^2 p); inference remains O(dp).
- Practical algorithm: SGD/SSGD with per-node norm constraints; initialization via greedy/CO2; good engineering details.
- Empirical performance: consistent improvements over strong greedy baselines across several datasets; reduced overfitting at larger depths.
- Flexibility: accommodates general losses (logistic, squared), and the framework suggests natural extensions (sparsity, kernels).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Magnitude of gains is often small; lack of statistical significance analysis (e.g., confidence intervals over multiple runs/random initializations).
- Comparisons are restricted to single trees; while justified to isolate effects, most practice uses ensembles (RF/GBDT). It would be useful to quantify whether the gains per tree translate into stronger forests or boosted ensembles.
- Theoretical aspects are limited to bounding and a scale monotonicity proposition; no generalization bounds or convergence guarantees (beyond standard SGD behavior on non-convex objectives).
- The Hamming-ball (radius-1) bound biases updates to local changes; discussion of how this impacts global optimality or whether larger radii are feasible would strengthen the work.
- Some implementation choices (e.g., SSGD leaf-assignment freezing) are heuristic; ablations quantifying their contribution are light.

Quality
- Technically sound and carefully derived surrogate with correct linkage to latent structured prediction. The fast inference trick is simple and effective.
- Claims are supported by experiments on multiple datasets with reasonable baselines. However, statistical rigor and ablation breadth could be improved (variance across seeds, contribution of SSGD, effect of ν and depth beyond what is shown).

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The connection to structured prediction is clearly explained; algorithmic steps are explicit. Some proofs are deferred; a brief sketch in the main text would help. A clearer description of how inactive leaves evolve under SSGD and an ablation on SSGD vs SGD would improve readability.

Originality
- The framing of hard-split tree training as latent structured prediction with an efficiently optimizable global surrogate is novel in this context. The Hamming-ball relaxation for tractable loss-augmented inference in trees is a neat idea. Relation to CO2 is acknowledged; the move from depth-1 greedy to joint, depth-d optimization is a meaningful step.

Significance
- Practical significance is moderate: single-tree accuracy improvements are consistent but modest; nonetheless, the approach addresses a long-standing limitation of greedy tree induction and could influence future tree/forest training methods. The method preserves tree inference speed, which is valuable in applications.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to random initialization and mini-batch ordering? Please report mean/std over multiple runs.
- What is the impact of SSGD vs standard SGD on accuracy and active-leaf utilization?
- Can you explore Hamming-ball radii >1 (perhaps adaptively) and quantify the trade-off between compute and accuracy?
- Do gains transfer to ensembles (e.g., replacing greedy trees in RF/GBDT with your trained trees)?
- Any observations on learned sparsity of oblique splits; could L1 or group-lasso per node further improve generalization and interpretability?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add statistical significance analysis and multiple-run averages.
- Include ablations for SSGD, ν, depth, and initialization (axis-aligned vs OC1 vs CO2).
- Report training wall-clock and memory vs depth across datasets; include deeper trees where feasible.
- Provide a brief proof sketch of the main bound in the paper body.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled, and efficient non-greedy training for decision trees.
- Preserves fast inference while enabling joint optimization.
- Consistent empirical improvements and reduced overfitting across benchmarks.
- Clear connection to, and extension of, latent structured prediction techniques.

Arguments against acceptance
- Improvements are generally small and lack statistical analysis.
- Limited evaluation scope (no ensembles, few large-scale/high-dimensional datasets).
- Theoretical contributions stop at an upper bound and scale monotonicity; no generalization guarantees.
- Reliance on a radius-1 Hamming relaxation may limit the globality of the optimization.

Overall, this is a solid and well-motivated contribution with practical relevance to tree learning. With stronger empirical rigor and broader evaluation, it would have high impact.
