Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a long-standing limitation of decision tree induction: greedy, node-by-node optimization can yield suboptimal trees. The authors propose a non-greedy, joint optimization of all internal split parameters and leaf distributions under a single global objective. They cast oblique split selection as structured prediction with latent variables, derive an upper bound (surrogate) on empirical loss that is convex-concave in the split parameters W, and optimize it with stochastic gradient descent. A key efficiency contribution is to restrict loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing per-example complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p). They also introduce a “Stable SGD” variant that freezes leaf assignments for several iterations to mitigate inactive leaves. Experiments on several multiclass datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) show consistent gains over greedy baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique splits, and a greedy one-node special case, CO2).

The work connects to global (non-greedy) tree optimization (Bennett), SVM-based margin trees (Bennett & Blue; Bennett et al.), hierarchical mixtures of experts (Jordan & Jacobs), and structural SVMs including latent-variable formulations (Tsochantaridis; Yu & Joachims). The latent-variable view of oblique split decisions and the associated bound are novel in the tree literature and related to prior work on loss-augmented inference in structured prediction.

Assessment by review criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall formulation is compelling, and the derivations are mostly clean. However, there are potential issues with the empirical loss upper bound and its exposition. The discussion around the loss bound (around the introduction of Eq. 7) is confusing, and I can construct simple counterexamples suggesting the stated upper bound may not hold as written unless additional conditions are imposed. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement is also incomplete/unclear. These points need clarification or correction.
- Algorithmic components (fast loss-augmented inference; per-row norm constraints; SSGD) are reasonable. The SSGD “fixed leaf assignment” heuristic seems ad hoc; a more principled connection to EM/CCCP or block-coordinate minimization would strengthen the justification.
- Experiments indicate consistent improvements over several greedy baselines, but the empirical study is limited: some relevant baselines are missing, ablations are incomplete, and the depth setting is pre-specified with limited discussion of its impact.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The structured prediction connection is explained clearly, and the computational complexity story is helpful.
- The bound presentation would benefit from more precise statements and proofs; in particular, please ensure the inequality truly upper-bounds the loss for all cases and clarify the role of “max” vs “argmax” in the bound expression. A minor editorial suggestion: in the loss bound, replace argmax with max where the expression uses a value rather than an optimizer.
- Terminology: “leaves” in the inference section should be defined precisely in context (e.g., whether “d+1 distinct values” refers to distinct reachable leaves under Hamming radius 1).
- The supplement should include full, self-contained proofs (not sketches) for key propositions.

Originality
- The idea of jointly optimizing all oblique split functions with leaf parameters through a latent-structured upper bound is original relative to standard greedy trees and prior oblique tree methods (e.g., OC1, margin trees), and distinct from soft routing models like HMEs. The Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference for scalability is a nice twist.
- Relation to CO2 is appropriate; showing that non-greedy joint optimization can improve over greedy upper-bound optimization is valuable.

Significance
- The direction—non-greedy, globally trained decision trees—is important and timely, with potential impact on single-tree accuracy and interpretability.
- Current shortcomings reduce the immediate significance: questions about the bound, limited baselines (e.g., greedy oblique splits trained via logistic/SVM without special initialization), and modest experimental depth (depth ≤ 16; no deeper trees or larger-scale settings) make it hard to assess generality. The practical impact of the fast inference restriction (Hamming ball) is not convincingly demonstrated.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Theory:
  - Clarify and, if necessary, correct the loss upper bound (Eq. 7). Provide conditions under which it holds and a complete proof. Address the potential counterexample; if the bound requires margin assumptions or restricted f(·), please state so.
  - Provide a complete proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement and discuss implications for optimization (e.g., why constraining per-row norms is preferable to letting the scale go to infinity).
- Algorithms:
  - Better motivate SSGD. Can you formalize it as alternating minimization with fixed assignments (E/M steps), or as CCCP on a difference-of-convex surrogate?
  - Empirically compare full loss-augmented inference vs Hamming-ball restriction on a range of depths and datasets. Does the restriction change the learned model materially?
- Experiments:
  - Add a baseline for greedy oblique splits trained with a discriminative criterion (e.g., SVM or logistic regression at each node), with and without your initialization.
  - Provide ablations: effect of initialization (random/axis-aligned/CO2), effect of ν, effect of SSGD vs vanilla SGD, and effect of Hamming-ball radius.
  - Discuss depth more thoroughly: while you vary depth, please comment on automatic depth selection (validation or pruning), and analyze sensitivity to depth and stopping criteria.
  - Expand runtime analysis: end-to-end training time vs baselines; scaling with n, p, and d.
- Minor: clarify what is meant by “inactive leaves” and whether they persist at convergence; define “leaves” precisely in the fast inference discussion; fix the “argmax vs max” usage in the inequality.

Pros
- Important problem: non-greedy global optimization for decision trees.
- Novel latent-structured formulation and usable upper bound with SGD training.
- Practical efficiency trick (Hamming-ball) and thoughtful complexity analysis.
- Consistent improvements over multiple greedy baselines.

Cons
- Potential flaw/ambiguity in the loss upper bound and incomplete proofs.
- Heuristic SSGD lacks stronger justification.
- Missing relevant baselines and ablations; limited evaluation of depth and scalability.
- The benefit of fast inference over full inference is not convincingly demonstrated in current settings.

Questions for the authors
- Under what precise conditions does your loss upper bound hold? Can you address counterexamples?
- How sensitive are results to initialization and ν? Can you show results with random initialization?
- How often do leaves remain inactive at convergence, and does SSGD consistently reduce this?
- How different are models learned with full vs Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference?

Recommendation
Borderline reject pending clarification of the bound and stronger empirical validation. I am open to raising my score if the rebuttal provides a correct and complete proof of the bound (and Prop. 1), clarifies SSGD’s rationale, and adds key ablations or baseline comparisons (especially full vs Hamming-ball inference and greedy oblique with discriminative splits). Confidence: moderate.
