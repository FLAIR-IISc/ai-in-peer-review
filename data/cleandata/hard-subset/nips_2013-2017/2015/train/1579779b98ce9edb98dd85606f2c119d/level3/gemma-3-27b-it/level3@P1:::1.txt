This paper presents a novel approach to decision tree learning, moving beyond the standard greedy algorithms that have dominated the field for decades. The authors formulate a non-greedy optimization problem for decision trees, aiming to jointly optimize split functions at all levels with the leaf parameters, based on a global objective. This is cleverly achieved by establishing a link between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, leading to a convex-concave upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss. The resulting surrogate objective can be efficiently optimized using stochastic gradient descent, making it feasible to train deep trees on large datasets. The paper is well-written and the ideas are presented clearly, demonstrating a strong understanding of existing work in decision tree learning (e.g., ID3 [21], CART [5], CO2 [18]) and structured prediction (e.g., [25], [24]).

The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical elegance and the potential for significant impact. The non-greedy approach directly addresses a known limitation of traditional decision tree induction, where optimization at one node is not informed by the results of lower levels. The connection to structured prediction is insightful and provides a solid mathematical foundation for the proposed algorithm. The authors also demonstrate a practical consideration for scalability, reducing the computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) through a modification of the surrogate objective. This efficiency is crucial for applying the method to deeper and more complex trees.

However, the experimental results are somewhat underwhelming. While the non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines on the benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST), the margin of improvement isn't substantial. The authors acknowledge this and appropriately temper expectations, suggesting that the primary contribution lies in the method itself rather than a dramatic leap in accuracy. This is a valid point, but it does raise a concern regarding the practical significance of the proposed approach. A potential avenue for future improvement, suggested by the authors themselves, involves post-processing the globally optimized tree. Specifically, dropping and re-optimizing the last split and its associated leaf node using standard greedy methods could potentially refine the results and address any lingering suboptimality.

A crucial consideration, and one briefly touched upon in the conclusion, is the potential for increased overfitting with a more globally optimized tree. The authors employ regularization (ν) to mitigate this, but a more thorough analysis of the trade-off between model complexity and generalization would strengthen the paper. Specifically, investigating the impact of different regularization strengths and comparing the complexity of the trees generated by the non-greedy and greedy methods would be valuable.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of techniques – applying structured prediction principles to decision tree learning and formulating a practical optimization strategy based on a convex-concave upper bound. While related work (e.g., [11], [1], [15]) has explored non-greedy tree learning, this paper offers a significantly different and more scalable approach. The thorough review of related work demonstrates that the authors are well aware of existing contributions and clearly position their work within the field.

The significance of this work is moderate, but potentially growing. While the current experimental results don't represent a breakthrough in accuracy, the proposed method opens up new avenues for research in decision tree learning. The ability to jointly optimize split functions across the entire tree could lead to more compact and interpretable models, and the framework is flexible enough to accommodate different loss functions and regularization techniques. The potential for extending this work to kernelized decision trees, as suggested by the authors, further enhances its significance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to decision tree learning.
*   Clear and well-written paper with a strong understanding of related work.
*   Practical considerations for scalability (O(d2p) complexity).
*   Potential to spur further research in the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental results are not dramatically superior to existing methods.
*   Limited analysis of overfitting.
*   Practical impact remains to be fully demonstrated.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. While the experimental results are not overwhelming, the theoretical elegance and potential for future research make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically a more detailed analysis of overfitting and exploration of the post-processing suggestion outlined above.
