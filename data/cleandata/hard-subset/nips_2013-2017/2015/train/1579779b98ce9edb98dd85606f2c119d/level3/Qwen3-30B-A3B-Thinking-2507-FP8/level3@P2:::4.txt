

# Review for "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a novel framework for global optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction by treating the binary split decisions as latent variables. They develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD), with computational complexity reduced from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) through a clever reformulation of the loss-augmented inference problem. The key technical contribution is demonstrating how to efficiently compute gradients for deep trees while maintaining the computational advantages of tree-based methods.

The paper makes a significant contribution by connecting decision tree optimization to structured prediction, a domain where efficient optimization techniques have been well-studied. This is a meaningful advancement over previous work such as Bennett's non-greedy multi-linear programming approach [1], which was limited to shallow trees and binary classification, and the CO2 method [18], which applies a similar upper bound but in a greedy manner. The authors also build upon the connection between decision trees and structured prediction established in prior work [25], but extend it to enable non-greedy global optimization.

**Strengths:**
1. **Originality**: The paper establishes a novel connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, which is both theoretically sound and practically valuable. This is a significant departure from traditional greedy approaches that have dominated decision tree learning for decades.
2. **Quality**: The theoretical development of the upper bound and the efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm are technically sound. The paper provides a rigorous proof of the upper bound and demonstrates how the complexity can be reduced from exponential to polynomial.
3. **Significance**: The approach addresses a fundamental limitation of greedy decision tree learning and offers a promising path toward more accurate single trees. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets, suggesting the method could be useful in practice.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity**: The description of the loss-augmented inference algorithm (Section 6.2) is somewhat opaque and would benefit from a clearer step-by-step explanation. The transition from the Hamming ball formulation to the efficient inference algorithm could be more explicitly motivated.
2. **Practical Concerns**: The paper fails to adequately address interpretability concerns. Standard decision trees are valued for their interpretability, but the L2 regularization used in the method likely produces dense, non-interpretable splits. The authors should discuss whether L1 regularization could be used to maintain sparsity while preserving the optimization benefits.
3. **Experimental Validation**: The experimental section lacks sufficient detail on baseline comparisons. The paper compares to greedy methods but does not evaluate against alternative global strategies, such as training multiple greedy trees on random subsets of features or data, which would provide a fairer comparison to the non-greedy approach. The title mentions forests but the paper does not evaluate forests, creating a disconnect between the title and content.
4. **Critical Ambiguity**: The paper leaves unresolved the behavior of zero-weight nodes (Section 6.2). The authors state that "zero-weight nodes implicitly take the right branch" but this is problematic as it introduces an arbitrary decision at critical points. A more principled approach to handling zero-weight nodes is needed, especially for adaptive tree growth.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically promising approach to non-greedy decision tree learning. The connection to structured prediction is clever and the technical development is rigorous. However, the lack of discussion on interpretability concerns, the ambiguity around zero-weight nodes, and insufficient experimental validation against appropriate baselines weaken the paper's impact. 

The authors should address these concerns in a revision by:
1. Providing a clearer explanation of the loss-augmented inference algorithm
2. Discussing the interpretability trade-offs and potential use of L1 regularization
3. Adding experimental comparisons with alternative global optimization strategies
4. Resolving the ambiguity around zero-weight nodes
5. Either modifying the title to reflect the focus on single trees or expanding the paper to include forest experiments

Given the novelty of the approach and the quality of the technical contributions, this paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to NIPS. However, the current version falls short in addressing practical concerns and providing adequate experimental validation. I recommend **weak acceptance** with the expectation that the authors will address the concerns outlined above in a revised submission. The paper would be significantly strengthened by resolving the ambiguities around zero-weight nodes and by providing a more comprehensive experimental evaluation.
