Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a non-greedy training algorithm for oblique decision trees by casting the split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a global objective. The key idea is to view the vector of node decisions h = sgn(Wx) as a latent structured output and optimize an upper bound on empirical loss inspired by structural SVMs with latent variables (Taskar et al., Tsochantaridis et al., Yu & Joachims). The authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on the tree loss that leads to a loss-augmented inference subproblem. A naive formulation would make loss-augmented inference exponential in depth; the paper introduces a tractable variant that restricts the maximization to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current decisions, yielding O(d^2 p) training-time complexity for depth d and input dimension p. Optimization proceeds with (stochastic) gradient updates over W and Θ, with projection to enforce per-row L2 constraints. A “Stable SGD” variant reduces flapping by freezing leaf assignments between updates.

The work is related to global tree optimization (Bennett), max-margin trees (Bennett & Blue; Bennett et al.), oblique trees (OC1), HME (Jordan & Jacobs), and hashing with latent structured formulations (Norouzi & Fleet). Unlike soft trees/HME which sacrifice test-time efficiency, the proposed method retains hard splits and O(dp) inference. Compared to greedy objectives (e.g., information gain) or nodewise CO2 optimization, this paper presents a joint global training criterion with an explicit surrogate bound and scalable optimization.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: clean reframing of decision-tree optimization as latent structured prediction, with a principled upper bound and a clear connection to margin rescaling.
- Algorithmic contribution: practical loss-augmented inference via the Hamming-ball restriction leads to O(d^2 p) training and maintains O(dp) test-time complexity. The updates are sparse along a single root-to-leaf path, enabling efficient SGD.
- Practicality: the method works with general losses (log loss, squared loss), handles multi-class leaves via Θ, and supports stochastic optimization on large datasets.
- Empirical evidence: on multiple LibSVM benchmarks the non-greedy trees generally outperform strong greedy baselines (axis-aligned CART, OC1, random oblique, and CO2). Ablations illustrate the effect of ν and the runtime benefits of the fast inference.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practical impact and baselines: Experiments focus on single trees and avoid ensembles; while this isolates the training effect, practitioners often use forests. It would help to include stronger single-tree baselines with careful pruning and hyperparameter sweeps (e.g., cost-complexity pruning, min leaf size, depth penalties), and simple global strategies (e.g., train multiple greedy trees on random subsets and choose best validation score). Clarify whether the greedy baselines received comparable tuning and pruning.
- Initialization and sensitivity: The method initializes W, Θ using a CO2-trained tree. Please quantify gains over CO2 with identical hyperparameter budgets and report sensitivity to initialization (random, zero, or axis-aligned CART). Does non-greedy optimization consistently improve CO2 or only in some regimes?
- Update sparsity and dynamics: Gradients only affect nodes along a root-to-leaf path and only where bits differ between ĝ and ĥ, often a very small set. This is computationally attractive, but may bias the optimization toward revising a few nodes repeatedly. Provide analysis/measurements showing how often updates change early vs deep nodes, and whether the method prefers “adding new splits” (activating deeper nodes) versus “refining existing splits.”
- Zero-weight and routing ambiguity: The model relies on sgn(Wx); the sgn(0) case is ambiguous. The draft suggests defaulting to one branch (e.g., right), which is brittle. Please specify and analyze the behavior at zero weights and near-zero inner products, and consider defining: (a) an early-leaf output at an internal node when its norm is zero (explicit pruning), or (b) tie-breaking that is stable across training. The SSGD scheme partly addresses instability but a clear semantics for inactive/empty leaves would strengthen the approach.
- Interpretability vs oblique dense splits: A common motivation for trees is interpretability. Row-wise L2 constraints will typically yield dense oblique splits, which are less interpretable than axis-aligned or sparse oblique nodes. The conclusion mentions sparsity-inducing regularization; a small experiment with L1 or group-sparse penalties (with accuracy/complexity trade-offs) would be valuable.
- Scope of claims: The paper discusses applicability to forests but does not evaluate ensembles. A brief experiment demonstrating whether non-greedy training improves over greedy trees within a bagging or random-subspace ensemble (with fixed compute) would bolster practical significance.
- Clarity: Sections 6.2/6.4 could be more explicit. A concise summary of loss-augmented inference, the Hamming-ball bound, and the resulting complexity would help. Pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference and for SSGD (with stopping criteria) would improve reproducibility.
- Regression: Although squared loss is discussed, all experiments are classification. A small regression result would support generality.

Quality
- The technical core is sound: the latent-variable reformulation, upper bounds (both full and Hamming-ball variants), and projected SGD are well justified. Proposition 1 and the regularization discussion are sensible, though the tension between tighter bounds and piecewise-constant behavior merits more empirical illustration.
- Empirical validation shows consistent gains over several baselines; however, additional ablations (initialization, effect of SSGD vs SGD, stronger pruning baselines) and reporting of variance/confidence intervals would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- Generally clear, with good notation and algorithmic flow. Improve presentation of the fast loss-augmented inference and SSGD; explicitly state which nodes receive updates per step and how inactive leaves are handled. Clarify dataset splits, hyperparameter search spaces, and compute budgets.

Originality
- The structured-prediction viewpoint and the Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference for trees appear novel and interesting. The work goes beyond prior nodewise CO2 and classical oblique trees, and differs from HME by retaining hard splits.

Significance
- If broadly adopted, this could improve single-tree accuracy and provide a principled alternative to greedy induction. The lack of forest experiments and limited analysis of optimization dynamics temper the immediate practical impact, but the idea is promising and likely to spur follow-up work.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled formulation with clear links to structured prediction.
- Efficient, scalable training with solid improvements over common baselines.
- Retains tree inference efficiency; broadly applicable to different losses.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental validation is limited in scope; missing stronger/practical baselines and ensembles.
- Some ambiguity in routing semantics (sgn at zero) and inactive leaf handling.
- Interpretability and sparsity trade-offs not explored.
- Sensitivity to initialization and update dynamics not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: initialization (CO2/random/zero), with/without SSGD, effect of ν beyond accuracy (e.g., number of active leaves).
- Strengthen baselines with thorough pruning and additional simple global strategies; if space permits, a small forest experiment.
- Clarify tie-breaking at zero and define explicit pruning/splitting behavior.
- Include a sparsity experiment (L1 or group lasso) to assess interpretability–accuracy trade-off.
- Provide pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference and SSGD; report runtime and memory vs depth.
- Consider a regression task to demonstrate generality.

Overall recommendation: borderline reject. The paper is clever and potentially impactful, but the current experimental scope and some practical ambiguities leave me unconvinced about readiness. With stronger empirical validation and clarified training behavior, this could be a solid NIPS contribution.

Confidence: 0.7 (familiar with decision trees, structured prediction, and optimization; did not reimplement).
