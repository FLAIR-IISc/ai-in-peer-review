This paper proposes a novel approach to learning decision trees by framing the search for the best fixed-depth decision tree as a structured prediction problem. The authors establish a link between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, and formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. The proposed method, called non-greedy decision tree learning, uses stochastic gradient descent (SGD) to optimize the upper bound and find a locally optimal configuration of the split functions.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and its relation to previous work. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method and demonstrate its efficiency on several benchmark datasets.

The main contribution of this paper is the formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem, which enables the use of efficient optimization techniques such as SGD. The proposed method is shown to outperform typical greedy tree-building methods in terms of test accuracy, although the improvements are generally small.

One of the strengths of this paper is its thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method. The authors provide a detailed breakdown of the time complexity of each component of the algorithm and demonstrate that the proposed method is efficient even for deep trees.

Another strength of this paper is its experimental evaluation. The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of the proposed method on several benchmark datasets and compare its performance to that of several baseline methods. The results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms the baseline methods in terms of test accuracy.

One potential weakness of this paper is that the proposed method is limited to binary classification problems. While the authors mention that the method can be extended to multi-class classification problems, they do not provide any experimental results to support this claim.

Another potential weakness of this paper is that the proposed method requires careful tuning of several hyperparameters, including the regularization parameter ν and the learning rate η. While the authors provide some guidance on how to tune these hyperparameters, they do not provide a thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the proposed method to these hyperparameters.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning and demonstrates the potential of framing decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem. The proposed method is efficient, effective, and easy to implement, making it a valuable addition to the toolkit of machine learning practitioners.
