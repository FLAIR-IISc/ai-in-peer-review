This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy learning of decision trees by reformulating the optimization problem as latent structured prediction using sign vectors as latent variables. The authors introduce a non-differentiable, non-convex loss and approximate it with a differentiable (still non-convex) upper bound, which is then minimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The main contributions of the paper are casting decision tree optimization as structured prediction and developing efficient optimization algorithms.

The approach is novel, interesting, and raises several follow-up questions. One of the key strengths of the paper is its ability to link decision tree learning to structured prediction, which enables the use of efficient optimization algorithms. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, which is essential for understanding its scalability.

However, there are several weaknesses in the paper. One of the main concerns is the ambiguity about zero-weight nodes, which is problematic and should be addressed. The authors imply taking the right branch, but this is not explicitly stated, and it is unclear how this would affect the optimization process. Additionally, the paper does not provide sufficient detail on the baselines and pruning strategies used in the experiments, which makes it difficult to compare the results.

Another concern is the use of L2 regularization, which may yield dense, non-interpretable splits. The authors should consider using L1 regularization instead, which could enforce sparsity but may degrade performance. This tradeoff must be tested and discussed in the paper.

The experimental evaluation is also limited, and the authors should consider comparing their method to additional simple global strategies, such as multiple greedy trees on random subsets, as fair baselines. Furthermore, the title of the paper mentions forests, but the paper does not address or evaluate forests, which is misleading.

In terms of clarity, the inference descriptions in Sections 6.2 and 6.4 could be summarized or made more explicit. The paper is well-written, but some of the sections are dense and require multiple readings to understand.

Overall, the paper presents a clever and efficient reformulation of decision tree optimization, but the practical usefulness and experimental validation are currently unconvincing. The authors should address the concerns mentioned above and provide more detailed experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.
