

# Review for "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a novel approach to decision tree learning by formulating the global optimization of split functions as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent. A key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity of gradient updates from exponential in tree depth (O(2^dp)) to O(d^2p), making training of deep trees feasible. The paper builds on related work in structured prediction (e.g., Yu and Joachims, ICML 2009; Taskar et al., NIPS 2003) and previous attempts at non-greedy tree learning (Bennett, 1999; Murthy and Salzburg, 1994), while addressing their limitations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Elegant formulation:** The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and well-executed. The paper clearly establishes how the binary latent variables (representing split decisions) enable formulating the tree's empirical loss as a structured prediction problem.

2. **Technical contribution:** The complexity reduction from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) is significant and well-justified. The fast loss-augmented inference algorithm (Section 6.2) is a key innovation that makes the approach practical.

3. **Theoretical grounding:** The paper provides solid theoretical analysis of the upper bound (Proposition 1) and demonstrates how norm regularization of the split parameters can improve generalization.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Empirical results are unconvincing:** The claimed advantages of non-greedy trees over greedy baselines are not consistently demonstrated across datasets. As noted in the key points, the performance gains are only significant on a few datasets, and the paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of when the approach truly excels. The comparison with CO2 (a greedy variant of their method) is interesting but doesn't sufficiently demonstrate the value of global optimization.

2. **Stable SGD description is unclear:** The paper introduces Stable SGD (SSGD) in Section 6.3 to address the issue of inactive leaves, but the description lacks sufficient detail. It's unclear how data points are assigned to leaves, what the "leaf assignment constraints" are, and how the algorithm transitions between leaf assignments. A pseudo-code for SSGD would significantly improve clarity.

3. **Figure 2 needs clarification:** It's not specified whether the results in Figure 2 use standard SGD or SSGD, and the meaning of "active leaves" (mentioned on page 10) isn't defined in the main text. This ambiguity undermines the interpretation of the results.

4. **Algorithm 1 requires clarification:** Line 10 updates Θ using the gradient of the loss, but the paper doesn't explain how Θ is projected onto the simplex in practice (as noted in the key points). The claim that this enforces each row summing to one needs explicit justification.

5. **Minor issues:** The paper contains several typos and unclear phrasings (e.g., lines 141, 238, 301, 404), and the supplementary material requires corrections (line 29 sign notation, line 30 loss formulation). The reference to Figure 1 in the text is confusing as the figure is not properly referenced.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and contains a significant theoretical contribution. However, the empirical validation is insufficient to fully support the claims of superior performance.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured but contains several sections that require clarification (SSGD, active leaves, Θ projection). The writing is generally clear, though some minor phrasing issues and typos detract from the overall presentation.

**Originality:** The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is novel, and the complexity reduction technique represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The paper properly situates itself within the literature on non-greedy tree learning.

**Significance:** While decision trees are widely used, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method provides significant practical advantages over existing approaches. The computational cost of the method (O(d^2p) vs. O(dp) for greedy methods) and the limited empirical gains suggest that the approach may not be broadly applicable.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel theoretical connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, with a significant technical contribution in reducing computational complexity. The method is well-motivated and theoretically sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The empirical evidence does not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over greedy baselines across multiple datasets. The computational cost is higher than greedy methods, and the paper fails to clearly demonstrate when the proposed approach should be preferred.

**Recommendation:** The paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound approach, but the empirical results don't sufficiently support the claimed advantages. The authors should address the clarity issues (SSGD, active leaves, Θ projection), provide a more comprehensive comparison across datasets, and clarify the computational trade-offs. With these improvements, the paper would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper is borderline for acceptance. I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that the core idea has merit and could be strengthened with additional experimental evidence and clarification.
