

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees" (NIPS 2015)

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a novel framework for training decision trees by jointly optimizing all split parameters and leaf posteriors through a single global optimization procedure, rather than the standard greedy approach. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. This bound enables efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent with O(d²p) complexity per update, where d is tree depth and p is feature dimension. The paper builds upon prior work on structured prediction (e.g., Yu & Joachims, ICML 2009) and non-greedy tree optimization (Bennett, 1998), but makes significant advances in computational efficiency and generality by handling general loss functions and enabling deep tree training.

## Strengths

The paper's most significant strength is its original and important research direction: investigating joint training for decision trees, which addresses a fundamental limitation of greedy approaches. The theoretical formulation linking decision trees to structured prediction with latent variables is elegant and well-motivated. The authors provide a clear derivation of the upper bound and the efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm, which is well-written and technically sound. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines (including CO2, which is the greedy version of their method) on multiple classification benchmarks, suggesting the approach has practical value.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper contains several notable weaknesses that affect its significance and credibility.

First, there is a potential flaw in the theoretical reasoning. The argument around line 216 (Proposition 1) claims the upper bound becomes tighter as the norm of W increases, but an example shows the claimed upper bound may not hold. The proof in the supplement appears incomplete, as it fails to consider cases where the loss term dominates the score term, which can happen when the scale of W is increased. This undermines the theoretical foundation of the approach.

Second, the paper reports that some leaves receive no data points during training, which suggests potential issues with the objective or initialization. The fixed-assignment heuristic used in the Stable SGD variant seems suboptimal and lacks proper motivation; it would be more convincing to use EM or CCCP as a principled alternative for handling the latent variables.

Third, the necessity of the efficient loss-augmented inference is questionable. The paper states that depth ≤ 16 makes brute-force feasible, yet the Hamming ball restriction is claimed to be essential for efficiency. The impact of this restriction is unclear and requires broader validation across different tree depths and datasets.

Fourth, the paper lacks a critical baseline: no comparison to greedy training of non-axis-aligned splits without special initialization. This omission makes it difficult to assess whether the improvements come from the joint training or simply from using oblique splits.

Fifth, the paper fails to address the limitation that tree depth must be pre-specified, which is a significant constraint in practice. The authors should have conducted experiments varying the depth to evaluate how this choice affects performance, as this is a common practice in tree learning.

The paper also contains minor issues: the use of "argmax" in the loss bound expression should be "max" for mathematical accuracy, and the terminology around "leaves" in the inference discussion requires clarification.

## Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting and important research direction in decision tree learning, but its current form has significant theoretical and empirical weaknesses that need to be addressed. The core idea of joint training is valuable, but the theoretical foundation is questionable, and the empirical evaluation is incomplete.

The authors should address the theoretical flaw in Proposition 1 with a corrected proof or alternative argument, provide the missing baseline comparison, conduct experiments varying tree depth, and clarify the terminology. If these issues can be resolved in a rebuttal, this paper could become a strong contribution to the field.

Given the current state of the paper, I recommend a marginal rejection. While the research direction is important and the core idea has merit, the current theoretical and empirical shortcomings significantly reduce the paper's impact and credibility. The authors have an opportunity to address these issues, but as presented, the paper does not yet meet NIPS standards for technical soundness and completeness.

## Final Assessment

Quality: 6/10 (Theoretical flaws and incomplete empirical evaluation undermine the paper's technical soundness)
Clarity: 8/10 (Well-written, but some terminology needs clarification)
Originality: 8/10 (Novel approach to joint tree training)
Significance: 5/10 (Important direction, but current implementation has notable shortcomings)

Overall: The paper presents a promising research direction but currently lacks the technical rigor and completeness expected for NIPS. A revised version addressing the identified weaknesses would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
