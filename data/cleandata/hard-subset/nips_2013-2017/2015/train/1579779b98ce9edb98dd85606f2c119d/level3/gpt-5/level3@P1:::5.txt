Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy training algorithm for decision trees with oblique (linear) splits by jointly optimizing all split parameters and leaf parameters under a global objective. The key technical idea is to cast the split decisions as latent binary variables, linking tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and optimize it with stochastic gradient descent (SGD) under per-row L2 norm constraints on W (one weight vector per internal node). They further introduce a fast loss-augmented inference procedure by restricting to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current split decisions, reducing per-step complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) while keeping inference at test time O(d p). A “Stable SGD” (SSGD) variant fixes leaf assignments periodically to stabilize training.

This connects to classic tree induction (CART/ID3), oblique trees (OC1), margin-based split learning (Bennett et al.), and soft trees/HME. It also relates to CO2 (continuous optimization of oblique splits at depth-1) and structural SVM with latent variables. The formulation generalizes to arbitrary losses (e.g., log loss, squared loss), preserving tree-time inference.

Strengths
- Elegant and principled formulation: the latent-structured view of split decisions yields a clean surrogate objective and naturally accommodates different loss functions.
- Efficient per-example optimization: the radius-1 loss-augmented inference brings training to O(d^2 p), maintaining O(d p) test-time complexity.
- Clear motivation for norm constraints and their role in tightening/loosening the bound and controlling generalization.
- Practical training tricks (initialization via greedy/CO2, projection onto per-row L2 balls, momentum/minibatches) make the method usable.
- On several benchmarks, non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines of similar depth and appear less prone to overfitting.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Empirical support is mixed: improvements over strong greedy baselines (OC1, random oblique) appear substantial only on a subset of datasets; elsewhere the margins are small and training is more computationally intensive. Please quantify training-time and wall-clock trade-offs versus each baseline for matched accuracy.
- Stable SGD (SSGD) is under-specified. Please:
  - Define precisely how leaf assignments a(x) are initialized, updated (frequency/criterion), and whether assignment uses sgn(Wx) or an alternative.
  - Provide pseudo-code, including stopping criteria and how inactive leaves are handled.
  - Clarify “active” vs “inactive” leaves, and report how their counts evolve during training.
- Figure 2: specify whether curves use SGD or SSGD (or both), and ensure the notion of “active leaves” is defined if referenced.
- Algorithm 1, line 10: Θ is described as unnormalized log-probabilities (θj) with a softmax at the leaf. If so, no simplex projection is needed. If instead Θ is intended to be probabilities, please explain the projection operation (method and frequency) and clarify the variable semantics throughout to avoid confusion.
- Missing ablations and baselines:
  - Compare the full bound (Eq. 7) vs the Hamming-ball bound (Eq. 13): what accuracy/training-time trade-off results?
  - Report the effect of ν and its interaction with depth (accuracy vs number of active leaves).
  - Compare to soft decision trees/HME trained by EM, and to strong greedy ensembles (e.g., gradient-boosted trees) under a fixed test-time budget or fixed number of tree evaluations. Even if the focus is single trees, such comparisons contextualize significance.
- Theory and optimization: beyond Proposition 1 and bound correctness, discuss convergence properties of SGD/SSGD on the non-convex objective, and provide empirical evidence of stability (e.g., loss curves, variance across runs).

Quality
- The surrogate objective and its derivation are sound, and the complexity reduction via radius-1 inference is well motivated. However, the experimental section should better substantiate when and why the non-greedy optimization delivers meaningful gains, with stronger baselines and clearer compute/accuracy trade-offs. More ablations (ν, initialization, SGD vs SSGD) would strengthen causal claims.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but several places need tightening (see minor issues). The SSGD description is currently too high-level; pseudo-code and clear definitions are needed. Please also detail data preprocessing, split of train/val/test per dataset, and hyperparameter search ranges.

Originality
- The latent-structured view and the specific upper-bound formulation for trees, coupled with an efficient radius-1 loss-augmented inference, are novel in this context. While inspired by structural SVM/latent-SVM, the adaptation to hard-split trees with O(d p) inference is interesting.

Significance
- Potentially impactful if the gains over strong baselines are consistent, given trees’ importance in large-scale ML and vision. As presented, the significance is moderate; stronger empirical evidence and clearer guidance on when non-greedy training pays off would elevate the contribution.

Specific questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to initialization (axis-aligned vs CO2 vs random W)?
- Does ν effectively act as implicit pruning? Can you report accuracy vs number of active leaves for varying ν?
- What are memory and training-time footprints relative to OC1 and random oblique trees at depth 12–18 on the largest datasets?
- Any failure modes where SSGD collapses many leaves to inactive? How to prevent irreversible deactivation?

Minor issues and typos
- Equations (7)–(9): “max” should be used (not “argmax”) on the RHS of the bound/objective.
- Figure 1 is introduced but not referenced later; refer to it where f(·) is defined.
- Several corrupted/misspelled sentences (e.g., lines 141, 238, 301, 404) and an undefined acronym “FSGD” (line 360).
- Lines 407–408: phrasing about features is confusing—please reword.
- Supplementary: line 29 sign notation should be “=”, not “∈”; line 30 replace sgn(Wx) inside the loss with Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)) to be consistent with the main text.

Pros
- Principled global objective with a tractable, efficient surrogate.
- Maintains O(d p) test-time inference; handles general losses.
- Regularization provides a clear knob for generalization and implicit pruning.

Cons
- Mixed empirical gains and higher training cost.
- SSGD and some implementation details unclear.
- Missing ablations and comparisons to strong alternatives.

Overall
A solid and elegant formulation whose impact depends on stronger empirical validation and clearer presentation. With clarified SSGD, additional ablations, and more comprehensive comparisons, this could be a valuable contribution; as is, I view it as borderline.
