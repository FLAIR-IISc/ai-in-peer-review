

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a novel connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized using stochastic gradient descent. Their key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity of the gradient update from O(2^dp) to O(d²p), enabling training of deep trees. The method outperforms greedy decision tree baselines across multiple classification benchmarks, with consistent improvements in test accuracy, particularly in reducing overfitting.

The paper relates well to previous work on non-greedy tree optimization. It contrasts with Bennett's multi-linear programming approach [1], which was limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss and had high computational complexity. It also distinguishes itself from soft-split methods like Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13], which sacrifice computational efficiency for smoothness. The authors properly acknowledge related work on margin-based tree learning [2, 3] and the inherent NP-completeness of optimal tree construction [11].

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a rigorous derivation of the convex-concave upper bound and thorough experimental validation. The authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses, noting that improvements over greedy methods are "generally small" but consistent across datasets. The empirical results on SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST benchmarks demonstrate that non-greedy trees achieve better generalization performance at various tree depths, with the regularization parameter ν providing a principled way to control overfitting.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical development to experimental validation. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and fast loss-augmented inference) are sufficiently detailed to understand the implementation. The connection between decision trees and structured prediction is well-motivated, though the paper could provide more intuition on why the non-greedy approach leads to better generalization.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing the structured prediction connection and developing an efficient upper bound formulation. While some prior work has explored non-greedy tree optimization, the authors' specific approach to reducing computational complexity represents a meaningful advancement. The paper properly cites all relevant work, including Bennett's earlier global tree optimization work and the Hierarchical Mixture of Experts model.

Significance: Decision trees remain widely used in practice due to their interpretability and efficiency, yet greedy construction has remained the standard despite known limitations. This paper addresses a fundamental limitation that has persisted for decades. While the performance improvements are modest, they are consistent and meaningful in the context of a well-studied problem. The framework could potentially be extended to other tree-based methods, and the authors provide promising directions for future work (e.g., sparse split functions, kernel methods).

Strengths: 
- Novel theoretical connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction
- Efficient algorithm with O(d²p) complexity for gradient updates
- Consistent empirical improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets
- Clear presentation of the method and its relationship to prior work
- Careful evaluation of strengths and limitations

Weaknesses:
- Performance gains over greedy methods are modest (though consistent)
- Limited discussion of why the non-greedy approach leads to better generalization
- Focus on classification tasks with limited exploration of regression applications
- The connection to structured prediction is technically correct but doesn't provide significant conceptual novelty beyond the specific upper bound formulation

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a technically sound approach to a long-standing problem in decision tree learning. The method is theoretically grounded, well-implemented, and demonstrated on multiple benchmarks with consistent improvements. The paper acknowledges limitations while clearly articulating the significance of its contribution to the field.

Arguments against acceptance: The performance improvements are relatively small, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the non-greedy approach works better. The connection to structured prediction, while correct, doesn't seem to offer substantial conceptual novelty beyond the specific optimization technique.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that advances the state of the art in decision tree learning. While the performance gains may not be dramatic, they are consistent and meaningful in the context of a problem that has been studied for decades. The paper's theoretical insights and practical implementation make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
