

This paper proposes an elegant reformulation of decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, using sign vectors to represent latent decisions at each node. The authors introduce a novel convex-concave upper bound on the non-differentiable loss function, enabling efficient optimization via SGD with loss-augmented inference. The key technical contribution lies in developing an O(d²p) algorithm for loss-augmented inference, making deep tree optimization feasible. The paper demonstrates that each SGD step only updates a single node's weights, suggesting potential for adaptive tree growth from zero weights.

The work is highly original, connecting decision tree learning to structured prediction in a way that opens new research directions. The efficiency gains for deep trees are significant, and the theoretical foundation is solid, with clear justification for the upper bound and regularization. The approach represents a meaningful departure from greedy tree induction methods.

However, several concerns undermine the paper's practical impact. The ambiguity around zero-weight nodes (where the paper assumes taking the right branch) creates interpretability issues, especially since decision trees are often used for their transparency. The use of L2 regularization likely produces dense, non-interpretable splits, contradicting a primary motivation for using decision trees. The paper fails to explore L1 regularization, which could enforce sparsity while maintaining interpretability, and the experimental section lacks sufficient detail on pruning strategies for baseline methods.

The title's mention of "forests" is misleading as the paper evaluates only single trees. The experimental validation is weak, with insufficient comparison to simple global baselines like multiple greedy trees on random subsets. Clarity issues persist in describing inference procedures (Sections 6.2 and 6.4), and the paper doesn't address whether the method behaves like greedy strategies or prefers adding nodes over revising existing ones.

While the theoretical contribution is strong and the optimization framework clever, the practical usefulness remains unconvincing due to interpretability concerns and experimental shortcomings. The paper would benefit from addressing these issues before being accepted as a significant contribution to the field.
