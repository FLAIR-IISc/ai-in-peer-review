

This paper proposes a novel approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables, enabling global optimization of split functions rather than the conventional greedy approach. The authors develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), with computational complexity O(d²p) for trees of depth d. The key innovation lies in connecting decision tree optimization to structured prediction, which provides a principled framework for global optimization.

The paper's main strength is its elegant theoretical formulation, which successfully bridges decision tree learning with structured prediction. This connection is well-motivated and represents a significant conceptual advance over prior work on non-greedy tree learning (e.g., Bennett [1]). The technical development of the upper bound and loss-augmented inference is sound, and the complexity analysis is thorough.

However, the experimental evaluation suffers from several critical weaknesses. The empirical results (Figure 2) are unconvincing: the proposed non-greedy approach only significantly outperforms greedy baselines on a few datasets, while being substantially more computationally intensive (Figure 4). The paper fails to clearly state whether the results in Figure 2 use standard SGD or the proposed Stable SGD (SSGD), and does not define "active" leaves. The SSGD procedure is described ambiguously without sufficient detail on data point assignment to leaves or a clear pseudo-code. Algorithm 1 (line 10) lacks explanation of how Θ is projected onto the simplex, which is crucial for understanding the implementation.

The paper also contains numerous minor issues: confusing phrasing about features, incorrect use of "argmax" in equations, undefined acronym FSGD, and several typos. Supplementary material requires corrections to sign notation and loss function formulation.

While the theoretical contribution is elegant, the experimental evidence does not convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of global optimization over greedy methods. The paper fails to establish that the computational overhead is justified by meaningful performance gains across multiple datasets. Therefore, despite the strong theoretical foundation, the experimental weaknesses significantly undermine the paper's significance and quality. The paper would benefit substantially from more comprehensive experiments demonstrating clear advantages of the global approach, better clarification of the SSGD procedure, and correction of the numerous technical and typographical errors.
