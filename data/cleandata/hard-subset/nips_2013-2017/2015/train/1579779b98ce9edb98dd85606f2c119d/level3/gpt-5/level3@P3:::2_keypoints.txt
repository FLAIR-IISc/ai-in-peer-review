- Paper proposes joint non-greedy training of decision trees by treating internal splits as latent binary variables and optimizing a surrogate upper-bound with stochastic optimization.  
- Connects decision-tree training to latent structured prediction and proposes exact loss-augmented inference and a Hamming-ball restricted fast variant.  
- Supports oblique (linear) splits and uses per-row norm constraints on W for regularization and bound control.  
- Empirical results show improvements over several greedy baselines on multiclass datasets.  

Strengths:
- Addresses an important, timely problem: joint non-greedy tree optimization.  
- Clear conceptual unification of routing and leaf estimation via a latent-variable view.  
- Computationally appealing: per-example gradient cost O(d) and Hamming-ball inference O(d^2 p) for moderate depths.  
- Principled regularization via per-row norm constraints.  
- Reported empirical gains over greedy baselines across datasets and depths.  

Weaknesses — Theory and bounds:
- The empirical loss bound notation/argument is confusing; argmax/max misuse and possible counterexamples require correction.  
- Proposition 1 proof sketch is incomplete; monotonicity wrt scaling W needs rigorous justification.  
- Fast Hamming-ball bound justification is terse; precise maximization set, formal proof, and bias/impact on convergence are not provided.  

Weaknesses — Optimization behavior:
- Many leaves become inactive during training, suggesting objective pathology or sensitivity to initialization.  
- SSGD “fixed-assignment” heuristic is poorly motivated; a clearer EM/CCCP-style alternating optimization would strengthen claims.  
- Necessity and empirical benefit of Hamming-ball inference unclear for depths ≤16; comparison to brute-force needed.  

Weaknesses — Experimental evaluation:
- Missing strong greedy oblique baseline (e.g., logistic/hinge splits via L-BFGS with restarts) to isolate joint training benefits.  
- Depth pre-specified without model-selection experiments or adaptive pruning regularizers.  
- Limited runtime reporting (Fig.4 only); need comprehensive runtime vs depth and method comparisons with wall-clock times.  
- Ablation studies lacking: sensitivity to ν, learning rate, initialization, batch size, momentum, and effects on active leaves.  
- Unclear whether leaf posteriors are calibrated; no standard deviations reported over runs.  

Clarity issues:
- Ambiguity in “leaves” definition in inference (on-path siblings vs any ≤1-bit differing leaves).  
- Loss-bound derivations should be self-contained in main paper and argmax/max issue fixed.  
- Need pseudo-code for SSGD with explicit objective during fixed-assignment phases.  

Originality:
- Joint latent-structured surrogate for whole-tree training is original and extends prior CO2/latent-SSVM ideas to efficient inference approximations.  

Significance:
- Potentially significant if theoretical issues are resolved and experiments strengthened; current unclarities and limited validation reduce impact.  

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel direction, clear formulation, efficient algorithm, and promising empirical results.  

Cons for acceptance:
- Doubts about correctness/completeness of loss bounds and Proposition 1.  
- Heuristic SSGD insufficiently justified; inactive leaves indicate instability.  
- Missing strong baselines and limited runtime/ablation evidence; unclear benefit of Hamming-ball beyond small depths.  

Questions for authors:
- Provide rigorous, self-contained proofs of main bounds and Prop. 1, or correct statements and conditions.  
- Quantify the effect of Hamming-ball radius r on accuracy, convergence, and runtime; compare to exact inference for depths ≤16.  
- Report frequency of inactive leaves under different ν and initializations and whether entropy/flow regularizers help.  
- Add the missing greedy oblique baseline and report results.  
- Relate SSGD to EM/CCCP and provide monotonicity guarantees for the surrogate.  

Minor suggestions:
- Replace argmax with max in the loss bound expression to avoid confusion.  
- Explicitly enumerate the d+1 candidates considered under B1 and clarify “leaves” in inference.  

Overall recommendation:
- Weak reject: promising and valuable direction but requires clarifications on theoretical guarantees and substantially stronger empirical validation to be acceptable.
