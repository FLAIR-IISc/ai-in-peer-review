This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy learning of decision trees by jointly optimizing the split functions at all levels of the tree under a global objective. The authors establish a link between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, which enables efficient learning of deep trees. The proposed solution is valuable, but it has some notable shortcomings that affect its significance.

The paper is well-written overall, but it contains potential flaws in the theoretical reasoning. The derivation is clean, but the experiments and their presentation need improvement. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several classification benchmarks, showing that the resulting non-greedy decision trees outperform greedy decision tree baselines. However, the comparison to a greedy training of non-axis-aligned splits without special initialization is missing.

One of the key contributions of this work is the formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The authors propose a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, which acts as a surrogate objective that is optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The use of SGD enables effective training with large datasets.

The paper also presents an efficient algorithm for loss-augmented inference, which is a key component of the proposed approach. The algorithm has a time complexity of O(d2p), making it feasible for deep trees. However, the necessity and impact of the efficient loss-augmented inference are questioned, as the depth of the tree is limited to 16, making brute-force feasible.

The authors also propose a variant of SGD, called Stable SGD (SSGD), which changes the assignment of data points to leaves more conservatively than SGD. SSGD relies on a different upper bound on loss, which is larger than the original bound.

The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, but they have some limitations. The authors only evaluate the performance of their approach on a limited number of datasets, and the comparison to other methods is not comprehensive. The presentation of the results could be improved, with more detailed analysis and discussion of the findings.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, but the claims are not well-supported by theoretical analysis or experimental results. The authors are careful about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work, but the paper is not a complete piece of work, as it lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but it could be improved. The organization is good, but some sections could be more clearly connected. The paper adequately informs the reader, but some terminology could be clarified, such as the meaning of "leaves" in the context of the inference discussion.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes an important and original research direction, investigating joint training for decision trees. The approach is novel, and the authors provide a unique perspective on decision tree learning. The related work is adequately referenced, and the authors clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions.

In terms of significance, the results are important, but the paper has some limitations. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, but the evaluation is limited, and the comparison to other methods is not comprehensive. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, but the impact of the proposed approach is not fully explored.

Overall, this paper proposes a valuable approach to decision tree learning, but it has some notable shortcomings that affect its significance. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, but the evaluation is limited, and the comparison to other methods is not comprehensive. With some improvements in the theoretical reasoning, experiments, and presentation, this paper could make a more significant contribution to the field.
