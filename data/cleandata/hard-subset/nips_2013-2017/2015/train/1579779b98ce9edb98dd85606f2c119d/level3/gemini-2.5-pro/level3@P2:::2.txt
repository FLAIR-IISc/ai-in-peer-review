This paper presents a novel framework for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The core idea is to jointly optimize all split functions (oblique splits) and leaf node parameters under a single global objective function. To make this challenging optimization problem tractable, the authors establish a connection to structured prediction with latent variables. They formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which can then be optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for the "loss-augmented inference" step, reducing its complexity from exponential to quadratic in the tree depth, thereby enabling the training of deeper trees. The method is evaluated on several multi-class classification benchmarks, where it is shown to outperform greedy baselines, including axis-aligned trees and the greedy oblique tree optimizer CO2.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles the important and long-standing problem of non-greedy decision tree induction. Moving beyond the locally-optimal, node-by-node greedy paradigm is a significant research direction. The proposed framework, which casts the problem as a global optimization of a surrogate loss function, is both elegant and novel. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables provides a principled foundation for this approach.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The formulation of the decision tree in terms of latent binary variables that guide the path to a leaf is insightful and effectively sets up the subsequent derivation of the surrogate objective.

3.  **Potential Impact:** If the method proves to be technically sound and robust, it could have a substantial impact. A principled way to train more optimal, compact decision trees could benefit numerous applications that rely on tree-based models, from computer vision to general machine learning tasks.

### Weaknesses and Questions for Authors

Despite the promising direction, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form, spanning theoretical soundness, methodological choices, and experimental evaluation.

1.  **Technical Soundness of the Loss Bound:** The central contribution of the paper is the surrogate objective in Eq. (9), which relies on the upper bound in Eq. (7). I have concerns about the validity of this bound. The argument for it is brief, and it is not immediately obvious that it holds for all g. For instance, if `g` directs the sample `x` to a leaf with a very high loss `l(ΘTf(g), y)`, it is not clear that the term `gTWx` will be large enough to compensate, especially if `g` and `sgn(Wx)` are very different. Could the authors provide a more rigorous, step-by-step proof? Furthermore, the proof for Proposition 1 provided in the supplementary material appears incomplete and does not fully justify the claim. These are critical points that undermine the paper's theoretical foundation.

2.  **Optimization Issues and Heuristics:** The authors report that during training, some leaf nodes may become "inactive" (i.e., receive no data points). This suggests a potential issue with the optimization landscape or the objective function itself. The proposed solution, Stable SGD (SSGD), feels like an ad-hoc fix. Fixing the data-to-leaf assignments for several iterations seems suboptimal and lacks strong theoretical motivation. Have the authors considered more principled approaches to address this instability, such as a coordinate-descent-like procedure (e.g., CCCP) or an EM-style algorithm that alternates between updating assignments and optimizing parameters?

3.  **Justification for Fast Loss-Augmented Inference:** The paper introduces a "fast" loss-augmented inference method (Eq. 13) by restricting the search for `g` to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around `sgn(Wx)`. While this reduces complexity, the practical necessity and impact are not well-supported by the experiments. The experiments are conducted on trees with a maximum depth of 18, where the brute-force O(2^d) approach might still be feasible. The paper lacks a clear analysis of the trade-off: how much accuracy is lost by this approximation? A more thorough validation is needed to demonstrate that this speed-up is both necessary and does not significantly harm performance, perhaps by showing results on much deeper trees where the original inference is truly intractable.

4.  **Insufficient Experimental Comparison:**
    *   **Missing Baseline:** The experiments compare against axis-aligned trees and CO2, which is used for initialization. However, a crucial baseline is missing: a standard greedy algorithm for learning oblique splits that is *not* initialized with an axis-aligned tree. This would help isolate the benefits of the non-greedy global optimization from the benefits of the specific initialization pipeline.
    *   **Fixed Tree Depth:** The method requires the tree depth to be pre-specified. This is a significant limitation compared to greedy methods that can grow the tree dynamically. The paper should discuss this limitation more explicitly and include experiments that analyze the model's sensitivity to the choice of depth.

### Minor Points and Suggestions

*   In the sentence preceding Eq. (7), it might be clearer to state that the loss is upper-bounded by the *value* of the maximization problem, rather than the `argmax`. For instance, "is upper bounded by `max_{g in Hm} (...)`".
*   In the discussion of loss-augmented inference, the terminology could be more precise. What exactly is meant by "off-the-path bits"? Clarification would be helpful.

### Summary

In conclusion, the paper presents a highly original and interesting approach to a fundamental problem in machine learning. However, there are serious concerns about the technical correctness of the core loss bound, the ad-hoc nature of the SSGD heuristic, and the thoroughness of the experimental validation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and challenging problem with a novel, principled approach.
*   The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction is insightful.
*   The method shows promising empirical results compared to standard greedy techniques.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Potential flaw in the theoretical foundation (the upper bound in Eq. 7).
*   The SSGD heuristic to deal with inactive leaves seems ad-hoc and is not well-motivated.
*   The practical impact of the "fast inference" approximation is not clearly demonstrated.
*   The experimental evaluation is missing key baselines and a discussion of important limitations (e.g., fixed depth).

I am currently leaning towards rejection, but I am willing to reconsider my score if the authors can provide a convincing rebuttal that rigorously addresses the concerns about the theoretical soundness of the loss bound and provides stronger experimental justification for their methodological choices.
