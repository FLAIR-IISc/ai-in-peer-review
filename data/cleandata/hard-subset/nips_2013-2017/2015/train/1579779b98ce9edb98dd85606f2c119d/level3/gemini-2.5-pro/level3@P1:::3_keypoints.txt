- Novel formulation: casts learning oblique decision trees as a latent structured prediction problem over sequences of binary node decisions.  
- Surrogate objective: derives a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss (inspired by latent-SSVMs) for joint learning of splits and leaves.  
- Optimization: uses SGD to jointly learn oblique hyperplanes (W) and leaf parameters (Θ).  
- Computational trick: reduces loss-augmented inference from exponential in depth to polynomial O(d^2 p) using a restricted Hamming-ball search.  
- Relation to prior work: departs from greedy methods (CART/ID3) and scales better than earlier global approaches, connecting to margin-based/tree-SVM and structured prediction literature.  
- Originality/significance: provides a principled, regularized global objective for a problem usually solved with heuristics.  
- Technical soundness: derivations and the fast inference insight are well-motivated and correct; SGD is appropriate for large-scale learning.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and explains the structured prediction connection clearly.  
- Empirical results: non-greedy trees consistently outperform several baselines on benchmarks, with reduced overfitting at larger depths.  
- Modest gains: test accuracy improvements are small (~1–2%), raising questions about practical trade-offs given higher training complexity.  
- Initialization sensitivity: non-convex optimization relies on strong greedy initialization (CO2), so gains may stem from initialization rather than global fine-tuning.  
- Limited evaluation: experiments only consider single trees and omit comparisons to ensemble methods (Random Forests, boosting), limiting practical assessment.  
- Unclear role of SSGD: the Stable SGD variant is introduced but its empirical impact versus standard SGD is not evaluated.
