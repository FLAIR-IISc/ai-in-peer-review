This paper proposes a novel approach to training decision trees by framing the problem as a structured prediction task with latent variables. The authors aim to overcome the limitations of traditional greedy algorithms, which often lead to suboptimal trees. They present a convex-concave upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss and utilize stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for efficient optimization, particularly for deep trees. Experimental results on several benchmark datasets demonstrate that the non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines, albeit with modest gains.

The work builds upon a rich history of decision tree research, stemming from foundational algorithms like ID3 [21] and CART [5], and extending to more recent advancements like random forests [4]. The core contribution lies in the re-formulation of the tree optimization problem, drawing inspiration from structured prediction [25, 24, 23], an area previously applied to tasks such as image segmentation and natural language processing. The paper correctly identifies the NP-completeness of finding optimal trees [11] as motivation for exploring non-greedy alternatives. Related work includes Bennett’s multi-linear programming approach [1], Mondrian Processes [15], and Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13], all of which attempt to address the limitations of greedy methods. The authors distinguish their work by providing an efficient learning framework suitable for large datasets and general loss functions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and provides a fresh perspective on a long-standing problem.
* **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate a reduction in computational complexity to O(d2p), enabling training of deeper trees, which is a significant improvement over naive approaches.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently show improvements over greedy baselines on multiple datasets, providing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The convex-concave upper bound provides a solid theoretical foundation for the optimization process.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the key ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Modest Gains:** While the proposed method consistently outperforms greedy baselines, the improvements in accuracy are relatively small. This raises the question of whether the added complexity is justified in all scenarios.
* **Initialization Dependency:** The method relies on initializing the tree with a greedy approach (CO2 [18]), which could potentially limit its ability to escape local optima. The sensitivity to initialization isn't thoroughly explored.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The regularization parameter ν plays a critical role in performance, and finding the optimal value requires careful tuning.  The paper acknowledges this but doesn't provide extensive guidance on the tuning process.
* **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons to several greedy methods are included, the paper doesn’t compare against more recent and powerful tree-based methods (e.g., extremely randomized trees or gradient boosted trees) which could provide a more robust benchmark.
* **Discussion of Practical Implications:** The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the practical implications of the findings, particularly regarding the trade-offs between accuracy, computational cost, and tree interpretability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to decision tree learning.
* The proposed method consistently demonstrates improved performance over greedy baselines.
* The scalability improvements enable training of deeper and potentially more accurate trees.
* The work addresses a long-standing challenge in the field of machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The improvements in accuracy are relatively modest.
* The method relies on careful hyperparameter tuning.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art tree-based methods is limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution that presents a novel and potentially valuable approach to decision tree learning. While the gains in accuracy are not dramatic, the theoretical grounding, scalability improvements, and consistent outperformance of greedy baselines make it a worthwhile addition to the literature. The clarity of the paper and the well-defined experimental setup are also commendable. I believe the paper is of sufficient quality for acceptance, with the caveat that the authors should consider addressing the weaknesses identified above in a revised version. Specifically, a more thorough comparison to stronger baselines and a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and practical considerations would strengthen the paper significantly.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
