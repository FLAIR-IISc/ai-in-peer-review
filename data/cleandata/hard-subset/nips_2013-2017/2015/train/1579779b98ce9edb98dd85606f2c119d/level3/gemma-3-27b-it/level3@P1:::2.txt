This paper introduces a novel approach to training decision trees by jointly optimizing split functions at all levels and leaf parameters using a global objective. This contrasts with the standard greedy algorithms commonly employed, which optimize splits node-by-node. The core contribution lies in formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables, leading to a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables tractable optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). While the idea of moving beyond greedy tree induction is valuable and potentially impactful, the paper exhibits several weaknesses in its theoretical justification, experimental validation, and presentation.

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and proposed methodology. It correctly identifies the limitations of greedy algorithms and positions the work within the broader context of structured prediction. The link between decision tree optimization and latent variable models is insightful.  However, a careful reading reveals potential flaws in the theoretical arguments. The reasoning surrounding line 216f is unclear, and the reviewer’s example suggests the claimed upper bound on the loss may not always hold. Furthermore, the proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material appears incomplete.  These theoretical inconsistencies raise concerns about the validity of the proposed approach.

The experimental section, while presenting results on benchmark datasets, is not entirely convincing.  A significant issue observed is that some leaves receive no training data points during optimization. This could indicate problems with the objective function, initialization procedure, or the chosen regularization scheme. The introduced Stable SGD (SSGD) heuristic, which aims to address this, feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks sufficient justification. A comparison to Expectation-Maximization (EM) or Capacitated Convex Concave Programming (CCCP) approaches could strengthen the motivation for this technique. Furthermore, the efficiency gains claimed from the “fast loss-augmented inference” are questionable given the relatively small tree depths (up to 16) explored. For these depths, a brute-force search is feasible, and the effect of restricting the search to a Hamming ball around the current solution requires more robust validation.  The discussion around the “leaves” in the context of inference also lacks clarity.

The originality of the work is noteworthy. Jointly optimizing all tree parameters represents a significant departure from the established greedy paradigm. The paper appropriately references related work, including earlier attempts at global tree optimization (e.g., Bennett [1]) and methods employing soft splits (e.g., Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13]). However, a key missing baseline is a comparison against a greedy training procedure using non-axis-aligned splits *without* the specialized initialization described in the paper. This would provide a clearer understanding of the benefit derived specifically from the proposed global optimization technique. Additionally, the pre-specification of tree depth is a limitation that should be addressed more thoroughly. Varying the depth parameter more systematically and discussing its impact on performance is crucial. 

The significance of the work is currently limited by the shortcomings mentioned above. While the proposed approach demonstrates some performance improvements over greedy baselines, the theoretical concerns and lack of thorough empirical validation prevent a strong assessment of its practical impact. The claim of overcoming overfitting with regularization is promising, but requires more in-depth analysis. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to decision tree learning with potential to overcome limitations of greedy algorithms.
*   Well-written and clearly presented methodology.
*   Demonstrates performance improvements over standard baselines on benchmark datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Potential flaws in the theoretical justification of the proposed upper bound.
*   Concerns about the robustness and effectiveness of the Stable SGD heuristic.
*   Questionable necessity and impact of the “fast loss-augmented inference.”
*   Missing baseline comparison to greedy non-axis-aligned split training.
*   Limited discussion and evaluation of the impact of tree depth.
*   Some leaves receiving no data points may indicate issues with the algorithm.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. However, the theoretical weaknesses and incomplete experimental evaluation raise serious concerns. I currently lean towards **rejecting** the paper in its current form. A strong rebuttal addressing the concerns raised, particularly regarding the theoretical justification, the rationale behind SSGD, and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation including the suggested baselines and a thorough investigation of tree depth, could potentially change this assessment.  I would be open to reconsidering the score after reviewing the rebuttal and any accompanying revisions.
