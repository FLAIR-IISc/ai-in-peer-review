Summary
This paper proposes a global, non-greedy method for learning decision trees with linear (oblique) split functions by optimizing a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss, regularized via per-row L2 constraints on W and optimized with stochastic gradient descent. A key conceptual contribution is to recast hard splits as latent binary variables, linking decision tree training to structured prediction with latent variables (cf. Yu & Joachims 2009). The authors derive two upper bounds that enable tractable “loss-augmented inference” and show how to reduce per-step complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) via a Hamming-ball-1 restriction. A “Stable SGD” (SSGD) variant aims to prevent leaves from becoming inactive during training by fixing data-to-leaf assignments for several updates. Experiments on several LibSVM datasets compare against axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and CO2 (a related greedy depth-1 bound-optimized baseline).

Relation to prior work
The formulation extends earlier global or lookahead tree methods (e.g., Bennett 2000; OC1), and relates to margin-based trees (Bennett et al. 2000). The latent structured perspective is in line with structural SVMs and latent SVMs (Taskar et al.; Tsochantaridis et al.; Yu & Joachims). Compared to HMEs/Jordan-Jacobs, the method preserves hard routing and tree-time inference while enabling global parameter updates.

Strengths
- Elegant reformulation: Casting oblique splits as latent codes h = sgn(Wx) and optimizing a tight, efficiently computable upper bound is conceptually clean and connects trees to well-studied structured prediction frameworks.
- Scalability insight: The Hamming-ball-1 bound yields O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference, making deeper trees feasible.
- Regularization motivation: The analysis of scale effects on the bound (Proposition 1) and the per-row L2 constraints on W provide a principled knob for controlling fit.
- Practicality: SGD-friendly gradients and sparse row updates are straightforward to implement.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evidence is unconvincing. The global optimization only substantially outperforms strong greedy baselines on a subset of datasets, and often by small margins; the computational cost is substantially higher. A single-tree regime is reasonable for isolating effects, but the method should also be evaluated against modern greedy oblique trees with thorough tuning and, crucially, against ensembles (RF/GBDT) at comparable inference budgets.
- Stable SGD (SSGD) is underspecified. It is a central component to avoid leaf collapse, yet the paper lacks a precise description of how assignments are maintained/updated and when. Without pseudocode, it is difficult to reproduce or attribute gains to SSGD versus the base SGD.
- Some notation/clarity issues (details below) impede reproducibility, including missing references to figures, undefined acronyms, and minor errors in the supplement.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the bounds are correct, the complexity arguments are plausible, and the optimization is consistent with the latent structured view. However, the experimental section does not adequately support the claim that global optimization is a clear win over well-tuned greedy methods, and runtimes are not reported in a way that enables cost–benefit comparisons at equal accuracy or equal inference cost.

Clarity
Generally clearly written with crisp motivation, but key procedural details are missing:
- SSGD needs explicit pseudocode, a definition of “active” leaves, and a precise schedule for freezing/unfreezing assignments.
- Figure 2 should state whether results are from SGD or SSGD and define “active” leaves. The text references “inactive” leaves; please quantify in plots or tables when/where this occurs and under which optimizer.
- Algorithm 1: line 10 suggests an unconstrained gradient step on Θ; elsewhere you imply projecting Θ. Please clarify. If you project θj to the probability simplex, state the projection used (e.g., Euclidean projection of [p(y|j)] with θj as probabilities rather than logits), note it enforces each row sums to one, and reconcile this with the softmax parameterization (with softmax, no simplex constraint is needed). If no projection is used, remove language implying projection.

Originality
The latent structured formulation for oblique trees and the specific Hamming-ball-1 upper bound for fast loss-augmented inference are novel and insightful. The connection to CO2 is acknowledged and extended from greedy depth-1 optimization to a global multi-level setting.

Significance
Potentially impactful if the global procedure consistently improves accuracy–depth–runtime trade-offs. As presented, gains are modest and inconsistent, and training cost is higher. Strengthening the empirical case (see suggestions) would significantly increase the paper’s significance.

Requests for clarification and reproducibility
- SSGD details and pseudocode:
  - Define a(x) precisely and how it is initialized (ĥ routing vs. leaf posterior argmax).
  - How long are assignments held fixed (fixed number of epochs/updates or validation-based criterion)? What is the “negligible improvement” threshold?
  - When assignments are fixed, which parameters are updated (both W and Θ)? Are updates restricted to paths/leaves currently active?
  - Pseudocode for one SSGD outer iteration with: assignment update; inner SGD steps with bound (14); stopping rules; mini-batch handling.
  - Complexity/runtime overhead of SSGD vs. SGD.
- Figure 2: Specify whether curves use SGD or SSGD; define “active leaf” explicitly (assigned ≥1 training point under current assignments); report proportion of active leaves per depth and ν.
- Algorithm 1:
  - Line 10: If projecting Θ onto a simplex, provide the exact projection algorithm (e.g., Condat 2016 or Duchi et al. 2008) and clarify whether rows of Θ are probabilities or logits. Otherwise, remove projection claims and keep Θ as unconstrained logits with softmax.
- Loss-augmented inference: Provide compact pseudocode for the Hamming-ball-1 variant and explicitly enumerate the d+1 candidates.

Experimental suggestions
- Add accuracy vs. training time plots and report wall-clock times for each method at each depth; include accuracy at equal training budgets.
- Include stronger greedy oblique baselines with robust hyperparameter search, and compare to small ensembles (e.g., RF/GBDT with comparable inference time to one deep oblique tree).
- Report statistical significance across multiple random splits; provide per-dataset active-leaf statistics.

Minor issues/typos
- Lines 407–408: confusing phrasing about features; please rephrase.
- Replace “argmax” with “max” in Eqs. (7)–(9) where referring to bound values, not argmax operators.
- Figure 1 is never referenced; add a reference where f(·) is introduced.
- Corrupted/misspelled sentences: lines 141, 238, 301, 404; please fix.
- Undefined acronym FSGD (line 360); define on first use or remove.
Supplement:
- Line 29: use “=” rather than “∈” for sign notation.
- Line 30: replace sgn(Wx) usage inside the loss with Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)) as per the main text.

Pros
- Elegant and principled formulation; clear connection to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient inference via Hamming-ball-1 bound; solid theoretical motivation for regularization.
- Practical SGD training with sparse updates.

Cons
- Empirical improvements over greedy trees are limited and not cost-normalized; runtime overhead is significant.
- SSGD and some implementation details are underspecified.
- Reproducibility is hindered by clarity/notation issues.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem formulation is elegant and potentially important, but the current experiments do not convincingly demonstrate a practical advantage over strong greedy baselines, and key algorithmic details (especially SSGD) need clarification. Strengthening the empirical evidence and tightening the exposition would make this a strong submission.
