

This paper presents an elegant approach to non-greedy optimization of decision trees through a novel convex-concave upper bound formulation, connecting decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a theoretical foundation for joint optimization of split functions across all tree levels, which represents a significant departure from conventional greedy approaches. The key insight is formulating the problem as a structured prediction task with latent variables, enabling efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent with O(d²p) complexity per gradient update (where d is tree depth and p is feature dimension).

The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and makes a technically sound contribution. The theoretical development is thorough, with appropriate connections to structured prediction literature (e.g., Yu & Joachims, 2009). The experimental results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms greedy baselines in test accuracy while being less prone to overfitting, particularly at deeper tree depths. The authors demonstrate the approach on multiple benchmark datasets, showing meaningful improvements over standard greedy methods.

Strengths include the clear theoretical motivation, elegant mathematical formulation, and the paper's accessibility to the broader ML community. The method's ability to handle any loss function while maintaining the tree's computational efficiency is particularly noteworthy. The paper's clarity is exceptional, providing sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The main weakness lies in the modest absolute performance gains (typically 1-3% over baselines), which may limit practical impact despite the methodological elegance. The paper does not sufficiently address whether the global optimization process might inadvertently increase overfitting despite regularization, as suggested in the key points. A more detailed analysis of the generalization gap across different tree depths would strengthen the claims.

A valuable suggestion for future work would be to post-optimize the last split and leaf node using standard methods, which could potentially yield additional improvements. Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that makes a meaningful theoretical advance in decision tree learning. While the experimental improvements are modest, the elegance of the approach and the potential for future extensions make this a strong candidate for publication. The work is clearly original, addresses an important problem, and will likely spur further research in non-greedy tree optimization.
