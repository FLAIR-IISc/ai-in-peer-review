## Review of “Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees”

This paper presents a novel approach to training decision trees by reformulating the optimization problem as a structured prediction task with latent variables representing split decisions. The core idea – casting global tree optimization within a structured prediction framework and leveraging efficient algorithms for loss-augmented inference – is compelling and offers a potentially significant departure from traditional greedy methods. The authors demonstrate a clever reduction in computational complexity, achieving O(d2p) per gradient update, enabling training of deeper trees. 

The work builds upon established concepts in structured prediction (e.g., [25, 24]) and connects them to the well-studied problem of decision tree induction. The introduction of a differentiable upper bound on the non-convex loss is a key technical contribution, allowing for the application of stochastic gradient descent. The sparse gradient updates, where each step primarily affects a single node’s weights, are a particularly interesting property, hinting at the possibility of adaptive tree growth from zero initialization – a direction the authors acknowledge. 

However, several weaknesses limit the current impact of this work. The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail regarding the baselines, particularly the pruning strategies employed for the naive/greedy methods. A more thorough comparison against simple global strategies, like multiple greedy trees trained on random subsets, would strengthen the claims of improvement. The ambiguity surrounding zero-weight nodes (implicitly treated as right branches) is a significant issue that needs clarification; a defined pruning/splitting behavior is crucial. Furthermore, the use of L2 regularization, while enabling optimization, risks sacrificing the interpretability inherent in plain decision trees. The tradeoff with L1 regularization, potentially enforcing sparsity, needs to be investigated.

The post-rebuttal note regarding zero-weight initialization highlights a critical case that requires further analysis. While random initialization may mask this issue, exploring a growth strategy from zero weights could reveal the method’s inherent bias towards adding or revising nodes. Finally, the title mentions “forests” but the paper focuses solely on single trees; this discrepancy should be addressed. While the inference descriptions (Sections 6.2 and 6.4) are technically sound, they could benefit from more concise summarization.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel reformulation of DT optimization, efficient optimization algorithms, potential for adaptive tree growth.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Insufficient experimental detail, ambiguity regarding zero-weight nodes, potential loss of interpretability, lack of forest evaluation.

**Overall:** This is a clever and technically sound paper with a promising approach. However, the practical usefulness and experimental validation are currently unconvincing. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses and provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
