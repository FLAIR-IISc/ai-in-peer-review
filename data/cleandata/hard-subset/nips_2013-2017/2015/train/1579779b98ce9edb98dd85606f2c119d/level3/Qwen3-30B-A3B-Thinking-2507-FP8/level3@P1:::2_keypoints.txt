- Proposes a non-greedy decision tree learning method that jointly optimizes split functions and leaf posteriors via a global objective.  
- Connects decision tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables and derives a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss.  
- Claims efficient SGD optimization with complexity O(d²p) for depth d and feature dimension p, enabling tractable joint training.  
- Key novelty is the joint optimization framework, argued to outperform greedy methods.  
- Strength: clear connection to structured prediction and a technically presented derivation of the upper bound.  
- Strength: empirical results claim better generalization than greedy baselines across multiple datasets.  
- Major concern: the claimed upper bound (Eq. 7) appears incorrect in the authors' own example, undermining theoretical foundations.  
- Major concern: proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement is incomplete, weakening theoretical guarantees.  
- Experimental weakness: missing critical baseline—greedy training with non-axis-aligned (oblique) splits without special initialization.  
- Experimental weakness: insufficient analysis of tree depth effects despite depth being a key pre-specified hyperparameter.  
- Questionable necessity: fast loss-augmented inference (O(d²p)) may be unnecessary for depths used (up to 18); benefit of Hamming-ball restriction not convincingly shown.  
- Optimization issue: some leaves receive no data during training; the proposed "stable SGD" heuristic is poorly motivated and lacks theoretical justification.  
- Suggested alternatives: prefer principled approaches like EM or CCCP for latent-variable optimization rather than ad-hoc heuristics.  
- Minor issues: inconsistent terminology for "leaves" in inference, missing citation to related latent structured prediction work (e.g., Yu & Joachims), and a notational suggestion to use "max" instead of "argmax."  
- Recommendation: paper is promising but requires fixing theoretical flaws, adding missing baselines, deeper depth analysis, and better-justified handling of inactive leaves to be a strong contribution.
