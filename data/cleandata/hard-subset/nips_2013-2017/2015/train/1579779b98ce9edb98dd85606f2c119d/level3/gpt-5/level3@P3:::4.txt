Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a global, non-greedy optimization scheme for decision trees with oblique splits. The key idea is to recast tree training as latent structured prediction by treating the vector of split decisions (signs at internal nodes) as latent variables. Since the empirical loss is non-differentiable and piecewise-constant in the split parameters, the authors derive a differentiable (though still non-convex) upper bound that enables gradient-based training. Minimization proceeds via SGD and requires repeated loss-augmented inference; the paper provides exact algorithms and a fast approximation that constrains the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current path, yielding O(d^2 p) complexity per step for tree depth d and feature dimension p. The method jointly updates internal-node weights and leaf parameters and includes a per-node L2 norm constraint that both tightens the bound and regularizes the model. Experiments on several multi-class datasets show improved test accuracy for single trees compared to standard greedy baselines and OC1-style oblique trees; the method is initialized from a greedy CO2-style procedure.

This work connects to: early attempts at global tree optimization (Bennett, Bennett & Blue; NP-completeness of exact global optimization), soft split models such as HME (Jordan & Jacobs) that trade hard decisions for smooth optimization, and structural SVMs with latent variables (e.g., Yu & Joachims). Relative to CO2 and OC1, the novelty is casting hard-split tree optimization as latent structured prediction with an efficiently optimizable surrogate and providing scalable loss-augmented inference.

Strengths
- Clear and interesting reformulation: binary split decisions as latent sign vectors, enabling a principled surrogate objective and SGD training.
- Efficient algorithms: exact and fast loss-augmented inference; per-step complexity O(d^2 p) makes deeper trees feasible.
- Insightful analysis: the bound’s dependence on the scale of W and the role of per-node norm constraints; discussion of instability and the SSGD variant.
- Sparse updates: with the Hamming-ball surrogate, each SGD step changes only the weight vector of a single internal node (plus leaf parameters), beyond mere computational benefit. This suggests adaptive capacity control and the intriguing possibility of growing a tree from zero weights.
- Empirical results: consistent single-tree accuracy gains over several baselines on multiple datasets.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practical utility vs. alternatives: The current experiments do not yet convincingly demonstrate superiority over strong, simple global heuristics or ensembles; only single trees are evaluated while the title/text allude to forests.
- Baselines and pruning: Details of the “naive/greedy” training, pruning strategies, and hyperparameter tuning are insufficient. It is unclear whether baselines were given comparable pruning or lookahead. Consider additional baselines such as multiple greedy trees trained on random subsets (choose best on validation), limited-depth lookahead, and stronger pruning (e.g., cost-complexity).
- Behavior of sparse updates: The method’s dynamics with sparse updates merit analysis. Does it behave like known greedy strategies (favor adding new nodes) or does it revise existing nodes? How often does the single-node update rewrite earlier splits vs. deepen the tree?
- Ambiguity at zero weights: The paper implies sgn(0) defaults to the right branch. This is problematic, especially when initializing from zero. A more principled specification is needed: allow a leaf output at an internal node (making the node a leaf), define explicit pruning/splitting rules, and state behavior for ties and zero vectors.
- Interpretability: A key motivation for decision trees is interpretability. Oblique splits with per-node L2 constraints will generally be dense and hard to interpret. L1 (or group-lasso) regularization could enforce sparse splits, but may trade off accuracy; this tradeoff should be empirically explored.
- Clarity: The inference procedures (Sections 6.2 and 6.4) could be summarized more explicitly with concise pseudocode, a clear definition of f(g), and a diagram of which nodes can change under the Hamming-ball restriction.
- Forests: The title/text mention forests, but the method is only evaluated on single trees. Either include ensembles (bagging/boosting) or adjust the scope.
- Surrogate looseness: The fast inference relies on a looser bound (radius-1 Hamming ball). Please quantify the empirical gap between the full and fast surrogates and provide ablations on accuracy vs. speed.
- Initialization and stability: Results rely on greedy initialization (CO2). How sensitive is performance to initialization? Provide runs from random and zero initialization, and analyze inactive-leaf dynamics with/without SSGD.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a correct reformulation, valid upper bounds, and solid complexity analysis. Claims of efficiency and single-tree accuracy improvements are supported. However, empirical evaluation lacks some key baselines, ablations (full vs fast bound, L1 vs L2), and a formal analysis of the sparse-update dynamics and zero-weight edge cases.

Clarity
Generally well written. The algorithmic core could be clearer: explicitly summarize loss-augmented inference (exact vs. fast), define behavior for sgn(0), and present pruning/leaf-assignment protocols for SSGD. Provide more details on baseline training and pruning.

Originality
High. Casting hard decision-tree learning as latent structured prediction with efficient loss-augmented inference is novel and interesting, beyond prior greedy oblique trees and soft-split models.

Significance
Promising. The approach could influence future work on global tree optimization and structured representations of trees. However, broader practical impact awaits stronger empirical validation, interpretability-aware regularization, and demonstration in forests and larger-scale tasks.

Requested clarifications and suggestions
- Precisely define behavior when any wi = 0 (and for ties). Consider allowing an output at internal nodes and formalizing pruning/splitting.
- Report ablations: full vs. fast loss-augmented inference; effect of ν on pruning/accuracy; SSGD vs. plain SGD; initialization (greedy vs. random vs. zero).
- Evaluate L1 or elastic-net per-node regularization for sparse, interpretable splits; measure sparsity vs. accuracy.
- Strengthen baselines: include multiple greedy trees trained on random subsets (select via validation), stronger pruning strategies, and limited lookahead.
- If forests are claimed, include forest experiments or remove the claim.
- Summarize Section 6.2/6.4 with explicit pseudocode and complexity, and make f(g) mapping fully explicit.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant and efficient reformulation with clear connections to latent structured prediction.
- Scalable training of deep, hard-split trees with provable per-step complexity.
- Consistent single-tree accuracy gains over standard greedy baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental evaluation lacks some strong and fair baselines and ablations.
- Ambiguity around zero-weight nodes and limited analysis of sparse-update dynamics.
- Interpretability concerns not addressed; no exploration of sparsity-inducing regularizers.
- Forests are not evaluated despite being mentioned.

Overall assessment
A clever and efficient approach to a long-standing problem with solid theory and promising empirical results. I find the idea novel and interesting, but I am not fully convinced about practical usefulness yet due to baseline and clarity gaps. I lean borderline accept if the authors address the requested clarifications and add stronger baselines/ablations; otherwise borderline reject.

Post-rebuttal note
Zero-weight initialization remains a critical case. Random initialization may avoid it, but analyzing zero-to-nonzero growth under sparse updates (and whether the method tends to add nodes vs. revise existing ones) is an interesting and important direction.
