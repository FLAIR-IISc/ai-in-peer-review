- Reformulates decision tree optimization as structured prediction using latent sign vectors and a differentiable upper bound for SGD.
- Key contributions: structured prediction formulation, efficient loss-augmented inference algorithms, sparse gradient updates, and a mechanism to grow very deep trees from zero weights.
- Novelty: establishes a new, theoretically grounded link between decision trees and structured prediction.
- Technical rigor: uses a convex-concave upper bound and fast inference algorithms; sparse updates reduce computation.
- Empirical performance: non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines across multiple datasets with better generalization.
- Scalability: fast loss-augmented inference makes the method feasible for deep trees and large datasets.
- Experimental shortcomings: insufficient baselines, missing comparisons to other global optimization and pruning methods.
- Misleading title: claims about forests are unsupported because no experiments on forests are presented.
- Interpretability concerns: L2-regularized dense weights harm tree interpretability; L1 effects are unexplored.
- Ambiguity in handling zero weights: unclear treatment of zero-weight nodes raises robustness and pruning questions.
- Clarity issues: inference sections (e.g., 6.2 and 6.4) are hard to follow and need clearer explanations/diagrams.
- Practical utility concerns: theoretical strengths are undercut by interpretability and limited experimental validation.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound approach with strong empirical results and scalable algorithms.
- Arguments against acceptance: inadequate experimental comparisons, unclear key mechanisms, and misleading scope claims.
- Recommendation: accept with revisions contingent on additional experiments, clearer exposition, analysis of zero-weight initialization, and exploration of interpretability/pruning.
