Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a principled, non-greedy training procedure for decision trees with oblique (linear) splits by jointly optimizing all split parameters and leaf distributions under a single global objective. The key technical idea is to cast the discrete routing decisions in a fixed-structure tree as latent binary variables and to upper bound the empirical loss with a convex–concave surrogate that resembles margin-rescaled latent structural SVM objectives. This leads to a nonconvex relaxation that can be optimized efficiently via SGD/SSGD. The authors derive both an exact loss-augmented inference procedure and a “fast” variant that restricts to a Hamming-1 neighborhood around the current routing, yielding per-update complexity O(d^2 p) for depth d and input dimension p. A per-row norm constraint on W regularizes the bound (tightness increases with the scale of W; Proposition 1) and empirically mitigates overfitting. Experiments on several multiclass benchmarks (LibSVM datasets) compare the method to greedy axis-aligned trees (CART-style), OC1, random oblique trees, and a greedy depth-1 bound-optimized baseline (CO2).

Relative to classic greedy induction (ID3/CART [21,5]) and oblique-tree work (OC1 [17], SVM-split trees [2,3]), this paper offers a global training objective and an efficient surrogate/optimizer that preserve the fast O(dp) inference of hard-split trees. It also contrasts with soft hierarchical mixtures of experts [13], maintaining computational advantages of hard splits. The connection to latent structured prediction [25,24] and the CO2 precursor [18] is well-situated; the present work provides the first end-to-end non-greedy training of fixed-depth trees using a tractable surrogate with scalable stochastic optimization.

Strengths
- Conceptual elegance and clarity: the latent-variable formulation and bound are clean and well-explained; the paper is very well written and organized.
- Solid technical contribution: a carefully derived upper bound, analysis of its scaling behavior, and an efficient O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference routine that makes deep trees trainable.
- Practical optimization: simple SGD/SSGD with projections, per-row norm constraints, and a clear complexity analysis.
- Potential for impact: a general framework that applies to arbitrary losses, invites sparse/structured regularization, and could spur follow-up work (e.g., kernelized splits, regression trees, better surrogates).

Weaknesses
- Empirical results, while generally favorable to the proposed method, are modest in some cases and somewhat disappointing given the ambition of global training; comparisons to strong modern tree baselines (e.g., boosted trees) are absent (though single-tree focus is acknowledged).
- Overfitting is discussed via the ν parameter and inactive leaves, but a deeper analysis of generalization vs model capacity is limited; training/test gaps and robustness to noise are not systematically quantified.
- Nonconvexity remains; there is limited study of sensitivity to initialization, restarts, and stability across runs.
- The fast inference uses a Hamming-1 neighborhood; the trade-off in bound looseness vs accuracy is not deeply examined.

Quality
- Technically sound with correct connections to latent structural SVMs and careful derivations. Proof details are deferred to the supplement as appropriate. Optimization is nonconvex, but the algorithm design is principled and computational claims are supported. The evaluation is adequate but not exhaustive; broader baselines and deeper ablations would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
- Excellent. The paper is elegant, clearly written, and easy to follow. The notation, algorithms, and complexity discussion are crisp. Figures relating ν to pruning and training dynamics help.

Originality
- High. While related to CO2 [18] and latent structured prediction [25], the global non-greedy training of fixed-depth hard-split trees via a scalable surrogate and efficient loss-augmented inference is novel.

Significance
- Moderate to high. Even with mixed empirical margins, the framework is likely to spur further research on globally trained trees/forests and hybrid objectives. The method has potential to influence practice, especially if combined with sparsity or integrated into ensembles.

Suggestions for improvement
- Post-optimization local refinement: After global optimization converges, drop the last split on each path and re-optimize the final split and its two leaf distributions with a standard greedy criterion (e.g., information gain for classification, squared loss for regression, or a small logistic/SVM fit), conditioned on the upstream splits. This inexpensive path-wise fine-tuning may recover performance lost due to the surrogate’s looseness at the bottom of the tree and could reduce variance.
- Overfitting assessment: Systematically evaluate whether global optimization increases overfitting:
  - Plot train vs validation/test accuracy as a function of depth and ν; include label-noise stress tests.
  - Compare to depth-matched pruned CART and to bagged/boosted ensembles to contextualize variance.
  - Report calibration (e.g., ECE) and leaf occupancy distributions; analyze inactive-leaf dynamics and how SSGD mitigates them.
- Ablations:
  - SGD vs SSGD across datasets; sensitivity to ν and η; effect of Hamming-1 vs exact loss-augmented inference on accuracy and runtime.
  - Multiple random initializations (CART vs OC1 vs random hyperplanes) to assess sensitivity.
- Baselines and scope:
  - Include a boosted trees reference (even if single-tree focus is maintained) to contextualize accuracy.
  - Report regression results to demonstrate generality beyond log loss.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, clearly written paper with a well-justified, general framework and efficient algorithm.
- Novel connection and relaxation enabling non-greedy global training of decision trees.
- Likely to inspire follow-up work (sparse splits, kernels, ensembles, better surrogates).

Cons against acceptance
- Empirical gains are not uniformly strong; limited comparison to state-of-the-art tree learners in practice.
- Limited analysis of generalization/overfitting and optimization stability.

Recommendation
- Accept (poster). The paper is elegant, technically sound, and clearly presented; despite somewhat disappointing empirical margins in places, the contribution is substantive and likely to stimulate further research.

Score justification
- The score is appropriate given the paper’s clarity, technical quality, and potential impact, tempered by modest empirical advantages and limited overfitting analysis.
