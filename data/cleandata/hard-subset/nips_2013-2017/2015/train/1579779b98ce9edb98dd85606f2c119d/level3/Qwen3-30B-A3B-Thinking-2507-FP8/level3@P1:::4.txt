

This paper presents a novel approach to decision tree learning by formulating the optimization problem as structured prediction with latent variables, enabling global optimization of split functions across all tree levels rather than the standard greedy one-node-at-a-time procedure. The authors establish a connection between decision trees and structured prediction by treating the binary split decisions as latent variables, then derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently using stochastic gradient descent. Their key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for loss-augmented inference with O(d²p) complexity, where d is tree depth and p is feature dimension, making training deep trees computationally feasible. Experiments on multiple classification benchmarks demonstrate that their non-greedy trees consistently outperform greedy baselines including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and random oblique trees.

The paper makes several strong contributions to the field. The reformulation of decision tree optimization as structured prediction with latent variables is both novel and insightful, connecting two important areas of machine learning. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for their approach, including a proof that their upper bound is valid and a demonstration that the bound becomes tighter with increased regularization. Their efficient optimization algorithm addresses a key limitation of previous non-greedy approaches that suffered from exponential complexity. The use of sparse gradient updates (changing only one node's weights per step) is particularly interesting, suggesting the possibility of adaptive tree growth where complexity increases only when needed.

The paper demonstrates significant improvements over greedy methods across multiple datasets, with non-greedy trees achieving better generalization performance and reduced susceptibility to overfitting. The regularization parameter ν implicitly controls pruning during training, providing a principled way to tune the model's complexity. The authors' careful treatment of the optimization problem and their experimental validation on standard benchmarks demonstrate high technical quality.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. The most significant is the lack of attention to decision trees' primary advantage: interpretability. The authors use L2 regularization, which produces dense splits that are difficult to interpret, undermining a key motivation for using decision trees. The paper does not explore L1 regularization to enforce sparsity, which would better preserve interpretability while potentially maintaining performance. The title mentions "forests," but the paper only evaluates individual trees without any forest experiments, leaving this aspect unexplored.

The experimental section lacks sufficient detail about baseline methods and pruning strategies. For instance, it's unclear how the greedy baselines were pruned or whether they used the same tree depth control as the non-greedy approach. The paper would benefit from comparing to additional simple global strategies, such as training multiple greedy trees on random subsets, as a more fair baseline. The ambiguity about zero-weight nodes (the paper implies taking the right branch when weights are zero) is problematic for practical implementation and requires clarification.

The clarity of the paper suffers from overly technical descriptions of inference procedures in Section 6.2 and 6.4, which could be summarized more explicitly for readers. The connection between the theoretical framework and practical implementation would be stronger if the authors addressed how their approach handles the critical case of zero-weight nodes, which is particularly important for adaptive tree growth.

Overall, this is a technically sound and innovative paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of decision tree optimization. The authors successfully bridge decision tree learning with structured prediction, and their efficient algorithm enables training deep trees that outperform greedy approaches. However, the lack of attention to interpretability and the ambiguity about zero-weight nodes weaken the practical impact of the work. While the experimental results are promising, they would be more convincing with more detailed comparisons to baselines and exploration of regularization strategies that preserve interpretability.

The paper meets NIPS's quality criteria with strong theoretical foundations and thorough experimental validation. Its originality is high, as it presents a novel formulation and efficient algorithm for a well-studied problem. The significance is moderate, as the method offers a principled alternative to greedy tree learning but does not directly address key practical concerns like interpretability. The clarity is good but could be improved with better explanations of the inference procedures and more attention to practical implementation details.

Pro: Novel formulation connecting decision trees to structured prediction with latent variables; efficient optimization algorithm with O(d²p) complexity; consistent experimental improvements over greedy baselines.

Con: Ignores interpretability advantage of decision trees; ambiguous handling of zero-weight nodes; lacks forest experiments despite title; insufficient detail on baseline comparisons; L2 regularization undermines key motivation for using trees.

Given these considerations, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the interpretability concerns and clarify the zero-weight node handling. The paper's technical contribution is significant, and the experimental results are convincing, but addressing these practical concerns would strengthen the paper's impact.
