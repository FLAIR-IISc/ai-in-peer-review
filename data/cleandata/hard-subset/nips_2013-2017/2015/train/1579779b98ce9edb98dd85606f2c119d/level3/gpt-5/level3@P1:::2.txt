This paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees that jointly optimizes all internal split functions (oblique hyperplanes) and leaf parameters under a single global objective. The key technical idea is to cast split assignments as binary latent variables and to derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling stochastic gradient optimization. A second contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference procedure that restricts search to a Hamming ball around the current split assignments, reducing per-example complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p). Experiments on several multiclass datasets show consistent gains over greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, and random-oblique), and the method scales to deeper trees via the efficient inference scheme. The work relates to and builds upon structured prediction with latent variables (e.g., Taskar et al., Tsochantaridis et al., Yu & Joachims), oblique tree induction (OC1), margin-based trees (Bennett et al.), and the recent CO2 approach, which the present method generalizes from depth-1 to whole-tree optimization. It contrasts with soft probabilistic trees (HME) by maintaining hard splits and the O(dp) inference advantage.

Quality
- Theory: The latent-variable reformulation and the bound-based surrogate are natural and potentially powerful. However, the loss bound around the discussion following Eq. (7) (l.216f in the submitted version) is confusing, and I can construct cases where the claimed inequality appears not to hold as stated; the precise conditions and the role of ties or non-unique maximizers should be clarified. Proposition 1 (monotonicity of the bound under scaling W) is important for motivating the max-norm constraints, but the proof in the supplement is incomplete; please provide a rigorous, self-contained argument and state any assumptions (e.g., finite losses, no ties). The fast inference bound that restricts g to a Hamming radius of 1 is intuitively plausible and computationally attractive, but it introduces an additional approximation; its effect on optimality and empirical performance needs more careful characterization (see below).
- Algorithms: The SGD/SSGD procedures and the projection onto per-row max-norm balls are standard and appropriate. The SSGD “fixed assignment” heuristic addresses leaf inactivity but is ad hoc; a more principled treatment via EM or CCCP on the latent variables (or block-coordinate updates that alternate between latent assignment and parameter updates) seems feasible and might avoid pathological collapse of leaves.
- Experiments: The reported accuracy improvements over greedy baselines are encouraging, but the evaluation is limited. Missing baselines include a greedy oblique tree trained without special initialization (e.g., logistic/SVM node splits trained per node with early stopping) to disentangle the effects of non-greediness versus better oblique optimization. The initialization via CO2 is reasonable but may advantage the proposed method; please include results from random or OC1-based initializations.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with a clean derivation and helpful intuition. A few points need clarification:
  - Terminology: In the fast loss-augmented inference section, please be explicit that “leaves” refer to terminal nodes reachable by flipping at most one decision along the current path (d+1 candidates). A figure illustrating the Hamming-1 neighborhood on a depth-d path would help.
  - Notation: In the bound expressions, using max rather than argmax (and clearly separating the values from the argmax sets) would avoid confusion. Please also be explicit about how ties in max are broken and whether the bound is invariant to tie-breaking.
  - Assumptions: State the conditions under which the bound is tight (e.g., when g equals the current latent ĥ) and when the Hamming-ball restriction remains an upper bound.
- Minor: Line-editing could reduce repetition between Sections 4–6; move some algebraic proofs to the main text if space permits.

Originality
- Joint, non-greedy optimization of hard-split trees via a latent-structured objective and a tractable upper bound appears novel and distinct from both OC1 and HME/soft trees. The link to latent SVMs is insightful. The fast Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference for hard trees is also an interesting idea. The work is well positioned relative to prior art, including CO2 as a depth-1 special case.

Significance
- If the bound and optimization are sound, the approach has the potential to make single trees substantially stronger and less overfit than standard greedy trees, with practical impact in vision and large-scale settings. That said, the current significance is tempered by (i) theoretical uncertainties in the bound and Prop. 1, (ii) limited empirical scope (four datasets, depths ≤ 16), and (iii) lack of key baselines and ablations. Demonstrating that the Hamming-ball restriction preserves accuracy while delivering substantial runtime savings would strengthen the case.

Requests and suggestions for rebuttal/revision
- Provide a corrected and complete proof of the loss upper bound and Prop. 1, and specify any conditions required. Include a small synthetic counterexample analysis if helpful to delineate when the inequality holds strictly.
- Quantify the impact of the Hamming-ball restriction: compare accuracy and training time between full loss-augmented inference and Hamming-1 across depths and datasets. Given d ≤ 16, the brute-force variant seems feasible; please show that the restriction does not degrade accuracy materially.
- Report results for additional baselines: (a) greedy oblique trees trained per node with logistic or hinge loss (no CO2 initialization), (b) your method with random initialization, and (c) ablations without SSGD and without per-row norm constraints.
- Discuss and empirically explore the depth hyperparameter: how sensitive is performance to depth, and can your ν-induced pruning reliably discover effective capacity? Consider including experiments that vary depth more broadly and report active leaf counts.
- Clarify the “inactive leaves” issue and consider a more principled latent-variable update (EM/CCCP), or add a regularizer on Θ to discourage collapse of leaf usage.
- Include a more thorough runtime evaluation: end-to-end training time versus depth, dataset size, and comparison to OC1/greedy oblique trees, as well as per-iteration costs.

Pros
- Conceptually novel non-greedy, latent-structured formulation for hard-split trees.
- Clean surrogate objective with efficient per-example gradient and scalable inference.
- Consistent accuracy gains over several greedy baselines; less overfitting at larger depths.
- Practical training algorithm (SGD/SSGD) and clear path to extensions (sparsity, kernels).

Cons
- Potential flaw/ambiguity in the empirical loss bound; incomplete proof of Prop. 1.
- Heuristic handling of inactive leaves; limited justification for SSGD.
- Unclear necessity/benefit of Hamming-ball restriction at the evaluated depths.
- Missing baselines and ablations; limited datasets and depth analysis.
- Requires pre-specified depth; capacity control via ν needs deeper analysis.

Overall, this is an important direction with promising ideas, but I require clarification and additional evidence on the theory and empirical comparisons. I am open to a more positive assessment if the authors address the bound/proof issues and strengthen the experimental validation in the rebuttal.
