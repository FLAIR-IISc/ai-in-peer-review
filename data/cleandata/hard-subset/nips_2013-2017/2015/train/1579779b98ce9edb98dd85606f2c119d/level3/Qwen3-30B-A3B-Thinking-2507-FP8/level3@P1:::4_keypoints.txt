- Novel formulation: casts decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables for global split optimization instead of greedy nodewise training.
- Derives a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss that can be optimized with stochastic gradient descent.
- Key algorithmic contribution: efficient loss-augmented inference with O(d²p) complexity, making training deep non-greedy trees feasible.
- Uses sparse gradient updates that change only one node's weights per step, enabling potential adaptive tree growth.
- Empirical results: non-greedy trees consistently outperform greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) on multiple benchmarks.
- Regularization parameter ν implicitly controls pruning during training, helping generalization and reducing overfitting.
- Theoretical contributions include a proof of the upper bound's validity and demonstration that the bound tightens with stronger regularization.
- Addresses prior non-greedy methods’ exponential complexity, presenting a scalable alternative.
- Paper demonstrates high technical quality and thorough experimental validation.
- Major weakness: neglects interpretability — L2 regularization yields dense, hard-to-interpret splits, undermining a key tree advantage.
- Fails to explore L1 or sparsity-promoting regularization that would preserve interpretability.
- Title mentions "forests" but only single-tree experiments are reported; forest experiments are missing.
- Experimental details are insufficient: unclear how greedy baselines were pruned or whether depth control was comparable.
- Lacks comparison to simple global baselines (e.g., ensembles of greedy trees on random subsets) for fairer benchmarking.
- Ambiguity about handling zero-weight nodes (implied right-branching) needs clarification for practical implementation.
- Clarity issues: Sections 6.2 and 6.4 are overly technical and should be summarized more explicitly for readers.
- Connection between theory and practice would be stronger if zero-weight node handling and adaptive growth were addressed.
- Overall verdict: technically sound, innovative, and significant; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address interpretability and implementation ambiguities.
- Assessment summary: originality high, significance moderate, clarity good but improvable.
- Pro/con recap: Pros — novel formulation, efficient O(d²p) algorithm, consistent empirical gains; Cons — ignores interpretability, ambiguous zero-weight handling, no forest experiments, insufficient baseline/pruning details, reliance on L2 regularization.
