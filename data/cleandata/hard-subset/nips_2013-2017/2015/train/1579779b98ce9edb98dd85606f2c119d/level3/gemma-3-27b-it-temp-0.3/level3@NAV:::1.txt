## Review of “Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees”

This paper presents an elegant and novel approach to optimizing decision trees by moving beyond the traditional greedy methods. The core idea – formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and deriving a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss – is well-motivated and technically sound. The authors successfully establish a connection to existing work in structured prediction, allowing them to leverage techniques like stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for efficient training, even with deep trees. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the technical details. It builds upon established concepts in decision tree learning (ID3, CART) and clearly articulates the limitations of greedy approaches, positioning their work as a significant step forward. 

The strength of this work lies in its theoretical grounding and the potential for improved performance through global optimization. The reduction of gradient complexity to O(d) is a crucial contribution, enabling the training of deeper trees than previously feasible. The connection to structured prediction is insightful and allows for the application of a well-developed toolkit. The authors also acknowledge the potential for overfitting and introduce norm regularization as a means to address this concern. 

However, the experimental results are somewhat disappointing. While the non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines, the margin of improvement isn’t substantial across all datasets. This raises questions about the practical impact of the proposed method. The authors do a good job of tuning hyperparameters, but a more extensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets, particularly those with high dimensionality, would strengthen the claims. 

A key area for future work, and a suggestion for the authors, is to explore a refinement of the optimization process. Specifically, after the global optimization phase, dropping and re-optimizing the last split and its corresponding leaf node using standard greedy methods could potentially yield further improvements. Additionally, a more thorough investigation into the regularization parameter (ν) and its effect on overfitting is warranted. It’s important to quantify the degree to which the global optimization increases overfitting, even with regularization, and to compare the generalization performance to ensembles of greedy trees.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and novel approach to a long-standing problem.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Potential to spur further research in non-greedy tree optimization.
*   Connection to established techniques in structured prediction.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental results are not overwhelmingly convincing.
*   Limited evaluation on a diverse set of datasets.



Overall, this is a strong paper that merits publication. Despite the somewhat modest experimental results, the theoretical contributions and potential for future work are significant. I believe this work will be of interest to researchers in machine learning and could lead to further advancements in decision tree algorithms.
