This paper proposes a non-greedy training procedure for decision trees by casting split selection as a latent structured prediction problem. The key idea is to treat the vector of split decisions across internal nodes as a latent binary code h ∈ {−1, +1}^m, so that routing to a leaf becomes a function of h. Using this view, the authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss that decouples loss-augmented inference (max over g) from standard inference (max over h), and optimize the bound with stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A naive implementation would require exponential-time loss-augmented inference in tree depth d; the paper’s main technical contribution is a fast variant that restricts g to a Hamming ball around sgn(Wx), reducing the per-example complexity to O(d^2 p). The method jointly optimizes all split parameters and leaf distributions under a global objective with per-row norm constraints on W, and includes a stable SGD variant that controls abrupt changes in leaf assignments. Experiments on several multi-class datasets show consistent improvements over greedy and oblique baselines for single trees.

Relation to prior work: Greedy induction (ID3, CART) optimizes splits locally and is known to be suboptimal; global optimization is NP-complete (Hyafil and Rivest). Prior global or lookahead methods either address tiny trees (Bennett) or use soft probabilistic trees (HME) that sacrifice hard-routing efficiency. Max-margin/tree-margin ideas exist for node-wise training. The present work is closest in spirit to structural SVMs and latent-variable structural SVMs (Taskar; Tsochantaridis; Yu & Joachims), and to CO2 (Norouzi et al.), which the authors position as a greedy special case. The contribution here is to (i) formulate oblique tree training as latent structured prediction with a tight, computable upper bound and (ii) make loss-augmented inference efficient enough to train deep trees with SGD.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: an elegant connection between decision tree learning and latent structured prediction, with a surrogate that admits SGD and per-example O(d^2 p) complexity.
- Practical efficiency: the fast Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference, sparse per-step updates limited to at most the nodes on a path, and the projection step enable scalable training of deep trees.
- Empirical signal: consistent single-tree accuracy gains over strong greedy/oblique baselines on several datasets; norm constraints act as a principled regularizer and implicitly control pruning.
- Well-motivated SSGD variant to stabilize leaf assignments and avoid collapse of tree capacity.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Practical usefulness vs. interpretability: Oblique splits trained with L2 constraints are typically dense, undermining an oft-cited advantage of trees. The paper mentions possible sparsity-inducing regularization but provides no experiments. Please evaluate L1 (or group sparsity per node) to quantify the tradeoff among accuracy, sparsity, and interpretability.
- Sparse update behavior and growth: Each SGD step updates a small subset of rows in W (often only along a single root-to-leaf path). This suggests a constructive “from zero” training scheme where the tree grows adaptively as rows leave zero. How would the method behave under zero initialization? Would it prefer adding new effective splits over revising existing ones? Analysis and/or experiments would be valuable.
- Ambiguity around zero-weight nodes and pruning: The manuscript implies defaulting to the right branch when wi = 0, which is arbitrary. A cleaner formulation would allow prediction at internal nodes (early stopping) and define pruning behavior precisely within the objective and inference, reducing sensitivity to initialization/symmetry breaking.
- Baselines and fairness: More detail is needed on how greedy baselines (e.g., OC1, axis-aligned trees) are tuned and pruned. Since post-pruning can dramatically change single-tree accuracy, matching regularization strength across methods is important. Consider additional simple global strategies as baselines: multiple greedy trees with random restarts and feature subsampling, selection of the best validation score; lookahead of limited depth; and stronger axis-aligned trees with state-of-practice pruning.
- Clarity: The loss-augmented inference procedures (Sections 6.2 and 6.4) would benefit from a concise algorithmic summary that explicitly states: which bits can differ between g and sgn(Wx); how candidate leaves are enumerated; and why the complexity is O(d^2 p).
- Scope: The paper emphasizes applicability to forests but does not present forest experiments. Even a small study showing that non-greedy single trees compose well in bagging/boosting would strengthen significance.
- Theory: While the surrogate is justified and the projection controls bound tightness, there is no convergence or generalization analysis (e.g., margin-based arguments or Rademacher bounds under per-row constraints). Even partial results or monotonicity properties under SSGD would help.

Quality: The optimization is sound, and the fast loss-augmented inference is a real advance. Claims are supported by experiments, but the empirical section needs stronger, better-documented baselines and an investigation of sparsity/interpretability and zero-init behavior.

Clarity: Generally well written; sections on efficient inference and SSGD should be summarized more explicitly with pseudo-code and a complexity table. Define the behavior for degenerate/zero rows and pruning.

Originality: The latent structured prediction formulation for non-greedy tree training and the efficient Hamming-ball upper bound are novel and interesting refinements over prior oblique and CO2 methods.

Significance: Improving single-tree optimization is important both scientifically and for applications where trees are preferred. However, the absence of results on forests/ensembles and interpretability limits immediate practical impact. With stronger baselines and a sparsity study, the contribution could be influential.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant reformulation with a practical, efficient optimizer for deep trees.
- Consistent accuracy gains over several baselines for single trees.
- Clear path to extensions (sparsity, kernels) within a principled framework.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing analysis/experiments on sparsity and interpretability.
- Baseline/pruning details insufficient; additional fair global baselines missing.
- Ambiguity around zero-weight nodes and pruning; limited theory on convergence/generalization.
- No empirical evidence on forests/ensembles.

Questions for authors:
- Can you report results with L1 or group-lasso regularization on W (sparsity vs. accuracy)?
- How does zero initialization of W behave in practice? Does the method “grow” effective depth adaptively?
- How are pruning/early stopping decisions handled for baselines and for your method? Can you add a principled internal-node prediction option?
- Can you provide a brief convergence/bound-decrease analysis for SSGD?
- Any preliminary results combining your trees into forests or boosted ensembles?

Overall: A clever and efficient approach with solid technical contributions. With additional experimental rigor and clarification, this could be a strong paper; in its current form, the practical validation is not yet fully convincing.
