This paper proposes a novel approach to jointly training all parameters of a decision tree – both the split functions and the leaf posteriors – via a single optimization process. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound to enable tractable optimization. This is a valuable contribution as standard decision tree algorithms rely on greedy, sequential splitting, potentially leading to suboptimal trees. The problem addressed is significant, and the idea of joint optimization is original and promising.

However, despite the clear exposition and elegant derivation, several issues detract from the overall significance of the work. My assessment is that the paper presents an interesting direction but requires substantial refinement before it can be considered a strong contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The joint optimization approach is a genuinely novel departure from standard greedy methods.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The formulation leveraging structured prediction and the derived upper bound demonstrate a strong theoretical understanding. The derivation is clean and well-presented.
*   **Potential for Efficiency:** The proposed fast loss-augmented inference aims to address the computational complexity inherent in jointly optimizing all tree parameters.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Flaws:** The empirical loss bound (around l.216f) appears questionable. The provided example suggests the claimed upper bound does not always hold. Furthermore, the proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material is incomplete, lacking sufficient detail to verify its correctness. This casts doubt on the validity of the core theoretical contribution.
*   **Objective/Initialization Issues:** The observation that some leaves receive no data points suggests a potential problem with the objective function or the initialization procedure. The fixed-assignment heuristic, while pragmatic, lacks strong motivation. Alternatives like Expectation-Maximization (EM) or Co-Collection-Concavity Programming (CCCP) might offer better performance and a more principled approach.
*   **Questionable Impact of Efficient Inference:** The claim that efficient loss-augmented inference is necessary due to the complexity of brute-force search is questionable, given that the paper restricts itself to a maximum tree depth of 16.  For such depths, brute-force exploration seems feasible, making the benefit of restricting the search to a Hamming ball unclear and requiring more robust validation.
*   **Terminology:** The meaning of “leaves” in the context of the inference discussion needs clarification. Is it referring to terminal nodes, or are intermediate nodes also considered ‘leaves’ within this framework?
*   **Missing Baseline:** A critical omission is the lack of comparison to a greedy training of non-axis-aligned splits *without* any special initialization (e.g., CO2 initialization). This baseline would isolate the impact of the joint optimization approach from any benefits derived from a potentially favorable initialization.
*   **Depth Sensitivity:** The pre-specification of tree depth is a significant limitation. The paper acknowledges this but fails to adequately discuss or experimentally evaluate the impact of varying depth. Experiments exploring performance across a range of depths are crucial.
*   **Minor Issue:** Replacing "argmax" with "max" in the loss bound expression would improve clarity and mathematical consistency.

**Experiments & Presentation:**

The experiments, while demonstrating improvements over some baselines, lack thoroughness.  A runtime comparison (beyond the limited comparison in Fig. 4) is needed to justify the computational complexity claims. More comprehensive empirical evaluation, including a wider range of datasets and hyperparameter settings, is necessary to strengthen the conclusions. The presentation of results could also be improved with statistical significance testing.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper proposes a genuinely novel approach to decision tree learning.
*   The theoretical foundation, despite the noted flaws, shows a solid understanding of optimization principles.
*   The experiments demonstrate some performance gains over existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Significant doubts regarding the correctness of the theoretical foundation.
*   Potential issues with the objective function and initialization procedure.
*   Questionable necessity and impact of the efficient loss-augmented inference.
*   Missing crucial baseline comparisons.
*   Insufficient experimental validation.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful idea, but it is currently flawed in both theory and experiment. The theoretical issues are particularly concerning. I believe the paper is currently below the acceptance threshold for NIPS. I would be willing to reconsider my assessment if the authors can address the aforementioned concerns, particularly providing a rigorous proof of the upper bound and demonstrating its validity with counterexamples, and presenting a more complete experimental evaluation including the suggested baseline. A clear rebuttal addressing these points is essential.
