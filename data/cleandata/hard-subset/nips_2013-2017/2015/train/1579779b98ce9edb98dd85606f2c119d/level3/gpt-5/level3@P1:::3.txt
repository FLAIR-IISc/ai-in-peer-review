Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a non-greedy training method for oblique decision trees that jointly optimizes split parameters and leaf parameters under a single global objective. The core idea is to cast tree induction as structured prediction with latent variables: the vector of split decisions along the path to a leaf is treated as a latent binary vector h ∈ {−1,+1}^m, with the optimal h for an input x obtained as argmax_h h^T W x. The empirical loss of the tree is upper-bounded by a convex-concave surrogate inspired by latent structural SVM [25], enabling optimization via (projected) SGD. A key practical contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference scheme that restricts the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current hard decisions sgn(Wx), reducing per-example complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p), where d is depth and p is input dimension. The method includes a norm constraint on each row of W to regulate the tightness of the bound and mitigate overfitting; a “Stable SGD” variant holds data-to-leaf assignments fixed for multiple updates to avoid collapsing leaves. Experiments on SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST compare single trees against greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) and CO2 [18]. The non-greedy trees generally achieve higher test accuracy and appear less prone to overfitting as depth increases, though the absolute improvements over strong baselines are often modest.

This work builds on and contrasts with: (i) classic greedy induction (ID3/CART) [21,5]; (ii) non-greedy but intractable or specialized global optimization approaches [1,2,3]; (iii) soft-split models (HME) [13] that sacrifice tree-time evaluation efficiency; and (iv) CO2 [18], a related upper-bound approach applied greedily one node at a time. The paper’s global, hard-split formulation with an efficiently optimizable surrogate appears novel and well positioned in this landscape.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean connection between hard-split decision trees and latent structured prediction, yielding a principled surrogate with loss-augmented inference and SGD training.
- Efficiency: Fast loss-augmented inference via Hamming-ball restriction lowers complexity to O(d^2 p), making deep trees feasible. Per-example gradient computation scales with depth rather than the number of leaves.
- Generality: The framework supports general losses (log loss, squared loss) and learns oblique splits with joint leaf parameter optimization.
- Regularization and stability: Per-row norm constraints on W and the SSGD variant address overfitting and inactive leaves, respectively; the role of the regularizer ν is clearly articulated and empirically probed.
- Empirical validation: Consistent gains over multiple greedy baselines across several datasets; ablations highlight runtime and the impact of ν on pruning and generalization.

Weaknesses
- Magnitude of gains: While consistent, improvements over strong greedy oblique baselines (e.g., OC1) and CO2 are often small; the practical impact for single-tree usage may be limited without ensemble evidence.
- Experimental scope: Focuses on individual trees and avoids comparison to widely used ensembles (random forests [4], boosting [8]), which weakens the significance claim for many applications where ensembles are standard.
- Optimization analysis: The surrogate is non-convex in W and Θ jointly; convergence behavior and sensitivity to initialization (from CO2) are not deeply analyzed, and SSGD remains heuristic.
- Practical scalability: Although O(d^2 p) is a substantial improvement, high-dimensional inputs and deep trees may still be expensive; memory and wall-clock training cost versus OC1 and strong greedy implementations could be detailed further.
- Generalization theory: The role of ν is well motivated, but there are no generalization bounds or margin-based guarantees akin to max-margin tree work [2,3].

Quality
The paper is technically sound. The upper bound derivations, the impact of scaling W, and the complexity analysis are clearly reasoned; loss-augmented inference under the Hamming-ball restriction is justified and leads to the claimed efficiency. The empirical methodology includes multiple datasets, tuning on validation sets, and comparisons to appropriate baselines. However, stronger ablations (e.g., effect of initialization, with/without SSGD, depth and p scaling) and comparisons to ensembles would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
Well organized and clearly written. The latent-variable framing is intuitive, and the algorithmic details (SGD with projection, fast inference) are explicit. Figures appear to convey trends versus depth and runtime. Some readers may benefit from a more concrete pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference, and from expanded discussion on stability vs. accuracy trade-offs in SSGD.

Originality
The link to latent structured prediction for hard-split tree learning and the specific surrogate with efficient loss-augmented inference are, to my knowledge, original. The formulation contrasts with prior soft-split likelihood-based methods and earlier non-greedy global optimizations that lack scalability.

Significance
Moderate. The approach offers a principled route to non-greedy, globally trained trees with runtime competitive with greedy baselines, and it yields consistent accuracy gains on benchmarks. Demonstrating benefits within ensembles (e.g., as a drop-in base learner in forests/boosting) would substantially increase impact for practice.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please report results when using your non-greedy trees as base learners in forests or boosting to assess practical significance.
- Provide ablations on initialization (axis-aligned vs random) and the effect of SSGD versus SGD on both accuracy and leaf utilization.
- Include wall-clock training time and memory comparisons to OC1 and strong greedy oblique implementations across depths and dimensions.
- Explore sparsity-inducing regularization on W (as mentioned in conclusions) to improve interpretability and speed for high-dimensional data.
- Clarify whether code and data splits will be released to facilitate reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled formulation; efficient algorithmic realization.
- Clear, careful presentation; solid theoretical and empirical grounding.
- Consistent improvements over relevant greedy baselines and CO2.

Cons for acceptance
- Gains are often modest; lack of ensemble comparisons reduces practical significance.
- Limited analysis of optimization landscape and sensitivity to initialization.
- Heuristic SSGD without theoretical backing.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound advance in non-greedy tree learning with a clear path to broader impact. Strengthening the empirical section—especially with ensemble results—would make the case more compelling. My confidence is high.
