This paper proposes a novel and interesting approach for the non-greedy, global optimization of decision trees. The core idea is to reformulate the tree learning problem as one of structured prediction with latent variables. Specifically, the sequence of branching decisions for a given input is represented by a "sign vector," which is treated as a latent variable. The non-differentiable and non-convex empirical loss is then relaxed to a differentiable (though still non-convex) upper bound, inspired by max-margin structured prediction literature. The authors derive an optimization procedure based on stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which requires solving a loss-augmented inference problem at each step, and they provide efficient algorithms for this subproblem.

The primary strength of this work lies in its originality and theoretical elegance. Casting the classic problem of decision tree optimization into a modern structured prediction framework is a significant conceptual contribution that opens up new research avenues. A particularly insightful observation is that the SGD updates are sparse, affecting only one node's parameters per step. This suggests a compelling direction for future work on adaptively growing trees from a zero-initialized state, allowing model complexity to increase as needed.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that undermine its practical significance and experimental validation. First, a key motivation for using decision trees is interpretability. The proposed L2 regularization on the oblique split parameters likely produces dense, uninterpretable weight vectors. The potential trade-off with L1 regularization for sparsity is an important practical consideration that is not explored. Second, the experimental evaluation is not fully convincing. Baselines, such as the pruned greedy trees, are not described in sufficient detail, and comparisons against other simple yet effective global strategies (e.g., training multiple greedy trees on random data subsets) are missing.

Furthermore, several points require clarification. The handling of zero-weight nodes is ambiguous and needs a precise definition, as it is a critical edge case. The paper's title mentions "forests," which is misleading as the method is only developed and evaluated for single trees.

In summary, this paper introduces a clever and technically sound reformulation for decision tree optimization. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the practical utility and experimental validation are currently unconvincing. Major revisions addressing the experimental design, interpretability trade-offs, and clarity issues are needed to substantiate the method's claimed advantages.
