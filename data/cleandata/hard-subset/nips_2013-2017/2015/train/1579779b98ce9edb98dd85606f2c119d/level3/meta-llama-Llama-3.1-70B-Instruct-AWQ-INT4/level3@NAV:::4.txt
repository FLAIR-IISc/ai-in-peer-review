This paper proposes a novel approach to optimizing decision trees by reformulating the problem as a latent structured prediction task. The authors introduce a non-differentiable, non-convex loss function and approximate it with a differentiable, yet still non-convex, upper bound. The minimization of this upper bound is achieved through stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which requires solving successive loss-augmented inference problems. The paper provides efficient algorithms for these problems, making it possible to train deep trees.

The main contributions of this work are the casting of decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem and the development of efficient optimization algorithms. The approach is novel and interesting, and it raises several follow-up questions.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to handle any general loss function while maintaining a computational complexity of O(dp), where d is the tree depth and p is the number of features. The authors also provide a natural way to regularize the joint optimization of tree parameters to discourage overfitting.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement. The paper's experimental section is limited, and the authors do not provide sufficient detail on the baselines and pruning strategies used for the naive/greedy method. Additionally, the title of the paper mentions forests, but the paper does not address or evaluate forests.

The authors also acknowledge some ambiguity about zero-weight nodes and propose allowing output at the node and defining pruning/splitting behavior precisely. Furthermore, the use of L2 regularization may yield dense, non-interpretable splits, and the authors suggest testing L1 regularization as an alternative.

In terms of clarity, the paper could benefit from more explicit descriptions of the inference algorithms, particularly in Sections 6.2 and 6.4. The authors also mention that the algorithm can be modified to learn sparse split functions via sparsity-inducing regularization on W, which could be an interesting direction for future work.

Overall, the paper presents a clever and efficient reformulation of decision tree optimization, but its practical usefulness and experimental validation are currently unconvincing. With some revisions to address the mentioned weaknesses and areas for improvement, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* Novel and interesting approach to decision tree optimization
* Efficient algorithms for loss-augmented inference problems
* Ability to handle any general loss function while maintaining a computational complexity of O(dp)

Arguments con acceptance:

* Limited experimental section
* Insufficient detail on baselines and pruning strategies
* Ambiguity about zero-weight nodes
* Potential issues with interpretability due to L2 regularization
* Clarity issues in the description of inference algorithms
