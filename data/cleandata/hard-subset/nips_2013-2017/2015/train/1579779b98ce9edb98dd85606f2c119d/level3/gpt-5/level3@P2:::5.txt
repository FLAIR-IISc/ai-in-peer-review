Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy training algorithm for decision trees with linear (oblique) split functions that optimizes a single global objective over all internal-node split parameters and leaf parameters. The key idea is to recast the routing decisions of a tree as latent binary variables and to upper-bound the empirical loss with a convex-concave surrogate inspired by structured prediction with latent variables. The bound admits efficient stochastic optimization: the naive loss-augmented inference is O(2^d p) but the authors introduce a tightened bound that restricts to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current routing, yielding O(d^2 p) per example for trees of depth d and input dimension p. They further add per-row L2 constraints on W to regularize the split functions and discuss a “Stable SGD” variant (SSGD) that freezes leaf assignments for several iterations to avoid collapsing leaves.

The work builds clear connections to structural SVMs and latent structural SVMs (Taskar et al.; Tsochantaridis et al.; Yu & Joachims), as well as to oblique trees (OC1), margin-based trees (Bennett and co-authors), soft/probabilistic trees (HME), and more recent CO2, which can be viewed as a greedy depth-1 instance of their bound. The contribution is to (i) formulate a global, non-greedy objective with a tractable surrogate and (ii) show SGD training at scale using a fast loss-augmented inference restricted to a local neighborhood of the current routing.

Quality
- Theory/derivations: The latent-variable formulation and the upper bounds (global and Hamming-ball) are sound and align well with structured prediction theory. The role of scaling of W and the need for regularization are well-motivated. The complexity reductions are clearly argued.
- Algorithm: SGD with projection onto per-row L2 balls is standard and appropriate. The SSGD idea is reasonable, but the current description is underspecified (see clarity comments).
- Experiments: The evaluation includes several multiclass datasets. However, the gains over strong greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, and CO2) appear modest and inconsistent; runtime/accuracy trade-offs are not fully quantified; and statistical variability is not reported. This weakens the empirical case for the proposed global optimization.

Clarity
- The core formulation is presented clearly and is easy to follow. The connection to structured prediction is elegant and well-explained.
- Important details are missing or ambiguous:
  - Stable SGD (SSGD): Please provide a precise description and pseudo-code. How are a(x) assignments initialized, updated, and scheduled? What stopping criterion triggers reassignment? How long are assignments held fixed? How is “active” vs “inactive” leaf defined and measured?
  - Figure 2: State explicitly whether curves correspond to vanilla SGD or SSGD, report the chosen ν and η per depth, and define “active leaf” in the caption if referenced.
  - Algorithm 1, line 10: You update Θ via the gradient of the loss. Because θ are unnormalized log-probabilities with a softmax at prediction time, no simplex constraint is necessary. If you in fact project Θ (or impose any additional constraints), please describe the projection operator precisely; otherwise clarify that no projection is performed.
  - Fast loss-augmented inference: The O(d^2 p) complexity claim should be accompanied by a short pseudo-code sketch indicating how you enumerate the d+1 candidate leaves within the Hamming ball and how you cache the required inner products across levels to achieve the stated complexity.

Originality
- The idea of viewing tree decisions as latent variables and importing loss-augmented inference from structural SVMs to obtain a global non-greedy objective is novel and insightful. The Hamming-ball bound that yields tractable O(d^2 p) inference is a neat engineering of the surrogate to match the tree’s structure.
- Relative to CO2 and OC1, the contribution is a principled, global training objective with an efficient optimizer, not just improved heuristics.

Significance
- Potential: If the empirical benefits were more consistent and the computational overhead small, this could influence how oblique trees are trained in practice, and it might inform non-greedy training in forests.
- Current evidence: The experimental results do not yet convincingly demonstrate a robust accuracy advantage over strong greedy baselines across datasets and depths. Additional analyses (e.g., variance across random seeds, wall-clock vs accuracy, memory) would strengthen claims of practical significance.

Specific questions and requests for clarification
- SSGD:
  - Provide pseudo-code and a concrete schedule for updating a(x).
  - Define “active leaf” precisely and report how the number of active leaves evolves over training.
  - Compare SSGD vs SGD in terms of accuracy, stability (variance), runtime, and the number of inactive leaves.
- Experiments:
  - Report mean and std across multiple runs (different random initializations/splits). Include significance tests where differences are small.
  - Provide wall-clock time per epoch and total training time for each method, and plot accuracy vs time.
  - Discuss sensitivity to ν and η; include validation curves to justify selected hyperparameters.
  - Include an experiment showing the effect of the Hamming-ball radius (e.g., radius 0, 1, 2) on accuracy and runtime to justify the chosen radius.
- Implementation:
  - Clarify whether you use momentum, mini-batches, and any additional regularization for Θ.
  - Describe how you initialize W and Θ from CO2 and whether results are sensitive to the initializer.

- Supplementary material:
  - Line 29: Replace “∈” with “=” in the sign notation as appropriate.
  - Line 30: Replace sgn(Wx) inside the loss with Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)) to be consistent with the main text.

Pros
- Elegant and principled connection between decision-tree training and latent structured prediction.
- Well-motivated surrogate bound with clear role for regularization.
- Efficient O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference via Hamming-ball restriction.
- Potential to influence non-greedy training beyond depth-1 (CO2).

Cons
- Empirical gains over strong greedy baselines are modest and dataset-dependent; no statistical analysis.
- Missing details for SSGD and some algorithmic components; unclear experimental protocol (SGD vs SSGD).
- Limited ablations (e.g., Hamming-ball radius, role of ν, initialization dependence).
- Computational overhead not fully quantified relative to baselines.

Recommendation
- Overall: Weak reject.
- Rationale: The formulation is strong and original, but the experimental section does not yet provide compelling, consistent evidence that the proposed global optimization yields significant practical benefits over competitive greedy methods, and several important algorithmic details are missing. With clarified SSGD, stronger and more thorough experiments (including variance and runtime analyses), this work could become a solid contribution.

Confidence
- Reviewer confidence: High on methodological assessment; medium on empirical interpretation pending clarified experimental details.
