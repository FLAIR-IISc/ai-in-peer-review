This paper proposes a global optimization algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. While the formulation is elegant and offers a potential path towards overcoming the limitations of greedy tree induction, the empirical results presented do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over standard greedy methods, and several practical details are insufficiently described.

**Quality:** The technical foundation of the paper appears sound, establishing a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction. The proposed upper bound on empirical loss and the associated optimization procedure are logically presented. However, the experimental validation is weak. While improvements are reported on some datasets, they aren’t substantial, and the computational cost associated with the global optimization is notably higher. The claim of outperforming greedy trees feels unsupported given the limited empirical evidence. The lack of clarity around the Stable SGD implementation raises concerns about reproducibility. The paper feels incomplete in its experimental evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but suffers from a lack of detail in crucial areas. The description of Stable SGD is particularly vague. It's unclear how data points are consistently assigned to leaves during the fixed assignment phase, and a pseudo-code illustration would significantly improve understanding. Figure 2 lacks sufficient context – it is not stated whether the results shown utilize SGD or Stable SGD, and the meaning of an “active” leaf needs to be defined. The projection step in Algorithm 1 (line 10) is not explained clearly; stating that it enforces each row summing to one would be helpful. Several minor errors and typos detract from the overall clarity (see below).

**Originality:** The formulation connecting decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables is a novel contribution. However, the broader context of global optimization for decision trees has been explored previously (e.g., Bennett [1]). The paper distinguishes itself by offering a scalable SGD-based approach, but its practical benefits remain questionable based on the presented results.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work lies in providing a principled framework for globally optimizing decision trees. If the computational challenges could be overcome, and more substantial performance gains demonstrated, this approach could have a significant impact. Currently, however, the practical advantages are limited, and the increased computational cost makes it less attractive than existing greedy methods. The exploration of regularization through the norm constraint (ν) is a worthwhile contribution, but its impact appears dataset-dependent.

**Strengths:**

*   Elegant theoretical formulation connecting decision tree learning to structured prediction.
*   Development of a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss.
*   Potential for scalability through the use of SGD.
*   Exploration of regularization via the norm of split function weights.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Weak empirical results – improvements over greedy trees are not consistently significant.
*   High computational cost compared to greedy methods.
*   Lack of clarity regarding the Stable SGD implementation.
*   Insufficient detail in the description of the projection step in Algorithm 1.
*   Minor errors and typos throughout the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical formulation.
*   Potential for future research and improvement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical validation.
*   High computational cost.
*   Lack of clarity in key implementation details.
*   Unconvincing evidence of significant advantage over existing methods.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework, the lack of compelling empirical evidence and the unclear implementation details prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The authors should focus on providing a more thorough experimental evaluation, clarifying the Stable SGD procedure, and addressing the minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the manuscript. A more substantial demonstration of the benefits of global optimization over greedy methods is essential for this work to be considered a significant contribution.
