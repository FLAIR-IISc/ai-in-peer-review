- Novel formulation: casts decision-tree split optimization as a structured prediction problem using latent split variables.  
- Optimization method: introduces a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss enabling efficient SGD and loss-augmented inference.  
- Computational efficiency: reduces gradient computation complexity to O(d^2 p), facilitating training of deeper trees on large datasets.  
- Theoretical support: provides proofs for the upper bound, discusses regularization ν, and includes Proposition 1.  
- Empirical results: consistently outperforms greedy baselines across datasets, but improvements are generally small.  
- Literature connection: builds on structured SVMs and relates to prior non-greedy tree work and CO2, giving a solid context.  
- Strength — novelty: fresh perspective on a long-studied problem with a joint optimization framework.  
- Strength — efficiency: fast loss-augmented inference and scalable optimization strategy.  
- Strength — clarity: paper is well written and organized.  
- Weakness — effect size: practical significance of the modest performance gains is unclear.  
- Weakness — initialization sensitivity: performance depends on initialization (axis-aligned tree/CO2) with limited analysis.  
- Weakness — hyperparameter tuning: insufficient guidance and analysis on selecting and understanding ν.  
- Weakness — complexity details: constant factors and real-world implications for very deep or high-dimensional trees are underexplored.  
- Weakness — limited baselines: lacks comparison to modern, strong tree-based methods (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM).  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, and scalable approach with consistent empirical benefits and potential to spur further research.  
- Argument against acceptance: limited practical impact, sensitivity to initialization/hyperparameters, incomplete comparisons, and insufficient complexity analysis.  
- Overall recommendation: solid contribution worth acceptance with minor revisions to address practical implications, initialization sensitivity, hyperparameter analysis, and broader comparisons.
