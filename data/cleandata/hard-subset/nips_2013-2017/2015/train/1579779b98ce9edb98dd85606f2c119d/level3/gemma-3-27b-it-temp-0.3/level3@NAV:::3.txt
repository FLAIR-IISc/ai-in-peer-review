## Review of “Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees”

This paper proposes a novel approach to training decision trees by framing the optimization of split functions as a structured prediction problem. The core idea is to establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. This allows for the use of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) to jointly optimize split functions and leaf parameters, overcoming the limitations of traditional greedy tree-building algorithms. 

The work builds upon decades of research in decision trees (Quinlan, Breiman) and more recent advances in structured prediction (Taskar et al., Yu et al.). It addresses a long-standing challenge – the suboptimality of greedy tree induction – by proposing a globally optimized alternative. The paper differentiates itself from prior non-greedy methods (Bennett, Lakshminarayanan et al.) by achieving computational efficiency through a clever reduction in the complexity of gradient computation from O(2dp) to O(d2p), where d is the tree depth and p is the input dimension. This efficiency is crucial for training deep trees on large datasets.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear theoretical justification. The formulation as a structured prediction problem is insightful, and the derived upper bound enables efficient optimization via SGD. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms greedy baselines across multiple datasets, though the improvements are generally modest. The authors are careful to discuss limitations and provide a thorough comparison with related work. The inclusion of a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm is a significant contribution to the practical applicability of the method.

**Weaknesses:** While the empirical improvements are statistically significant, the magnitude of the gains is relatively small. The paper doesn’t deeply investigate *why* the non-greedy approach leads to better performance, beyond stating it promotes more compact trees. The sensitivity analysis of the regularization parameter ν is somewhat limited, and a more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different components of the proposed method would be beneficial. The reliance on initializing the optimization with a greedy tree (CO2) could potentially bias the results, and exploring random initializations might be worthwhile.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a well-studied problem.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and efficient algorithm.
*   Consistent empirical improvements over strong baselines.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Modest magnitude of performance gains.
*   Limited analysis of the underlying reasons for improvement.
*   Potential bias from initialization strategy.

**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the performance gains are not dramatic, the theoretical novelty, algorithmic efficiency, and consistent empirical improvements warrant acceptance. The paper is well-written and clearly explains a complex approach. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a more in-depth analysis of the results and exploration of alternative initialization strategies.
