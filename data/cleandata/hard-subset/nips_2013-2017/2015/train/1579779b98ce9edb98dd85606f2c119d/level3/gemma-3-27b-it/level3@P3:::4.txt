This paper proposes a novel approach to globally optimizing decision trees by recasting the problem as a structured prediction task with sign vectors as latent variables. The authors introduce a non-differentiable, non-convex loss function and address its optimization via a differentiable upper bound and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core contributions lie in this reformulation and the development of efficient algorithms for minimizing the surrogate objective, achieving O(d2p) complexity per gradient update.

The idea is intriguing and represents a significant departure from traditional greedy tree induction methods. The connection drawn between decision tree optimization and structured prediction is insightful and provides a fresh perspective. The resulting formulation does allow for joint optimization of splits, potentially overcoming the limitations of greedy approaches. The achieved computational efficiency, particularly the reduction to O(d2p), is commendable and makes the method applicable to deeper trees and larger datasets. The sparse gradient updates observed—where each SGD step appears to affect only one node’s weights—are a particularly interesting characteristic, hinting at the possibility of adaptive complexity through infinite depth.

However, several weaknesses and areas for improvement limit the current impact of this work. A major concern is the ambiguity surrounding zero-weight nodes. The paper implicitly suggests taking the right branch in this case, which is a potentially arbitrary choice. A more principled approach would be to allow outputs at zero-weight nodes and explicitly define pruning/splitting behavior based on these outputs, leading to a clearer interpretation of the learned tree structure.

Furthermore, the practical implications of L2 regularization are worrying. While regularization is crucial for generalization, L2 regularization is likely to produce dense, non-interpretable splits, negating one of the key benefits of using decision trees – their inherent interpretability. The authors acknowledge this but don’t provide sufficient experimental evidence on the tradeoff between sparsity enforced by L1 regularization and performance degradation. A thorough investigation of L1 versus L2 regularization is essential.

The experimental evaluation also suffers from weaknesses. The description of the baselines, particularly the “naive/greedy” method and its pruning strategy, lacks sufficient detail. It’s difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison without a precise understanding of how these baselines were implemented and tuned. Comparing against additional simple global strategies, such as training multiple greedy trees on random subsets of the data, would establish a stronger baseline for evaluating the gains achieved by the proposed method.

Clarity could be improved in the descriptions of inference (Sections 6.2 and 6.4). A more concise and explicit summary of these processes would enhance readability.  The title mentions “forests” but the paper entirely focuses on single trees; this is misleading and should be addressed.

The post-rebuttal comment regarding the critical nature of zero-weight initialization is well-taken. While random initialization might mask issues with starting from zero weights, exploring a zero-to-nonzero growth strategy represents a promising direction for future work. The authors correctly identify this as an interesting avenue for investigation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and insightful reformulation of decision tree optimization as structured prediction.
*   Efficient optimization algorithms with favorable computational complexity (O(d2p)).
*   Interesting property of sparse gradient updates, opening possibilities for adaptive tree complexity.
*   Potential to overcome limitations of greedy decision tree induction methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Ambiguity surrounding zero-weight nodes and lack of a principled pruning strategy.
*   Potential for L2 regularization to yield non-interpretable splits.
*   Insufficient detail in the experimental evaluation, particularly regarding baseline implementations.
*   Limited experimental validation and lack of comparison to strong baselines.
*   Title mentions forests without addressing or evaluating them.
*   Unclear how the method relates to/differs from existing greedy strategies in terms of node addition vs. revision.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a clever and efficient reformulation of decision tree optimization. However, the practical usefulness and experimental validation are currently unconvincing. The concerns regarding interpretability, baseline comparisons, and the handling of zero-weight nodes need to be addressed before this work can be considered a significant advancement in the field. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage the authors to address these weaknesses and resubmit a revised version. The core ideas are promising and warrant further investigation.
