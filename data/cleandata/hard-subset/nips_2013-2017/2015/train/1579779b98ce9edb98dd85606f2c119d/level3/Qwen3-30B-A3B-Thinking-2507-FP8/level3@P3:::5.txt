

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees" (NIPS 2015)

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees with linear split functions by optimizing an upper bound on empirical loss, regularized with L2 norms and optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key insight is connecting decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables, where the latent variables represent the binary decisions at each split node. The authors formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and develop an efficient algorithm for loss-augmented inference with O(d²p) complexity, making deep tree learning feasible.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by establishing a novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables. This formulation is elegant and well-motivated, extending previous work on structured prediction [23, 24, 25] to the decision tree context. The authors also build upon their prior CO2 method [18], which was a greedy variant of their approach.

However, the paper's empirical claims are problematic. Figure 2 shows that the proposed non-greedy trees only significantly outperform greedy baselines on a few datasets (SensIT, Connect4), with marginal improvements on others. The computational cost is substantially higher (Figure 4 shows a significant time increase for deeper trees), and the paper fails to provide statistical significance tests for the performance improvements. The claim that "the resulting non-greedy decision trees outperform greedy decision tree baselines" is not convincingly supported by the experimental evidence.

The description of Stable SGD (SSGD) is particularly unclear. The paper states that SSGD "fixes the assignments of data points to leaves for a number of gradient updates" but never defines how these assignments are determined or how often they are updated. The term "active leaf" is used without definition in Figure 2. Algorithm 1 line 10 states that Θ is projected onto the simplex, but the paper doesn't explain how this projection is implemented in practice or that it enforces each row of Θ to sum to one.

Several critical issues need addressing:
1. Figure 2 does not specify whether the results use SGD or SSGD
2. Line 10 of Algorithm 1 requires clarification on the simplex projection
3. Minor typos and confusing phrasing (lines 141, 238, 301, 404)
4. Undefined acronym FSGD (line 360)
5. Supplementary material corrections: line 29 should use "=" not "∈", line 30 should replace sgn(Wx) with Θ^T f(sgn(Wx))

The paper's strengths lie in its elegant theoretical formulation and the efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm. However, the weaknesses in experimental evaluation, unclear description of key components, and lack of convincing evidence for the practical benefits of global optimization outweigh these strengths.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction, but the experimental evidence does not convincingly support the claim that global optimization provides significant practical advantages over greedy methods. The computational cost is higher without sufficiently compelling performance gains. The paper would benefit from clearer descriptions of SSGD, better experimental validation, and more convincing evidence of the practical benefits of the proposed approach.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Elegant theoretical formulation connecting decision trees to structured prediction
- Novel efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm
- Potentially useful for future extensions (e.g., sparse split functions, kernel methods)

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental results fail to convincingly demonstrate significant advantages over greedy methods
- Computational cost is substantially higher without sufficient performance gains
- Critical algorithmic details (SSGD, simplex projection) are unclear
- Several minor but distracting errors and typos

Overall, while the theoretical contribution is valuable, the experimental evidence does not sufficiently support the practical significance of the approach, and the paper would benefit from significant revisions before acceptance. The problem is important, but the proposed solution does not convincingly advance the state of the art.
