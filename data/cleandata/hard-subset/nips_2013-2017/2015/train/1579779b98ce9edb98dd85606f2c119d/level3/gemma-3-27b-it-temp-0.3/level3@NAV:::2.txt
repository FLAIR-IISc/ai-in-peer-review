## Review of “Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees”

This paper tackles the important and original problem of jointly training all parameters of a decision tree – both the split functions and the leaf parameters – via a single optimization process. The authors cleverly formulate this as a structured prediction problem with latent variables and propose a convex-concave upper bound to enable tractable optimization. The motivation for moving beyond greedy tree induction is well-articulated, and the potential benefits of a globally optimized tree are significant. 

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the core ideas. The derivation of the upper bound and the proposed optimization algorithm are clean and easy to follow. The connection drawn between decision tree optimization and structured prediction is insightful and provides a solid theoretical foundation. However, the theoretical reasoning contains potential flaws. Specifically, the argument surrounding l.216f regarding the empirical loss bound is confusing, and the provided example doesn’t fully convince that the claimed upper bound always holds. Furthermore, the proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material appears incomplete.

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating improvements over greedy baselines, needs substantial improvement. The fact that some leaves receive no data points suggests potential issues with the objective function or initialization strategy. The use of a fixed-assignment heuristic seems suboptimal and lacks sufficient justification; exploring alternatives like Expectation-Maximization (EM) or Co-Coordinate Ascent Programming (CCCP) could be beneficial. The necessity and impact of the efficient loss-augmented inference are also questionable, given the relatively small tree depths (≤16) used, where brute-force search is feasible. A more thorough validation of its benefits is required. 

Several points require clarification. What exactly is meant by “leaves” in the context of the inference discussion? Additionally, a crucial baseline is missing: a comparison to a greedy training of non-axis-aligned splits *without* the special initialization proposed by the authors. Finally, the pre-specification of tree depth is a significant limitation that should be discussed more carefully, ideally with experiments varying the depth and analyzing the impact on performance. 

In summary, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, but its significance is currently limited by theoretical concerns and shortcomings in the experimental evaluation. I recommend a rebuttal addressing the points raised, particularly regarding the theoretical justification and the experimental design. A stronger rebuttal, including additional experiments (runtime comparison, more thorough empirical evaluation), may lead to a higher score. Currently, I lean towards a **weak accept** contingent on a convincing rebuttal.
