- Reformulates decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, enabling joint optimization of all split functions.  
- Introduces a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss to enable optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD).  
- Proposes a modified surrogate objective that reduces gradient computation from O(2dp) to O(d^2p), improving efficiency for deep trees.  
- Empirical results show the non-greedy method outperforms standard greedy baselines on benchmark datasets.  
- Connects decision tree optimization to structured prediction literature and contrasts with greedy methods like ID3 and CART.  
- Acknowledges prior global approaches (e.g., multi-linear programming) but emphasizes superior scalability.  
- Highlights inherent sparsity in gradient updates, suggesting potential for growing very deep trees from zero-initialized weights.  
- Strength: novel, theoretically motivated reformulation that enables scalable global optimization of trees.  
- Strength: significant computational efficiency gains that make training deeper trees feasible.  
- Weakness: ambiguous treatment of zero-weight nodes (implicit right-branch assumption) lacks theoretical justification.  
- Weakness: insufficiently rigorous definition of splitting and pruning behavior, especially for zero-weight cases.  
- Concern: use of L2 regularization may reduce interpretability; L1 regularization should be considered and evaluated.  
- Experimental evaluation lacks detail—pruning strategies for greedy baselines and comparisons to simple global strategies (e.g., ensembles on random subsets) are missing.  
- Title misleadingly mentions "forests" despite experiments focusing solely on single trees.  
- Presentation issue: inference descriptions (Sections 6.2 and 6.4) need clearer, more concise explanations.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address zero-weight handling, experimental details, forest claim, and regularization/interpretability tradeoffs.
