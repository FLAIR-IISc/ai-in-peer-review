This paper proposes a global algorithm for learning decision trees with oblique (linear) splits. The core idea is to frame the problem of finding optimal splits as a structured prediction task with latent variables, where the path taken through the tree for a given data point is the latent structure. The authors then derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective function. This objective, regularized with an L2-norm constraint on the split parameters, is optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). This work connects to a long line of research attempting to overcome the limitations of greedy decision tree induction (e.g., CART, ID3) and also draws inspiration from advances in latent variable structural SVMs.

The primary strength of this work is its elegant and novel problem formulation. Casting decision tree learning as a latent variable structured prediction problem provides a principled framework for global, non-greedy optimization, which has long been an NP-complete challenge. This approach is theoretically appealing and cleanly integrates regularization into the learning process.

However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its unconvincing empirical results. The central claim is that this global optimization procedure produces better trees, yet the experiments show it only significantly outperforms standard greedy methods on a few datasets. Given the increased computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, the lack of a consistent and substantial performance gain makes it difficult to justify its adoption over simpler, faster baselines.

Furthermore, the paper suffers from several clarity issues that hinder reproducibility. The description of "Stable SGD" is vague; the mechanism for assigning data points to leaves and updating these assignments is not detailed, and no pseudo-code is provided. Figure 2 is also ambiguous, as it does not specify whether the results correspond to the standard SGD or Stable SGD variant, nor does it define what an "active" leaf is. The practical implementation of projecting Θ onto the simplex in Algorithm 1 also requires further explanation.

In conclusion, while the theoretical formulation is a noteworthy contribution, the experimental evidence does not sufficiently support the claim that this global optimization method offers a significant advantage over established greedy techniques. The paper would be substantially improved by demonstrating stronger empirical gains or by providing a clearer, more reproducible description of the proposed algorithms.
