This paper proposes a novel method for non-greedy, global optimization of decision trees. The central idea is to jointly train all parameters of the tree—the split functions at internal nodes and the posterior distributions at the leaves—by minimizing a single global objective function. Traditional decision tree algorithms like CART and ID3 are greedy, optimizing one node at a time, which can lead to suboptimal solutions. This work addresses this long-standing limitation by framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables, where the path taken through the tree for a given data point is a latent variable. To make the non-convex and discontinuous empirical loss function tractable, the authors derive a continuous, convex-concave upper bound. This surrogate objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which allows for efficient training on large datasets. A key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for the loss-augmented inference step, reducing its complexity from exponential to polynomial in the tree depth, making the training of deeper trees feasible.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and challenging problem in machine learning: the greedy nature of decision tree induction. The proposed direction of performing joint, global optimization is highly original and significant. Moving beyond the greedy paradigm could lead to more compact and accurate trees, and this work provides a principled framework for doing so.

2.  **Novel Formulation:** The connection established between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is insightful. This formulation provides a new lens through which to view the problem and allows the authors to leverage powerful tools from the structured prediction literature, namely the use of a loss-augmented surrogate objective.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The derivation of the surrogate objective function is clean and easy to follow, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithm.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

The paper, while promising, has several weaknesses in its theoretical reasoning and experimental validation that temper its overall impact.

1.  **Potential Flaw in Theoretical Bound:** The core theoretical contribution, the upper bound on the empirical loss (Eq. 7), appears to have a flaw. The argument justifying the bound is confusing, particularly around lines 216f (referring to the proof of Prop. 1). A simple counterexample seems to show that the claimed inequality may not hold universally. Furthermore, the proof for Proposition 1 provided in the supplementary material is incomplete and not fully convincing. This is a critical issue that undermines the technical soundness of the paper's foundation. A rigorous and complete proof is necessary.

2.  **Optimization and Objective Issues:**
    *   The paper notes that some leaves receive no data points during training. This is a significant concern, as it suggests the full capacity of the tree is not being utilized and may indicate problems with the objective function, the initialization, or the SGD optimization dynamics.
    *   The proposed "Stable SGD" (SSGD) heuristic, designed to address this issue by fixing leaf assignments, feels ad-hoc and is not well-motivated. A more principled approach, such as an Expectation-Maximization (EM) or a Concave-Convex Procedure (CCCP) framework, could provide a more theoretically grounded way to alternate between optimizing assignments and model parameters.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is not thorough enough to fully support the paper's claims.
    *   **Impact of Fast Inference:** The necessity and impact of the "fast loss-augmented inference" (restricting the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1) is unclear. For the tree depths tested (up to 16), a brute-force search over all `m+1` leaves might still be computationally feasible. The authors should provide experiments on deeper trees or a direct comparison of performance with and without this approximation to justify its use and quantify its effect on accuracy.
    *   **Missing Baseline:** A crucial baseline is missing: a greedy approach for training oblique (non-axis-aligned) decision trees without the special CO2 initialization. Comparing against such a baseline would help isolate the performance gains attributable to the non-greedy optimization versus the initialization strategy.
    *   **Tree Depth as a Hyperparameter:** The tree depth must be pre-specified, which is a significant limitation. This should be more explicitly discussed as a limitation of the method. The experiments should also more carefully evaluate the effect of this choice, showing how performance and overfitting vary across a wider range of depths.
    *   **Missing Runtime Comparison:** The paper lacks a runtime comparison against the baseline methods. While Figure 4 compares the two proposed inference schemes, it does not show how the overall training time of the non-greedy method compares to standard greedy algorithms. This is essential for understanding the practical trade-offs.

**Questions and Minor Suggestions:**

*   **Terminology:** In the discussion of loss-augmented inference, what is meant by "solving (11) for every j"? Does this refer to all `m+1` leaves in the tree, or only a subset? Please clarify.
*   **Notation:** In the loss bound expression (e.g., Eq. 7), `max` should be used instead of `argmax`, as the expression evaluates to the maximum value, not the argument that achieves it.

In summary, this paper introduces an original and valuable approach to the important problem of non-greedy decision tree learning. The core idea is strong and the formulation is elegant. However, the work is hampered by a potential flaw in its central theoretical claim and an experimental evaluation that is not comprehensive enough to fully demonstrate the method's advantages and limitations. Addressing the theoretical concerns with a rigorous proof and strengthening the experiments with additional baselines and analyses would significantly improve the paper. I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can provide a clear rebuttal that addresses these critical points.
