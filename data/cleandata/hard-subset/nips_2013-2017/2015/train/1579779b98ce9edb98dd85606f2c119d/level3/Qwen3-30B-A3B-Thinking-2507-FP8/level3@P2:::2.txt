

# Review for "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a novel approach to decision tree learning by formulating the joint optimization of all split functions and leaf parameters as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, deriving a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient optimization using stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is an efficient loss-augmented inference procedure with O(d²p) complexity, making deep tree training feasible.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds upon the well-established observation that greedy decision tree induction (e.g., ID3, CART) has limitations due to its sequential, local optimization approach. Previous work has explored non-greedy methods (e.g., Bennett [1]), but these often face computational intractability or are limited to specific loss functions. The authors make a significant contribution by framing decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables, which enables the use of recent advances in structured prediction to develop an efficient optimization framework. This approach differs from soft-split methods (e.g., Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13]) and margin-based approaches (e.g., Bennett et al. [3]) by maintaining the computational advantages of hard splits while enabling global optimization.

## Strengths

**Originality (5/5):** The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is novel and well-motivated. The paper makes a clear contribution to a long-standing problem in machine learning, with the approach being significantly different from recent NIPS papers on decision trees (e.g., [18, 22]).

**Significance (4/5):** The problem addressed is important for practitioners using decision trees, as non-greedy optimization could lead to better generalization. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets, suggesting potential impact.

**Clarity (4/5):** The paper is generally well-written, with clear exposition of the main ideas and a logical flow. The algorithmic details are well-presented, though some terminology (e.g., "leaves" in the inference context) could be clarified.

## Weaknesses

**Quality (3/5):** The paper contains a critical theoretical flaw in the empirical loss bound. Specifically, the argument around line 216 (and Proposition 1 in the supplement) is incomplete and potentially incorrect. An example can be constructed where the claimed upper bound does not hold, undermining the theoretical foundation of the approach. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement is incomplete, as it fails to address the case when Wx has elements near zero.

The implementation has notable issues: some leaves receive no data points (indicating potential problems with the objective function or initialization), and the fixed-assignment heuristic (SSGD) appears suboptimal. The authors do not adequately justify why this heuristic is better than standard EM or CCCP approaches for latent variable models.

The necessity of the efficient loss-augmented inference procedure is questionable. The paper states that the tree depth is limited to ≤16, which makes brute-force loss-augmented inference feasible (O(2^d) for d=16 is about 65,000 operations, manageable for modern hardware). The authors should provide a broader validation of the Hamming ball approach beyond just demonstrating computational speedup.

**Significance (3/5):** The paper's significance is reduced by several limitations: 1) Tree depth must be pre-specified (a significant limitation not adequately addressed), 2) Missing baseline comparison to greedy training of non-axis-aligned splits (only compared to axis-aligned trees), 3) The theoretical flaw affects confidence in the approach.

## Specific Comments

1. The bound in Equation (7) appears incorrect as stated. Consider a case where Wx has one element exactly at zero, and the loss term is large for some g. The bound may not hold in this case.

2. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement is incomplete. It should address the case when Wx has elements near zero, which is critical for the bound's tightness.

3. The term "leaves" in the inference context (e.g., "leaves that are assigned to at least one training data point") is confusing. In standard decision tree terminology, leaves are the terminal nodes, but the paper uses "leaves" to refer to both the terminal nodes and the assignment of data points to those nodes.

4. The paper should replace "argmax" with "max" in the loss bound expression, as the maximum may not be attained at a unique point.

5. The authors should provide a runtime comparison between their fast loss-augmented inference and brute-force inference for deeper trees (e.g., d=20) to better justify the need for the Hamming ball approach.

6. The limitation that tree depth must be pre-specified is significant and should be discussed more thoroughly. The paper should include experiments varying tree depth to demonstrate how the method behaves as depth increases.

7. The paper should include a comparison to a greedy training of non-axis-aligned splits (e.g., OC1 [17] trained greedily on the same datasets) as a baseline.

## Overall Assessment

This paper addresses an important problem with a potentially valuable approach, but the theoretical flaw in the empirical loss bound and the incomplete proof of Proposition 1 significantly undermine the quality of the work. The experimental results are promising, but the missing baseline comparison and the questionable necessity of the efficient inference procedure limit the significance of the findings.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Important research direction, clear theoretical connection to structured prediction, consistent experimental improvements over greedy baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Theoretical flaw in the empirical loss bound, incomplete proof of Proposition 1, missing baseline comparison, questionable necessity of efficient inference procedure, limitation of pre-specified tree depth.

Given these concerns, I recommend rejection. The theoretical flaw is fundamental to the approach and cannot be easily fixed without major revisions. The authors would need to provide a corrected theoretical foundation before the paper could be considered for acceptance.
