This paper proposes a non-greedy, globally optimized training procedure for decision trees with fixed topology, casting oblique split learning as a latent structured prediction problem. The authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss, enabling stochastic optimization of all splits and leaf parameters jointly. A key computational contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference restricted to a Hamming ball around current decisions, reducing update cost to O(d^2 p) and making deep trees feasible. The work is situated relative to global tree optimization (Bennett), latent/structural SVMs (Taskar; Tsochantaridis; Yu & Joachims), oblique trees (OC1), and the CO2 approach; it also contrasts with soft trees/HME (Jordan & Jacobs) and online forests (Mondrian).

Quality: The formulation and surrogate are technically sound and carefully motivated; the optimization is nonconvex but well-specified, with clear pseudocode and complexity analysis. However, the empirical gains over strong greedy baselines are modest on some datasets, and the paper would benefit from a deeper examination of generalization (beyond illustrative plots) and an ablation of the surrogate’s tightness/regularization.

Clarity: The paper is elegant and very clearly written. Notation, assumptions, and algorithms are explicit, and the relationship to prior work is well explained.

Originality: The link between tree optimization and latent structured prediction, plus the fast loss-augmented inference, is novel within the tree-learning literature and distinct from CO2’s nodewise optimization.

Significance: Joint optimization of tree parameters is an important problem; the proposed relaxation is likely to spur follow-up work on better surrogates, hybrid training, and scaling. That said, the current empirical improvements are not uniformly strong.

Suggestions for improvement:
- After global optimization, drop the last level and re-optimize the final split and leaf parameters via standard greedy/CART-style criteria (or refit leaf models) to recover local adjustments that the global surrogate may miss.
- Provide a thorough overfitting analysis: report train–validation gaps versus depth and ν; compare to cost-complexity post-pruning; test stability under label noise; and evaluate whether increased global flexibility exacerbates overfitting.
- Broaden empirical context: include comparisons to soft trees/HME and modern oblique/boosted baselines; assess whether per-tree gains transfer to small ensembles.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation with novel connection to latent structured prediction
- Solid algorithmic contribution (fast inference) and good clarity
- Addresses an important, long-standing limitation of greedy trees

Arguments against acceptance:
- Mixed empirical improvements and limited overfitting analysis
- Nonconvex surrogate’s practical advantages over strong baselines not uniformly compelling
- Fixed topology assumption; topology learning/pruning is only indirectly handled via inactivity
