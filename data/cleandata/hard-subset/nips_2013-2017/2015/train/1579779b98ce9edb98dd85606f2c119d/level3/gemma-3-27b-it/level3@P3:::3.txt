This paper proposes a novel approach to training decision trees by framing the optimization of split functions as a structured prediction problem. The core idea is to introduce latent variables representing split decisions and formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling efficient training via stochastic gradient descent. While the experimental results demonstrate improvements over greedy baselines, these improvements are generally small.

The paper builds upon decades of work in decision tree induction (Quinlan, 1993; Breiman et al., 1984) which are traditionally greedy algorithms like ID3 and CART. Recent efforts have focused on improving these with new objective functions (Nowozin, 2014), but this work directly addresses the limitation of greedy methods by proposing a joint optimization framework. The connection to structured prediction (Taskar et al., 2004; Yu & Joachims, 2009) is insightful, drawing parallels to related problems and leveraging techniques like loss-augmented inference.  Specifically, the formulation echoes work on structured SVMs and their use of upper bounds for efficient optimization (Joachims et al., 2009). Related work on non-greedy tree learning exists (Bennett, 1998; Bennett & Blue, 1998), but often suffers from scalability issues, which this work attempts to alleviate through its efficient optimization strategy. The comparison to CO2 (Norouzi et al., 2015) provides a good point of reference, demonstrating the benefits of the full non-greedy optimization.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The framing of decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem is a significant contribution, offering a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem.
* **Efficient Optimization:** The development of a convex-concave upper bound and the reduction of gradient computation complexity to O(d2p) are crucial for enabling the training of deep trees on large datasets. The fast loss-augmented inference is particularly valuable.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a clear theoretical analysis, including a proof of the upper bound and a discussion of the regularization parameter ν. Proposition 1 is a useful insight.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, while modest in magnitude, consistently show improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Improvement:** The improvements over greedy baselines are generally small. The practical significance of these gains warrants further investigation. The paper doesn’t thoroughly discuss the scenarios where the proposed method is *most* beneficial.
* **Dependency on Initialization:** The performance seems to be reliant on the initialization with an axis-aligned tree and CO2. The sensitivity to this initialization isn’t thoroughly analyzed.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The tuning of the regularization parameter ν is crucial, and the discussion on how to select it is limited. A more detailed analysis of the impact of ν on performance and tree structure would be beneficial.
* **Complexity Analysis:** While the complexity is reduced to O(d2p), the constant factors and practical implications of this complexity, especially for extremely deep trees and high-dimensional data, aren’t fully addressed.
* **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons to several baselines are made, a comparison to more recent and advanced tree-based methods (e.g., gradient boosting machines like XGBoost or LightGBM) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to decision tree learning.
* The proposed optimization strategy is efficient and scalable, addressing a key limitation of previous non-greedy methods.
* The experimental results, although modest, demonstrate consistent improvements over established baselines.
* The work has the potential to inspire further research in non-greedy tree learning and structured prediction.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The practical impact of the improvements is limited.
* The performance is sensitive to initialization and hyperparameter tuning.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is incomplete.
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the computational complexity and its practical implications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The novel formulation and efficient optimization strategy are commendable. While the practical improvements are modest, the theoretical foundations and potential for future research are significant. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of the results and a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.  The authors should also address the sensitivity to initialization in the revised manuscript.
