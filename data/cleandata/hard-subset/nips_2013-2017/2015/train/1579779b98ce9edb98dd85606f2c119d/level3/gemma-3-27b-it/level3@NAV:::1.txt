## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper presents an elegant approach to optimizing decision trees globally, moving beyond the limitations of traditional greedy methods. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss that facilitates efficient stochastic gradient descent training. The core idea of linking tree learning to structured prediction is a clever one, and the resulting formulation allows for joint optimization of split functions and leaf parameters. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the technical details accessible. It builds upon and references relevant prior work in decision tree learning (e.g., Bennett [1], Murthy & Salzburg [17]) and structured prediction (e.g., Taskar et al. [24]), demonstrating a good understanding of the field. 

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical grounding and the potential for improvement over greedy methods. The proposed algorithm’s complexity of O(d2p) is a significant advancement, making the training of deep trees feasible. The regularization strategy, addressing potential overfitting concerns, is also well-considered. 

However, the experimental results are somewhat disappointing. While the authors report outperforming greedy baselines, the gains appear modest and are not consistently demonstrated across all datasets. The performance difference isn’t dramatic enough to definitively establish the superiority of the proposed method, especially given the increased complexity. The paper acknowledges this and appropriately frames the score as acceptable, but stronger results would significantly bolster the contribution. 

I strongly suggest, as a follow-up, implementing the reviewer’s suggested approach of dropping and re-optimizing the last split and leaf node using standard methods after the global optimization. This could potentially refine the tree structure and improve performance. Furthermore, a more thorough investigation into the potential for overfitting is crucial. It’s essential to quantify whether the global optimization, while improving training performance, leads to a detrimental impact on generalization, even with regularization. Investigating the learned tree structures and comparing their complexity with those generated by greedy methods would be insightful. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Technically sound and novel approach to a well-known problem.
*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   Potential for significant impact, sparking further research in non-greedy tree optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Experimental results are not overwhelmingly convincing.
*   Need for more rigorous analysis of overfitting potential.



Overall, this is a valuable contribution with a strong theoretical foundation. Addressing the concerns regarding experimental validation and overfitting would significantly strengthen the paper and justify its publication.
