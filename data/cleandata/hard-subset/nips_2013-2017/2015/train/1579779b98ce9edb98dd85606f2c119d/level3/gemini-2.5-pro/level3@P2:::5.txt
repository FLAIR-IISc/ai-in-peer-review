This paper proposes a novel, non-greedy algorithm for learning decision trees with oblique (linear) split functions. The core idea is to frame the problem of jointly optimizing all split functions and leaf parameters as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The sequence of binary decisions that routes an example to a leaf is treated as a latent variable. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective function. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm that reduces the complexity of each SGD update from being exponential in the tree depth to being quadratic, making the approach feasible for deeper trees. This work is related to a long line of research on decision tree induction (e.g., CART, ID3), but departs from the standard greedy, node-by-node optimization in favor of a global objective. It also builds upon recent advances in structured SVMs with latent variables.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Elegance of Formulation:** The primary strength of this paper is its elegant and novel formulation. Casting the notoriously difficult problem of global decision tree optimization as a structured prediction task with latent variables is a significant conceptual contribution. This provides a principled optimization framework for a problem typically solved with heuristics.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss is sound. The development of the "fast loss-augmented inference" method to make the optimization practical is a valuable technical contribution that addresses a critical scalability bottleneck. The use of SGD makes the method applicable to large datasets.

3.  **Generality:** The proposed framework is general. It can handle various loss functions (as demonstrated with log loss and squared loss) and offers a natural way to incorporate regularization to control model complexity and prevent overfitting. The authors also note potential extensions to sparse splits or kernels, highlighting the framework's potential significance.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Unconvincing Empirical Results:** The main weakness of the paper lies in its experimental evaluation. The central claim is that a non-greedy, global optimization approach produces better trees. However, the results presented in Figure 2 do not provide compelling evidence for this claim. The performance gains of the proposed "non-greedy" trees over the greedy baselines (particularly the closely related CO2) are marginal on several datasets. Given that the proposed method is significantly more computationally intensive than greedy approaches, the small and inconsistent improvements in accuracy may not justify the additional complexity. The paper would be much stronger if it demonstrated a more substantial and consistent advantage.

2.  **Lack of Clarity:** The paper suffers from several clarity issues that hinder understanding and reproducibility.
    *   The description of the "Stable SGD" (SSGD) algorithm is vague. It is not clear how the leaf assignments `a(x)` are initialized or updated. This is a crucial detail for an algorithm presented as a key variant of the optimization procedure.
    *   The experimental results in Figure 2 do not specify whether they were generated using the standard SGD or the SSGD variant. This makes it difficult to assess the performance of either specific algorithm.
    *   The term "active leaf" is used in Figure 3 but is never defined in the text, leaving the reader to guess its meaning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a highly novel and elegant formulation that connects decision tree learning to structured prediction, providing a new and principled perspective on a classic problem.
*   The technical contributions, particularly the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm, are sound and address a key computational challenge.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental results are weak and do not convincingly support the central claim that the proposed global optimization method yields significantly better trees than simpler, greedy baselines.
*   The paper has significant clarity issues, especially in the description of the Stable SGD algorithm, which compromises the reproducibility of the work.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

I believe the paper's formulation is interesting and promising, but the current manuscript is not yet ready for publication. I would encourage the authors to address the following points to strengthen the paper:

1.  **Clarity of Stable SGD:** Please provide a much more detailed description of the Stable SGD (SSGD) algorithm. How is the leaf assignment `a(x)` in Eq. (14) determined and updated? At what frequency are these assignments re-evaluated? A pseudo-code block for the SSGD procedure would be invaluable for clarity and reproducibility.

2.  **Clarity in Figures:** In Figure 2, please specify whether the results for the "non-greedy" method were obtained using standard SGD or SSGD. Also, please provide a clear definition of an "active leaf" in the main text before it is mentioned in the caption of Figure 3.

3.  **Algorithm 1 Details:** In Algorithm 1, line 10 describes the gradient update for the leaf parameters Θ. Since these are unnormalized log-probabilities for a softmax, can you clarify how the gradient `∂/∂Θ l(ΘTf(ĝ), y)` is computed?

4.  **Minor Corrections:**
    *   In Equations (7), (8), and (9), the operators `argmax` should be replaced with `max`, as the bound relies on the value of the maximum, not the argument that achieves it.
    *   Figure 1 is included but never referenced in the main text.
    *   The acronym "FSGD" is used on line 360 but is never defined.
    *   Please proofread the manuscript for minor typos and awkward phrasing (e.g., lines 141, 238, 301, 404, 407-408).

5.  **Supplementary Material:** Please review the notation in the supplementary material. On line 29, the notation should specify equality (e.g., `sgn(w_i^T x) = -1`) rather than set membership. On line 30, the argument to the loss function `l` should be the vector of leaf parameters `Θ^T f(sgn(Wx))`, not `sgn(Wx)`.

Overall, this paper presents an exciting idea, but its claims need to be substantiated with stronger empirical evidence and the presentation must be improved for clarity and reproducibility.
