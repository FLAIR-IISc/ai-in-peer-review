This paper presents a novel non-greedy algorithm for learning decision trees with oblique (linear-combination) splits. The central idea is to formulate the joint optimization of all split functions and leaf node parameters as a single global objective. To make this intractable problem feasible, the authors establish an elegant connection to structured prediction with latent variables, where the path an example takes through the tree is treated as a latent variable. They derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is a "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm that reduces the complexity of each SGD step from being exponential in tree depth to quadratic, making the approach scalable to deeper trees. This work departs from traditional greedy, node-by-node tree induction methods (e.g., CART, ID3) and offers a more holistic optimization framework, aiming to produce more compact and accurate trees.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper tackles a classic, challenging, and important problem in machine learning: the sub-optimality of greedy decision tree induction. The proposed framework is a significant step towards a more principled, global optimization approach.

**Originality and Significance:**
The primary strength of this paper is its novel formulation. Casting decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables is an original and insightful contribution. This approach provides a well-defined optimization framework for a problem that has historically been addressed with heuristics. If the method proves to be sound and effective, it could have a significant impact on how tree-based models are trained, potentially leading to better performance and more compact models.

**Quality:**
The derivation of the surrogate objective is presented cleanly. However, there are several points concerning the technical quality and experimental evaluation that weaken the submission.

1.  **Potential Flaw in the Loss Bound:** The validity of the main upper bound in Equation (7) is a critical concern. The argument provided is not fully convincing, and it seems possible to construct a counterexample where the inequality does not hold. A rigorous proof is necessary to substantiate this central claim. Similarly, the proof for Proposition 1, which justifies the regularization on W, is incomplete in the supplementary material. These potential theoretical issues need to be resolved, as they form the foundation of the entire method.

2.  **Optimization Issues and Heuristics:** The paper notes that the optimization can lead to "inactive leaves" (leaves that are not assigned any training data), which suggests a potential issue with the objective function or the optimization dynamics. The proposed solution, Stable SGD (SSGD), which temporarily fixes data-to-leaf assignments, feels like an ad-hoc heuristic. It would strengthen the paper to motivate this approach more formally, perhaps by connecting it to established optimization schemes like Expectation-Maximization (EM) or the Concave-Convex Procedure (CCCP), which employ similar alternating optimization steps.

3.  **Impact of Fast Inference:** The paper proposes a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm to improve scalability. While the theoretical speed-up from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) is clear, the practical necessity and impact of the approximation are not well-established. The experiments are run on trees with depths up to 18. The "fast" version achieves its speed by restricting the search for the loss-augmenting path to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current best path. This is a very strong restriction. The paper needs to provide a more thorough analysis of the trade-off: how much accuracy is lost for the gain in speed? An ablation study comparing the final accuracy of models trained with the exact vs. the fast inference on a smaller tree would be highly valuable.

4.  **Incomplete Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed non-greedy trees outperform several baselines. However, a crucial baseline is missing: a greedy method for learning oblique splits that is not initialized in a special way (e.g., a standard implementation of OC1). Comparing against axis-aligned and random-split trees does not fully isolate the benefit of the non-greedy approach over a strong greedy oblique-split competitor. Furthermore, the tree depth must be pre-specified, which is a major limitation. The paper should include a more thorough discussion of this limitation and an experimental analysis of the model's sensitivity to this key hyperparameter.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. A few minor points could be improved:
*   In the loss bound expressions (e.g., Eq. 7), it is more conventional to use `max` to denote the value of the maximization, rather than `argmax` which denotes the maximizing argument.
*   The terminology around "leaves" in the context of the loss-augmented inference could be clarified to avoid ambiguity.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and long-standing problem of non-greedy decision tree learning.
*   Presents a highly original and elegant formulation connecting tree learning to latent structured prediction.
*   The proposed global optimization framework is principled and shows promising empirical performance against several baselines.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Potential theoretical flaws in the core upper bound (Eq. 7) and its justification (Prop. 1) that need urgent clarification.
*   Practical issues during optimization (inactive leaves) are handled by a heuristic (SSGD) that lacks strong motivation.
*   The significance of the "fast inference" approximation is not sufficiently validated in terms of its impact on accuracy.
*   The experimental evaluation is missing a key greedy oblique-tree baseline and a sensitivity analysis for tree depth.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces a valuable and original research direction for decision tree optimization. The connection to structured prediction is compelling. However, the potential theoretical issues with the loss bound and the shortcomings in the experimental evaluation are significant concerns. I would be willing to reconsider my assessment pending a clear and convincing author rebuttal that addresses these critical points, particularly the soundness of the upper bound.
