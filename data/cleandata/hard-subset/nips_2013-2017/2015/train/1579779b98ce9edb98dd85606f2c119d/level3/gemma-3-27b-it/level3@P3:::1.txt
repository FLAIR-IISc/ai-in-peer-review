This paper proposes an elegant and promising approach to globally optimizing decision trees with a fixed structure, moving beyond the traditionally greedy methods. The formulation, linking decision tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables and leveraging a convex-concave upper bound, is technically sound and well-presented. The paper is clearly written, logically organized, and provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand and potentially reproduce the results. It appropriately references related work, demonstrating awareness of the field. This work addresses a challenging problem and, despite some disappointing experimental results, has the potential to spur further research in non-greedy tree optimization.

The core contribution lies in formulating a tractable surrogate objective for global optimization. The reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is significant, enabling training of deeper trees. The connection to structured prediction and the introduction of a regularizer to control overfitting are valuable additions. The paper demonstrates a good understanding of existing methods and clearly articulates the limitations of greedy approaches.

However, the experimental results are somewhat underwhelming. While the paper reports outperformance compared to greedy baselines, the magnitude of improvement appears modest. The comparison to CO2, a greedy method based on the same upper bound formulation, is useful, but the gains are not substantial across all datasets. The impact of the regularization parameter ν is well-analyzed, but further investigation into the interplay between regularization, tree depth, and dataset size would be beneficial.

A significant point that the authors acknowledge, and warrants further exploration, is the potential for overfitting with global optimization. While the regularization attempts to mitigate this, a targeted analysis specifically evaluating the generalization gap (difference between training and test performance) for the non-greedy approach versus greedy baselines is crucial. It's important to quantify whether the global optimization truly leads to better generalization, or simply a more complex model that fits the training data more closely.

I suggest a simple yet potentially effective post-processing step: after the global optimization, drop the last split and its corresponding leaf node, then re-optimize *only* that split using a standard greedy approach (e.g., information gain). This localized refinement could potentially correct any suboptimal decisions made during the global optimization process, without entirely sacrificing the benefits of the joint optimization. This could be framed as a form of ‘local search’ after the global optimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach to global decision tree optimization via a convex-concave surrogate is novel and addresses a long-standing challenge.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical justification and the reduction in computational complexity are well-motivated and rigorously presented.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Potential Impact:** The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of decision tree learning, opening up new avenues for research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Modest Experimental Gains:** The empirical results, while positive, are not overwhelmingly convincing.
*   **Overfitting Concerns:** The potential for overfitting with global optimization requires further investigation.
*   **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the O(d2p) complexity is a significant improvement, a discussion of scalability to very large datasets and high-dimensional feature spaces would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance. While the experimental results are not dramatic, the technical innovation and the potential for future research outweigh this weakness. Addressing the concerns regarding overfitting and exploring the proposed post-processing step could further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to include a more detailed analysis of generalization performance and a discussion of the proposed local refinement strategy.
