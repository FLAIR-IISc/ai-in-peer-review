- Proposes non-greedy training for oblique decision trees by casting split selection as a latent structured prediction problem with binary split bits.
- Derives a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss and optimizes it with (projected) stochastic gradient descent.
- Introduces a fast loss-augmented inference that restricts search to a Hamming ball of radius 1, reducing per-example inference from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p).
- Regularizes each row of the split matrix W with per-row norm constraints to improve generalization and prevent bound degeneration.
- Proposes Stable SGD that holds leaf assignments fixed for several updates to avoid leaf inactivity.
- Experiments on multiple LibSVM multiclass datasets show consistent but modest test accuracy gains over greedy baselines (CART, OC1, random oblique) and CO2 across depths.
- Complexity measurements support scalability claims for the fast inference variant.
- Situates work well relative to greedy, oblique, soft/probabilistic trees, and the authors’ prior CO2; links to latent structural SVMs and max-margin methods.
- Technical derivations and bounds appear sound; Hamming-ball argument (≤ d+1 affected leaves) is plausible and aligns with tree structure.
- Empirical support is limited by absence of statistical significance tests and relatively few ablations (initialization, radius choice).
- Manuscript is clear and well-organized, with helpful intuition, pseudocode, and reasonable use of supplementary proofs.
- Novelty lies in mapping tree training to latent structured prediction and the convex–concave bound with a practical radius-1 inference trick.
- Methodological significance is high as a scalable framework others can extend; practical impact is moderate because ensembles dominate real-world accuracy.
- Pros: principled formulation, efficient algorithmic innovation, per-row regularization, consistent improvements, and practical training details.
- Cons: small accuracy gains, no comparisons to ensemble baselines, unclear robustness across seeds/splits, sensitivity to initialization, limited theoretical guarantees, and unexplored larger Hamming radii.
- Suggestions: report variance and statistical tests, compare to pruned greedy/soft trees and ensembles, perform sensitivity and ablation studies, explore sparse splits and adaptive radii, and release code/hyperparameters.
- Verdict: Solid, original, and well-executed work; recommend acceptance conditional on stronger empirical robustness and better contextualization against ensemble baselines.
