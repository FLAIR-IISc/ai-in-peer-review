

This paper proposes a novel approach to decision tree learning by framing the optimization of fixed-depth trees as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree induction and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized using stochastic gradient descent. Their key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p), making deep tree training feasible. The method outperforms standard greedy approaches on several classification benchmarks, though improvements are modest (typically 0.5-1.5% accuracy gains).

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding, showing how the decision tree problem maps to structured prediction with latent variables and deriving a valid upper bound. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple baselines (including OC1, CO2, and random oblique trees) across four datasets. The authors carefully address overfitting through norm regularization and provide detailed ablation studies on hyperparameters.

Strengths include a clear theoretical contribution that bridges decision trees and structured prediction, a well-motivated solution to a longstanding problem in tree learning, and solid empirical validation. The paper is well-written with clear problem formulation and logical flow. The approach is significant as it offers a principled non-greedy alternative to standard tree induction methods.

Weaknesses include the modest empirical gains that may not justify the added complexity for many practitioners. The paper doesn't sufficiently explore the trade-off between computational cost and performance improvement, and the connection to structured prediction feels somewhat superficial rather than deeply integrated. The significance is somewhat limited by the small absolute gains, though the method could be valuable for specialized applications where tree quality matters more than efficiency.

Pro: Novel framing of decision tree learning as structured prediction, theoretically sound approach with efficient implementation, thorough empirical evaluation.
Con: Modest performance gains relative to computational complexity, limited discussion of practical utility, connection to structured prediction could be deeper.

Overall, this is a technically sound contribution that advances the state of the art in decision tree learning. While the improvements are not revolutionary, the method provides a principled non-greedy alternative to standard approaches. The paper would be a good fit for NIPS given its theoretical contributions to a fundamental machine learning problem. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better contextualize the practical significance of the modest improvements.
