### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel and elegant approach for the global optimization of decision trees. Standard tree induction algorithms, such as ID3 and CART, are greedy, optimizing split functions one node at a time. This local optimization can lead to suboptimal tree configurations. The authors address this long-standing issue by proposing a non-greedy algorithm that jointly optimizes all split functions (for an oblique tree with a fixed structure) and leaf node parameters under a global objective.

The key insight is to formulate the decision tree learning problem as one of structured prediction with latent variables. The sequence of binary decisions that routes an example to a leaf is treated as a latent variable. This perspective allows the authors to derive a continuous, convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a tractable surrogate objective. This objective is then optimized using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). A significant technical contribution is a method for "fast loss-augmented inference" that reduces the per-example gradient computation complexity from exponential to polynomial in the tree depth, making the optimization of deep trees feasible.

This work relates to a long line of research on decision tree optimization. It moves beyond the limitations of early non-greedy approaches like Bennett [1], which were computationally prohibitive, and offers a more principled global alternative to methods that perform "look-ahead" or use soft splits, like Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts [13]. The core optimization framework builds upon recent advances in structured SVMs with latent variables [25].

**Strengths (Pros)**

*   **Clarity and Elegance:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and the proposed formulation is elegant. The connection between global decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and novel. The derivation of the surrogate objective and the optimization procedure are presented very clearly.
*   **Originality and Significance:** This work tackles a fundamental and difficult problem in machine learning. The proposed framework is a principled and promising direction for non-greedy tree learning. By casting the problem in a well-understood optimization framework (structured prediction), the paper opens the door to numerous extensions (e.g., different regularizers, kernelization mentioned by the authors). This contribution is likely to spur significant follow-up research.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation of the convex-concave upper bound is sound. The development of the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm is a crucial technical contribution that makes the approach practical for reasonably deep trees, overcoming a major computational hurdle.

**Weaknesses (Cons)**

*   **Experimental Results:** While the proposed non-greedy trees consistently outperform the greedy baselines across several datasets, the performance gains are somewhat modest. Given the sophistication of the optimization machinery, one might have hoped for more substantial improvements in accuracy. The results are sufficient to demonstrate the method's potential but are not a knockout.
*   **Analysis of Overfitting:** The authors claim their method is less susceptible to overfitting due to the norm regularization on the split parameters. While this is plausible, the paper lacks a direct empirical analysis to support this claim. A global optimization procedure has more degrees of freedom than a greedy one and could potentially be more prone to fitting noise in the training data. It is important to explicitly evaluate whether this global optimization approach increases the risk of overfitting compared to a well-regularized greedy baseline of the same complexity.
*   **Fixed Tree Structure:** The method optimizes the parameters of a tree with a predefined structure (i.e., fixed depth). The algorithm is initialized with a greedily grown tree and then refines its parameters. The influence of this initialization on the final performance is not deeply explored, and the method does not address the structural aspect of tree learning.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  To potentially improve empirical results, it might be beneficial to combine the strengths of global and local optimization. After the global SGD optimization has converged, the final splits (especially near the bottom of the tree) may not be perfectly aligned with the data partitions they receive. I suggest the authors try a post-processing step: for the last layer of splits, drop the learned split function and its leaf nodes, and re-optimize them using a standard greedy criterion (e.g., information gain) on the data that reaches those parent nodes. This hybrid approach could yield a practical performance boost.

2.  To strengthen the claims about overfitting, the authors should include an experiment that directly measures it. For instance, a plot showing the gap between training accuracy and test accuracy as a function of tree depth for both the proposed method and the strongest baseline. This would provide more convincing evidence for the regularizing effect of the method.

**Final Assessment**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant contribution to the field. The formulation is elegant, the approach is novel, and the work is technically sound. It addresses a classic problem in a principled way and is very likely to inspire future research. While the experimental results are somewhat disappointing in their magnitude, they are positive and demonstrate the validity of the approach. The paper clearly merits publication at NIPS.
