

# Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

This paper presents a novel approach to decision tree optimization by reformulating it as a latent structured prediction problem. The authors propose using sign vectors as latent variables to represent decision paths through the tree, enabling a global optimization of split functions across all tree levels rather than the conventional greedy approach. The key insight is to formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, which serves as a differentiable surrogate objective that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent. The authors develop efficient algorithms for loss-augmented inference with O(d²p) complexity, where d is the tree depth and p is the feature dimension, making training deep trees feasible.

The paper makes two main contributions: (1) establishing a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, and (2) developing efficient optimization algorithms for this problem. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms greedy decision tree baselines on multiple classification datasets, particularly showing better generalization performance and reduced overfitting.

**Strengths:** The reformulation of decision tree optimization as latent structured prediction is clever and conceptually clean. The authors successfully bridge a gap between two distinct areas of machine learning (tree-based methods and structured prediction) with significant theoretical justification. The efficient inference algorithms they develop are technically sound and well-motivated, with clear complexity analysis. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets, providing empirical validation of the approach.

**Weaknesses:** Several critical issues undermine the paper's practical contribution and clarity. First, the paper's title mentions "forests" but does not address or evaluate forests, creating confusion. Second, the ambiguity around zero-weight nodes (the paper implies taking the right branch when weights are zero) is problematic; the authors should specify how to handle these cases and define pruning/splitting behavior precisely. Third, the use of L2 regularization likely yields dense, non-interpretable splits, which directly contradicts one of the primary motivations for using decision trees (interpretability). While L1 regularization might enforce sparsity, its potential performance tradeoffs are not tested.

The experimental section has significant weaknesses: baselines lack sufficient detail (e.g., no description of pruning strategies for greedy methods), and the comparison to simple global strategies like multiple greedy trees on random subsets is missing. The paper also fails to analyze whether their method behaves like known greedy strategies or prefers adding new nodes over revising existing ones—this is crucial for understanding the algorithm's behavior.

The paper's claim about "sparse gradient updates" (where each SGD step appears to change only one node's weight vector) is interesting but insufficiently analyzed. This property could enable growing infinitely deep trees from zero weights, but the paper does not explore this direction beyond a brief mention. The post-rebuttal point about zero-weight initialization being a critical case is particularly important—random initialization may mask this issue, but the paper does not provide evidence that the method actually grows trees from zero weights.

**Overall assessment:** This paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to non-greedy decision tree optimization. The reformulation as latent structured prediction is clever, and the efficient inference algorithms make the approach practical. However, the practical usefulness of the method is currently unconvincing due to the interpretability concerns with L2 regularization, ambiguous handling of zero-weight nodes, and insufficient experimental validation. The paper's title and content misalign with the claim of addressing forests, and the experimental section lacks necessary details for proper comparison.

While the theoretical contribution is strong and the approach is interesting, the paper falls short in demonstrating practical utility for the intended application of decision trees (interpretability) and providing sufficient experimental validation to convince reviewers of its significance. The authors should address the interpretability concerns, clarify zero-weight node behavior, provide more detailed baseline comparisons, and clarify the forest-related claims in the title.

**Recommendation:** Weak reject. The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to decision tree optimization, but the current experimental validation and practical considerations undermine its significance for the NIPS community. With substantial revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, this could become a strong contribution.
