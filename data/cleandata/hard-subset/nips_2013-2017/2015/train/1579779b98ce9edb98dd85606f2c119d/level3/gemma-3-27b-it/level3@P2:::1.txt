This paper presents an elegant and technically sound approach to globally optimizing decision trees, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field. The authors formulate a non-convex relaxation allowing for joint optimization of split functions and leaf parameters, moving beyond the limitations of traditional greedy algorithms. The connection drawn between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and well-motivated, leading to a tractable surrogate objective suitable for stochastic gradient descent. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the technical details. It’s a complete piece of work, and the authors demonstrate awareness of both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

The core idea – globally optimizing tree parameters – is a significant contribution. Existing methods, as rightly pointed out, are largely stuck with greedy approaches despite decades of research (references [5, 21, 4, 9, 14]). The proposed formulation and optimization strategy offer a promising alternative. The reduction in gradient computation complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a key technical achievement, enabling training of deeper trees. The connection to structured prediction [25, 23, 24] provides a strong theoretical foundation and leverages advancements in that area.  Related work is thoroughly covered, acknowledging prior attempts at global optimization [1, 15, 13, 17, 2, 3] and clearly differentiating this work from them.  The use of a regularization term to prevent overfitting is a sensible addition.

However, the experimental results, while positive in demonstrating the effectiveness of the non-greedy approach compared to greedy baselines, are somewhat modest. The improvements over the baselines appear incremental rather than substantial. While the authors acknowledge that absolute accuracy varies between datasets, stronger empirical validation with more significant gains would bolster the impact of the work.  The suggestion to potentially improve results by post-optimizing the final split and leaf node using standard methods (as noted in the key points) is a worthwhile avenue for future exploration and could yield more compelling results.

A crucial aspect not fully explored is the potential for increased overfitting with global optimization. While regularization is included, a more detailed analysis examining the generalization gap and the effect of different regularization strengths would be valuable. It's important to demonstrate that the improved performance isn't simply a result of fitting the training data more closely, but rather a genuine improvement in generalization ability.  Furthermore, a more detailed ablation study to analyze the contribution of individual components of the proposed method (e.g., the specific form of the upper bound, the hard constraints on W) would provide further insights.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach to global decision tree optimization is novel and addresses a long-standing problem.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation and optimization strategy are well-justified and theoretically sound.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Efficiency:** The reduction in gradient computation complexity is a significant practical contribution.
*   **Connection to Structured Prediction:** Leveraging ideas from structured prediction provides a strong foundation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Modest Empirical Gains:** The experimental results, while positive, are not overwhelmingly convincing.
*   **Overfitting Analysis:** A more thorough analysis of the potential for increased overfitting is needed.
*   **Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study would help understand the contribution of individual components.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The connection to structured prediction is insightful and provides a strong theoretical foundation.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to a broad audience.
*   The work has the potential to spur further research in global decision tree optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical gains are modest and may not justify the added complexity.
*   The analysis of overfitting is incomplete.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. While the empirical results could be stronger and the overfitting analysis more thorough, the novelty, technical soundness, and clarity of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly a more in-depth investigation of the generalization performance and potential for overfitting.  A clearer presentation of the impact of the regularization parameter ν would also be beneficial.
