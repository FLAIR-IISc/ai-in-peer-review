This paper presents a novel non-greedy algorithm for learning oblique decision trees. The core idea is to globally optimize all split functions and leaf parameters jointly by minimizing a surrogate objective function. The authors make an elegant connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, where the path taken by an example through the tree is treated as a latent variable. Based on this formulation, they derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. This surrogate objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm that reduces the complexity of a gradient step from exponential to quadratic in the tree depth, making the approach feasible for deeper trees. The work is positioned as an improvement over traditional greedy, node-by-node tree induction methods (e.g., CART, ID3) and other non-greedy approaches.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The primary strength of this paper is its formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. This is a theoretically sound and insightful connection that places tree optimization within a well-understood, principled optimization framework. This perspective could inspire future work, such as applying kernel methods or different regularization schemes to decision trees.

2.  **Principled Global Optimization:** Unlike heuristic or greedy methods, this work proposes a global objective function—a continuous and piecewise-smooth upper bound on the empirical loss—that is optimized directly. This is a more principled approach to finding a good configuration of tree parameters. The use of L2-norm constraints on the split weights provides a clear mechanism for regularization to prevent overfitting.

3.  **Efficient Optimization for Deep Trees:** The authors astutely identify the computational bottleneck of their initial formulation (exponential dependence on tree depth `d`) and propose a "fast loss-augmented inference" method that restricts the search space. This reduces the per-step complexity to O(d²p), a significant improvement that makes the global optimization approach practical for moderately deep trees.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unconvincing Empirical Results:** The main weakness is that the experimental results do not provide compelling evidence that the proposed non-greedy approach offers a significant advantage over simpler, greedy methods. While the proposed method outperforms the baselines on some datasets, the margin is often small. Given the substantially higher computational cost and complexity of the global optimization procedure, the practical benefit is questionable. A stronger empirical validation, perhaps including comparisons against state-of-the-art ensemble methods like Gradient Boosted Trees, would be necessary to demonstrate the significance of the contribution.

2.  **Lack of Clarity on Stable SGD (SSGD):** A key component of the method, the "Stable SGD" (SSGD) variant, is not clearly explained. The paper states that data point assignments to leaves are fixed for several updates, but it is unclear how these assignments (`a(x)` in Eq. 14) are determined and when they are updated. This lack of detail hinders the reproducibility and understanding of a crucial part of the algorithm.

3.  **Limited Scope of Comparison:** The baselines are mostly classical greedy tree algorithms. While comparing to CO2 is a good self-contained experiment, the paper would be much stronger if it included comparisons to modern, highly-optimized decision tree and forest implementations (e.g., from Scikit-learn or LightGBM) or other non-greedy tree learning methods beyond the ones cited.

### Detailed Comments and Questions for Authors

The paper presents an interesting idea, but several aspects require clarification and revision to improve its quality and impact.

**Major Issues:**

*   **Stable SGD:** Please provide a much more detailed description of the Stable SGD (SSGD) algorithm. How are the leaf assignments `a(x)` initialized and updated? Is there a fixed schedule, or is it based on the convergence of the inner optimization loop? A clear pseudo-code for SSGD would be very beneficial.
*   **Figure 2:** Please clarify whether the results reported for your method in Figure 2 were generated using the standard SGD (Algorithm 1) or the Stable SGD variant. The performance difference could be significant, and this information is critical for interpreting the results.
*   **"Active" Leaves:** The term "active leaf" is used to motivate SSGD and appears in Figure 3's caption, but it is never formally defined. Please provide a precise definition (e.g., a leaf that is assigned at least one training example in the current epoch).
*   **Algorithm 1 Details:** Line 10 shows an update for Θ. Since the leaf parameters represent a probability distribution via softmax, they effectively lie on a simplex. How is this constraint handled in practice? Is there a projection step, or is it implicitly managed by the negative log-likelihood loss? Please clarify.

**Minor Issues and Typos:**

*   **Equations (7)-(9):** The use of `argmax` in these equations seems incorrect. Since the value of the maximization is used in the objective function, not the argument that achieves it, `max` should be used instead of `argmax`.
*   **Figure 1:** This figure is illustrative but is never referenced in the body of the paper.
*   **Undefined Acronym:** On line 360, the acronym "FSGD" is used. I assume this refers to SGD with *fast* loss-augmented inference, but this should be explicitly defined.
*   **Text Corrections:**
    *   Line 141: "hence g ∈ B1(sgn(Wx)) implies that g and sgn(Wx) differ in at most one bit." The sentence structure is slightly awkward.
    *   Line 238: The sentence starting "Note that sgn(Wx)TWx is constant in g..." is difficult to parse. Please rephrase for clarity.
    *   Line 301: "...comparison between non-greedy and CO2 [18] trees concentrates on the non-greediness..." Rephrase for better flow.
    *   Lines 407-408: "...applying the kernel trick to the linear split parameters W , making our overall model applicable to learning higher-order split functions or training decision trees on examples in arbitrary Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces." This sentence is a bit dense and could be broken up or simplified.

**Supplementary Material Corrections:**

*   Line 29: The notation `g[i] ∈ sgn(w_i^T x)` should probably be `g[i] = sgn(w_i^T x)` if you are setting bits to match the sign.
*   Line 30: In the proof for the bound, the argument `sgn(Wx)` is used inside the loss function `l(sgn(Wx), y)`. This is inconsistent with the paper's formulation, which should be `l(Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)), y)`.

In summary, the paper introduces an elegant and theoretically interesting approach to non-greedy decision tree optimization. However, its practical significance is not convincingly demonstrated by the current experiments, and several key algorithmic details are insufficiently explained. Addressing the clarity issues and providing stronger empirical validation would significantly improve the paper.
