- Proposes global optimization for decision trees by casting tree induction as latent structured prediction with sign vectors as latent variables.  
- Introduces a differentiable surrogate upper-bounding empirical loss optimized by SGD (non-convex).  
- Key algorithmic contribution: loss-augmented inference with an exact O(mp) routine and a fast O(d^2p) Hamming-ball (radius-1) variant.  
- Connects to latent SSVMs and structured prediction literature; contrasts with OC1, CO2, HME, Bennett’s global tree optimization.  
- Experiments on multiclass datasets report accuracy gains over greedy baselines.  
- Theoretical derivations and the bounding argument are sound and clearly presented.  
- Fast loss-augmented inference is elegant and enables efficient SGD updates.  
- Radius-1 Hamming-ball yields sparse gradient steps—often touching only one node’s weights per update.  
- Practical concern: zero-weight initialization semantics are under-specified and the implicit “take right branch” default is brittle.  
- Practical concern: L2-regularized oblique splits likely produce dense, non-interpretable features, hurting interpretability.  
- Practical concern: experimental baselines and pruning strategies for greedy methods are insufficiently detailed, weakening practical claims.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well written but Sections 6.2/6.4 need compact pseudo-code and a small running example to clarify inference and path-bit updates.  
- Clarity: title mentions forests, but forests are neither developed nor evaluated.  
- Originality: casting global DT optimization as latent structured prediction with efficient loss-augmented inference is novel and interesting.  
- Significance: approach is promising and could inspire follow-ups (adaptive depth, alternative regularizers), but current experiments don’t prove clear practical gains.  
- Suggestion: specify zero-weight node semantics and principled pruning/splitting rules.  
- Suggestion: evaluate L1 or elastic-net regularization and report interpretability metrics.  
- Suggestion: strengthen baselines by detailing pruning and adding simple global strategies (e.g., multiple greedy trees on random subsets).  
- Suggestion: either include forests experimentally or change the title.  
- Suggestion: analyze whether the method prefers adding new nodes versus revising existing ones.  
- Acceptance pro: clever, principled reformulation with efficient algorithms and intriguing sparse updates.  
- Rejection con: ambiguities in edge cases, interpretability issues, limited/underspecified baselines, and title–content mismatch.
