- Proposes a novel non-greedy algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions by optimizing a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss, regularized with L2 and optimized via SGD.  
- Establishes an elegant connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, providing a new perspective.  
- Uses SGD to optimize the bound, enabling scalable training on large datasets.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly explains the overall approach and theoretical connection.  
- Empirical results show non-greedy trees often outperform greedy baselines across depths, but improvements are not significant on all datasets.  
- Computational complexity of the non-greedy algorithm is higher than that of greedy baselines.  
- Describes a variant called Stable SGD (SSGD) that assigns data to leaves more conservatively, but the description is unclear and lacks pseudo-code.  
- Lacks clear explanation of how the exponential growth of latent decision variables with depth d impacts computational complexity.  
- Insufficient explanation of why and how the norm of W is regularized and its effect on performance.  
- Some notation and terminology (e.g., "active" leaf) are not clearly defined.  
- Claims about scale-invariance of a loss term (Eq. 7) are not clearly explained.  
- Originality is strong in the algorithm and the latent-variable connection, but use of SGD is not novel and needs contextualization with prior work.  
- Addresses an important problem and offers a promising non-greedy alternative to greedy tree learning.  
- Empirical evidence and practical applicability need strengthening for convincing impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel algorithm, elegant latent-variable connection, SGD enables scalability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unconvincing empirical gains on some datasets, higher computational cost, and unclear SSGD description.  
- Recommended improvements: provide practical use-case context, clarify and pseudo-code SSGD, detail W-norm regularization effects, clarify complexity scaling with depth, and compare more thoroughly to prior work and with additional experiments.
