This paper presents a novel approach to decision tree learning by framing it as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The core idea is to jointly optimize split functions at all levels of the tree, moving beyond the traditionally greedy, node-by-node approach. This is achieved by introducing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, allowing for optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors demonstrate efficiency gains through a modified surrogate objective, reducing the gradient computation complexity from O(2dp) to O(d^2p), where d is the tree depth and p is the input dimension. Experimental results on benchmark datasets show that the proposed non-greedy decision trees outperform greedy baselines.

This work builds upon a rich history of decision tree research, notably contrasting with the established greedy methods of ID3 [21] and CART [5]. While recent efforts have focused on improved objective functions *within* the greedy framework [20, 12], this paper takes a fundamentally different direction by tackling global optimization.  The connection drawn between decision tree optimization and structured prediction [25] is a key contribution, inspired by advances in areas like [23, 24].  Related work like Bennett [1]’s multi-linear programming approach is acknowledged, but highlighted as limited in scalability. The authors appropriately position their work as improving upon prior art, particularly in terms of efficient learning and the ability to handle general loss functions while maintaining reasonable computational complexity.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. The reformulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem is insightful and innovative. The reduction in computational complexity, from O(2dp) to O(d^2p), is crucial for enabling the training of deep trees, and the use of SGD makes the method scalable to large datasets. The experimental results, while needing further refinement (discussed below), demonstrate promising improvements over greedy baselines.  The inherent sparsity in the gradient updates—where each step primarily impacts a single node’s weights—is a particularly interesting observation.  This suggests the intriguing possibility of growing extremely deep trees from zero-initialized weights, adapting complexity dynamically during training.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The treatment of zero-weight nodes is ambiguous and potentially problematic. The current assumption (implicitly taking the right branch) lacks a clear theoretical justification and could lead to unexpected behavior.  A more rigorous definition of splitting and pruning behavior is needed. Furthermore, the use of L2 regularization, while promoting a tighter bound, could diminish the interpretability of the resulting trees – a key advantage of traditional decision trees.  Exploring L1 regularization as an alternative to encourage sparsity in the split functions would be valuable, but the potential performance tradeoff must be thoroughly investigated.

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating improvements, lacks sufficient detail. The baselines used, while standard, could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison. Specifically, a comparison to simple global strategies like training multiple greedy trees on random subsets of the data would provide a fairer assessment of the proposed method’s benefits.  Details regarding pruning strategies employed for the greedy baselines are lacking, making it difficult to isolate the gains from the non-greedy optimization.  The title alludes to "forests," yet the paper entirely focuses on single trees, a discrepancy that should be addressed. Clarity in the descriptions of inference (Sections 6.2 and 6.4) could be improved with more concise summaries and explicit explanations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound reformulation of decision tree learning.
*   Significant improvement in computational efficiency compared to naive global optimization.
*   Empirically demonstrates improved performance over standard greedy methods.
*   Opens interesting avenues for future research (e.g., zero-weight initialization, dynamic tree growth).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Ambiguity surrounding zero-weight nodes and lack of a clear splitting/pruning definition.
*   Potential loss of interpretability due to L2 regularization.
*   Insufficient detail in the experimental evaluation, particularly regarding baselines and pruning.
*   Limited scope—focuses solely on single trees despite the title's mention of forests.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a clever and well-motivated paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The reformulation as a structured prediction problem and the development of efficient optimization algorithms are commendable. However, the practical usefulness and experimental validation are not entirely convincing in their current form. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the issues related to zero-weight nodes, experimental details, and the forest claim.  The authors should carefully consider and address the potential tradeoff between regularization and interpretability. The post-rebuttal note about zero-weight initialization further underscores the importance of investigating this critical case.
