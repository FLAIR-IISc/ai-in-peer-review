This paper proposes a novel approach to decision tree optimization by reformulating the problem as a latent structured prediction task. The authors introduce a non-differentiable, non-convex loss function and approximate it with a differentiable, yet still non-convex, upper bound. They develop efficient algorithms for minimizing this upper bound using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which requires solving successive loss-augmented inference problems.

The main contribution of this work is casting decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem and developing efficient optimization algorithms for this task. The approach is novel, interesting, and raises several follow-up questions. One of the key benefits of this method is the sparse gradient updates, where each SGD step appears to change only one node's weight vector. This property has implications beyond mere computational advantage, suggesting the possibility of growing an infinitely deep tree from zero weights, letting complexity increase adaptively.

However, there are some concerns and ambiguities that need to be addressed. For instance, the paper implies that when a node's weight is zero, the decision should be made by taking the right branch. However, this is problematic, and instead, the authors should allow output at the node and define pruning/splitting behavior precisely. Furthermore, the use of L2 regularization may yield dense, non-interpretable splits, which could be a concern for practical applications. It is unclear whether L1 regularization would enforce sparsity without degrading performance, and this tradeoff must be tested.

The experimental evaluation of the method has some weaknesses. For instance, there is insufficient detail on the baselines and pruning strategies used for the naive/greedy method. Additionally, it would be beneficial to compare the proposed method to additional simple global strategies, such as multiple greedy trees on random subsets, as fair baselines. The inference descriptions in Sections 6.2 and 6.4 could be summarized or made more explicit to improve clarity.

Another issue is that the title mentions forests, but the paper does not address or evaluate forests. It is unclear whether the method can be extended to forests and how it would perform in such cases.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The optimization problem is well-defined, and the authors provide a clear and concise description of the algorithm and its components.

The clarity of the paper is good, but there are some areas that could be improved. The writing is clear and concise, but some of the notation and terminology could be better explained. The organization of the paper is logical, and the figures and tables are well-placed and help to illustrate the main points.

The originality of the paper is high, as it proposes a novel approach to decision tree optimization. The authors draw on ideas from structured prediction and latent variable models to develop a new framework for decision tree optimization. The paper is well-positioned in the context of previous work, and the authors provide a clear and concise discussion of the related work.

The significance of the paper is moderate, as it proposes a new approach to decision tree optimization that could have implications for practical applications. However, the experimental evaluation is limited, and more work is needed to fully establish the benefits and limitations of the method.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise description of the algorithm and its components. However, there are some concerns and ambiguities that need to be addressed, and the experimental evaluation could be improved.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to decision tree optimization that is technically sound and well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results.
* The method has the potential to improve the performance of decision trees and could have implications for practical applications.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise description of the algorithm and its components.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The experimental evaluation is limited, and more work is needed to fully establish the benefits and limitations of the method.
* There are some concerns and ambiguities that need to be addressed, such as the handling of zero-weight nodes and the use of L2 regularization.
* The paper does not address or evaluate forests, which could be an important extension of the method.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns and ambiguities mentioned above and provide additional experimental results to fully establish the benefits and limitations of the method.
