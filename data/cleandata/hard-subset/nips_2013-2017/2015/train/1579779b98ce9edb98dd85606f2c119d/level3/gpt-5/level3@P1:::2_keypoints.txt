- Proposes a non-greedy training framework that jointly optimizes oblique split functions and leaf parameters via binary latent variables and a convex-concave upper bound, enabling SGD.  
- Introduces a fast loss-augmented inference restricting search to a Hamming ball around current assignments, reducing per-example complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p).  
- Empirical results show consistent accuracy gains over greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random-oblique) and scalability to deeper trees using the efficient inference.  
- Relates to structured prediction with latent variables, oblique tree induction (OC1), margin-based trees, CO2 (as depth-1 special case), and contrasts with soft probabilistic trees by keeping hard splits and O(dp) inference.  
- Theory concern: the loss upper-bound discussion (around Eq. (7)) is confusing and may be incorrect in some cases; conditions, tie-handling, and non-unique maximizers must be clarified.  
- Proof concern: Proposition 1 is important but the provided proof is incomplete; a rigorous, self-contained proof with stated assumptions (e.g., finite losses, no ties) is required.  
- Approximation concern: the Hamming-ball (radius-1) restriction is plausible but introduces additional approximation; its impact on optimality and empirical performance needs careful characterization.  
- Algorithmic note: SGD/SSGD and per-row max-norm projection are appropriate, but the SSGD “fixed assignment” heuristic for inactive leaves is ad hoc and may cause pathological collapse.  
- Suggestion: consider principled latent-variable updates (EM, CCCP, or block-coordinate alternation) to avoid heuristic fixes for inactive leaves.  
- Experimental limitation: evaluation is limited (four datasets, depths ≤ 16) and missing important baselines and ablations.  
- Baseline request: include greedy oblique trees trained per node (logistic/SVM) without special initialization to disentangle non-greedy benefits from better oblique optimization.  
- Initialization concern: current CO2 initialization may advantage the method; report results with random and OC1-based initializations.  
- Clarity: clarify that “leaves” in the fast inference section mean terminal nodes reachable by flipping ≤1 decision (d+1 candidates) and add a figure for the Hamming-1 neighborhood.  
- Notation: use max rather than argmax in bound expressions, separate values from argmax sets, and state how ties are broken and whether the bound is tie-invariant.  
- Assumptions: explicitly state when the bound is tight (e.g., when g equals current latent ĥ) and when the Hamming-ball restriction remains an upper bound.  
- Minor editing: reduce repetition between Sections 4–6 and consider moving algebraic proofs into main text if space permits.  
- Originality: joint non-greedy optimization of hard-split trees via a latent-structured upper bound and Hamming-ball inference appears novel and distinct from prior work.  
- Significance: approach could make single trees stronger and less overfit, but current significance is tempered by theoretical uncertainties, limited empirical scope, and missing baselines.  
- Rebuttal requests: provide corrected proofs, quantify Hamming-ball impact by comparing full vs Hamming-1 inference, and include additional baselines and ablations.  
- More experiments requested: vary depth more broadly, report active leaf counts, test sensitivity to depth and ν-induced pruning, and empirically address inactive-leaf behaviour.  
- Runtime evaluation: include thorough end-to-end training time vs depth and dataset size, and compare to OC1/greedy oblique trees and per-iteration costs.  
- Pros summary: novel latent-structured formulation, clean surrogate objective, efficient per-example gradient and scalable inference, consistent accuracy gains, practical SGD/SSGD algorithm.  
- Cons summary: ambiguous/possibly flawed loss bound and incomplete Prop. 1 proof, heuristic handling of inactive leaves, unclear necessity/benefit of Hamming-ball restriction, missing baselines/ablations, pre-specified depth and limited capacity-control analysis.  
- Overall: promising and important direction, but requires clarified theory and stronger empirical validation before a more positive assessment.
