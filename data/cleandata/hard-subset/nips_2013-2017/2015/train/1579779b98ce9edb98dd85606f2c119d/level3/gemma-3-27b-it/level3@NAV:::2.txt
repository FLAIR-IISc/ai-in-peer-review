## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper tackles the important problem of jointly training decision tree parameters – both splits and leaf posteriors – via a novel optimization approach using an upper-bound approximation for tractability. The motivation is strong: greedy tree induction, despite its efficiency, often leads to suboptimal results. The work attempts to bridge the gap between structured prediction and decision tree learning, offering a potentially valuable contribution to the field. The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the problem and proposed solution.

However, despite the promising premise, several issues impact the significance of the work. The theoretical reasoning, particularly surrounding the empirical loss bound (l.216f), feels shaky. The example provided suggests the claimed upper bound doesn’t always hold, and the proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material appears incomplete. Furthermore, the use of a fixed-assignment heuristic feels suboptimal and lacks strong justification; alternatives like Expectation-Maximization (EM) or Co-Coordinate-Ascent-Programming (CCCP) weren’t adequately considered or explained why they weren’t pursued.

The experimental evaluation also requires substantial improvement. While the paper demonstrates performance gains over greedy baselines, the necessity and impact of the efficient loss-augmented inference aren’t convincingly established. Given the relatively small tree depth explored (≤16), brute-force search is feasible, casting doubt on the benefits of restricting the search to a Hamming ball. Clarification is needed on what is meant by “leaves” in the context of inference discussion. A crucial baseline is missing: a greedy training of non-axis-aligned splits *without* any special initialization. Finally, the pre-specified tree depth is a limitation that requires more careful discussion and exploration through experiments varying depth.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Original and important research direction - joint training of tree parameters.
*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   Demonstrated improvement over greedy baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Potential flaws in theoretical reasoning and incomplete proofs.
*   Weak justification for chosen heuristics.
*   Insufficient experimental validation and missing baselines.
*   Limitations of fixed tree depth not adequately addressed.

**Overall:**

The paper presents an interesting idea with potential. However, the theoretical concerns, coupled with the limitations in the experimental evaluation, prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. A thorough rebuttal addressing these points, with supporting analysis and potentially additional experiments (runtime comparison, deeper tree exploration), could significantly alter my assessment. I currently lean towards **reject**, but am open to reconsideration pending a compelling rebuttal. Replacing `argmax` with `max` in the loss bound expression is a minor suggestion.
