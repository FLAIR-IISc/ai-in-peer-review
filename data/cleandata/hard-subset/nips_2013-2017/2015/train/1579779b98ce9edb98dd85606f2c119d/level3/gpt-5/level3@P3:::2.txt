Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles non-greedy training of decision trees by jointly optimizing all internal split parameters and leaf posteriors under a single global objective. The key technical idea is to cast the tree decisions as latent binary variables and derive an upper-bound surrogate of the empirical loss, enabling stochastic optimization. The approach connects decision tree training to structured prediction with latent variables, and proposes both an exact loss-augmented inference and a faster variant that restricts search to a Hamming ball around the current routing. The method supports oblique (linear) splits and uses per-row norm constraints on W to regularize and control bound tightness. Experiments on several multiclass datasets show improvements over greedy baselines.

The paper is well positioned within prior work on greedy trees (CART/ID3), oblique trees (e.g., OC1), max-margin trees, CO2, hierarchical mixtures of experts, and latent-SSVM. The link to latent structured prediction is natural and useful, and the surrogate is reminiscent of margin-rescaling bounds.

Strengths
- Important problem: joint, non-greedy optimization of decision trees is impactful and timely.
- Conceptual clarity: the latent-variable view neatly unifies routing and leaf estimation; the surrogate is clean and computationally amenable.
- Efficient training: per-example gradient cost O(d) with the Hamming-ball inference reduced to O(d^2 p) is attractive for moderate-depth trees.
- Regularization perspective: per-row norm constraints provide a principled handle on overfitting and bound tightness.
- Empirical gains: reported improvements over several greedy baselines across datasets and depths.

Weaknesses and concerns
Theory and bounds
- Empirical loss bound: the argument around the inequality (lines ~216f) is confusing. In particular, the use of argmax vs max in the bound expression appears inconsistent; please replace argmax with max where appropriate so the quantities are scalars. Beyond notation, a simple constructed example suggests the claimed upper bound may not hold as stated; please provide a precise statement with all conditions and a self-contained proof in the main text, not only in the supplement.
- Proposition 1: the proof sketch in the supplement is incomplete. The monotonicity of the bound with respect to scaling W hinges on how the loss-augmented maximizer changes with a; this requires care (e.g., continuity/compactness arguments or a comparison inequality). As written, I cannot verify the claim.
- Fast bound (Hamming ball): while including sgn(Wx) in B1 suggests the inequality should still upper-bound the loss, the manuscript’s justification is terse; please give a clean proof and specify the precise set over which g is maximized. Additionally, discuss the bias introduced by restricting g to a local neighborhood and its impact on convergence/fixed points.

Optimization behavior
- Inactive leaves: several leaves receive no data during training, indicating either objective pathology or sensitivity to initialization. The SSGD “fixed-assignment” heuristic is under-motivated. A clearer treatment as a majorization-minimization/CCCP or EM-like alternating optimization (fix latent routes, optimize W,Θ; then re-route) would strengthen both intuition and guarantees (e.g., monotone decrease of the surrogate).
- Necessity of fast loss-augmented inference: for depths ≤16, brute-force over leaves may be feasible; the empirical benefit of the restricted Hamming-ball search is unclear. Please include broader validation (accuracy and runtime) comparing exact vs Hamming-ball (vary radius r ∈ {0,1,2,∞}) across datasets and depths.

Experimental evaluation
- Missing baseline: compare against a strong greedy oblique tree trained without special initialization, e.g., at each node fit a logistic/hinge split via L-BFGS with random restarts, or a recent non-axis-aligned greedy method, not just OC1 and CO2. This isolates the benefit of joint training from that of oblique splits and initialization.
- Depth selection: depth is pre-specified. Please discuss this limitation and include experiments varying depth with a principled model-selection protocol (validation curves), and/or explore regularizers that adaptively prune (e.g., min-flow constraints or leaf penalties).
- Runtime: Fig. 4 shows one dataset; include comprehensive runtime vs depth comparisons (exact vs fast inference; SGD vs SSGD) on all datasets, and report wall-clock times and speedups.
- Ablations: report sensitivity to ν, learning rate, initialization (axis-aligned vs random), mini-batch size, and momentum. Show effect on number of active leaves and generalization.
- Reporting: clarify whether leaf posteriors are calibrated; include standard deviations over multiple runs.

Clarity
- Generally well written, with a clean derivation. However:
  - Clarify terminology of “leaves” in the inference section: do you mean leaf indices reachable by altering at most one on-path bit (i.e., siblings along the root-to-leaf path), or all leaves whose codes differ in ≤1 bit from sgn(Wx), even if off-path?
  - Make the loss-bound derivations self-contained in the main paper and fix the argmax/max issue.
  - Add pseudo-code for SSGD with explicit objective being optimized during “fixed assignment” phases.

Originality
- The joint optimization via a latent-structured surrogate is original in the tree-learning context and meaningfully extends prior CO2/latent-SSVM ideas to whole-tree training with an efficient inference approximation.

Significance
- Potentially significant if theoretical issues are addressed and empirical evidence is strengthened. As it stands, unclarities in the bounds and limited experimental breadth temper the impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and important direction (joint non-greedy tree training).
- Clear formulation with a plausible, efficient training algorithm.
- Promising empirical improvements and useful regularization insights.

Cons for acceptance
- Questions about the correctness/completeness of the loss bound and Prop. 1.
- Heuristic optimization (SSGD) insufficiently justified; inactive leaves point to instability.
- Missing strong baselines; limited runtime and ablation studies; unclear benefit of Hamming-ball restriction beyond small depths.

Questions for the authors
- Provide a rigorous, self-contained proof of the main bound(s) (both global and Hamming-ball versions), and a complete proof of Prop. 1. If the current statement is incorrect, please correct it and state conditions.
- Quantify the impact of Hamming-ball radius r on accuracy, convergence, and runtime; compare to exact loss-augmented inference for depths ≤16.
- How often do leaves go inactive under different ν and initializations? Can a simple entropy/flow regularizer prevent collapse?
- Add the missing greedy oblique baseline without special initialization and report results.
- Can you relate SSGD to EM/CCCP and provide monotonicity guarantees for the surrogate?

Minor suggestions
- Replace argmax with max in the loss bound expression to avoid confusion.
- Clarify what is meant by “leaves” in the inference discussion and explicitly enumerate the d+1 candidates considered under B1.

Overall recommendation
Weak reject. I find the direction valuable and the formulation appealing, but I am concerned about the theoretical justifications and the limited empirical validation. I would be willing to raise my score if the rebuttal clarifies the bounds (with complete proofs), demonstrates the effect of the Hamming-ball restriction, and adds the missing baseline and runtime/ablation studies.
