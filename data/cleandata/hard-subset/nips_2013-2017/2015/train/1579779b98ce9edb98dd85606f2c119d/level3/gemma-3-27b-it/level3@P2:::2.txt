This paper proposes a novel approach to training decision trees by jointly optimizing split functions at all levels with leaf parameters, contrasting with the standard greedy methods. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss that allows for efficient stochastic gradient descent training. The core idea – moving beyond greedy tree induction – is original and addresses a long-standing challenge in the field.

The paper is generally well-written and presents a clear derivation of the proposed method. The motivation is well-articulated, highlighting the limitations of greedy approaches and the potential benefits of joint optimization. The authors appropriately relate their work to previous research in decision trees (Breiman, 1984; Quinlan, 1986), structured prediction (Taskar et al., 2004; Yu & Joachims, 2009), and related techniques like CO2 (Norouzi et al., 2015). The connection drawn to structured prediction is a significant contribution, providing a principled framework for non-greedy learning.

However, despite the promising premise, there are notable weaknesses. The theoretical justification for the proposed upper bound on the empirical loss is not entirely convincing. The argument around line 216f is unclear and, based on a simple example constructed by the reviewer, the claimed upper bound does not consistently hold. Further, the proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material is incomplete, lacking crucial details to validate the claim. These issues raise concerns about the technical soundness of the core theoretical contribution.

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating improvements over greedy baselines, is not entirely satisfactory. The comparison lacks a crucial baseline: a greedy training of non-axis-aligned splits without the special initialization used in the experiments. This would help isolate the benefit derived from the joint optimization versus simply using oblique splits trained greedily. Furthermore, the choice of pre-specifying the tree depth is a limitation that should be discussed more thoroughly. Experiments varying the tree depth, and analyzing the impact of this parameter on performance, are necessary. The observation that some leaves receive no data points suggests potential issues with the objective function or initialization, and warrants further investigation. The Stable SGD (SSGD) heuristic, while seemingly effective, feels somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from stronger theoretical motivation (e.g., connection to Expectation-Maximization or Concave-Convex Procedure (CCCP)). 

The necessity and impact of the efficient loss-augmented inference are also questionable. Given that the experiments only use depths up to 16, a brute-force search for the optimal split at each node is feasible, making the benefit of restricting the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 unclear. This aspect requires broader validation. Clarification is also needed regarding terminology: what exactly is meant by "leaves" in the context of the inference discussion, as it’s unclear if these refer to internal or terminal nodes. A minor stylistic suggestion is to replace ‘argmax’ with ‘max’ in the loss bound expression for consistency. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The approach of jointly training decision trees is a novel and important research direction.
*   **Theoretical Framework:** Establishing a connection to structured prediction provides a principled foundation for the proposed method.
*   **Empirical Improvement:** The experiments demonstrate that non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines on several datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Flaws:** Concerns regarding the validity of the upper bound on the empirical loss and an incomplete proof in the supplement.
*   **Incomplete Experimental Evaluation:** Missing baseline, limited exploration of tree depth, and a potentially suboptimal heuristic (SSGD).
*   **Questionable Efficiency Gains:** The impact of the efficient loss-augmented inference is unclear given the relatively small tree depths used in the experiments.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of traditional decision tree algorithms.
*   The proposed method shows promising empirical results, suggesting a potential improvement over existing techniques.
*   The connection to structured prediction is a significant contribution, opening up new avenues for research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The theoretical justification for the upper bound is not entirely sound, raising concerns about the overall validity of the approach.
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete and lacks key baselines, limiting the conclusions that can be drawn.
*   The efficiency gains of the proposed method are not clearly demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning, but is hampered by theoretical weaknesses and an incomplete experimental evaluation. The significance of the work is currently limited by these shortcomings. I currently lean towards **reject**, but would be willing to reconsider a higher score if the authors can address the concerns raised in the rebuttal, particularly regarding the validity of the upper bound and the completeness of the experimental evaluation. A clear and convincing rebuttal, including additional experiments and a more rigorous justification of the theoretical claims, is essential for acceptance. I request a runtime comparison between the proposed method and brute force search for smaller tree depths.
