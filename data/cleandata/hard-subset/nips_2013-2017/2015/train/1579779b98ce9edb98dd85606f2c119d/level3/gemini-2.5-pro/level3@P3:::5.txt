### Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel algorithm for the non-greedy, global optimization of decision trees with oblique (linear) splits. The central idea is to frame the tree learning problem as one of structured prediction with latent variables, where the binary outcomes of the splits for a given input are treated as latent variables. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective function. This objective, regularized by constraining the L2 norm of the split function weights, is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). This formulation provides a principled way to jointly optimize all split functions and leaf node parameters, in contrast to traditional greedy, node-by-node procedures like CART or ID3. The work relates to prior efforts in global tree optimization and structured prediction, offering a more scalable approach than methods like multi-linear programming and a more direct global objective than soft-split models like Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Elegance:** The primary strength of this paper is its elegant and novel formulation. Casting decision tree optimization as structured prediction with latent variables is a conceptually insightful contribution that connects two well-established fields. It provides a coherent, global objective function for a problem that is almost exclusively handled with greedy heuristics.
*   **Principled Optimization Framework:** The derivation of a continuous, piecewise-smooth upper bound on the discontinuous empirical loss allows the use of powerful gradient-based optimization methods like SGD. This makes the approach applicable to large datasets, a key advantage over many previous global optimization attempts. The framework is also general, potentially allowing for extensions like different loss functions or sparsity-inducing regularizers.
*   **Efficient Inference Algorithm:** The authors present an efficient "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm that reduces the per-step complexity of SGD from exponential to quadratic in the tree depth, making the training of deeper trees feasible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Unconvincing Empirical Results:** The experimental validation does not convincingly demonstrate the practical superiority of the proposed global optimization method. In Figure 2, the performance gains over greedy baselines (like CO2, which is a greedy application of a similar idea) are marginal on several datasets (e.g., Connect4, Protein). Given the significantly higher computational complexity of the non-greedy approach (acknowledged in Figure 4), the practical benefit is questionable. The central claim that joint optimization leads to substantially better trees is not strongly supported by the evidence provided.
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper suffers from several clarity issues that hinder a full understanding and would make reproduction difficult. The "Stable SGD" variant is poorly described, key details in figures are missing, and the manuscript contains several typos and grammatical errors that detract from its quality.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Experimental Justification:** The main weakness is the experimental section. While the non-greedy trees achieve the best test performance on some datasets, the improvements are often small. Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of *when* and *why* their global method is expected to outperform greedy approaches? For instance, are there specific data characteristics where the "cooperation between split nodes" provides a significant advantage? A more compelling case needs to be made that the added complexity of global optimization is justified by performance gains.

2.  **Clarity on "Stable SGD" (SSGD):** The description of Stable SGD (lines 304-320) is too high-level. The paper states that "the assignments of data points to leaves are fixed for a number of gradient updates" and that leaf assignments are updated "When the improvement in the bound becomes negligible". This is ambiguous.
    *   How is the initial assignment `a(x)` for each data point determined? Is it based on the current model `sgn(W(t)x)`?
    *   What is the specific criterion for "negligible improvement" that triggers an update to the assignments?
    *   A pseudo-code implementation of the SSGD procedure would be invaluable for clarity and reproducibility.

3.  **Clarifications for Figure 2:**
    *   Please specify whether the results for "Non-greedy (ours)" were generated using the standard SGD (Algorithm 1) or the Stable SGD variant. This is critical for interpreting the results.
    *   The term "active" leaf is used in the SSGD section (line 307) but is never formally defined. Please provide a clear definition.

4.  **Algorithm 1, Line 10:** Line 10 describes the update for the leaf parameters `Θ`. The text suggests a standard gradient descent step on the loss with respect to `Θ`. No projection is mentioned, and none seems necessary, as the softmax function in Eq. (1) ensures the output is a valid probability distribution. Please confirm this interpretation is correct.

5.  **Technical Correction in Equations (7)-(9):** The use of "argmax" in Equations (7), (8), and the objective function in (9) appears to be incorrect. The objective function depends on the *maximum value* of the expression, not the argument that achieves the maximum. These instances of `argmax` should be replaced with `max`.

6.  **Minor Issues and Typos:** The paper would benefit from careful proofreading. Here are some specific issues:
    *   **Figure 1:** This figure provides a helpful illustration of the tree navigation function but is never referenced in the main body of the text.
    *   **Line 141:** "the results of the initial tests determine which subsequent tests are performed. It is not clear how this dependence of binary tests should be formulated." This sentence seems incomplete or corrupted.
    *   **Line 238:** "we can essentially ignore the off-the-path bits by subtracting sgn(Wx) T Wx from (11) to obtain" should likely be "sgn(Wx)TWx".
    *   **Line 301:** The phrase "some of the leaves may end up not being assigned to any data points" is slightly awkward. Consider rephrasing, e.g., "some leaves may not be assigned any data points".
    *   **Line 360:** The acronym "FSGD" is used in the caption for Figure 4 but is not defined in the text. I assume it stands for "Fast SGD".
    *   **Lines 407-408:** The sentence "The choice of ν is therefore closely related to the generalization of the learned trees" is a bit confusing in context. It seems to imply a direct relationship, but the following sentence discusses pruning. Please clarify the intended meaning.

7.  **Corrections for Supplementary Material:**
    *   **Line 29:** The notation `sgn(a) ∈ {+1 if a > 0, -1 if a < 0}` should use "=" instead of "∈", as it is a definition.
    *   **Line 30:** The derivation uses `argmax_g(g^T Wx + l(Θ^T f(g), y))`. Inside the loss term, the argument should be `Θ^T f(g)` as written, not `Θ^T f(sgn(Wx))` as the prompt's comment might imply. However, please double-check the derivation to ensure consistency. The paper seems correct as written, but it's worth a review.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces a highly original and theoretically elegant framework for globally optimizing decision trees. The connection to structured prediction is a significant conceptual contribution. However, the work is let down by an experimental section that fails to provide compelling evidence for its practical advantages over simpler, greedy methods. Furthermore, several key aspects of the methodology, particularly the Stable SGD variant, require clearer explanation to ensure reproducibility. While the theoretical formulation is strong, the paper in its current form does not fully demonstrate a significant advance in the state of the art for decision tree learning. Addressing the clarity issues and providing stronger empirical justification would substantially improve the work.
