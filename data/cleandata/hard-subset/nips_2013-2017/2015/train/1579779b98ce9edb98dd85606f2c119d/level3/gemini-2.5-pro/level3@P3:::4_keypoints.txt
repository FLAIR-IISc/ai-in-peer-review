- Proposes reframing decision tree split learning as a latent structured prediction problem, treating routing decisions as latent sign vectors.  
- Defines a global empirical loss for the entire tree and derives a continuous convex-concave upper bound as a surrogate objective.  
- Minimizes the surrogate with SGD, requiring loss-augmented inference over an exponentially large latent space.  
- Main technical contribution: algorithms that reduce inference complexity from exponential to polynomial in depth (O(d^2 p)), enabling deep-tree optimization.  
- Connects classical decision tree induction with modern structured prediction, diverging from greedy methods like ID3/CART.  
- Original and insightful formulation that could inspire new research directions for tree-based models.  
- Provides a principled global objective allowing joint optimization of all split parameters, unlike local greedy criteria.  
- Efficient optimization algorithms are a crucial practical advance making global optimization feasible.  
- Interpretability concern: oblique splits with L2 regularization produce dense, hard-to-interpret hyperplanes; L1 sparsity should be explored.  
- Sparse gradient updates imply each SGD step may only update one node, suggesting potential for adaptive tree growth—this dynamic is not analyzed.  
- Ambiguity for zero-weight nodes (w^T x = 0) is not addressed; treating zero-weight nodes as leaves is a principled alternative that could enable implicit pruning.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak: baseline details (e.g., pruning) are missing and could bias comparisons.  
- Recommend stronger baselines (e.g., multiple greedy trees on random subsets) and inclusion/evaluation of forests if claimed in the title.  
- Clarity could be improved: loss-augmented inference sections are dense and would benefit from a high-level summary or full pseudocode.  
- Significance is high conceptually, but practical utility is unclear without further analysis and more rigorous empirical validation.
