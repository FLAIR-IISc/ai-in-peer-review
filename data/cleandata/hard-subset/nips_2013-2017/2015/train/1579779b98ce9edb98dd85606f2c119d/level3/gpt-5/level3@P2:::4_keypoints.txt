- Proposes non-greedy training for oblique decision trees by treating node split decisions h = sgn(Wx) as latent structured outputs and optimizing a global upper bound inspired by latent-structured SVMs.  
- Derives a convex–concave upper bound that leads to a loss-augmented inference subproblem and introduces a tractable Hamming-ball (radius 1) restriction to make training feasible.  
- Training complexity is O(d^2 p) per update with O(dp) test-time inference; uses projected (stochastic) gradient updates for W and Θ and a “Stable SGD” variant to reduce routing instability.  
- Conceptual novelty: reframes tree learning as latent structured prediction with a principled surrogate bound and link to margin rescaling.  
- Algorithmic contribution: efficient loss-augmented inference via Hamming-ball restriction and sparse path updates enabling scalable SGD.  
- Practicality: supports general losses (log, squared), multi-class leaves, and stochastic optimization for large datasets.  
- Empirically, non-greedy trees generally outperform strong greedy baselines on several LibSVM benchmarks; ablations show effects of ν and fast inference benefits.  
- Experiments focus only on single trees and avoid ensembles, limiting practical impact since practitioners commonly use forests.  
- Baseline tuning unclear: need confirmation that greedy baselines received comparable pruning and hyperparameter sweeps (e.g., cost-complexity pruning, min leaf size).  
- Initialization sensitivity not quantified: method initialized from CO2 trees — unclear whether improvements hold with identical budgets or other initializations (random, zero, CART).  
- Update sparsity may bias optimization toward a few nodes; lacking analysis of how often early vs deep nodes are updated and whether the method activates new splits or refines existing ones.  
- Ambiguity at sgn(0): routing/tie-breaking semantics are brittle; need clear treatment of zero or near-zero inner products and handling of inactive/empty leaves.  
- Interpretability concern: L2 row constraints yield dense oblique splits; no experiments with sparsity-inducing regularizers (L1/group lasso) to assess accuracy–interpretability trade-offs.  
- Claims about applicability to forests are not supported by experiments; a small ensemble experiment would strengthen practical significance.  
- Clarity issues: Sections 6.2/6.4, fast loss-augmented inference, and SSGD need clearer exposition and pseudocode for reproducibility; dataset splits and hyperparameter search spaces should be clarified.  
- Regression applicability asserted but not empirically validated; include a regression experiment to demonstrate generality.  
- Technical core is sound (latent reformulation, bounds, projected SGD), but tension between tighter bounds and piecewise-constant behavior needs more empirical illustration.  
- Empirical validation shows consistent gains but requires more ablations (initialization, SSGD vs SGD), variance reporting, and stronger/practical baselines.  
- Original contribution: structured-prediction viewpoint and Hamming-ball inference for trees appear novel and go beyond prior nodewise/global oblique-tree work.  
- Significance: promising approach that could improve single-tree accuracy, but current experimental scope and optimization-ambiguity issues limit immediate practical impact.  
- Suggested improvements: add initialization and SSGD ablations, stronger pruning baselines and a small forest experiment, tie-breaking/pruning semantics, sparsity experiments, pseudocode, and runtime/memory reporting.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline reject — clever and promising but needs stronger empirical validation and clarification before acceptance.
