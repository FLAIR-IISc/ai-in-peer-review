- Reframes global decision-tree learning as a latent-variable structured prediction problem.  
- Associates a binary latent vector with input-specific split decisions to determine leaf paths.  
- Replaces intractable empirical loss with a continuous, piecewise-smooth (convex–concave) upper-bound surrogate inspired by latent structured SVMs.  
- Optimizes the surrogate objective via Stochastic Gradient Descent.  
- Major technical contribution: a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm reducing complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth (O(d^2 p)).  
- Connects decision-tree induction to the structured prediction framework, moving beyond greedy node-by-node methods (e.g., CART, ID3).  
- Originality/significance: novel, elegant formulation that opens avenues like kernelization and alternative regularization.  
- Technical quality: sound derivations and practical algorithmic improvements that make the approach viable for deep trees.  
- Empirical results: non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines on multiple datasets and appear less prone to overfitting due to L2 regularization.  
- Experimental rigor concern: baseline implementation details (e.g., post-pruning) are unclear, risking unfair comparisons.  
- Missing baselines: should compare to simple global strategies (multiple greedy restarts, ensemble selection) as stronger baselines.  
- Interpretability concern: L2-regularized oblique splits likely produce dense, hard-to-interpret trees; no L1/sparsity experiments presented.  
- Misleading claims: abstract/intro mention randomized forests but the method is not evaluated or extended to forests.  
- Clarity issues: inference algorithms (Section 6) need clearer exposition and pseudocode for reproducibility.  
- Theoretical gap: undefined behavior when a split weight vector is zero; edge case not addressed.  
- Unexplored insight: SGD updates are sparse (few rows of W update), suggesting a link to greedy node-wise updates that is not discussed.  
- Overall: technically impressive and promising, but needs stronger experiments and deeper discussion of practical trade-offs to be fully convincing.
