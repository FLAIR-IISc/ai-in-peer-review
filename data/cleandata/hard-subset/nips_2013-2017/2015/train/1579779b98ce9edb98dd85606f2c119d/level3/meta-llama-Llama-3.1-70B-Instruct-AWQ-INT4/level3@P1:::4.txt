This paper proposes a novel approach to decision tree optimization by formulating it as a latent structured prediction problem. The authors introduce a non-differentiable, non-convex loss function and approximate it with a differentiable upper bound. The minimization of this upper bound is achieved through stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which requires solving successive loss-augmented inference problems. The authors provide efficient algorithms for these inference problems, making the approach computationally feasible.

The main contributions of this paper are twofold. Firstly, the authors cast decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem, which is a novel and interesting approach. Secondly, they develop efficient optimization algorithms for this problem, which is a significant improvement over previous methods.

One of the key strengths of this approach is its ability to handle non-greedy optimization of decision trees. Traditional decision tree algorithms are greedy, meaning they optimize the split functions one node at a time. In contrast, this approach optimizes the split functions jointly, leading to better generalization performance.

The authors also highlight the possibility of sparse gradient updates, where each SGD step only changes one node's weight vector. This property has implications for growing infinitely deep trees from zero weights, allowing complexity to increase adaptively.

However, there are some concerns and ambiguities in the paper. For instance, the behavior of zero-weight nodes is not clearly defined, and it is unclear whether the method would behave like known greedy strategies or prefer adding nodes over revising existing ones. Additionally, the use of L2 regularization may lead to dense, non-interpretable splits, which could be a concern for practical applications.

The experimental evaluation of the approach is also limited. The authors compare their method to several baselines, but the details of these baselines and their pruning strategies are not clearly explained. It would be useful to compare the approach to additional simple global strategies, such as multiple greedy trees on random subsets, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

In terms of clarity, the inference descriptions in Sections 6.2 and 6.4 could be summarized and made more explicit. Additionally, the title of the paper mentions forests, but the paper does not address or evaluate forests.

Overall, the paper presents a clever and efficient reformulation of decision tree optimization, but its practical usefulness and experimental validation are currently unconvincing. Further work is needed to address the concerns and ambiguities raised in this review.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to decision tree optimization.
* The authors develop efficient optimization algorithms for this problem.
* The approach has the potential to lead to better generalization performance than traditional greedy decision tree algorithms.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper has some concerns and ambiguities, such as the behavior of zero-weight nodes and the use of L2 regularization.
* The experimental evaluation is limited, and more comprehensive comparisons to baselines are needed.
* The practical usefulness and experimental validation of the approach are currently unconvincing.
