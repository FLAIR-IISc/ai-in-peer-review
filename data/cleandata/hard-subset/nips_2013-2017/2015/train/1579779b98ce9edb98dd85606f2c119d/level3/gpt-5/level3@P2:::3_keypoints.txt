Paper proposes a non-greedy training algorithm that jointly optimizes all internal split functions and leaf parameters under a global latent-structured objective.  
Key idea: cast split decisions as latent binary variables and derive a convex–concave differentiable upper bound amenable to stochastic optimization.  
Technical contribution: a loss-augmented inference reduction to O(d^2 p) per example using a Hamming-ball radius-1 relaxation, preserving O(dp) test-time inference.  
Algorithm: SGD/SSGD with per-node norm constraints, initialization from greedy/CO2, and practical engineering for scalability.  
Empirical result: oblique trees trained with this method consistently outperform several greedy baselines on multiple LibSVM benchmarks, with modest but stable gains and less overfitting at larger depths.  
Relation to prior work: connects tree training to latent structured prediction (latent structural SVMs) and parallels SVM margin regularization via per-row norm constraints.  
Scalability claim: improves on prior NP-hard global tree optimizations by using SGD and a tailored surrogate to scale beyond shallow trees.  
Distinction from related models: preserves hard splits and fast inference unlike Hierarchical Mixture of Experts; extends CO2 from depth-1 to joint depth-d optimization.  
Strength — conceptual: provides a principled latent-structured formulation and global surrogate objective.  
Strength — efficiency: offers a simple, effective fast inference trick and maintains tractable inference complexity.  
Strength — practical: includes a scalable training procedure with useful initialization and supports general loss functions and extensions (sparsity, kernels).  
Weakness — empirical magnitude: performance gains are often small and lack statistical significance analysis across runs.  
Weakness — evaluation scope: experiments focus on single trees rather than ensembles, leaving uncertain whether gains transfer to RF/GBDT.  
Weakness — theoretical limits: provides an upper bound and scale monotonicity but lacks generalization bounds and strong convergence guarantees for the non-convex objective.  
Weakness — Hamming-ball bias: radius-1 relaxation biases toward local changes and its impact on global optimality or larger radii is not explored.  
Weakness — heuristics/ablations: implementation choices like SSGD leaf-freezing are heuristic and ablation studies are limited.  
Quality: technically sound derivations and effective tricks, but experimental statistical rigor and ablation breadth need improvement.  
Clarity: well-written with clear connections to structured prediction, though some proofs are deferred and SSGD behavior needs clearer description and ablation.  
Originality: novel framing of hard-split tree training as latent structured prediction and a neat Hamming-ball relaxation for tractable inference.  
Significance: moderate practical significance — addresses greedy induction limitations and preserves inference speed, but single-tree gains are modest.  
Questions for authors: sensitivity to initialization and mini-batch order, impact of SSGD vs SGD, feasibility of Hamming-ball radii >1, transfer to ensembles, and observed sparsity of learned splits.  
Suggestions: add statistical significance and multiple-run averages, more ablations (SSGD, ν, depth, initialization), report runtime/memory vs depth, and include a brief proof sketch in the main text.  
Arguments for acceptance: principled, novel, efficient joint optimization preserving fast inference with consistent empirical gains.  
Arguments against acceptance: small/insufficiently analyzed improvements, limited evaluation scope, theoretical gaps, and potential optimization locality due to radius-1 relaxation.
