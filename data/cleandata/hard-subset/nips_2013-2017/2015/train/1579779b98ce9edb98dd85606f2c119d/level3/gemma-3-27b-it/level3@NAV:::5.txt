## Review of “Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees”

This paper proposes a novel algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees with linear split functions, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, allowing for optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core idea of connecting tree learning to structured prediction is elegant and provides a principled approach to joint optimization of splits and leaf parameters. This work attempts to address a longstanding limitation of traditional greedy tree induction methods.

The strength of the paper lies in its theoretical formulation. The derivation of the upper bound and the analysis of its computational complexity (reducing it to O(d²) per iteration) are valuable contributions. The attempt to address the issue of inactive leaves with “Stable SGD” is also a noteworthy direction. The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the shortcomings of greedy methods and positioning their approach as a potential improvement.

However, the empirical evaluation fails to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed global optimization approach. The reported results show only modest improvements over greedy baselines on a limited number of datasets, and the computational cost is significantly higher. This raises questions about the practical utility of the method. The description of “Stable SGD” is insufficient – a pseudo-code or a more detailed explanation of how data point assignments are handled is crucial for reproducibility and understanding. Furthermore, Figure 2 lacks clarity regarding whether the results presented utilize SGD or Stable SGD, and the definition of an "active" leaf is missing. 

Algorithm 1 requires clarification on how the projection of Θ onto the simplex is implemented in practice (line 10) and a note that this enforces row-sum-to-one constraints. Several minor issues need addressing, including inconsistencies in equation formatting (replacing "argmax" with "max" in Eqs. 7-9), referencing Figure 1, and correcting typos/corrupted sentences throughout the manuscript and supplementary material (as detailed in the provided notes).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation connecting decision tree learning to structured prediction.
*   Theoretical analysis demonstrating computational efficiency improvements.
*   Potential for future research building on the proposed framework.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Empirical results are not compelling – limited improvements and higher computational cost.
*   Lack of clarity in the description of “Stable SGD”.
*   Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the paper.



**Overall, while the problem formulation is elegant and the theoretical contributions are valuable, the lack of strong empirical evidence and the clarity issues prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. Significant revisions addressing the experimental validation and clarity concerns are necessary.**
