

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach for learning decision trees with linear (oblique) splits by formulating tree optimization as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, deriving a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient stochastic gradient descent (SGD) optimization. The key insight is recognizing the tree navigation as a latent variable problem, where the optimal split configuration is implicitly defined by maximizing a score function. The surrogate objective, regularized via row-wise L2 constraints on the split weight matrix, allows for efficient gradient updates with O(d²p) complexity per step (d = tree depth, p = features), avoiding the exponential complexity of naive approaches. This work extends prior non-greedy methods (e.g., Bennett [1]) by handling general loss functions and enabling deep tree learning.

**Strengths:**  
The formulation is elegant and technically sound. The linkage to structured prediction (citing Yu & Joachims [25]) provides a principled framework for global tree optimization, addressing a long-standing limitation of greedy methods. The convex-concave upper bound is theoretically well-motivated, and the complexity analysis is rigorous. The proposed Stable SGD (SSGD) variant, which conservatively updates leaf assignments, demonstrates practical utility. The method’s generality (applicable to classification/regression with any loss) and scalability to deep trees are significant contributions over prior work like Bennett [1] or CO2 [18].

**Weaknesses:**  
*Experimental evidence is insufficient to support the claimed superiority.* While Fig. 2 shows non-greedy trees outperforming greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) on Connect4 and MNIST, gains are marginal on SensIT and Protein (e.g., <1% accuracy on Protein at depth 14). The paper does not clarify whether results use standard SGD or SSGD (Fig. 2 caption is ambiguous), nor does it define "active leaves" (a critical concept for SSGD). Crucially, the computational cost (O(d²p) vs. greedy O(d)) is not justified by consistent performance gains across datasets. For instance, on SensIT, greedy OC1 achieves comparable test accuracy to non-greedy trees with significantly less training time (Fig. 4).

*Clarity issues undermine reproducibility.* The SSGD description (Section 6.3) lacks algorithmic detail: it states leaf assignments are "fixed for a number of gradient updates" but omits *how* assignments are initialized, updated, or constrained. Pseudo-code would resolve this. Algorithm 1 (Line 10) claims Θ is projected onto the simplex but does not explain *how* (e.g., via softmax or explicit normalization), which is essential for implementation. The term "FSGD" (Line 360) is undefined, and Figure 1 is never referenced in the text. Minor errors persist: Eqs. (7)-(9) use "argmax" instead of "max" (a critical typo), and supplementary material has inconsistent notation (Line 29: "∈" should be "=").

**Significance and Impact:**  
The theoretical contribution is strong, but the empirical significance is limited. The paper claims "better generalization," yet results show only modest gains on a subset of datasets, with no analysis of when global optimization *fails* (e.g., on high-dimensional data). The computational overhead (Fig. 4 shows ~3× slowdown vs. greedy for depth 14) must be offset by clear advantages—this is not demonstrated. While the method could inspire future work (e.g., kernel extensions), it does not advance the state-of-the-art for practical decision tree applications, where greedy methods (CART, RFs) remain dominant due to their speed and robustness.

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a technically interesting contribution to the theory of tree learning but fails to convincingly demonstrate its practical value. The core idea is novel and well-executed, but the experimental validation is inadequate for acceptance. To strengthen the paper, the authors must: (1) clarify SSGD with pseudo-code and define "active leaves"; (2) report results consistently for SGD vs. SSGD; (3) provide a thorough comparison of runtime vs. accuracy gains across all datasets; (4) fix all notation errors and typos. Without these revisions, the claim of "better generalization" lacks empirical grounding. Given the limited experimental support, I recommend *weak acceptance* contingent on major revisions addressing these points. The work is potentially valuable but currently falls short of NIPS’s standards for significance and evidence.
