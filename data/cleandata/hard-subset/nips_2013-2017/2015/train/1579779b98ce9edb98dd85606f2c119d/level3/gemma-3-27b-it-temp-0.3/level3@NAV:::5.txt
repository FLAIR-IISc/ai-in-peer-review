## Review of “Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees”

This paper proposes a novel algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees with linear split functions, framed as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The authors present a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core idea of connecting tree learning to structured prediction is elegant and offers a potentially powerful alternative to traditional greedy methods. The formulation allows for a global objective, addressing a known limitation of standard decision tree induction. The paper builds upon existing work in structured prediction and attempts to adapt those techniques to the specific challenges of tree learning, notably the exponential growth of latent variables with tree depth.

The strength of this work lies in its theoretical formulation. The derivation of the upper bound and the analysis of its computational complexity are well-presented. The attempt to reduce the complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a significant contribution, aiming to make global optimization feasible for deeper trees. However, the empirical results fail to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. While the authors report improvements on some datasets, the gains are not consistent or substantial enough to justify the increased computational cost. The comparison to greedy baselines, including CO2, is not overwhelmingly favorable, and the performance improvements are often marginal. 

Several points require clarification. The description of “stable SGD” is unclear; the paper needs to detail how data points are assigned to leaves during this process and provide a pseudo-code illustrating the procedure. Figure 2 lacks crucial information – it’s unclear whether the results shown utilize standard SGD or the proposed stable SGD, and the definition of an “active” leaf is missing. Algorithm 1 (line 10) requires further explanation regarding the practical implementation of projecting Θ onto the simplex, explicitly stating that this ensures each row sums to one. 

Furthermore, there are minor issues that need addressing: the phrasing on lines 407-408 is confusing, “argmax” should be replaced with “max” in Eqs. (7)-(9), Figure 1 is never referenced, and several sentences contain corrupted text or misspellings (lines 141, 238, 301, 404). The acronym FSGD is undefined on line 360. Finally, the supplementary material contains errors (line 29 should use "=" instead of "∈", and line 30 requires a correction to the loss function).

**Arguments for acceptance:** Elegant theoretical formulation, novel connection to structured prediction, potential for future development.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Unconvincing empirical results, lack of clarity in key algorithmic details, numerous minor errors.

**Overall:** While the problem formulation is insightful and the theoretical contributions are noteworthy, the lack of compelling experimental evidence and the clarity issues prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the experimental shortcomings and clarify the algorithmic details.
