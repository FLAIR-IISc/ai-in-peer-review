

This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy decision tree learning by jointly optimizing all split parameters and leaf posteriors through a single optimization procedure, framed as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and demonstrate how to compute gradients efficiently with O(d²p) complexity, enabling training of deep trees via stochastic gradient descent.

The research direction is highly significant and original, addressing a fundamental limitation of greedy decision tree induction that has persisted for decades. The paper is well-written and the theoretical derivation is generally clean, with the connection to structured prediction being particularly insightful.

However, several critical issues undermine the paper's technical contribution. The theoretical claims contain notable flaws: the argument around line 216 is confusing, and a simple counterexample shows the claimed upper bound may not hold. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement is incomplete, and the paper fails to address how the objective handles leaves that receive no data points (indicating potential issues with the optimization). The fixed-assignment heuristic seems suboptimal and lacks proper motivation (EM/CCCP would be more suitable).

The experimental evaluation is insufficiently rigorous. The necessity of the efficient loss-augmented inference is questionable since the depth ≤16 makes brute-force feasible, and the paper fails to compare against a greedy baseline for non-axis-aligned splits without special initialization. The tree depth must be pre-specified, yet the paper lacks experiments varying depth to assess this limitation. The presentation of results is also unclear in places (e.g., terminology for "leaves" in inference).

While the core idea is valuable and promising, these theoretical and experimental shortcomings significantly reduce the paper's impact. The authors should address these issues before the approach can be considered robust. A clear rebuttal addressing the theoretical flaws and providing the missing experiments would be necessary for acceptance. The paper's significance is diminished by these concerns, though the research direction itself is important.
