Summary: The paper proposes a global algorithm for learning oblique decision trees by optimizing a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss, regularized via per-node L2 constraints and optimized with SGD. A key contribution is the elegant reformulation that casts tree induction as structured prediction with latent variables (h = sgn(Wx)), enabling loss-augmented inference and a fast variant constrained to a Hamming ball that reduces complexity to O(d^2 p). The approach connects to structural SVMs and latent-variable methods (Taskar et al.; Tsochantaridis et al.; Yu & Joachims) and relates to prior non-greedy tree learning (Bennett; Bennett & Blue) and soft-split models such as HME (Jordan & Jacobs). Empirically, the paper compares against axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, and CO2 baselines.

Quality: The formulation and bounds appear technically sound, and the complexity analysis is careful. However, the experimental evidence is mixed: the global method substantially outperforms greedy trees on only a subset of datasets, while incurring higher computational cost. Important optimization details (especially for the “stable SGD”) are insufficiently specified to ensure reproducibility.

Clarity: The main idea is clearly motivated, but several presentation issues hinder understanding:
- Stable SGD (SSGD): Please provide a precise description and pseudocode. How are data-to-leaf assignments a(x) initialized, updated (frequency, stopping rule), and maintained across minibatches? How does SSGD ensure all leaves receive updates?
- Figure 2: Specify whether results use SGD or SSGD, whether “fast” loss-augmented inference is used, and define “active” vs. “inactive” leaf.
- Algorithm 1 (line 10): Clarify whether Θ is projected onto the probability simplex per row (enforcing rows sum to 1) or optimized unconstrained with softmax; if projection is used, give the practical projection routine.
- Clarify the relationship between the “tightness” parameter ν and pruning; indicate how ν is chosen and whether pruning is comparable across baselines.
- Minor issues/typos: lines 407–408 (confusing phrasing about features); replace “argmax” with “max” in Eqs. (7)–(9); Figure 1 is never referenced in the text; several corrupted/misspelled sentences (lines 141, 238, 301, 404); undefined acronym FSGD (line 360).
- Supplementary: line 29, the sign relation should be “=”, not “∈”; line 30, use Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)) inside the loss, not sgn(Wx).

Originality: The latent-structured formulation and bound-driven optimization for oblique trees are novel and elegant beyond CO2. The Hamming-ball restriction for fast inference is a neat idea.

Significance: Moderate. The method is principled and potentially influential, but the current experiments do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over strong greedy/ensemble baselines, and training cost is higher.

Pros:
- Elegant, principled formulation linking trees to latent structured prediction.
- Clear surrogate objective with complexity-aware inference.
- Potentially general and extensible framework.

Cons:
- Empirical gains are limited and inconsistent; higher compute cost.
- Missing algorithmic details (SSGD, Θ updates) hinder reproducibility.
- Presentation/typo issues and figure ambiguities.

Recommendation: Weak reject unless the authors strengthen experiments (including runtime/accuracy trade-offs vs. strong baselines) and provide the requested clarifications and corrections.
