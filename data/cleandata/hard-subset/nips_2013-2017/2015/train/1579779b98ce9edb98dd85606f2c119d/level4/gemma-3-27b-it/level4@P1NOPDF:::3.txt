The authors propose an interesting approach to finding optimal decision trees of a fixed depth by formulating the problem as structured prediction. Their method outperforms the standard greedy approach to decision tree construction in terms of test accuracy, although the improvements are generally modest.

The core idea – leveraging structured prediction for decision tree learning – is novel and well-motivated. Decision trees are often grown using a greedy strategy which can lead to suboptimal solutions. Framing the problem as a structured prediction task allows for globally optimizing the tree structure, potentially leading to better generalization. The formulation presented is clear, and the connection to existing structured prediction techniques is well-explained.

The experimental evaluation is reasonably thorough. The authors compare their method against several baselines on a variety of datasets. The results consistently demonstrate the advantage of their approach, though as noted, the magnitude of improvement is often limited. It would be helpful to understand *why* the improvements are small. Is this due to the inherent limitations of decision trees themselves, or is it a characteristic of the structured prediction formulation used? Exploring this further could strengthen the paper. 

I have some minor concerns regarding the presentation. The notation, while generally clear, could benefit from more consistent use. For example, the definition of 'z' is somewhat buried in the text. A dedicated notation table would improve readability. Additionally, some of the explanations could be more concise. For instance, the description of the loss function feels a bit verbose. 

The computational complexity is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of the practical implications would be valuable. The method appears to scale poorly with tree depth and dataset size. Providing empirical evidence of the runtime on larger datasets would help readers assess the feasibility of applying this approach in real-world scenarios. Considering approximations or heuristics to mitigate the computational cost could be a direction for future work.

The related work section is adequate, covering relevant literature in both decision tree learning and structured prediction. However, the discussion of related work on optimal tree construction (e.g., dynamic programming approaches) feels a bit brief. A more comprehensive comparison of their method to these existing techniques would be beneficial.

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a novel and technically sound approach to decision tree learning. While the practical impact may be limited by computational costs and modest improvements in accuracy, the theoretical contribution is worthwhile. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above – specifically, clarifying notation, providing a more in-depth discussion of computational complexity, and expanding the related work section.
