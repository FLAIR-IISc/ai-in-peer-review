- Reformulates global decision tree optimization as latent structured prediction using sign-vector latents and minimizes a differentiable upper bound of a non-differentiable loss via SGD with loss-augmented inference.  
- Main contributions are the novel structured-prediction reformulation and efficient algorithms for the resulting objective.  
- SGD updates exploit problem structure, but are highly sparse—each step changes only one node's weight—which is framed as a computational benefit but has deeper implications.  
- Sparsity suggests a possible "infinite tree" or incremental activation approach (initialize weights at zero and activate nodes), which could let model complexity adapt to data.  
- Open questions about the incremental activation idea: is it equivalent to greedy strategies, is adding nodes always better than revising existing ones, and does regularization yield finite trees?  
- Ambiguity in handling zero-weight nodes: default right-branching seems arbitrary; treating zero-weight nodes as leaves (pruning analogue) is a more principled option and needs precise definition.  
- Practical concern: interpretability—L2 regularization likely yields dense, non-interpretable splits; L1 could enforce sparsity but may reduce accuracy; this trade-off needs empirical study.  
- Practical concern: experimental baselines are under-specified—pruning strategies for greedy baselines are not described and baselines should be tuned similarly; include other global heuristics (e.g., ensembles of greedy trees on subsets).  
- Clarity issues: inference algorithm descriptions (Sections 6.2 and 6.4) could be clearer—e.g., present Section 6.2 as a two-stage maximization (paths then leaves).  
- Overall: clever and thought-provoking method, but practical utility is uncertain due to interpretability concerns and incomplete experimental validation.  
- PS: Title mentions "forests" but the paper does not address forests.  
- Post-rebuttal: zero-weight handling remains a critical issue despite random initialization in rebuttal; tree-growing from zero weights is a promising future direction, but motivation and experiments are still not fully convincing.
