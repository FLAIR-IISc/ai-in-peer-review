- Proposes a novel framework for non-greedy decision tree optimization by formulating learning as structured prediction with latent variables.
- Introduces a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized with stochastic gradient descent.
- Enables joint optimization of split functions across all tree levels under a global objective, addressing greedy induction limitations.
- Connects decision tree learning to structured prediction, providing a new theoretical perspective.
- Achieves efficient learning of deep oblique trees with O(d²p) training complexity.
- Maintains standard tree inference complexity O(dp), avoiding the inefficiency of soft-split methods.
- Builds on prior non-greedy and CO2 approaches but overcomes their scalability limitations.
- Theoretically grounded formulation is a significant contribution to the literature.
- Presents an efficient optimization algorithm with practical computational complexity.
- Comprehensive experiments show consistent, but modest, accuracy improvements over greedy baselines.
- Provides clear theoretical analysis of the upper bound and a proposition on the effect of scaling W.
- Main weakness: absolute improvements over greedy baselines are modest (typically 1–2%).
- Main weakness: Focuses on oblique trees while many applications use axis-aligned trees, limiting immediate applicability.
- Main weakness: The structured prediction connection may require domain familiarity to fully appreciate.
- Quality: Technically sound with rigorous theory and thorough experiments, though gains are modest.
- Clarity: Well-organized and clearly explained, with some technical details requiring careful reading.
- Originality: Novel connection between decision trees and structured prediction, clearly distinguished from prior work.
- Significance: Addresses greedy induction limitation and could influence future work, but direct impact may be limited by small gains.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theory, efficient algorithm, empirical improvements, clear writing, and extensible theoretical insights.
- Arguments against acceptance: modest empirical gains, limited axis-aligned applicability, niche appeal of the structured prediction link, and limited case for impact versus other contributions.
- Overall recommendation: technically strong and noteworthy; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to emphasize significance and clarify prior-art relationships.
