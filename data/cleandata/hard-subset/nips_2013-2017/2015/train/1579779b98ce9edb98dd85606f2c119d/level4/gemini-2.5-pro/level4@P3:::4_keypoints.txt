- Reformulates learning oblique decision-tree splits as structured prediction with latent binary path vectors and optimizes a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss via SGD.  
- Key technical contribution: efficient loss-augmented inference reducing complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth, enabling deeper tree optimization.  
- Empirical claim: outperforms standard greedy tree induction on several classification benchmarks.  
- Strength: original and novel connection between global tree learning and structured prediction.  
- Strength: solid technical derivation of the surrogate objective and practical algorithmic development.  
- Strength: framework suggests promising future directions (e.g., exploiting sparse gradient updates to grow trees dynamically).  
- Weakness: practical motivation unclear—oblique dense splits reduce interpretability and niche where this trade-off is preferable isn’t well justified.  
- Weakness: L1 regularization and sparsity (to recover interpretability/axis-aligned splits) are not explored.  
- Weakness: experimental validation is insufficient—baselines’ hyperparameter tuning (e.g., pruning) is not detailed, so comparisons may be unfair.  
- Weakness: missing comparisons to simple non-greedy baselines (e.g., train many greedy trees on random subsets and pick best).  
- Weakness: clarity issues in inference sections—needs a high-level summary before technical details.  
- Weakness: ambiguous handling of zero-weight split nodes (sign undefined at zero), important for initialization and regularization behavior.  
- Question: could sparse updates plus L1 regularization allow simultaneous structure and parameter learning, effectively growing a compact tree?  
- Question: have the authors tried L1 to induce sparse/axis-aligned splits and compare directly to C4.5/CART?  
- Question: clarify scope—title/abstract mention forests but paper focuses on single trees; either restrict scope or explain extension to forests.  
- Conclusion: elegant and technically strong approach, but practical significance and experimental rigor need clearer justification and stronger empirical comparisons.
