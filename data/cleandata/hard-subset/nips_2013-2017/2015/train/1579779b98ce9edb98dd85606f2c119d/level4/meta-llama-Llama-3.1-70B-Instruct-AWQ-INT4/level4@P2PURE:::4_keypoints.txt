- Proposes global decision-tree optimization by casting it as structured prediction with latent sign-vector variables and optimizing a differentiable upper-bound loss via SGD and loss-augmented inference.

- Key contributions: novel formulation as structured prediction and efficient algorithms for loss-augmented inference and optimization.

- Advantage: SGD updates are sparse — each step changes only one node's weight — enabling exploitation of tree structure.

- Raises the intriguing possibility of growing an infinitely deep tree by initializing weights at zero and gradually making them nonzero, but this is not analyzed.

- Questions whether such gradual growth is equivalent to known greedy strategies or whether appropriate regularization would yield a compact tree.

- Ambiguity about the rule when a node's weight equals zero (paper implies always taking the right branch); reviewer argues it should output a decision instead to allow later splitting, linking to pruning analogies.

- Practical concern: decision trees are valued for interpretability, but L2 regularization likely produces dense, non-interpretable splits; L1 could help but may hurt performance when enforcing 1-sparse splits.

- Experimental concerns: lack of detail about pruning/regularization baselines for the greedy method and missing comparisons to simple global strategies (e.g., greedy on random subsets with validation selection).

- Clarity issues: inference description could be clearer (Section 6.2 should state maximizing over g per leaf then over leaves; Section 6.4 needs more explicitness).

- Overall: clever and interesting method, but unclear practical usefulness due to interpretability worries and insufficient experimental details.

- Post-rebuttal: zero-weight case remains a critical unresolved issue; reviewer encourages exploring zero-initialization and gradual weight growth.
