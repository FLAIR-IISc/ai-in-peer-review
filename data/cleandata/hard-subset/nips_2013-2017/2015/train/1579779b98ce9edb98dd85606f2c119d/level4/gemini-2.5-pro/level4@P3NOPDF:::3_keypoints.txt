- Proposes treating an entire decision tree as a structured output learned with a global objective rather than greedy top-down induction.  
- Conceptually novel and well-motivated; addresses greedy methods' tendency to get stuck in local optima.  
- Principled formulation by casting tree induction in the structured prediction framework, enabling use of established optimization/theory.  
- Empirical results show consistent but modest test accuracy improvements over greedy baselines, raising questions about practical impact.  
- No theoretical or empirical analysis of computational cost or scalability despite NP-hardness of optimal tree search.  
- Missing runtime comparisons and complexity bounds (w.r.t. data size, features, and tree depth).  
- Experimental baselines are limited to greedy methods; lacks comparison to other non-greedy/optimal tree induction approaches (e.g., dynamic programming, SAT-based).  
- Requests clarification on trade-off between marginal accuracy gains and increased computational complexity, and how training time scales with depth.  
- Asks why gains are small—whether greedy trees are near-optimal on benchmarks or the method/formulation is limited.  
- Suggests evaluating initialization from greedy trees to test if the method is fine-tuning or finds fundamentally different structures.  
- Overall: conceptually strong and potentially inspiring, but borderline due to modest empirical gains and insufficient computational analysis; leans toward acceptance if concerns are addressed.
