

Summary: This paper proposes a method for jointly optimizing all parameters (decision splits and posterior probabilities) of decision trees, addressing a key limitation of current greedy, layer-by-layer training approaches. The problem is formulated as a single optimization task, which is then upper-bounded and approximated to yield a tractable solution.  

Quality: The paper is well-written but contains a potential flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound (see detailed comments).  
Clarity: The theoretical derivation is clear, though the experimental results and their presentation require strengthening.  
Originality: Jointly training decision tree parameters is an important direction.  
Significance: The proposed approach is valuable, though notable limitations exist (see comments).  

Comments:  
1. **Flaw in the empirical loss bound derivation (lines 216–217):** The argument states that for all $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right-hand side of the bound increases. However, consider the counterexample: $W\bx = [5; -5]$, so $\hat\bh = \sgn(W\bx) = [1; -1]$ and $\hat\bh^T W\bx = 10$. Choosing $\bg = [1; 1]$ yields $\bg^T W\bx = 0$, making the right-hand side of Eq. (7) equal to $0 - 10 + \ell(\cdot) = -10 + \ell(\cdot)$. This is strictly smaller than $\ell(\cdot)$, violating the upper-bound requirement. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not address this case, rendering it incomplete.  

2. **Unassigned leaves:** Some leaves in the tree are not assigned any data points, which seems anomalous and may indicate an issue with the objective function or initialization. The heuristic for fixed data-point assignment to leaves appears suboptimal; a justification via expectation maximization or the concave-convex procedure (CCCP), as used in clustering, would strengthen the argument.  

3. **Loss-augmented inference efficiency:** The tree depth is capped at 16, making a brute-force search over $2^{16} = 65,536$ values feasible. How significantly does the restricted search space (via the Hamming ball) impact performance? The supplementary material provides only one result; does this generalize? Additionally, the term "leaves" in the context of the Hamming ball is unclear.  

4. **Missing baseline:** A critical baseline is absent: a standard greedy method for training non-axis-aligned split functions (without initialization). This comparison is essential to isolate the contribution of joint optimization.  

5. **Tree depth limitation:** The depth must be predetermined, a constraint that warrants deeper discussion. The authors should provide a more thorough analysis of depth sensitivity in experiments.  

Postscript: Thanks to the authors for clarifying the reasoning behind Eq. (7) in the rebuttal (replacing $\arg\max$ with $\max$). I have adjusted my score accordingly. To strengthen the paper, an experimental runtime comparison would be valuable, and a more rigorous evaluation of the method is recommended for readers. While the problem is well-motivated, the experimental scope is limited, and the derivation flaw requires resolution.
