### Review

**Summary**
This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters of a decision tree, including both the decision splits and the posterior probabilities in the leaves. This is a significant contribution, as conventional methods typically train trees in a greedy, layer-by-layer fashion. The authors formulate this joint training task as a single optimization problem, which is then made tractable by introducing an approximate upper bound.

**Overall Assessment**
The paper addresses the important problem of end-to-end decision tree training and is generally well-written. The mathematical derivation is presented cleanly, and the proposed solution is valuable. However, the reasoning behind a key theoretical bound is difficult to follow, and the experimental evaluation could be significantly strengthened. The paper's originality and significance are high, but its value is tempered by these potential shortcomings.

**Detailed Comments**

1.  **Clarity of the Empirical Loss Bound:** The justification for the empirical loss bound in Equation (7) is confusing, specifically the argument presented in lines 216f: `... for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$ ...`. This claim does not seem to hold in all cases. To illustrate, consider an example where `W\bx = [5; -5]`. Here, `$\hat{\bh} = \sgn(W\bx) = [1; -1]$`, which gives `$\hat{\bh}^T W\bx = 10$`. If we choose a different vector, such as `$\bg = [1; 1]$`, we get `$\bg^T W\bx = 0$`. In this case, the right-hand side of Equation (7) becomes `$\bg^T W\bx - \hat{\bh}^T W\bx + \ell(...) = -10 + \ell(...)$`, which could be smaller than the original loss `$\ell(...)$`, thus violating the conditions for a valid upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not appear to cover this scenario and is therefore incomplete. *[Post-rebuttal note: The authors' response clarified this point; however, the derivation in the main paper should be revised for clarity to avoid this confusion. A minor suggestion is to replace `\arg\max` with `\max` in the relevant expression.]*

2.  **Empty Tree Leaves:** The experiments show that some tree leaves are not assigned any data points. This is an unusual outcome that may indicate an underlying problem with the optimization objective or the initialization procedure. The authors suggest a heuristic of a fixed data-point-to-leaf assignment. This approach could be better motivated, perhaps by drawing a connection to established techniques like the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm or the Concave-Convex Procedure (CCCP), which are used in related clustering problems.

3.  **Necessity of Efficient Loss-Augmented Inference:** The paper emphasizes the need for an efficient design for loss-augmented inference. However, given that the maximum tree depth in the experiments is 16, a brute-force search over all `2^16 = 65,536` leaves seems computationally feasible. It is therefore unclear how critical the proposed restricted search space (the Hamming ball) is. The paper should quantify the performance impact of this restriction, clarify if the single result in the supplementary material generalizes, and better define what "leaves" refers to in this context.

4.  **Missing Baseline Comparison:** The experimental evaluation is missing a crucial baseline: a standard, greedy, layer-by-layer training method for trees with non-axis-aligned split functions, trained from scratch without the proposed initialization scheme. Including this comparison would better contextualize the performance of the proposed joint training method.

5.  **Pre-specified Tree Depth:** A notable limitation of the method is that the tree depth must be specified in advance. This constraint warrants a more thorough discussion in the paper. Additionally, a more detailed experimental analysis of the model's sensitivity to the depth parameter would be valuable for the reader.

**Suggestions for Improvement**
While the problem is interesting and the approach is promising, the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough experimental evaluation. In addition to the points above, an experiment comparing the runtime of the proposed method against relevant baselines would be a valuable addition.
