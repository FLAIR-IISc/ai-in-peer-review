Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a non-greedy algorithm for training decision trees with linear (oblique) splits by jointly optimizing all split parameters and leaf distributions under a single global objective. The key idea is to view the vector of split decisions as a latent variable and to optimize a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The paper connects decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables, enabling a loss-augmented inference formulation. A naïve version of the bound leads to O(2^d p) complexity per update (d = depth, p = input dimension), but the authors introduce a restricted Hamming-ball formulation that reduces this to O(d^2 p), making deep trees feasible. A norm constraint on each split’s weight vector regularizes the bound and controls overfitting. Experiments on several multiclass benchmarks compare the proposed non-greedy trees to standard greedy trees (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) and to CO2 (a greedy, depth-1 special case of the proposed bound), showing improvements on some datasets.

This builds on and contrasts with: classic greedy induction (ID3, CART), prior non-greedy/global approaches (e.g., Bennett’s MILP-based optimization), margin-based trees (SVM at splits), and soft-split models like Hierarchical Mixture of Experts (which sacrifice tree-time efficiency). The paper’s main novelty is a tractable latent-structured surrogate with per-example O(d^2 p) complexity for loss-augmented inference, supporting general losses while retaining hard-split inference cost O(d p).

Strengths:
- Conceptually elegant link between decision trees and latent structured prediction.
- Well-motivated surrogate objective with a clear computational pathway from an intractable O(2^d p) formulation to an efficient O(d^2 p) algorithm.
- The norm constraint on each split is a principled regularizer that also provides an interpretable knob to control generalization and implicit pruning.
- Practical training recipe (initialization via greedy/CO2, SGD with momentum/minibatches) is clearly described.

Weaknesses:
- The empirical gains over strong greedy baselines appear limited: the non-greedy method substantially outperforms on only a subset of datasets, while being computationally more intensive.
- Some important algorithmic details (e.g., Stable SGD, the notion of “active” leaves) are insufficiently specified, which makes it difficult to reproduce or fully understand the reported improvements.
- Clarity issues in figures/references and several typos reduce readability.

Clarifications and questions:
- Stable SGD (SSGD): Please describe precisely how data-to-leaf assignments are fixed and updated. A brief pseudo-code would greatly help. What criterion triggers reassignment, and how many SGD steps occur between reassignments?
- Figure 2: Are the curves produced with vanilla SGD or SSGD? Please define “active” leaf exactly (e.g., assigned at least one training example under current routing).
- Algorithm 1, line 10: You mention updating Θ and then projecting onto a simplex. How is this projection implemented in practice? It would be useful to note that this ensures each row of Θ sums to one. (If instead Θ stores unnormalized log-probabilities with softmax at leaves, please clarify the discrepancy.)
- Computational cost: Beyond asymptotics, please report wall-clock times for SGD vs fast loss-augmented inference across depths, and specify hardware to contextualize the overhead relative to greedy baselines.
- CO2 comparison: Since CO2 is a greedy special case of your bound, an ablation separating the effects of joint (non-greedy) optimization from other design choices would strengthen the empirical case.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and original formulation tying decision trees to latent structured prediction.
- Practical, scalable algorithmic refinement (Hamming-ball bound) with sound complexity analysis.
- Regularization perspective that helps mitigate overfitting and explains implicit pruning behavior.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited and dataset-dependent empirical gains over strong greedy baselines, alongside higher computational cost.
- Missing details for SSGD and some implementation choices reduce reproducibility.
- Several clarity issues in the exposition.

Overall assessment:
The optimization framework is elegant and potentially impactful, but the current empirical evidence does not convincingly demonstrate a consistent advantage over greedy trees given the added complexity. Clarifying SSGD, tightening the experimental analysis (especially runtime and ablations), and addressing the noted presentation issues would significantly strengthen the paper.
