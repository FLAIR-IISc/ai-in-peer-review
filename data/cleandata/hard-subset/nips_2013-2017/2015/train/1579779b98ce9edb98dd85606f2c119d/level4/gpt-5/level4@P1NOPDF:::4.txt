The submission reformulates global optimization of decision trees as (latent) structured prediction, where latent variables are sign vectors encoding decisions at internal nodes. It introduces a non-differentiable, non-convex loss, upper-bounded by a differentiable (but still non-convex) surrogate. Minimization via SGD reduces to a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided. Experiments compare the method to a standard greedy training baseline. The core contributions are the structured-prediction casting and the associated efficient optimization procedures. The approach is novel and thought-provoking, and it raises several interesting questions.

As a method for global DT optimization, the formulation is compelling. Framing training as structured prediction enables SGD through loss-augmented inference that exploits problem structure. More intriguingly, if I understand correctly, the resulting gradient updates are sparse: each step modifies only a single node’s weight vector. The paper presents this primarily as a computational benefit, but I believe it opens up more fundamental possibilities that are not fully explored.

In particular, one could imagine an infinitely deep tree initialized with all weights set to zero, and then gradually growing the tree by activating additional weights as needed—allowing model capacity to increase adaptively with data. This invites several questions. Would such a procedure reduce to, or resemble, a known greedy training strategy? Might it always be preferable to introduce a new node rather than revise an existing node’s weights? With suitable regularization, would it instead converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes (as Fig. 2 seems to suggest)? This seems central to the value proposition of the method relative to naive DTs and merits deeper analysis than is currently provided.

A related ambiguity is critical: what happens when a node’s weight vector is exactly zero? The discussion on page 3 suggests defaulting to the right branch, which appears questionable. A more principled alternative would be to emit a decision at that node; later, the weight could become nonzero, effectively splitting the node. This choice has a clear connection to pruning in greedy training, so the behavior should be precisely specified.

I also have a significant practical concern about motivation. Plain DTs are typically attractive because they are interpretable; if interpretability is not required, other models often outperform trees. As presented, the method would likely yield non-interpretable trees, since L2 regularization tends to produce dense weight vectors. L1 regularization might help, but performance could degrade if we regularize strongly enough to force exactly 1-sparse splits. This trade-off should be empirically evaluated.

The experimental section lacks sufficient detail on baselines. The paper does not specify which pruning strategies were used for the greedy baseline. Since the proposed method is tuned over a range of regularization parameters, the greedy approach should likewise be evaluated under multiple pruning strategies and hyperparameters. It would also be reasonable to include trivial global baselines, such as training multiple greedy trees on random subsets of the data and selecting the tree that minimizes the target loss on the full training or a validation set.

On clarity: the paper is generally well written, though parts of the inference description could be clearer. For example, Section 6.2 can be summarized as first maximizing the objective over all g associated with a fixed leaf, then maximizing over leaves. Section 6.4 could also be made more explicit about the optimization flow.

In summary, this is a clever reformulation that yields efficient optimization and raises important questions. However, its practical utility remains uncertain given: (a) the interpretability issue (if the trees are not interpretable, why use trees at all?) and (b) the limited experimental analysis of baselines and pruning strategies. Additionally, the title mentions forests, but the paper does not address that case.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
I still view the zero-weight case as critical and under-analyzed, for the reasons above. The rebuttal suggests random weight initialization, which likely avoids the issue empirically. Nevertheless, the most compelling direction, in my view, is to initialize all weights at zero and allow them to grow gradually, leveraging the sparse update property to adaptively expand the tree. I encourage the authors to explore this path. Overall, the submission presents a clever structured-prediction formulation and efficient solver, but the motivation and experimental support remain less convincing.
