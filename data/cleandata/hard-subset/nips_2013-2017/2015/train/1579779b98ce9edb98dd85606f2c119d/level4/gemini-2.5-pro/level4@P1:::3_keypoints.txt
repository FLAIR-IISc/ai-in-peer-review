- Addresses non-greedy, global optimization of decision trees by jointly learning all split functions and leaf parameters.  
- Reframes tree induction as structured prediction with latent binary path variables.  
- Derives a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss as a tractable surrogate objective.  
- Minimizes the surrogate using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD).  
- Introduces a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm that reduces complexity from exponential in depth to O(d²p).  
- Empirical results: non-greedy trees consistently outperform greedy axis-aligned and oblique baselines on benchmarks.  
- Shows better generalization and reduced overfitting (smaller train-test accuracy gap); uses L2 constraint for principled regularization.  
- Main strength: principled, global formulation that moves beyond traditional greedy criteria.  
- Main technical strength: sound upper-bound derivation and clever algorithmic reduction of inference complexity.  
- Weakness: empirical accuracy gains are often modest, especially versus CO2, questioning practical benefit given added complexity.  
- Weakness: method is more computationally intensive (per-step O(d²p)) and lacks total wall-clock training-time comparisons to greedy baselines.  
- Weakness: dependence on greedy initialization raises concerns about sensitivity and getting stuck in local basins.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to single trees; no comparison to ensemble methods (Random Forests, boosting), limiting practical relevance.  
- Questions for authors: provide wall-clock comparisons, analyze sensitivity to initialization, justify SSGD vs other optimizers (e.g., Adam), discuss kernelization trade-offs and potential integration into ensembles.
