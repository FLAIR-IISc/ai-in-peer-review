Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy method for training decision trees with linear (oblique) splits by optimizing a global surrogate objective rather than fitting each node greedily. The key idea is to recast split decisions as latent variables and to derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling stochastic gradient descent (SGD) over both split weights and leaf parameters. The authors further introduce a fast loss-augmented inference variant based on a Hamming-ball restriction, reducing per-example complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p), where d is depth and p the input dimension. Experiments on several multiclass datasets compare the method to standard greedy trees (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) and to CO2, a related greedy optimization of oblique splits.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables (e.g., structural SVM and latent SVM formulations). It builds on upper-bound techniques used in structured prediction and relates to max-margin decision tree approaches and greedy oblique split algorithms (e.g., OC1, CO2). The formulation preserves the inference efficiency of trees while enabling global coordination of splits.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The latent-variable formulation and associated upper bound provide a principled optimization view of tree learning.
- Algorithmic contribution: The fast loss-augmented inference (Hamming-ball radius 1) is a practical advance that makes training deep trees feasible with O(d^2 p) complexity.
- Practical training: The use of SGD with per-row norm constraints is well-motivated and computationally sensible; the Stable SGD (SSGD) variant is a thoughtful response to leaf inactivity.
- Empirical scope: Multiple datasets and comparisons to strong greedy baselines, including oblique and axis-aligned trees.

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical significance: Gains over greedy baselines appear modest and dataset-dependent; in several cases improvements are small relative to the extra training cost. A more thorough analysis of wall-clock time vs. accuracy would help assess practical value.
- Clarity on SSGD: The mechanism for fixing and updating data-to-leaf assignments is under-specified. Please include a concise pseudo-code and define “active” vs. “inactive” leaves precisely (e.g., based on nonzero assignment counts over a window of iterations).
- Figure 2: Specify whether curves correspond to SGD or SSGD. If mixed, separate plots or annotations would improve interpretability.
- Surrogate tightness vs. efficiency: The fast bound restricts loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1. Please discuss the impact on bound tightness and performance, and provide an ablation comparing full vs. fast loss-augmented inference (where feasible) to quantify any loss in accuracy.
- Initialization sensitivity: Since W and Θ are initialized via greedy/CO2 procedures, how sensitive are results to initialization? A brief study (e.g., random vs. CO2) would be informative.
- Leaf parameter constraints: The text suggests updating Θ via gradient steps with softmax at prediction time. If a simplex projection is used in practice, please describe the projection method; otherwise clarify that no projection is required because of the softmax parameterization.
- Comparison scope: Since single-tree accuracy is often limited versus ensembles, consider reporting results with standard post-pruning, bagging, or boosting to show whether the non-greedy optimization confers additive benefits in more realistic settings.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The theoretical development is sound and the optimization is carefully motivated. However, the empirical evidence for substantial accuracy gains over competitive greedy oblique trees is mixed. More rigorous runtime/accuracy trade-off analyses would strengthen the case.
- Clarity: Overall well written and organized, but SSGD, the notion of “active” leaves, and some implementation details (Θ updates) need clearer exposition. Tie Figure 1 and algorithmic variants more explicitly to the main text.
- Originality: The latent-variable link and the fast loss-augmented inference within a global non-greedy tree learning framework are novel and interesting combinations of ideas from structured prediction and decision trees.
- Significance: Potentially significant if the global optimization consistently improves performance or reduces tree size for a given accuracy. Current experiments suggest benefits on some datasets but not uniformly; broader and deeper evaluations would clarify impact.

Pros
- Elegant latent-variable formulation and principled surrogate objective.
- Efficient training via fast loss-augmented inference and SGD.
- Regularization on split weights provides a clear bias–variance control.
- Empirical improvements on several benchmarks.

Cons
- Modest or inconsistent gains relative to increased training cost.
- Insufficient detail on SSGD and “active” leaf handling.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to ν, initialization, and surrogate choice (full vs. fast).
- Comparison mostly limited to single trees; missing ensemble/pruning context.

Minor comments and typos
- Lines 407–408: “number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree” is unclear; standard trees typically consider all features per node unless restricted (e.g., random subspace).
- Equations (7), (8), (9): use “max,” not “argmax,” for expressions that return values rather than argmaximizers.
- Figure 1 is not referenced in the main text; please cite and explain it where the navigation function is introduced.

- Line 360: Define “FSGD” on first use if different from SGD/SSGD.

Overall
This is a well-motivated and technically solid paper with an elegant formulation. To strengthen the case for acceptance, I recommend clarifying SSGD and Θ updates, providing ablations on the fast vs. full surrogate and initialization, and adding more thorough runtime/accuracy trade-off analyses. The empirical section needs to more convincingly demonstrate that the global optimization yields practically meaningful gains over strong greedy baselines.
