

I maintain that this paper merits publication due to the elegance of its nonconvex relaxation approach for global optimization of decision trees with a fixed structure. The core method is well-motivated and clearly presented, offering a promising direction for future work in this area. However, the empirical results are less compelling than the theoretical contribution suggests. While the approach is theoretically sound, the reported performance gains over baseline methods are marginal, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the global optimization strategy significantly outperforms standard greedy alternatives on standard benchmarks.  

A straightforward improvement would be to retain the global optimization for all but the final split in each tree, then re-optimize the last split and its associated leaf node using conventional techniques. This hybrid approach might better balance global structure learning with local refinement, potentially yielding stronger empirical results. Additionally, the paper should explicitly address whether the global optimization inherently increases overfitting risk—a concern given that the method prioritizes structural optimization over regularization. The current evaluation does not sufficiently explore this trade-off.  

That said, the paper’s conceptual contribution is strong enough to justify publication even with the current experimental results. The approach is novel, well-executed, and likely to stimulate further research on nonconvex optimization for tree-based models. If the authors can incorporate the suggested refinement in future work (e.g., the hybrid optimization strategy), the empirical impact could be significantly enhanced. For the current submission, however, the results, while not invalidating the method, do not fully justify the paper’s theoretical promise. A higher score would be warranted with more compelling empirical validation, but the current evaluation is appropriate given the evidence provided. The writing is clear and the technical arguments are sound, making this a valuable contribution to the field despite the experimental limitations.
