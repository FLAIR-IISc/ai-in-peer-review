

Summary: This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters (decision splits and posterior probabilities) of decision trees, addressing a key limitation of current greedy, layer-by-layer training approaches. The authors frame the problem as a single optimization task and derive a tractable formulation by establishing a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. This work builds on connections to structured prediction with latent variables, extending recent advances in that field to the decision tree context.

Quality - The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear optimization framework. However, there appears to be a critical flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound (Equation 7). The argument that "for all values of g ≠ sgn(Wx), the right hand side can only become larger than when g = sgn(Wx)" is questionable, as demonstrated by a counterexample: when Wx = [5; -5], choosing g = [1; 1] yields g^T Wx = 0, resulting in a right-hand side of -10 + ℓ(...) which could be smaller than ℓ(...), potentially violating the upper bound property. The supplementary material's proof of Proposition 1 does not address this case. The paper is well-written but the derivation requires careful re-examination.

Clarity - The mathematical derivation is technically clean and logically presented. However, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail and comparison. The presentation of results, particularly regarding leaf assignments and tree depth, could be improved with more thorough analysis and visualization. The connection between the regularization parameter ν and tree pruning is not sufficiently explained.

Originality - The investigation of joint training for decision trees represents an important contribution to the field, moving beyond the standard greedy approach. While previous work has explored non-greedy tree optimization (e.g., Bennett [1]), this paper establishes a novel connection to structured prediction with latent variables and provides an efficient optimization framework. The approach differs significantly from prior methods by enabling joint optimization with O(d²p) complexity.

Significance - The proposed solution addresses a fundamental limitation in decision tree training with potential for improved generalization. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the unresolved theoretical issue in the bound derivation. The method's practical value for large-scale applications remains promising, particularly for deep tree structures where greedy approaches often struggle.

Comments:  
- Critical flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound (Equation 7): The argument that the right-hand side increases for g ≠ sgn(Wx) is incorrect, as demonstrated by the counterexample with Wx = [5; -5] and g = [1; 1]. The supplementary proof of Proposition 1 does not address this case, making the bound's validity questionable. This requires substantial clarification or correction.

- The paper observes that some leaves become inactive during training, which seems problematic for a tree-based method. The authors should explain why this occurs and whether it indicates a fundamental issue with the objective function or optimization procedure. The proposed "Stable SGD" heuristic for maintaining leaf assignments lacks theoretical justification and should be better motivated.

- The efficiency gains from the Hamming ball restriction in loss-augmented inference are not convincingly demonstrated. For trees with depth ≤16 (as in the experiments), checking all 2^d possibilities remains feasible. The paper should provide a more thorough analysis of the impact of this restriction on both performance and runtime, including generalization beyond the specific datasets tested.

- A crucial baseline is missing: a standard greedy approach for training non-axis-aligned trees without the proposed initialization or non-greedy optimization. This would better isolate the contribution of the joint optimization framework.

- The requirement to specify tree depth in advance is a significant limitation not adequately discussed. The paper should provide a more careful analysis of how depth selection impacts performance and explore potential ways to address this constraint in future work. The experimental evaluation of depth as a hyperparameter is insufficient.

- The authors should clarify the meaning of "leaves" in the context of the Hamming ball (B₁(sgn(Wx))) when discussing fast loss-augmented inference.

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of training times between the standard loss-augmented inference and the proposed fast version, particularly for larger datasets.

The submission addresses an interesting problem with a theoretically grounded approach, but the critical flaw in the bound derivation requires resolution. The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and would be strengthened by the additional baselines and more thorough analysis described above. I'm willing to adjust my score upon receiving a careful explanation of the bound derivation in the rebuttal.
