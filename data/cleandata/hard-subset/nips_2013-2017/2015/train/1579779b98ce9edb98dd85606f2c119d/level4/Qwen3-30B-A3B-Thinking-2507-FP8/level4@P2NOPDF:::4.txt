

The submission proposes a novel approach to global optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as latent structured prediction, where sign vectors represent node decisions. A non-differentiable loss is approximated via a differentiable upper bound, enabling minimization through SGD with efficient loss-augmented inference. The key contributions—casting global DT optimization as structured prediction and providing efficient algorithms—represent a technically interesting advancement. The method’s sparsity in gradient updates (modifying only one node’s weights per step) is particularly intriguing, suggesting potential for natural tree growth by incrementally activating weights from zero. This raises compelling questions about equivalence to greedy methods or convergence to minimal trees under regularization, yet the paper inadequately explores these implications.

**Critical concerns center on practical utility and experimental rigor.** First, the interpretability motivation is undermined: standard DTs are valued primarily for interpretability, but the method’s L2 regularization produces dense weight vectors, yielding non-interpretable models. While L1 regularization could enforce sparsity (e.g., one non-zero weight per node), its impact on performance is untested. The paper must demonstrate whether such regularization preserves accuracy or necessitates sacrificing performance for interpretability—a fundamental gap given the stated goal of improving DTs.

Second, experiments lack sufficient baseline comparison. The naive greedy method’s pruning strategies are unspecified, despite the proposed method testing multiple regularization parameters. To fairly evaluate performance gains, the baseline should be tested with analogous pruning approaches (e.g., varying complexity parameters). Additionally, trivial global optimization baselines (e.g., training multiple greedy trees on random data subsets and selecting the best-performing one) are omitted, weakening the claim of superiority.

Third, the handling of zero-weight nodes is unresolved and critical. The paper (p.3) assumes zero weights default to the right branch—a questionable design choice. A more principled approach would treat zero weights as *pending splits* (e.g., triggering a split when weights become non-zero), directly linking to pruning strategies in greedy training. This ambiguity affects the method’s theoretical foundation and practical implementation. The authors’ rebuttal notes random initialization avoids this issue, but the method’s most compelling potential arises when weights start at zero and grow incrementally. This direction warrants explicit exploration, as it could yield a tree-growing mechanism distinct from greedy approaches.

**Clarity issues are minor but notable.** Section 6.2’s inference description could be streamlined (e.g., "maximize the objective over all leaves after fixing leaf-specific variables") and Section 6.4 would benefit from greater precision. However, the paper’s overall writing is clear.

**In summary, the method is technically clever and raises valuable theoretical questions.** Yet, its practical relevance is questionable due to:  
1) The interpretability conflict (dense weights contradict the core purpose of DTs),  
2) Insufficient experimental validation (missing baseline comparisons and regularization analysis),  
3) Unaddressed edge cases (zero weights).  

The authors should:  
- Test L1 regularization’s impact on accuracy and interpretability.  
- Compare the baseline with standardized pruning strategies.  
- Re-evaluate zero-weight behavior and explicitly model weight activation as tree growth.  

Without addressing these, the work risks being an elegant algorithmic exercise rather than a practically useful DT improvement. The title’s mention of "forests" is also unsupported, as the method addresses single trees only.  

**Post-rebuttal note:** While the rebuttal clarifies that random initialization avoids zero-weight issues, the reviewer remains convinced that the method’s true value lies in *zero-initialized* weight growth—a direction the authors should pursue. The current work, though insightful, does not yet demonstrate why this approach is preferable to existing methods for interpretable DTs.
