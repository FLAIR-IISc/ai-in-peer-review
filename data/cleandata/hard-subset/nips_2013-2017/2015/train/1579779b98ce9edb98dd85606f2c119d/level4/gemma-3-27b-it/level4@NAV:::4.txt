The submission introduces an algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees by reformulating the problem as (latent) structured prediction, where the latent variables represent decision paths encoded as sign vectors at each internal node. A non-differentiable, non-convex loss function is proposed and approximated by a differentiable, though still non-convex, upper bound. Minimization of this upper bound via SGD involves solving a series of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided. The paper then presents experiments comparing the proposed method to a standard greedy training approach.

The key contributions lie in framing global decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem and in developing efficient optimization algorithms. This approach is novel and presents several interesting research directions. However, I have concerns regarding practical motivations and the magnitude of performance gains compared to simpler methods.

Considering the method solely as a global optimization technique for decision trees, it is intriguing. Casting the problem as structured prediction allows the use of SGD through a sequence of loss-augmented optimization problems leveraging the problem's inherent structure. More notably, the resulting gradient updates appear to be sparse—each gradient step seems to modify only one node's weight vector. While the submission highlights this as a computational benefit, I believe it opens more significant possibilities that are not fully explored. 

Specifically, one could envision optimizing an infinitely deep tree by initializing all weights to zero and then incrementally growing the tree by selectively increasing weights, allowing model complexity to adapt to the data. This raises questions regarding its relationship to existing greedy strategies; for example, is it equivalent or a variation of them? Would it always be preferable to add new nodes over revising existing ones? Or, with appropriate regularization, would it converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes, as suggested by Fig. 2? Investigating this dynamic would clarify the method’s value proposition relative to standard decision trees. 

A critical ambiguity concerns the behavior when a node’s weight equals zero. The current discussion suggests following the right branch, which appears suboptimal. Outputting a decision at such a node, with the option of later increasing the weight to induce a split, seems more appropriate and parallels pruning strategies used in greedy training. Precisely defining this behavior is crucial.

I also have a practical concern regarding the motivation. Traditional decision trees are valued primarily for their interpretability; if interpretability is not a priority, other methods typically outperform them. Given the use of L2 regularization, I anticipate this method will produce non-interpretable results. While L1 regularization could potentially address this, it might come at a performance cost if enforced to achieve exact 1-sparse splits.  This trade-off requires investigation.

Finally, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail regarding baselines. The paper does not specify the pruning strategies employed for the naive approach. Given the parameter tuning performed for the proposed method, a similar exploration of pruning parameters and strategies for the naive method is warranted.  Including comparisons to other simple global optimization strategies—such as training a greedy method on random data subsets and selecting the best performing model—would also be beneficial. 

The paper is generally well-written, although the descriptions of the inference process could be clearer. For example, Section 6.2 could be concisely summarized as maximizing the objective first for all *g* corresponding to a given leaf, then over all leaves. Section 6.4 could also benefit from more explicit explanation.

In summary, this is a clever approach that raises important questions. However, its practical utility remains unclear due to the interpretability issue—the rationale for using decision trees when interpretability is sacrificed—and the lack of comprehensive experimental analysis.

P.S. While the title mentions forests, the paper does not address this case.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS

The case of zero weights remains a critical point for further analysis, as discussed in my original review. The rebuttal suggests that random weight initialization prevents this from being a practical issue. However, I believe that initializing weights to zero and gradually increasing them could be a particularly interesting direction for exploration.

The submission presents a clever reformulation of global decision tree optimization as structured prediction, coupled with an efficient optimization algorithm. However, aspects of the motivation and experimental results are not fully persuasive.
