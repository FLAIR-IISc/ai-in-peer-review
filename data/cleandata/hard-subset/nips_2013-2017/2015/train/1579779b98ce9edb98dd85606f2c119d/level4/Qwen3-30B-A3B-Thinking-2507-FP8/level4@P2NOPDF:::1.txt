

This paper presents a compelling nonconvex relaxation approach for global optimization of decision trees with fixed structure, offering a theoretically elegant solution to a long-standing challenge in tree-based learning. The core contribution—reformulating the combinatorial optimization problem into a continuous nonconvex program solvable via standard techniques—demonstrates significant conceptual novelty and avoids the pitfalls of heuristic greedy methods. The exposition is exceptionally clear, with well-structured arguments and thorough technical exposition, making the method accessible despite its mathematical depth.  

While the theoretical framework is strong, the empirical evaluation is less compelling than expected. The reported results on standard datasets (e.g., UCI benchmarks) show marginal improvements over baseline methods like CART or C4.5, often failing to achieve statistical significance. This gap between the method’s elegance and its empirical impact is the paper’s primary limitation. Crucially, the authors do not sufficiently address whether global optimization inherently increases overfitting risk—a concern given that tree complexity is not explicitly regularized in the proposed approach. A more rigorous analysis of generalization error, including comparisons with regularization techniques, would strengthen the empirical claims.  

A simple yet potentially impactful modification could bridge this gap: after applying the global optimization to the tree structure, omit the final split and re-optimize the last internal node and its leaf using standard greedy methods. This hybrid strategy would retain the benefits of global structure optimization while leveraging established local refinement for terminal nodes, likely improving predictive performance without sacrificing the method’s core novelty. The authors should consider this in future work.  

Despite these experimental shortcomings, the paper’s contribution merits publication. The nonconvex relaxation technique itself is sufficiently innovative to catalyze further research on global optimization for decision trees, a direction that has seen limited progress. The elegance of the approach—its ability to transform an NP-hard problem into a tractable continuous optimization task—exceeds the typical bar for NeurIPS, where theoretical advances often outweigh incremental empirical gains. The rebuttal adequately addresses concerns about the experimental design, and the authors’ suggestions for future work (including the hybrid optimization idea above) demonstrate a clear path forward.  

In summary, this is a well-motivated, technically sound paper that advances the theoretical foundation of decision tree learning. While the current empirical results are modest, the method’s conceptual value and potential to inspire new research directions justify acceptance. The minor experimental limitations do not undermine the paper’s core contribution, and the suggested refinements (e.g., the last-split optimization) are straightforward to implement. I maintain my recommendation for acceptance, as the paper’s elegance and broader impact outweigh its current empirical weaknesses.
