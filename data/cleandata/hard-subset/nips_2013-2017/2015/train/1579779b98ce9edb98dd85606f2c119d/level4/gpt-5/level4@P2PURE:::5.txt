This paper proposes an algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions at internal nodes. The method optimizes all tree parameters globally—both the split-function weights and the output distribution at each leaf—rather than following the standard greedy training of trees. It introduces an upper bound on the empirical loss of the tree and, to smooth the optimization landscape, regularizes this bound with an L2 penalty on the split weights. The algorithm then uses stochastic gradient descent to minimize the bound and estimate the tree parameters.

The optimization is elegantly formulated by connecting the problem to structured prediction with latent variables. However, the empirical evidence does not convincingly show that global optimization is a superior alternative to greedy tree learning. The globally optimized trees significantly outperform greedy trees on only a few datasets and are more computationally expensive.

The paper is well organized, but several points need clarification:
- The “stable” version of SGD is not described clearly. How are data points assigned to leaves? Including pseudo-code would likely help.
- Figure 2: Are these results obtained using SGD or stable SGD? What precisely constitutes an “active” leaf?
- Algorithm 1, line 10: Θ is updated and then projected onto a simplex. In practice, how do you perform this projection? It may also be worth noting that this projection enforces that each row of Θ sums to one.
