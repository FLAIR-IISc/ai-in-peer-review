This work introduces a method for training decision trees that utilize linear classifiers at their internal nodes. In contrast to conventional greedy, top-down approaches, this algorithm performs a global optimization of all tree parameters simultaneously, including the split function weights and the leaf output distributions. The authors derive a regularized upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. This objective function, which includes L2 regularization on the split function weights, facilitates a smoother optimization problem. Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) is then employed to find the tree parameters that minimize this regularized upper bound.

The formulation of the tree optimization problem is a notable strength, drawing an elegant connection to structured prediction with latent variables. Despite this, the empirical evidence presented is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of global optimization over standard greedy tree induction. The proposed method shows significant improvements on only a small subset of the tested datasets, and it comes at a higher computational cost compared to the greedy baseline.

The manuscript is well-organized; however, several aspects require further clarification:
- The description of the stable SGD variant is unclear. Specifically, the mechanism for assigning data points to leaves lacks detail. Including pseudo-code for this procedure would greatly enhance clarity.
- Regarding Figure 2, it is not specified whether the results were generated using the standard SGD or the stable SGD variant. Furthermore, the term "active" leaf should be explicitly defined.
- Algorithm 1, line 10, mentions projecting Θ onto a simplex after the update step. The practical method used to solve this projection problem should be described. It would also be beneficial to explicitly state that this projection enforces the constraint that each row of Θ constitutes a valid probability distribution (i.e., sums to one).

Minor Comments and Typos:
- Lines 407-408: The statement about tuning the "number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree" is confusing. Typically, standard algorithms evaluate all available features at each node to find the best split.
- Equations (7), (8), (9): "argmax" should be "max".
- Figure 1 is not referenced in the main body of the paper.
- Line 141: The end of the sentence "...to the index of the leaf on by this path" appears to be grammatically incorrect.
- Line 238: "...the solution to loss-augmented inference..." should be "...the solutions to...".
- Line 301: "An key observation..." should be "A key...".
- Line 360: The acronym FSGD is used without prior definition.
- Line 404: The phrase "...a tree with minimum training errors" seems incorrect in this context; perhaps "validation" or "tuning" errors was intended.

In the supplementary material:
- Line 29: The notation max{g ∈ sgn...} seems incorrect; perhaps max{g = sgn...} was intended.
- Line 30: It appears 'g' should be replaced with 'sgn(Wx)' within the loss function term, resulting in sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(Θ^Tf(sgn(Wx)), y).

In summary, while the paper presents a clever problem formulation, its empirical validation is unconvincing.
