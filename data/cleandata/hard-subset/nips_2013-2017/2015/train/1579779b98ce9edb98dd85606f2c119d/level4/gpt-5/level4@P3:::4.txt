Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a non-greedy learning algorithm for decision trees by casting global tree optimization as structured prediction with latent variables. Each internal node decision is represented as a binary latent variable; the full routing of an example to a leaf is a latent code. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that serves as a surrogate objective, and optimize it with stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is an efficient loss-augmented inference scheme. The general upper bound would lead to O(2^d p) complexity, but a radius-1 Hamming-ball relaxation yields O(d^2 p), making training of deep trees practical. The method supports general loss functions and includes per-node norm constraints on split parameters to control regularization. A “stable SGD” variant reduces leaf inactivity by periodically freezing assignments.

The work connects to prior global tree optimization (Bennett, 1990s), oblique trees (OC1), hierarchical mixtures of experts (Jordan & Jacobs), and margin-based tree learning. The latent structured formulation and loss-augmented inference draw on structural SVMs with latent variables (Tsochantaridis, Yu & Joachims). The paper also relates to CO2, a greedy depth-1 special case optimized node-by-node. Empirically, single trees trained with the proposed method outperform greedy baselines on several LibSVM multiclass datasets.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clean and compelling reduction of global tree learning to latent structured prediction, with a tractable and principled surrogate objective.
- Technical contributions: an upper bound that supports efficient loss-augmented inference; a fast O(d^2 p) variant via a Hamming-ball relaxation; projected SGD with per-row constraints and a stabilization strategy (SSGD).
- Practicality: per-example gradient computation scales with depth; sparse updates (under the radius-1 variant) affect at most one node’s weights per step; compatible with large datasets.
- Generality: supports arbitrary losses (e.g., log loss, squared loss); regularization parameter ν provides a knob to trade off fit and generalization; initialization via standard greedy procedures is straightforward.
- Empirical promise: consistent improvements over axis-aligned CART, OC1, random oblique splits, and CO2 across depths, with reduced overfitting.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practical motivation and interpretability: Oblique splits with ℓ2 constraints will generally be dense and less interpretable than axis-aligned trees. If interpretability is not a goal, comparisons to stronger non-tree baselines (e.g., linear/Kernel SVMs, multinomial logistic regression, or boosted trees) would help situate the contribution. If interpretability is a goal, experiments with sparsity-inducing regularizers (ℓ1 or group-sparsity) are important to assess whether the method can produce sparse, interpretable splits without large accuracy loss.
- Experimental comparisons: Baselines are limited to single-tree greedy methods. Standard pruning strategies (e.g., cost-complexity pruning), and sweep over pruning strengths, are not reported. Since the proposed method tunes ν and learning rates, the greedy baselines should analogously tune pruning parameters and minimal leaf sizes. Additional “global” but simple baselines (e.g., training multiple greedy trees with data subsampling and selecting via validation loss) would strengthen claims. Given the strong motivation from forests, results for ensembles (bagging/Random Forests, and possibly boosting) would be informative.
- Behavior at ties/zero weights: The paper is not explicit about tie-breaking when w_i^T x = 0 (or when parameters are initialized at or near zero). A deterministic tie-breaking rule, a small bias, or a soft-split warm start could avoid brittle behavior. More broadly, the sparse-update property suggests an appealing “grow-on-demand” variant: start from zero weights and allow splits to emerge as needed under sparsity regularization. An analysis of whether this recovers or differs from known greedy strategies would be valuable.
- Clarity of inference sections: Section 6.2 could be summarized more directly: maximize the loss-augmented score for all codes g that route to a given leaf, then take the maximum over leaves. Section 6.4 should emphasize that, with the Hamming-ball bound, the loss-augmented solution differs from sgn(Wx) in at most one bit; thus each SGD step updates at most one node’s weights (plus Θ), explaining both efficiency and stability.
- Reproducibility details: Provide more detail on SSGD (freeze duration, convergence/tolerance criteria), initialization (random vs. CO2 vs. axis-aligned CART), momentum/minibatch settings, projection frequency, and the schedule for ν. Report how often leaves become inactive and how SSGD affects this.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reduction, bound, and optimization are well motivated. The fast inference scheme appears correct and is a key enabler. Some implementation details (ties, SSGD schedule) merit clarification.
- Empirical support: The reported gains are consistent but the set of baselines is narrow and pruning is under-specified. Ablations (e.g., full vs. fast bound; with/without SSGD; effect of ν on pruning and accuracy) would help.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. Small edits to Sections 6.2 and 6.4 (as noted) would aid readability. A brief algorithmic summary for fast loss-augmented inference would be helpful.

Originality
- High. The latent structured perspective and the efficient Hamming-ball relaxation for loss-augmented inference represent a novel and useful way to move beyond greedy tree induction.

Significance
- Moderate to high. If strengthened empirically and extended with sparsity, the approach could impact how oblique trees (and potentially forests) are trained at scale. Demonstrating gains over strong pruned/ensembled baselines or providing interpretability via sparsity would increase the impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments with standard pruning baselines and tuned parameters; report minimal leaf sizes and pruning costs.
- Compare against ensembles (bagging/Random Forests) and, if feasible, boosted trees; clarify whether the method extends naturally to forests.
- Evaluate sparsity-inducing regularizers on W (ℓ1 or elastic net) and report sparsity-accuracy trade-offs.
- Clarify tie-breaking and the behavior with zero or near-zero weights; consider a “grow-on-demand” variant initialized at zero with sparsity.
- Provide ablations: full vs. fast bound; with vs. without SSGD; sensitivity to ν and η; analysis of inactive leaves.
- Improve exposition in Sections 6.2/6.4 as noted.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled formulation with clear technical contributions.
- Efficient and scalable training for deep oblique trees.
- Consistent accuracy gains over several greedy baselines.
- General framework supporting different losses and regularization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited experimental baselines; lack of pruning/ensemble comparisons.
- Unaddressed interpretability concerns; no sparsity results.
- Some practical details (tie-breaking, SSGD schedule) are under-specified.

Overall recommendation
A clever and well-motivated approach that advances non-greedy training of decision trees. I lean toward borderline acceptance, contingent on strengthening the empirical evaluation (especially pruning/ensemble baselines) and clarifying practical details. Exploring sparsity to recover interpretability would further enhance the paper’s impact.

Post-rebuttal note
Clarifying initialization helped; however, the behavior at zero weights remains conceptually important. A variant that starts from zero and grows splits under sparsity constraints could make the approach even more compelling. I encourage the authors to investigate this direction.
