The submission introduces an algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees by reformulating the task as (latent) structured prediction. The latent structure consists of sign vectors that represent the decisions taken at each internal node.

It proposes a non-differentiable, non-convex loss, which is then approximated by a differentiable (yet still non-convex) upper bound.

Optimizing this loss with SGD requires solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient procedures are provided. Experiments compare the approach against a standard greedy training baseline.

The main contributions are the recasting of global decision tree optimization as structured prediction and the development of efficient algorithms to optimize the resulting objective. The approach is novel and thought-provoking, and it raises interesting questions.

I do have concerns about practical aspects—particularly the motivation for the method and the magnitude of the performance gains over simple baselines.

As a technique for global DT optimization, the method is appealing in several respects.

The first benefit of the structured prediction view is that it enables SGD through a series of loss-augmented optimization problems that exploit the problem’s structure.

However, what I find most striking is that the gradient updates are sparse—if I understand correctly, each update only modifies the weight vector of a single node.

The paper frames this chiefly as a computational advantage, but I think this property suggests deeper possibilities that are not fully explored.

One could, for example, optimize an infinitely deep tree by initializing all weights to zero and progressively activating weights, allowing the model’s complexity to grow in a data-driven way.

This prompts several questions: might such a procedure be equivalent to, or a variant of, some known greedy strategy? This could occur if it is consistently better to introduce a new node rather than adjust an existing node’s weights.

Alternatively, with suitable regularization, would the method converge to a tree with a finite number of nodes (as Fig. 2 seems to suggest)?

I believe this issue goes to the heart of the method’s value relative to naive DTs and warrants more careful analysis than is currently provided.

A key unresolved case is the behavior when a node’s weight is exactly zero. The discussion on page 3 implies taking the right branch, which seems questionable.

It would seem preferable to output a decision at that node and only later make the weight nonzero to effect a split. Precisely specifying this behavior matters, as it directly parallels pruning strategies used in greedy training.

I have one substantial practical concern regarding motivation. In my view, plain DTs are useful primarily because they are interpretable; if interpretability is not required, other methods typically deliver stronger performance.

As formulated, I would expect the approach to yield non-interpretable trees, since L2 regularization tends to produce dense weight vectors.

Switching to L1 might help, but performance could suffer if the level of regularization needed to enforce exactly 1-sparse splits is high. This should be empirically tested.

My other main concern is that the experimental section lacks sufficient detail about the baselines. No information is provided about which pruning strategies were used for the naive method.

Given that the proposed approach is evaluated over a range of regularization parameters, I would expect the naive baseline to be tested with multiple pruning strategies and/or parameter settings as well.

It would also be reasonable to include other simple global search strategies, such as training greedy trees on random subsets of the data and picking the model that minimizes the target loss on the full training or validation set.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written, though parts of the inference description could be clearer.

Section 6.2, for instance, could be summarized as first maximizing the objective over all g for a fixed leaf, and then maximizing over the leaves.

Section 6.4 could also be made more explicit.

In sum, this is a very clever approach that raises several intriguing questions. That said, its practical utility is uncertain at this stage, both due to the interpretability issue (why use plain DTs if they are not interpretable?) and due to the limited detail in the experimental evaluation.

PS: although the title mentions forests, the paper does not cover that setting.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
I remain convinced that the zero-weights case is critical and merits deeper analysis, for the reasons stated above. From the rebuttal, it appears weights are likely initialized randomly, which may explain why the issue does not arise empirically. Still, I suspect the most compelling variant would initialize all weights at zero and increase them gradually. I encourage the authors to explore this path. The submission offers a clever reformulation of global decision tree optimization as structured prediction, with an efficient solution method; however, aspects of the motivation and the experimental validation remain unconvincing.
