Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy algorithm for training decision trees by jointly optimizing all split parameters and leaf predictors under a single global objective. The key idea is to view the binary routing decisions at internal nodes as latent variables and to relate tree training to structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A fast loss-augmented inference procedure, restricted to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current routing decisions, reduces the per-example complexity from exponential in depth to O(d^2 p), making training of deep oblique trees feasible. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass benchmarks show consistent improvements over greedy baselines.

Relation to prior work
Classical tree induction (ID3, CART) is greedy and optimizes node-by-node. Prior non-greedy or global methods include multi-linear programming approaches and max-margin trees, but these are often limited in scalability or generality. Hierarchical mixtures of experts use soft splits but lose the computational advantages of hard decision trees. The proposed formulation builds on structured SVMs with latent variables and related margin-rescaling ideas, and connects to recent work on CO2 (greedy depth-1 upper-bound optimization). The contribution here is a unified, global objective with an efficiently optimizable surrogate, along with a practical fast loss-augmented inference scheme tailored to trees.

Quality
- Technical soundness: After the authors’ clarification, the loss upper bound (Eq. 7) appears correct. It would help to make the argument explicit that the right-hand side is a difference of two maxima; for g equal to sgn(Wx), the RHS equals the LHS, and for any other g the first max cannot decrease this value, ensuring an upper bound. Replacing argmax by max (as you suggested) and stating tie-breaking for sgn at zero would further improve clarity.
- Optimization: The surrogate objective is well motivated, and the per-row norm constraint on W is a reasonable and interpretable regularizer. The SGD/SSGD procedures and the projection step are standard and appropriate.
- Empirics: The paper demonstrates accuracy gains over several greedy baselines and shows that fast loss-augmented inference scales more gracefully with depth. However, the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive (see weaknesses and requests below).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The connection to latent structured prediction is clearly explained, and the complexity analysis is helpful. A few presentation issues remain:
- Eq. (5) and (7): prefer “max” to “argmax” when used inside other expressions, and specify tie-breaking for sgn(Wx).
- Fast loss-augmented inference: please clarify “leaves” as used in the supplementary material and define precisely which d+1 candidates are considered in the Hamming ball formulation.
- SSGD: the rationale for fixing data-to-leaf assignments for multiple steps is sensible but would benefit from a short connection to EM or concave–convex procedures to situate it conceptually.

Originality
The paper’s main novelty is the latent-variable reformulation of tree training with a tractable convex–concave upper bound and a fast, structure-aware loss-augmented inference scheme. While related in spirit to CO2 and latent structured SVMs, the joint, non-greedy tree optimization and the efficient Hamming-ball approach represent a meaningful advance.

Significance
Improving single-tree training is practically important, both for standalone trees and as stronger base learners in ensembles. The method’s scalability and consistent gains suggest it could be adopted in practice. The significance would be strengthened by broader experiments and clearer guidance on hyperparameters and initialization.

Strengths
- Principled global objective linking trees to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient optimization with per-example complexity independent of the number of leaves.
- Clear, interpretable regularization that mitigates overfitting and implicitly prunes.
- Consistent accuracy improvements over multiple greedy baselines.
- Practical algorithmic details (SGD, projection, fast loss-augmented inference) that make the approach usable.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Experimental scope: The datasets are limited to several LibSVM benchmarks. Please add larger-scale or image datasets and report variability (multiple random seeds) and statistical significance.
- Baselines: Include a greedy oblique tree baseline trained from scratch without axis-aligned initialization (e.g., optimizing a logistic or hinge objective at each node), and clarify how OC1 and “random oblique” were initialized and tuned. Given that your method uses a strong initialization (axis-aligned + CO2), a “from-scratch” oblique baseline would help isolate the benefit of the non-greedy joint optimization.
- Depth selection: The depth must be set a priori. Please discuss model selection, report validation curves across depths, and consider simple automatic stopping/pruning criteria to illustrate robustness.
- Runtime and scalability: Provide wall-clock training time vs depth and dataset size, comparing exact loss-augmented inference to the Hamming-ball variant and to greedy baselines. This would quantify the practical benefit of the fast inference.
- Effect of Hamming-ball restriction: Beyond the one example in the supplement, report accuracy and bound-tightness comparisons across datasets/depths when using exact vs restricted loss-augmented inference to demonstrate that the restriction does not harm accuracy.
- Inactive leaves: Several leaves become inactive during training. Please quantify how often this occurs, its impact on accuracy and depth utilization, and whether SSGD prevents degeneracy. A brief EM/CCCP perspective or an entropy/balancing regularizer over path decisions could justify and possibly improve stability.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear, principled method with a solid theoretical surrogate.
- Efficient and scalable training for deep oblique trees.
- Consistent empirical gains over strong greedy baselines.
- Addresses an important and long-standing limitation of greedy tree induction.

Cons:
- Experimental evaluation is relatively narrow and lacks several informative baselines and runtime analyses.
- Some presentation details (bounds, tie-breaking, SSGD motivation) could be clearer.
- Sensitivity to initialization and depth selection is not fully explored.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution that advances non-greedy decision tree learning with a well-motivated surrogate and efficient optimization. With the authors’ clarification, the loss bound is sound. I recommend acceptance, contingent on strengthening the experimental section (additional baselines, runtime comparisons, and ablations) and improving clarity around the bound, fast inference, and SSGD.
