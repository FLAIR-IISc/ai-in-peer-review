- Proposes a novel non-greedy framework that jointly learns split functions across all tree levels under a global objective.  
- Frames decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables and derives a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss.  
- Shows a refined upper bound reduces exponential complexity of naive latent-variable approaches to polynomial time, enabling deep tree training.  
- Develops an SGD training procedure with O(d²p) complexity per iteration (d = tree depth, p = feature dimension).  
- Method is theoretically grounded, handles arbitrary loss functions, and includes row-wise L2 constraints to prevent overfitting.  
- Fast loss-augmented inference (Hamming-ball around sgn(Wx)) addresses the computational bottleneck elegantly.  
- Experimental results on SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST show consistent but modest improvements over greedy baselines (≤1% on MNIST at depth 12).  
- Empirical gains are limited, with minimal improvements on some datasets and high variance for small regularization (ν).  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written with clear problem formulation, precise algorithmic details, and thorough complexity analysis; supplementary material supports proofs.  
- Originality is high: generalizes prior global tree optimization to arbitrary losses and achieves polynomial-time training, a notable technical advance.  
- Significance lies in potential to spur future work (e.g., L1 sparsity, kernelized splits, ensembles) despite incremental current empirical gains.  
- Pros: strong theoretical novelty, elegant upper bound, scalable complexity, loss-agnostic framework, clear presentation.  
- Cons: marginal empirical improvement questions real-world impact; overfitting not fully mitigated; some heuristics (e.g., "last split") untested.  
- Recommendation: accept — theoretical contribution and potential justify publication; suggest testing "last split" tweak, adding comparisons to newer baselines, and deeper overfitting/regularization analysis.
