## Review of “Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees”

This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters – decision splits and posterior probabilities – of decision trees. This is a valuable contribution, as current methods typically rely on greedy, layer-by-layer training, which can lead to suboptimal trees. The authors formulate the problem as a single optimization problem, then derive an upper bound and approximation to achieve a tractable formulation amenable to stochastic gradient descent.

**Quality:** The paper is generally technically sound and well-written, though some aspects of the theoretical justification require closer scrutiny (see detailed comments below). The formulation is novel and the derivation, while complex, is presented clearly. The completeness of the work is reasonable for a conference submission, but further investigation into the stability and sensitivity of the method would be beneficial. The authors acknowledge and attempt to address potential overfitting through regularization, demonstrating careful consideration of the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

**Clarity:** The mathematical derivations are generally clear and well-explained. However, the presentation of experiments and their interpretation could be improved. The paper would benefit from more detailed explanations of hyperparameter choices and a more thorough discussion of the impact of the proposed optimizations on runtime. The connection between the proposed method and related work, particularly in structured prediction, is well-articulated.

**Originality:** Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important and relatively unexplored area. While building on techniques from structured prediction, the application to decision trees and the development of the efficient optimization scheme represent a novel contribution. The connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is a particularly insightful aspect of this work.

**Significance:** The proposed solution addresses a fundamental limitation of traditional decision tree learning. If successful, the method could lead to improved accuracy and generalization performance, especially in scenarios where greedy methods struggle. The potential to handle general loss functions and the scalability achieved through the proposed optimization techniques are also significant advantages. However, the practical impact is somewhat limited by the reliance on pre-defined tree depth and the relatively modest improvements demonstrated in the experimental results.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Potential Flaw in Empirical Loss Bound:** The justification for the upper bound in Eq. (7) requires careful consideration. The reasoning presented in lines 216-218 is potentially flawed, as illustrated by the example provided in the original review. While the authors addressed this concern in the rebuttal, the use of `\max` instead of `\argmax` does not entirely resolve the issue. A more rigorous proof of the upper bound's validity is recommended.
*   **Inactive Leaves:** The observation that some leaves are not assigned any data points during training is concerning. The authors suggest a heuristic to address this, but a more principled explanation for this phenomenon and a more robust solution are desirable. Exploring connections to expectation-maximization or concave-convex procedures, as suggested, could be fruitful.
*   **Efficiency of Loss-Augmented Inference:** The authors argue for the efficiency of their fast loss-augmented inference method. However, more detailed analysis is needed to quantify the performance gains compared to a naive, exhaustive search, especially for deeper trees. The impact of the Hamming ball restriction on performance requires further investigation, and the definition of "leaves" in the supplementary material needs clarification.
*   **Missing Baseline:** The absence of a baseline training non-axis-aligned split functions in a standard greedy manner (without initialization from an axis-aligned tree) is a notable omission. This baseline would provide a clearer assessment of the benefits of the joint optimization approach.
*   **Tree Depth Limitation:** The requirement to pre-define the tree depth is a significant limitation. The authors should discuss this limitation more thoroughly and consider exploring techniques for dynamically determining the optimal tree depth, potentially through cross-validation or pruning strategies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to decision tree learning with the potential for improved accuracy.
*   Sound theoretical foundation, despite some potential issues requiring clarification.
*   Scalable optimization scheme enabling training with large datasets.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method and experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Potential flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound.
*   Observation of inactive leaves during training raises concerns about the optimization process.
*   Limited experimental evaluation and lack of a crucial baseline.
*   Reliance on pre-defined tree depth limits the applicability of the method.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. While some technical details require further clarification and the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the novelty and potential impact of the proposed method justify consideration for acceptance. I recommend acceptance contingent on addressing the concerns raised in the detailed comments and providing a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach in the final version. I am happy to adjust my score given a satisfactory response to these points during the rebuttal phase.
