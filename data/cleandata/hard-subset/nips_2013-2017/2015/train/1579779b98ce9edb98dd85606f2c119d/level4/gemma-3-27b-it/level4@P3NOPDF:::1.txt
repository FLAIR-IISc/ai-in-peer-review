This paper proposes an elegant nonconvex relaxation for the global optimization of decision trees with a fixed structure. The approach is well-motivated and clearly presented, and I believe it has the potential to stimulate further research in related areas. Following discussion with other reviewers and consideration of the rebuttal, I recommend acceptance, though with reservations stemming from the experimental results.

The core idea—relaxing the discrete decision tree structure into a continuous space to enable global optimization—is compelling. The mathematical formulation is sound and the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges involved in nonconvex optimization. The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible. 

However, the experimental evaluation presents a mixed picture. While the methodology is sound, the reported results are somewhat disappointing. The gains over baseline methods are not substantial, and in some cases, performance is comparable or even slightly worse. This raises concerns about the practical impact of the proposed approach in its current form.

The authors acknowledge this limitation in their rebuttal and offer explanations. However, the explanations do not fully address the gap between the theoretical elegance of the method and its empirical performance. 

I believe a relatively simple modification could potentially improve the results. Specifically, after applying the global optimization procedure, removing the final split in each tree and optimizing it (along with the associated leaf nodes) using a standard greedy approach might yield better performance. The global optimization would establish a strong initial structure, and then local refinement could address potential issues arising from the relaxation.

Furthermore, it's important to consider that global optimization techniques, while powerful, can be prone to overfitting, particularly when dealing with complex models like decision trees. The authors should explicitly discuss this trade-off and potentially investigate regularization strategies to mitigate overfitting.  The rebuttal doesn't fully address this, and further experimentation or discussion of this point would be beneficial.

Despite these weaknesses in the experimental results, I believe the paper's contribution lies in its novel approach and potential for future work. The authors have identified a promising direction for improving decision tree optimization, and their work provides a solid foundation for further investigation. If the results were significantly stronger, I would rate this paper higher. However, given the current performance, I believe the assigned score is appropriate, reflecting both the innovation and the limitations of the work.
