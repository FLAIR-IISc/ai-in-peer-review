**Summary**

The authors propose a method for jointly training all parameters of decision trees, including decision splits and posterior probabilities. This is an important topic, as current methods typically train trees in a greedy, layer-by-layer manner. The task is formulated as a single optimization problem, which is then upper-bounded and approximated to obtain a tractable formulation.

**Quality**

The paper is well-written, but there may be flaws in the reasoning. Specifically, the derivation of the empirical loss bound appears to have a potential issue, and the presentation of the experiments could be improved.

**Clarity**

The derivation of the method is clean and well-presented, but the experiments and their presentation could be enhanced. For example, the authors could provide more details on the experimental setup and the results.

**Originality**

Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important and original topic. The authors' approach is novel and has the potential to improve the state-of-the-art in decision tree training.

**Significance**

The proposed solution is valuable, but there appear to be some shortcomings. The authors' method has the potential to improve the performance of decision trees, but the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and there may be a flaw in the derivation.

**Comments**

1. **Flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound?**

The argument in lines 216-217 is confusing, and the example provided in the supplementary material does not cover all possible cases. Specifically, the proof of Proposition 1 does not address the case where the right-hand side of Equation (7) is smaller than the left-hand side. This could potentially lead to an invalid upper bound.

For instance, consider the example where Wx = [5; -5], and the optimal solution is \hat{b} = [1; -1] = sgn(Wx). In this case, the right-hand side of Equation (7) is g^T Wx - \hat{b}^T Wx + ℓ(...) = -10 + ℓ(...), which could be smaller than ℓ(...). This contradicts the claim that the right-hand side is an upper bound.

2. **Unassigned leaves**

Some leaves are not assigned any data points, which seems strange and could indicate a problem with the objective or the initialization. The suggested heuristic of a fixed assignment of data points to tree leaves appears suboptimal and could be improved using expectation-maximization or concave-convex procedures, similar to other clustering techniques.

3. **Importance of efficient loss-augmented inference**

The depth of the tree is at most 16, and greedily checking 2^16 = 65536 values is still feasible. The impact of the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball on performance is unclear, and more results are needed to demonstrate its effectiveness. Additionally, the term "leaves" is not clearly defined.

4. **Baseline comparison**

A baseline that trains non-axis aligned split functions of trees in a standard greedy manner is missing. This comparison would help to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

5. **Tree depth limitation**

The depth of the tree needs to be specified ahead of time, which is a significant limitation. The authors should discuss this limitation more carefully and provide a more thorough experimental evaluation regarding the depth parameter.

**Additional comments**

The authors' explanation of the loss bound (Equation 7) is clear, and the use of \arg\max could be replaced with \max. An experiment comparing the runtime of the proposed method with other approaches would strengthen the paper. A more careful evaluation of the method would be desirable for the reader.

Overall, the submission considers an interesting problem of jointly training all parameters of decision trees. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and there may be a flaw in the derivation. A careful explanation in the rebuttal would be necessary to address these concerns.
