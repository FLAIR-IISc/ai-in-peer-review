This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters (decision splits and posterior probabilities) of decision trees. This is an important topic, as current methods typically train trees in a greedy, layer-by-layer fashion. The core idea is to formulate the training as a single optimization problem, then derive an upper bound and approximation to achieve a tractable formulation.

**Quality:** The paper is generally well-written, but potential flaws exist in the reasoning, particularly regarding the derivation of the empirical loss bound. The proposed method appears technically sound, but the completeness of the theoretical justification warrants closer scrutiny.

**Clarity:** The derivation is relatively clean, though the experimental presentation could be improved for better readability. Providing more details regarding implementation choices and hyperparameter tuning would enhance clarity.

**Originality:** Investigating joint training for decision trees is a significant research direction. While the idea of non-greedy training isn’t entirely novel (referenced work exists), the specific formulation using structured prediction with latent variables and the proposed optimization techniques represent a valuable contribution.

**Significance:** The proposed solution holds potential, although some limitations and unanswered questions remain. Successfully addressing the challenges of non-greedy tree training could lead to substantial improvements in performance and generalization, particularly for complex datasets.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Potential Flaw in Loss Bound Derivation:** The argument surrounding lines 216-218 regarding the validity of the upper bound requires further clarification. The example provided in the original review (W\bx = [5;-5], \hat \bh = [1;-1], g = [1;1]) illustrates a scenario where the proposed bound might not hold. While the authors have addressed this concern in a rebuttal, explicitly stating the change from \arg\max to \max in the manuscript would be helpful. The complete proof in the supplementary material still needs careful verification considering this potential case.

*   **Unassigned Leaves:** The observation that some leaves remain unassigned to any data points is intriguing and potentially problematic. This suggests either an issue with the objective function, the initialization strategy, or the optimization process. The authors' suggestion of a fixed assignment heuristic (akin to expectation maximization) is a reasonable approach, but a more thorough justification and experimental evaluation would be beneficial.

*   **Efficiency of Loss-Augmented Inference:** The efficiency gains from the Hamming ball restriction in the loss-augmented inference merit further discussion. Given the relatively modest tree depths (up to 16) explored in the experiments, the computational burden of a naive exhaustive search might not be prohibitive. Quantifying the performance impact of this restriction (e.g., comparing results with and without it) would strengthen the paper. Clarifying what "leaves" refer to in the supplementary material would also enhance understanding.

*   **Missing Baseline:** The absence of a baseline that trains non-axis-aligned split functions using a standard greedy approach is a noticeable omission. Such a baseline would help isolate the benefits of the non-greedy optimization and better contextualize the experimental results.

*   **Tree Depth as a Hyperparameter:** The fixed tree depth represents a limitation that should be discussed more comprehensively. Exploring adaptive depth selection strategies or evaluating the sensitivity of performance to different depth values would be valuable. A more in-depth experimental analysis focused specifically on the depth parameter is needed.



**Summary of Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in decision tree learning.
*   Presents a novel formulation based on structured prediction with latent variables.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results on benchmark datasets, outperforming greedy baselines.
*   Offers a potentially efficient optimization algorithm with complexity scaling favorably with tree depth.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Potential flaws in the derivation of the empirical loss bound require further clarification and verification.
*   The phenomenon of unassigned leaves raises concerns about the objective function or optimization process.
*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, lacking key baselines and a comprehensive analysis of hyperparameters.
*   The fixed tree depth is a limitation that requires more thorough discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. While some concerns remain regarding the theoretical justification and experimental evaluation, the proposed approach is promising and warrants further investigation. With a strong rebuttal addressing the raised concerns, this paper could be a valuable addition to NIPS.
