

This paper presents an algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions at the nodes. The approach optimizes tree parameters (i.e., weights in the split functions and the output distribution at each leaf) globally, as opposed to the standard greedy method. The paper proposes an upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which is regularized via L2 regularization of the split function weights to yield a smoother optimization problem. The algorithm then employs stochastic gradient descent to identify tree parameters minimizing this upper bound.

The tree optimization problem is elegantly formulated through a connection to structured prediction with latent variables. However, the empirical results fail to convincingly demonstrate that global optimization is superior to greedy tree learning. The globally optimized tree outperforms the greedy tree only on a few datasets, while exhibiting significantly higher computational cost.

The paper is well-structured, but several points require clarification:
- The stable SGD variant is not clearly described. Specifically, how are data points assigned to leaves? A pseudo-code would be helpful.
- Figure 2: Are these results obtained using standard SGD or stable SGD? What precisely constitutes an "active" leaf?
- Line 10 of Algorithm 1 updates $\Theta$ and then projects it onto a simplex. How is this projection solved in practice? It would also be useful to note that this projection ensures each row of $\Theta$ sums to one.
