

The submission presents an intriguing reformulation of global decision tree (DT) optimization as a latent structured prediction problem, leveraging sign vectors to encode node decisions. The core contribution lies in casting the non-differentiable, non-convex DT optimization problem as a structured prediction task, enabling gradient-based training via efficient loss-augmented inference. The proposed SGD approach solves a sequence of structured inference problems, with the key novelty being sparse gradient updates—each step modifies only a single node's weights. This property suggests a compelling theoretical pathway for *automatically growing* tree complexity by incrementally activating weights (starting from zero), raising profound questions about its relationship to greedy strategies or pruning. However, the paper insufficiently explores these implications, treating sparsity merely as a computational advantage rather than a foundational mechanism.

**Strengths:** The reformulation is mathematically sound and conceptually novel. The structured prediction framework elegantly connects DT optimization to a well-studied paradigm, and the inference algorithms (Sections 6.2–6.4) are technically solid, though slightly opaque in presentation (e.g., Section 6.2 could clarify the two-stage maximization). The method’s theoretical promise—particularly the potential for *adaptive tree growth*—is the paper’s most compelling aspect.

**Critical Concerns:**  
1. **Interpretability Gap:** The paper’s motivation is undermined by a fundamental misalignment with DTs’ primary utility. DTs are widely adopted *because* of their interpretability, which requires sparse, binary splits (e.g., weight vectors with exactly one non-zero entry per node). The proposed method uses L2 regularization, producing dense weights that destroy interpretability. While L1 regularization could enforce sparsity, the paper provides no evidence that such regularization preserves performance (e.g., Fig. 2 suggests dense weights). The authors must address: *How does this method deliver interpretable trees, or why would anyone prefer it over non-interpretive alternatives (e.g., random forests, gradient boosting) when interpretability is irrelevant?* Without resolving this, the method’s practical relevance is questionable.

2. **Theoretical Ambiguity (Zero Weights):** The handling of zero-weight nodes is a critical flaw. The paper (p.3) states that a zero weight defaults to the right branch—a questionable heuristic with no justification. In reality, a zero weight should trigger a *decision* (e.g., a leaf), with potential future activation via gradient updates. This choice directly impacts the proposed tree-growth mechanism: if zero weights are treated as "unsplit" nodes, the method may simply converge to a fixed tree structure, negating the growth advantage. The rebuttal notes random initialization avoids this issue, but the *theoretical appeal hinges on initialization from zero*. The authors must clarify the zero-weight semantics and analyze the growth dynamics *when weights start at zero*.

3. **Insufficient Experimental Comparison:** The experiments lack rigor in benchmarking against baselines. The "naive" greedy method is compared without specifying pruning strategies (e.g., cost-complexity pruning, depth limits), while the proposed method tests multiple regularization parameters. For fairness, the baseline must be evaluated across equivalent hyperparameter regimes (e.g., with and without pruning). Additionally, trivial global optimization baselines—such as training multiple greedy trees on random data subsets and selecting the best-performing one—should be included. The absence of these comparisons weakens the claimed performance gains.

**Clarity and Scope:** The paper is generally well-written, but Sections 6.2 and 6.4 would benefit from concise summaries (e.g., "First maximize over splits per leaf, then over leaves"). The title references "forests," yet the method addresses only single trees—a significant oversight requiring correction.

**Conclusion:** This work proposes a clever mathematical reformulation with intriguing theoretical potential, particularly regarding adaptive tree growth. However, it fails to demonstrate practical utility due to unresolved interpretability issues, ambiguous theoretical foundations (zero weights), and inadequate experimental validation. The method’s value proposition—*why use DTs at all if interpretability is sacrificed?*—remains unconvincing. The authors should:  
- Explicitly address how interpretability is preserved (e.g., via L1 regularization with performance validation),  
- Rigorously analyze tree growth under zero-initialization,  
- Expand experiments to include fair baseline comparisons (pruning strategies, trivial global baselines).  

Without these, the contribution risks being a technically elegant but practically irrelevant extension of existing DT optimization. The theoretical insights are promising, but the paper does not yet justify its place in the applied machine learning landscape.  

**Final Recommendation:** Weak Accept (with major revisions addressing interpretability, zero-weight analysis, and experimental rigor). The core idea is compelling, but the current presentation lacks the necessary grounding to convince reviewers of its practical significance.
