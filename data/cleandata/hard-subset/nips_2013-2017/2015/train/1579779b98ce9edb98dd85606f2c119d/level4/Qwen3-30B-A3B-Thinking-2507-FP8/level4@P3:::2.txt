

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper addresses an important problem in decision tree learning: the standard greedy approach to tree construction, which optimizes splits one node at a time, often leads to suboptimal trees. The authors propose a novel framework for non-greedy optimization of decision trees by jointly training all split parameters and leaf parameters under a global objective. They establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is showing how to reduce the computational complexity from exponential to O(d²p) through a modified surrogate objective, enabling training of deeper trees.

The paper builds on prior work in decision tree learning (e.g., Breiman's random forests [4], CART [5]) and structured prediction (e.g., Yu and Joachims [25]). It extends Bennett's non-greedy tree optimization [1] by handling general loss functions and providing an efficient optimization method, while addressing limitations of soft-split approaches [13] that lose the computational advantages of decision trees.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear theoretical foundation. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is well-motivated, and the convex-concave upper bound formulation is a significant contribution. However, there appears to be a flaw in the derivation of Equation (7), the key bound on the empirical loss. The draft review's example demonstrates that for Wx = [5; -5], choosing g = [1; 1] leads to a right-hand side of -10 + ℓ(...), which could be smaller than ℓ(...) and thus violate the upper bound property. The supplementary proof of Proposition 1 does not address this case, making the bound's validity questionable. The authors should clarify this point or provide a corrected derivation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written with a clean mathematical derivation. However, the experimental section could be improved. The authors compare with several baselines (greedy axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2 [18]), but omit a critical baseline: greedy training of non-axis-aligned splits without the non-greedy initialization. This omission makes it difficult to isolate the contribution of the non-greedy approach from that of the initialization procedure. The presentation of results (Fig. 2) is somewhat unclear regarding the exact performance gains of the non-greedy method over greedy methods. The paper also fails to explain why some leaves become inactive during training, which appears to be a significant issue that could affect the model's capacity.

**Originality:** The paper's core contribution—jointly optimizing all tree parameters rather than using a greedy approach—is genuinely novel and addresses a long-standing limitation of decision tree learning. The authors' connection to structured prediction with latent variables is insightful, and the efficient implementation via the Hamming ball approximation represents a significant technical advance over prior non-greedy methods [1, 3]. This work clearly differentiates itself from previous NIPS papers on decision trees (e.g., [9, 14, 17]) by providing a scalable optimization method for non-greedy tree learning.

**Significance:** The proposed method has the potential to significantly improve decision tree performance, as demonstrated in the experiments where non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines across multiple datasets. The ability to train deep trees efficiently with general loss functions is particularly valuable for applications requiring high accuracy. However, the paper's significance is somewhat diminished by the unexplained "inactive leaves" issue and the lack of a proper baseline for comparison. The authors' claim that the method "leads to better generalization performance" is supported by experiments, but the mechanism behind this improvement (beyond the norm regularization) is not sufficiently explained.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by providing an efficient, non-greedy approach to decision tree learning that addresses a fundamental limitation of standard methods. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is well-motivated, and the technical innovation in reducing computational complexity to O(d²p) is substantial. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets.

**Weaknesses:** The most critical weakness is the potential flaw in the theoretical bound (Equation 7), which undermines the foundation of the proposed method. The missing baseline for greedy non-axis-aligned trees makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the non-greedy approach. The paper also fails to adequately address the "inactive leaves" issue, which appears to be a significant problem in the optimization procedure. The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including a more thorough analysis of the tree depth parameter and a runtime comparison demonstrating the efficiency gains of the proposed method.

**Recommendation:** The paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound approach, but the potential flaw in the theoretical derivation is concerning. If the authors can provide a corrected derivation that resolves the issue highlighted in the draft review, this would be a strong contribution. As currently written, the paper has significant merit but requires clarification on the theoretical bound and additional experimental comparisons to fully establish its contribution. I recommend a borderline acceptance pending clarification of the theoretical issue and additional experiments addressing the missing baseline and inactive leaves problem.

**Summary of Pro/Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel approach to non-greedy decision tree learning
- Clear connection to structured prediction with latent variables
- Efficient optimization method with O(d²p) complexity
- Demonstrated improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets

*Con:*
- Potential flaw in the key theoretical bound (Equation 7)
- Missing critical baseline (greedy non-axis-aligned trees)
- Unexplained "inactive leaves" issue
- Experimental evaluation could be more thorough

The paper tackles a fundamental limitation in decision tree learning with a technically interesting approach. With clarification of the theoretical issue and additional experimental comparisons, this would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, it is a promising but incomplete work that would benefit from a more careful theoretical treatment and experimental analysis.
