This paper introduces a novel algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions, departing from the standard greedy approach. The core idea is to jointly optimize tree parameters – split function weights and leaf distributions – via a global objective function. To enable tractable optimization, the authors propose a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, regularized with an L2 penalty on split function weights, and employ stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for minimization. The formulation cleverly establishes a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables.

The work builds upon a rich history of decision tree research [5, 4, 7, 10], addressing a long-standing challenge: the suboptimality arising from greedy tree induction [21, 5]. While recent efforts have focused on improving greedy algorithms via novel objective functions [20, 12], this paper pursues a fundamentally different strategy—global optimization. The proposed approach offers a potential pathway to overcome the limitations inherent in sequential, local optimization, potentially creating more compact and generalizable trees. Related work in global tree optimization, such as Bennett [1], suffers from computational complexity. This paper addresses this limitation with a scalable algorithm.  The use of structured prediction techniques [25, 23, 24] to formulate the decision tree learning problem is a significant contribution, as is the development of an efficient gradient computation (O(d)). 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to decision tree learning, moving beyond the traditional greedy paradigm.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:**  The connection to structured prediction with latent variables provides a strong theoretical justification for the proposed method. The formulation and the derived upper bound are well-motivated.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The reduction of gradient complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a crucial contribution, enabling training of deeper trees with large datasets.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the key ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Empirical Results:** While the experiments demonstrate improvements over greedy baselines, the gains are not consistently substantial across all datasets. The improvement is only significant on a few datasets and the computational cost is higher. A more compelling demonstration of the method's superiority is needed.
*   **Clarity of SSGD:** The "Stable SGD" (SSGD) variant is not explained sufficiently clearly. The process of assigning data points to leaves needs to be detailed, and a pseudocode example would be highly beneficial. What constitutes an "active" leaf needs a more precise definition.
*   **Implementation Details:** The implementation details regarding the projection onto a simplex in line 10 of Algorithm 1 are missing. Elaborating on how this is practically handled would improve clarity.
*   **Minor Issues:** Several minor grammatical errors and typos exist throughout the paper, as noted below. These detract slightly from the overall polish.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to decision tree learning.
*   The computational efficiency improvements are significant and enable scaling to deeper trees.
*   The connection to structured prediction provides a valuable perspective on the problem.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical results, while positive, are not consistently strong enough to warrant a major departure from established greedy methods.
*   The explanation of SSGD and some implementation details are insufficient for reproducibility.
*   The presence of minor grammatical errors and inconsistencies.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to decision tree learning. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the computational efficiency gains are valuable. However, the empirical results are not entirely convincing, and some aspects of the method (particularly SSGD) require further clarification. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, specifically a more compelling empirical evaluation, a clearer explanation of SSGD, and correction of the identified errors. A stronger demonstration of the performance benefits relative to the computational cost would significantly strengthen the paper.
