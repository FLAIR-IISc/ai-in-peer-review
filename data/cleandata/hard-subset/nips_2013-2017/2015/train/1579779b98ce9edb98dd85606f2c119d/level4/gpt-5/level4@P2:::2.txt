Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy method for training decision trees by jointly optimizing all split functions (oblique, linear-combination tests) and leaf parameters under a single global objective. The authors draw a connection to structured prediction with latent variables by treating the vector of split decisions as latent, and derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized with stochastic gradient descent (SGD). They further develop a fast loss-augmented inference procedure based on restricting to a Hamming ball around the current decisions, reducing per-example complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p), where d is depth and p is input dimension. Experiments on several multiclass benchmarks compare the proposed non-greedy trees to greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) and the CO2 method, reporting accuracy gains and reduced overfitting across depths.

Relation to prior work: The paper contrasts with classical greedy induction (ID3, CART) and with soft-split models (HME) that trade inference efficiency for smooth objectives. It positions itself relative to earlier attempts at global tree optimization (e.g., Bennett and collaborators) and margin-based tree methods. The structured prediction framing relates to structural SVMs and latent variable methods.

Evaluation

- Quality: The optimization framework is well motivated and the bound-driven SGD is appealing. However, the derivation and interpretation of the upper bound in Eq. (7) were initially confusing; see specific comments. The proposed fast loss-augmented inference is technically neat and practically important for deeper trees. The empirical evaluation is promising but somewhat limited in breadth and diagnostics.

- Clarity: The exposition of the formulation and the connection to structured prediction is clear. Some aspects of the experiments (terminology, baselines, and the behavior of inactive leaves) need clearer explanation. A few proofs and justifications in the supplement could be tightened.

- Originality: Joint, non-greedy training of oblique splits with a global objective and a structured latent-variable bound is a novel and worthwhile perspective. The Hamming-ball restriction for efficient loss-augmented inference in trees is also a useful contribution.

- Significance: If robust, the approach could influence how trees (and potentially forests) are trained, offering better accuracy–depth trade-offs and more principled control via norm constraints. The practical impact would be clearer with stronger baselines and runtime analyses.

Detailed comments and questions

1) Upper bound on empirical loss (Eq. 7). The text states that for any g ≠ sgn(Wx), the RHS can only increase relative to g = sgn(Wx). As written, this can be confusing. For example, with W x = [5, −5], ĥ = [1, −1] gives ĥ^T W x = 10. Choosing g = [1, 1] yields g^T W x = 0, so the RHS becomes −10 + ell(…), which could be smaller than ell(…) and thus appear to violate an upper bound. In the rebuttal, the authors clarified the role of the outer max and the pairing of both max terms. Please incorporate a clearer, self-contained justification in the paper and consider replacing argmax with max in the statement, as suggested. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement should also explicitly cover such cases.

2) Inactive leaves. During training, some leaves end up with no assigned data. This raises concerns about either the objective or the optimization dynamics. The proposed “Stable SGD” that freezes assignments for several steps is a reasonable heuristic; it might be possible to motivate it more formally as an EM/CCCP-like alternating optimization over latent assignments and parameters. Please discuss why leaves become inactive, whether this is desirable regularization or an artifact, and report how often inactive leaves re-activate under SSGD.

3) Fast loss-augmented inference and Hamming-ball restriction. Trees in the experiments have depth up to 16; an exhaustive search over 2^16 configurations is still feasible in principle, though not per SGD step and not for larger d. Please quantify the empirical trade-off: how much accuracy (if any) is lost by restricting to a Hamming ball of radius 1, and does the single example in the supplement generalize? Also clarify the term “leaves” used in the runtime discussion if it means something other than terminal nodes.

4) Baselines. A key missing baseline is a standard greedy oblique-split tree learned without the specialized initialization (i.e., greedy non-axis-aligned splits trained from scratch). Including this would help disentangle the gains from non-greedy joint optimization versus better split families/initialization.

5) Depth selection. The method requires specifying the maximum depth a priori. Please discuss this limitation and provide a more systematic evaluation of depth selection (e.g., validation curves, sensitivity to ν and depth, automatic stopping/pruning criteria). It would also help to clarify how ν implicitly prunes leaves and how this interacts with tree capacity.

6) Runtime and scalability. A direct runtime comparison between the full loss-augmented inference and the Hamming-ball variant would strengthen the paper. Reporting wall-clock times and throughput across depths and datasets would help readers assess practicality.

Pros
- Clear and principled formulation connecting tree training to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient SGD training with per-example complexity O(d^2 p) via fast loss-augmented inference.
- Empirical improvements over several greedy baselines and reduced overfitting across depths.
- Norm constraints on rows of W provide an intuitive regularization knob.

Cons
- Initial presentation of the loss bound is confusing; proofs need tightening.
- Some training dynamics (inactive leaves) are opaque; SSGD is heuristic without formal grounding.
- Missing strong greedy oblique baseline without initialization.
- Limited analysis of depth selection and of the accuracy/runtime trade-offs from the Hamming-ball restriction.

Post-rebuttal note
Thanks for clarifying the reasoning behind the loss bound (Eq. 7); please replace argmax with max where appropriate and expand the proof to cover edge cases. A runtime comparison would also strengthen the work. Overall, this paper addresses an important problem—joint, non-greedy training of decision trees—with a promising approach; with the above clarifications and additional evaluation, it would make a solid contribution. I have adjusted my score accordingly.
