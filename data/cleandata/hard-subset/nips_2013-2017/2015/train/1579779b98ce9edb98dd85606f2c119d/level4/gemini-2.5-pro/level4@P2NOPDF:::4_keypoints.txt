- Novel formulation: frames decision tree training as latent structured prediction with sign vectors as latent branching decisions.  
- Optimization: proposes a non-differentiable loss approximated by a differentiable upper bound and minimized via SGD with loss-augmented inference.  
- Algorithms: provides efficient algorithms to solve the loss-augmented inference sub-problem.  
- Main contribution: elegant reformulation plus efficient inference algorithms offering a fresh perspective on global tree optimization.  
- Sparse updates: gradient updates are sparse (often affecting a single node), which offers computational benefits and suggests a natural tree growth mechanism.  
- Zero-weight nodes: handling of nodes with zero-weight vectors is ambiguous; the default “take right branch” is arbitrary and should be reinterpreted as a leaf that can later split.  
- Grow-from-zero idea: the sparse-update behavior could enable starting from an all-zero tree and gradually growing complexity, but this is not explored.  
- Relationship to greedy methods: unclear whether introducing new splits vs refining existing ones is optimal; need analysis of this relationship.  
- Regularization and interpretability: using L2 likely yields dense oblique splits, undermining interpretability—key motivation for single trees—so the practical niche vs ensembles is unclear.  
- L1 sparsity question: authors should investigate L1 regularization to encourage sparse (potentially 1-sparse) splits and quantify trade-offs with performance.  
- Experimental baselines: greedy baseline evaluation is insufficiently tuned and described; baseline hyperparameter tuning and pruning strategies should be matched to the proposed method.  
- Additional baselines: recommend stronger/simple global baselines (e.g., train multiple greedy trees on random subsets and select by validation) for a fairer comparison.  
- Clarity and presentation: Sections 6.2 and 6.4 need clearer exposition (6.2 could be framed as two-stage maximization; 6.4 needs more details).  
- Title mismatch: paper title mentions “forests” but the work focuses on single decision trees and should be corrected.  
- Overall conclusion: theoretically interesting and promising, but practical utility is unproven without deeper analysis of sparse updates, zero-weight handling, interpretability, and stronger experiments.
