

The submission formulates global decision tree optimization as (latent) structured prediction, using sign vectors to encode node decisions. It introduces a non-differentiable, non-convex loss approximated by a differentiable upper bound, minimizing which via SGD requires solving loss-augmented inference problems addressed with efficient algorithms. Experiments compare the method to standard greedy training.

Key contributions are reframing global DT optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient optimization algorithms. The approach is novel and raises intriguing questions, particularly regarding the sparsity of gradient updates (each step modifies only one node's weight vector). This sparsity suggests potential for growing trees dynamically from zero-initialized weights—allowing complexity to adapt to data—yet the paper inadequately explores implications like equivalence to greedy strategies or convergence to limited-node trees (as Fig. 2 hints). A critical ambiguity remains: the handling of zero-weight nodes (e.g., defaulting to right branch, as implied on p.3) is problematic and analogous to pruning strategies, requiring precise definition.

Practical concerns arise regarding motivation: standard DTs are valued for interpretability, but the method’s L2 regularization likely produces dense weights, compromising interpretability. L1 regularization may be needed to enforce 1-sparse splits, but performance trade-offs are untested. Experiments also lack detail: baselines omit pruning strategy specifics for the greedy method (despite varying regularization in the proposed approach), and trivial global optimization baselines (e.g., greedy training on random data subsets) are unexplored.

The paper is well-written overall, though Sections 6.2 and 6.4 could clarify inference. In summary, the reformulation is clever and raises fundamental questions, but its practical utility is unclear due to interpretability concerns and insufficient experimental depth. The title mentions forests, but the paper does not address this.

PS: The forest extension is unaddressed despite the title.  
POST-REBUTTAL: The zero-weight case remains critical. While the rebuttal notes random initialization avoids it, the method's most compelling potential lies in zero-initialization with gradual weight growth—this direction warrants deeper exploration. The core motivation and experimental validation remain insufficiently convincing.
