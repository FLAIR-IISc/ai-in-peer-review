- Novel formulation: decision tree learning cast as structured prediction with latent variables enabling non-greedy global optimization.
- Technical contribution: convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss that can be optimized with stochastic gradient descent.
- Joint optimization: split functions at all tree levels are optimized under a single global objective rather than greedily per node.
- Complexity reduction: gradient update cost reduced from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) via a modified surrogate objective, making deep tree training feasible.
- Relation to prior work: builds on Bennett (non-greedy trees) while addressing its binary-only and high-complexity limitations.
- Contextualization: explains Murthy and Salzberg’s skepticism (overfitting without regularization) and contrasts with soft-split methods that lose computational efficiency.
- Theoretical foundation: clear proofs of the upper bound and tightness properties; solid connection to structured prediction literature (Yu & Joachims; Taskar et al.; Tsochantaridis et al.).
- Experimental evaluation: comprehensive comparisons against greedy axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2 across multiple benchmarks.
- Empirical results: non-greedy trees consistently show better generalization and reduced overfitting compared to greedy approaches.
- Regularization insight: analysis showing the regularization parameter ν implicitly controls tree pruning, a useful practical insight.
- Clarity and reproducibility: problem formulation, algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 1), and figures are clear and implementable.
- Presentation quality: concise, professional writing with helpful figures (e.g., tree navigation, training time comparisons).
- Originality: novel use of structured prediction with latent variables in decision trees and an efficiently optimizable surrogate objective.
- Generality: method handles arbitrary loss functions while preserving decision tree computational advantages.
- Significance: practical implications for improving tree quality without sacrificing efficiency; potential real-world impact (e.g., Kinect example).
- Research directions: opens avenues like learning sparse split functions and applying kernel methods to split parameters.
- Strengths summarized: theoretical novelty, efficient algorithm, strong experimental evidence, general loss support, and clear exposition.
- Weaknesses summarized: absolute performance gains are modest (typically 1–3% accuracy), necessitating more nuanced analysis of when benefits arise.
- Comparative gap: limited comparison to ensemble methods (random forests), which are relevant baselines for addressing greedy limitations.
- Recommendation: technically sound, practically relevant, and well-presented work; reviewer recommends acceptance despite modest empirical gains.
