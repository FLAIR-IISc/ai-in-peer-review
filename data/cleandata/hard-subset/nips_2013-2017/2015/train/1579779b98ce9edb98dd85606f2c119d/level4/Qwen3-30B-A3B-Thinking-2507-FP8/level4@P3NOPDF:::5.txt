

This paper proposes a novel global optimization approach for learning decision trees with linear split functions at internal nodes, contrasting with standard greedy methods. The authors formulate the tree learning problem as structured prediction with latent variables, derive an upper bound on the empirical loss, and regularize this bound via L2 penalties on split weights to enable smoother optimization. The core algorithm employs stochastic gradient descent (SGD) to jointly optimize split weights and leaf distributions. While the theoretical formulation is elegant and the problem setup is well-motivated, the empirical evaluation fails to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of global optimization over greedy learning. The globally optimized tree outperforms the greedy baseline on only two datasets (Table 2), while incurring significantly higher computational costs (e.g., 3–5× longer training times on the largest dataset). Given that the primary claim hinges on the advantage of global optimization, this limited empirical support weakens the paper's contribution.  

The paper is well-structured and the technical approach is clearly articulated, but several critical ambiguities require clarification:  
1. **Stable SGD**: The "stable" variant of SGD (Section 4.1) lacks sufficient detail. Specifically, the mechanism for assigning data points to leaves during optimization is unclear. A pseudocode description of the stable SGD procedure would greatly enhance reproducibility.  
2. **Figure 2**: The results depicted are ambiguous. Are they obtained using standard SGD or stable SGD? Additionally, the term "active leaf" (Figure 2 caption) is undefined—please specify whether this refers to leaves with non-zero weight in the output distribution or another criterion.  
3. **Algorithm 1**: The projection of $\Theta$ onto a simplex (line 10) is mentioned but not explained. How is this projection implemented in practice? It would be valuable to note that this step ensures each row of $\Theta$ sums to 1, preserving its interpretation as a leaf distribution.  

**Minor issues requiring correction**:  
- Lines 407–408: The phrase "tune the *number of features evaluated*" is misleading. Standard axis-aligned decision trees evaluate *all* features at each node; this parameter is redundant and should be rephrased (e.g., "tune the *feature subset size* for evaluation").  
- Equations (7)–(9): "argmax" should be "max" (consistent with loss-augmented inference).  
- Figure 1: Never referenced in the main text—please add a brief mention.  
- Line 141: "the leaf on by this path" → "the leaf traversed by this path."  
- Line 238: "the solution to loss-augmented inference" → "the solutions to loss-augmented inference."  
- Line 301: "An key observation" → "A key observation."  
- Line 360: "FSGD" is undefined—please spell out or clarify.  
- Line 404: "a tree with minimum training errors" → likely intended as "a tree with *minimized* training errors" or "tuned for minimum training errors."  

**Supplementary material corrections**:  
- Line 29: "max{g \in sgn...}" → "max{g = sgn...}"  
- Line 30: "l(\Theta^Tf(g), y)" → "l(\Theta^Tf(sgn(Wx)), y)" (consistent with the corrected argument).  

The theoretical foundation is promising, particularly the connection to structured prediction with latent variables. However, the paper’s central claim—that global optimization consistently improves performance over greedy methods—lacks sufficient empirical validation. The limited datasets where the global approach succeeds (and the computational cost) suggest the method may only be beneficial in niche scenarios. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should:  
(i) Provide a more comprehensive comparison (e.g., on additional datasets or with more baselines),  
(ii) Analyze *why* global optimization helps on specific datasets (e.g., feature correlations), and  
(iii) Quantify the computational trade-off more clearly (e.g., runtime vs. accuracy gains).  

Without these, the paper risks overstating its contribution. While the algorithmic idea is interesting, the current results do not justify the shift from greedy to global optimization as a general improvement. The authors have laid a strong theoretical groundwork but must address the empirical shortcomings to demonstrate practical relevance. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the conditions under which global optimization is advantageous versus computationally prohibitive.  

Overall, the work is technically sound but the empirical evidence falls short of supporting the paper’s core thesis. With revisions addressing the clarity gaps and strengthening the experimental validation, this could be a valuable contribution. As presented, the limited performance gains and computational overhead diminish the paper’s impact.
