This paper proposes a method for training decision trees with oblique (linear) splits by jointly optimizing all split weights and leaf parameters under a single, global objective, instead of the standard node-wise greedy strategy. The authors derive an upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss, introduce L2 regularization on the split weights to make the optimization more well-behaved, and minimize this surrogate using stochastic gradient descent to learn both the internal node weights and the leaf distributions.

The problem is cast neatly by connecting decision tree training to structured prediction with latent variables. However, the empirical evaluation does not fully substantiate the advantage of the global approach over greedy induction: clear improvements appear on only a subset of datasets, while the proposed procedure requires more computation.

The paper is well organized, but several aspects need clarification:
- The Stable SGD variant is not sufficiently detailed. How are examples assigned to leaves during the “frozen” phases? Including pseudocode would clarify the procedure.
- Figure 2: were these results obtained using standard SGD or Stable SGD? Also, please define precisely what constitutes an “active” leaf.
- In line 10 of Algorithm 1, Θ is updated and then projected onto a simplex. How is this projection implemented in practice? It may also be worth noting that this projection enforces each row of Θ to sum to one.

- Lines 407–408: you mention tuning “the number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree.” In standard decision tree induction, all features are typically considered at each split; please clarify what is being tuned here.
- In Eqs. (7), (8), and (9), “argmax” should be replaced with “max.”
- Figure 1 is never cited in the main text.
- Line 141: “...to the index of the leaf on by this path.” The ending of the sentence appears incorrect.
- Line 238: “...the solution to loss-augmented inference...” → “the solutions to...”
- Line 301: “An key observation...” → “A key...”
- Line 360: What does FSGD stand for?
- Line 404: “...a tree with minimum training errors.” Do you mean “tuning” errors?

Overall, the optimization framework is elegant, but the empirical evidence is not yet convincing.
