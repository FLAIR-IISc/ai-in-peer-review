- Proposes a novel global, non-greedy decision tree optimization by framing it as structured prediction with latent binary vectors for internal-node decisions.  
- Derives a convex-concave upper bound surrogate for the non-differentiable empirical loss and minimizes it with SGD.  
- Requires solving loss-augmented inference repeatedly and provides efficient algorithms for that step.  
- Empirical results show the method outperforms standard greedy training.  
- Key contributions: elegant structured-prediction formulation and an efficient optimization algorithm.  
- Strength: casting as structured prediction enables SGD and tractable loss-augmented inference.  
- Strength: gradient updates are sparse (often modify only a single node), suggesting interesting growth dynamics and theoretical questions.  
- Weakness: practical motivation and interpretability are weak because oblique L2-regularized splits produce dense, hard-to-interpret trees.  
- Suggestion: investigate sparsity-inducing regularization (e.g., L1) to recover interpretable, axis-aligned splits.  
- Weakness: experimental evaluation is incomplete—unclear if baseline pruning/hyperparameters were tuned comparably and lacking comparisons to simple global heuristics.  
- Weakness: clarity issues in describing inference algorithms; high-level summaries and explicit trade-offs for fast inference are needed.  
- Ambiguity: handling of zero-weight nodes is unclear (defaulting to right branch seems arbitrary); a principled mechanism analogous to pruning is preferred.  
- Weakness: scope claim about randomized forests is misleading since experiments focus only on single trees; extension to ensembles needs clarification.  
- Summary: conceptually elegant and promising, but interpretability concerns and weaker experimental comparisons limit practical impact.  
- Post-rebuttal: zero-weight-split issue remains critical; authors encouraged to explore zero initialization and growth dynamics as a principled tree-growing procedure.
