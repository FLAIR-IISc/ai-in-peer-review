Summary
This paper formulates global training of oblique decision trees as a latent structured prediction problem. The authors introduce binary latent variables representing split decisions at all internal nodes and derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss. This surrogate enables stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with loss-augmented inference. A key algorithmic contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference procedure based on restricting to a Hamming ball around the current split decisions, reducing per-update complexity to O(d^2 p), where d is tree depth and p the input dimension. The method jointly optimizes split parameters and leaf predictions, and shows improved test accuracy over greedy baselines on several multiclass datasets. The paper also proposes Stable SGD (SSGD) to stabilize leaf assignments and uses per-row L2 norm constraints to regularize W.

Relation to prior work
Global optimization of decision trees is known to be NP-complete (Hyafil & Rivest). Greedy induction (ID3/CART) and oblique variants (e.g., OC1) are standard baselines; max-margin approaches at nodes (Bennett et al.) and soft, probabilistic trees (HMEs) are alternative lines. The CO2 method can be seen as a greedy depth-1 special case of the proposed bound. The paper’s link to structured prediction with latent variables (e.g., structural SVMs with latent variables) is clear and well-motivated.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Casting tree induction as latent structured prediction with a tractable surrogate objective is elegant and well grounded.
- Algorithmic efficiency: The fast loss-augmented inference (Hamming ball radius 1) and per-example O(d^2 p) complexity make deep-tree training feasible.
- Generality: The framework accommodates general losses (e.g., log loss, squared loss) and preserves O(dp) test-time inference.
- Regularization: Per-row norm constraints offer a principled control of fit/tightness and appear to mitigate overfitting in practice.
- Empirical results: Non-greedy trees consistently outperform greedy baselines across depths on several benchmarks.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical motivation and interpretability: A primary appeal of decision trees is interpretability. With L2 regularization, splits will typically be dense and less interpretable; the paper does not evaluate L1 or structured sparsity to recover axis-aligned or near-sparse splits. It is unclear whether performance remains competitive when interpretability is enforced.
- Baselines and pruning details: The experimental section lacks details on pruning strategies for greedy trees. Since the proposed method tunes ν and learning rates, greedy baselines should be evaluated under multiple pruning regimes/parameters. Additional simple global strategies (e.g., training multiple greedy trees on random subsets and selecting by validation loss) would better contextualize gains.
- Edge cases and initialization: The behavior when a split weight vector is exactly zero is under-specified. The description suggests defaulting to one branch, which seems arbitrary; a natural alternative would be to output a leaf decision at that node until a split is justified, linking the method to pruning/growing strategies.
- Learning dynamics and sparsity: A notable property is the sparsity of gradient updates: each SGD step modifies weights only along a single path. This is presented as a computational benefit, but it suggests richer model-growth strategies (e.g., starting from zero weights and growing the tree as needed). The paper does not explore or analyze this regime.
- Clarity in inference sections: Section 6.2 could be clarified by stating explicitly that the inner maximization fixes a leaf and maximizes over compatible g, then maximizes over leaves. Section 6.4 could be more explicit about which bits differ between ĥ and ĝ and how that yields O(dp) updates once the fast inference returns the leaf.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation of the bound and optimization scheme appears correct; the fast inference trick is convincing. However, empirical methodology would benefit from stronger and more transparent baselines and ablations (e.g., ν’s effect on implicit pruning, SSGD vs. SGD).
- Completeness: The core contribution is well developed; practical deployment questions (interpretability, pruning equivalences, initialization) are less fully addressed.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. Minor suggestions: make the loss-augmented inference flow explicit (fix leaf → maximize over g → maximize over leaves), and spell out the bit-difference bookkeeping for O(dp) updates.

Originality
- High. The latent-structured perspective, the specific convex–concave bound, and the fast Hamming-ball inference for trees are novel in this context.

Significance
- Moderate to high if the performance gains hold broadly and if interpretability or sparsity can be incorporated without large accuracy loss. As presented, the method advances non-greedy tree learning but its practical adoption may be limited by interpretability concerns.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant and novel formulation with clear algorithmic contributions
- Efficient training for deep trees with general losses
- Consistent empirical gains over strong greedy baselines

Cons against acceptance
- Insufficient baseline/pruning comparisons and ablations
- Limited discussion of interpretability and sparse splits
- Ambiguity around zero-weight nodes and growth/pruning behavior
- Title mentions forests, but ensembles are not addressed experimentally

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate L1/structured sparsity to assess interpretability–accuracy trade-offs.
- Provide detailed pruning baselines and additional simple global-selection baselines.
- Clarify behavior at zero weights and discuss connections to dynamic tree growth/pruning.
- Expand clarity in Sections 6.2 and 6.4 as noted.
- Either remove “forests” from the title or provide ensemble results.

Post-rebuttal comments
The zero-weight case remains crucial. The rebuttal suggests random initialization, which may avoid this in practice, but starting from zero weights and growing the tree gradually could be particularly compelling and might connect to principled pruning/growing schemes. I encourage exploring this direction. Overall, the reformulation and optimization are clever and potentially impactful, though the experimental validation and practical framing need strengthening.
