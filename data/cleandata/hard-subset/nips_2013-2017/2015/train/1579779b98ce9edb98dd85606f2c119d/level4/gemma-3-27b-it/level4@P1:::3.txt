The authors propose an interesting approach to decision tree optimization by reformulating the problem as structured prediction. This allows for a non-greedy optimization of split functions at all levels of the tree, jointly with the leaf parameters, based on a global objective. The proposed method demonstrates improved test accuracy compared to standard greedy decision tree algorithms across several benchmark datasets. However, the performance gains are generally modest.

The paper begins by highlighting the limitations of traditional greedy decision tree induction methods, such as ID3 and CART, which optimize split functions one node at a time. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss. A key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity of gradient calculation from O(2dp) to O(d2p), where d is the tree depth and p is the input dimensionality, enabling training of deeper trees. The effectiveness of the approach is validated through experiments on datasets including SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The reformulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem is a novel and insightful contribution. This allows for joint optimization and potentially overcomes the limitations of greedy methods.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is significant, making the training of deeper trees feasible.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms greedy baselines, albeit with relatively small improvements.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem is well-defined, and the mathematical formulation is clearly presented.
*   **Connection to Related Work:** The authors appropriately position their work within the context of existing research on decision trees, structured prediction, and optimization techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Modest Improvements:** While the method consistently outperforms baselines, the reported improvements in accuracy are often small. This raises the question of whether the increased complexity is justified by the practical gains.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the regularization parameter ν, requiring careful tuning. The discussion of parameter tuning could be expanded to provide more practical guidance.
*   **Initialization Dependence:** The initialization of the model using a greedy tree (CO2) may influence the final results. It is unclear how robust the method is to different initialization strategies.
*   **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper does not deeply analyze the scenarios where the method fails to outperform the baselines or provide insights into the reasons for these failures.
*   **Clarity of SSGD:** The explanation of the Stable SGD (SSGD) algorithm could be more detailed, specifically regarding the criteria for updating leaf assignments and the benefits of this approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to decision tree learning.
*   The reduction in computational complexity is a significant contribution.
*   The empirical results, although modest, consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
*   The work is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The improvements in accuracy are relatively small, potentially limiting the practical impact of the method.
*   The method requires careful hyperparameter tuning.
*   The analysis of failure cases is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed method is innovative, technically sound, and demonstrates consistent improvements over existing techniques. While the performance gains are not dramatic, the work offers a promising direction for future research. I believe that this paper is of sufficient quality to be accepted to NIPS, but would benefit from further exploration of the limitations and potential improvements. The authors should consider elaborating on the hyperparameter sensitivity, analyzing failure cases, and potentially investigating alternative initialization strategies.
