- Reformulates global decision-tree training as a latent structured prediction problem using sign vectors for internal-node decisions.
- Replaces a non-differentiable, non-convex loss with a differentiable upper bound and optimizes it via SGD, reducing to loss-augmented inference problems solved efficiently.
- Main contributions: (i) casting global tree optimization as structured prediction and (ii) designing efficient optimization procedures.
- Structured-prediction view enables SGD updates that exploit problem structure through repeated loss-augmented inference.
- Gradient updates are sparse—each SGD step modifies only one node’s weight vector—which may enable new modeling possibilities beyond computational benefits.
- Proposal suggests the possibility of growing an infinitely deep tree by activating weights from zero, raising questions about algorithmic behavior and model complexity control.
- Open questions: does the method prefer adding new nodes over revising existing ones, and with regularization, does it converge to compact trees as suggested by Fig. 2?
- Ambiguity about zero-weight nodes: paper implies taking the right branch for zero weights, but more natural/practical behavior (e.g., outputting a decision) is not specified and matters for pruning analogies.
- Interpretability concern: L2 regularization yields dense (non-interpretable) splits; L1 could induce sparsity but may degrade performance—this trade-off needs empirical evaluation.
- Experimental evaluation lacks sufficient baseline detail—unspecified pruning strategies and parameter settings for the greedy baseline make comparisons unclear.
- Recommend including simple alternative global baselines (e.g., training multiple greedy trees on random subsets and selecting by training/validation loss).
- Clarity issues: inference sections (notably 6.2 and 6.4) should be made more explicit and succinct about steps and assumptions.
- Summary: clever, novel, and computationally promising reformulation, but practical utility is unclear due to interpretability issues and insufficient/limited experimental baselines.
- Title/claim mismatch: paper mentions forests but does not actually address them.
- Post-rebuttal: zero-weight initialization remains a critical issue—random initialization sidesteps it, but initializing at zero and growing weights is a more interesting regime that should be explored.
