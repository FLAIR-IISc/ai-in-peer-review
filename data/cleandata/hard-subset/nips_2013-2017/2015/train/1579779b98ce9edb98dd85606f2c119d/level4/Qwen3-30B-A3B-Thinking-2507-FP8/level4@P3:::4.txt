

This paper proposes a novel approach to non-greedy optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, introducing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient training via stochastic gradient descent. The key insight is that the problem of optimizing split functions across all tree levels can be transformed into a structured prediction problem with latent decision variables, where each internal node's split decisions are modeled as binary latent variables. The authors demonstrate that their surrogate objective allows for efficient gradient updates with O(d²p) complexity per iteration, where d is tree depth and p is feature dimension, making deep tree learning feasible.

The paper builds upon prior work in structured prediction (e.g., Yu and Joachims, ICML 2009) and decision tree learning (e.g., Bennett, 1995; Murthy and Salzburg, 1994), but makes a significant contribution by establishing this novel connection and developing efficient algorithms for the resulting optimization problem. The authors show that their method outperforms greedy baselines across multiple classification benchmarks, with the non-greedy trees achieving better generalization performance at various tree depths.

The main strengths of this paper include:
- **Originality**: The connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is novel and well-motivated. The paper clearly articulates how this perspective enables efficient global optimization.
- **Quality**: The technical development is sound, with a rigorous derivation of the upper bound and proof of its properties. The algorithmic details are well-explained, and the computational complexity analysis is thorough.
- **Significance**: The method provides a principled approach to global tree optimization, potentially addressing a long-standing limitation in decision tree learning. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts. The algorithmic descriptions are precise and accessible.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing:

- **Interpretability concerns**: The authors use L2 regularization on the split weights, which produces dense weight vectors and undermines the primary advantage of decision trees—interpretability. The paper does not explore how to maintain interpretability while using the non-greedy approach. This is a significant issue, as the main appeal of decision trees over black-box models is their interpretability. The authors should investigate L1 regularization or other techniques to produce sparse, interpretable splits.

- **Experimental details**: The paper lacks sufficient details about the experimental setup for comparison with baselines. It does not specify which pruning strategies were used for the greedy methods, which is critical since pruning significantly affects tree performance. The authors should clarify whether they applied standard pruning techniques (e.g., reduced error pruning) to all methods or only to their proposed approach.

- **Critical case handling**: The paper does not adequately address the case when a weight vector is zero (Section 3). The authors state that "we append a constant '-1' to the input feature vector" but do not specify how the algorithm handles zero weights. This is a crucial point since it directly relates to tree pruning strategies and could significantly impact the model's behavior.

- **Title misalignment**: The title mentions "forests," but the paper focuses solely on single decision trees. The authors should either revise the title or include a brief discussion of how their method could be extended to forests.

- **Motivation**: The paper does not sufficiently justify why one would choose this non-greedy approach over other high-performing methods (e.g., random forests, gradient boosting) when interpretability is not required. The authors should clarify the specific scenarios where this method would be preferable to existing alternatives.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to decision tree learning, but the practical utility of the method is limited by the interpretability concerns and insufficient experimental details. The connection to structured prediction is novel and well-executed, but the paper fails to fully address the most critical aspect of decision trees: their interpretability.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction
- Efficient algorithm with rigorous complexity analysis
- Demonstrated improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets
- Well-written and technically sound

Cons for acceptance:
- Significant interpretability concerns due to L2 regularization
- Insufficient experimental details for fair comparison with baselines
- Critical case of zero weights not properly addressed
- Title misalignment with content (forests vs. single trees)

In conclusion, this is a technically interesting paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of decision tree optimization. However, the lack of attention to interpretability and insufficient experimental details significantly limit its practical impact. I recommend rejection unless the authors address these concerns in a revision, particularly by clarifying the interpretability issue and providing more detailed experimental comparisons with baselines. The core idea is promising, but the current presentation does not fully demonstrate its value for the NIPS community.
