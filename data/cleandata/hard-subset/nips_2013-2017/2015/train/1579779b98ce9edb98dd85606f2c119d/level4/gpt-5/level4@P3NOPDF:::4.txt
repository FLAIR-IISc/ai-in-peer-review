Summary
The paper reformulates global optimization of decision trees as a latent structured prediction problem, where latent variables are sign vectors defining internal-node decisions. It proposes a non-differentiable, non-convex loss, upper-bounded by a differentiable (still non-convex) surrogate, and optimizes this surrogate with SGD via a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems for which efficient algorithms are provided. Experiments compare the approach to a standard greedy training baseline. The key novelty is the structured-prediction formulation and the resulting efficient optimization procedures.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant reformulation that enables principled, global training beyond greedy splitting.
- Clear algorithmic contributions: reduction to loss-augmented inference and efficient solvers.
- A notable property of the optimization is the sparsity of gradient updates—each step appears to modify only a single node’s weight vector—yielding computational benefits and suggesting interesting model-growth dynamics.

Main concerns and questions
- Role of sparse updates and implications for model growth. The fact that updates are localized to one node per step is more than a computational convenience; it suggests a natural path to grow a tree from a trivial model. One could initialize all node weights at zero and gradually “activate” nodes as needed. This raises theoretical and practical questions:
  - Is there a regime where it is always preferable to introduce a new node rather than revise an existing split, making the method akin to a (regularized) greedy procedure?
  - With appropriate regularization, does the procedure converge to a finite tree with a limited number of active nodes (as suggested by Fig. 2), and under what conditions?
  - A careful analysis of these dynamics would clarify what advantage this global approach has over strong greedy baselines.

- Ambiguity at zero weights. The paper implies that if a node’s weight is zero, the right branch is taken. This choice seems arbitrary and potentially suboptimal. An alternative is to output a leaf decision at such a node and allow future updates to create a split (transitioning from a leaf to an internal node). This behavior has a direct analogy to pruning/growing strategies and should be precisely defined, as it affects both interpretability and optimization stability.

- Practical motivation and interpretability. If the goal is to improve accuracy irrespective of interpretability, there are strong alternatives (e.g., boosted ensembles, neural networks) that often outperform oblique trees. If interpretability is central, L2-regularized oblique splits will typically be dense and thus hard to interpret. The paper should:
  - Evaluate L1 (or mixed L1/L2, group sparsity) regularization to encourage single- or few-feature splits.
  - Report sparsity metrics (e.g., number of nonzeros per node/path) and the accuracy–sparsity trade-off.
  - Discuss whether the learned trees remain meaningfully interpretable and under what regularization.

Experimental evaluation
- Baselines and pruning details. The greedy baseline is insufficiently specified. Please detail the pruning strategies, selection criteria, and hyperparameter ranges. Given that multiple regularization parameters are explored for the proposed method, the greedy method should be tuned comparably across:
  - Cost-complexity pruning, minimal cost-complexity with cross-validation, reduced error pruning, and early-stopping heuristics.
  - Splitting criteria and oblique vs. axis-aligned variants, if applicable.

- Additional baselines. Consider:
  - Simple “global selection” heuristics, such as training multiple greedy trees on random data subsets or feature subsets and selecting by validation loss aligned with your objective.
  - Stronger non-greedy or oblique baselines known in the literature.
  - Standard non-tree models (e.g., random forests, gradient boosting) to contextualize accuracy–interpretability trade-offs.

- Reporting. Include training time, convergence behavior, depth/size of learned trees, and how these vary with regularization. An ablation on the tightness of the surrogate bound and its effect on final accuracy would help justify the chosen objective.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear, but inference sections could be streamlined:
  - Section 6.2 can be summarized as: for each leaf, maximize over g restricted to that leaf, then maximize the resulting value across leaves.
  - Section 6.4 would benefit from an explicit step-by-step description of the search space, constraints, and how the maximization is decomposed.
- Explicitly specify initialization (random vs. zero), behavior at zero weights, and any tie-breaking rules during inference.

Scope
- The title mentions forests, but the paper does not develop or evaluate the forest case. Either narrow the title or add a brief extension showing how the method scales to ensembles and whether the same inference/optimization efficiencies hold.

Post-rebuttal comments
I remain convinced that the zero-initialization case is important. While random initialization may avoid the issue empirically, starting from zero and growing the tree via localized updates seems the most compelling aspect of this framework and could yield principled complexity control and closer ties to pruning/growing strategies. I encourage the authors to analyze and experiment with zero-initialization, formalize the transition from leaf to internal node under regularization, and relate the resulting procedure to known greedy methods.

Overall assessment
A clever and promising reformulation with efficient algorithms and intriguing properties. However, the practical motivation (particularly regarding interpretability) and the experimental evaluation (baseline breadth, pruning details, and reporting) need strengthening. Addressing the zero-weight behavior, adding interpretability-focused regularization and metrics, and broadening/tuning baselines would substantially improve the paper.
