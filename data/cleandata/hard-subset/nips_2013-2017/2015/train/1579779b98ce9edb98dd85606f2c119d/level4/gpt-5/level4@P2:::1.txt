After reading the rebuttal and discussing with the other reviewers, I continue to view this as a strong paper with an elegant and well-motivated approach. While the empirical gains are modest, the formulation is principled and likely to catalyze further work. My score reflects this balance.

Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy method for training oblique decision trees by jointly optimizing all split functions and leaf parameters under a global objective. It reformulates split decisions as latent variables, links the problem to structured prediction with latent variables, and derives a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss that can be optimized with SGD under per-node norm constraints. A key technical contribution is an efficient loss-augmented inference strategy: replacing a full exponential search over latent codes with a Hamming-ball (radius 1) approximation reduces the per-update complexity to O(d^2 p), making deep trees feasible. The authors also introduce a “Stable SGD” variant to mitigate inactive leaves during training. Experiments on several multiclass benchmarks show that single non-greedy trees typically outperform greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) and the CO2 variant, with lower overfitting across depths.

This work connects classical tree learning (ID3/CART), oblique splits and margin methods (e.g., SVM-at-node), and soft hierarchical mixtures (HME) to latent structural SVMs. Unlike HME, the model preserves hard splits and O(dp) inference; unlike global MILP-style optimizers, it scales; and unlike purely greedy oblique trees, it optimizes a global surrogate with explicit regularization.

Quality
The theoretical development is sound and clearly presented. The latent-variable view, the bound, and the norm-constraint regularization are well justified; proofs are deferred to the supplement but the arguments are plausible. The complexity analysis for the fast loss-augmented inference is helpful and aligns with the empirical timing plots.

The main weakness is empirical: improvements over strong greedy baselines are often modest, and the evaluation is limited to single trees. The paper would be stronger with (i) results for ensembles (forests, boosted trees) using the proposed non-greedy learner as a base, (ii) comparisons to stronger oblique baselines (e.g., logistic-regression or max-margin splits with regularization), and (iii) variance across multiple random splits. Sensitivity to initialization (axis-aligned + CO2), the regularization parameter ν, and the impact of SSGD versus vanilla SGD would benefit from more thorough ablations. The claim that the method overfits less is plausible due to the row-norm constraints, but a more explicit analysis of generalization (training vs validation curves, active leaf counts) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is very clearly written and well organized. The link to latent structured prediction is explained cleanly, and the algorithmic details (SGD, projections, fast inference) are easy to follow. Minor suggestions:
- Provide a brief proof sketch of the key bound and Proposition 1 in the main text.
- Clarify how initialization influences convergence and whether multiple restarts are needed.
- Report standard deviations over multiple runs and ensure baselines are equally tuned.
- Include wall-clock times and memory usage for all methods, not just scaling plots.

Originality
The latent-structure formulation of decision-tree training and the Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference for tractable optimization are novel and insightful. While related to CO2 and margin-based oblique trees, the global surrogate with per-row constraints and the efficient bound-tightened inference constitute a meaningful advance.

Significance
As a framework paper, the contribution is important: it opens a clear optimization-based path to non-greedy tree learning that preserves hard-split efficiency. Even with modest single-tree gains, the approach is likely to influence subsequent work (e.g., sparse split learning, kernelized splits, integration into forests/boosting). Demonstrating stronger empirical wins, especially in ensembles, would increase its practical impact.

Pros
- Elegant and principled formulation linking trees to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient training via fast loss-augmented inference and simple SGD with projections.
- Maintains O(dp) inference; scalable to deep trees.
- Clear exposition and solid theoretical grounding.
- Consistent, if modest, accuracy gains and improved generalization across depths.

Cons
- Empirical improvements are smaller than one might hope; evaluation limited to single trees.
- Missing comparisons to the strongest oblique/ensemble baselines and lack of variance reporting.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to ν, initialization, and SSGD’s contribution.
- Theoretical results are mostly in the supplement; main text could include more intuition.

Suggestions for improvement
- After global optimization, perform a simple post-hoc refinement: reoptimize the last split and its leaf parameters greedily (e.g., by cross-entropy or impurity) to capture residual local structure at the bottom of the tree.
- Provide a more systematic overfitting analysis: training/validation curves across ν, counts of active leaves, and pruning effects induced by the norm constraint.
- Compare against strong oblique baselines (regularized logistic/SVM splits) and ensemble methods (random forests, gradient boosting) at matched complexity; report wall-clock and memory.
- Add ablations for Hamming-ball radius, SSGD vs SGD, and initialization strategies; report multiple seeds with mean±std.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite somewhat disappointing experimental gains, the approach is clear, technically sound, and likely to spur future research. With stronger empirical validation (especially in ensembles), this could be a notable contribution.
