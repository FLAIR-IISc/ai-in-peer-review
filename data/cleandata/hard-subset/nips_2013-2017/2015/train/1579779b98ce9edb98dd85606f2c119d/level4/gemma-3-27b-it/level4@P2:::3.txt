This paper addresses the well-known limitations of greedy algorithms in decision tree induction, which often lead to suboptimal trees. The authors propose a novel non-greedy approach, framing decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. They establish a connection to structured prediction and formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss, enabling efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core contribution lies in reducing the computational complexity of gradient computation to O(d), where d is the tree depth, making the training of deep trees feasible. Experiments on several benchmark classification datasets demonstrate that their non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines, though the improvements are modest.

This work builds upon decades of research in decision trees [5, 21, 4, 10, 7], acknowledging the enduring popularity of greedy methods like ID3 [21] and CART [5] despite their known limitations.  Recent attempts to improve upon greedy algorithms have focused on new objective functions [20, 12], but the fundamental greedy approach remains prevalent in applications like real-time pose estimation [22].  The authors’ contribution directly addresses this limitation by offering a global optimization framework.  The paper also draws connections to structured prediction [25, 23, 24], leveraging techniques from that field to formulate a tractable optimization problem. Related work includes attempts at global tree optimization [1, 11], online training of forests [15], and the use of soft splits [13], each with its own limitations in terms of scalability or computational cost. The authors appropriately position their work relative to these existing approaches, highlighting the advantages of their method in terms of both efficiency and performance.  

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Formulation:** The connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is a clever and insightful contribution.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** Reducing the gradient computation complexity to O(d) is a significant achievement, enabling the training of deeper, potentially more accurate trees.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms greedy baselines on multiple datasets, indicating the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the technical details in a clear and concise manner.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of related work, demonstrating an understanding of the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Modest Improvements:** While the method outperforms baselines, the improvements in accuracy are relatively small.  The paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of *why* the non-greedy approach yields improvements, and under what conditions those improvements are most pronounced.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The results seem sensitive to the regularization parameter ν, requiring careful tuning.  A discussion of the robustness of the method to different hyperparameter settings would be valuable.
*   **Initialization Dependence:** The use of a greedy tree for initialization might influence the results. Exploring different initialization strategies could be beneficial.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on classification. Exploring the performance of the method on regression tasks or other types of data could broaden its appeal.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** While the authors provide a practical algorithm and empirical results, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the SGD optimization would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a long-standing problem in machine learning.
*   The reduction in computational complexity makes the method practical for training deep decision trees.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, even if the improvements are modest.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The improvements in accuracy are relatively small, raising questions about the practical significance of the method.
*   The method appears sensitive to hyperparameter settings, requiring careful tuning.
*   The theoretical analysis is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. While the improvements in accuracy are not dramatic, the novel formulation, computational efficiency, and empirical validation make this work worthy of acceptance. The authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the existing literature and have proposed a technically sound and well-motivated method. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically providing more insight into the reasons for the observed improvements and exploring the robustness of the method to different hyperparameter settings.
