This paper presents an algorithm for learning a decision tree with linear split functions at the nodes. The algorithm optimizes the tree parameters (i.e. the weights in the split functions and the distribution of the output variable at each leaf) in a global way, as opposed to the standard greedy way of learning trees. An upper bound on the tree's empirical loss is proposed, and to achieve a smoother optimization problem, this upper bound is regularized, via a L2 regularization of the split function weights. The algorithm then consists in using a stochastic gradient descent in order to identify the tree parameters that minimize the upper bound.

The tree optimization problem is elegantly formulated, by establishing a link with structured prediction with latent variables. The paper is well structured, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and the proposed solution. The empirical results show that the globally optimized tree outperforms the greedy tree on several datasets, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the results.

However, some points need to be clarified:

- The stable version of SGD is not described very clearly. How are data points assigned to leaves? It would probably help to have a pseudo-code.
- Figure 2: are these results obtained when applying SGD or stable SGD? What does an "active" leaf mean exactly?
- In line 10 of Algorithm 1, \Theta is updated and then projected on a simplex. In practice, how do you solve this projection problem? It would also maybe be worth mentioning that this projection ensures that the each line of \Theta sums up to one.

Arguments for acceptance:

- The paper presents a novel algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions.
- The algorithm is based on a sound theoretical framework, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and the proposed solution.
- The empirical results show that the globally optimized tree outperforms the greedy tree on several datasets.

Arguments against acceptance:

- The results are not uniformly better across all datasets, and the authors do not provide a clear explanation for the variability in the results.
- The algorithm is more computationally intensive than the standard greedy algorithm, which may be a limitation for large datasets.
- Some points in the paper need to be clarified, such as the stable version of SGD and the projection problem in Algorithm 1.
