

The authors present a novel framework for non-greedy optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. This approach establishes a connection between tree induction and latent structured prediction [25], deriving a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient global optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from exponential (O(2^dp)) to O(d²p) through a modified surrogate objective, making deep tree training feasible. Experiments on LibSVM benchmarks demonstrate consistent test accuracy improvements (1-2% across datasets) over greedy baselines (including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and random oblique trees) without significant overfitting, as evidenced by superior generalization curves in Figure 2. The method also outperforms CO2 [18], a greedy variant of their upper bound approach, validating the non-greedy optimization benefit.

Strengths:  
1. **Quality**: The convex-concave upper bound (Equation 7) is rigorously derived and proven to tighten with increased W norm (Proposition 1). The O(d²p) complexity reduction is technically sound and addresses a well-known limitation in global tree optimization (NP-completeness [11]).  
2. **Originality**: The latent variable formulation bridges decision trees and structured prediction, differing significantly from prior non-greedy work (e.g., Bennett [1]’s exponential method or soft splits [13]). It handles arbitrary loss functions while preserving the tree’s O(dp) inference efficiency.  
3. **Significance**: Enables training deep trees for large-scale applications (e.g., Microsoft Kinect [22]), with practical gains in generalization over standard greedy methods. The regularization parameter ν provides a principled way to control overfitting (Figure 3), addressing a key weakness in prior approaches [3].  
4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized, with clear problem formulation (Section 3), algorithmic details (Algorithm 1), and thorough experimental validation. The loss-augmented inference explanation is particularly accessible.  

Weaknesses:  
1. **Modest absolute gains**: While consistent, the test accuracy improvements (e.g., +1.2% on Protein at depth 18) are relatively small compared to state-of-the-art forest methods [4, 15], though the paper correctly focuses on single-tree performance.  
2. **Limited ablation**: The role of ν in pruning (Figure 3) is noted but not deeply analyzed—e.g., how ν interacts with tree depth or dataset size.  
3. **Scope**: The method targets binary trees; extension to N-ary trees is mentioned but not explored, limiting applicability to certain structured prediction tasks.  

Pro: Novel complexity reduction enabling deep non-greedy trees; strong theoretical foundation with practical validation; clear generalization gains over greedy baselines.  
Con: Marginal absolute performance gains; insufficient exploration of regularization dynamics.  

This work advances the state of the art in decision tree learning by resolving a fundamental computational bottleneck while maintaining theoretical rigor. The contributions are significant for practitioners needing scalable, high-accuracy tree models, and align well with NIPS’ focus on principled ML methods. The paper meets all quality criteria and is suitable for acceptance.
