Summary: The paper introduces a method to jointly optimize all parameters of a decision tree (both split functions and leaf posterior distributions). This is important because most existing approaches train trees greedily, node by node. The authors formulate the task as a single global optimization problem and then upper-bound and approximate it to obtain a tractable surrogate objective.

Quality – The manuscript is clearly written, but there may be issues with parts of the reasoning; see below. Clarity – The derivation is tidy, though the experiments and their presentation could be strengthened. Originality – Joint training of decision tree parameters is a valuable direction. Significance – The proposed approach is useful, albeit with some potential shortcomings noted below.

Comments:
- Possible issue in the derivation of the empirical loss bound. At minimum, the statement in l.216f is confusing: “... for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$ ...”. Consider the following example to illustrate the concern: let $W\bx = [5; -5]$, hence $\hat \bh = [1; -1] = \sgn(W\bx)$ and $\hat \bh^\top W\bx = 10$. Choosing $\bg = [1; 1]$ yields $\bg^\top W\bx = 0$. Thus the RHS of Eq. (7) becomes
$\bg^\top W\bx - \hat \bh^\top W\bx + \ell(\cdot) = -10 + \ell(\cdot)$,
which could be smaller than $\ell(\cdot)$ and therefore not an upper bound. Also, the proof of Prop. 1 in the supplement does not address this situation and appears incomplete.
- Why do some leaves receive no data assignments? This is surprising and may point to an issue with the objective or initialization. The proposed heuristic of fixing data-to-leaf assignments for several updates seems suboptimal; perhaps it could be motivated via an EM/CCCP-style argument, akin to related clustering methods?
- On the necessity of efficient loss-augmented inference: with tree depth at most 16, a brute-force check over $2^{16} = 65{,}536$ configurations seems feasible. How much does constraining the search to a Hamming ball affect performance? One result is provided in the supplement—does this generalize? Also, what exactly is meant by “leaves” in that context?
- A baseline is missing that trains oblique (non-axis-aligned) splits greedily in the standard way, i.e., without any special initialization.
- The tree depth must be specified a priori. This limitation deserves a more careful discussion, and the experiments should more thoroughly analyze sensitivity to the depth parameter.

--------------- Thanks to the authors for clearly clarifying the logic behind the loss bound (Eq. 7). You might consider replacing $\arg\max$ with $\max$. As noted in my summary, I have adjusted my score.

Including a runtime comparison experiment would strengthen the paper. A more thorough empirical evaluation would also benefit readers. Overall, the submission targets an interesting problem—jointly training all parameters of decision trees. The current experimental study is somewhat limited, and there might be an issue in the derivation. I am happy to further adjust my score given a careful explanation in the rebuttal.
