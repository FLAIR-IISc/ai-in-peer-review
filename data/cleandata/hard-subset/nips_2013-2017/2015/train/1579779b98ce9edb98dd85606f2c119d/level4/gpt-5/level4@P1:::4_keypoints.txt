- Proposal: Non-greedy algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees by casting induction as latent structured prediction with internal decisions h = sgn(Wx).  
- Technical contribution: Derivation of a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss enabling SGD with loss-augmented inference.  
- Algorithmic speedup: Fast loss-augmented inference reduces per-update complexity from exponential in depth to O(d^2 p) by exploiting tree structure.  
- Joint optimization: Method jointly optimizes oblique splits and leaf parameters under per-row norm constraints on W.  
- Test-time behavior: Retains hard splits with O(d p) inference at test time, unlike soft-split HME models.  
- Relation to prior work: Connects to latent structured prediction and max-margin split learning; improves on CO2 by non-greedy joint optimization.  
- Strength — novelty: Elegant and novel reformulation of tree learning with a principled surrogate objective and efficient inference.  
- Strength — sparsity of updates: Sparse gradient updates often affect only a small subset of nodes, suggesting computational advantages and interesting model-growth behavior.  
- Strength — regularization: Row-norm constraints provide clear regularization that controls bound tightness and implicitly prunes leaves, improving generalization.  
- Empirical results: Consistent improvements over several greedy baselines and a CO2 comparison that isolates non-greedy benefits.  
- Weakness — interpretability: Oblique splits with L2 regularization are likely dense and non-interpretable; sparse (L1/group) variants are not evaluated.  
- Weakness — baselines/pruning: Missing details on pruning for greedy baselines and lack of comparisons to multiple pruning strategies or simple global-selection heuristics.  
- Conceptual question: Sparse-update behavior raises unexamined possibilities of growing trees from zero weights and whether this diverges from or mirrors greedy strategies.  
- Edge-case ambiguity: Behavior when node weights are exactly zero (or wi^T x − bi = 0) is unclear; current convention to take a branch is unsatisfying and pruning-like prediction at nodes may be preferable.  
- Clarity issues: Some inference sections (e.g., Sec. 6.2, 6.4) need clearer exposition about maximization steps and bit differences limiting updates.  
- Scope concern: Paper and abstract mention forests, but experiments focus on single trees and forests are not empirically addressed.  
- Quality assessment: Technical development is sound and well-motivated, but practical edge cases and broader baseline comparisons need attention.  
- Originality: High — the latent structured prediction viewpoint and tailored bound/inference are novel.  
- Significance: Promising but limited by interpretability concerns and narrow experimental scope; sparse regularization and more baselines would strengthen practical impact.  
- Pros summary: Elegant reformulation, efficient optimization, sparse updates, principled regularization, and empirical gains.  
- Cons summary: Potential loss of interpretability, incomplete baseline/pruning comparisons, zero-weight ambiguity, unexplored model-growth behavior, and no forest experiments.  
- Post-rebuttal note: Zero-weight initialization and gradual activation is an important direction; random initialization avoids the issue in practice but explicit treatment is recommended.
