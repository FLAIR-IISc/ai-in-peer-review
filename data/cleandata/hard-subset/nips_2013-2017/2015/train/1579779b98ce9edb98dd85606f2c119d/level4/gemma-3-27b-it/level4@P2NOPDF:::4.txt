This submission presents a novel algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees by reformulating the problem as (latent) structured prediction. The core idea is to represent the tree’s decisions as sign vectors, enabling the use of structured prediction techniques. A differentiable upper bound is derived for a non-differentiable and non-convex loss function, and efficient algorithms are proposed for minimizing this bound via Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The work is evaluated by comparing it to a standard greedy training method.

The key contributions lie in recasting decision tree global optimization as a structured prediction problem and developing efficient optimization algorithms. This approach is innovative and raises several interesting theoretical questions. However, I have some concerns regarding the practical motivation and the magnitude of performance gains compared to simpler alternatives.

While the structured prediction formulation offers advantages – particularly enabling SGD via loss-augmented inference leveraging the problem’s structure – the most intriguing aspect is the sparsity of the gradient updates. If I understand correctly, each update modifies only a single node’s weight vector. The paper frames this as a computational benefit, but I believe it unlocks more significant possibilities currently unexplored. Specifically, one could envision an infinitely deep tree initialized with zero weights, gradually grown by increasing weights, allowing model complexity to adapt to the data. This prompts questions about its relation to existing greedy strategies: would it converge to an equivalent solution, or represent a novel variation? Or, with appropriate regularization, might it converge to a tree of limited depth, as suggested by Figure 2? Addressing these questions is crucial to establishing the value proposition of this method over traditional decision trees.

A critical ambiguity relates to the handling of zero-weighted nodes. The current discussion implies traversing the right branch, which seems suboptimal. Outputting a decision at such a node, with the option to later increase its weight and induce a split, appears more logical and connects to pruning strategies in greedy training. A precise definition of this behavior is essential.

My primary practical concern centers on the motivation for this work. Plain decision trees are valuable primarily for their interpretability; when interpretability is not required, more powerful (though less transparent) methods typically outperform them. Given the use of L2 regularization, I anticipate the proposed method to produce dense, non-interpretable weight vectors. While L1 regularization might mitigate this, performance could suffer if sparsity is enforced too strongly. This trade-off requires thorough investigation. 

The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail concerning baseline methods. No information is provided about the pruning strategies used for the greedy approach. A fair comparison would involve exploring various pruning strategies and parameters for the baseline method, mirroring the parameter tuning performed for the proposed approach. Additionally, simple global optimization strategies, such as training a greedy method on random data subsets and selecting the best performer, should be considered. 

The paper’s clarity is generally good, though the inference description could be improved. For example, Section 6.2 could be succinctly summarized as maximizing the objective first for all *g* corresponding to a given leaf, then maximizing over all leaves. Section 6.4 could also benefit from greater explicitness.

In summary, this is a clever and thought-provoking approach. However, its practical utility remains unclear, due to both the interpretability concerns – why utilize decision trees if interpretability is sacrificed? – and the lack of comprehensive experimental results.

PS: Despite the title’s mention of forests, the paper does not address this case.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS:

I still believe the case of zero weights warrants further investigation, for the reasons outlined in my review. The rebuttal suggests random initialization avoids this issue in practice. However, I suspect the most compelling application of this method would arise from initializing weights at zero and gradually increasing them. I encourage the authors to explore this direction. The submission offers a clever reformulation of global decision tree optimization as structured prediction, accompanied by an efficient optimization algorithm. While insightful, aspects of the motivation and experimental validation remain unconvincing.
