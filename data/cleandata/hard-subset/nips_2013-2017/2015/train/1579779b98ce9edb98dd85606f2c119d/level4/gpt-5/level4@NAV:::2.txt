Review- Summary: The paper proposes a method to jointly learn all parameters of decision trees (both split functions and leaf posterior probabilities). This is a timely problem, as most existing approaches train trees greedily, level by level. The task is cast as a single optimization, which is then upper-bounded and approximated to obtain a practical surrogate.

Quality - The paper reads well, but there may be issues in the reasoning. See details below. Clarity - The derivations are neat, but the experiments and their presentation could be strengthened. Originality - Studying joint training for decision trees is an important direction. Significance - The method appears valuable, though there are apparent limitations. See below.

Comments: - Possible flaw in the empirical loss bound derivation? At minimum, the argument around l.216f is confusing: `... for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$ ...' Consider the following example that motivates my confusion:
W\bx = [5;-5]
--> \hat \bh = [1;-1] = \sgn(W\bx)
--> \hat \bh^T W\bx = 10
Picking g = [1;1] gives g^T W\bx = 0. Hence the right-hand side of Eq. (7) becomes
g^T W\bx - \hat \bh^T W\bx +
\ell(...) = -10 + \ell(...)
which could be smaller than \ell(...), and thus not a valid upper bound. Also, the proof of Prop. 1 in the supplement does not address this case and appears incomplete.
- Why do some leaves end up with no assigned data? This is unusual and may point to an issue with the objective or the initialization. The proposed heuristic of fixing leaf assignments seems suboptimal, though it might be justifiable via an EM/concave-convex procedure analogy, as in clustering.
- I am uncertain about the practical importance of the efficient loss-augmented inference design. With tree depth at most 16, exhaustively checking 2^16 = 65536 values seems tractable. How much does restricting the search to a Hamming ball affect performance? There is one result in the supplement, but does it generalize? Also, what exactly does “leaves” mean in that context?
- A baseline that greedily trains oblique (non-axis-aligned) splits without any initialization is missing.
- The tree depth must be fixed in advance. This constraint should be discussed more carefully, and a more thorough experimental study of the depth parameter would be helpful.
--------------- Many thanks to the authors for clarifying the reasoning behind the loss bound (Eq. 7). You may want to replace \arg\max with \max. As noted in my summary, I have updated my score.
A runtime comparison would further strengthen the paper. A more thorough empirical evaluation would benefit readers. The submission tackles an interesting problem—jointly training all parameters of decision trees. However, the experimental assessment is somewhat limited, and there may be an issue in the derivation. I am willing to adjust my score given a careful rebuttal.
