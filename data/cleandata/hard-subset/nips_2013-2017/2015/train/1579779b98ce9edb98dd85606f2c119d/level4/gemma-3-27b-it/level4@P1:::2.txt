Summary: This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters (decision splits and posterior probabilities) of decision trees. This is an important topic since current methods typically train trees in a greedy, layer-by-layer fashion. The authors formulate the problem as a single optimization problem, then upper-bound and approximate it to obtain a tractable formulation.

Quality – The paper is technically sound and well-written, although there are potential issues with the reasoning behind the loss bound, as detailed below. Clarity – The derivation is generally clean, but the presentation of experiments and their results could be improved. Originality – Investigating joint training for decision trees is a valuable and novel direction. Significance – The proposed solution is significant and offers a promising improvement over greedy approaches, although some shortcomings remain, as discussed below.

Comments:

- There appears to be a potential flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound. Specifically, the argument in lines 216-218 is unclear. Consider the following example to illustrate the potential issue: Wx = [5; -5] → ̂h = [1; -1] = sgn(Wx) → ̂hTWx = 10. Choosing g = [1; 1] results in gTWx = 0. Therefore, the right-hand side of Eq. (7) becomes gTWx - ̂hTWx + ℓ(...) = -10 + ℓ(...), which could potentially be *smaller* than ℓ(...) and therefore not a valid upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not appear to address this case and is therefore incomplete.

- Why are some leaves not assigned any data points during training? This is unexpected and could indicate a problem with the objective function or the initialization procedure. The heuristic of fixing data point assignments to tree leaves seems sub-optimal, but could potentially be motivated by an expectation-maximization or concave-convex procedure, similar to techniques used in clustering.

- The efficiency gains from the proposed fast loss-augmented inference are notable. However, given that the maximum tree depth used in the experiments is 16, and a greedy check of 2^16 = 65536 values seems feasible, I wonder how much performance was impacted by restricting the search space to the Hamming ball. The supplementary material provides one result on this, but it’s unclear whether this generalizes. Additionally, the meaning of “leaves” in the context of this experiment needs clarification.

- A crucial baseline is missing: a comparison with training non-axis-aligned split functions in a standard, greedy manner (i.e., without any initialization from an axis-aligned tree).

- The tree depth is fixed *a priori*. This is a significant limitation that should be discussed more thoroughly. The authors should provide a more careful experimental evaluation concerning the effect of the depth parameter on performance.
