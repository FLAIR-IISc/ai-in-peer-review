

This paper proposes a global optimization approach for decision trees with linear split functions, contrasting with standard greedy methods. The algorithm jointly optimizes split weights and leaf output distributions by minimizing an upper bound on empirical loss, regularized via L2 penalties on split weights. Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) is employed to identify optimal tree parameters. While the formulation elegantly connects to structured prediction with latent variables, the empirical results fail to convincingly demonstrate advantages over greedy learning. The globally optimized tree significantly outperforms greedy trees on only a few datasets, at the cost of substantially higher computational complexity.

The paper is well-structured but requires clarification on several technical points:

1.  **Stable SGD Mechanism:** The assignment of data points to leaves during stable SGD is unclear. A pseudocode description would greatly improve reproducibility.
2.  **Figure 2 Interpretation:** It is ambiguous whether the results in Figure 2 stem from standard SGD or stable SGD. Additionally, the precise meaning of an "active" leaf requires definition.
3.  **Simplex Projection:** Algorithm 1 projects Θ onto a simplex after updates (line 10). The practical implementation of this projection (e.g., using efficient algorithms for simplex constraints) needs specification. It should also be explicitly noted that this projection ensures each row of Θ sums to one.

The core contribution – a global optimization framework for linear-split trees – is theoretically sound and well-motivated. However, the empirical evaluation is insufficient to justify the increased computational cost. The claim that global optimization is "better" lacks strong evidence, as significant performance gains are observed only sporadically. The paper would benefit from:
*   A more comprehensive comparison (e.g., on more datasets, including larger ones).
*   A clearer analysis of the computational trade-off (e.g., wall-clock time vs. accuracy gains).
*   Explicit discussion of why global optimization succeeds on some datasets but not others.

The promising formulation is undermined by unconvincing empirical results. While the method is technically interesting, the lack of robust performance gains relative to the computational burden weakens the paper's main claim. Addressing the clarity issues and providing stronger empirical evidence would significantly strengthen the submission.
