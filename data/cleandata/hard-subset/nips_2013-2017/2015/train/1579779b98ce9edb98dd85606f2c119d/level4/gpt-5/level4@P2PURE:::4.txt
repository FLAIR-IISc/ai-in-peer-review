The submission proposes an algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees by reformulating the problem as (latent) structured prediction, where the latent structured variables are sign vectors that encode the decisions at each internal node. It introduces a non-differentiable, non-convex loss that is upper-bounded by a differentiable (though still non-convex) surrogate. Minimizing this objective via SGD requires solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided. Experiments compare the approach against a standard greedy training procedure. The central contributions are the structured-prediction formulation of global decision-tree optimization and the development of efficient algorithms to optimize the resulting objective. The approach is novel and interesting, and it raises several thought-provoking questions.

I have concerns about practical aspects—especially the motivation for the method and the magnitude of its performance gains over naive alternatives. Considered purely as a method for globally optimizing decision trees, the approach is intriguing. One benefit of casting the problem as structured prediction is the ability to perform SGD by solving structured loss-augmented subproblems that exploit the problem’s special structure. More striking, however, is that the gradient updates are sparse: if I understand correctly, each SGD step modifies only a single node’s weight vector. While the paper presents this primarily as a computational advantage, I believe it opens up more interesting possibilities that are not acknowledged.

In particular, one could imagine optimizing an infinitely deep tree by initializing all weights to zero and gradually growing the tree as more weights become nonzero, allowing the model’s complexity to increase naturally with the data. This raises several questions. Would such a procedure be equivalent to (or a variant of) known greedy strategies if it is always preferable to introduce a new node rather than revise an existing node’s weights? Alternatively, with appropriate regularization, would the method converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes (as Fig. 2 seems to suggest)? This issue strikes at the core of what makes the proposed method compelling relative to naive decision trees and merits more analysis than the paper currently provides.

A critical ambiguity concerns the case where a node’s weight vector is exactly zero. The discussion on page 3 suggests taking the right branch, which seems dubious. A more appealing behavior might be to output a decision at that node and, later, make the weight nonzero if a split becomes advantageous—directly paralleling pruning strategies in greedy training. Precisely specifying this behavior matters because of its tight connection to pruning and model selection.

I also have a significant practical concern about the motivation. Plain decision trees are primarily valuable for their interpretability; if interpretability is not a priority, other methods typically deliver better performance. As currently posed, the method is likely to yield non-interpretable trees because L2 regularization tends to produce dense weight vectors. Switching to L1 regularization could help, but performance might degrade if one must regularize strongly enough to obtain strictly 1-sparse splits. This trade-off should be tested empirically.

On the experimental side, the baselines are under-specified. The paper does not detail which pruning strategies were used for the naive approach. Since the proposed method was evaluated over a range of regularization parameters, I would expect the naive baseline to be similarly tuned across pruning strategies and parameters. It would also be fair to include trivial global strategies such as training multiple greedy trees on random subsets of the training data and selecting the one that minimizes the target loss on the full training set or a validation set.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written, but the inference sections could be clearer. For example, Section 6.2 could be summarized as first maximizing the objective over all g corresponding to a fixed leaf, and then maximizing over leaves. Section 6.4 could also be made more explicit.

In summary, this is a clever approach that raises interesting questions. However, its practical utility remains unclear, both due to the interpretability concern (why use plain decision trees if they are not interpretable?) and gaps in the experimental comparisons. PS: although the title refers to forests, the paper does not address that setting.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
I still consider the zero-weight case to be critical and in need of deeper analysis, for the reasons stated above. From the rebuttal, it seems weights are likely initialized randomly, which may explain why this case does not arise in practice. Nevertheless, I suspect the most compelling direction would be to initialize all weights at zero and grow them gradually. I encourage the authors to explore this avenue. Overall, the submission presents a clever reformulation of global decision-tree optimization as structured prediction, along with an efficient optimization algorithm, but some aspects of the motivation and the experimental evaluation remain unconvincing.
