

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree optimization by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, introducing a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. This bound enables efficient training using stochastic gradient descent with complexity O(d²p), where d is tree depth and p is feature dimension, making deep tree training computationally feasible. The method is evaluated on multiple classification benchmarks, demonstrating consistent but modest improvements over greedy decision tree baselines.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it establishes a novel theoretical framework connecting decision tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables, which is a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem. Second, the authors develop a computationally efficient algorithm with rigorous complexity analysis, addressing the key computational challenges of non-greedy tree learning. Third, the experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves better generalization performance than greedy approaches across multiple datasets, with the improvements being consistent across different tree depths.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical contribution is technically sound, with clear mathematical justification for the upper bound and its optimization. The complexity analysis is thorough and convincing, showing how the algorithm scales with tree depth. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with comparisons against multiple strong baselines (including OC1, CO2, and axis-aligned trees) across several standard datasets. The results consistently show that non-greedy trees achieve better test accuracy and are less prone to overfitting than greedy alternatives, with improvements that persist across different tree depths.

The main weakness is that the performance improvements over greedy baselines, while statistically significant, are relatively modest in magnitude across the datasets tested. The paper does not sufficiently discuss scenarios where this method would be particularly advantageous compared to other approaches (e.g., random forests or gradient boosting). The connection to previous work could be strengthened by more explicit contrast with Bennett's earlier non-greedy approaches [1] and Mondrian forests [15], particularly regarding their computational limitations and scalability.

In terms of the four review criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a well-justified theoretical framework. The experimental results support the claims, though the improvements are somewhat marginal. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the modest performance gains.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations. However, the connection between decision trees and structured prediction could be made more intuitive for readers unfamiliar with the latter. The algorithm descriptions are precise but could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the fast loss-augmented inference step.

Originality: This is a strong point of the paper. The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is novel, and the resulting formulation represents a significant departure from traditional greedy approaches. The authors have carefully reviewed related work and clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous efforts.

Significance: While the method represents an interesting theoretical advance, the practical significance is somewhat limited by the modest performance gains. The paper would benefit from a more compelling discussion of potential applications where the non-greedy approach would be particularly valuable (e.g., in resource-constrained environments where tree depth is critical, or in settings where interpretability is paramount). The authors note potential extensions (sparsity-inducing regularization, kernel methods), but these remain largely unexplored.

Pro: Novel theoretical framework connecting decision trees to structured prediction with latent variables, sound mathematical justification, comprehensive experimental evaluation, efficient algorithm with clear complexity analysis.

Con: Modest performance improvements over greedy baselines, limited discussion of practical advantages, insufficient contrast with previous non-greedy approaches.

The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of decision tree optimization. While the practical improvements are modest, the method represents a significant step toward more principled global optimization of decision trees. Given the consistent improvements over strong baselines and the clear theoretical contribution, I recommend acceptance for NIPS.
