**Summary**

This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters of a decision tree, including its decision splits and leaf posterior probabilities. This is a notable contribution, as conventional methods train trees in a greedy, layer-by-layer fashion. The authors formulate the joint training task as a single optimization problem and then derive a tractable upper-bound on the empirical loss for optimization.

**Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance**

The paper is generally well-written, and the mathematical derivations are presented clearly. The problem of jointly training decision trees is original and significant, making the proposed solution potentially valuable. However, I have some concerns regarding potential flaws in the theoretical reasoning and the breadth of the experimental evaluation, which are detailed below.

**Detailed Comments**

1.  **Potential flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound:** I have a concern regarding the validity of the upper bound in Equation (7). The argument on lines 216f, which states "... for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$...", appears to be incorrect. Consider the following counterexample:
    Let $W\bx = [5; -5]$. Then the optimal path is $\hat{\bh} = \sgn(W\bx) = [1; -1]$, and the corresponding score is $\hat{\bh}^T W\bx = 10$.
    If we choose a different path, for instance $\bg = [1; 1]$, we get a score of $\bg^T W\bx = 0$.
    In this case, the right-hand side of Eq. (7) becomes:
    $\bg^T W\bx - \hat{\bh}^T W\bx + \ell(...) = 0 - 10 + \ell(...) = \ell(...) - 10$.
    This value is smaller than $\ell(...)$, which contradicts the claim that this is an upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not seem to address this case and may therefore be incomplete.

2.  **Empty leaf nodes:** The authors report that some leaf nodes are not assigned any data points. This is an unusual outcome and might suggest an issue with the optimization objective or the initialization procedure. The paper proposes a heuristic of a fixed data-to-leaf assignment to address this. Could the authors provide more intuition for this phenomenon? This heuristic seems sub-optimal; perhaps it could be more formally motivated, for example, through a connection to Expectation-Maximization or a concave-convex procedure, similar to those used in clustering algorithms.

3.  **Necessity of efficient loss-augmented inference:** The paper motivates the design of an efficient loss-augmented inference method. However, for the reported maximum tree depth of 16, a brute-force search over all $2^{16} = 65,536$ paths seems computationally feasible. Could the authors clarify the practical importance of the proposed efficient inference? Furthermore, how much does the Hamming ball restriction on the search space impact performance? The supplementary material provides one result, but it is unclear if this finding generalizes across different datasets and settings.

4.  **Missing baseline:** The experimental evaluation would be strengthened by including a baseline that trains a tree with non-axis-aligned split functions using a standard, greedy, layer-by-layer approach (i.e., without any special initialization). Such a baseline would provide a more direct comparison to assess the benefits of the proposed joint training.

5.  **Fixed tree depth:** The proposed method requires the tree depth to be pre-specified. This is a significant limitation and warrants a more thorough discussion. The paper would also benefit from a more comprehensive experimental evaluation of the model's sensitivity to this hyperparameter.

**Conclusion**

This submission considers the interesting and important problem of jointly training all parameters of a decision tree. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and there is a potential flaw in a key theoretical derivation. A more careful evaluation, including runtime comparisons and additional baselines, would be desirable. I am happy to adjust my score given a careful explanation of the derivation concern in the rebuttal.
