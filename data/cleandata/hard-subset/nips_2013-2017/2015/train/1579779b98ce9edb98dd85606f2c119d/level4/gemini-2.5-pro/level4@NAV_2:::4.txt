This paper presents a novel algorithm for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The core idea is to reframe the global training of tree parameters—both the oblique split functions at internal nodes and the predictors at the leaves—as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. These latent variables correspond to the binary decisions made at each split node. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss to serve as a tractable surrogate objective. This objective is then minimized using stochastic gradient descent, for which the paper provides an efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm. Experiments on several benchmark datasets show that the resulting non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines.

The primary contribution of this work is the elegant formulation of decision tree optimization within the latent structured prediction framework. This is a novel and insightful connection that enables the use of a well-understood optimization paradigm for a notoriously difficult problem. The resulting optimization algorithm, which leverages the problem's structure through loss-augmented inference, is a key strength. An especially interesting consequence of this approach is the sparse nature of the gradient updates; as I understand it, each SGD step modifies the weight vectors of only a small subset of nodes. While presented mainly as a computational benefit, this property suggests intriguing possibilities that are not fully explored. For instance, one could envision an algorithm that grows the tree by initializing all weights to zero and allowing the optimization to selectively make them non-zero, thereby letting the model complexity adapt to the data. This raises interesting questions: would such a procedure be equivalent to a known greedy strategy, or could it, with proper regularization, converge to a finite tree? A deeper analysis of these implications would significantly enhance the paper's contribution.

However, I have some concerns regarding practical considerations and the experimental evaluation.

A critical technical detail remains ambiguous: the handling of split nodes where the weight vector is zero. The paper seems to imply a default decision (e.g., taking the right branch), which seems arbitrary. A more principled definition for this case is needed, as it is directly analogous to pruning strategies in greedy training and is central to the idea of growing a tree from a zero-initialized state.

Furthermore, a key motivation for using single decision trees is their interpretability. The proposed method, with its L2 regularization, is likely to produce dense, non-interpretable weight vectors for the oblique splits. If interpretability is sacrificed, it is unclear what the practical advantage of this method is over other high-performance but non-interpretable models like random forests or gradient boosted trees. The paper would be stronger if it addressed this trade-off. While switching to an L1 regularizer might induce sparsity and improve interpretability, the potential impact on performance would require empirical validation.

The experimental evaluation could also be more robust. The descriptions of the baseline methods lack important details, such as which, if any, pruning strategies were used. For a fair comparison, the baselines should be tuned as thoroughly as the proposed method (e.g., by exploring different pruning parameters). It would also be beneficial to compare against other simple global optimization strategies, for instance, selecting the best tree from multiple runs of a greedy algorithm on random subsets of the data.

Regarding clarity, the paper is reasonably well-written. However, the descriptions of the inference procedures could be more explicit. For example, the core idea of the loss-augmented inference in Section 6 could be stated more directly: for each leaf, find the optimal decision path `g`, and then maximize the resulting objective over all leaves.

In summary, this paper introduces a clever and theoretically appealing approach to global decision tree optimization. The connection to structured prediction is a significant contribution that opens up promising research avenues. At present, however, the practical utility of the method is not fully established, due to the unaddressed issue of interpretability and an experimental comparison that could be more rigorous. Finally, the title's mention of "forests" is somewhat misleading, as the method and experiments focus exclusively on single trees.
