

This paper presents a compelling nonconvex relaxation approach for global optimization of decision trees with fixed structure, offering significant theoretical elegance and a promising foundation for future work. The core contribution—transforming a combinatorial tree optimization problem into a continuous relaxation amenable to efficient gradient-based methods—is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The method avoids the local optima pitfalls of standard greedy tree induction, and the exposition is exceptionally clear, making the technical contributions accessible despite the inherent complexity. Given the paper’s novelty and potential to catalyze research in structured optimization for interpretable models, it merits publication on these grounds alone.  

That said, the empirical evaluation presents a notable limitation. The results on standard datasets (e.g., UCI benchmarks) show only marginal improvements over baseline tree learners (e.g., CART, Random Forests), with some tasks exhibiting statistically insignificant gains. This underwhelming performance weakens the practical impact of the method, particularly when compared to recent advances in tree-based learning. For instance, the paper reports a 1–2% accuracy improvement on average across datasets, yet fails to demonstrate consistent superiority or scalability to larger problems. Crucially, the authors do not adequately address whether the global optimization inherently increases overfitting risk—a critical concern for tree-based models. The absence of cross-validation or regularization analysis on the optimized trees leaves this question unresolved, which is a significant oversight for a paper claiming to optimize globally.  

A minor but potentially impactful adjustment could substantially improve results: after applying the global optimization, omit the final split in each tree and re-optimize this split and its associated leaf node using conventional methods (e.g., CART’s recursive partitioning). This hybrid approach would retain the global optimization’s structural benefits while allowing local refinement at the terminal nodes, where greedy methods often excel. The authors should test this modification, as it might bridge the gap between theoretical promise and empirical performance.  

The paper would also benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between global optimization and overfitting. While the method guarantees a globally optimal tree *for the fixed structure*, the lack of explicit regularization could lead to trees that fit noise rather than signal. The authors should compare overfitting metrics (e.g., train-test error gaps) between their approach and standard methods, and explore whether adding regularization (e.g., L1 penalties on split weights) mitigates this risk without sacrificing the global optimality guarantee.  

Despite these shortcomings, the paper’s theoretical contribution is strong enough to justify publication. The elegance of the relaxation framework—its ability to transform a discrete problem into a smooth optimization task—opens avenues for future work in structured prediction and interpretable ML. The method’s conceptual simplicity and potential for integration with modern deep learning pipelines (e.g., end-to-end tree training) further underscore its value. With the experimental limitations acknowledged and the suggested refinements addressed in future work, this paper will likely serve as a foundational reference.  

In summary, the paper’s theoretical novelty and clarity outweigh its modest empirical results. I recommend acceptance, as the work’s potential to inspire new research on global optimization for structured models justifies its contribution to the field. The authors should, however, explicitly discuss overfitting risks and consider the hybrid optimization suggestion in future revisions to strengthen the empirical case.
