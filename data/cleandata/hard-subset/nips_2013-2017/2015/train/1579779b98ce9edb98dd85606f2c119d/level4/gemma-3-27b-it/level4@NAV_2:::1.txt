This paper proposes a nonconvex relaxation for global optimization of decision trees with a fixed structure. The core idea is to reformulate decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, enabling the use of techniques from that field to derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. The authors demonstrate that the gradient of this surrogate objective can be computed efficiently, allowing for the training of deep trees with stochastic gradient descent. While the experimental results are somewhat disappointing, the approach is elegant and represents a worthwhile contribution to the field.

The paper begins by clearly outlining the limitations of greedy decision tree induction methods, highlighting the lack of cooperation between split nodes at different levels of the tree. It positions the work within the broader context of decision tree and forest algorithms [5, 21, 4, 10, 7], noting the continued reliance on decades-old greedy approaches even in modern applications like Microsoft Kinect [22]. The connection drawn between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables [25] is a key contribution, allowing the authors to leverage advances in that area [23, 24]. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical soundness and technical innovation. The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound and the reduction in gradient computation complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) are significant achievements. The proposed stochastic gradient descent algorithm appears well-designed and efficient. The paper is also very clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the technical details.

However, the experimental results do not fully justify the theoretical elegance of the approach. While the non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines, the improvement is not substantial across all datasets. The authors acknowledge this and discuss potential reasons, such as the need for careful hyperparameter tuning, particularly the regularization constant ν.  It would be valuable to investigate a simple post-processing step, such as re-optimizing the final split and leaf node using standard greedy techniques, which might mitigate some overfitting and improve performance. The paper also lacks a more thorough analysis of the regularization parameter's effect on tree complexity and generalization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to global decision tree optimization.
*   Efficient gradient computation allows for training deep trees.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method.
*   Connection to structured prediction provides a new perspective on decision tree learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental results are not convincingly superior to existing greedy methods.
*   The effect of regularization on tree complexity and generalization needs further investigation.
*   Limited discussion of practical considerations and potential limitations.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. Although the experimental results are somewhat modest, the elegance of the approach and the potential for future research warrant acceptance. The authors have clearly demonstrated an understanding of the relevant literature and have developed a technically sound and innovative method. I believe that this work will stimulate further investigation into non-greedy optimization techniques for decision trees and other related models.
