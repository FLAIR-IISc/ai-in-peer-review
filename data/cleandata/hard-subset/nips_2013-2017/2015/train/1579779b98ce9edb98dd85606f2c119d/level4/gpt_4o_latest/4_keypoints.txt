- Paper reformulates global decision-tree optimization as (latent) structured prediction using sign vectors for internal-node decisions.
- Authors introduce a non-differentiable, non-convex loss and derive a differentiable (still non-convex) upper bound for optimization.
- Optimization via SGD reduces to solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, with efficient algorithms provided.
- Main contributions: the structured-prediction reformulation and efficient algorithms for the resulting objective.
- Approach is novel and thought-provoking, raising interesting theoretical and practical questions.
- Reviewer highlights a key computational advantage: gradient updates are sparse, typically modifying only one node's weight per step.
- Sparse updates suggest more ambitious uses (e.g., initializing zero weights and growing an infinite tree), but this is not explored in the paper.
- Open question whether the method would mimic or diverge from greedy strategies when growing or revising nodes.
- Ambiguity about behavior when a node's weight is zero (paper implies taking the right branch), which needs clarification and ties to pruning strategies.
- Practical motivation is weak: decision trees are chiefly valued for interpretability, yet the method with L2 regularization likely yields non-interpretable dense splits.
- Suggests investigating L1 regularization or enforcing 1-sparse splits to retain interpretability and studying performance trade-offs.
- Experimental comparisons lack detail: unclear which pruning strategies and parameters were used for the naive baseline.
- Recommends testing naive methods across pruning parameters and comparing to other simple global strategies (e.g., greedy on random subsets).
- Some clarity issues in presentation (e.g., Sections 6.2 and 6.4 need clearer exposition of optimization ordering).
- Title mentions forests but the paper does not address ensembles; this discrepancy should be fixed or explained.
- Post-rebuttal: zero-weight issue remains critical; authors likely used random initialization so zero-weights didn't arise, but exploring zero-initialization and gradual activation would strengthen the work.
- Overall conclusion: creative and innovative reformulation with efficient algorithms, but practical utility and interpretability remain unconvincing without further analysis and stronger experimental comparisons.
