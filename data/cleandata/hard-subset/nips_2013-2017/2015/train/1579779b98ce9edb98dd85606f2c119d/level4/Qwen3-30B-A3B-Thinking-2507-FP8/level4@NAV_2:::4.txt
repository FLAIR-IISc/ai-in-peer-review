

The submission proposes a novel approach for global optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors introduce a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient training using stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is showing how to reduce the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p), making deep tree training feasible while maintaining the computational advantages of decision trees.

The paper makes a significant contribution by connecting decision tree optimization to structured prediction, which is both theoretically interesting and practically useful. The reformulation enables sparse gradient updates where only one node's weights are updated per step, a clever technical insight that could have broader implications for tree-based methods. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves better generalization and is less prone to overfitting than standard decision trees across multiple classification benchmarks.

However, I have several concerns that need to be addressed. First, the paper's motivation for pursuing global optimization of decision trees is not sufficiently compelling. Decision trees are primarily valued for their interpretability, yet the authors' method with L2 regularization produces dense weight vectors that would make the trees less interpretable. The paper should clarify whether this loss of interpretability is acceptable for the performance gains achieved, and whether L1 regularization could be used to maintain sparsity while still achieving good performance.

Second, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail about the baseline methods. The authors compare their method to standard greedy approaches but do not specify the pruning strategies used for the baselines. Given that they tune multiple hyperparameters for their method, it would be appropriate to compare against baselines with similarly tuned parameters or pruning strategies.

Third, the paper does not adequately address the critical case when a weight vector is zero. The paper states that the right branch is taken in this case, which seems arbitrary and could significantly impact the tree structure. The authors should clarify how they handle this scenario and whether it relates to pruning strategies.

Fourth, the title mentions "forests," but the paper only addresses single trees. The authors should either modify the title or extend their method to handle forests.

The paper is generally well-written, though some sections describing the inference process could be clearer. For instance, Section 6.2 could be simplified by stating that the objective is first maximized for all g corresponding to a given leaf, then maximized over all leaves.

In summary, the paper presents a technically interesting approach to global decision tree optimization with a clever reformulation as structured prediction. However, the practical significance is unclear due to the interpretability concerns and insufficient experimental details. The authors should address these concerns to strengthen their contribution.
