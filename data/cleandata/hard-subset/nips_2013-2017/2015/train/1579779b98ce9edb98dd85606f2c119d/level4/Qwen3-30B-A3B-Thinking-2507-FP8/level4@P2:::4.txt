

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach for optimizing decision trees by reformulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, introducing a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. This surrogate objective enables efficient optimization using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), with computational complexity O(d²p) for tree depth d and input dimension p. The method jointly optimizes split functions across all tree levels under a global objective, contrasting with conventional greedy approaches that optimize nodes sequentially.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a novel theoretical connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, which is a fresh perspective on a long-standing problem. Second, it develops an efficient algorithm for loss-augmented inference that scales to deep trees, addressing the computational challenges of global optimization. Third, it demonstrates empirically that the non-greedy approach outperforms greedy baselines across multiple classification benchmarks. The work builds on prior efforts such as Bennett's global tree optimization [1], but overcomes their computational limitations and extends to handle general loss functions.

Regarding quality, the paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-justified theoretical foundation. The convex-concave upper bound is rigorously derived, and the algorithmic details for efficient inference are thoroughly developed. The experimental section demonstrates clear improvements over greedy baselines, including conventional axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, and random oblique trees. However, the paper could provide more details on hyperparameter tuning and the impact of different regularization strategies. The comparison with CO2 [18] is insightful, but additional baselines such as pruning strategies for greedy trees would strengthen the evaluation.

The paper is generally well-written, though some sections could benefit from clearer exposition. Section 6.2 could be more concise by emphasizing that loss-augmented inference for each leaf is solved independently and then the best leaf is selected. The explanation of the Hamming ball constraint in the fast loss-augmented inference (Section 6.3) would be clearer if it explicitly stated that this constraint limits the search to neighbors of the current decision path.

The originality of the work is strong. While global optimization of decision trees has been studied before [1, 17], the authors' novel reformulation as structured prediction with latent variables and the efficient algorithms developed to solve this problem represent significant advances. The connection to structured prediction is particularly insightful, as it enables leveraging advances in that field. The paper also carefully checks against prior NIPS work and demonstrates that the approach is distinct from previous submissions.

The significance of the work is somewhat questionable. The paper claims that non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines, but it doesn't fully address why this method is preferable to other state-of-the-art approaches. Decision trees are often used for their interpretability, but the proposed method with L2 regularization produces dense weight vectors that likely compromise interpretability. The authors acknowledge this issue but don't sufficiently explore alternatives like L1 regularization, which would produce sparse splits. Without addressing this core tension between performance and interpretability, the practical utility of the method remains unclear.

A critical concern is the handling of zero-weight nodes. The paper states that when a weight vector is zero, the algorithm takes the right branch (page 3), but this seems arbitrary and potentially problematic. If weights are initialized to zero (as suggested in the rebuttal), this could lead to suboptimal tree structures. The paper doesn't explore how the method would behave when starting with zero weights and gradually increasing them, which could enable natural tree growth and potentially connect to known greedy strategies. This is a fundamental aspect of the method that deserves more thorough analysis.

The experimental section has several weaknesses. The paper doesn't specify which pruning strategies were used for the greedy baselines, making it difficult to fairly compare the methods. Since the proposed method uses regularization parameters, it would be appropriate to tune the baselines with multiple pruning strategies and parameters. The paper also doesn't compare against simple alternatives like training multiple greedy trees on random subsets and selecting the best one, which would provide a better baseline for the "global" optimization claim.

The title mentions "forests," but the paper focuses exclusively on single trees. While the authors note that forests could be built using the proposed tree learning method, the lack of discussion on forest optimization is a missed opportunity to demonstrate broader applicability.

The authors have made a clever theoretical contribution that connects decision tree learning to structured prediction. The algorithmic innovations are technically sound and well-executed. However, the practical significance is undermined by the interpretability concern and insufficient experimental comparison. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the zero-weight node issue more thoroughly, exploring regularization alternatives that preserve interpretability, and providing more detailed experimental comparisons with appropriately tuned baselines.

In summary, the paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution with a novel reformulation and efficient algorithm. The technical quality is strong, and the experimental results show promise. However, the practical utility is questionable without addressing interpretability concerns, and the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive. The authors should consider how their method might be adapted to maintain interpretability while achieving the performance gains demonstrated.

The paper would benefit from addressing these concerns before acceptance. As it stands, it represents a technically sound but not yet practically compelling contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The connection to structured prediction is valuable, but the work would be more impactful if it could demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods in practical applications where interpretability matters.
