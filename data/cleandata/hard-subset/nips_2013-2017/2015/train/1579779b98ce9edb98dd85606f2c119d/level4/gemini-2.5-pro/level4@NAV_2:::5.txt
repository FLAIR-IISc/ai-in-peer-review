This paper introduces a novel algorithm for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees with oblique (linear-combination) splits. The core idea is to learn the parameters of all split functions and leaf nodes jointly by minimizing a global objective function, in contrast to standard greedy, node-by-node procedures. The authors establish an insightful connection between this problem and structured prediction with latent variables. This formulation allows them to derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), with a proposed modification to make the training of deep trees computationally feasible.

The main strength of this work lies in its elegant and principled formulation. By framing decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, the paper provides a fresh perspective on a classic and challenging machine learning problem. This approach is novel and theoretically interesting.

However, the primary weakness is in the experimental validation. The empirical results do not provide a compelling case for the superiority of this non-greedy approach over traditional greedy methods. While the proposed algorithm shows improvements on some datasets, the gains over baselines are often marginal and may not be sufficient to justify the method's increased computational complexity. The significance of the contribution would be strengthened if the performance benefits were more substantial and consistent across a wider range of tasks.

The paper is generally well-structured, but several points require clarification:
*   The description of the Stable SGD (SSGD) variant is somewhat unclear. Could the authors provide more detail on the mechanism for assigning data points to leaves and how these assignments are fixed and updated? Including pseudocode for the SSGD procedure would be highly beneficial for reproducibility and understanding.
*   Regarding the experiments in Figure 2: Which algorithm was used to obtain these results—the standard SGD or the Stable SGD? Additionally, the term "active leaf" is used but not formally defined. Please provide a precise definition.
*   Could the authors clarify the update for the leaf parameters `Θ` in Algorithm 1, Line 10? Given that `θj` represents unnormalized log-probabilities for a softmax function, is the standard gradient update sufficient, or are there additional normalization or projection steps involved that are not explicitly stated?

Minor comments and typos:
*   In the initialization procedure, the text mentions tuning the "number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree". For a standard decision tree, all features are typically considered at each node. Please clarify this part of the procedure.
*   In equations (7), (8), and (9), the "argmax" operator should be "max," as the expression being maximized is a scalar value.
*   Figure 1 is included but is not explicitly referenced or discussed in the main text.
*   Figure 4 and the related discussion refer to "FSGD." This abbreviation is not defined in the text.
*   The model selection criterion is described as using "minimum training errors." This should likely be "minimum validation error."
*   Grammatical suggestions: "the solution to loss-augmented inference" (line 238) might be better as "the solutions to..."; "An key observation" (line 301) should be "A key observation".

Comments on the supplementary material:
*   Line 29: `max{g ∈ sgn...}` appears to be a typo and should likely be `max{g = sgn...}` or otherwise clarified.
*   Line 30: The expression `sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(Θ^Tf(g), y)` seems incorrect in this context and should probably be `sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(Θ^Tf(sgn(Wx)), y)`.

In summary, the paper presents an elegant formulation for a difficult problem, but the experimental results are not yet convincing enough to demonstrate a clear practical advantage over simpler, greedy methods.
