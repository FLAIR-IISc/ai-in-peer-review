This paper presents an algorithm for learning a decision tree with linear split functions at the nodes. The algorithm optimizes the tree parameters (i.e., the weights in the split functions and the distribution of the output variable at each leaf) in a global way, as opposed to the standard greedy way of learning trees. An upper bound on the tree's empirical loss is proposed, and to achieve a smoother optimization problem, this upper bound is regularized via a L2 regularization of the split function weights. The algorithm then consists of using a stochastic gradient descent to identify the tree parameters that minimize the upper bound.

The paper provides a clear and concise summary of the main ideas, relating them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors establish a link between the decision tree optimization problem and structured prediction with latent variables, which is an interesting contribution. The paper is well-structured and easy to follow.

However, the empirical results presented in the paper do not convincingly demonstrate that global optimization is a better alternative to greedy learning of the tree. The globally optimized tree outperforms the greedy tree significantly on only a couple of datasets, while being more computationally intensive. This raises questions about the practical benefits of the proposed approach.

The paper could be improved by providing more clarity on certain aspects, such as the stable version of SGD. A pseudo-code for the stable SGD algorithm would be helpful, as well as a clearer explanation of how data points are assigned to leaves. Additionally, the authors should clarify what is meant by an "active" leaf in Figure 2 and specify whether the results were obtained using SGD or stable SGD.

Minor comments and typos include:

* In lines 407-408, the authors mention tuning the "number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree." However, all features are typically evaluated at each node when building a standard decision tree.
* In equations (7), (8), and (9), "argmax" should be replaced with "max."
* Figure 1 is not mentioned in the main text.
* Line 141 contains a grammatical error: "...to the index of the leaf on by this path."
* Line 238 should read "the solutions to..." instead of "the solution to..."
* Line 301 contains a typo: "An key observation..." should be "A key observation..."
* Line 360 refers to "FSGD," which is not defined.
* Line 404 mentions "a tree with minimum training errors," which should be "tuning errors."

In the supplementary material, there are a few minor errors:

* Line 29 should read "max{g = sgn...}" instead of "max{g ∈ sgn...}"
* Line 30 contains a typo: "sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(\Theta^Tf(g), y)..." should be "sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(\Theta^Tf(sgn(Wx)), y)"

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting contribution to the field of decision tree learning, the empirical results and certain aspects of the paper could be improved for better clarity and impact.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to decision tree learning, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors establish a link between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, which is an interesting and non-trivial contribution.
* The paper is well-structured and easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate the benefits of global optimization over greedy learning.
* The paper could benefit from more clarity on certain aspects, such as the stable version of SGD.
* The computational intensity of the proposed approach may be a limitation in practice.
