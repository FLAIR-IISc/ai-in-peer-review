- Proposes a global optimization method for learning decision trees with linear splits, framed as structured prediction with latent variables.  
- Derives an upper bound on empirical loss, regularized by L2 penalties, and optimizes split weights and leaf distributions jointly via SGD.  
- Theoretical formulation is elegant and the problem setup is well-motivated.  
- Empirical results are weak: the global method outperforms greedy learning on only two datasets (Table 2).  
- Global optimization incurs substantial computational overhead (e.g., 3–5× longer training on the largest dataset).  
- The primary claim that global optimization improves over greedy methods is not convincingly supported, weakening the contribution.  
- "Stable SGD" is insufficiently described—unclear how data points are assigned to leaves; pseudocode is needed for reproducibility.  
- Figure 2 is ambiguous: unclear whether results use standard or stable SGD and the term "active leaf" is undefined.  
- Algorithm 1 omits details on projecting Θ onto the simplex; implementation and the fact that rows must sum to 1 should be stated.  
- Multiple minor textual and notation errors need correction (misleading phrasing about feature evaluation, argmax→max in Eqs. 7–9, Figure 1 not referenced, several typos, undefined "FSGD", awkward phrasing about training errors).  
- Supplementary material contains errors (sign/argument mistakes in lines 29–30) that need fixing.  
- Suggested fixes: expand experimental comparison (more datasets/baselines), analyze why global optimization helps on certain data (e.g., feature correlations), and quantify runtime vs. accuracy trade-offs.  
- Overall: technically sound groundwork but empirical shortcomings and high computational cost reduce practical impact; stronger experiments and clarity are required to justify the shift from greedy to global optimization.
