

This paper proposes a novel non-greedy framework for optimizing decision trees by jointly learning split functions across all tree levels under a global objective. The authors establish a connection to structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient stochastic gradient descent (SGD) training with O(d²p) complexity per iteration (where d is tree depth and p is feature dimension). Crucially, they demonstrate that the exponential complexity of naive latent variable approaches can be reduced to polynomial time through a refined upper bound, making deep tree training feasible. The method is theoretically grounded, handles arbitrary loss functions, and incorporates row-wise norm regularization to prevent overfitting. Experiments on standard benchmarks (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) show consistent improvements over greedy baselines (including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and CO2 [18]), with non-greedy trees achieving better generalization and reduced susceptibility to overfitting at deeper depths.

**Quality**: The technical contribution is strong and well-justified. The paper rigorously proves the convex-concave upper bound (Eq. 7) and its scaling properties (Prop. 1), and the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm (Section 6.2) elegantly addresses the computational bottleneck. The regularization strategy (row-wise L2 constraints) is principled and aligns with prior work on margin-based tree optimization [3, 18]. However, the empirical gains are modest: on MNIST, non-greedy trees achieve only 0.5–1.0% higher test accuracy than CO2 at depth 12, with minimal improvement on other datasets (Fig. 2). The authors acknowledge this in their rebuttal, noting that further hyperparameter tuning (e.g., ν) could yield marginal gains. While the theoretical foundation is sound, the experimental results do not yet demonstrate *substantial* superiority over state-of-the-art greedy methods, which is a limitation for significance.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem formulation (Section 3) and latent variable reformulation (Section 4) are lucidly presented, with clear notation (e.g., the navigation function f(·)). The algorithmic details (Alg. 1, SSGD) are precise, and the computational complexity analysis is thorough. The supplementary material adequately supports the proofs. Minor suggestions: the connection to Mondrian forests [15] in Related Work could be strengthened to clarify how this work differs from online tree extension. The figure captions (e.g., Fig. 1) could more explicitly link the navigation function to the tree structure.

**Originality**: The core insight—reducing decision tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables and deriving an efficient upper bound—is highly novel. While Bennett [1] proposed global tree optimization, his method is limited to 0-1 loss and suffers from exponential complexity. This work generalizes to arbitrary losses and achieves polynomial-time training, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field (citing [11] on NP-completeness). The fast inference technique (B1(sgn(Wx)) Hamming ball) is a key technical advance not present in prior art. The paper properly contextualizes its contribution against related work (e.g., soft splits [13], max-margin trees [3]) and avoids overclaiming novelty.

**Significance**: The method’s true significance lies in its *potential* to catalyze future research. By providing a theoretically grounded, scalable framework for global tree optimization, it opens avenues for extensions (e.g., sparse splits via L1 regularization, kernelized splits, or ensemble methods). While current empirical results are incremental, the elegance of the approach and its compatibility with standard SGD make it a compelling foundation for subsequent work. The authors’ claim that "the method is likely to lead to more research" is well-supported; the technique is sufficiently general to be adopted across multiple applications (e.g., computer vision [22], medical imaging [7]). The modest gains in current experiments do not diminish this potential impact.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:  
*Pros*: Theoretical novelty is high; the upper bound formulation is elegant and rigorously derived; the O(d²p) complexity enables deep tree training; the framework is general (loss-agnostic) and regularized; the paper is exceptionally clear.  
*Cons*: Empirical gains are marginal (≤1% on key datasets), raising questions about real-world impact; the overfitting risk (despite regularization) is not fully mitigated (e.g., validation curves in Fig. 3 show high variance for small ν); the "last split" heuristic (suggested in draft) could yield immediate improvements but is untested.  

**Recommendation**: I recommend acceptance. The paper’s theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed, with clear pathways for future work. While the empirical results are not groundbreaking, the method’s elegance, scalability, and potential to inspire follow-up research justify publication at NIPS. The modest experimental gains are understandable given the inherent difficulty of global tree optimization, and the authors’ acknowledgment of this in the rebuttal is reasonable. To strengthen future versions, I suggest: (1) testing the "last split" post-optimization tweak; (2) comparing against more recent greedy baselines (e.g., [12]); (3) deeper analysis of the overfitting-regularization trade-off via learning curves. This work is a valuable step toward principled global optimization of tree-based models.
