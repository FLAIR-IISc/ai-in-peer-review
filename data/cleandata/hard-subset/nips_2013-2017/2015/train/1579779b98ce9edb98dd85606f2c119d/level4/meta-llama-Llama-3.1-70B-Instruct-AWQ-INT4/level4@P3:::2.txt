This paper proposes a novel approach to jointly train all parameters of decision trees, overcoming the limitations of traditional greedy methods. The authors formulate the problem as a single optimization task and develop an upper bound on the empirical loss, which is then approximated to obtain a tractable solution.

Quality: The paper is well-written, but there are potential flaws in the reasoning. Specifically, the derivation of the empirical loss bound in Equation (7) is confusing, and the argument in lines 216-217 is not clear. A counterexample is provided to illustrate the issue, where choosing g = [1;1] results in a smaller right-hand side of Equation (7), potentially invalidating the upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not cover this case, making it incomplete.

Clarity: The derivation is clean, but the experiments and their presentation could be improved. The authors should consider adding more details about the experimental setup, such as the choice of hyperparameters and the initialization of the decision trees.

Originality: Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important topic, and the authors' approach is novel and relevant.

Significance: The proposed solution is valuable, but there seem to be some shortcomings. The authors should address the potential flaws in the derivation and provide a more thorough experimental evaluation.

Comments:

* The authors should clarify the reasoning behind the empirical loss bound and address the potential counterexample.
* The phenomenon of some leaves not being assigned any data points is strange and could indicate a problem with the objective or initialization. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation and consider alternative approaches, such as expectation maximization or concave-convex procedures.
* The importance of the efficient loss-augmented inference is unclear, given that the depth of the tree is relatively small (at most 16). The authors should provide more results to demonstrate the impact of the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball.
* A baseline that trains non-axis aligned split functions of trees in a standard greedy manner (without initialization) is missing and should be included for comparison.
* The authors should discuss the limitation of specifying the depth of the tree ahead of time and provide a more careful experimental evaluation regarding the depth parameter.

Overall, the paper has potential, but the authors need to address the raised concerns and provide a more thorough evaluation to strengthen their claims.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper proposes a novel approach to jointly train decision trees, which is an important topic in machine learning.
* The authors develop an upper bound on the empirical loss and provide a tractable solution, which is a significant contribution.
* The experimental results show promising performance of the proposed method compared to greedy baselines.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The derivation of the empirical loss bound is potentially flawed, and the authors need to address the raised concerns.
* The experimental evaluation is limited, and the authors should provide more results to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.
* The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed method, particularly regarding the specification of the tree depth.
