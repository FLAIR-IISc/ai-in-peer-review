- Paper proposes joint training of all decision tree parameters via a single optimization problem as an upper-bounded, tractable approximation — an advance over greedy layer-wise methods.  
- Manuscript is well-written and derivation is clear, but there are potential reasoning flaws and experimental presentation weaknesses.  
- Joint training for decision trees is an original and significant direction, though the proposed solution may have limitations.  
- The empirical loss bound (Equation 7) appears flawed; a concrete counterexample shows the right-hand side may not be an upper bound and Proposition 1’s proof is incomplete.  
- Data-to-leaf assignments look suboptimal with empty leaves, suggesting issues with the objective or initialization; fixed-assignment heuristics may be inferior to EM or concave–convex approaches.  
- The role and benefit of the efficient loss-augmented inference are unclear for shallow trees; the Hamming-ball restricted search space’s impact is not well validated and “leaves” are ambiguously defined.  
- A baseline that trains non-axis-aligned split functions greedily is missing and should be included for fair comparison.  
- Requiring tree depth to be specified a priori is a notable limitation; authors should discuss this and experimentally evaluate depth sensitivity.  
- Minor suggestions: replace argmax with max for clarity, add runtime comparisons to other methods, and address the possible derivation flaw in the rebuttal (score may change after clarification).
