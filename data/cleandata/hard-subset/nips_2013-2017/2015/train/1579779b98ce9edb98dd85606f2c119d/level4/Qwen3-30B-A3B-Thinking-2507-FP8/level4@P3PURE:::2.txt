

Summary: This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters (decision splits and posterior probabilities) of decision trees, addressing an important limitation of current greedy, layer-by-layer approaches. The authors formulate the task as a single optimization problem, which they upper-bound and approximate to yield a tractable solution.

Quality: The paper is well-written but contains a potential flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound (see Comments). The reasoning requires careful verification.  
Clarity: The mathematical derivation is clean, but the experimental design and presentation require significant improvement.  
Originality: Investigating joint training for decision trees represents a meaningful contribution to the field.  
Significance: The proposed solution offers value, though several shortcomings warrant attention (see Comments).

Comments:  
1. **Flaw in empirical loss bound derivation (lines 216–217):** The argument states that "for all values of $\mathbf{g} \neq \text{sgn}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{x})$, the right-hand side can only become larger than when $\mathbf{g} = \text{sgn}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{x})$." This is questionable. Consider the counterexample: $\mathbf{W}\mathbf{x} = [5; -5]$, so $\hat{\mathbf{h}} = \text{sgn}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{x}) = [1; -1]$, and $\hat{\mathbf{h}}^\top \mathbf{W}\mathbf{x} = 10$. For $\mathbf{g} = [1; 1]$, $\mathbf{g}^\top \mathbf{W}\mathbf{x} = 0$, making the right-hand side of Equation (7) equal to $0 - 10 + \ell(\cdot) = -10 + \ell(\cdot)$. Since $\ell(\cdot) \geq 0$, this expression could be smaller than $\ell(\cdot)$, violating the purported upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not address this case, rendering it incomplete.  

2. **Unexplained leaf assignments:** Some leaves are assigned no data points, which is problematic and may indicate an issue with the objective or initialization. The heuristic for fixed data-point assignment to leaves appears suboptimal; its justification via expectation maximization or the concave-convex procedure (as used in clustering) should be clarified.  

3. **Justification for loss-augmented inference:** The paper claims efficiency gains from the Hamming-ball search, but with a maximum tree depth of 16, greedily checking $2^{16} = 65,536$ values remains feasible. How significantly does the restricted search space impact performance? The single supplementary result does not establish generalizability. Additionally, the term "leaves" in this context is ambiguous.  

4. **Missing baseline:** A critical baseline is absent: a standard greedy method for training trees with non-axis-aligned split functions (without any initialization). This comparison is essential for evaluating the proposed approach.  

5. **Tree depth specification:** The method requires specifying tree depth *a priori*, a limitation that should be discussed more thoroughly. The authors should also provide a more rigorous experimental analysis of the depth parameter's impact.  

*Post-rebuttal note:* Thanks to the authors for clarifying the reasoning behind the loss bound (Equation 7). Please replace $\arg\max$ with $\max$ as noted. I have adjusted my score based on your explanation. An experiment comparing runtime would strengthen the paper. A more careful evaluation of the method is recommended for readers. The submission addresses an interesting problem, but the experimental evaluation is limited, and a potential flaw in the derivation remains a concern.
