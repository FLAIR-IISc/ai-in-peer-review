The submission introduces an algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees by reformulating the problem as (latent) structured prediction, where the latent variables represent sign vectors encoding decisions at each internal node. A non-differentiable, non-convex loss is proposed and approximated by a differentiable (though still non-convex) upper bound. Minimization via SGD involves solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided. Experiments demonstrate a comparison to standard greedy training.

The key contributions lie in framing global decision tree optimization as structured prediction and developing efficient optimization algorithms. This approach is novel and raises intriguing research questions. However, I have concerns regarding practical motivations and the significance of performance gains over simpler methods. 

While the structured prediction formulation enables SGD through loss-augmented optimization leveraging the problem’s structure, the most interesting aspect is the sparsity of the gradient updates – if my understanding is correct, each step modifies only one node’s weight vector. The paper highlights this as a computational advantage, but I believe it opens more compelling possibilities, currently underexplored. Specifically, one could envision optimizing an infinitely deep tree by initializing all weights to zero and gradually growing it by increasing weights, allowing model complexity to adapt to the data. This raises questions about its relationship to existing greedy strategies – would it be equivalent or a variation? Would it converge to a limited-depth tree (as suggested by Fig. 2) with appropriate regularization, or always favor adding new nodes over revising existing ones? This issue is central to the method’s value proposition compared to standard decision trees and warrants deeper investigation.

A critical ambiguity concerns the behavior when a node’s weight is zero. The discussion on page 3 suggests taking the right branch, which seems questionable. Outputting a decision at such a node, with the option to later increase the weight and split it, would be preferable, mirroring pruning strategies in greedy training. A precise definition of this behavior is crucial.

I also have a significant practical concern regarding the work’s motivation. Plain decision trees are primarily valuable for their interpretability; when interpretability is not a priority, other methods generally outperform them. The proposed method, with its L2 regularization, is likely to produce non-interpretable, dense weight vectors. While L1 regularization could help, performance might suffer if forced to achieve exact 1-sparse splits, requiring further testing.

Furthermore, the experiments lack sufficient detail regarding baselines. No pruning strategies used for the naive approach are specified. Given the range of regularization parameters explored for the proposed method, a similar exploration of pruning strategies/parameters for the naive method is expected. Including other global optimization strategies, such as training a greedy method on random data subsets and selecting the best performing model, would also be beneficial.

The paper is generally well-written, though the description of inference could be clearer. Section 6.2 could be concisely summarized as maximizing the objective first for all *g* corresponding to a given leaf, then over all leaves. Section 6.4 could also benefit from greater explicitness.

In summary, this is a clever approach with the potential to raise interesting questions. However, its practical utility remains unclear due to the interpretability concerns (why use decision trees if not for interpretability?) and the limited detail in the experimental evaluation.

PS: The title mentions forests, but the paper does not address this case.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
I maintain that the case of zero weights is a critical area for further analysis, as outlined in my review. The rebuttal suggests this issue doesn’t arise in practice due to random weight initialization. However, I believe the most compelling application of this method would be initializing weights to zero and gradually increasing them. I encourage the authors to explore this direction. The submission presents a clever reformulation of global decision tree optimization as structured prediction, accompanied by an efficient optimization algorithm. However, certain aspects of the motivation and experimental results remain unconvincing.
