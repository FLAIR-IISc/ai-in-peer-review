## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission

**Summary:** This paper proposes a novel method for jointly training all parameters – decision splits and posterior probabilities – of decision trees. This is a significant contribution, as current tree-building algorithms typically employ a greedy, layer-by-layer approach. The authors formulate the joint training problem as a single optimization problem, then employ techniques to upper-bound and approximate it, resulting in a tractable formulation for implementation.

**Quality:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. However, the theoretical justification contains a potential flaw, detailed in the comments below. 

**Clarity:** The derivation of the core theoretical results is clean and easy to follow. However, the presentation of experiments and their results could be improved with more comprehensive analysis and discussion.

**Originality:** Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important and original direction. While decision tree optimization has been extensively studied, the proposed joint approach represents a distinct departure from conventional methods.

**Significance:** The proposed solution is valuable and addresses a key limitation in current decision tree learning. However, the potential shortcomings outlined below, and the limited experimental validation, currently temper its overall significance.

**Comments:**

1. **Empirical Loss Bound Derivation:** I have concerns regarding the derivation of the empirical loss bound, specifically around the argument presented in lines 216-218. The claim that “...for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$…” appears to be incorrect. Consider the following counterexample:  Let $W\bx = [5; -5]$.  Then $\hat{\bh} = \sgn(W\bx) = [1; -1]$, and $\hat{\bh}^T W\bx = 10$.  If we choose $\bg = [1; 1]$, then $\bg^T W\bx = 0$. Applying this to Eq. (7) yields $g^T W\bx - \hat{\bh}^T W\bx + \ell(...) = -10 + \ell(...)$, which can be *smaller* than $\ell(...)$, invalidating the upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not adequately address this scenario and therefore remains incomplete. *[Note to authors: The authors have addressed this concern in their response; replacing  \arg\max with \max will likely resolve the issue.]*

2. **Unassigned Data Points:** Several leaves appear to be assigned no data points in the experiments. This is unusual and suggests a potential problem with the optimization objective or initialization strategy. The authors employ a fixed assignment of data points to leaves, which seems sub-optimal. This heuristic could be better motivated by drawing parallels to expectation maximization (EM) or concave-convex procedures, as seen in other clustering techniques.

3. **Loss-Augmented Inference Efficiency:** The paper highlights the efficiency gains of loss-augmented inference. However, the reported tree depths are relatively small (at most 16), making a brute-force search over $2^{16} = 65536$ split configurations potentially feasible. The impact of the Hamming ball restriction on performance is unclear. While a result is provided in the supplementary material, it is not clear if this generalizes to other datasets or tree depths. Furthermore, the definition of “leaves” within this context requires clarification.

4. **Baseline Comparison:** The experimental evaluation lacks a crucial baseline: a standard, greedy training procedure for non-axis-aligned split functions, trained *without* any initialization. Including this baseline would help to better isolate the benefits of the proposed joint training approach.

5. **Depth Parameter Sensitivity:** The method requires specifying the tree depth *a priori*. This limitation should be discussed more thoroughly. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation varying the depth parameter would also be valuable, providing insight into the method’s sensitivity to this choice.

**Overall Assessment:**

This submission considers an interesting and important problem – the joint training of decision tree parameters. While the proposed method shows promise, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and a potential flaw exists in the theoretical derivation. I am willing to adjust my score based on a satisfactory rebuttal addressing these concerns, particularly a clarification and correction of the loss bound derivation, and further analysis of the method’s performance relative to relevant baselines. A runtime comparison would also strengthen the paper. A more thorough evaluation would significantly enhance the reader's understanding of the method's strengths and weaknesses.
