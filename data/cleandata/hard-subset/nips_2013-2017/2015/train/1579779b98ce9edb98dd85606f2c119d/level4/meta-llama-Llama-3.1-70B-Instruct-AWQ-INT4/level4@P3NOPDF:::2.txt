Summary: 
The paper proposes a novel method for jointly training all parameters of decision trees, including decision splits and posterior probabilities. This is an important contribution, as current methods typically train trees in a greedy, layer-by-layer manner. The authors frame the task as a single optimization problem, which is then upper-bounded and approximated to obtain a tractable formulation.

Quality: 
The paper is well-written, but there may be flaws in the reasoning, as discussed below. The clarity of the paper is good, with a clean derivation, but the experiments and their presentation could be improved.

Originality: 
Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important and original topic.

Significance: 
The proposed solution is valuable, although there seem to be some shortcomings, as discussed below.

Comments:

1. Flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound?
The argument in lines 216f is confusing, specifically the statement that "for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right-hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$." A counterexample illustrates this concern:
Consider $W\bx = [5;-5]$, which yields $\hat \bh = [1;-1] = \sgn(W\bx)$. Then, $\hat \bh^T W\bx = 10$. However, choosing $\bg = [1;1]$ results in $\bg^T W\bx = 0$. Consequently, the right-hand side of Eq. (7) becomes $-10 + \ell(...)$, which could potentially be smaller than $\ell(...)$ and is therefore not a valid upper bound. The proof of Prop. 1 in the supplementary material does not cover this case and is thus incomplete.

2. Unassigned data points in the leaves
Some leaves are not assigned any data points, which seems unusual and may indicate a problem with the objective or initialization. The suggested heuristic of a fixed assignment of data points to tree leaves appears suboptimal but could potentially be motivated via expectation maximization or the concave-convex procedure, similar to other clustering techniques.

3. Importance of efficient loss-augmented inference
The depth of the tree is at most 16, and greedily checking $2^{16} = 65536$ values still seems feasible. It is unclear how much the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball impacts performance. While one result is provided in the supplementary material, it is unclear whether this generalizes. Furthermore, the term "leaves" is not clearly defined.

4. Missing baseline
A baseline that trains non-axis aligned split functions of trees in a standard greedy manner, without any initialization, is missing.

5. Limitation of fixed tree depth
The depth of the tree needs to be specified ahead of time, which is a significant limitation. The authors should discuss this limitation more carefully and provide a more thorough experimental evaluation regarding the depth parameter.

Additional suggestions:

* The authors should consider replacing $\arg\max$ with $\max$ in Eq. 7.
* An experiment comparing the runtime could strengthen the paper.
* A more careful evaluation of the method would be desirable for the reader.

The submission considers an interesting problem of jointly training all parameters of decision trees. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and there may be a flaw in the derivation. A careful explanation in the rebuttal could lead to an adjustment of the score.
