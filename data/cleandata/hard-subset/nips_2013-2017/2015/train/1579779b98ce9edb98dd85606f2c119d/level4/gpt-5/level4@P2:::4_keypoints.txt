- Formulates global training of oblique decision trees as a latent structured prediction problem with a convex–concave surrogate enabling SGD with loss-augmented inference.  
- Introduces fast loss-augmented inference by restricting to a Hamming ball (radius 1), reducing per-update complexity to O(d^2 p).  
- Jointly optimizes split parameters and leaf predictions; proposes Stable SGD (SSGD) and per-row L2 norm constraints for regularization.  
- Shows improved test accuracy over greedy baselines on several multiclass datasets.  
- Situates work relative to NP-complete global tree optimization, greedy/oblique baselines (ID3/CART/OC1), max-margin node methods, and latent-structured prediction literature; CO2 is a depth-1 special case.  
- Strength: conceptually novel and well-grounded formulation casting tree induction as latent structured prediction.  
- Strength: algorithmic efficiency makes deep-tree training feasible.  
- Strength: framework is general to different losses and preserves O(dp) test-time inference.  
- Strength: per-row norm constraints provide principled regularization and appear to reduce overfitting.  
- Strength: consistent empirical gains over greedy baselines across depths.  
- Weakness: interpretability concern—L2-regularized splits are typically dense; no L1 or structured-sparsity experiments to assess axis-aligned or sparse splits.  
- Weakness: experimental comparisons lack pruning details for greedy baselines and omit simple global-selection baselines for contextualizing gains.  
- Weakness: ambiguous handling of zero-weight split vectors; defaulting to one branch seems arbitrary and alternatives (e.g., treating node as leaf) are not discussed.  
- Weakness: learning dynamics under sparse per-path SGD updates are not explored (e.g., starting from zero and growing the tree).  
- Weakness: clarity issues in Sections 6.2 and 6.4—loss-augmented inference flow and bit-difference bookkeeping should be made explicit.  
- Quality: derivation and fast inference appear technically sound, but empirical methodology needs stronger baselines and ablations (e.g., ν effects, SSGD vs SGD).  
- Completeness: core contribution is well developed, but practical deployment aspects (interpretability, pruning/growth behavior, initialization) are under-addressed.  
- Clarity: paper is generally clear and organized, with minor suggestions to make inference steps explicit.  
- Originality: high—novel latent-structured perspective, convex–concave bound, and Hamming-ball inference for trees.  
- Significance: moderate–high if accuracy gains generalize and sparsity/interpretability can be integrated without large loss.  
- Pros for acceptance: elegant novel formulation, clear algorithmic contributions, efficient training, and consistent empirical improvements.  
- Cons against acceptance: insufficient baseline/pruning comparisons and ablations, limited interpretability/sparsity discussion, ambiguity around zero-weight nodes, and title mentions forests without ensemble experiments.  
- Key suggestions: evaluate L1/structured sparsity, add detailed pruning/global-selection baselines, clarify zero-weight behavior and ties to growth/pruning, expand Sections 6.2/6.4, and remove or justify “forests” in the title with ensemble results.  
- Post-rebuttal note: zero-weight case remains important; exploring growing-from-zero strategies could strengthen connections to principled pruning/growing schemes.
