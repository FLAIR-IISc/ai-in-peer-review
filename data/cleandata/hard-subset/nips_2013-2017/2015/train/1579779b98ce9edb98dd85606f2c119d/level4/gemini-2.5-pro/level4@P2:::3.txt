### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees. Traditional decision tree induction algorithms, such as ID3 and CART, operate greedily, optimizing split functions at each node independently without considering the impact on lower levels of the tree. The authors address this fundamental limitation by formulating the joint optimization of all split functions and leaf parameters as a structured prediction problem with latent variables.

The core idea is to represent the sequence of binary decisions for a given input as a latent variable. This allows the tree's empirical loss to be expressed in a form common to structured prediction. To overcome the non-differentiable and non-convex nature of the true objective, the authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, inspired by latent structural SVMs. This surrogate objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is the development of a "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm, which reduces the complexity of each SGD update from being exponential in the tree depth (d) to quadratic (O(d²p)), making the approach computationally feasible for deep trees. Experiments on several multiclass classification benchmarks show that the resulting non-greedy trees consistently outperform greedy baselines, particularly in terms of generalization.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The connection between global decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is a significant conceptual contribution. It reframes a classic, challenging problem within a well-understood optimization framework, moving beyond heuristic or highly constrained approaches seen in prior work (e.g., Bennett [1]). This formulation naturally incorporates regularization and is compatible with general loss functions.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Efficiency:** The derivation of the surrogate objective as a convex-concave upper bound is technically sound. More importantly, the paper provides a practical solution to the otherwise intractable problem of inference over an exponentially large latent space. The proposed "fast loss-augmented inference," which restricts the search to a Hamming ball of radius one, is a clever and crucial innovation that makes the entire method viable for reasonably deep trees.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the value of the non-greedy approach. Across multiple datasets and tree depths, the proposed method consistently achieves higher test accuracy than several relevant baselines, including axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, and a greedy variant (CO2). The results in Figure 2 also suggest that the proposed method is less prone to overfitting than its greedy counterparts, which is a valuable practical property.

4.  **Significance and Potential for Future Work:** The paper tackles a long-standing and important problem in machine learning. By providing an efficient, non-greedy training algorithm, it has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art for single decision trees. The framework is also extensible; as the authors note, it opens the door to extensions like sparsity-inducing regularizers for feature selection or the use of the kernel trick for non-linear splits.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Modest Performance Gains:** While the proposed method is consistently better than the baselines, the improvements in test accuracy are often modest (e.g., ~1-2% on most datasets). Given the increased computational complexity and implementation effort compared to standard greedy methods, a more detailed discussion of the trade-off between accuracy and training cost would be beneficial.

2.  **Lack of Comparison to Ensembles:** Decision trees are most powerful when used in ensembles like Random Forests or Gradient Boosted Trees. The paper focuses exclusively on single-tree performance. It is unclear whether the benefits of a more optimally trained individual tree would translate to significant gains in an ensemble context, where the diversity introduced by bagging or boosting might mitigate the sub-optimality of individual greedy learners. Including even a small-scale experiment with ensembles would greatly strengthen the paper's claims of significance.

3.  **Limited Computational Analysis:** The paper provides a theoretical complexity analysis and shows an empirical comparison between the full and fast inference methods (Figure 4). However, it lacks a direct comparison of wall-clock training time against the greedy baselines. Such a comparison would help practitioners understand the practical cost of the improved accuracy.

4.  **Clarity on Initialization and SSGD:** The performance of non-convex optimization methods can be sensitive to initialization. The paper mentions a multi-stage initialization procedure (axis-aligned -> CO2 -> non-greedy). The importance and sensitivity to this specific procedure could be further clarified. Similarly, the "Stable SGD (SSGD)" variant is introduced as a practical improvement, but its impact versus standard SGD is not empirically quantified.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a high-quality, novel, and technically sound contribution to the problem of learning decision trees. The formulation as a structured prediction problem is elegant and the proposed optimization algorithm is both clever and practical. The consistent empirical improvements over greedy methods validate the approach. The primary weaknesses relate to the magnitude of these improvements relative to the increased complexity and the absence of experiments in an ensemble setting. Nevertheless, the conceptual advance of providing a feasible global optimization framework for decision trees is a significant achievement that is likely to be influential and inspire future research. The paper is a clear fit for NIPS and I recommend its acceptance.
