- Paper reformulates global decision-tree optimization as latent structured prediction using sign-vector latent variables for node decisions.
- Proposes a non-differentiable, non-convex loss and a differentiable (still non-convex) upper bound approximation.
- Optimizes the bound with SGD requiring repeated loss-augmented inference; the paper provides efficient procedures for this.
- Main contributions: recasting global DT optimization as structured prediction and developing efficient optimization algorithms.
- Experiments compare the method only to a standard greedy training baseline.
- The approach is novel and thought-provoking but practical motivation and empirical gains over simple baselines are unclear.
- A notable property is extremely sparse gradient updates that typically modify only a single node’s weights per update.
- This sparsity is framed as a computational benefit but may enable deeper ideas (e.g., progressively activating nodes, effectively growing model complexity).
- Raises the question whether progressive activation would reduce to a known greedy strategy or consistently prefer adding nodes over adjusting existing ones.
- Asks whether with appropriate regularization the method would converge to a finite tree (as suggested by Fig. 2); this needs analysis.
- The behavior when a node’s weight is exactly zero is unresolved and important; paper’s implied tie-breaking to the right branch seems questionable.
- Suggests it may be better to output a decision at zero-weight nodes and later activate weights—this parallels pruning in greedy training and needs precise specification.
- Practical concern: method may produce non-interpretable trees (L2 yields dense weights); switching to L1 could help but may hurt performance and should be tested empirically.
- Experimental section lacks detail about baselines (e.g., pruning strategies); naive baseline should be evaluated with multiple pruning/parameter settings.
- Recommends including other simple global search baselines (e.g., greedy trees trained on random subsets then selected by validation loss).
- Clarity issues: parts of the inference description could be clearer—Section 6.2 and 6.4 need more explicit explanation/summarization.
- PS: Title mentions forests but the paper does not address forest settings.
- Post-rebuttal: zero-weight issue remains critical; random initialization may mask it empirically, but zero-initialization and progressive growth should be explored.
- Overall verdict: clever and efficient reformulation but motivation and experimental validation remain unconvincing.
