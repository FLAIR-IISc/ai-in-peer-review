

This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a theoretical connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized using stochastic gradient descent. This represents a significant departure from standard greedy tree learning methods, which optimize splits one node at a time and often lead to suboptimal trees.

The paper's strongest contribution is its elegant theoretical formulation that enables global optimization of tree parameters. The authors correctly identify the connection between decision tree navigation and latent variable structured prediction, and they develop a rigorous upper bound on the empirical loss. They then demonstrate how this bound can be optimized efficiently with a time complexity of O(d²p), where d is the tree depth and p is the number of features. This is a substantial improvement over previous approaches, such as Bennett's [1] non-greedy multi-linear programming method, which had exponential complexity and was limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical development to experimental validation. The authors effectively position their work in relation to previous literature, particularly highlighting how their approach differs from soft-split methods like the Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13], which sacrifice the computational advantages of decision trees for smoother optimization.

The main strength of the paper is its theoretical novelty and the clear demonstration that global optimization can lead to better generalization performance. The experimental results show that the non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines on several classification benchmarks, particularly in terms of generalization (as evidenced by test performance across different tree depths), which is a significant contribution.

However, the empirical evidence supporting the superiority of the proposed method is somewhat limited. The non-greedy approach shows clear advantages on only a few datasets in the experiments, with more marginal improvements on others. The paper would be significantly strengthened by more comprehensive experiments across a wider range of datasets and problem settings to better establish the generality of the approach. Additionally, the computational cost of the proposed method is higher than standard greedy approaches, and a more detailed comparison of training times would help contextualize this trade-off.

The paper has several clarity issues that need addressing. The Stable SGD variant is not well-explained; it's unclear how data points are assigned to leaves or how these assignments are updated. A pseudo-code for this algorithm would greatly improve clarity. Figure 2 should specify whether the results were obtained using standard SGD or Stable SGD, and the paper should clearly define what constitutes an "active" leaf.

The paper also contains several minor issues that should be fixed:
- The projection of Θ in Algorithm 1 is described but not explained; the authors should clarify that this projection ensures each row of Θ sums to one, which is necessary for the softmax function used in the model.
- The phrase "number of features evaluated" in lines 407-408 is misleading, as standard decision trees evaluate all features at each node.
- Several equations (7, 8, 9) incorrectly use "argmax" instead of "max".
- Figure 1 is referenced but never discussed in the main text.
- The supplementary material contains formatting errors that need correction (e.g., "max{g ∈ sgn..." should be "max{g = sgn...}").

Overall, this paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to decision tree learning by providing a principled way to optimize tree parameters globally. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is novel and well-executed. While the empirical results could be more compelling, the theoretical contribution is strong and the approach has clear potential for practical applications. The paper addresses all four key criteria for NIPS submission:
- Quality: The theoretical development is sound and the experimental results support the claims, though they could be more extensive.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-written but would benefit from addressing the clarity issues noted above.
- Originality: The connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is novel, and the approach clearly differs from previous work.
- Significance: The method enables more efficient training of deeper trees with better generalization, which could be valuable for practitioners dealing with large-scale classification tasks.

Given the strong theoretical contribution and the clear potential for practical impact, I recommend acceptance, with the understanding that the authors should address the clarity and empirical issues in a revised version.
