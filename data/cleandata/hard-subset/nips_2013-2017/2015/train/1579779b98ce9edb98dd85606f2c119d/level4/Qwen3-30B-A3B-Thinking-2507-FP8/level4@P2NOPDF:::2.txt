

Summary: This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters (decision splits and posterior probabilities) of decision trees, addressing a key limitation of current greedy, layer-by-layer approaches. The authors frame the task as a single optimization problem, which they upper-bound and approximate to yield a tractable formulation.

Quality: The paper is well-written, but the derivation of the empirical loss bound (Equation 7) contains a critical flaw (detailed below). The technical reasoning requires careful scrutiny.  
Clarity: The core derivation is presented cleanly, but experimental design and presentation lack sufficient detail.  
Originality: Joint training of tree parameters is a meaningful direction for decision tree research.  
Significance: The approach offers potential value, though key limitations (discussed below) warrant careful consideration.

Comments:  
1. **Flaw in the empirical loss bound derivation (Equation 7):** The argument in lines 216–217 claims that for all $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the RHS of Equation 7 is larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$. This is incorrect. Consider $W\bx = [5; -5]$, so $\hat{\bh} = \sgn(W\bx) = [1; -1]$ and $\hat{\bh}^T W\bx = 10$. Choosing $\bg = [1; 1]$ yields $\bg^T W\bx = 0$, making the RHS:  
$\bg^T W\bx - \hat{\bh}^T W\bx + \ell(\cdot) = -10 + \ell(\cdot)$,  
which is *smaller* than $\ell(\cdot)$ (the RHS when $\bg = \hat{\bh}$). Thus, this does not establish a valid upper bound. The supplementary proof of Proposition 1 fails to address this case, rendering the bound incomplete.  

2. **Unassigned leaves in training:** The paper reports leaves with no assigned data points (e.g., Table 1). This is unexpected and suggests a potential issue with the objective function or initialization. The authors should clarify whether this is a methodological artifact or a numerical artifact. The heuristic for fixed data-point assignment to leaves appears sub-optimal; justification via EM or CCCP (as used in clustering) would strengthen the approach.  

3. **Efficiency of loss-augmented inference:** The paper restricts search to a Hamming ball to handle tree depth (max 16, implying $2^{16}$ candidates). Given that greedy enumeration of $2^{16}$ splits remains computationally feasible, the impact of this restriction on performance needs rigorous evaluation. The supplementary result (Table S4) is insufficient; the authors should demonstrate whether the Hamming ball constraint generalizes to other datasets and clarify the term "leaves" (does it refer to tree leaves or something else?).  

4. **Missing baseline:** A critical baseline is absent: standard greedy training of non-axis-aligned trees (without the proposed joint training or initialization). This comparison is essential to isolate the contribution of the joint optimization.  

5. **Tree depth limitation:** The need to specify tree depth *a priori* is a significant constraint. The authors should discuss its practical implications (e.g., sensitivity to depth choice) and provide a thorough experimental analysis of depth as a hyperparameter (e.g., performance vs. depth).  

Additional Suggestions:  
- The authors’ clarification on replacing $\arg\max$ with $\max$ in Equation 7 is minor; the core issue is the incomplete bound proof.  
- A runtime comparison against standard greedy training would strengthen the empirical evaluation.  
- A more detailed analysis of the depth parameter’s effect on performance is needed.  

Final Assessment: The paper tackles an important problem with a technically interesting approach. However, the unresolved flaw in the loss bound derivation, coupled with limited experimental validation (missing baseline, depth analysis, runtime comparison), undermines confidence in the method’s validity and practical utility. The authors should address the proof gap and provide the suggested experiments in the rebuttal. I am willing to raise my score if these issues are resolved with a clear, technical rebuttal. The work has potential but requires significant strengthening before acceptance.
