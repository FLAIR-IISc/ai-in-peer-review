

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by optimizing split functions and leaf parameters jointly under a global objective, rather than using the standard greedy approach that optimizes each node independently. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key technical contribution is showing how to reduce the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p), making deep tree learning feasible.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by linking decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables, which is a novel perspective that opens up new possibilities for optimization. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the proposed surrogate objective provides a principled way to regularize the joint optimization of tree parameters. The connection to structured prediction is well-motivated and elegantly presented, making the paper an interesting addition to the literature on decision trees.

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, though some aspects of the methodology require clarification. The stable SGD (SSGD) variant is not sufficiently explained - it's unclear how data points are assigned to leaves, and providing pseudo-code would significantly improve the clarity. Regarding Figure 2, it would be helpful to specify whether the results were obtained using standard SGD or SSGD, and to define what constitutes an "active" leaf in the context of the method.

The empirical results are somewhat limited in their convincing power. While the non-greedy trees do outperform greedy baselines on some datasets (particularly SensIT and Protein), the gains are not consistent across the benchmark datasets tested (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST). On several datasets, including Connect4 and MNIST, the improvements are marginal or non-existent, which weakens the argument for the necessity of non-greedy optimization. The paper does not provide sufficient explanation for why the gains are only observed on certain datasets, nor does it thoroughly analyze the computational trade-offs in terms of training time versus accuracy gains.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent work on decision trees. While the authors compare with standard greedy baselines, they do not sufficiently contextualize their results within the broader landscape of decision tree research. For instance, the connection to recent work on Mondrian forests [Lakshminarayanan et al., 2014] or other non-greedy approaches could be strengthened. The comparison with CO2 [Norouzi et al., 2015] is helpful but limited to the specific case of depth-1 trees.

Minor issues that need addressing include:
- In line 10 of Algorithm 1, the projection of Θ onto a simplex is mentioned but not explained; it would be helpful to clarify how this projection is implemented in practice.
- The phrase "tune the number of features evaluated" in lines 407-408 is confusing, as standard decision trees evaluate all features at each node.
- Several equations contain "argmax" where "max" should be used (equations 7, 8, 9).
- Figure 1 is referenced but never discussed in the main text.
- There are several grammatical errors (e.g., "An key observation" should be "A key observation").

The paper's quality is strong theoretically, with sound mathematical formulation and clear technical contributions. The clarity is generally good, though some methodological details require further elaboration. The originality is high, as the connection to structured prediction with latent variables represents a novel perspective on decision tree learning. The significance is promising but not fully demonstrated, as the empirical results do not consistently show clear advantages over greedy approaches.

The work has potential significance for decision tree research, particularly in cases where global optimization might lead to more compact and generalizable trees. However, the current results do not sufficiently demonstrate this potential, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more comprehensive evaluation that demonstrates the robustness of the non-greedy approach across a wider range of datasets and problem settings, along with a clearer explanation of the conditions under which the method provides meaningful improvements over standard greedy methods.
