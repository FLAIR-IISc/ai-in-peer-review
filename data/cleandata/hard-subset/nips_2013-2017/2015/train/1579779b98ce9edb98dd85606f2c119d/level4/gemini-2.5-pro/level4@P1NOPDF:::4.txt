This paper proposes an algorithm for the global optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as latent structured prediction. In this formulation, latent structured variables are sign vectors that encode the decisions at each internal node. The authors define a non-differentiable and non-convex loss, which they approximate with a differentiable, non-convex upper bound. This objective is then minimized via SGD, which requires solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which the authors provide efficient algorithms. The experiments compare this method to a standard greedy training algorithm.

The key contributions are twofold: casting global decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem and devising an efficient algorithm to solve the resulting objective. This approach is novel and interesting, and it opens up several promising research directions.

The primary advantage highlighted by the authors is that this formulation enables optimization via SGD, which leverages the problem's structure through a sequence of loss-augmented inference subproblems. However, perhaps the most compelling consequence of this approach is that the gradient updates are sparse—if understood correctly, each gradient step modifies the weight vector of only a single node. While the paper frames this primarily as a computational benefit, this property suggests more profound possibilities that are not fully explored.

For instance, one could envision optimizing an infinitely deep tree by initializing all weights to zero. The tree could then be "grown" gradually by updating weights from zero to non-zero values, allowing the model's complexity to adapt naturally to the data. This raises fascinating questions: Would such a method be equivalent to, or a variation of, a known greedy strategy? This might be the case if it is always optimal to introduce a new node rather than revise an existing one. Alternatively, could appropriate regularization guide the method to converge to a finite tree, as Figure 2 seems to suggest? A deeper analysis of these points would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution by clarifying what makes the proposed method fundamentally different from or superior to standard greedy methods.

A critical issue that remains ambiguous is the handling of nodes with a zero weight vector. The discussion on page 3 implies that the right branch is taken by default, which seems like an arbitrary choice. A more natural alternative would be to treat such a node as a leaf that outputs a decision. Later in training, its weights could become non-zero, effectively splitting the node. A precise definition of this behavior is crucial, as it provides a direct analog to the pruning strategies used in greedy training and is central to the "tree growing" perspective mentioned above. It appears this case may be avoided in practice by random weight initialization, but exploring a zero-initialization scheme could be the most interesting future direction for this work.

A significant practical concern relates to the motivation for this work. Arguably, single decision trees are most useful for their interpretability. When interpretability is not a requirement, other methods such as random forests or gradient boosted trees generally offer far superior predictive performance. As proposed, the method uses L2 regularization, which is likely to produce dense weight vectors at each node, rendering the resulting tree uninterpretable. While switching to L1 regularization could encourage sparsity, it is unclear if this would achieve 1-sparse splits (i.e., traditional, axis-aligned splits) without a significant drop in performance. This is a critical trade-off that should be evaluated experimentally.

The experimental evaluation could also be strengthened. The paper compares against a standard greedy approach but provides no details about its implementation, such as which pruning strategies were used. Given that the proposed method was tested with a range of regularization parameters, the baseline should be afforded the same diligence, for instance by evaluating several pruning strategies or parameters. Furthermore, it would be valuable to include other simple baselines for global optimization. One example would be to train multiple greedy trees on random subsets of the data and select the best one based on performance on the full training or a validation set.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written, but the description of the inference procedure could be improved. For example, Section 6.2 could be more concisely summarized by stating that the objective is first maximized over all paths `g` leading to a given leaf, and then this result is maximized over all leaves. Section 6.4 could also be more explicit in its explanation. Finally, it should be noted that while the title mentions "forests," the paper only develops the method for single trees.

In summary, this paper presents an innovative and thought-provoking approach to decision tree optimization. However, its practical utility remains unclear. The potential loss of interpretability raises questions about why one would use this method over other high-performance models, and the experimental results are not yet convincing due to a lack of detail and comparison to adequately tuned baselines.
