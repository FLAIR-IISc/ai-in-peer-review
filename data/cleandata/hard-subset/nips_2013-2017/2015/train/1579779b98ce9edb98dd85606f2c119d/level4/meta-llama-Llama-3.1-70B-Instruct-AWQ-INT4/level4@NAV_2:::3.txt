The authors propose an interesting way to search for the best decision tree of a specific depth by posing it as a structured prediction problem. This approach enables the global optimization of decision trees, which has been a challenging task due to the discrete and sequential nature of the splitting decisions within the tree. The proposed method formulates the decision tree learning problem as a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, which can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the formulation of the decision tree learning problem, the derivation of the convex-concave upper bound, and the optimization procedure. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms typical greedy approaches for decision tree building in terms of test set accuracy. However, the improvements tend to be small, and it is not clear whether the proposed method is significantly better than other existing methods.

One of the strengths of the paper is that it provides a new perspective on decision tree learning by posing it as a structured prediction problem. This approach has the potential to lead to new insights and improvements in decision tree learning. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is important for understanding its scalability.

However, there are a few limitations to the paper. First, the experimental results are limited to a few datasets, and it is not clear whether the proposed method will generalize well to other datasets. Second, the paper does not provide a detailed comparison with other existing methods, such as random forests or gradient boosting machines, which are widely used in practice. Finally, the paper does not discuss the interpretability of the decision trees learned by the proposed method, which is an important aspect of decision tree learning.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a new perspective on decision tree learning. However, it would benefit from more extensive experimental results, a detailed comparison with other existing methods, and a discussion of the interpretability of the learned decision trees.
