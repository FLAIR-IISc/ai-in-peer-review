This submission presents an algorithm for the global optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as latent structured prediction. In this framework, the latent variables are sign vectors that encode the branching decisions at each internal node. The authors propose a non-differentiable and non-convex loss function, which they approximate with a differentiable, non-convex upper bound. This objective is then minimized using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), where each step requires solving a loss-augmented inference problem, for which the paper provides efficient algorithms. The experimental evaluation compares the proposed method against a standard greedy training approach.

The primary contributions are the novel reformulation of global decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem and the development of efficient algorithms for the resulting objective. This approach is compelling and raises several interesting questions.

A key advantage of the structured prediction framework is that it enables optimization via SGD, where each update is derived from solving a loss-augmented inference problem that exploits the problem's structure. However, the most significant consequence of this approach appears to be the sparsity of the gradient updates; each step modifies the weight vector of only a single node. While the paper frames this primarily as a computational benefit, this property suggests deeper conceptual possibilities that are not fully explored. For instance, this sparsity allows for optimizing an effectively infinite tree by initializing all weights to zero and incrementally activating nodes by making their weights non-zero. This would allow the model's complexity to adapt to the data. This raises fundamental questions that warrant further analysis:
*   Would this procedure be equivalent to, or a variation of, existing greedy strategies?
*   Could it be that introducing a new node is always preferable to revising an existing one?
*   Alternatively, with appropriate regularization, would this method naturally converge to a finite-sized tree, as Figure 2 seems to suggest?
A more thorough analysis of these points would strengthen the paper's contribution by clarifying the fundamental advantages of this method over standard decision tree induction algorithms.

A critical ambiguity in the current formulation is the handling of nodes with zero-weight vectors. The paper suggests a default right-branch decision, which seems arbitrary. A more principled approach might be to treat such a node as a leaf, which could later become an internal node if its weights become non-zero. A precise definition is crucial, as this mechanism is analogous to pruning in greedy training methods.

I also have two main practical concerns. First, a primary motivation for using single decision trees is their interpretability. Without this feature, other methods often provide superior predictive performance. The proposed method, particularly with L2 regularization, is likely to produce dense weight vectors at each node, resulting in non-interpretable splits. While L1 regularization could enforce sparsity, it is unclear whether this can be achieved without a significant drop in performance. This trade-off between sparsity for interpretability and predictive accuracy requires experimental validation.

Second, the experimental comparison to baselines lacks sufficient detail. The paper does not specify which pruning strategies were employed for the greedy baseline. To ensure a fair comparison, the baseline should be tuned with various pruning strategies or parameters, just as the proposed method was evaluated over a range of regularization parameters. Furthermore, the evaluation would benefit from including other simple global optimization heuristics, such as training an ensemble of greedy trees on random data subsets and selecting the best-performing one on a validation set.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written, but the sections describing the inference algorithms could be clearer. For example, the procedure in Section 6.2 could be more concisely described as a two-stage maximization: first over all paths `g` leading to a specific leaf, and then over all leaves. Similarly, the description in Section 6.4 could benefit from greater detail.

In summary, this paper introduces a clever and thought-provoking approach to decision tree optimization. However, its practical utility remains uncertain due to concerns about the interpretability of the resulting trees and the lack of a more comprehensive experimental evaluation.

PS: The title mentions "forests," but this topic is not addressed in the paper.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
The handling of zero-weight vectors remains a critical issue that requires further analysis, for the reasons outlined in the initial review. The rebuttal suggests that weights are initialized randomly, which likely circumvents this problem in practice. Nevertheless, the most interesting application of this method may be a "tree-growing" approach where weights are initialized at zero and then updated. I strongly encourage the authors to explore this direction for future work. While the paper's reformulation of global decision tree optimization as structured prediction and its associated efficient algorithm are clever, the motivation and experimental validation are not yet fully convincing.
