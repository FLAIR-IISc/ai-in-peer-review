This paper proposes a novel algorithm for the global, non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The core idea is to reframe the optimization problem as structured prediction with latent variables, where the latent variables are binary vectors encoding the sequence of decisions made at each internal node. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's non-differentiable empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective. This objective is then minimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which requires solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems. The paper presents efficient algorithms for this inference step. Experimental results demonstrate that this method outperforms standard greedy training procedures.

The key contributions are the elegant formulation of global decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem and the development of an efficient algorithm to optimize the resulting objective. This approach is novel and thought-provoking.

**Strengths:**

A major advantage of casting the optimization as structured prediction is the ability to leverage SGD by solving a sequence of tractable loss-augmented inference problems. A particularly interesting consequence of this formulation, which the paper presents mainly as a computational benefit, is the sparsity of the gradient updates. If I understand correctly, each gradient step only modifies the weight vector of a single node. This property suggests intriguing possibilities that are not fully explored. One could envision an optimization process that begins with an infinitely deep tree where all weights are initialized to zero. The model's complexity could then grow naturally to fit the data as the optimization makes more weights non-zero. This raises fascinating theoretical questions: would such a method be equivalent to, or a variation of, a known greedy strategy? Or, with appropriate regularization, would it converge to a tree with a finite number of nodes, as Figure 2 seems to suggest? This line of inquiry cuts to the core of what makes the proposed method a valuable alternative to standard decision trees and deserves more thorough analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Practical Motivation and Interpretability:** A significant concern relates to the practical motivation for this work. A primary advantage of single decision trees over more powerful ensemble methods is their interpretability. The proposed method, however, learns oblique splits with L2 regularization, which typically results in dense weight vectors. This makes the resulting tree difficult to interpret, thereby diminishing a key reason for using a single decision tree. If interpretability is not a goal, it is unclear why one would not turn to established high-performance methods like random forests or gradient boosting. The paper could be strengthened by exploring whether sparsity-inducing regularization (e.g., L1) could restore interpretability by producing sparse (e.g., axis-aligned) splits without a significant loss in performance.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental comparison to baselines could be more thorough. The paper does not specify which, if any, post-pruning strategies were employed for the greedy baseline methods. Since a range of regularization parameters was tuned for the proposed method, a fair comparison would require tuning the corresponding hyperparameters for the baselines (such as pruning parameters) with the same rigor. Furthermore, the evaluation would be more convincing if it included other simple heuristics for global optimization, such as training multiple greedy trees on random subsets of the data and selecting the best-performing one on a validation set.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is reasonably well-written, but the sections describing the inference algorithms could be clearer. For example, the explanation of loss-augmented inference could be improved by first providing a high-level summary—stating that the objective is maximized over all paths `g` leading to a given leaf, and this result is then maximized over all possible leaves—before diving into the algorithmic details. Similarly, the discussion on the fast inference variant could be more explicit about the trade-offs involved. A critical issue that remains ambiguous is the handling of nodes whose weight vectors are zero. The text implies that the right branch is taken by default, which seems arbitrary. A more principled approach, perhaps outputting a decision at that node, would be preferable, as this has a direct analogy to pruning in greedy methods.

4.  **Scope:** While the abstract mentions randomized forests, the paper's methods and experiments focus exclusively on single decision trees. It would be helpful to clarify if or how the proposed framework could be extended to ensembles.

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a clever and theoretically elegant approach to the challenging problem of global decision tree optimization. The connection to latent structured prediction is insightful and opens up promising avenues for future research. However, the practical significance of the method is currently undermined by concerns about the interpretability of the resulting trees and an experimental evaluation that could be more comprehensive in its comparison to baselines. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.
