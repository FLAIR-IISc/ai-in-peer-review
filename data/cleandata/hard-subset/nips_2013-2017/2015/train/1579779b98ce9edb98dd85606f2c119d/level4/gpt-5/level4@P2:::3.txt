Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a non-greedy method for training oblique decision trees by jointly optimizing all split functions and leaf parameters under a single global objective. The core idea is to recast split decisions as latent binary variables and view tree training as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss and optimize it via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference procedure based on restricting to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current routing, which reduces the complexity of per-example gradient computations from exponential in depth to O(d^2 p), making deep trees feasible. They also propose a “Stable SGD” variant that stabilizes leaf assignments. Experiments on several LibSVM benchmarks show that the resulting trees generally outperform greedy baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, and a related CO2 approach) with improved generalization and reduced overfitting.

This work connects classic greedy decision tree induction (ID3/CART) and oblique trees (e.g., OC1) with structured prediction (max-margin Markov networks, structural SVMs, and latent structural SVMs). It also relates to margin-based trees (using SVMs at splits), soft probabilistic trees such as Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts, and recent decision forest variants (e.g., Mondrian forests). The paper builds on the CO2 framework but differs by jointly optimizing all splits using a global surrogate objective.

Strengths:
- Novel and well-motivated formulation: establishing a link between tree training and latent structured prediction, with a clear surrogate objective.
- Technical contribution in efficiency: the Hamming-ball restriction yields an O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference, enabling scalable SGD for deep trees.
- Practical algorithm: per-example gradients are simple, constraints on row norms of W act as regularization, and initialization from a greedy tree is straightforward.
- Empirical results show consistent, if modest, gains over strong greedy baselines across datasets and depths, and reduced overfitting.
- The framework accommodates general loss functions (classification and regression) while retaining O(dp) inference at test time.

Weaknesses:
- Improvements in test accuracy are often small; the practical significance relative to well-tuned greedy trees may be limited.
- Experimental scope is narrow: primarily single trees on a handful of datasets; no evaluation on ensembles (RF, boosted trees), which are the standard baselines in practice.
- Dependence on greedy initialization suggests sensitivity to local minima; ablations on initialization quality and SSGD vs. SGD are limited.
- Limited analysis of hyperparameter ν (row-norm constraint): while empirically important for pruning and generalization, guidance and theory are sparse.
- Comparisons against modern boosted oblique trees or gradient boosting (contemporary strong baselines) are missing; time–accuracy trade-offs beyond one dataset are not fully reported.
- Claims about handling general losses and regression are not supported by experiments.

Quality:
- The surrogate bound and optimization are technically sound, with clear derivations and an intuitive link to latent structured prediction. The complexity reductions are plausible and well-argued. Nonconvexity is acknowledged; the training procedure is reasonable and stable in practice. Some proofs are deferred to supplemental material.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and organized, with helpful notation and an algorithm outline. The discussion of the bound’s dependence on the scale of W and the role of ν is insightful, though it could benefit from clearer intuition and more empirical guidance. More detail on experimental setup (hyperparameters, runtime on all datasets, variability across runs) would aid reproducibility.

Originality:
- The joint non-greedy optimization of all splits via a latent-structured objective and the fast loss-augmented inference are novel relative to prior oblique/CO2 methods. The SSGD stabilization is a practical addition.

Significance:
- For researchers working on decision trees and oblique splits, the method is a meaningful advance, offering a principled, scalable alternative to greedy induction with measurable gains. Broader impact may be constrained by modest improvements and the dominance of ensemble methods in applications, but the approach could inform better single-tree learners and potentially stronger forests.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include comparisons to strong ensemble baselines (e.g., gradient boosting, random forests with oblique splits) and report both accuracy and runtime.
- Provide ablations on initialization, ν, and SSGD; report variance over multiple random seeds.
- Evaluate regression tasks and higher-dimensional datasets to substantiate generality.
- Expand runtime comparisons across all datasets and deeper trees.
- Analyze the potential bias introduced by the Hamming-radius-1 bound and its effect on solution quality.

Pros:
- Principled non-greedy formulation via latent structured prediction.
- Efficient, scalable loss-augmented inference (O(d^2 p)).
- Consistent improvements and reduced overfitting across datasets.
- Supports general loss functions and retains efficient inference.

Cons:
- Accuracy gains are modest; limited practical advantage over tuned greedy methods.
- Experimental evaluation lacks ensembles and broader baselines.
- Sensitivity to initialization and regularization not fully characterized.
- Limited empirical support for regression/general-loss claims.

Overall assessment:
A solid and well-executed paper with a clear methodological contribution and consistent empirical gains. While the improvements are not dramatic and the evaluation could be broader, the approach is principled and likely of interest to the NIPS community. I lean toward a weak accept, contingent on strengthening the experimental comparisons and analysis.
