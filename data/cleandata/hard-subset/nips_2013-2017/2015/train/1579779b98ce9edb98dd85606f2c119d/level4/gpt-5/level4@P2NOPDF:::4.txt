This paper reformulates global decision tree optimization as a latent structured prediction problem, where latent variables are sign vectors encoding internal-node decisions. It introduces a non-differentiable, non-convex loss and optimizes a differentiable (though still non-convex) upper bound via SGD. Each SGD step requires a loss-augmented inference problem, for which efficient procedures are provided. Experiments compare the method to a standard greedy training baseline. The main contributions are the structured-prediction formulation of global DT optimization and the design of efficient optimization algorithms. The approach is novel and thought-provoking.

Strengths. Beyond enabling SGD through loss-augmented inference that exploits problem structure, the most compelling aspect is the sparsity of the gradient updates: if I understand correctly, each step modifies only a single node’s weight vector. While presented primarily as a computational benefit, this property suggests richer modeling possibilities. For instance, one could consider an “infinitely deep” tree initialized with all-zero weights and grow it adaptively by activating weights as needed, letting model complexity increase with the data. This raises important questions: under what conditions would the procedure effectively mimic or refine known greedy strategies? Is it generally better to introduce a new node than to revise an existing node’s parameters? With suitable regularization, would the method converge to a compact tree (as hinted by Fig. 2)? These issues go to the heart of why this approach might be preferable to naive DTs and merit deeper analysis.

Ambiguity around zero weights. A critical edge case is what happens when a node’s weight vector is zero. The paper (p.3) seems to resolve ties by going right, which is unsatisfying. A more principled alternative would be to output a decision at such a node (i.e., treat it as a leaf), with the option to later make the weight nonzero and split the node. Precisely specifying this behavior matters, both conceptually and because it closely parallels pruning strategies used in greedy training.

Practical motivation and interpretability. A major motivation gap concerns interpretability. Plain decision trees are most valuable when they are human-interpretable; if interpretability is not required, alternative models typically deliver superior accuracy. As presented, L2 regularization will generally produce dense node weights, sacrificing interpretability. L1 regularization might restore sparsity and interpretability, but could also reduce accuracy if enforced strongly enough to yield essentially 1-sparse splits. This trade-off should be tested empirically and discussed explicitly, as it directly affects whether the method is a practical alternative to off-the-shelf non-interpretable models.

Experimental evaluation. The experimental section lacks detail on baselines and may understate the difficulty of fair comparison. In particular:
- The greedy baseline’s pruning strategy (and its hyperparameters) are not specified. Since the proposed method is tuned over regularization parameters, the greedy baseline should likewise be evaluated across multiple pruning strategies/strengths.
- Additional simple baselines for “global” selection should be included, such as training multiple greedy trees on random subsets or bootstraps and selecting the model that minimizes the target loss on the full training or a validation set.
- Clarify whether all methods share identical feature preprocessing, depth constraints, and stopping criteria.

Clarity. The paper is generally clear, but the inference sections could be streamlined:
- Section 6.2 could be summarized as: first maximize the objective over all g associated with a fixed leaf, then maximize over leaves.
- Section 6.4 would benefit from a more explicit step-by-step description of the loss-augmented inference and how each step yields a sparse parameter update (i.e., which node is updated and why).

Summary. This is a clever and original formulation of global decision tree training with efficient optimization and intriguing properties, especially the sparsity of SGD updates. However, its practical value is not yet clear due to (i) unresolved interpretability concerns—arguably the main reason to prefer decision trees in the first place—and (ii) limited detail and breadth in the experimental comparisons. Strengthening the discussion of zero-weight behavior, exploring grow-as-needed training under zero initialization with appropriate regularization, and expanding/clarifying the baselines would significantly improve the case for the method.

PS: Although the title mentions forests, the paper does not treat that setting.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
I still view the zero-weight case as fundamental. The rebuttal suggests this issue does not arise in practice because weights are randomly initialized. Nevertheless, the most interesting regime, in my view, is to start from zero weights and grow capacity gradually. This could connect the method to principled tree growth and pruning and exploit the sparse-update property more fully. I encourage the authors to analyze and experiment with this direction.

In sum, the submission offers a smart reformulation and an efficient algorithm, but the motivation and empirical support need to be strengthened to establish practical impact.
