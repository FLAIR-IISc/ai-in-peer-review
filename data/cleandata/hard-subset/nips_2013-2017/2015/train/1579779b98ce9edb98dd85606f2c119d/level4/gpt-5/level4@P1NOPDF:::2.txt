Summary: The paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters of decision trees (both decision splits and leaf posterior probabilities). This is an important direction, as most existing methods train trees greedily, layer by layer. The task is formulated as a single optimization problem that is then upper-bounded and approximated to obtain a tractable surrogate.

Quality: The paper is generally well written, but there may be flaws in the reasoning (see below). 
Clarity: The derivations are clean, though the experiments and their presentation could be improved. 
Originality: Studying joint training for decision trees is an important and timely topic. 
Significance: The proposed approach is valuable, though there appear to be some shortcomings (see below).

Comments:
- Possible flaw in the empirical loss bound derivation. At minimum, the argument around lines 216f is confusing: “... for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right-hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$ ...” Consider the example:
  W\bx = [5; -5]
  ⇒ $\hat \bh = [1; -1] = \sgn(W\bx)$
  ⇒ $\hat \bh^\top W\bx = 10$
  Choosing $\bg = [1; 1]$ yields $\bg^\top W\bx = 0$. Therefore, the right-hand side of Eq. (7) is
  $\bg^\top W\bx - \hat \bh^\top W\bx + \ell(\cdot) = -10 + \ell(\cdot)$,
  which could be smaller than $\ell(\cdot)$ and thus would not constitute a valid upper bound. Note also that the proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not cover this case and appears incomplete.
- Why are some leaves assigned no data points? This is unexpected and may indicate a problem with the objective or the initialization. The suggested heuristic of fixing data-to-leaf assignments seems suboptimal, though it might be justifiable via an EM/CCCP-style argument, similar to other clustering methods. Providing such a motivation (or an ablation) would help.
- Efficient loss-augmented inference: How critical is the specialized design here? With depth at most 16, a naive exhaustive check over $2^{16} = 65{,}536$ assignments still seems feasible. How much does the Hamming-ball-restricted search space affect performance and optimality in practice? You report one result in the supplement; does it generalize across datasets and depths? Also, what precisely does “leaves” refer to in that context?
- Missing baseline: Please include a baseline that trains non–axis-aligned split functions in the standard greedy manner, i.e., without any special initialization, to better contextualize gains from joint training.
- Tree depth must be specified a priori. This limitation deserves a more careful discussion, along with a more thorough empirical evaluation of sensitivity to the depth parameter.
