

This paper presents a novel non-greedy approach for optimizing decision trees by jointly optimizing split functions at all tree levels with a global objective, rather than the conventional greedy one-node-at-a-time procedure. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. This surrogate objective enables efficient training using stochastic gradient descent with computational complexity O(d²p), where d is tree depth and p is input dimension, making deep tree learning feasible. The approach differs from prior work (e.g., Bennett's non-greedy multi-linear programming approach) by handling general loss functions while maintaining the computational advantages of tree structures.

The paper demonstrates significant technical quality through its elegant theoretical formulation, rigorous complexity analysis, and well-justified optimization approach. The key contribution of reducing complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) is particularly impressive, and the connection to structured prediction with latent variables provides a principled framework for non-greedy tree optimization. The method is well-motivated, with clear explanations of the theoretical underpinnings and algorithmic details.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making the complex technical content accessible to readers. The mathematical formulations are precise and well-explained, with sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results. The experimental section, while somewhat limited, is presented with appropriate methodological rigor.

The originality of the work is strong, as it introduces a fundamentally different approach to decision tree learning that addresses a long-standing limitation of greedy methods. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is particularly novel, and the paper properly situates its contributions relative to prior work (e.g., Bennett's work, soft decision trees, and hierarchical mixture models).

Regarding significance, the approach has the potential to advance the state of the art in decision tree learning by enabling more effective global optimization. The method could inspire further research on non-greedy tree learning and might lead to improved tree-based models in applications where decision trees are currently used. However, the experimental results, while consistent with the theoretical claims, are somewhat marginal compared to greedy baselines across multiple datasets, which somewhat limits the immediate practical impact.

The paper has several notable strengths: the elegant theoretical formulation, the clear exposition, the thorough computational complexity analysis, and the well-motivated connection to structured prediction. The experimental results, while promising, are somewhat disappointing in terms of the magnitude of improvement over baselines, particularly when compared to the substantial theoretical contribution.

I have two specific suggestions to improve the paper. First, the authors might consider a simple post-processing step after global optimization: pruning the last split in each tree and optimizing it and its leaf node using conventional methods, which could potentially yield better results. Second, the paper would benefit from a more thorough investigation of overfitting behavior, as global optimization might inherently lead to more overfitting compared to greedy methods, and this should be explicitly evaluated.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper with elegant theoretical contributions that should stimulate further research in non-greedy decision tree learning. While the experimental results don't demonstrate dramatic improvements over baselines, the theoretical foundation is compelling and the approach is likely to be influential in future work. The paper's clarity and technical soundness make it a valuable contribution to the field, and I believe it should be accepted for publication at NIPS.
