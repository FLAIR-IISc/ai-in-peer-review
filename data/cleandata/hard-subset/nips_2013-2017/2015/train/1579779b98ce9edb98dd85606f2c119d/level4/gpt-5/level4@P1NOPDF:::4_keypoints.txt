- Reformulates global decision-tree optimization as latent structured prediction using sign vectors for internal-node decisions.

- Proposes a non-differentiable, non-convex loss with a differentiable (still non-convex) upper-bound surrogate optimized by SGD via loss-augmented inference.

- Provides efficient algorithms for the resulting loss-augmented inference problems.

- Core contributions are the structured-prediction formulation and associated efficient optimization procedures; the approach is novel and thought-provoking.

- Gradient updates are sparse (each SGD step changes only a single node’s weight), which is presented as a computational benefit but has deeper implications.

- Suggests the possibility of starting with an all-zero, infinitely deep tree and growing it adaptively by activating weights, a direction not explored in the paper.

- Raises questions about whether adaptive growth reduces to known greedy strategies, whether adding nodes is preferable to revising nodes, and how regularization affects final tree size.

- Critical ambiguity: handling of exactly zero node-weight vectors is underspecified (paper defaults to right branch), and a principled alternative (emit decision and split later) should be defined due to ties with pruning.

- Practical concern about interpretability: L2 regularization likely yields dense, non-interpretable trees; trade-offs with L1 regularization and performance impacts need empirical evaluation.

- Experimental section lacks detail on greedy baselines and pruning strategies; greedy baselines should be tuned comparably and include simple global baselines (e.g., many greedy trees on random subsets).

- Clarity is generally good, but Sections 6.2 and 6.4 (inference/optimization flow) could be clearer and more explicit.

- Overall: clever and efficient formulation, but practical utility is uncertain due to interpretability issues, limited experimental analysis, and the paper’s title mentioning forests without addressing them.

- Post-rebuttal point: zero-initialization remains a critical case; random initialization may avoid it empirically, but exploring zero-start adaptive growth that leverages sparse updates is encouraged.
