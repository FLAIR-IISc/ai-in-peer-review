

This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach for optimizing decision trees by jointly optimizing split functions at all tree levels under a global objective. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. Their key technical contribution is showing how to reduce the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) through a modified surrogate objective, making deep tree training feasible with stochastic gradient descent. The approach is theoretically grounded, with a proof showing the upper bound tightens as the norm of the weight matrix increases, and the authors provide an efficient algorithm for loss-augmented inference.

The paper makes a strong case for the novelty of its approach. While prior work on non-greedy tree optimization (e.g., Bennett [1]) has existed, it was limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss and high computational complexity. The authors correctly note that previous approaches either suffer from exponential complexity (Bennett [1]) or sacrifice the computational advantages of decision trees (Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13]). Their formulation handles any general loss function while maintaining the O(dp) inference complexity, representing a significant advance over prior art.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound. The theoretical development is rigorous, with careful attention to the connection between decision trees and structured prediction. The authors provide a detailed proof of their upper bound and its relationship to the empirical loss. The algorithmic details are well-explained, including the efficient loss-augmented inference procedure that enables practical implementation. The paper's main limitation is in the experimental evaluation, where the performance gains over greedy baselines are modest across multiple datasets.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The writing is precise and well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and helpful figures. The authors effectively explain the connection between decision trees and structured prediction, which is crucial for understanding their approach. The technical exposition is accessible to experts in machine learning, and the paper would be straightforward to reproduce given the level of detail provided.

The originality of the work is strong. The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is novel, and the authors properly contextualize their contribution within the broader literature. They cite relevant prior work on non-greedy tree optimization (Bennett [1], Murthy and Salzburg [17]), structured prediction (Yu and Joachims [25]), and decision tree methods (Breiman [4], CART [5]), demonstrating a thorough understanding of the field. The approach is distinct from existing methods that use soft splits [13] or incremental tree building [15], and it extends the CO2 approach [18] to truly global optimization.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the experimental results. While the approach is theoretically elegant and represents a principled advance in decision tree optimization, the empirical improvements over greedy baselines are modest across multiple benchmarks. The authors acknowledge this limitation, noting that "non-greedy trees achieve the best test performance across tree depths across multiple datasets" but don't provide strong evidence of substantial improvements. The comparison with CO2 [18] shows only marginal gains, which suggests the non-greedy optimization may not be as impactful as the authors hope.

The paper makes a compelling case for the elegance of the approach, but the experimental results are somewhat disappointing. The authors acknowledge that their method might lead to more overfitting compared to greedy approaches, though they mitigate this with norm regularization. The claim that the method "leads to better generalization performance" would be more convincing with stronger evidence. The modest performance gains across datasets suggest that the theoretical advantages may not translate to significant practical benefits.

Strengths:
- Novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables
- Rigorous theoretical development with a tight upper bound
- Efficient algorithm with O(d²p) complexity for deep trees
- Well-written with clear exposition and good organization
- Extends prior work (CO2 [18]) to truly global optimization

Weaknesses:
- Modest experimental improvements over greedy baselines
- Limited evidence of substantial generalization gains
- No thorough analysis of when the non-greedy approach provides significant advantages
- The claim of "better generalization" is not strongly supported by results
- The paper could have better addressed the trade-off between complexity and performance gains

This paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to decision tree optimization that is likely to stimulate further research on non-greedy tree learning. While the experimental results don't fully justify the theoretical elegance, the approach is sufficiently novel and well-executed to warrant publication. The modest performance gains across multiple datasets are understandable given the inherent difficulty of the problem, and the authors provide a principled framework for future work. I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field, and I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the practical benefits may require further exploration in subsequent work. The paper's clarity and theoretical depth significantly outweigh the limitations in the empirical evaluation.
