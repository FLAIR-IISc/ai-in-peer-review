Summary: This paper proposes a method for the joint training of all parameters – decision splits and posterior probabilities – of decision trees. This is a significant problem, as current approaches typically train trees greedily, layer by layer. The authors formulate the task as a single optimization problem, then employ upper bounding and approximation techniques to arrive at a tractable formulation.

Quality – The paper is generally well-written, though the reasoning contains potential flaws detailed below. Clarity – The derivation is clean; however, the presentation of experiments could be improved. Originality – Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important and novel direction. Significance – The proposed solution is valuable, despite some apparent shortcomings, as discussed further below.

Comments:
- There appears to be a flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound. Specifically, the argument in lines 216-217 is unclear: “…for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$…” Consider the following example: Let $W\bx = [5;-5]$, then $\hat \bh = [1;-1] = \sgn(W\bx)$, and $\hat \bh^T W\bx = 10$. If we choose $g = [1;1]$, then $g^T W\bx = 0$. Consequently, the right hand side of Eq. (7) becomes $g^T W\bx - \hat \bh^T W\bx + \ell(...) = -10 + \ell(...)$, which could potentially be *smaller* than $\ell(...)$ and thus invalidate the upper bound. Note that the proof of Prop. 1 in the supplementary material does not address this case and is therefore incomplete.
- The observation that some leaves are not assigned any data points is concerning and may indicate a problem with the objective function or the initialization procedure. The authors’ heuristic of a fixed data point assignment to tree leaves seems sub-optimal, but could potentially be justified through an expectation maximization or concave-convex procedure, analogous to other clustering techniques.
- The importance of the design of the efficient loss-augmented inference procedure is unclear. Given that the tree depth is at most 16, exhaustively checking $2^{16} = 65536$ values still appears feasible. How significantly did the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball impact performance? The supplementary material provides one result, but it’s unclear if this generalizes. Furthermore, the meaning of ‘leaves’ in this context should be clarified.
- A baseline comparing against a standard greedy training approach for non-axis aligned split functions is missing. This would provide crucial context for evaluating the proposed method.
- The tree depth must be specified *a priori*. This limitation should be discussed more thoroughly, and the authors should provide a more detailed experimental evaluation varying the depth parameter.

--------------- Thanks to the authors for their clear explanation of the loss bound (Eq. 7). I recommend replacing $\arg\max$ with $\max$. As stated in my summary, I have adjusted my score accordingly. 

An experiment demonstrating runtime performance would strengthen the paper. A more comprehensive evaluation of the method would be beneficial for readers. The submission addresses an interesting problem – the joint training of all decision tree parameters – but the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and a potential flaw exists in the derivation. I am willing to reconsider my score pending a thorough rebuttal addressing these points.
