- Novel formulation: casts global decision-tree optimization as structured prediction with latent variables for internal-node decisions.  
- Loss approximation: introduces a non-differentiable, non-convex loss and optimizes a differentiable upper bound.  
- Optimization method: uses stochastic gradient descent requiring repeated loss-augmented inference problems.  
- Algorithms: provides efficient algorithms to solve the loss-augmented inference steps.  
- Sparse updates: gradient updates are sparse (modify one node weight per step), enabling potential optimization of very deep trees by gradually introducing non-zero weights.  
- Key contribution: reframing tree learning as structured prediction and devising efficient optimization algorithms is conceptually interesting.  
- Motivation/performance: unclear motivation and insufficient evidence that performance gains over naive/greedy methods are significant.  
- Infinite-depth idea: gradual weight introduction could let model complexity grow naturally, but may be equivalent to greedy strategies or require specific regularization to limit nodes.  
- Zero-weight handling: treatment of zero weights (implicit “take right branch”) is questionable and needs precise definition; alternative is outputting a decision and later introducing non-zero weights.  
- Interpretability: L2 regularization may produce non-interpretable trees; switching to L1 could help but might hurt performance—needs testing.  
- Experimental gaps: experiments lack detail on baselines and pruning strategies; trivial global-optimization baselines (e.g., greedy training on random subsets) are not explored.  
- Clarity: paper is generally clear but some sections (notably inference) need clearer exposition.  
- Overall: method is clever and thought-provoking, but practical utility is uncertain and requires further analysis and more thorough experimental validation.
