The submission proposes an algorithm for global optimization of decision trees based on a reformulation of the problem as (latent) structured prediction. The latent structured variables are sign vectors encoding the decisions made at each internal node in the tree. A non-differentiable and non-convex loss is proposed, which is then approximated by a differentiable (but still non-convex) upper bound. Minimizing this loss via Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) entails solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are given. Experiments are shown comparing this method to a standard greedy training method.

The key contributions of the work are casting the global Decision Tree (DT) optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms to optimize the objective. The approach is novel and interesting, raising several thought-provoking questions. However, I have some concerns regarding practical considerations, particularly the motivation for the method and the significance of the performance gains over naive methods.

Considered purely as a method to globally optimize DTs, the approach is intriguing in several ways. One advantage gained by casting the optimization as structured prediction is the ability to perform SGD by solving a sequence of loss-augmented optimization problems that leverage the special structure of the problem. Furthermore, the gradient updates are sparse, with each gradient step involving changes to only one node's weight vector. This property raises interesting possibilities, such as optimizing over an infinitely deep tree by initially setting all weights to zero and gradually growing the tree by making more weights nonzero, allowing the model's complexity to grow naturally to fit the data.

This raises several questions, including whether such a method would prove equivalent to or a variation of any known greedy strategy, and whether, with appropriate regularization, it would converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes. I believe this issue is crucial in understanding what makes the proposed method worthwhile compared to naive DTs and deserves more careful analysis.

One critical issue left ambiguous is what happens when some node's weight is equal to zero. The discussion implies that we take the right branch, which seems like a dubious choice. It would be preferable to output a decision at this node, allowing for the possibility of making this weight nonzero later, corresponding to splitting the node. Precisely defining what happens in this case is essential, as it has a direct analog to pruning strategies for greedy training.

I also have a significant practical concern regarding the motivation for the work. Plain DTs are useful mainly due to their interpretability; if this requirement is dropped, other methods generally offer better performance. I expect the method to produce non-interpretable results, as the L2 regularization would produce dense weight vectors. Although switching to L1 regularization might help, it is possible that performance would drop if we were forced to regularize to the point where exactly 1-sparse splits were produced. This would need to be tested.

Another concern is that the experiments lack detail regarding other baselines. No information is provided on the pruning strategies employed for the naive approach. Since a range of regularization parameters were tried for the proposed method, I would expect the naive method to be similarly tried with a few different pruning strategies and/or parameters. It would also be fair to try other trivial strategies for global optimization, such as training a greedy method with random subsets of the training data and choosing the one that minimizes the desired loss on the full training or validation set.

Regarding clarity, the paper is reasonably well-written. However, the parts describing inference could be clearer. Section 6.2 could be summarized by stating that the objective is first maximized for all g corresponding to a given leaf, which is then maximized over all leaves. Section 6.4 could also be more explicit.

In summary, I think this is a clever approach that raises interesting questions. However, it is unclear whether this is a practically useful method at this point, both due to the interpretability issue and the lack of details in the experiments.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS

I still believe that the case of zero weights is a critical case that deserves further analysis. Reading the rebuttal, it sounds like the weights are probably initialized randomly, which is why this issue doesn't seem to come up in practice. However, I think what would really make this method interesting would be the case where the weights are initially zero and are gradually increased. I encourage the authors to consider this direction.
