This paper presents an algorithm for the global optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as one of latent structured prediction. Within this framework, the latent variables are sign vectors that encode the branching decisions at each of the tree's internal nodes. The authors propose a non-convex and non-differentiable loss function, which is then relaxed to a differentiable, though still non-convex, upper bound. This objective is minimized via Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), a process that requires solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which the paper provides efficient algorithms. The experimental evaluation compares this approach to a standard greedy training method.

The primary contributions are the novel formulation of global decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem and the development of efficient algorithms to optimize the corresponding objective. This approach is both original and thought-provoking, raising several interesting questions for future research.

However, I have some reservations regarding the method's practical considerations, particularly concerning its motivation and the significance of the performance gains over standard methods.

From a methodological perspective, the approach to globally optimizing decision trees is intriguing in several respects. The formulation as a structured prediction problem enables optimization via SGD by leveraging the problem's unique structure to solve a series of loss-augmented inference subproblems.

Yet, I believe the most compelling consequence of this approach—one that is understated in the paper—is the sparsity of the gradient updates. If I understand correctly, each gradient step modifies the weight vector of only a single node. While the authors present this primarily as a computational advantage, this property suggests more profound possibilities that are not fully explored.

For example, one could envision optimizing an infinitely deep tree by initializing all weights to zero and then organically growing the tree by making weights non-zero, thereby allowing the model's complexity to adapt naturally to the data. This line of thinking leads to fundamental questions: Would such a procedure be equivalent to, or a variation of, a known greedy strategy? This might be the case if it is always more advantageous to introduce a new node than to update an existing one. Alternatively, could appropriate regularization guide the method to converge to a tree of a finite size, as Figure 2 seems to suggest?

This issue is central to establishing the value of the proposed method compared to conventional decision trees and warrants a more thorough analysis. For instance, a critical point left ambiguous is the handling of nodes with zero-weight vectors. The discussion on page 3 implies that the right branch is taken by default, which seems an arbitrary choice. It might be more natural to treat such a node as a leaf that outputs a prediction; later, the optimization could make its weight non-zero, effectively splitting the node. A precise definition of this mechanism is essential, as it has a direct parallel to pruning strategies in greedy tree construction.

I have a significant practical concern regarding the work's motivation. Decision trees are primarily valued for their interpretability. If this requirement is relaxed, other methods typically offer superior performance. The proposed method, using L2 regularization, is likely to produce dense weight vectors, leading to non-interpretable splits. While switching to L1 regularization could potentially enforce the sparsity needed for interpretability (e.g., producing 1-sparse splits), this might come at the cost of performance. This trade-off would need to be experimentally validated.

Another major concern is that the experiments lack sufficient detail regarding the baselines. The paper does not specify which pruning strategies, if any, were applied to the standard greedy method. Since a range of regularization parameters was tested for the proposed method, a fair comparison would demand that the baseline method be similarly tuned with different pruning strategies or parameters. Furthermore, the evaluation would be strengthened by including other simple global optimization heuristics, such as training multiple greedy trees on random data subsets and selecting the best one based on validation loss.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written. However, the sections describing the inference algorithms could be clearer. Section 6.2, for example, could be succinctly summarized by stating that the objective is maximized first across all `g` vectors for a given leaf, and then this maximum is taken over all leaves. Section 6.4 would also benefit from being more explicit.

In summary, this is a very clever approach that raises several interesting questions. However, its practical utility is currently unclear, owing to the unresolved interpretability issue (which undermines the primary motivation for using decision trees) and the lack of detail in the experimental comparisons.

PS: Although the title mentions forests, this case is not addressed in the paper.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
I maintain that the case of zero-weight vectors is a critical scenario that deserves further analysis for the reasons outlined in my initial review. The rebuttal suggests that weights are initialized randomly, which likely circumvents this issue in practice. However, I feel that the most interesting potential of this method would be realized by initializing weights to zero and allowing them to increase gradually, thus growing the tree. I encourage the authors to consider this direction. The submission proposes a clever reformulation of global optimization of decision trees as structured prediction, along with an efficient algorithm to solve the optimization. Some aspects of the motivation for the work and experimental results are not that convincing, however.
