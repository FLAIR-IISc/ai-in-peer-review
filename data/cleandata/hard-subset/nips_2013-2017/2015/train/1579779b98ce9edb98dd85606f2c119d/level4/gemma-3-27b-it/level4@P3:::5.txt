This paper presents a novel algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions, moving beyond the standard greedy approach. The core idea is to jointly optimize all split parameters and leaf distributions via a global objective, formulated through a connection to structured prediction with latent variables. An upper bound on the empirical loss is derived and regularized with an L2 penalty on split function weights, enabling optimization using stochastic gradient descent. The paper demonstrates the feasibility of training deep trees efficiently and shows performance improvements over greedy baselines on several classification benchmarks.

The work addresses a long-standing challenge in decision tree learning – the suboptimality of greedy induction. Establishing the link between decision tree optimization and structured prediction is a significant contribution, providing a theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The development of a computationally efficient algorithm, achieving O(d^2p) complexity (where d is tree depth and p is input dimension) through fast loss-augmented inference, is also commendable. The paper is well-structured and the motivation is clearly presented, situating the work within the broader context of decision tree research, referencing relevant prior work such as Bennett [1], Murthy & Salzburg [17], and Breiman [4]. 

However, while the technical contributions are solid, the empirical results presented do not consistently demonstrate a substantial advantage over strong greedy baselines. The performance gains are dataset-dependent, and the computational cost of the non-greedy approach is a significant consideration. The significance of the improvement needs to be more convincingly established. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Formulation:** The paper presents a compelling and theoretically sound formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The development of a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm enables training deep trees, a key practical advantage.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the core ideas accessible.
*   **Relevant Background:** The paper provides a good overview of related work and appropriately positions the contribution within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Gains:** The performance improvements over greedy baselines are not consistently observed across datasets, and the magnitude of improvement is sometimes modest.
*   **Computational Cost:** The non-greedy approach is more computationally expensive than standard greedy methods, which may limit its applicability to large-scale datasets.
*   **Clarity of Stable SGD:** The description of the Stable SGD algorithm is insufficient. The mechanism for data point assignment to leaves is unclear and a pseudo-code would be helpful.
*   **Minor Details & Typos:** Several minor clarifications and corrections are needed (detailed below).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to decision tree learning.
*   The proposed algorithm addresses a fundamental limitation of greedy tree induction.
*   The achieved computational efficiency makes the approach feasible for deep trees.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical results do not consistently demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.
*   The computational cost of the proposed approach may limit its practical applicability.
*   The description of Stable SGD requires further clarification.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. However, the limited empirical gains and the lack of clarity surrounding the Stable SGD algorithm raise concerns. While the theoretical framework and the computational efficiency are noteworthy, the practical benefits are not fully convincing. I recommend **weak reject**. I believe the authors could significantly strengthen the paper by providing more compelling empirical evidence, clarifying the Stable SGD algorithm, and addressing the minor points raised in the review. If the authors can address these issues in a revised version, I would be happy to reconsider the paper.
