

Summary: This paper proposes a non-greedy approach for training decision trees by jointly optimizing all split functions and leaf parameters under a global objective. The authors frame the problem as structured prediction with latent variables, deriving a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss that enables efficient training via stochastic gradient descent. This addresses a key limitation of conventional greedy tree induction methods that optimize splits layer by layer without considering their interdependencies.

Quality: The paper presents a technically sound framework that establishes a novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables. The derivation of the upper bound (Equation 7) is generally well-motivated, though a critical issue arises in the proof. Specifically, the claim that "for all values of g ≠ sgn(Wx), the RHS can only become larger" (line 216) is problematic, as demonstrated by the counterexample: when Wx = [5; -5], choosing g = [1; 1] yields gTWx = 0, resulting in a bound value of -10 + ℓ(...) that may be smaller than ℓ(...), violating the upper bound requirement. The supplementary proof of Proposition 1 does not address this case, leaving the bound's validity questionable. The experimental results are generally convincing, but the paper would benefit from more thorough ablation studies.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical derivations are clean. However, several aspects could be clarified: 1) The explanation of "inactive leaves" (Section 6.2) is insufficient; it's unclear why leaves become unassigned during training and how this affects model performance. 2) The experimental section lacks a proper baseline for non-axis-aligned greedy trees (i.e., standard greedy optimization of oblique splits without the proposed non-greedy approach). 3) The significance of the "leaves" mentioned in the efficiency discussion (Section 6.2) is ambiguous without clarification.

Originality: The core idea of non-greedy joint optimization of decision tree parameters is novel and addresses an important limitation of existing approaches. While previous work has explored global tree optimization (e.g., Bennett [1]), this paper's connection to structured prediction with latent variables and the efficient optimization method represent significant advances. The paper adequately distinguishes itself from related work, particularly from the hierarchical mixture of experts [13] and Bennett's earlier work [1].

Significance: The proposed method offers a theoretically sound approach to decision tree learning that improves generalization performance across multiple benchmarks. The ability to train deep trees efficiently with stochastic gradient descent represents a valuable contribution. However, the practical significance is somewhat limited by the need to specify tree depth in advance (a common limitation in tree-based methods), and the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of this constraint and its implications.

Strengths:
- Establishes a theoretically grounded connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables
- Proposes an efficient optimization method with O(d²p) complexity for deep trees
- Demonstrates consistent performance improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets
- Provides a principled regularization approach via row-wise norm constraints

Weaknesses:
- The key upper bound derivation contains a logical flaw that undermines the theoretical foundation
- The "inactive leaves" phenomenon requires more thorough explanation
- Missing baseline for standard greedy training of non-axis-aligned trees
- Insufficient discussion of the tree depth specification constraint
- Experimental section lacks runtime comparisons to demonstrate the efficiency gains

Recommendation: I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel approach that has clear theoretical and empirical merits. However, the identified flaw in the upper bound derivation requires careful correction in the final version. The authors should also provide more thorough explanations for the "inactive leaves" phenomenon, include the missing baseline, and discuss the tree depth specification constraint more thoroughly.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of decision tree learning by presenting a non-greedy optimization framework that significantly outperforms greedy methods. With the theoretical issues addressed and the experimental section strengthened, this work would be a valuable addition to NIPS.
