### Summary

This paper introduces a novel method for the non-greedy, global optimization of decision trees. Departing from traditional algorithms that build trees in a greedy, node-by-node fashion, this work formulates the training of all split functions and leaf node parameters as a single, joint optimization problem. The key contributions are twofold: first, the problem is elegantly framed as structured prediction with latent variables, where the path taken through the tree is a latent outcome. Second, to make this intractable problem solvable, the authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. This surrogate objective can then be optimized efficiently using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A crucial technical contribution is a "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm that reduces the complexity of each SGD update, making the approach feasible for deep trees. Experiments on several classification benchmarks demonstrate that the resulting non-greedy trees outperform various greedy baselines.

### Strengths and Positive Aspects

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles the long-standing and important problem of suboptimal, greedy decision tree induction. The formulation of global tree training as a latent-variable structured prediction problem is a novel and principled approach that connects tree learning to a well-established optimization paradigm.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed optimization framework, based on a convex-concave upper bound of the empirical loss, is technically sound and well-motivated. The derivation of an efficient `O(d^2p)` algorithm for gradient updates is a strong technical result that makes the method practical.

3.  **Performance:** The experimental results in Figure 2 are promising, showing that the non-greedy trees consistently achieve better test accuracy and appear less prone to overfitting compared to several greedy baselines across multiple datasets and tree depths.

### Weaknesses and Questions for Authors

1.  **Clarity of the Upper Bound Derivation:** The justification for the upper bound in Equation (7) could be made clearer in the main text. The argument that the right-hand side (RHS) can only increase for `g != sgn(Wx)` is not immediately obvious, since the first term, `g^T Wx`, could decrease and potentially make the entire expression smaller than the loss. For example, if `Wx = [5; -5]`, then `h_hat = [1; -1]` and `h_hat^T Wx = 10`. If we choose `g = [1; 1]`, then `g^T Wx = 0`. The RHS becomes `0 - 10 + loss = loss - 10`, which is not an upper bound. While the proof in the supplementary material may resolve this, a more detailed explanation in the main paper would significantly improve clarity. (A post-rebuttal clarification from the authors on this point was helpful).

2.  **"Inactive Leaves" Phenomenon:** The paper observes that during training, some leaves may end up with no data points assigned to them. This is an interesting and potentially problematic behavior that warrants more discussion. What aspects of the SGD optimization cause this? The proposed "Stable SGD" (SSGD) appears to be a practical heuristic to mitigate this. However, its motivation could be strengthened. Could this procedure be framed more formally, perhaps as an instance of a Concave-Convex Procedure (CCCP) or an EM-like algorithm where leaf assignments are treated as latent variables that are periodically updated?

3.  **Justification for Fast Loss-Augmented Inference:** The motivation for the fast loss-augmented inference, which restricts the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1, could be better supported experimentally. While the asymptotic complexity improvement is clear, the experiments use tree depths up to 18. For a depth of 16, a brute-force search over `2^16 ≈ 65,000` paths, while expensive, might still be feasible for offline training. A direct experimental comparison is needed to show the performance trade-off (in terms of accuracy) incurred by this approximation. How much accuracy is lost for the gain in speed? Figure 4 demonstrates the runtime difference, but the impact on final model quality is not fully explored.

4.  **Missing Baseline Comparison:** The experimental evaluation would be more compelling with the inclusion of a standard greedy algorithm for learning *oblique* (non-axis-aligned) decision trees. The current baselines include axis-aligned trees, OC1 (which uses coordinate descent), and CO2 (a greedy special case of the proposed method). A baseline that greedily optimizes a linear split at each node using a standard method would help to isolate the benefits of the *non-greedy global optimization* from the general benefits of using oblique splits.

5.  **Pre-specified Tree Depth:** A practical limitation of the method is that the tree depth must be fixed in advance, whereas traditional greedy methods can grow the tree until a stopping criterion (e.g., node purity, minimum samples) is met. This limitation should be discussed more explicitly. An analysis of the model's sensitivity to this hyperparameter would be valuable for practitioners.

### Concluding Remarks

The submission considers the important and challenging problem of jointly training all parameters of a decision tree. The proposed framework is novel, principled, and shows promising empirical results. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened with additional baselines and analysis, and the clarity of some derivations could be improved, the core contribution is significant. The paper successfully translates a difficult combinatorial problem into a tractable continuous optimization framework, advancing the state-of-the-art in decision tree learning.
