

This paper presents an innovative approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables, enabling non-greedy optimization of the entire tree structure. The authors establish a novel connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, proposing a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized using stochastic gradient descent. Their method jointly optimizes split functions at all levels of the tree under a global objective, addressing a fundamental limitation of conventional greedy approaches that optimize splits one node at a time. The key technical contribution is showing how to reduce the computational complexity of the gradient updates from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) through a modified surrogate objective, making deep tree training feasible.

The paper relates well to previous work in the field. It acknowledges and builds upon Bennett's early work on non-greedy tree optimization while addressing its limitations (binary classification only, high complexity). The authors properly contextualize their approach relative to Murthy and Salzburg's skepticism about non-greedy methods (which they correctly identify as stemming from overfitting without regularization) and contrast their method with soft-split approaches like Hierarchical Mixture of Experts that sacrifice computational efficiency. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables (Yu & Joachims, 2009) provides a solid theoretical foundation, and the authors appropriately cite related work on structured SVMs (Taskar et al., 2003; Tsochantaridis et al., 2004).

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear proofs of the upper bound and its tightness properties. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple relevant baselines (greedy axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2) across several benchmark datasets. The results consistently show that non-greedy trees achieve better generalization performance than greedy approaches, with the improvements being particularly notable in terms of reduced overfitting. The authors also provide a thoughtful analysis of how the regularization parameter ν implicitly controls tree pruning, which is an important practical consideration.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The problem formulation is well-structured and accessible, with clear definitions of the tree navigation function and loss terms. The connection to structured prediction is explained logically, and the algorithm descriptions (particularly Algorithm 1) are precise and implementable. The paper effectively uses figures to illustrate key concepts (e.g., the tree navigation function in Figure 1 and the comparison of training times in Figure 4). The writing is concise and professional, with sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

The originality of the work is significant. While non-greedy tree optimization has been attempted before (Bennett, 1998), this paper presents a fundamentally different approach that leverages structured prediction with latent variables to create an efficiently optimizable surrogate objective. The authors demonstrate how their method can handle any general loss function while maintaining the computational advantages of decision trees, which is a notable advancement over prior work. The connection to structured prediction is novel in the context of decision trees, and the paper adequately differentiates itself from previous contributions by carefully analyzing related work.

The significance of this work is substantial. Decision trees remain widely used in practice due to their interpretability and computational efficiency, yet their performance is often limited by the greedy induction process. This paper provides a principled method for improving tree quality without sacrificing computational efficiency, which has direct implications for real-world applications (as evidenced by the Microsoft Kinect example in the introduction). The approach also opens new research directions, such as learning sparse split functions or applying kernel methods to the linear split parameters. The results demonstrate that non-greedy trees can achieve better generalization performance across multiple datasets, suggesting this method could become a standard approach for decision tree learning.

Strengths:
- Novel theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction with latent variables
- Efficient optimization algorithm with reduced complexity (O(d^2p) vs O(2^dp))
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing improved generalization over greedy baselines
- Theoretical foundation supporting practical application to any loss function
- Clear exposition of the problem formulation and algorithm

Weaknesses:
- The improvements over greedy methods, while statistically significant, tend to be modest in absolute terms (e.g., 1-3% accuracy gains on most datasets)
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of when the non-greedy approach is particularly advantageous (e.g., for specific data characteristics or problem domains)
- The comparison with random forests is limited; it would be valuable to see how non-greedy trees compare to ensemble methods that inherently address the limitations of greedy tree construction

Pro-acceptance arguments: The paper presents a technically sound, novel approach that addresses a fundamental limitation of decision tree learning. The theoretical foundation is strong, the experimental results are comprehensive, and the method has clear practical significance. The improvements in generalization performance are consistent across multiple datasets, and the approach is well-positioned to advance the state-of-the-art in decision tree learning.

Con-acceptance arguments: While the theoretical contributions are significant, the empirical improvements over existing methods are relatively modest. The paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of when and why the non-greedy approach provides the most benefit, as well as comparison with ensemble methods that have been shown to outperform single greedy trees.

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The method is theoretically grounded, efficiently implementable, and demonstrates clear improvements over standard greedy approaches. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. While the absolute improvements in performance are not dramatic, the theoretical insights and practical applicability of the approach make this a valuable contribution to the literature. I recommend acceptance.
