This paper presents a novel approach for the global optimization of decision trees. The core idea is to reframe the training process as a latent structured prediction problem. In this formulation, the latent variables are sign vectors that represent the branching decisions at each internal node of the tree. The authors propose a non-differentiable, non-convex loss function, which they approximate with a differentiable (though still non-convex) upper bound. This objective is then minimized using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), where each step requires solving a loss-augmented inference problem. The paper provides efficient algorithms for this inference sub-problem and presents experimental results comparing the proposed method against a standard greedy training algorithm.

**Strengths and Contributions**

The primary contributions of this work are the elegant reformulation of decision tree optimization as structured prediction and the development of efficient algorithms to solve the resulting objective. This is a novel and thought-provoking perspective on a classic problem.

A particularly interesting consequence of this approach is the sparsity of the gradient updates. As the authors note, this provides a computational advantage. However, I believe this property has deeper implications that are not fully explored. Specifically, the fact that each gradient step updates only a single node's weight vector suggests a natural mechanism for tree growth. One could envision an optimization procedure starting from a tree with all-zero weights (effectively, a single leaf node) and gradually adding complexity by making weights non-zero. This could allow the model complexity to adapt to the data in a principled manner.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

Despite the novelty of the approach, I have several concerns regarding the practical motivation and the experimental evaluation.

1.  **The Role of Sparse Updates and the Zero-Weight Case:** The sparse update property raises fundamental questions about the relationship between this method and traditional greedy approaches. For instance, is it always optimal to introduce a new split (i.e., make a zero weight non-zero) rather than refining an existing one? Could an L1-regularized variant naturally converge to a finite-sized tree? These questions are central to understanding the method's value. A critical and ambiguous point is the handling of nodes with zero-weight vectors. The paper suggests a default action (e.g., taking the right branch), which seems arbitrary. A more natural interpretation would be for a zero-weight node to act as a leaf, making a prediction. Subsequently, the optimization could make its weight non-zero, effectively splitting the node. This mechanism is analogous to pruning and growing in greedy methods and is crucial for the 'grow-from-zero' learning scenario I described above. I strongly encourage the authors to provide a more thorough analysis of this case, as it could be the most significant aspect of their framework.

2.  **Motivation and Interpretability:** A key motivation for using single decision trees over more powerful ensemble methods is their interpretability. This typically relies on sparse splits, where each decision node tests only a single feature. The proposed method, particularly with the L2 regularization used in the experiments, is likely to produce dense weight vectors at each node, resulting in non-interpretable oblique splits. If interpretability is lost, it is unclear why one would prefer this globally optimized single tree over high-performance ensembles like Random Forests or Gradient Boosted Trees. While the authors could consider L1 regularization to encourage sparsity, it is an open question whether this would achieve 1-sparse splits without a significant drop in performance. This trade-off needs to be experimentally validated to justify the method's practical niche.

3.  **Experimental Baselines:** The experimental comparison to the 'naive' greedy method is not sufficiently detailed. The paper reports tuning regularization parameters for the proposed method but does not specify the equivalent tuning for the baseline (e.g., exploring different pruning strategies or parameters). For a fair comparison, the greedy baseline should be similarly optimized. Furthermore, the comparison could be strengthened by including other simple global optimization heuristics, such as training multiple greedy trees on random data subsets and selecting the best one on a validation set. This would provide a more robust benchmark for the performance gains achieved by this sophisticated approach.

**Clarity and Presentation**

The paper is generally well-written. However, the sections describing the inference algorithms (6.2 and 6.4) could be improved for clarity. For example, Section 6.2 could be more accessible if it were framed as a two-stage maximization: first over all paths `g` leading to a specific leaf, and then maximizing this result over all leaves. Similarly, Section 6.4 would benefit from more explicit details. Finally, the title mentions 'forests,' but the paper focuses exclusively on single decision trees. This should be rectified.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper introduces a clever and theoretically interesting reformulation of decision tree optimization. The connection to structured prediction and the resulting sparse gradient updates open up promising research directions. However, in its current form, the practical utility of the method is not yet established. The key concerns are the potential loss of interpretability—a primary reason for using single trees—and an experimental evaluation that needs stronger, more carefully tuned baselines. I believe the paper's contribution would be significantly strengthened by addressing these points, particularly by further investigating the implications of the sparse updates and the handling of zero-weight nodes.
