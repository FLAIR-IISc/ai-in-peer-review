- Overall assessment: Strong, elegant, well-motivated paper with principled formulation; empirical gains modest but work likely to catalyze further research.  
- Method summary: Proposes non-greedy training of oblique decision trees by jointly optimizing split functions and leaf parameters via a latent-variable structured prediction formulation.  
- Technical contribution: Derives a convex–concave upper bound and an efficient loss-augmented inference using a Hamming-ball (radius 1) approximation to reduce per-update complexity to O(d^2 p).  
- Optimization/detail: Introduces per-node norm constraints and a “Stable SGD” variant to mitigate inactive leaves and enable scalable SGD training.  
- Empirical results: Single non-greedy trees typically outperform greedy baselines and CO2 with lower overfitting across depths, but improvements are modest.  
- Relation to prior work: Connects ID3/CART, oblique splits, margin methods, and HME to latent structured SVMs while preserving hard splits and scalable inference.  
- Theoretical quality: Sound and clearly presented derivations; proofs in supplement; complexity analysis aligns with timing plots.  
- Main weaknesses: Limited empirical evaluation (only single trees), missing comparisons to stronger oblique/ensemble baselines, and lack of variance/sensitivity analyses.  
- Clarity: Very clear and well organized; algorithmic details and links to latent-structured prediction are easy to follow.  
- Minor clarity suggestions: Include proof sketches for key bounds, clarify initialization effects and need for restarts, and report standard deviations and wall-clock/memory for all methods.  
- Originality: Novel latent-structure formulation and Hamming-ball inference make a meaningful advance over prior oblique/margin-based methods.  
- Significance: Framework paper with important optimization perspective; greater empirical validation (especially ensembles) would boost practical impact.  
- Pros (concise): Principled formulation, efficient training, scalable inference, clear exposition, consistent accuracy/generalization gains.  
- Cons (concise): Small empirical gains, limited evaluation scope, missing strong baselines and ablations, theoretical details relegated to supplement.  
- Suggested improvements: Post-hoc greedy refinement, systematic overfitting analysis (train/val curves, active leaf counts), comparisons to regularized oblique splits and ensembles, and ablations for Hamming-ball radius, SSGD, and initialization.  
- Recommendation: Accept — technically sound and influential framework despite modest experimental gains.
