The submission proposes an algorithm for global optimization of decision trees based on a reformulation of the problem as structured prediction, where latent structured variables are sign vectors encoding the decisions made at each internal node in the tree. A non-differentiable and non-convex loss is proposed, which is then approximated by a differentiable (but still non-convex) upper bound. Minimizing this loss via stochastic gradient descent (SGD) entails solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are given. Experiments are shown comparing this method to a standard greedy training method.

The key contributions of the work are casting the global decision tree optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms to optimize the objective. The approach is novel and interesting, raising intriguing questions. However, I have concerns about practical considerations, particularly regarding the motivation for the method and the significance of the performance gains over naive methods.

Considered purely as a method to globally optimize decision trees, the approach is intriguing in several ways. The first advantage gained by casting the optimization as structured prediction is being able to perform SGD by solving a sequence of loss-augmented optimization problems that leverage the special structure of the problem. Moreover, the gradient updates are sparse, with each gradient step involving changing only one node's weight vector. This property raises possibilities that are not fully acknowledged in the paper.

One could imagine optimizing over an infinitely deep tree by initially setting all weights to zero and then gradually growing the tree by making more weights nonzero, allowing the model's complexity to grow naturally to fit the data. This raises interesting questions, such as whether such a method would be equivalent to or a variation of any known greedy strategy. Alternatively, with appropriate regularization, would this method converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes? This issue is crucial in understanding what makes the proposed method worthwhile compared to naive decision trees and deserves more careful analysis.

A critical issue left ambiguous is what happens when some node's weight is equal to zero. The discussion implies taking the right branch, which seems like a dubious choice. It would be preferable to output a decision at this node, with the possibility of making this weight nonzero later, corresponding to splitting the node. Precisely defining what happens here is important, as it has a direct analog to pruning strategies for greedy training.

I have significant practical concerns regarding the motivation for the work. Decision trees are mainly useful due to their interpretability, and if this requirement is dropped, other methods generally offer better performance. I expect the method to produce non-interpretable results, as the L2 regularization would produce dense weight vectors. Although switching to L1 regularization might help, it is possible that performance would drop if forced to regularize to the point where exactly 1-sparse splits were produced.

My other main concern is that the experiments lack detail regarding other baselines. No details are given about the pruning strategies employed for the naive approach. Since a range of regularization parameters were tried for the proposed method, I would expect the naive method to be similarly tried with different pruning strategies and/or parameters. It would also be fair to try other trivial strategies for global optimization, such as training a greedy method with random subsets of the training data and choosing the one that minimizes the desired loss on the full training or validation set.

Regarding clarity, the paper is reasonably well-written. However, the parts describing inference could be clearer. Section 6.2 could be summarized by stating that the objective is first maximized for all g corresponding to a given leaf, which is then maximized over all leaves. Section 6.4 could also be more explicit.

In summary, this is a clever approach that raises interesting questions. However, it is unclear whether this is a practically useful method at this point, due to the interpretability issue and the lack of details in the experiments.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
I still think that the case of zero weights is a critical case that deserves further analysis. Although the rebuttal suggests that weights are probably initialized randomly, which is why this issue doesn't seem to come up in practice, I believe that what would really make this method interesting is the case where the weights are initially zero and are gradually increased. I encourage the authors to consider this direction.
