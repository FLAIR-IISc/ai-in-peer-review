Summary: The paper proposes a method to jointly train all parameters of a decision tree—both split functions and leaf posteriors—by casting training as a single optimization problem, then deriving an upper bound and tractable approximation. The topic is important, as most tree training is still greedy and layerwise.

Quality: The paper is generally well written, but there may be issues in the theoretical reasoning (see below).

Clarity: The derivation is clean, though the experiments and their presentation could be stronger.

Originality: Studying joint training for decision trees is timely and valuable.

Significance: The approach is promising, but several aspects require clarification or further evaluation (see below).

Comments:
- Possible flaw or ambiguity in the empirical loss bound. At minimum, the argument around lines 216ff is confusing: “... for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right-hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$ ...”. Consider the example $W\bx = [5; -5]$, so $\hat\bh = [1; -1] = \sgn(W\bx)$ and $\hat\bh^\top W\bx = 10$. If we choose $\bg = [1; 1]$, then $\bg^\top W\bx = 0$. The right-hand side of Eq. (7) becomes
  $\bg^\top W\bx - \hat\bh^\top W\bx + \ell(\cdot) = -10 + \ell(\cdot)$,
  which could be smaller than $\ell(\cdot)$ and thus not a valid upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement does not seem to address this case and therefore appears incomplete.
- Unused leaves. Some leaves receive no data. This is unusual and may indicate an issue with the objective or initialization. The proposed heuristic that fixes data-to-leaf assignments seems suboptimal; can it be motivated as an EM or CCCP step (akin to other clustering formulations)? Clarifying this could strengthen the methodological foundation.
- Efficient loss-augmented inference. How critical is the specialized inference design in practice? With tree depth at most 16, a brute-force check over $2^{16}=65{,}536$ assignments may be feasible. How much does restricting to a Hamming ball impact performance and solution quality? The supplement provides one result—does this generalize across datasets and depths? Also, please clarify what “leaves” refers to in that context (leaf nodes vs. assignments).
- Missing baseline. Please include a baseline that trains non-axis-aligned split functions greedily without initialization, to isolate the benefit of joint optimization relative to a strong conventional approach.
- Fixed depth. The need to pre-specify tree depth is a notable limitation. This design choice should be discussed more thoroughly, and experiments varying depth would help assess robustness and guide practitioners.

Post-rebuttal note: Thank you for the clear explanation of the loss bound (Eq. 7). You may wish to replace “$\arg\max$” with “$\max$” where appropriate. I have updated my score accordingly.

Additional suggestions:
- A runtime comparison (training and inference) would materially strengthen the paper.
- A more comprehensive empirical evaluation—ablation of the Hamming-ball radius, sensitivity to depth and initialization, and comparison to a greedy oblique-split baseline—would greatly aid readers.

Overall, the paper addresses an interesting and important problem: jointly training all parameters of decision trees. While the experimental evaluation is limited and the derivation initially raised concerns, I am willing to adjust my assessment based on the authors’ clarifications and with additional empirical support.
