Summary:
This paper casts the training of a fixed-depth decision tree as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, enabling joint, non-greedy optimization of all split functions together with leaf parameters under a global objective. The authors derive an upper bound on empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently with stochastic gradient descent, and they report better test accuracy than standard greedy tree-induction methods.

Strengths:
- The formulation is conceptually appealing: treating split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a global objective provides a principled alternative to traditional greedy training.
- Empirically, the method consistently outperforms typical greedy approaches on test accuracy.
- The focus on a fixed tree depth makes the comparison fair and the optimization well-scoped.

Weaknesses:
- The accuracy improvements over standard greedy baselines are generally small. While consistent gains are valuable, the practical impact may be limited if the computational and implementation complexity is higher than for standard methods.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The approach appears technically sound and grounded in a solid optimization framework for structured prediction with latent variables. The empirical evaluation demonstrates improvements over greedy baselines.
- Clarity: The main idea—non-greedy, global optimization of a fixed-depth tree framed as structured prediction—is clearly presented.
- Originality: Framing decision tree induction as structured prediction is an interesting and non-greedy perspective relative to classic greedy training.
- Significance: Despite consistent improvements, the gains are modest, which may limit the practical significance unless the method also delivers other advantages (e.g., robustness or reduced tree size) that offset the added complexity.

Pros:
- Principled non-greedy training of decision trees at fixed depth.
- Consistent test accuracy gains over greedy baselines.

Cons:
- Improvements tend to be small, which may temper the overall impact.
