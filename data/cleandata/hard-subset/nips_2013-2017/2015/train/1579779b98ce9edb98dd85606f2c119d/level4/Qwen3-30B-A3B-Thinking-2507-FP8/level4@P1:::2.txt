

This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach for optimizing decision trees by jointly training all split functions and leaf parameters under a global objective. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. This surrogate objective enables efficient training via stochastic gradient descent, with gradient computation complexity of O(d) per training exemplar (where d is tree depth), making deep tree learning feasible. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms greedy decision tree baselines on multiple classification benchmarks.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on decision trees, which have traditionally been trained using greedy, layer-by-layer procedures. While greedy tree induction (e.g., ID3, CART) remains dominant despite decades of research, this work addresses the fundamental limitation of greedy approaches: the inability to optimize split functions with knowledge of lower-level tree structure. The authors' connection to structured prediction with latent variables is particularly insightful, as it provides a principled framework for joint optimization. This approach differs from prior attempts such as Bennett's multi-linear programming method [1], which is limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss and high computational complexity, and soft-split approaches like Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13], which sacrifice the computational efficiency of decision trees.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-motivated theoretical foundation. The derivation of the upper bound (Equation 7) is theoretically compelling, though I have concerns about its completeness as noted below. The experimental evaluation demonstrates consistent improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets, supporting the main claim. However, the proof of the upper bound appears incomplete, as the argument in lines 216-217 does not account for cases where the RHS could be smaller than the LHS (see detailed comment below).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The problem formulation and connection to structured prediction are particularly well-explained. However, the experimental section would benefit from more detailed comparisons (e.g., runtime comparisons, ablation studies of key components) and clearer presentation of results. The description of the "inactive leaves" phenomenon (Section 6.3) is somewhat vague and could be better justified.

**Originality:** This work addresses a fundamental limitation of decision tree learning that has persisted for decades. The authors' formulation of decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables is novel, and their convex-concave upper bound approach provides a tractable solution to an otherwise NP-complete problem. This represents a meaningful advancement beyond prior work on non-greedy tree learning [1, 15, 17].

**Significance:** The proposed method has clear practical significance for applications requiring high-accuracy decision trees, particularly in computer vision and large-scale machine learning. The ability to train deep, non-greedy trees with better generalization than greedy alternatives addresses a real-world problem. The authors also suggest promising directions for future work, including sparse learning and kernel methods.

**Specific Concerns:**

1. **Completeness of the upper bound proof:** The derivation of the upper bound in Equation (7) appears incomplete. The argument that "for all values of g ≠ sgn(Wx), the RHS can only become larger than when g = sgn(Wx)" is questionable. Consider Wx = [5; -5], so sgn(Wx) = [1; -1]. Choosing g = [1; 1] gives gTWx = 0, so RHS = 0 - 10 + ℓ(...) = -10 + ℓ(...), which could be less than ℓ(...) and thus not a valid upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not address this case.

2. **Inactive leaves issue:** The paper notes that some leaves become "inactive" (not assigned any data points) during training. This phenomenon requires more explanation and justification. Why does this happen, and how does it affect the model's performance? The authors' heuristic of fixing leaf assignments for multiple gradient updates (SSGD) seems ad hoc and could benefit from a more principled motivation (e.g., relating to EM or CCCP).

3. **Efficiency of loss-augmented inference:** The paper claims that the Hamming ball approach (Equation 13) is necessary for scalability, but with tree depths at most 16 (as shown in Figure 2), checking all 2^16 = 65536 possibilities would be feasible for most datasets. How much does the Hamming ball approximation impact performance? The supplementary material provides limited evidence on this point.

4. **Baseline comparison:** The authors compare with CO2 [18] (a greedy variant of their method), but omit a baseline that trains non-axis-aligned trees using standard greedy methods (e.g., OC1 [17]) without their initialization. This comparison is crucial for establishing the value of the non-greedy approach.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to non-greedy decision tree learning with promising empirical results. The main concern is the incomplete proof of the upper bound, which requires clarification. The other issues (inactive leaves, efficiency of loss-augmented inference, baseline comparisons) are important but less critical than the theoretical concern.

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel connection to structured prediction with latent variables
- Theoretical framework for joint optimization of tree parameters
- Demonstrated improvements over greedy baselines on multiple benchmarks
- Efficient algorithm enabling training of deep trees

**Con Acceptance:**
- Incomplete proof of the upper bound (Equation 7)
- Insufficient explanation of "inactive leaves" phenomenon
- Limited comparison with standard greedy non-axis-aligned tree methods
- Incomplete justification for the Hamming ball approximation

I recommend a **weak accept** with the expectation that the authors will address the theoretical concerns in the final version. The paper makes a valuable contribution to a well-established area, and the proposed approach has clear potential for practical application.
