This paper presents an intriguing approach to decision tree induction, framing the problem as structured prediction to search for optimal trees of a pre-defined depth. The proposed method consistently outperforms standard greedy tree building algorithms in terms of test accuracy. However, the observed gains are typically modest.

The core idea – leveraging structured prediction for decision tree construction – is novel and well-motivated. Traditional greedy methods often get stuck in local optima, and this work offers a potentially more effective exploration of the tree space. The formulation allows for considering dependencies between splits, which is a key advantage. 

The experimental evaluation is reasonably thorough, covering a range of datasets. The comparisons to baseline greedy algorithms are clear and well-presented. The authors demonstrate a consistent, though often small, improvement in accuracy. It would be helpful to see a more detailed breakdown of *where* these improvements are occurring. For example, are there specific datasets or problem characteristics where the proposed method shines? A discussion of failure cases – datasets where the performance is comparable or even worse than greedy approaches – would also strengthen the analysis.

The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the computational complexity. While the authors acknowledge the increased cost compared to greedy methods, quantifying this cost (e.g., runtime scaling with tree depth and dataset size) would be valuable. Understanding the practical limitations of applying this method to larger, deeper trees is important. Is it feasible to apply this approach to trees beyond a certain depth? 

Furthermore, the connection to related work on structured prediction and its application to tree-structured outputs could be more explicitly drawn. While the authors cite relevant papers, a more detailed comparison with existing approaches would clarify the unique contributions of this work. Specifically, how does this method differ from or improve upon existing structured prediction techniques applied to similar problems?

Minor points: The presentation is generally clear, but some figures could be made more readable with larger fonts or clearer labeling. The notation is mostly consistent, but a quick glossary of symbols might be helpful for readers unfamiliar with the specific terminology.

Overall, this is a solid contribution with a novel idea. While the performance gains are not dramatic, the consistent improvements and the interesting framing of decision tree induction as a structured prediction problem warrant acceptance. Addressing the questions regarding computational complexity, dataset-specific performance, and connections to related work would further strengthen the paper.
