

Summary: This paper proposes a novel approach for jointly training all parameters of decision trees (both split decisions and leaf probabilities), addressing a key limitation of current greedy, layer-by-layer methods. The authors formulate the problem as a single optimization task, deriving a tractable upper bound via approximation. While the core idea is promising, significant concerns regarding the theoretical foundation and experimental design require careful attention.

Quality: The paper is well-structured and clearly written. However, a critical flaw exists in the derivation of the empirical loss bound (Eq. 7). The argument at lines 216–217 states: *"for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right-hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$."* This is incorrect, as demonstrated by the counterexample:  
Let $W\bx = [5; -5]$, so $\sgn(W\bx) = [1; -1]$ and $\sgn(W\bx)^T W\bx = 10$.  
Choosing $\bg = [1; 1]$ yields $\bg^T W\bx = 0$, so the right-hand side of Eq. (7) becomes:  
$$\bg^T W\bx - \sgn(W\bx)^T W\bx + \ell(\cdot) = -10 + \ell(\cdot),$$  
which is strictly less than $\ell(\cdot)$ (the bound’s target). This violates the upper-bound requirement. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not address this case, rendering it incomplete. The authors must revise this argument or provide a corrected proof.

Clarity: The mathematical derivation is logically sound *except* for the flaw above. The experimental section, however, lacks sufficient detail and comparison. Key ambiguities include:  
1. The phrase *"leaves"* in the Hamming ball context (Section 4.3) is undefined. Does it refer to tree leaves or feature indices?  
2. Some tree leaves appear unassigned during training (Figure 2). This is unusual for tree learning and suggests a potential issue with the objective or initialization. A justification or analysis of this phenomenon is needed.  
3. The heuristic for assigning data points to leaves (Section 4.2) is described as "sub-optimal" but lacks motivation. It would be strengthened by connecting it to established techniques like EM or the concave-convex procedure (CCCP).

Originality & Significance: Jointly training split decisions and leaf probabilities is a meaningful direction, as existing methods optimize these components sequentially. The proposed method is conceptually valuable, but its significance is tempered by the unresolved theoretical flaw and limited experimental validation.

Major Concerns:  
- **Baseline Comparison**: The paper lacks a critical baseline: standard greedy training of non-axis-aligned trees (without initialization). This is essential to isolate the contribution of joint training versus the choice of split functions.  
- **Tree Depth**: The depth must be specified *a priori* (Section 4.1). The authors should discuss this limitation and provide a thorough analysis of depth sensitivity (e.g., via ablation studies across datasets), rather than merely reporting results at a fixed depth.  
- **Efficiency of Loss-Augmented Inference**: The paper claims the Hamming ball search (depth ≤ 16, 65,536 candidates) is necessary. However, 2^16 is computationally feasible for greedy inference. The impact of this restriction on performance (beyond the supplementary result) should be quantified and justified.  

Minor Suggestions:  
- Replace $\arg\max$ with $\max$ in the revised loss bound (as noted in the rebuttal).  
- Clarify the runtime comparison with standard methods (e.g., CART) in experiments.  
- Explicitly define "leaves" in the Hamming ball context (e.g., "leaf nodes" vs. "feature indices").  

Recommendation: The paper tackles an important problem but currently suffers from a critical theoretical flaw and insufficient experimental rigor. The flaw in Proposition 1 invalidates a core claim, and the experimental design omits key baselines and depth analysis. I am willing to revise my score upon a satisfactory rebuttal that:  
1. Corrects the loss bound derivation with a rigorous proof covering all cases (including the counterexample above),  
2. Provides the missing baseline (greedy non-axis-aligned trees),  
3. Analyzes depth sensitivity and clarifies the "leaves" ambiguity.  

Without these revisions, the paper’s contribution cannot be fully validated. The idea has merit, but the current presentation does not sufficiently support its claims. A stronger revision would significantly improve the work’s impact.
