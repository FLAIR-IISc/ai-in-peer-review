- Proposes non-greedy decision tree optimization by reformulating as structured prediction with binary latent variables for internal splits.  
- Introduces a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss enabling efficient training via stochastic gradient descent.  
- Claims per-iteration complexity O(d² p) (d = tree depth, p = feature dim), making deeper trees feasible.  
- Empirically outperforms greedy baselines on multiple classification benchmarks.  
- Builds on prior structured prediction and decision tree work but establishes a novel connection and algorithmic development.  
- Strength — Originality: novel, well-motivated linkage between trees and structured prediction.  
- Strength — Quality: rigorous derivation, proofs, and clear algorithmic presentation.  
- Strength — Significance: principled global optimization addressing a long-standing limitation of greedy trees.  
- Strength — Clarity: well-written, logically organized, accessible algorithm descriptions.  
- Weakness — Interpretability: L2 regularization yields dense split weights, undermining tree interpretability; no exploration of L1/sparse alternatives.  
- Weakness — Experimental details: insufficient reporting (e.g., pruning strategies) for fair baseline comparisons.  
- Weakness — Zero-weight handling: inadequate treatment of the case when a weight vector is zero, affecting pruning and behavior.  
- Weakness — Title misalignment: title references "forests" but paper treats only single trees.  
- Weakness — Motivation: unclear when to prefer this non-greedy method over strong alternatives (random forests, gradient boosting) if interpretability is not required.  
- Conclusion/recommendation: technically interesting and promising but practically limited by interpretability and experimental gaps; recommend rejection unless authors address interpretability and provide fuller experimental comparisons.
