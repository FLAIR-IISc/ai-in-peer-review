This paper proposes an algorithm for learning an oblique decision tree, where nodes contain linear split functions. In contrast to standard, greedy, top-down approaches, the proposed algorithm optimizes all tree parameters—the weights of the split functions and the class distributions at the leaves—jointly. To achieve this, the authors formulate the problem by minimizing a regularized upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. This formulation creates a smoother optimization landscape, and the tree parameters are then learned by minimizing this upper bound using stochastic gradient descent (SGD).

The authors present an elegant formulation of the tree optimization problem, drawing a connection to structured prediction with latent variables. This is a novel and principled approach to a classic machine learning problem. However, the empirical results presented in the paper are not sufficiently convincing to demonstrate the superiority of this global optimization approach over standard greedy methods. The proposed method shows significant improvement over the greedy baseline on only a few datasets, and this comes at a considerably higher computational cost. While the formulation is a strong theoretical contribution, the practical benefits remain unclear.

The paper is well-structured, but several points require clarification to improve reproducibility and understanding:

*   The description of the "stable SGD" variant is unclear. Specifically, the mechanism for assigning data points to leaves during training needs further explanation. Including pseudo-code for this procedure would greatly enhance clarity.
*   Regarding Figure 2: It is unclear whether these results were generated using the standard SGD or the stable SGD variant. Additionally, the term "active leaf" is not defined and requires clarification.
*   In Algorithm 1 (line 10), the parameter Θ is updated and then projected onto a simplex. The paper should specify the practical method used to solve this projection problem. It would also be beneficial to explicitly state that this projection ensures the rows of Θ sum to one, maintaining its interpretation as a probability distribution.

**Minor Comments and Typos:**

*   **Lines 407-408:** The text mentions tuning the "number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree." This is confusing, as standard decision tree algorithms typically evaluate all features at each node. Please clarify this hyperparameter.
*   **Equations (7), (8), and (9):** The "argmax" operator should be "max."
*   **Figure 1:** The figure is not referenced in the main text.
*   **Line 141:** The sentence "...to the index of the leaf on by this path." appears to be grammatically incorrect or incomplete.
*   **Line 238:** "...the solution to loss-augmented inference..." should likely be plural: "...the solutions to..."
*   **Line 301:** "An key observation..." should be "A key observation..."
*   **Line 360:** The acronym "FSGD" is used without prior definition.
*   **Line 404:** The phrase "...a tree with minimum training errors" is ambiguous. It is unclear if this refers to training error or validation/tuning error used for model selection.

**Supplementary Material:**

*   **Line 29:** `max{g ∈ sgn...}` seems incorrect. It should likely be `max{g = sgn...}` or the set over which the maximization occurs should be clarified.
*   **Line 30:** There appears to be a typo. The second term, `l(Θ^T f(g), y)`, should likely be `l(Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)), y)` to be consistent with the definition of `g`.

In summary, while the paper presents an elegant and principled formulation for globally optimizing decision trees, the empirical evidence provided is not yet strong enough to justify its adoption over simpler, greedy approaches, especially given the increased computational complexity.
