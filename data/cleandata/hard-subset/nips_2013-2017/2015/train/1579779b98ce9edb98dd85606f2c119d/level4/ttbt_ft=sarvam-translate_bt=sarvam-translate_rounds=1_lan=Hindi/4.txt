This presentation proposes an algorithm for global optimization of decision trees, based on redefining the problem as (unlabeled) structured prediction. Unlabeled structured variables are vector representations that encode the decisions made at each internal node in the tree.

A non- differentiable and non-convex loss is proposed, which is then estimated by a differentiable (but still non-convex) upper bound.

Minimizing this loss through SGD involves solving a sequence of loss-augmented estimation problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided. Experiments comparing this method to a standard greedy training method are shown.

The main contributions of this work are presenting global DT optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms to optimize the objective.

The way this is done is new and interesting, and raises interesting questions.

However, I have some concerns about some practical considerations – specifically, the motivation for the method and how significant the performance advantage over inexperienced methods is.

Considering DT solely as a method for global optimization, this method is interesting in many ways.

The first advantage gained from presenting optimization as structured prediction is being able to solve a sequence of loss-augmented estimation problems that take advantage of the special structure of the problem.

However, I think the most interesting result of this approach is that gradient updates are sparse – if I'm not mistaken, each gradient step involves changing the weight vector of only one node.

This presentation primarily presents it as a computational advantage, but I believe this property creates more interesting possibilities that are not actually acknowledged in the paper.

One can imagine optimizing on an infinitely deep tree by initially setting all weights to zero and then gradually increasing the tree by making more weights non-zero, allowing the model's complexity to increase naturally in line with the data.

This raises interesting questions: for example, would such a method prove to be equivalent to or a variation of any known greedy strategy?

This may be the case if it turns out that introducing a new node instead of modifying the weights of an old node is always better.

Or, with proper regularization, would such a method converge on a tree with a limited number of nodes instead (as shown in Figure 2)?

I think this issue is at the root of why the proposed method is meaningful compared to inexperienced DT, and perhaps deserves more careful analysis than is currently presented in the paper.

For example, an important issue is left unclear: what happens if some nodes have weights equal to zero?

The discussion on page 3 implies that we take the right branch, which seems like a dubious choice.

It seems better to output a decision on this node; later, we can decide to make this weight non-zero, which corresponds to partitioning the node.

It is important to precisely define what happens here, as it directly corresponds to pruning strategies for greedy training.

I have a significant practical concern about the motivation for the work.

That is, I would say that simple DTs are mainly useful to the extent that they are interpretable; if the need to interpret is removed, it is generally better to use other methods, which generally provide much better performance.

As stated, I hope the method will yield non-interpretable results, as L2 regularization will generate dense weight vectors.

Although switching to L1 regularization may help, it is possible that if we are forced to regularize to the point where exactly 1-sparse partitions are generated, performance will drop.

Therefore, it will definitely need to be tested.

My second main concern is that the experiments do not go into enough detail about other baselines.

No details are given as to which pruning strategies were used for the inexperienced approach.

Since a series of regularization parameters were attempted for the proposed method, I hope that the inexperienced method will also be tried in a similar way with some different pruning strategies and/or parameters.

I think it would also be appropriate to try some other trivial strategies for global optimization, such as training a greedy method with a random subset of the training data, and choosing the one that minimizes the desired loss on the full training or validation set.

Regarding clarity, the paper is written quite well. My only comment is that the parts describing the estimates may be a little unclear.

For example, section 6.2 can be summarized by saying that the objective is maximized for all g's that correspond to the first leaf given, which is then maximized over all leaves.

Section 6.4 could also be a little clearer.

In summary, I think this is a very clever approach that raises some very interesting questions.

However, it is not clear whether this is a practically useful method at this point, both due to the issue of the aforementioned need to interpret (why use simple DTs in the first place if they are not interpretable?) and the lack of detail in the experiments.

Re-consideration: Although the forests are mentioned in the title, the paper does not address this matter.

Re-consideration comments
I still think the case of zero weights is an important one that deserves further analysis, for the reasons I raised in my review.

Upon reading the re-consideration, it seems that the weights were probably initialized randomly, which is why this issue does not arise in practice.

However, I think the thing that will really make this method interesting will be the case where the weights are initially zero and gradually increasing.

I encourage the authors to consider this direction. The presentation redefines global optimization of decision trees as structured prediction, as well as an efficient algorithm for solving optimization. However, some aspects of the motivation for the work and the experimental results are not so convincing.
