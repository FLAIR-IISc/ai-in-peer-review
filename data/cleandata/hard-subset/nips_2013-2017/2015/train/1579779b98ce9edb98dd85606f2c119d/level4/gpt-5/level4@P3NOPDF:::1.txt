Summary
The paper proposes a nonconvex relaxation for globally optimizing decision trees with a fixed structure. The formulation is elegant and the exposition is clear, making a complex idea accessible. After reading the rebuttal and discussing with other reviewers, I remain positive: the approach is conceptually strong and likely to spur follow-up work on global optimization for tree models. However, the empirical results are weaker than I would expect given the promise of the method.

Strengths
- Clear, well-structured presentation that makes the technical content easy to follow.
- Elegant and novel relaxation for globally optimizing tree parameters given a fixed topology.
- Conceptually appealing and likely to influence future research on tree learning and nonconvex optimization.

Weaknesses
- Empirical performance is underwhelming relative to strong baselines, limiting the practical impact in its current form.
- The paper does not convincingly address the potential for increased overfitting that can arise from global optimization of a highly nonconvex objective.
- The evaluation does not fully explore simple hybrid refinements that could strengthen results.

Suggestions for improvement
- Hybrid refinement. After the global optimization, remove the final split in each branch and refit that split and its leaf parameters using a standard local method (e.g., greedy split selection with local leaf fitting). This small change could mitigate sensitivity in the deepest parts of the tree and improve generalization. More generally, consider a short local refinement (coordinate descent or greedy hill-climbing) on splits/leaves initialized by the global solution.
- Overfitting assessment. Since global optimization may overfit:
  - Report train vs. test performance gaps and learning curves.
  - Use nested cross-validation and/or a separate validation set to tune regularization and early stopping.
  - Compare against pruned versions of the globally optimized tree and quantify the effect of pruning.
  - Perform ablations on regularization strength within the relaxation and its effect on margins and stability.
- Stronger empirical evaluation.
  - Include competitive baselines (e.g., CART with pruning, oblique trees, boosted trees) and ensure careful hyperparameter tuning for all methods.
  - Evaluate across more datasets with varying dimensionality and sample sizes, and report statistical significance (e.g., multiple random splits with confidence intervals).
  - Provide runtime and scalability measurements with respect to depth, number of features, and dataset size.
- Analysis and diagnostics.
  - Add sensitivity analyses to initialization, relaxation parameters, and tree topology.
  - Visualize the learned decision boundaries or split orientations to better understand what the relaxation is capturing compared to greedy trees.

Minor comments
- A brief positioning with respect to prior global tree learning methods (e.g., oblique trees, mixed-integer programming formulations) would strengthen the context.
- Clarify any assumptions required for the relaxation and discuss failure modes (e.g., cases where the relaxation leads to degenerate or overly confident splits).

Overall evaluation
Despite the somewhat disappointing empirical results, the paper’s core idea is elegant and clearly communicated. I expect it to motivate further work on principled, globally optimized tree learning and hybrid approaches that blend global relaxations with local refinements. With stronger experimental validation, I would be inclined to rate the paper even higher. Given the current evidence, I believe it is a good paper that merits publication for its conceptual contribution and potential to catalyze new research.
