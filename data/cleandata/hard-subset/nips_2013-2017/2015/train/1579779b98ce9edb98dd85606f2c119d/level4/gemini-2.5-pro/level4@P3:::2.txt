This paper proposes a novel method for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees. Instead of the conventional greedy, node-by-node training procedure, the authors formulate the joint optimization of all split functions and leaf node parameters as a single global objective. The core idea is to frame decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem where the sequence of binary split decisions for a given input is treated as a latent variable. To make the discontinuous and piecewise-constant empirical loss function tractable, the authors derive a convex-concave upper bound inspired by latent variable structural SVMs. A key technical contribution is an efficient, approximate "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm that reduces the complexity of each stochastic gradient descent (SGD) update from being exponential in the tree depth `d` to polynomial (O(d²p)), making the approach practical for deeper trees.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and long-standing challenge of moving beyond greedy decision tree induction. The formulation connecting global tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables is novel and provides a principled framework for a difficult problem. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire future work in this area.
2.  **Technical Contribution:** The development of a tractable surrogate objective is a solid contribution. Furthermore, the derivation of the "fast loss-augmented inference" method, which makes the optimization scalable to deeper trees, is a valuable technical insight that directly addresses a major computational bottleneck of the initial formulation.
3.  **Generality:** The proposed framework is flexible and can accommodate different loss functions (e.g., log loss, squared loss), making it broadly applicable to both classification and regression tasks.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The main weaknesses concern a potential flaw in the central theoretical claim and the thoroughness of the experimental evaluation.

1.  **Potential Flaw in the Empirical Loss Bound (Quality):** I have a significant concern regarding the validity of the upper bound presented in Equation (7). The paper argues that for `g ≠ sgn(Wx)`, the right-hand side (RHS) of the inequality can only increase. However, this does not seem to hold in all cases. Consider a simple example where `Wx = [5; -5]`.
    *   The optimal `h` is `h_hat = sgn(Wx) = [1; -1]`, which gives `h_hat^T Wx = 10`.
    *   The loss on the left-hand side (LHS) is `ℓ(Θ^T f(h_hat), y)`.
    *   Now, let's choose a different `g = [1; 1]`. Then `g^T Wx = 0`.
    *   The RHS of Equation (7) becomes `(g^T Wx + ℓ(Θ^T f(g), y)) - h_hat^T Wx = (0 + ℓ(Θ^T f(g), y)) - 10 = ℓ(...) - 10`.
    *   This value could easily be smaller than the LHS, which would invalidate the bound. The proof of Proposition 1 provided in the supplementary material does not seem to cover this case and thus appears incomplete. I would appreciate a careful clarification from the authors on this point, as it is central to the paper's claims.

2.  **"Inactive Leaves" and the SSGD Heuristic (Quality):** The paper notes that during training, some leaf nodes may end up with no data points assigned to them. The proposed "Stable SGD" (SSGD) method, which fixes data-to-leaf assignments for several iterations, feels like a heuristic. Could the authors provide more intuition for why this phenomenon occurs? Does it indicate an issue with the objective function or the optimization procedure? Furthermore, could the SSGD heuristic be motivated more formally, perhaps by drawing a connection to expectation-maximization (EM) or concave-convex procedures (CCCP), where assignments are fixed while parameters are updated?

3.  **Experimental Evaluation (Quality & Significance):** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened in several ways:
    *   **Necessity of Fast Inference:** The paper's main technical claim is the efficiency gain from the fast loss-augmented inference. However, the experiments are on trees with a maximum depth of 18. The original loss-augmented inference would require iterating over at most `m+1` leaves, where `m < 2^d`. For `d=16`, this is `2^16 = 65,536` paths, which might still be computationally feasible. It would be very insightful to see a direct comparison of both runtime *and* final performance between the full inference and the Hamming ball approximation on the tested datasets. This would help quantify the trade-off between speed and accuracy introduced by the approximation.
    *   **Missing Baseline:** A crucial baseline is missing: a standard, greedily-trained oblique decision tree (i.e., one with linear combination splits, but without the global optimization). The current comparison to OC1 and random oblique trees is useful, but a direct comparison to a greedy version of the authors' own split optimization would more effectively isolate the benefit of the non-greedy approach itself.
    *   **Fixed Tree Depth:** The model requires the tree depth to be specified in advance, which is a significant limitation. A more detailed discussion of this limitation and a more thorough experimental analysis of the model's sensitivity to this hyperparameter would be valuable for the reader.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper tackles an interesting and important problem: the global, non-greedy training of decision trees. The proposed formulation is elegant and the efficiency improvements are a notable technical contribution.

However, my enthusiasm is tempered by a potential flaw in the derivation of the core theoretical result (the loss upper bound in Eq. 7) and a somewhat limited experimental evaluation that lacks key comparisons.

I am currently on the borderline, but given the potential severity of the issue with the loss bound, I am leaning towards rejection. I am very willing to reconsider and adjust my score if the authors can provide a convincing explanation in their rebuttal that clarifies my misunderstanding of the bound. A more complete experimental evaluation would also significantly strengthen the submission.

**Minor Point:** In Equation (7) and elsewhere, you might consider replacing `argmax` with `max` when the value of the maximization, not the argument, is what is being used.
