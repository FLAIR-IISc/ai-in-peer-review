- Paper proposes a novel reformulation of global decision-tree optimization as (latent) structured prediction with sign-vector latents and a differentiable upper-bound loss solved by SGD.  
- Key technical contribution: efficient algorithms for the required loss-augmented inference and a sparsity property where each SGD update touches only a single node’s weight vector.  
- The sparsity property suggests a promising tree-growth behavior (starting from zeros and activating nodes as needed), but the paper does not explore whether this yields better trees or how it compares to greedy node addition.  
- Ambiguity about zero-weight nodes: the paper implies taking the right branch for zero weights, which is questionable; zero-weight nodes should arguably be treated as unsplit/pruned until activated.  
- The authors do not analyze whether regularization can reliably control final tree size or whether the optimization favors adding nodes vs. revising existing ones.  
- Major practical concern: decision trees’ main value is interpretability, but L2 regularization here yields dense, likely non-interpretable splits; the trade-off with L1 sparsity is not evaluated.  
- Experimental shortcomings: baseline greedy training lacks details (especially pruning strategy), so comparisons are not clearly fair; baselines should match regularization/pruning procedures.  
- Additional baseline suggestions missing: simple global baselines (e.g., train many greedy trees on subsets and select by full-set loss) are not tested.  
- Paper clarity issues: sections on inference (notably 6.2 and 6.4) need clearer, more explicit explanations of the maximization steps.  
- Claim-versus-scope mismatch: title mentions "forests" but the paper does not address random/ensemble forests.  
- Post-rebuttal stance: initializing weights randomly may avoid zero-weight issues in practice, but the promising zero-initialization growth idea remains unexplored and should be investigated.  
- Overall judgment: clever and novel reformulation with efficient algorithms, but practical utility is unclear due to interpretability concerns and insufficiently rigorous/controlled experiments.
