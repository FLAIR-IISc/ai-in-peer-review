This paper presents an algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions at the nodes, moving beyond the standard greedy approach. The core idea is to jointly optimize tree parameters – split function weights and leaf output distributions – using a global objective based on an upper bound of the empirical loss. This bound is regularized with an L2 penalty on the split function weights to encourage smoothness and prevent overfitting. The optimization is then performed using stochastic gradient descent.

The formulation elegantly connects decision tree optimization to the well-studied problem of structured prediction with latent variables. This allows the authors to leverage techniques from that field to develop a tractable optimization procedure. However, the empirical results presented do not definitively demonstrate a substantial advantage over greedy tree learning methods. While improvements are observed on some datasets, they are not consistent across all benchmarks, and the computational cost of the non-greedy approach is higher.

The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written. However, a few areas could benefit from further clarification:

*   **Stable SGD:** The description of the Stable SGD algorithm is somewhat opaque. It would be helpful to include a more detailed explanation of how data point assignments to leaves are managed and updated, potentially with pseudo-code.
*   **Figure 2:** The figure’s caption does not specify whether the results shown are obtained using standard SGD or Stable SGD. Clarification on this point is crucial. The meaning of an "active" leaf also requires further explanation.
*   **Algorithm 1, Line 10:** The practical implementation of the projection of Θ onto a simplex is not discussed. Details about the method used for this projection (and a mention that it ensures each row sums to one) would improve understanding.

Minor comments and potential typos:

*   Lines 407-408: The phrasing "number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree" is confusing, as all features are typically evaluated at each node.
*   Equations (7), (8), and (9): "argmax" should be replaced with "max."
*   Figure 1 is referenced but never discussed in the main text.
*   Line 141: "...to the index of the leaf on by this path" is grammatically incorrect.
*   Line 238: "...the solution to loss-augmented inference..." should be "...the solutions to..."
*   Line 301: "An key observation..." should be "A key observation..."
*   Line 360: The abbreviation "FSGD" is undefined.
*   Line 404: "...a tree with minimum training errors" likely means "...tuning errors."

In the supplementary material:

*   Line 29: "max{g \in sgn...}" should be "max{g = sgn...}"
*   Line 30: "sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(\Theta^Tf(g), y)..." should be "sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(\Theta^Tf(sgn(Wx)), y)"

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to decision tree learning.
*   The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and allows for the application of established techniques.
*   The proposed regularization strategy is a sensible approach to prevent overfitting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical results do not consistently demonstrate a significant improvement over existing greedy methods.
*   The computational cost of the proposed method is higher than that of greedy algorithms.
*   Some aspects of the algorithm (e.g., Stable SGD) lack sufficient detail for full understanding and reproducibility.

**Overall:** While the paper introduces a valuable theoretical contribution and a promising approach to decision tree learning, the limited empirical evidence and lack of clarity in certain areas raise concerns. I would recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage the authors to address the concerns raised in this review and resubmit a revised version. Specifically, more compelling empirical results and a clearer explanation of the Stable SGD algorithm are needed to justify the increased complexity of the proposed method.
