This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters (decision splits and posterior probabilities) of decision trees. This is an important topic since current methods train trees in a greedy manner, layer by layer. The task is phrased as a single optimization problem which is then upper-bounded and approximated to obtain a tractable formulation.

The paper is well written but there might be flaws in the reasoning. The derivation is clean but experiments and their presentation could be improved. Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important topic. The proposed solution is valuable although there seem to be some shortcomings.

The main idea of the paper is to establish a link between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables. The authors propose a novel formulation of decision tree learning that associates a binary latent decision variable with each split node in the tree and uses such latent variables to formulate the tree's empirical loss. The paper then develops a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and proposes a stochastic gradient descent algorithm to optimize the surrogate objective.

One potential issue with the paper is the derivation of the empirical loss bound. The argument in line 216 is confusing, and it is not clear if the upper bound is valid in all cases. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not cover all cases and is therefore incomplete.

Another issue is that some of the leaves are not assigned any data points, which seems strange and could indicate a problem with the objective or the initialization. The suggested heuristic of a fixed assignment of data-points to tree leaves seems sub-optimal and could be motivated via expectation maximization/concave-convex procedure, similar to other clustering techniques.

The importance of the efficient loss-augmented inference is also not clear. The depth of the tree seems to be at most 16, and greedily checking 2^16 = 65536 values still seems feasible. It is not clear how much the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball impacted performance.

Additionally, a baseline that trains non-axis aligned split functions of trees in a standard greedy manner is missing. The depth of the tree needs to be specified ahead of time, and this limitation should be discussed more carefully. The authors could also provide a more careful experimental evaluation regarding the depth parameter.

However, the paper is well written, and the authors have made a significant effort to explain the reasoning behind the loss bound. A more careful evaluation of the method would be desirable for the reader. The submission considers an interesting problem of jointly training all parameters of decision trees, and the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited.
