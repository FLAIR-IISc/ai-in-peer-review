### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel algorithm for the non-greedy, global optimization of decision trees. The core idea is to reframe the problem of learning optimal oblique split functions as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. Here, the latent variables are binary vectors representing the path taken through the tree for a given input. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective function. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for the loss-augmented inference step required by SGD, reducing the complexity from exponential to quadratic in the tree depth, thereby making the optimization of deep trees feasible. The method is evaluated on several classification benchmarks, where it is shown to outperform standard greedy tree induction methods.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Novelty:** The primary strength of this work lies in its reformulation of decision tree optimization. Casting the global learning problem as structured prediction with latent variables is a clever and insightful approach that connects two previously disparate areas of machine learning. This fresh perspective is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a sound technical framework. The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss is well-founded. The development of an efficient algorithm for loss-augmented inference is crucial for the method's practicality and demonstrates a solid understanding of the computational challenges involved.

3.  **Potential for Future Work:** The proposed framework raises several interesting theoretical questions and opens avenues for future research. For example, the authors note that the gradient updates are sparse. This property could potentially be leveraged to develop methods that grow trees dynamically. One could imagine initializing all node weights to zero and allowing the optimization process to selectively activate and refine splits, letting the model's complexity adapt to the data. This connects the work to concepts of greedy-wise learning and regularization paths, suggesting a depth and potential beyond what is presented.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection)**

1.  **Practical Motivation and Significance:** A significant concern is the practical utility of the proposed method. Decision trees are often favored for their interpretability, a property that is largely lost when using dense, oblique splits produced by L2-regularized linear classifiers. If interpretability is not a requirement, other methods like Random Forests or Gradient Boosted Trees typically offer superior performance. The paper does not clearly articulate the specific application domain or scenario where this particular trade-off—sacrificing interpretability for a modest performance gain over a single greedy tree—is advantageous. While L1 regularization could potentially induce sparsity and restore some interpretability, this is not explored, and it remains uncertain whether performance would be maintained.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experimental comparison with baselines is not entirely convincing.
    *   **Baseline Tuning:** The paper compares against standard greedy methods but provides no details on the baselines' hyperparameter tuning, such as the pruning strategies employed. Since the proposed method's regularization parameter (`ν`) was carefully tuned, a fair comparison would require a similarly rigorous tuning process for the baselines (e.g., evaluating different pruning parameters on a validation set).
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The paper could be strengthened by including other simple, non-greedy baselines. For instance, a simple global optimization strategy would be to train multiple greedy trees on different random subsets of the data and select the one that performs best on a validation set. Comparing against such a method would help contextualize the performance gains achieved by the proposed complex optimization framework.

3.  **Clarity and Ambiguity:**
    *   **Inference Sections:** While generally well-written, the clarity of the sections describing the inference algorithms (e.g., Sections 6) could be improved. A more high-level summary explaining the logic—first maximizing over all paths leading to a specific leaf, and then maximizing over the leaves—before diving into the details would enhance readability.
    *   **Zero-Weight Case:** A critical technical detail is left ambiguous: the behavior of a split node when its weight vector `w_i` is zero. The sign function is undefined at zero, and the paper does not specify whether the left or right branch is taken. This is an important edge case, especially considering the possibility of initializing weights to zero or having them driven to zero by regularization. This behavior is directly analogous to the decision of whether to split a node or turn it into a leaf in greedy algorithms.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   The connection between the sparse gradient updates and the potential for a tree-growing algorithm seems particularly promising. Could the authors comment on whether their method, with appropriate regularization (e.g., starting with zero weights and applying L1 regularization), might naturally converge to a tree of limited depth, effectively learning the structure and parameters simultaneously?
*   Have the authors considered L1 regularization to produce sparse, axis-aligned, or nearly axis-aligned splits? This could address the interpretability concern and provide a more direct comparison to methods like C4.5 or CART while retaining the global optimization framework.
*   The title mentions "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees," yet the abstract and introduction also reference "randomized forests." The paper, however, focuses exclusively on single decision trees. It would be clearer to focus the paper's scope on single trees or explicitly discuss how the framework extends to forests.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces a highly clever and original approach to the long-standing problem of global decision tree optimization. The formulation as a latent structured prediction problem is elegant, and the resulting optimization algorithm is technically sound. However, the work is weakened by an unclear motivation regarding its practical niche and an experimental evaluation that lacks sufficient rigor in its comparison to baselines. While the theoretical contribution is strong, its practical significance remains to be demonstrated more convincingly.
