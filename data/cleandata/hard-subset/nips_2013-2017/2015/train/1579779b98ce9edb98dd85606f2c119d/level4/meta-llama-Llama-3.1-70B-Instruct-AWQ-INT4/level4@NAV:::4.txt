The authors present an innovative approach to global optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables representing the decision-making process at each internal node. A non-differentiable and non-convex loss function is introduced, which is approximated using a differentiable upper bound. The proposed method utilizes stochastic gradient descent to minimize the loss, involving the solution of a series of loss-augmented inference problems. Efficient algorithms are provided to tackle these problems. The experimental results compare the proposed method to a standard greedy training approach.

The key contributions of this work lie in casting global decision tree optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms for optimizing the objective. This novel approach raises intriguing questions and offers a fresh perspective on the problem. The method's ability to perform stochastic gradient descent by solving loss-augmented optimization problems, leveraging the problem's special structure, is a significant advantage. Furthermore, the sparse gradient updates, which involve modifying only one node's weight vector at each step, present opportunities for optimizing infinitely deep trees by gradually introducing non-zero weights.

However, several concerns regarding practical considerations need to be addressed. The motivation behind the method and the significance of its performance gains over naive approaches are not entirely convincing. The submission mainly highlights the computational advantages of sparse gradient updates, but this property also raises interesting possibilities that warrant further exploration. For instance, optimizing over an infinitely deep tree by gradually introducing non-zero weights could lead to a model whose complexity grows naturally to fit the data. This approach may be equivalent to known greedy strategies or, with appropriate regularization, converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes.

A critical issue that requires clarification is the handling of zero weights. The current discussion implies taking the right branch, which seems questionable. Instead, outputting a decision at the node and later introducing non-zero weights could be a more suitable approach. This issue has a direct analog in pruning strategies for greedy training and deserves precise definition.

Another significant concern is the interpretability of the results. Decision trees are primarily useful due to their interpretability, and dropping this requirement often leads to better performance with other methods. The proposed method may produce non-interpretable results due to L2 regularization, which could be mitigated by switching to L1 regularization. However, this may compromise performance, and further testing is necessary.

The experimental results lack sufficient detail, particularly regarding baselines. The pruning strategies employed for the naive approach are not specified, and other trivial strategies for global optimization, such as training greedy methods with random subsets of the training data, are not explored. The paper's clarity is generally good, but some sections, such as those describing inference, could be improved.

In summary, this approach is clever and thought-provoking, but its practical utility is uncertain due to concerns regarding interpretability and experimental details. Further analysis and testing are necessary to fully appreciate the method's potential.
