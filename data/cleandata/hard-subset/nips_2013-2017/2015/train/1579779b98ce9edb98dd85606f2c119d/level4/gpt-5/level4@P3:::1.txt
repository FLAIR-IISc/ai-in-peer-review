Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy method for training oblique decision trees by optimizing a single global objective over all internal split functions and leaf parameters. The key idea is to cast the split decisions at internal nodes as latent variables and derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss inspired by structured prediction with latent variables (e.g., structural SVMs with margin rescaling). The authors show that naive loss-augmented inference would be exponential in tree depth, and they introduce a fast surrogate that restricts loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius one around the current hard decisions, yielding O(d^2 p) complexity per SGD step (d = depth, p = feature dimension). Training proceeds via SGD with per-row norm constraints on W to control the tightness of the bound and prevent degenerate solutions; a “stable SGD” variant fixes data-to-leaf assignments for several updates to avoid deactivating leaves prematurely. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare single non-greedy trees with axis-aligned greedy trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and a greedy one-node-at-a-time variant (CO2). The method typically achieves higher test accuracy and appears less prone to overfitting at larger depths.

Relation to prior work
The work connects classical non-greedy tree optimization (e.g., Bennett’s MILP approach) and margin-based tree methods to modern structured prediction with latent variables. It contrasts with soft/probabilistic tree models such as Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts, which sacrifice the computational efficiency of hard trees. Compared to OC1 and related oblique-split heuristics, the contribution is a principled, jointly optimized surrogate objective with efficient loss-augmented inference. The CO2 method is covered as a special-case greedy variant of the proposed bound. The paper also discusses known hardness results for optimal tree construction and common regularization/pruning practices in greedy trees.

Quality
- Theory: The latent-variable reformulation, bound derivation, and complexity analysis are clear and technically sound. The effect of the W-norm on bound tightness is well-motivated, and the per-row norm constraint is a reasonable regularizer given independent split functions.
- Algorithms: The fast loss-augmented inference via a radius-1 Hamming ball is a practical and elegant speedup. The projection step for W, and the gradients for both W and Θ, are straightforward.
- Empirics: The experimental methodology is reasonable (multiple datasets, depth sweeps, tuning ν and learning rate on validation sets). However, improvements over strong baselines are sometimes modest; the study focuses on single trees, whereas ensembles dominate practice. There is limited analysis of robustness to initialization and of the SSGD schedule. Regression experiments, although supported by the framework, are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is very well written and organized. The latent-variable perspective, navigation function, and upper-bound derivations are explained cleanly, and pseudocode is helpful. A few small improvements would help: briefly restate the intuition behind the Hamming-ball restriction when it is introduced, and specify the SSGD schedule (how many steps between reassignments, stopping criteria) more concretely.

Originality
The link between hard decision trees and latent structured prediction with an efficiently optimizable surrogate is novel and insightful. The fast loss-augmented inference and the use of per-row norm constraints within this framework are also original contributions beyond prior oblique-split methods and the authors’ CO2 variant.

Significance
Conceptually, this paper opens a promising direction: non-greedy, globally trained hard trees with tractable optimization and inference. Practically, the single-tree gains are not dramatic across all datasets, which tempers the immediate impact. Still, the approach is likely to stimulate further research (e.g., sparse or kernelized splits, better schedules for leaf assignment, hybrid training with greedier refinements) and could improve state-of-the-art when integrated into ensembles.

Strengths
- Elegant, principled formulation of non-greedy tree training via a latent-variable upper bound.
- Efficient training with O(d^2 p) per update using fast loss-augmented inference.
- Clear connection to structured prediction that may enable transfer of additional techniques.
- Careful analysis of norm regularization and its role in generalization and leaf pruning.
- Strong clarity and reproducibility of the core method.

Weaknesses
- Empirical gains over baselines are sometimes modest; benchmarks are limited to a few multiclass datasets and single-tree settings.
- No demonstration on regression tasks, despite a general loss formulation.
- Optimization remains nonconvex and sensitive to initialization; SSGD heuristics are not fully characterized.
- Limited analysis of overfitting and leaf utilization beyond anecdotal observations; no statistical significance tests.
- No comparison to soft/differentiable tree baselines in practice, or to modern ensemble methods (even briefly) to contextualize achievable accuracy.

Suggestions for improvement
- Examine overfitting more systematically: learning curves vs. depth and ν, fraction of inactive leaves, and stability of leaf assignments over training.
- Evaluate a simple hybrid: after global optimization, discard and re-optimize the last split (and its leaves) greedily; or jointly optimize upper levels and refine the last level via standard criteria. This could yield consistent accuracy gains at low extra cost.
- Report regression results to demonstrate generality.
- Provide ablations on initialization (axis-aligned vs. random) and SSGD schedules.
- Include ensembles: demonstrate whether globally-trained trees improve forests (bagging/boosting) relative to greedy baselines at comparable computation.
- Consider sparse regularization for W to improve interpretability and speed, and explore small-kernel feature maps to test the promised kernelization.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, original conceptual contribution with a clean and efficient algorithm.
- Clear, well-executed paper likely to catalyze follow-up work on non-greedy tree learning.
- Demonstrated accuracy improvements and reduced overfitting trends across multiple datasets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental evidence is not uniformly compelling; gains are modest in places and limited to single trees on a small set of benchmarks.
- Some practical aspects (regression, ensembles, SSGD schedule) are not fully evaluated.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written and elegant paper that advances the methodology of decision tree learning by framing global training within a latent structured prediction framework and providing an efficient optimization strategy. While the empirical results are not uniformly strong, the technical contribution and potential for impact justify acceptance. With stronger experimental validation (especially hybrid refinements and ensembles), the work would be even more compelling.
