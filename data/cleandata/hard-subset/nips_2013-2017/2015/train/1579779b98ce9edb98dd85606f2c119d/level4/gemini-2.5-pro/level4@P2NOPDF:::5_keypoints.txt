- Proposes joint/global optimization of oblique decision trees (linear split functions and leaf distributions) via minimizing a regularized upper bound on empirical loss with SGD.  
- Novel, principled formulation linking tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables.  
- Empirical results are weak: significant improvements over greedy baselines appear on only a few datasets.  
- Method incurs considerably higher computational cost, making practical benefits unclear.  
- Reproducibility/clarity needed for the "stable SGD" variant—mechanism for assigning data points to leaves during training is not explained; pseudo-code requested.  
- Figure 2: unclear whether results use standard SGD or stable SGD; the term "active leaf" is undefined.  
- Algorithm 1 (line 10): projection of Θ onto a simplex needs a specified practical solver and explicit statement that rows of Θ are projected to sum to one (preserving probability interpretation).  
- Line 407–408: clarify the hyperparameter described as "number of features evaluated" for axis-aligned trees (standard trees typically evaluate all features).  
- Equations (7), (8), (9): replace "argmax" operator with "max."  
- Figure 1 is not referenced in the main text.  
- Line 141: sentence fragment/grammar issue ("...to the index of the leaf on by this path.").  
- Line 238: grammatical number error—"the solution to loss-augmented inference" likely should be plural.  
- Line 301: typo—"An key observation..." should be "A key observation...".  
- Line 360: acronym "FSGD" used without prior definition.  
- Line 404: ambiguous phrase "...a tree with minimum training errors"—clarify whether this refers to training or validation/tuning error.  
- Supplementary Line 29: incorrect/max set notation `max{g ∈ sgn...}`—should be `max{g = sgn...}` or clarify the maximization set.  
- Supplementary Line 30: probable typo in loss term—`l(Θ^T f(g), y)` should likely be `l(Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)), y)` for consistency.  
- Overall summary: strong theoretical contribution and elegant formulation, but insufficient empirical evidence and high computational cost limit practical adoption.
