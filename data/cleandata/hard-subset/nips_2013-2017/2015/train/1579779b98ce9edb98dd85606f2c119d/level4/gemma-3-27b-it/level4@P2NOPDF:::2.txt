This paper introduces a method for jointly training all parameters – decision splits and posterior probabilities – of decision trees. This is a significant contribution, as current approaches typically employ a greedy, layer-by-layer training process. The authors formulate the problem as a single optimization task, which they then upper-bound and approximate to arrive at a tractable solution.

**Quality:** The paper is generally well-written, but a potential flaw exists in the reasoning supporting the empirical loss bound, detailed below.

**Clarity:** The derivation is presented cleanly; however, the experiments and their presentation could be improved for greater understanding.

**Originality:** Investigating joint training for decision trees represents an important and novel research direction.

**Significance:** The proposed solution holds value, despite some apparent limitations outlined below.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Empirical Loss Bound:** I have concerns regarding the derivation of the empirical loss bound. Specifically, the argument presented around line 216 is unclear. To illustrate, consider the following example: Let $W\bx = [5; -5]$. Then $\hat \bh = [1; -1] = \sgn(W\bx)$, and $\hat \bh^T W\bx = 10$. If we choose $g = [1; 1]$, then $g^T W\bx = 0$. Therefore, the right-hand side of Equation (7) becomes $g^T W\bx - \hat \bh^T W\bx + \ell(\dots) = -10 + \ell(\dots)$, which could be *smaller* than $\ell(\dots)$, thus invalidating the upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not address this case, rendering it incomplete. *[Note: I acknowledge the authors' clarification regarding this point; a replacement of $\arg\max$ with $\max$ may resolve this issue, and my score reflects this potential correction.]*

*   **Unassigned Data Points:** I observe that some leaves are not assigned any data points. This is counterintuitive and might indicate an issue with the objective function or initialization procedure. The current heuristic of a fixed assignment of data points to leaves seems sub-optimal. Could this be motivated by an expectation-maximization or concave-convex procedure, analogous to other clustering methods?

*   **Loss-Augmented Inference Efficiency:** The paper discusses the importance of designing efficient loss-augmented inference. However, the tree depth is limited to a maximum of 16, and a greedy search over $2^{16} = 65536$ values appears feasible. It would be helpful to understand how significantly the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball impacted performance. The supplementary material provides one result, but it’s unclear if this generalizes. Furthermore, the meaning of “leaves” in this context requires clarification.

*   **Missing Baseline:** A crucial baseline comparison is missing: training non-axis-aligned split functions with a standard, greedy approach *without* any initialization. This would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed method's advantages.

*   **Fixed Tree Depth:** The requirement to pre-specify the tree depth is a limitation that needs more thorough discussion. The authors should provide a more extensive experimental evaluation exploring the impact of different depth parameters.

**Overall:**

This submission addresses an interesting and important problem – the joint training of all decision tree parameters. While the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and potential issues exist in the derivation, the core idea is promising. A more detailed analysis of runtime performance, coupled with a more comprehensive experimental evaluation and a resolution to the concerns about the loss bound, would significantly strengthen the paper. I am willing to adjust my score based on a compelling rebuttal addressing these points.
