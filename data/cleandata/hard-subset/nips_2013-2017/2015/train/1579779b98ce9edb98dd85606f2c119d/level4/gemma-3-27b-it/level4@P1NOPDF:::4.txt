The submission proposes an algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees based on a reformulation of the problem as (latent) structured prediction. The latent structured variables are sign vectors encoding the decisions made at each internal node in the tree. A non-differentiable and non-convex loss is proposed, which is then approximated by a differentiable (but still non-convex) upper bound. Minimizing this loss via SGD involves solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided. Experiments compare this method to a standard greedy training method. 

The key contributions are casting global decision tree optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms to optimize the objective. This approach is novel and interesting, and it raises several compelling questions.

I have some concerns, however, regarding practical considerations—particularly the motivation for the method and the significance of the performance gains over simpler approaches. Considered solely as a method for globally optimizing decision trees, the work is intriguing. 

One advantage of framing the optimization as structured prediction is the ability to perform SGD by solving a sequence of loss-augmented optimization problems that leverage the problem’s structure. However, I believe the most interesting result is the sparsity of the gradient updates; if I understand correctly, each gradient step changes only one node’s weight vector. The submission highlights this mainly as a computational advantage, but I think this property opens up more intriguing possibilities that are not fully explored in the paper. 

One could imagine optimizing over an infinitely deep tree by initially setting all weights to zero and then gradually growing the tree by making more weights non-zero, allowing the model’s complexity to grow naturally with the data. This raises questions: would such a method be equivalent to, or a variation of, any known greedy strategy? This might be the case if it’s always preferable to introduce a new node rather than revise existing weights. Alternatively, with appropriate regularization, would the method converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes, as suggested by Fig. 2? This issue is central to the value proposition of the proposed method compared to naive decision trees and deserves more careful analysis.

For instance, a critical ambiguity is this: what happens when a node’s weight is equal to zero? The discussion on page 3 implies taking the right branch, which seems questionable. It seems preferable to output a decision at that node, with the possibility of later making the weight non-zero to trigger a split. Precisely defining this behavior is important, as it directly relates to pruning strategies used in greedy training.

I have a significant practical concern regarding the motivation for the work. Plain decision trees are valuable primarily because of their interpretability. If interpretability is not a requirement, other methods generally offer superior performance. As presented, I expect this method to produce non-interpretable results due to the dense weight vectors resulting from L2 regularization. While switching to L1 regularization might help, performance could suffer if regularization is too strong and forces exactly 1-sparse splits. This should be investigated.

My other main concern is the lack of detail in the experimental evaluation. No details are provided regarding the pruning strategies employed for the baseline greedy approach. Since a range of regularization parameters were explored for the proposed method, the baseline should be evaluated with several different pruning strategies and/or parameters. It would also be fair to test other straightforward global optimization strategies, such as training a greedy method with random subsets of the training data and selecting the model that minimizes the desired loss on the full training or validation set.

Regarding clarity, the paper is reasonably well-written. Inference could be described more clearly. For example, Section 6.2 could be summarized as first maximizing the objective for all *g* corresponding to a given leaf, and then maximizing over all leaves. Section 6.4 could also be more explicit.

In summary, this is a clever approach that raises interesting questions. However, it’s unclear whether it’s a practically useful method at this point, given the interpretability issue—why use plain decision trees if interpretability is not desired?—and the lack of detail in the experiments.

PS: Although the title mentions forests, the paper does not address this case.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
I still believe the case of zero weights is a critical one that warrants further analysis, for the reasons I outlined in my review. Reading the rebuttal, it appears the weights are likely initialized randomly, which is why this issue doesn't arise in practice. However, I suspect that the most compelling aspect of this method would be the case where weights are initially zero and then gradually increased. I encourage the authors to explore this direction. The submission proposes a clever reformulation of global decision tree optimization as structured prediction, alongside an efficient algorithm for solving the optimization. While the approach is promising, some aspects of the motivation and experimental results are not fully convincing.
