**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method for the non-greedy, global optimization of decision trees. Whereas standard algorithms train trees in a greedy, node-by-node fashion, this work formulates the training of all split functions and leaf parameters as a single joint optimization problem. The key idea is to establish a connection to structured prediction with latent variables, where the path an example takes through the tree is treated as a latent variable. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a tractable surrogate objective. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent, with an efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm that makes the approach practical for deep trees.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and the core derivation is presented clearly. The formulation connecting decision tree learning to latent structured prediction is elegant and novel. However, there are some potential issues with the technical reasoning and the experimental evaluation could be more thorough.

**Originality and Significance**

The problem of non-greedy decision tree optimization is long-standing and important. By framing it in a modern optimization context (latent SVMs), the authors provide a valuable new perspective. If technically sound, the proposed solution would be a significant contribution, offering a principled way to train trees that could lead to better performance and more compact models.

**Detailed Comments**

1.  **Concern about the Empirical Loss Bound:** I have a significant concern regarding the validity of the upper bound derived in Equation (7). The argument on lines 215-217 states that for any **g** ≠ sgn(W**x**), the right-hand side (RHS) of the inequality can only increase. However, this does not seem to hold in general. Consider the following simple example:
    *   Let W**x** = [5, -5]ᵀ.
    *   Then **ĥ** = sgn(W**x**) = [1, -1]ᵀ, and the score **ĥ**ᵀW**x** = 10.
    *   The left-hand side (LHS) of Eq. (7) is `ℓ(Θᵀf(ĥ), y)`.
    *   Now, choose a different path **g** = [1, 1]ᵀ. The score for this path is **g**ᵀW**x** = 0.
    *   The first term on the RHS becomes max_**g**(**g**ᵀW**x** + `ℓ(Θᵀf(**g**), y)`). For our chosen **g**, this term is `0 + ℓ(Θᵀf([1,1]ᵀ), y)`.
    *   The full RHS, evaluated for this **g**, would be `0 + ℓ(...) - 10`. This value could easily be smaller than the LHS, `ℓ(...)`, which would violate the upper bound.
    The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not seem to address this case and may therefore be incomplete. A clarification on this point is crucial for assessing the technical soundness of the paper.

2.  **Inactive Leaves and the SSGD Heuristic:** The paper notes that during training, some leaf nodes may end up with no data points assigned to them. This is a curious phenomenon that could indicate an issue with the objective function or initialization. The proposed "Stable SGD" (SSGD) algorithm, which fixes the data-to-leaf assignments for several updates, appears to be a somewhat ad-hoc heuristic. Could this procedure be more formally motivated? For instance, it bears resemblance to alternating optimization schemes and might be justifiable within an Expectation-Maximization or a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) framework.

3.  **Motivation for Fast Loss-Augmented Inference:** The paper introduces a "fast" inference method that restricts the search for **ĝ** to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around **ĥ**. While this clearly improves the asymptotic complexity, its practical necessity for the experiments presented is not fully established. The maximum tree depth tested is 18. An exhaustive search over all leaves requires evaluating O(m) = O(2^d) paths. For a depth of 16, this is 2^16 ≈ 65,000 evaluations, which seems computationally feasible. A more thorough ablation study is needed to show the performance trade-off. How much classification accuracy is lost by restricting the search space with the Hamming ball approximation? The single result in the supplement is insufficient to assess if this finding generalizes.

4.  **Experimental Baselines:** The experimental comparison could be strengthened. Specifically, a key baseline appears to be missing: a standard greedy algorithm for learning oblique decision trees that is not used as part of the proposed method's initialization. While OC1 is included, comparing the fully non-greedy method against a purely greedy oblique tree learner (trained from scratch) would help to more clearly isolate the benefits of the global optimization strategy.

5.  **Fixed Tree Depth:** The proposed method requires the tree depth to be specified in advance. This is a practical limitation that should be discussed in more detail. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation analyzing the method's sensitivity to the depth parameter would also be valuable for readers.

**Conclusion**

This paper addresses the important problem of global decision tree training with a novel and interesting approach. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and more importantly, there appears to be a potential flaw in the derivation of the core theoretical result (the loss upper bound). I am willing to reconsider my assessment if the authors can provide a careful and convincing explanation in their rebuttal.
