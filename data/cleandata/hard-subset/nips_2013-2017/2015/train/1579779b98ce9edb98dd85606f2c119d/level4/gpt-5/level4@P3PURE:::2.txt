Summary: The paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters of decision trees (both decision splits and posterior probabilities). This is an important topic because most existing approaches train trees greedily, typically layer by layer. The authors recast the task as a single optimization problem, then derive an upper bound and approximations to obtain a tractable formulation.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The paper is well written, though there may be flaws in the reasoning (see comments below).
- Clarity: The derivations are clean, but the experiments and their presentation could be improved.
- Originality: Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important and timely topic.
- Significance: The proposed solution is valuable, although there appear to be some shortcomings (see comments below).

Comments and questions:
1) Possible flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound (Eq. 7). At the very least, the argument in l.216f is confusing: “... for all values of g ≠ sgn(Wx), the right-hand side can only become larger than when g = sgn(Wx) ...” Consider the following example illustrating my confusion:
- Wx = [5; −5]
- ĥ = [1; −1] = sgn(Wx)
- ĥ^T Wx = 10
Choosing g = [1; 1] gives g^T Wx = 0. Therefore, the right-hand side of Eq. (7) is
g^T Wx − ĥ^T Wx + ℓ(...) = −10 + ℓ(...)
which could be smaller than ℓ(...), and hence would not constitute a valid upper bound. In addition, the proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not cover this case and therefore appears incomplete.

2) Unassigned leaves. Some leaves are not assigned any data points. This is surprising and could indicate a problem with the objective or the initialization. The suggested heuristic of a fixed assignment of data points to tree leaves seems suboptimal, though it might be possible to motivate it via an expectation–maximization or concave–convex procedure argument, similar to other clustering techniques.

3) Efficient loss-augmented inference. The tree depth in the experiments appears to be at most 16; a greedy check over all 2^16 = 65,536 assignments still seems feasible. How much does restricting the search to a Hamming ball impact performance in practice? You report one result in the supplementary material—does this generalize to other settings? Also, please clarify precisely what is meant by “leaves” in that context.

4) Missing baseline. Please include a baseline that trains non–axis-aligned split functions using a standard greedy procedure without any special initialization.

5) Depth selection. The tree depth must be specified a priori. This limitation should be discussed more carefully, and a more systematic experimental evaluation of the depth parameter would be helpful.

Post-rebuttal update:
- Thanks to the authors for carefully explaining the reasoning behind the loss bound (Eq. 7). You might want to replace argmax with max. As stated in my summary, I adjusted my score accordingly.
- An experiment comparing runtime would strengthen the paper, and a more thorough empirical evaluation would be valuable for readers.

The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and there could potentially be a flaw in the derivation. I indicated I would be happy to adjust my score given a careful explanation in the rebuttal.
