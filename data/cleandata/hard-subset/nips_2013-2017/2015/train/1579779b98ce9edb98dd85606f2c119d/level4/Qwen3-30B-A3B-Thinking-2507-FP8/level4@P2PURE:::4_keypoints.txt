- Contribution: Reformulates global decision-tree optimization as latent structured prediction with sign-vector latent variables and provides efficient algorithms for loss-augmented inference.
- Novelty: Casting global tree learning as structured prediction is novel and interesting.
- Loss: Introduces a non-differentiable, non-convex loss and a differentiable upper bound, optimized via SGD.
- Computational claim: Claims sparsity in gradient updates (each step modifies one node) as a primary computational benefit.
- Missing analysis: Sparse updates suggest a natural tree-growth procedure (start with zero weights and grow), but the paper fails to analyze whether this reduces to known greedy strategies or yields meaningful pruning behavior.
- Zero-weight ambiguity: It is unclear what decision is taken when a node's weight is zero; authors imply always taking the right branch, which is problematic and needs precise definition.
- Regularization/interpretability tension: L2 regularization likely produces dense, non-interpretable splits; trade-offs with L1 sparsity and resulting performance are not explored empirically.
- Practical motivation weak: The paper does not justify why non-interpretable, dense global trees are preferable to other models that typically outperform trees.
- Experimental shortcomings: Experiments lack sufficient baseline details (e.g., pruning strategies for greedy baselines) and comparisons to simple global strategies like training multiple greedy trees on random subsets.
- Title mismatch: The title mentions "forests" but the paper does not address or evaluate forests.
- Presentation issues: Section 6.2 should clarify the nested maximization (over g for a leaf, then over leaves); Section 6.4 needs more explicitness.
- Rebuttal gap: Authors claim random initialization avoids zero-weight issues, but the compelling zero-to-grown-tree idea remains unexplored and should be pursued.
