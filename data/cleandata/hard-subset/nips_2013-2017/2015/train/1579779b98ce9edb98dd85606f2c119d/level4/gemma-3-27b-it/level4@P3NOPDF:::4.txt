The submission proposes an algorithm for global optimization of decision trees based on a reformulation of the problem as (latent) structured prediction. The latent structured variables are sign vectors encoding the decisions made at each internal node in the tree. A non-differentiable and non-convex loss is proposed, which is then approximated by a differentiable (but still non-convex) upper bound. Minimizing this loss via SGD entails solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are given. Experiments are shown comparing this method to a standard greedy training method.

The key contributions of the work are casting global decision tree optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms to optimize the objective. This approach is novel and interesting, raising several thought-provoking questions. However, I have some concerns regarding the practical motivation and the significance of the performance gains.

The primary advantage of framing the optimization as structured prediction is the ability to leverage the problem’s structure with SGD by solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems. More interestingly, the gradient updates appear to be sparse – if my understanding is correct, each step modifies only one node’s weight vector. While the paper highlights this as a computational advantage, it hints at potentially more significant possibilities that remain largely unexplored. 

Specifically, one could envision optimizing an infinitely deep tree by initializing all weights to zero and gradually growing it by introducing non-zero weights, allowing model complexity to emerge naturally from the data. This raises questions about its relationship to existing greedy strategies. Would this method be equivalent to, or a variation of, a known greedy approach? Perhaps it’s always preferable to add new nodes rather than revise existing ones. Alternatively, with appropriate regularization, could it converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes, as suggested by Fig. 2? This question strikes at the core of the method’s value proposition compared to standard decision trees and warrants a more thorough investigation.

A critical ambiguity arises concerning the behavior when a node’s weight equals zero. The discussion on page 3 implies following the right branch, which seems questionable. Outputting a decision at such a node, with the possibility of later making the weight non-zero to induce a split, seems more sensible. A precise definition of this behavior is vital, as it directly mirrors pruning strategies used in greedy training.

I have a significant practical concern about the motivation. Plain decision trees are valuable largely due to their interpretability; when interpretability is unimportant, other methods usually perform better. The proposed method, as presented, is likely to produce non-interpretable results due to the dense weight vectors induced by L2 regularization. Switching to L1 regularization might improve interpretability, but performance could suffer if regularization is strong enough to enforce strict sparsity. This trade-off requires investigation.

My other main concern relates to the experiments. They lack sufficient detail regarding baselines. No information is provided on the pruning strategies used for the naive approach. Given the parameter tuning for the proposed method, it’s reasonable to expect similar exploration of pruning strategies and parameters for the naive method. Including other straightforward global optimization strategies, such as training a greedy method on random subsets of the data and selecting the best performer on the full training or validation set, would also strengthen the evaluation.

The paper is reasonably well-written, but the explanation of inference could be clearer. For example, Section 6.2 could be concisely summarized as maximizing the objective first for all *g* corresponding to a given leaf, then maximizing over all leaves. Section 6.4 could also benefit from more explicit details.

In summary, this is a clever approach that sparks interesting questions. However, its practical utility remains unclear, due to the interpretability concerns – why use a decision tree if interpretability is sacrificed? – and the limited detail in the experiments. 

PS: The title mentions forests, but the paper doesn't address this case.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**

I maintain that the case of zero weights is a critical aspect deserving further analysis, as previously outlined. The rebuttal suggests random initialization mitigates the issue in practice. However, I believe that initializing weights to zero and allowing them to grow organically would be where this method truly shines. I encourage the authors to explore this direction. The submission presents a clever reformulation of global decision tree optimization as structured prediction, coupled with an efficient algorithm for its solution. However, certain aspects of the motivation and experimental results require further strengthening.
