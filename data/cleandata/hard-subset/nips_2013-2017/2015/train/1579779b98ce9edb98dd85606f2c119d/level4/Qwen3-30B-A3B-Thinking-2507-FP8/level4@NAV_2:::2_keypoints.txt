- Proposes jointly training all decision-tree parameters via a single optimization using a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss.  
- Technical approach appears sound but contains a critical flaw in the empirical loss bound derivation (Equation 7).  
- Counterexample (Wx = [5; -5], g = [1; 1]) shows the RHS can be smaller than ℓ(·), questioning the upper-bound claim.  
- Supplementary proof of Proposition 1 does not address this counterexample; derivation needs careful re-examination or correction.  
- Mathematical presentation is clean, but the experimental section lacks sufficient detail, comparisons, and visualizations (e.g., leaf assignments, depth analysis).  
- The connection between regularization parameter ν and tree pruning is not adequately explained.  
- Joint training for decision trees is an important, novel contribution linking trees to structured prediction with latent variables.  
- Method differs from prior non-greedy work and claims efficient O(d²p) joint optimization.  
- Practical significance is promising for deep trees, but the unresolved theoretical issue reduces confidence in results.  
- Some leaves become inactive during training; authors must explain whether this is due to the objective or optimization, and justify the "Stable SGD" heuristic theoretically.  
- Efficiency claims for the Hamming-ball restriction in loss-augmented inference are unconvincing, since depth ≤16 allows brute-force enumeration.  
- A key baseline is missing: standard greedy training for non-axis-aligned trees without the proposed initialization/optimization.  
- Requiring tree depth to be specified in advance is a significant limitation that is insufficiently analyzed experimentally.  
- Clarify the meaning of "leaves" when referring to the Hamming ball B₁(sgn(Wx)) in fast loss-augmented inference.  
- Provide a detailed comparison of training times between standard loss-augmented inference and the proposed fast version, especially on large datasets.  
- Reviewer is willing to revise their score if the authors provide a careful, convincing explanation or fix of the bound derivation in the rebuttal.
