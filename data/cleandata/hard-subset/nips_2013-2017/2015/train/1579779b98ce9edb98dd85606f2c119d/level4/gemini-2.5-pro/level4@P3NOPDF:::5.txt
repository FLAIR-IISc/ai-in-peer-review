### **Review Summary**

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for learning a decision tree with linear split functions. The key contribution is a global optimization approach that jointly learns all tree parameters—the weights of the splitting hyperplanes and the label distributions at the leaves. This is framed as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, where the path a data point takes through the tree is the latent variable. The authors derive a convex upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, regularize it, and minimize it using stochastic gradient descent (SGD).

While the problem formulation is elegant and provides a principled alternative to the standard greedy, node-by-node training of decision trees, the empirical results presented are not sufficiently convincing. The proposed global optimization method demonstrates a significant advantage over a greedy baseline on only a few datasets, a marginal gain that may not justify its increased computational complexity.

### **Strengths**

The main strength of this work is its elegant and novel formulation of the decision tree learning problem. By establishing a connection to structured prediction with latent variables, the authors move away from the traditional greedy and heuristic-driven approaches. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for globally optimizing the entire tree structure, which is a commendable and non-trivial contribution. The derivation of a convex upper bound on the empirical loss allows the problem to be tackled with well-understood optimization techniques like SGD.

### **Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its empirical evaluation. For a new, more complex, and computationally intensive algorithm to be adopted, it must demonstrate a clear and consistent advantage over existing, simpler methods. The results presented here do not make a compelling case.

1.  **Limited Performance Gain:** The globally optimized tree significantly outperforms the greedily trained tree on only a couple of the datasets. On many others, the performance is comparable or only marginally better. This raises the question of whether the additional complexity of the global optimization is worthwhile in practice.
2.  **Computational Cost:** The paper acknowledges that the proposed method is more computationally intensive. Given the modest performance gains, the trade-off between accuracy and computational cost appears to favor the simpler greedy approach in many scenarios. A more detailed analysis of training times would be beneficial to quantify this trade-off.

### **Questions and Suggestions for Clarification**

The paper is generally well-structured, but several key aspects of the methodology and experiments require clarification to improve understanding and reproducibility.

1.  **Stable SGD:** The description of the "stable" variant of SGD is unclear. The mechanism for assigning data points to specific leaves during updates is not sufficiently detailed. Providing pseudo-code for this algorithm would be extremely helpful.
2.  **Figure 2 Details:** For the results presented in Figure 2, it is not specified whether they were obtained using the standard SGD or the stable SGD variant. Furthermore, the term "active" leaf is used without a clear definition. Please clarify what constitutes an active leaf in this context.
3.  **Projection onto a Simplex:** In Algorithm 1 (line 10), the parameter Θ is updated and subsequently projected onto a simplex. Could the authors elaborate on how this projection problem is solved in practice? It would also be worth explicitly mentioning that this projection step ensures that each row of Θ represents a valid probability distribution (i.e., its elements are non-negative and sum to one).

### **Minor Comments and Typos**

-   **Lines 407-408:** The text states that you tune the "number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree." Standard decision tree algorithms typically evaluate all features at each node. Please clarify if this refers to a method with feature sub-sampling, such as that used in Random Forests.
-   **Equations (7), (8), (9):** The "argmax" operator should be replaced with "max," as the goal is to find the maximum value of the expression, not the argument that maximizes it.
-   **Figure 1:** Figure 1 is not referenced in the main body of the text.
-   **Line 141:** The sentence "...to the index of the leaf on by this path" is grammatically incorrect. It should likely read "...to the index of the leaf reached by this path."
-   **Line 238:** "...the solution to loss-augmented inference..." should be plural: "...the solutions to..."
-   **Line 301:** "An key observation..." should be "A key observation..."
-   **Line 360:** The acronym "FSGD" is used without prior definition. Please define it.
-   **Line 404:** The phrase "...a tree with minimum training errors" is mentioned in the context of hyperparameter tuning. This should likely refer to validation or tuning error, as minimizing training error can lead to overfitting.
