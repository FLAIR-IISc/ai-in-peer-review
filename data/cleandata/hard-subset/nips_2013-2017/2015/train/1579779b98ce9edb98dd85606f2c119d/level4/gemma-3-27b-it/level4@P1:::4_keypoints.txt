- Reformulates global decision-tree optimization as (latent) structured prediction using sign-vector latent variables.  
- Proposes a non-differentiable, non-convex loss and a differentiable (still non-convex) upper bound for optimization.  
- Optimizes the bound via SGD, requiring a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided.  
- Key contributions: casting DT optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms; approach is novel and interesting.  
- Sparse-gradient property: each SGD update appears to change only one node’s weight — noted as computationally and conceptually intriguing.  
- Raises possibility of growing an infinitely deep tree by initializing weights to zero and activating weights gradually; needs analysis whether this reduces to greedy strategies or yields bounded trees with regularization.  
- Ambiguity about zero-weight nodes: paper implies taking the right branch for zero weights, which is questionable; behavior should be precisely defined (output vs. split).  
- Interpretability concern: L2 regularization likely yields dense (non-interpretable) splits; L1 could sparsify but may hurt performance and requires testing.  
- Experimental deficiencies: insufficient detail on baselines, particularly pruning strategies used for the naive method.  
- Need for fair comparisons: naive method should be evaluated across pruning/regularization settings, and other simple global strategies (e.g., greedy on random subsets with validation selection) should be tried.  
- Clarity issues in inference exposition (Sections 6.2, 6.4); suggested summaries and more explicit descriptions would help.  
- Title mentions forests but the paper does not address forest/ensemble cases.  
- Post-rebuttal: zero-weight initialization remains a critical issue; random initialization may hide it, but initializing at zero and gradual growth is an important direction.  
- Overall: clever and novel approach that raises interesting questions, but practical motivation, interpretability, and experimental validation are not yet convincing.
