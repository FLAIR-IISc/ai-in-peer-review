

The submission proposes an algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees by reformulating the problem as (latent) structured prediction, where latent structured variables are sign vectors encoding decisions at each internal node. It introduces a non-differentiable, non-convex loss, approximated by a differentiable (though still non-convex) upper bound. Minimizing this loss via SGD requires solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided. Experiments compare the method to standard greedy training. The key contributions are casting global decision tree optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms for the objective.

The approach is novel and raises interesting questions. However, I have significant concerns regarding practical motivation and the magnitude of performance gains over naive methods. While the method is intriguing for global tree optimization, the primary computational advantage—sparsity in gradient updates (each step modifies only one node's weight vector)—deserves deeper exploration. The paper presents this as a computational benefit, but this sparsity could enable natural tree growth: starting with all weights zero, gradually making weights nonzero to expand the tree. This raises critical questions: (1) Would such a process yield a known greedy strategy, e.g., if splitting a new node is always preferable to revising existing weights? (2) With regularization, would the method converge to a tree with limited nodes (as suggested by Fig. 2)? This issue is central to the method's value over naive trees but lacks rigorous analysis.

A critical ambiguity remains: what occurs when a node's weight is zero? The paper (p. 3) implies taking the right branch, which is problematic. It would be preferable to output a decision at the node, with the weight later becoming nonzero to split it. Precisely defining this case is essential, as it directly parallels pruning strategies in greedy training.

My most significant practical concern is the motivation. Plain decision trees are valued primarily for interpretability; if interpretability is irrelevant, other methods typically offer superior performance. The proposed method likely produces non-interpretable results due to L2 regularization inducing dense weight vectors. Switching to L1 regularization might enforce sparsity but could degrade performance if forced to produce exactly 1-sparse splits. This trade-off requires empirical validation.

Additionally, the experiments lack sufficient detail on baselines. The naive method's pruning strategies are unspecified, despite the proposed method testing multiple regularization parameters. It would be fair to compare against trivial global optimization strategies, such as training greedy trees on random data subsets and selecting the one minimizing the loss on the full training/validation set.

The paper is reasonably well-written, though Section 6.2 could clarify that inference first maximizes the objective over all \(g\) for a given leaf, then over all leaves. Section 6.4 also needs greater explicitness.

In summary, the approach is clever and raises compelling questions. However, its practical utility is unclear due to unresolved interpretability concerns (why use non-interpretable trees?) and insufficient experimental detail. The title mentions "forests," but the paper does not address this case.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS  
I maintain that the zero-weight case requires deeper analysis. The rebuttal suggests weights are initialized randomly (avoiding the issue), but the method's most compelling aspect—starting from zero weights and growing the tree—remains unexplored. I encourage the authors to pursue this direction. The submission proposes a clever reformulation and efficient algorithm, but the motivation and experimental support are insufficient to establish practical utility.
