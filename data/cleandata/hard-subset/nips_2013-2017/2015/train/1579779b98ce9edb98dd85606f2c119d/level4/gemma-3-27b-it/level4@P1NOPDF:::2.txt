Summary: This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters—decision splits and posterior probabilities—of decision trees. This is an important problem, as current methods typically train trees in a greedy, layer-by-layer fashion. The authors formulate the task as a single optimization problem, then upper-bound and approximate it to arrive at a tractable formulation.

Quality: The paper is well-written, though there may be flaws in the reasoning, as detailed below. Clarity: The derivation is clean, but the experiments and their presentation could be improved. Originality: Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important and novel direction. Significance: The proposed solution is valuable, although some shortcomings are apparent, as discussed below.

Comments: 

- Is there a flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound? At the very least, the argument in lines 216-217 is confusing: “…for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right-hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$…” Consider the following example to illustrate my concern: Let $W\bx = [5; -5]$. 
--> $\hat \bh = [1; -1] = \sgn(W\bx)$
--> $\hat \bh^T W\bx = 10$
Choosing $\bg = [1; 1]$ results in $\bg^T W\bx = 0$. Therefore, the right-hand side of Eq. (7) is
$\bg^T W\bx - \hat \bh^T W\bx + \ell(...) = -10 + \ell(...)$, 
which could potentially be *smaller* than $\ell(...)$, and thus not a valid upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not cover this case and is therefore incomplete.

- Why are some leaves not assigned any data points? This seems unusual and could indicate a problem with the objective or initialization. The authors’ suggested heuristic of a fixed assignment of data points to tree leaves appears sub-optimal, but could potentially be motivated via an expectation maximization/concave-convex procedure, similar to other clustering techniques?

- I question the importance of the efficient loss-augmented inference design. The depth of the tree seems limited to a maximum of 16, and greedily checking $2^{16} = 65536$ values still seems feasible. How much did the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball impact performance? While one result is provided in the supplementary material, does this generalize? Also, the meaning of “leaves” in this context needs clarification.

- The paper lacks a baseline that trains non-axis-aligned split functions of trees in a standard greedy manner, i.e., without any initialization. Including such a baseline would provide a valuable point of comparison.

- The depth of the tree must be specified *a priori*. This limitation should be discussed more thoroughly, and the authors should provide a more careful experimental evaluation concerning the impact of the depth parameter.

Thank you to the authors for carefully clarifying the reasoning behind the loss bound (Eq. 7). I suggest replacing $\arg\max$ with $\max$. As stated in my summary, I have adjusted my score accordingly. 

An experiment comparing runtime performance would strengthen the paper. A more thorough evaluation of the method would be beneficial for the reader. Overall, the submission considers an interesting problem – jointly training all parameters of decision trees. The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and there is a potential flaw in the derivation. I am happy to adjust my score further given a compelling explanation in the rebuttal.
