The submission introduces a method for globally optimizing decision trees by recasting training as a latent structured prediction problem, where the latent variables are sign vectors representing the binary decisions at internal nodes. The original empirical loss is non-differentiable and non-convex; the authors upper-bound it with a differentiable (though still non-convex) surrogate. Optimization proceeds via SGD, requiring repeated loss-augmented inference, for which the paper provides efficient procedures. Experiments compare the proposed approach against standard greedy induction. The main contributions are the structured-prediction formulation of global tree learning and algorithms that make the optimization tractable. The approach is novel and thought-provoking, and it prompts several questions.

I have reservations about some practical aspects—especially the motivation and the magnitude of gains over simple baselines. As a method for global optimization of decision trees, the work has several appealing facets. Casting the problem as structured prediction allows SGD with loss-augmented inference that leverages the tree’s combinatorial structure. More intriguingly, the resulting gradient steps appear sparse—in particular, if I understand correctly, each update may modify only a single node’s weight vector. While presented as a computational advantage, this property seems to open up additional possibilities that the paper does not explore. For instance, one could start with an infinitely deep tree with all weights at zero and gradually “grow” the tree by activating weights, letting model complexity increase as needed. This raises questions: would such a procedure collapse to a known greedy strategy if creating new splits is consistently preferable to revising existing ones? With suitable regularization, would it instead settle on a finite tree (as the trends in Fig. 2 might hint)? This issue bears directly on what makes the proposed approach preferable to naive tree induction and merits deeper analysis. A related ambiguity concerns nodes with zero weights: the text suggests defaulting to the right branch, which seems questionable. It might be more sensible to output a prediction at such nodes and only later introduce a split by making the weight nonzero. Pinning down this behavior is important, as it parallels pruning choices in greedy trees.

A key practical concern is the motivation. Plain decision trees are often valued for interpretability; if interpretability is not central, other models typically outperform trees. The proposed method is likely to produce non-interpretable splits, since L2 constraints encourage dense oblique tests. Switching to L1 might improve sparsity and interpretability, but performance could degrade if one must regularize toward strictly 1-sparse splits. This trade-off warrants empirical evaluation.

On the experimental side, important baseline details are missing. The paper does not specify which pruning strategies were used for the greedy baseline. Because the proposed method is tuned over regularization parameters, the greedy baseline should likewise be tuned across pruning schemes and settings. It would also be informative to include simple “globalization” baselines, e.g., train multiple greedy trees on random subsets and select the model minimizing the target loss on the full training/validation set.

Regarding clarity, the manuscript is generally well-written, but the inference sections could be streamlined. For example, Section 6.2 could simply state that one maximizes over all g consistent with a particular leaf and then maximizes over leaves. Section 6.4 could also be more explicit about the mechanics.

In sum, this is a clever reformulation with compelling ideas. However, its practical value remains uncertain due to the interpretability issue and limited experimental detail. PS: although the title references forests, the paper does not actually address ensembles.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
I remain convinced that the zero-weights case is pivotal for the reasons outlined above. The rebuttal suggests that random initialization avoids this in practice, but the more interesting scenario, in my view, is initializing all weights at zero and allowing them to grow gradually. I encourage the authors to investigate this direction. Overall, the paper offers a neat reformulation and an efficient optimization scheme, but aspects of the motivation and empirical validation are still not fully persuasive.
