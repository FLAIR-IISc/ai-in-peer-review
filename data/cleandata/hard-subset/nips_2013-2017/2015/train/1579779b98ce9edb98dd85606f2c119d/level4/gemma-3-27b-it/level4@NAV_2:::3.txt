This paper presents a novel approach to decision tree optimization, framing it as a structured prediction problem and proposing a non-greedy algorithm for jointly optimizing split functions and leaf parameters. This addresses a long-standing limitation of traditional greedy methods like ID3 [21] and CART [5], which often lead to suboptimal trees. The authors establish a connection to structured prediction with latent variables [25], formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, and demonstrating efficient gradient computation (O(d)) for trees of depth d. Experiments on benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) show that their non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines, though the gains are often modest.

The key strength of this work lies in its theoretically grounded approach to a practical problem. The formulation as a structured prediction problem is insightful and allows the application of optimization techniques developed in that field. The reduction of gradient complexity to O(d) is a significant achievement, enabling the training of deeper trees. The empirical results, while not dramatic, consistently demonstrate the advantage of the non-greedy approach over standard greedy methods and related work like CO2 [18], which represents a greedy application of similar ideas. The careful consideration of regularization (ν) and the introduction of Stable SGD (SSGD) to address potential issues with leaf activation are also commendable.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the experiments demonstrate improvement, the magnitude of the gains is relatively small. A more in-depth analysis of the conditions under which the non-greedy approach excels (e.g., specific dataset characteristics, tree depth) would be valuable. The paper could also benefit from a more detailed comparison to more recent and sophisticated tree-based methods, such as those utilizing gradient boosting [8] or Mondrian processes [15]. The discussion of related work, while comprehensive, could be more critically engaged with, highlighting specific nuances and limitations of prior approaches in relation to the proposed method. The motivation for certain design choices, like the specific form of the regularization, could also be further elaborated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to decision tree optimization.
*   Demonstrated empirical improvement over standard greedy methods.
*   Efficient gradient computation enables training of deeper trees.
*   Careful consideration of regularization and algorithmic stability.
*   Clear presentation and well-written paper.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Modest magnitude of performance gains.
*   Limited analysis of conditions for success.
*   Could benefit from comparison to more recent advanced methods.
*   Some design choices lack detailed justification.



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field. The paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated approach to non-greedy decision tree learning. While the improvements are not transformative, the work represents a valuable step forward and opens up interesting avenues for future research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
