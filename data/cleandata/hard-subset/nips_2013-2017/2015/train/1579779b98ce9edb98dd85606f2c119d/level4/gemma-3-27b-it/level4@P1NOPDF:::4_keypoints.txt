- Novel formulation: casts global decision-tree optimization as (latent) structured prediction using sign-vector latent variables.
- Algorithmic contribution: proposes a non-differentiable/non-convex loss, a differentiable upper bound, and efficient loss-augmented inference algorithms solved via SGD.
- Empirical claim: compares the method to a standard greedy training baseline.
- Key strength: algorithmic novelty and efficient optimization leveraging problem structure.
- Important observation: gradient updates are sparse—each step appears to change only one node’s weight—an intriguing property beyond mere computational speedups.
- Suggestion/idea: initialize weights at zero and grow an infinitely deep tree by making weights non-zero, potentially letting model complexity adapt to data.
- Open question: could the zero-init growth behave like known greedy strategies or converge to compact trees with proper regularization?
- Critical ambiguity: unclear behavior when a node’s weight is exactly zero (paper implies taking the right branch), which affects pruning and split semantics.
- Interpretability concern: L2-regularized dense weight vectors may harm interpretability—the main appeal of plain decision trees.
- Regularization tradeoff: L1 could improve sparsity/interpretability but may hurt performance if it forces 1-sparse splits; needs investigation.
- Experimental weakness: insufficient detail on baseline pruning strategies and lack of comparable baseline hyperparameter sweeps or alternative global approaches.
- Suggestion for experiments: evaluate baselines with multiple pruning/regularization settings and test simple global strategies (e.g., greedy on random subsets with selection by validation loss).
- Clarity issues: inference description could be clearer—e.g., summarize Section 6.2 as maximizing over g per leaf then over leaves; Section 6.4 needs more explicitness.
- Title mismatch: paper title mentions forests but does not address or evaluate forests.
- Post-rebuttal: zero-weight issue remains critical; random initialization likely hides it in practice, but the zero-init growth regime is promising and should be explored.
- Overall verdict: clever and promising reformulation with efficient algorithms, but practical motivation, interpretability implications, and experimental validation are not yet convincing.
