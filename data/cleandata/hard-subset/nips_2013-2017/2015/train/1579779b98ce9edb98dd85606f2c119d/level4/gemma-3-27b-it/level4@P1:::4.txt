The submission proposes an algorithm for global optimization of decision trees based on a reformulation of the problem as (latent) structured prediction. The latent structured variables are sign vectors encoding the decisions made at each internal node in the tree.
A non-differentiable and non-convex loss is proposed, which is then approximated by a differentiable (but still non-convex) upper bound.
Minimizing this loss via SGD entails solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are given. Experiments are shown comparing this method to a standard greedy training method.
The key contributions of the work are casting the global DT optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms to optimize the objective.
The approach is novel and interesting, and raises several intriguing questions.

However, I have some concerns regarding practical considerations—especially concerning the motivation for the method and the significance of the performance gains over simpler methods.
Viewed purely as a method to globally optimize DTs, the approach is intriguing.
The primary advantage of casting the optimization as structured prediction is enabling SGD by solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems that leverage the problem’s structure.
However, the most interesting result, in my opinion, is that the gradient updates are sparse—if I understand correctly, each gradient step involves changing only one node’s weight vector.
The submission presents this primarily as a computational advantage, but I believe this property opens up more intriguing possibilities that the paper doesn’t fully acknowledge.
One could imagine optimizing over an infinitely deep tree by initially setting all weights to zero, and then gradually growing the tree by making more weights non-zero, allowing the model's complexity to grow naturally with the data. 
This raises questions: for example, would such a method be equivalent to, or a variation of, any known greedy strategy?
This might be the case if introducing a new node is always preferable to revising an old node’s weights. 
Alternatively, with appropriate regularization, would such a method converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes (as Fig. 2 suggests)?
This issue strikes at the core of what makes the proposed method worthwhile compared to naive DTs and deserves more careful analysis.
For instance, a critical issue left ambiguous is: what happens if a node's weight is equal to zero?
The discussion on page 3 implies taking the right branch, which seems questionable.
It seems preferable to output a decision at this node; later, we might decide to make this weight non-zero, corresponding to splitting the node.
Precisely defining this behavior is important, as it directly relates to pruning strategies for greedy training.

I have a significant practical concern regarding the motivation for the work. Plain DTs are useful primarily to the extent that they are interpretable; if interpretability isn’t required, other methods typically offer better performance.
As stated, I expect the method to produce non-interpretable results, as the L2 regularization would produce dense weight vectors.
While switching to L1 regularization might help, performance could drop if we were forced to regularize to the point where exactly one non-zero split was produced. This would definitely need to be tested.

My other main concern is that the experiments don’t provide sufficient detail regarding other baselines.
No details are given as to which pruning strategies were employed for the naive approach. 
Since a range of regularization parameters were tried for the proposed method, I would expect the naive method to be similarly evaluated with different pruning strategies and/or parameters. 
It would also be fair to try other straightforward global optimization strategies, such as training a greedy method with random subsets of the training data and choosing the one that minimizes the desired loss on the full training or validation set.

Regarding clarity, the paper is reasonably well-written. My only comment is that the parts describing inference could be clearer.
Section 6.2, for example, could be summarized by stating that the objective is first maximized for all *g* corresponding to a given leaf, which is then maximized over all leaves.
Section 6.4 could also be more explicit.

In summary, this is a clever approach that raises some very interesting questions. However, it’s unclear whether this is a practically useful method at this point, both due to the interpretability issue (why use plain DTs if they are not interpretable?) and due to the lack of detail in the experiments.
PS: although the title mentions forests, the paper does not address this case.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
I still think the case of zero weights is a critical one that deserves further analysis, for the reasons I raised in my review.
Reading the rebuttal, it sounds like the weights are probably initialized randomly, which is why this issue doesn’t seem to arise in practice. 
However, I believe what would make this method truly interesting is the case where the weights are initially zero and are gradually increased. 
I encourage the authors to consider this direction. The submission proposes a clever reformulation of global optimization of decision trees as structured prediction, along with an efficient algorithm to solve the optimization. However, some aspects of the motivation for the work and the experimental results are not entirely convincing.
