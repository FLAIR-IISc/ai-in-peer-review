Review - Summary: This paper introduces a method for the joint optimization of all parameters – decision splits and posterior probabilities – within decision trees. This is a significant contribution, as existing approaches typically employ a greedy, layer-wise training procedure. The problem is formulated as a single optimization challenge, subsequently upper-bounded and approximated to yield a tractable solution.

Quality - The paper demonstrates strong writing quality, however, potential issues exist within the theoretical reasoning, as detailed below. Clarity - The derivation is presented clearly, but the experimental results and their presentation could benefit from improvement. Originality - The investigation of joint training for decision trees represents an important research direction. Significance - The proposed solution holds value, although certain limitations appear to be present, as discussed below.

Comments:
- There appears to be a potential flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound. Specifically, the argument presented in lines 216-217 is unclear: “… for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$…” To illustrate the confusion, consider the following example: W\bx = [5;-5]
--> \hat \bh = [1;-1] = \sgn(W\bx)
--> \hat \bh^T W\bx = 10
Choosing g = [1;1] results in g^T W\bx = 0. Therefore, the right hand side of Eq. (7) is
g^T W\bx - \hat \bh^T W\bx +
\ell(...) = -10 + \ell(...)
which may be smaller than \ell(...) and thus invalidate the upper bound. The proof of Prop. 1 in the supplementary material does not address this scenario and is therefore incomplete.
- The observation that some leaves are not assigned any data points is concerning and may indicate an issue with the objective function or the initialization procedure. The current heuristic of a fixed assignment of data points to leaves seems sub-optimal; however, this could potentially be justified through an expectation maximization or concave-convex procedure, analogous to other clustering techniques.
- The importance of the design choices within the efficient loss-augmented inference procedure is unclear. Given the limited tree depth (maximum of 16), a greedy search over the 2^16 = 65536 possible values appears feasible. How significantly did the Hamming ball restriction impact performance? While one result is provided in the supplementary material, its generalizability is uncertain. Furthermore, the meaning of ‘leaves’ in this context requires clarification.
- The absence of a baseline that trains non-axis aligned split functions using a standard, greedy approach (without initialization) is notable.
- The requirement to pre-specify the tree depth is a limitation that should be discussed more thoroughly. The authors should also provide a more detailed experimental evaluation concerning the impact of the depth parameter.
