This paper proposes an algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions at internal nodes. Unlike standard greedy tree induction, the method jointly optimizes all tree parameters—both the split function weights and the output distribution at each leaf—by minimizing a global upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss. To obtain a smoother optimization objective, the bound is regularized with an L2 penalty on the split weights. The parameters are then learned via stochastic gradient descent (SGD).

Strengths
- The optimization problem is elegantly formulated by drawing a clear connection to structured prediction with latent variables.
- The idea of globally optimizing both split functions and leaf distributions under a regularized upper bound is principled and appealing.

Main concerns
- The empirical evidence does not convincingly show that global optimization is a superior alternative to greedy tree induction. The globally optimized trees significantly outperform greedy baselines on only a few datasets, while incurring notably higher computational cost.
- As a result, the practical advantage of the proposed approach remains unclear given the trade-off between accuracy gains and computational burden.

Clarifications needed
- Stable SGD: The “stable” version of SGD is not described clearly. How are data points assigned to leaves during training? Providing pseudocode would greatly help.
- Figure 2: Are the reported results obtained with standard SGD or stable SGD? What precisely constitutes an “active” leaf?
- Algorithm 1 (line 10): After updating Θ, you project it onto a simplex. How is this projection implemented in practice? It would also be useful to note explicitly that this projection enforces each row of Θ to sum to one.

Minor comments and typos
- Lines 407–408: You mention tuning “the number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree.” In standard decision trees, all features are typically considered at each node, so this statement is confusing and needs clarification.
- Equations (7), (8), and (9): “argmax” should be replaced by “max.”
- Figure 1: It is never referenced in the main text.
- Line 141: “...to the index of the leaf on by this path.” The end of this sentence appears to be malformed.
- Line 238: “...the solution to loss-augmented inference...” should be “the solutions to...”
- Line 301: “An key observation...” should be “A key...”
- Line 360: What is FSGD? Please define the acronym.
- Line 404: “...a tree with minimum training errors.” Do you mean “tuning” (validation) errors?

Supplementary material
- Line 29: “max{g ∈ sgn...}” should be “max{g = sgn...}”
- Line 30: “sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(Θ^T f(g), y)” should be “sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)), y)”

Conclusion
The optimization framework is well motivated and nicely connected to structured prediction with latent variables, but the experimental results do not yet provide compelling evidence that global optimization offers a consistent advantage over greedy tree learning, especially given the increased computational cost. Clarifying the training variants (SGD vs. stable SGD), their implementation details, and the evaluation setup would strengthen the paper.
