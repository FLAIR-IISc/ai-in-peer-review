

The paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy optimization of decision trees by reformulating global tree training as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, deriving a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient stochastic gradient descent (SGD) training. Key technical contributions include a computationally efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm (O(d²p) complexity) and a principled regularization approach that controls tree complexity via row-wise norm constraints on the split weight matrix. Experiments demonstrate consistent accuracy improvements over greedy baselines (including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and CO2) across multiple classification benchmarks, with non-greedy trees showing better generalization and reduced overfitting.

**Strengths:**  
The reformulation of decision tree optimization as structured prediction with latent variables is theoretically elegant and well-motivated. The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound and its efficient optimization via SGD (with the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm) represents a significant technical advance over prior NP-complete global optimization approaches. The method’s computational efficiency (O(d²p) per SGD step) enables training deep trees, addressing a key limitation of previous non-greedy methods. The empirical results are convincing, showing 2-5% accuracy gains over greedy baselines across diverse datasets while maintaining better generalization (Fig. 2). The regularization strategy (row-wise L₂ constraints) provides a natural mechanism to control tree complexity, aligning with standard practice for preventing overfitting.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Significance and Motivation:** The paper fails to adequately justify why non-greedy decision trees are practically valuable *despite* the loss of interpretability. Standard decision trees are primarily used for interpretability; this method produces dense, non-axis-aligned splits (due to L₂ regularization), undermining this key advantage. The authors should address: (a) whether L₁ regularization (to enforce sparse splits) maintains performance, and (b) whether the accuracy gains justify sacrificing interpretability for typical applications. Without this, the method risks being a niche technique rather than a broadly useful contribution to NIPS.  
2. **Experimental Rigor:** The comparison to greedy baselines lacks critical details. The paper does not specify pruning strategies used for baseline trees (e.g., max depth, minimum samples per leaf), which significantly impact performance. Given that the proposed method tunes ν and η via validation, the baselines should similarly be optimized across multiple pruning parameters. Additionally, the claim that "non-greedy optimization improves upon CO2" (Fig. 2) requires context: CO2 is a greedy variant of the same framework, so the comparison should clarify whether the gains stem from non-greediness or the specific regularization.  
3. **Critical Implementation Gap:** The paper’s handling of zero-weight nodes (Section 3, p. 4) is unjustified. It states that sgn(wᵢᵀx − bᵢ) = +1 when wᵢ = 0, but this arbitrarily assigns all inputs to the right branch—a problematic assumption that could distort training. The rebuttal mentions random initialization avoids this, but the method’s core appeal (gradual tree growth from zero weights) remains unexplored. The authors should analyze the behavior at wᵢ = 0 and its implications for tree growth strategies.  
4. **Scope Misalignment:** The title mentions "forests," but the paper exclusively addresses single trees. This misalignment with the stated scope weakens the contribution.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the *optimization* of decision trees, advancing the state of the art in non-greedy training with a novel structured prediction perspective. The method is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. However, its practical significance is undermined by the interpretability trade-off and insufficient experimental rigor. The accuracy gains (while consistent) do not clearly justify the method’s complexity for applications where interpretability is required—precisely the use case for decision trees. The authors should address these concerns to elevate the work from a promising technical advance to a broadly impactful contribution.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Reject**. The method is technically novel and well-executed, but the lack of justification for the interpretability trade-off and incomplete experimental comparisons prevent it from meeting NIPS’s significance criteria. With substantial revisions addressing the significance gap and experimental details, this could be a strong contribution.
