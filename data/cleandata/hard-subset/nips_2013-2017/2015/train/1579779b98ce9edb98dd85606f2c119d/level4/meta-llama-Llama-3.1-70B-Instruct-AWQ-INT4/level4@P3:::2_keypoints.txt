- Paper proposes a novel method to jointly train all parameters of decision trees by formulating a single optimization and deriving an upper bound on empirical loss.

- Writing quality is good but there are potential reasoning flaws, notably a confusing derivation of the empirical loss bound (Eq. 7) and unclear argument in lines 216–217.

- Reviewer provides a counterexample (g = [1;1]) showing the RHS of Eq. 7 can be smaller, which may invalidate the claimed upper bound.

- Proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement is incomplete and does not cover the counterexample case.

- Derivation is otherwise clean, but experimental presentation lacks important details (hyperparameters, initialization).

- The approach is original and tackles an important problem (joint training of decision trees).

- The method is potentially significant, but shortcomings in theory and evaluation need addressing.

- Authors should clarify the empirical loss bound derivation and directly address the provided counterexample.

- The occurrence of leaves with no assigned data points is concerning and may indicate issues with the objective or initialization; alternatives like EM or CCCP should be considered.

- The practical importance of the efficient loss-augmented inference is unclear for shallow trees (depth ≤ 16); more results are needed to justify the Hamming-ball search restriction.

- A baseline training non-axis-aligned split functions with standard greedy training (without initialization) is missing and should be added.

- The limitation of pre-specifying tree depth is not discussed sufficiently; experiments should evaluate depth sensitivity.

- Overall: promising and novel but requires fixes to theoretical claims and a more thorough experimental evaluation to strengthen acceptance arguments.

- For acceptance: novel joint-training approach, tractable upper-bound development, and promising empirical performance.

- Against acceptance: potentially flawed derivation, limited experiments, and insufficient discussion of method limitations (notably tree depth).
