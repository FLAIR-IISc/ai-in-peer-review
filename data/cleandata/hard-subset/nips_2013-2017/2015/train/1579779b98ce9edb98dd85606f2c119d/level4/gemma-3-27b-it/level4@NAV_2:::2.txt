Summary: This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters – decision splits and posterior probabilities – of decision trees, addressing a key limitation of current greedy layer-by-layer training approaches. The core idea is to formulate the problem as a single optimization task, upper-bound the empirical loss, and then approximate it to achieve a tractable formulation. This work establishes a link between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables.

Quality – The paper presents a technically sound approach to a challenging problem. However, potential issues exist in the derivation of the empirical loss bound, as detailed below. The work appears complete, representing a significant effort toward non-greedy tree learning. The authors acknowledge limitations and discuss potential future directions. Clarity – The derivation is generally clear and well-structured. However, the presentation of experiments and results could be improved with more detailed analysis. Originality – Investigating joint training for decision trees is a valuable and novel research direction. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables provides a fresh perspective. Significance – The proposed solution is significant, offering a potentially valuable alternative to greedy tree induction. Despite identified shortcomings, the method shows promising results and could advance the state-of-the-art.

Comments: - A potential flaw exists in the derivation of the empirical loss bound (Eq. 7). Specifically, the reasoning in lines 216-218 regarding the upper bound’s validity when g ≠ sgn(Wx) is unclear. Consider the example: Wx = [5; -5] → ̂h = [1; -1] = sgn(Wx) → ̂hTWx = 10. Choosing g = [1; 1] results in gTWx = 0, making the right-hand side of Eq. (7) -10 + ℓ(…), which could be *smaller* than ℓ(…), invalidating the upper bound. The proof of Prop. 1 in the supplementary material appears incomplete as it doesn't explicitly address this case. - The observation that some leaves are not assigned any data points is puzzling and warrants further investigation. It suggests a potential issue with the objective function or initialization procedure. The proposed heuristic of fixing data point assignments to leaves seems sub-optimal; its motivation through expectation maximization or a concave-convex procedure (similar to clustering techniques) could be beneficial. - The importance of the efficient loss-augmented inference is unclear given the relatively modest tree depths (up to 16). A naive check of 2^16 = 65536 values may be feasible. How did restricting the search space using the Hamming ball impact performance? The result provided in the supplementary material is insufficient to assess its generalizability. Clarification on what is meant by “leaves” in this context is also needed. - A key baseline is missing: non-axis-aligned split functions trained using a standard greedy approach (without initialization from a greedy axis-aligned tree). Including this baseline would provide a more comprehensive comparison. - The requirement to pre-specify the tree depth is a limitation that should be discussed more thoroughly. A more detailed experimental evaluation exploring the impact of this parameter would be valuable.
