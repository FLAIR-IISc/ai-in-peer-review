This paper proposes an algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions at the nodes, where the tree parameters (i.e., the weights in the split functions and the distribution of the output variable at each leaf) are optimized globally, as opposed to the standard greedy approach. The authors introduce an upper bound on the tree's empirical loss and regularize it via L2 regularization of the split function weights to achieve a smoother optimization problem. They then employ stochastic gradient descent (SGD) to identify the tree parameters that minimize the upper bound.

The tree optimization problem is elegantly formulated by establishing a connection with structured prediction with latent variables. However, the empirical results presented in the paper do not convincingly demonstrate that global optimization is a better alternative to greedy learning of the tree. The globally optimized tree significantly outperforms the greedy tree on only a couple of datasets, while being more computationally intensive.

The paper is well-structured, but some points require clarification:

* The stable version of SGD is not clearly described. Specifically, how are data points assigned to leaves? Including a pseudo-code would be helpful.
* Figure 2: Are these results obtained using SGD or stable SGD? What does an "active" leaf mean exactly?
* In line 10 of Algorithm 1, Θ is updated and then projected onto a simplex. In practice, how do you solve this projection problem? It would also be worth mentioning that this projection ensures that each line of Θ sums up to one.

* Figure 1 is not mentioned in the main text.

* Line 301: "An key observation..." should be "A key observation..."
* Line 360: What is FSGD?
* Line 404: "...a tree with minimum training errors." This should likely be "...tuning errors?"

In the supplementary material:

The tree optimization problem is elegantly formulated, but the results presented are not convincing.
