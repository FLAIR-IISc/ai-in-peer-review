- Paper proposes a non-greedy algorithm for training oblique decision trees by treating split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a convex–concave upper bound via SGD.  
- Connects decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables and introduces loss-augmented inference.  
- Naïve bound has O(2^d p) update cost; authors propose a Hamming-ball restriction reducing cost to O(d^2 p), enabling deeper trees.  
- A norm constraint on split weight vectors acts as principled regularization and an implicit pruning control.  
- Experiments compare to greedy axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, and CO2, showing improvements on some datasets but not consistently.  

Strengths:
- Elegant conceptual link between decision trees and latent structured prediction.  
- Well-motivated surrogate objective and clear path from intractable to efficient algorithm.  
- Norm constraint provides interpretable regularization and pruning behavior.  
- Practical training recipe (initialization, SGD with momentum/minibatches) clearly described.  

Weaknesses:
- Empirical gains are limited and dataset-dependent while incurring higher computational cost.  
- Important algorithmic details (e.g., Stable SGD, “active” leaves) are under-specified, hindering reproducibility.  
- Readability suffers from unclear figures/references and multiple typos.  

Clarifications / Questions (requested by reviewer):
- Precisely define Stable SGD: how and when are data-to-leaf assignments fixed/updated, and provide pseudocode and reassignment criteria and frequency.  
- Indicate whether Figure 2 uses vanilla SGD or SSGD and define “active” leaf (e.g., assigned at least one training example).  
- Clarify Algorithm 1 line 10: how Θ is projected onto a simplex in practice and whether Θ stores normalized probabilities or unnormalized logits.  
- Report wall-clock runtimes for SGD vs fast loss-augmented inference across depths and specify hardware.  
- Provide an ablation to separate joint (non-greedy) optimization effects from other design choices, especially relative to CO2.  

Minor comments / typos to fix:
- Clarify line about features evaluated for axis-aligned trees (feature subsampling vs evaluating all features).  
- Replace “argmax” with “max” in Equations (7)–(9).  
- Reference Figure 1 where f(·) is defined.  
- Fix malformed sentence at line 141 and various wording/grammar errors (lines 238, 301, 360, 404).  
- Define FSGD on first use.  

Supplementary material corrections:
- Fix expressions on lines 29–30 (sgn notation and argument of ℓ).  

Pros for acceptance:
- Original formulation tying decision trees to latent structured prediction.  
- Scalable algorithmic refinement (Hamming-ball bound) with sound complexity analysis.  
- Regularization viewpoint that explains implicit pruning and helps generalization.  

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited empirical advantage over strong greedy baselines combined with higher computational cost.  
- Missing SSGD and implementation details reduce reproducibility.  
- Multiple clarity and presentation issues throughout the paper.  

Overall assessment:
- Elegant and potentially impactful optimization framework, but empirical evidence and missing implementation details weaken the case; addressing SSGD, runtime/ablation analyses, and presentation issues would strengthen the paper.
