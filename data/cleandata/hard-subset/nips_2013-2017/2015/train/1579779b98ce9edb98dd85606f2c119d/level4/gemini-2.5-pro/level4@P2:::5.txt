### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees with oblique (linear) splits. The central idea is to formulate the tree learning problem as one of structured prediction with latent variables, where the path an example takes through the tree is determined by a set of latent binary decisions. The authors derive a global, convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective function. This objective, which jointly considers all split functions and leaf parameters, is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm that reduces the complexity of each SGD step from exponential to polynomial in the tree's depth, making the approach viable for deeper trees. The experimental results compare the proposed non-greedy trees against several greedy baselines on a number of benchmark classification datasets.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The main strength of this work lies in its novel and elegant formulation of a long-standing problem. Moving away from the traditional greedy, node-by-node construction of decision trees towards a global optimization framework is a significant conceptual contribution. The connection established between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and opens a principled path for applying powerful optimization tools to this domain. The derivation of a smooth surrogate objective function and the use of SGD make the approach applicable to large-scale datasets, which is a crucial requirement for modern machine learning methods.

However, the primary weakness of the paper is that the empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of this complex, non-greedy approach over simpler, greedy methods. While the proposed method shows the best test performance, the margin of improvement over baselines like CO2 (a greedy variant of the same framework) and even standard oblique trees is often marginal. Given that the proposed global optimization is computationally more intensive than greedy methods, the limited performance gains call into question the practical significance of the contribution. The paper would be substantially stronger if it could either demonstrate more significant performance improvements or provide a deeper analysis of the specific scenarios or data characteristics where a global optimization approach is critically advantageous.

Furthermore, the paper's clarity could be improved in several areas, particularly concerning the algorithmic details, which hinders reproducibility.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**For Acceptance:**
*   **Originality:** The paper presents a highly original and theoretically appealing framework for a classic and challenging problem in machine learning.
*   **Conceptual Contribution:** The formulation connecting decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables is a novel contribution that could inspire future work in this area.
*   **Technical Quality:** The derivation of the surrogate objective and the development of the efficient "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm are technically sound contributions.

**Against Acceptance:**
*   **Limited Empirical Significance:** The experimental results are not compelling enough to justify the additional complexity of the proposed method. The performance gains over greedy baselines are modest across several datasets.
*   **Lack of Clarity:** Key algorithmic details, especially for the "Stable SGD" variant, are not described with sufficient clarity, making the work difficult to understand fully and reproduce.
*   **High Computational Cost:** The proposed method is inherently more computationally demanding than greedy alternatives, and the observed performance benefits do not appear to outweigh this cost for general use cases.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

The paper is well-structured, but several points require clarification to improve the manuscript's quality and reproducibility.

**Clarity Issues:**
1.  The "Stable SGD" (SSGD) variant is introduced as an important practical component, but its description is too brief. How exactly are the data point-to-leaf assignments `a(x)` determined and updated during training? The mechanism for this is central to the algorithm, and providing pseudocode would be highly beneficial.
2.  In the experiments (Figure 2), it is unclear whether the results for the "non-greedy" method were obtained using the standard SGD (Algorithm 1) or the Stable SGD variant. Please clarify this.
3.  The term "active leaf" is used in the discussion of Figure 3 to describe a form of implicit pruning. Could you please provide a precise definition of what constitutes an "active" versus an "inactive" leaf?
4.  The term "FSGD" is used once (line 360) without prior definition. It is presumably a shorthand for SGD with fast loss-augmented inference, but this should be stated explicitly.

**Minor Comments and Typos:**
*   Equations (7), (8), (9): The `argmax` operator should be `max`, as the goal is to obtain the maximum value of the expression, not the argument that maximizes it.
*   Line 238: "the solution to loss-augmented inference..." should likely be "the solutions to..." since there could be multiple maximizers.
*   Line 301: "An key observation..." should be "A key observation...".
*   Line 404: "a tree with minimum training errors." Given that this is part of the hyperparameter tuning process, do you mean "validation error" here instead of "training error"?
*   Line 407-408: You mention tuning the "number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree". In a standard decision tree algorithm like CART, all features are typically evaluated at each node to find the best split. Please clarify what this hyperparameter refers to.

**In the Supplementary Material:**
*   Line 29: `max{g \in sgn...}` should probably be `max{g = sgn...}` or simply substitute `sgn(Wx)` for `g` in the expression that follows.
*   Line 30: The term `l(Θ^Tf(g), y)` should be `l(Θ^Tf(sgn(Wx)), y)` to be consistent with the substitution mentioned for line 29.
