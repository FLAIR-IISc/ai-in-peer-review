This paper presents an elegant non-convex relaxation for globally optimizing decision trees with a fixed structure. The core idea is to formulate decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, allowing for a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. This enables efficient training via stochastic gradient descent, achieving O(d) gradient computation per example where d is the tree depth. While the experimental results are somewhat modest, the approach is technically sound and represents a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper builds upon decades of work in decision tree induction [5, 21, 4], addressing the well-known limitations of greedy algorithms like ID3 [21] and CART [5]. Recent attempts to improve upon greedy methods have focused on new objective functions [20, 12], but the fundamental greedy approach remains prevalent in applications [9, 14]. This work directly tackles this limitation by proposing a joint optimization framework. It's also related to, but distinct from, approaches like Bennett's multi-linear programming [1], which is limited in scope and scalability. The authors appropriately position their work alongside relevant methods like Mondrian Processes [15] and Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts [13], highlighting the trade-offs between efficiency and accuracy.  The connection drawn to structured prediction with latent variables [25] is a key contribution, enabling the use of techniques from that area to formulate a tractable optimization problem.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the derivation of the convex-concave upper bound appears well-justified. The complexity analysis (O(d) gradient computation) is a significant achievement, making deep tree training feasible.
* **Novelty:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem, and the resulting optimization framework, are novel and offer a fresh perspective on a classic problem.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the complex ideas. The notation is consistent, and the steps of the algorithm are presented logically.
* **Potential Impact:** While the current experimental results are not groundbreaking, the approach has the potential to lead to significant improvements in decision tree accuracy and efficiency. It opens up avenues for further research, such as exploring different regularization techniques and kernel methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Results:** The performance gains over greedy baselines are not dramatic, and in some cases, the results are comparable. The authors acknowledge this, but stronger empirical evidence would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Overfitting Concerns:** The paper acknowledges the potential for overfitting with non-greedy optimization but doesn’t fully explore this issue in the experiments. A more thorough analysis of the regularization parameter (ν) and its impact on generalization would be beneficial.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The paper mentions initializing the optimization with a greedy tree (CO2 [18]), but a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to this initialization would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to a fundamental problem in machine learning.
* The technical contributions, particularly the complexity analysis, are significant.
* The clarity of the writing makes the paper accessible to a broad audience.
* The work has the potential to inspire further research in non-greedy decision tree learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The experimental results are not compelling enough to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* The potential for overfitting is not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a conceptually elegant and technically sophisticated approach to decision tree optimization. While the experimental results are somewhat limited, the potential impact of the work and the clarity of the presentation outweigh this weakness. I believe the paper is worthy of publication and, with further investigation of the experimental results and regularization strategies, could become a highly influential contribution to the field. The suggestion from the discussion with other reviewers (throwing away the last split and optimizing it greedily) is a worthwhile consideration for future work.

**Score:** 7/10 (Accept)
