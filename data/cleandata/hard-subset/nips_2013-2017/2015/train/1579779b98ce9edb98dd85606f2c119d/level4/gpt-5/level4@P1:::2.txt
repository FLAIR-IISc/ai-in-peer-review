Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy algorithm for jointly training all parameters of a decision tree—both the internal split functions and the leaf distributions—under a single global objective, rather than optimizing splits one node at a time. The key idea is to relate oblique (linear) splits to structured prediction with latent variables and to optimize a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors show that naïve loss-augmented inference is exponential in tree depth but derive a fast variant with O(d^2 p) time (for depth d and input dimension p), enabling training of deeper trees. Experiments on several multi-class benchmarks suggest non-greedy trees generalize better than greedy baselines, including axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique splits, and the CO2 method (a depth-1 special case).

Relation to prior work: The paper connects tree learning to latent structured prediction (e.g., structural SVMs and latent-variable variants), and contrasts with greedy induction (ID3/CART), oblique splits (OC1), max-margin trees, and probabilistic soft-split models such as Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts. It also situates the approach relative to CO2 and online/streaming forest methods (e.g., Mondrian forests).

Evaluation
- Quality: The formulation is elegant and the derivation mostly clear, but there appear to be issues in the presentation of the empirical loss bound (see below). The fast loss-augmented inference is well motivated. The experimental section shows encouraging results, but the evaluation is limited and some important baselines and ablations are missing.
- Clarity: The theory is generally well written; however, certain arguments are confusing (Eq. 7), and the empirical section could be clearer and more comprehensive. Please clarify terminology (e.g., “leaves” in the supplementary) and explain why some leaves end up unused.
- Originality: The link between decision trees and latent structured prediction, and the specific convex–concave surrogate with efficient loss-augmented inference, are novel and interesting.
- Significance: Joint optimization for trees is an important problem. If the bound and optimization are sound, the method could be influential. The current empirical evidence is promising but would benefit from stronger comparisons and analysis.

Pros
- Addresses a well-known limitation of greedy induction with a principled joint optimization framework.
- Connects tree learning to latent structured prediction and derives a tractable surrogate objective.
- Efficient training via SGD with O(d) per-example gradient and O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference.
- Empirical gains over several greedy baselines; appears less prone to overfitting as depth increases.

Cons
- The derivation and interpretation of the loss upper bound (Eq. 7) are confusing; the text could mislead.
- Some leaves receive no data, suggesting instability or an issue with the objective/optimization.
- The benefit and potential bias of restricting loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball are not fully quantified.
- Missing baseline: greedy training of oblique splits without the proposed initialization.
- The tree depth must be fixed a priori; the paper under-discusses this limitation and its practical impact.
- Experimental evaluation is limited; more runtime and ablation studies would strengthen the case.

Detailed comments and questions
- Possible issue in the loss bound (Eq. 7): The statement “for all g ≠ sgn(Wx), the RHS can only become larger than when g = sgn(Wx)” is confusing. Consider Wx = [5; −5]. Then ĥ = sgn(Wx) = [1; −1] with ĥ^T Wx = 10. If we choose g = [1; 1], then g^T Wx = 0. The RHS becomes g^T Wx − ĥ^T Wx + ℓ(…) = −10 + ℓ(…), which could be smaller than ℓ(…) and thus not an upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplement does not appear to cover this case and seems incomplete. Please clarify the precise conditions under which Eq. 7 holds and adjust the wording (see note below after rebuttal).
- Inactive leaves: You report that some leaves end up with no assigned data. This is surprising and may indicate a shortcoming in the objective or the optimization dynamics/initialization. The proposed stabilization heuristic (fixing assignments for several updates) seems ad hoc. Could this be better motivated via EM or a concave–convex procedure analogy, as in other clustering/latent assignment methods?
- Efficient loss-augmented inference: For depths up to 16, a brute-force search over 2^16 = 65,536 patterns might be feasible in practice. How critical is the Hamming-ball restriction for both runtime and accuracy? To what extent does restricting to B1(sgn(Wx)) bias the solution? The supplement shows one result; does this generalize? Also, please clarify what you mean by “leaves” in that context.
- Missing baseline: Please include a baseline that trains oblique (non-axis-aligned) splits greedily from scratch (no specialized initialization), to isolate the benefit of non-greedy joint training versus simply allowing oblique splits.
- Depth selection: The depth must be specified in advance. This is a practical limitation that merits more discussion. Could you provide a more systematic evaluation of depth selection (e.g., validation curves, sensitivity analysis), and comment on interactions with ν and the observed implicit pruning behavior?
- Experimental design: A direct runtime comparison of “full” loss-augmented inference versus the fast Hamming-ball variant would help quantify the efficiency/accuracy tradeoff. More ablations (e.g., effect of ν, initialization schemes, SSGD versus SGD) would also aid interpretability.

Post-rebuttal update
Thanks for the clear explanation of the loss bound and the conditions under which Eq. 7 holds. In the text, consider replacing argmax with max where appropriate to avoid ambiguity. I have adjusted my score accordingly.

Overall, this submission addresses an important problem with an interesting and potentially impactful approach. A stronger and clearer theoretical presentation of the bound, plus a more thorough experimental evaluation (including runtimes and additional baselines), would substantially improve the paper.
