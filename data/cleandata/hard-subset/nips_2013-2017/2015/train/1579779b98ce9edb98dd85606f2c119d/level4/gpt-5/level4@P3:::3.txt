Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a non-greedy training procedure for decision trees that jointly optimizes all split parameters and leaf predictions under a single global objective. The key idea is to cast tree training as structured prediction with latent variables: each internal node’s binary decision is treated as a latent variable h ∈ {−1, +1}^m, and a point’s route to a leaf is encoded via a navigation function f(h). The authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss (Eq. 7), and introduce a tighter, computationally efficient variant based on a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx) (Eq. 13). This yields a surrogate objective whose gradients can be computed in O(d) per example and whose loss-augmented inference can be solved in O(d^2 p), enabling training of deep oblique trees by stochastic gradient descent with per-row norm constraints on W. A “stable SGD” variant (SSGD) fixes leaf assignments for several steps to avoid collapsing leaves.

The work builds on and contrasts with: (a) classical greedy axis-aligned tree induction (ID3, CART) [21, 5]; (b) oblique trees such as OC1 [17]; (c) max-margin/tree-SVM methods [2, 3]; (d) soft splits in Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts [13]; (e) global but computationally heavy MILP/MILP-like approaches [1]; (f) online forests (Mondrian) [15]; and especially (g) CO2 [18], which can be seen as a depth-1 special case of the proposed bound. Experiments on SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST show consistent, though generally modest, gains over these greedy baselines when training single trees, with reduced overfitting and better accuracy across depths.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The surrogate objective is well motivated by latent structured prediction. The upper bound (Eq. 7) is valid and becomes computationally tractable with the Hamming-ball restriction (Eq. 13). Proposition 1 clarifies how the scale of W affects the bound; the choice to constrain per-row norms is reasonable and aligns with the tree’s semantics. The loss-augmented inference and SGD updates are clearly derived, and the complexity reductions from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) are convincing. That said, the method is necessarily non-convex; no convergence or generalization guarantees are provided beyond standard SGD behavior.
- Empirical support: The paper reports consistent performance gains of non-greedy oblique trees over axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, and CO2 baselines across several datasets and depths. Figures suggest improved test accuracy and robustness to overfitting, and the runtime benefits of the fast bound are demonstrated. However, the improvements appear modest, and significance tests are not reported. Comparisons are limited to single trees; comparisons to strong ensembles (random forests, boosted trees) are discussed but not executed, which limits practical impact claims.
- Reproducibility: Initialization and hyperparameter selection are described (initial greedy/CO2 tree; tuning ν and learning rate per depth using validation), and pseudocode is provided. Some important details would further aid reproduction: exact dataset preprocessing, batch sizes, momentum, number of epochs/steps, and whether all baselines received comparable tuning budgets.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The mapping from split decisions to latent variables and the role of the navigation function f are explained clearly, and Algorithm 1 is helpful. The fast loss-augmented inference is well motivated. Suggestions to improve clarity:
- Provide a compact, self-contained description of the loss-augmented inference for the Hamming-ball bound (Eq. 13) with an explicit outline of the O(d^2 p) procedure.
- Summarize the SSGD schedule (how long leaf assignments are fixed, the stopping criterion for reassignment).
- Include a small, running example tree showing how g and h differ and how f(g) changes with a single-bit flip.

Originality
The connection between decision tree training and latent structured prediction, together with a tractable upper bound that permits joint optimization of all splits at once, is novel and interesting. The fast Hamming-ball bound and the use of per-row norm constraints to regularize the global objective distinguish this work from prior oblique and max-margin tree methods, and extend CO2 beyond depth-1 greedy optimization.

Significance
As a single-tree training method, the contribution is solid and principled, offering consistent gains and improved regularization for deep oblique trees with feasible complexity. The impact would be higher with evidence that the approach scales to ensembles (e.g., forests or boosted trees) and meaningfully advances state of the art in those settings. In its current form, the improvements over strong greedy baselines for single trees are modest but consistent, suggesting a useful technique rather than a breakthrough.

Pros
- Principled non-greedy formulation via latent structured prediction and an optimizable surrogate bound.
- Efficient training for deep oblique trees (O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference; O(d) gradients).
- Consistent accuracy improvements across datasets and depths, with reduced overfitting.
- Clear articulation of the role of norm constraints (ν) in controlling bound tightness and implicit pruning.

Cons
- Reported gains are relatively small; no statistical significance analysis.
- Evaluations focus on single trees; no results for forests/boosting, which limits practical impact.
- Limited ablations: effect of SSGD vs. SGD, sensitivity to ν and Hamming-ball radius, and initialization dependence are not fully explored.
- Comparisons to soft/probabilistic tree models (e.g., HME) are discussed but not empirically evaluated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: SGD vs. SSGD; full bound (Eq. 7) vs. Hamming-ball (Eq. 13) where feasible; sensitivity to ν and depth; and initialization (axis-aligned vs. random).
- Report statistical tests or confidence intervals for accuracy differences.
- Include results for ensembles (forests/boosting) using the proposed non-greedy trees as base learners, to assess practical impact.
- Compare against soft-split models (HME) to highlight accuracy–efficiency trade-offs.
- Provide more implementation details and, if possible, code to facilitate reproducibility.

Recommendation
Borderline to weak accept. The paper offers a well-motivated, technically sound, and efficient non-greedy training method for decision trees with consistent empirical benefits. While the gains are modest and the scope of evaluation could be broadened, the formulation is elegant and likely to influence subsequent work on globally trained tree models and their use within ensembles.
