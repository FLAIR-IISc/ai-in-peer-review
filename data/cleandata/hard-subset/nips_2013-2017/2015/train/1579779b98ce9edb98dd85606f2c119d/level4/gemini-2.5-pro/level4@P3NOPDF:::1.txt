**Review Summary**

This paper presents a novel and elegant approach for the global optimization of decision trees with a predefined structure. The authors formulate the training process as a nonconvex optimization problem and propose a relaxation to find a solution. The paper is exceptionally well-written, and the core idea is both theoretically appealing and a departure from the standard greedy methods. While the experimental results are somewhat modest, the novelty of the formulation and its potential to inspire future research make this a valuable contribution that merits publication.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The primary strength of this work is its elegant formulation of the decision tree optimization problem. By framing it as a nonconvex relaxation, the paper introduces a principled global optimization method that is a significant departure from the standard greedy, top-down training algorithms.
2.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is very clearly written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and explaining their proposed solution, making a complex topic accessible.
3.  **Potential for Future Research:** This approach is likely to inspire further research into non-greedy methods for training decision trees and related models. The paper opens up a promising new direction for the field.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Underwhelming Empirical Results:** The main weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. The performance improvements over standard, greedily trained decision trees are not as substantial as one might hope, given the sophistication of the proposed optimization framework. This raises questions about the practical utility of the method in its current form.

2.  **Potential for Overfitting:** A significant concern with any global optimization procedure is the potential for increased overfitting. The paper does not adequately address or evaluate this risk. It would be important for future work to include experiments that specifically measure the degree of overfitting compared to standard methods, for instance, by analyzing the gap between training and test performance across various datasets.

3.  **Suggestion for a Hybrid Approach:** The authors could potentially improve their empirical results with a simple post-processing or hybrid step. After performing the global optimization, one could prune the final split in each branch and re-optimize the new terminal leaf nodes using a standard, local procedure. This hybrid approach might combine the benefits of global structure optimization with the fine-tuning of a local method, potentially leading to better generalization.

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment**

After discussion with the other reviewers and considering the author rebuttal, my positive assessment of this paper remains. I acknowledge the limitations in the experimental results, which the other reviewers also noted. However, I maintain that the paper's primary contribution is the novelty and elegance of its formulation. This line of research has significant potential, and this paper serves as an important step that is likely to stimulate further work in the community. While stronger empirical results would have certainly warranted a higher score, the current evaluation is sufficient to demonstrate the method's viability. Therefore, I stand by my recommendation for publication on the basis of its strong conceptual contribution.
