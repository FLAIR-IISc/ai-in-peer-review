Summary
The paper proposes a method to jointly train all parameters of a decision tree—both internal split functions and leaf posterior distributions—via a single optimization objective that is upper-bounded and approximated to yield a tractable learning procedure. This addresses a well-known limitation of standard greedy, layer-wise training and seeks to improve global optimality.

Evaluation
- Quality: The paper is clearly written and the derivation is presented cleanly. However, I am concerned about a potential flaw (or at least a gap) in the derivation of the empirical loss bound and would like a more complete justification (see below).
- Clarity: The main ideas and derivations are easy to follow, but the experimental setup and its presentation could be strengthened and clarified.
- Originality: Joint training of tree splits and leaf posteriors is an important and timely direction beyond greedy tree induction.
- Significance: The approach is promising and potentially impactful, though current limitations in theory and evaluation temper the overall strength.

Main comments and questions
1) Validity of the empirical loss upper bound (Eq. 7)
The argument around line 216 (“for all values of g ≠ sgn(Wx), the right-hand side can only become larger than when g = sgn(Wx)”) is confusing and, as written, appears incorrect. Consider:
- Wx = [5, −5]^T
- ĥ = sgn(Wx) = [1, −1]^T, so ĥ^T Wx = 10
- Choose g = [1, 1]^T, giving g^T Wx = 0
Then the RHS of Eq. (7) becomes g^T Wx − ĥ^T Wx + ℓ(…) = −10 + ℓ(…), which can be strictly smaller than ℓ(…). This would violate the claimed upper bound unless additional conditions hold. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not appear to address this case, so it seems incomplete.
- Please clarify the precise statement and assumptions needed for the bound to hold. If the inequality requires constraints on g, margin assumptions, or an alternative decomposition, these should be stated explicitly. If the bound holds only for g constrained within a Hamming ball or some other restricted set, please reflect that in the main text and proof.
- If the bound must be modified, please provide a corrected proof and discuss any implications for the algorithm.

2) Unused leaves
Some leaves reportedly receive no data. This is surprising and may indicate issues with the objective, regularization, or initialization.
- Please report how frequently this occurs and whether it persists across datasets and depths.
- Is the objective implicitly pruning leaves (e.g., by making certain paths unattractive)? If so, can this be characterized?
- The current heuristic that fixes data-to-leaf assignments seems suboptimal. Consider an EM/CCCP-style procedure where leaf assignments (latent variables) are updated given current splits, followed by re-optimizing splits and leaf posteriors. At minimum, motivate the chosen heuristic and compare against such a variant.

3) Efficient loss-augmented inference and search space restriction
You emphasize efficient loss-augmented inference via a restricted search (e.g., a Hamming ball). Given depths up to 16, exhaustive evaluation of all 2^16 = 65,536 leaf codes is not obviously infeasible per example in small-to-moderate datasets.
- Please quantify the runtime benefit of the Hamming-ball restriction versus exhaustive search, and provide accuracy trade-offs as the radius varies. The single supplementary result is helpful but not sufficient to establish generality.
- A runtime breakdown (loss-augmented inference vs. parameter updates) would be useful.
- Please clarify terminology around “leaves”: in places it reads as “leaves’,” which is ambiguous. If you mean “leaf assignments” or “leaf codes,” please standardize.

4) Missing baseline: greedy oblique trees
A strong and relevant baseline is a greedily trained oblique tree with non-axis-aligned splits learned at each node (e.g., by optimizing a logistic/hinge impurity) trained from scratch, without specialized initialization. This would help isolate the benefit of global joint training versus local oblique optimization. Please include such a baseline or discuss why it is not applicable.

5) Fixed depth
The depth must be specified a priori. This is a practical limitation.
- Please discuss the trade-offs and provide an empirical study showing performance as a function of depth, including over/underfitting behavior and training/runtime scaling.
- Consider simple mechanisms to mitigate this (e.g., training a deeper tree with sparsity or pruning penalties, or early stopping along certain paths).

Experimental evaluation
- The current experiments are informative but limited. In addition to the baselines above, please:
  - Include runtime comparisons against greedy axis-aligned and oblique trees.
  - Report the incidence of empty leaves and its effect on accuracy.
  - Evaluate sensitivity to the Hamming radius and depth.
  - Clarify dataset sizes, feature dimensions, and hyperparameter selection.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally well written. Please ensure the bound statements precisely match what is proven.
- In Eq. (7), “argmax” appears where “max” is intended; please correct this.
- Define all notation the first time it appears and standardize terms for leaf-related quantities.

Post-rebuttal update
Thank you for the careful explanation of the loss bound. Your clarification addressed my primary concern about Eq. (7). Please reflect the necessary conditions in the camera-ready and replace argmax with max where appropriate. I have adjusted my score accordingly. A runtime comparison and a more comprehensive evaluation (as outlined above) would further strengthen the paper.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important problem—jointly training the entire decision tree—and proposes a principled optimization framework with promising results. Assuming the corrected and clearly stated bound, the approach is sound and potentially impactful. The main weaknesses lie in the experimental breadth and the lack of a strong greedy oblique baseline. With these additions and clarifications, this work would make a solid contribution.
