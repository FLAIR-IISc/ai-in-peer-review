The submission proposes an algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees by reformulating the problem as (latent) structured prediction. The latent structured variables are sign vectors encoding the decisions made at each internal node in the tree.
A non-differentiable and non-convex loss is proposed, which is then approximated by a differentiable (but still non-convex) upper bound.
Minimizing this loss via SGD entails solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided. Experiments compare this method to a standard greedy training method.
The key contributions of the work are casting global decision tree optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms to optimize the objective.
The approach is novel and interesting, and raises intriguing research questions.
However, I have concerns regarding practical considerations – particularly the motivation for the method and the significance of the performance gains over simpler approaches.
Considering the method solely as a technique for global decision tree optimization, it is intriguing in several ways.
A primary advantage of formulating the optimization as structured prediction is the ability to perform SGD by solving a sequence of loss-augmented optimization problems that leverage the problem’s inherent structure.
More importantly, I believe the gradient updates are sparse – if my understanding is correct, each gradient step modifies only one node’s weight vector.
The submission presents this primarily as a computational advantage, but this property suggests more intriguing possibilities that are not fully explored in the paper.
One could envision optimizing an infinitely deep tree by initializing all weights to zero, then gradually growing the tree by making weights nonzero, allowing the model’s complexity to grow organically with the data. 
This raises questions: would such a method be equivalent to, or a variation of, a known greedy strategy? 
This might be the case if introducing a new node is always preferable to revising existing node weights. Alternatively, with appropriate regularization, might such a method converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes, as suggested by Fig. 2?
This issue is central to the value proposition of the proposed method compared to standard decision trees and deserves more careful analysis.
For example, a critical ambiguity concerns the behavior when a node’s weight is zero.
The discussion on page 3 implies taking the right branch, which seems questionable.
It seems preferable to output a decision at that node, with the possibility of later making the weight nonzero to enable a split.
Precisely defining this behavior is important, as it directly relates to pruning strategies used in greedy training.
I have a significant practical concern regarding the work's motivation.
Plain decision trees are useful primarily due to their interpretability; if interpretability is not a requirement, other methods typically offer superior performance.
As presented, I expect this method to yield non-interpretable results, as L2 regularization will likely produce dense weight vectors.
While switching to L1 regularization might help, performance could suffer if regularization is strong enough to enforce exactly 1-sparse splits.
This requires investigation.
My other main concern is insufficient experimental detail regarding baselines. 
No details are provided on the pruning strategies used for the naive approach.
Given the range of regularization parameters explored for the proposed method, the naive method should be tested with a few different pruning strategies and/or parameters.
It would also be fair to evaluate some other straightforward global optimization strategies, such as training a greedy method with random subsets of the training data and selecting the model that minimizes the desired loss on the full training or validation set.
Regarding clarity, the paper is reasonably well-written. The description of inference could be more concise.
For example, Section 6.2 could be summarized by stating that the objective is first maximized for all *g* corresponding to a given leaf, and then maximized over all leaves.
Section 6.4 could also be more explicit.
In summary, this is a clever approach that raises very interesting questions.
However, its practical utility remains unclear, both due to the interpretability issue (why use standard decision trees if interpretability is sacrificed?) and the lack of experimental detail.
PS: although the title mentions forests, the paper does not address that case.
POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
I still believe the case of zero weights is critical and warrants further analysis, for the reasons outlined in my review.
The rebuttal suggests the weights are initialized randomly, which explains why this issue does not arise in practice.
However, I suspect that the most interesting aspect of this method would be the case where weights are initially zero and gradually increased.
I encourage the authors to explore this direction. The submission presents a clever reformulation of global decision tree optimization as structured prediction, accompanied by an efficient algorithm for solving the optimization. However, some aspects of the motivation and experimental results are not fully convincing.
