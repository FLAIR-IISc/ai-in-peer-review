This paper presents a novel approach for the global optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as latent structured prediction. The core idea is to represent the traversal path through the tree for a given data point as a latent variable, specifically a vector of signs corresponding to the decisions at each internal node. The authors propose a non-convex and non-differentiable loss function, which they relax to a differentiable (though still non-convex) upper bound. This objective is then minimized using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), where each step requires solving a loss-augmented inference problem, for which efficient algorithms are provided. Experiments compare this approach to a standard greedy training method.

The primary strength of this work lies in its novel and elegant formulation of decision tree training. The key contributions—casting the problem as structured prediction and developing efficient algorithms for the resulting objective—are significant. The sparse nature of the gradient updates, where only a single node's parameters are updated in each step, is particularly thought-provoking. While the authors frame this primarily as a computational benefit, it opens up a fascinating connection to constructive tree-growing algorithms. One could envision an optimization process that begins with a tree of all-zero weights and gradually "grows" by making specific node weights non-zero. This suggests a principled way to learn the tree structure and complexity directly from the data.

This perspective raises compelling theoretical questions that are not fully explored in the paper: Would such a procedure be equivalent to a known greedy strategy, or would it offer a new perspective on balancing model complexity and fit? For instance, with appropriate regularization, could this method naturally converge to a compact tree, as hinted at in Figure 2? This line of inquiry cuts to the core of what makes the proposed method a valuable alternative to standard decision trees and deserves a more thorough analysis.

Despite the novelty of the approach, I have several concerns regarding its practical motivation and experimental validation.

**1. Motivation and Interpretability**

A major concern relates to the practical motivation for this work. A primary reason for using single decision trees over more powerful models like SVMs or ensembles is their interpretability, which stems from simple, axis-aligned splits. The proposed method, particularly with the L2 regularization used, is likely to produce dense weight vectors at each node. This results in oblique splits (linear combinations of features), which severely undermines the model's interpretability. If interpretability is lost, it becomes unclear why one would prefer this globally optimized but complex tree over other high-performance, non-interpretable models.

While L1 regularization might induce sparsity in the weight vectors, it is not guaranteed to produce the 1-sparse splits of a traditional decision tree, and achieving this level of sparsity might come at a significant cost to performance. This critical trade-off between performance and interpretability should be experimentally validated and discussed.

**2. Methodological Ambiguity: The Case of Zero Weights**

There is a critical ambiguity in the model's definition concerning nodes with zero-weight vectors. The paper suggests a default path (e.g., the right branch) in this case, which seems arbitrary. This scenario is not merely a theoretical edge case; it is central to the tree-growing interpretation discussed earlier. A more natural behavior might be for a zero-weight node to act as a leaf, making a prediction. Subsequently, the optimization could make its weights non-zero, effectively splitting the node. Clarifying this behavior is crucial, as it directly relates to concepts of tree pruning and growth. It seems this issue may not arise in practice if weights are initialized randomly, but exploring initialization from zero could make the method significantly more interesting.

**3. Experimental Evaluation**

The experimental comparison with the baseline greedy method is insufficient to demonstrate a clear advantage.
*   **Baseline Details:** The configuration of the baseline is not detailed. Standard greedy methods (e.g., CART) often employ pruning to control complexity and improve generalization. The authors should clarify if pruning was used for the baseline and, if not, compare their regularized method against a baseline tuned with various pruning parameters.
*   **Additional Baselines:** The paper could be strengthened by including other simple "global" optimization heuristics as baselines. For example, a common technique is to train multiple greedy trees on random subsets of the data and select the best one based on validation performance. Comparing against such a straightforward baseline would better contextualize the performance gains of the proposed method.

**Clarity and Scope**

The paper is generally well-written, but the description of the inference algorithms could be improved for clarity.
*   The description of the maximization in Section 6.2 could be more concisely summarized as a two-step process: first maximizing over all paths `g` leading to a specific leaf, and then maximizing over all leaves.
*   The explanation in Section 6.4 could also be more explicit.
*   The title mentions "Decision Forests," but the paper's scope is limited to single decision trees. The title should be adjusted to more accurately reflect the content.

**Conclusion**

In conclusion, this paper introduces a clever and theoretically interesting reformulation of decision tree optimization as a structured prediction problem. The resulting optimization algorithm and its sparse update property are elegant and suggest promising future research directions, particularly regarding adaptive structure learning. However, the paper's current contribution is diminished by concerns about the practical utility of the method—namely the loss of interpretability—and an experimental evaluation that lacks sufficient detail and stronger baselines. While the core idea is valuable, its practical significance remains unclear without addressing these issues. I encourage the authors to explore the connection to tree growing from zero-initialized weights, as this may be the most impactful direction for this work.
