**Summary**

This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters of a decision tree, including the decision splits and the posterior probabilities at the leaves. This is an important contribution, as standard methods train trees greedily, one layer at a time. The authors formulate the joint training task as a single optimization problem, which is then made tractable by deriving an approximate upper bound on the objective.

**Quality**

While the paper is well-written, there appears to be a potential flaw in the derivation of a key theoretical bound, as detailed below.

**Clarity**

The derivation of the objective function is clear. However, the experimental section and its presentation could be significantly improved.

**Originality**

The paper addresses the important and original problem of moving beyond greedy, layer-wise training for decision trees.

**Significance**

The proposed solution is a valuable contribution to the field. However, its significance is currently limited by the potential theoretical flaw and a somewhat narrow experimental evaluation.

**Comments**

1.  **Potential flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound.** The argument in l.216f justifying the upper bound in Equation (7) is not convincing. The authors state that "... for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$ ...". To illustrate why this may not hold, consider the following counterexample:
    Let $W\bx = [5; -5]$. Then $\hat{\bh} = \sgn(W\bx) = [1; -1]$, which gives $\hat{\bh}^T W\bx = 10$.
    Now, choose another path $\bg = [1; 1] \neq \hat{\bh}$. For this path, we have $\bg^T W\bx = 0$.
    The right-hand side of Equation (7) becomes $\bg^T W\bx - \hat{\bh}^T W\bx + \ell(...) = 0 - 10 + \ell(...) = \ell(...) - 10$.
    This value is clearly smaller than $\ell(...)$, which violates the condition for a valid upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not seem to account for this case and is therefore incomplete. On a related note, the use of `\argmax` in the bound seems incorrect; `\max` should be used instead.

2.  **Unassigned data points at leaves.** The authors report that some leaves are not assigned any data points after training. This is a surprising outcome that may indicate an underlying issue with the objective function or the initialization procedure. The proposed heuristic of fixing the data-to-leaf assignments seems sub-optimal. Could this step be better motivated? For example, could it be framed within a well-known optimization scheme like the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm or a concave-convex procedure (CCCP), which are common in clustering-style problems?

3.  **Necessity of efficient loss-augmented inference.** The motivation for designing an efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm could be stronger. Given that the maximum tree depth in the experiments is 16, a brute-force search over all $2^{16} = 65,536$ leaves seems computationally feasible. How much did restricting the search space via the Hamming ball heuristic actually impact performance? The supplement provides a single result, but it is not clear if this finding generalizes. Additionally, the term 'leaves' as used in this part of the supplement is ambiguous and requires clarification.

4.  **Missing baseline.** The experimental evaluation would be more convincing with the inclusion of a crucial baseline: a decision tree with non-axis-aligned split functions trained in the standard, greedy, layer-by-layer fashion. This would allow for a direct comparison between the proposed joint training and the conventional greedy approach for the same class of models.

5.  **Fixed tree depth.** A key limitation of the proposed method is that the tree depth is a hyperparameter that must be specified in advance. This limitation should be discussed more explicitly. Furthermore, an ablation study analyzing the model's sensitivity to the depth parameter would significantly strengthen the paper's experimental section.

6.  **Runtime analysis.** The paper would benefit from an experimental comparison of the training runtime of the proposed method against the baselines. This would help readers assess the practical trade-offs of the joint optimization approach.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper addresses the interesting and important problem of jointly training all parameters of a decision tree. However, the submission has two main weaknesses: a potential flaw in the derivation of a key theoretical bound and an experimental evaluation that is somewhat limited. I am willing to reconsider my score if the authors can provide a convincing explanation for these points in their rebuttal.
