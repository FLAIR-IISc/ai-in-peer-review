This paper proposes a non-greedy algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees by recasting tree induction as structured prediction with latent variables. Each internal decision in the tree is represented as a binary latent vector h = sgn(Wx). The authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with loss-augmented inference. They further introduce a fast loss-augmented inference scheme that leverages the tree structure to reduce per-update complexity from exponential in depth to O(d^2 p). The approach jointly optimizes oblique splits and leaf parameters under per-row norm constraints on W, and is evaluated against greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) and CO2, a depth-1 continuous optimization method applied greedily.

Relation to prior work: The method addresses the long-standing suboptimality of greedy induction (ID3/CART) and the intractability of exact global optimization. It connects tree learning to latent structured prediction (e.g., structural SVM with latent variables) and improves upon soft-split models (HME) by retaining hard, O(d p) inference at test time. It also relates to max-margin split learning and CO2, but differs in its joint, non-greedy optimization under a global surrogate.

Strengths:
- A novel and appealing reformulation of tree learning as latent structured prediction, with a principled surrogate objective and efficient loss-augmented inference.
- Sparse gradient updates that appear to modify only a small subset of nodes per example (if I am not mistaken, potentially a single node in many steps), which is computationally advantageous and suggests interesting model-growth behavior.
- A clear regularization mechanism (row-norm constraints) that controls the tightness of the bound and implicitly prunes leaves, with empirical evidence that this improves generalization.
- Consistent empirical gains over several greedy baselines across multiple datasets; the comparison to CO2 isolates the benefit of non-greedy joint optimization.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Practical motivation and significance: Plain decision trees are often used for interpretability. Oblique splits with L2 regularization are likely dense and thus non-interpretable. If interpretability is not central, competing non-tree models often offer stronger accuracy. The paper should address whether L1 (or group-sparse) regularization can produce sparse, more interpretable splits without sacrificing performance; this needs to be tested.
- Experimental baselines: Details on pruning for the greedy baselines are missing. Since the proposed method is carefully tuned (including ν and learning rates), greedy baselines should be run with multiple well-known pruning strategies/parameters. It would also be fair to include simple global-selection heuristics, e.g., training multiple greedy trees on random subsets and choosing the one with lowest validation loss under the same loss used by the proposed method.
- Conceptual point about sparsity and model growth: The sparse nature of the updates suggests an intriguing possibility that is not explored: start with all-zero weights and gradually “grow” the tree by turning on weights as needed, allowing complexity to increase adaptively. This raises questions about equivalence to, or deviations from, greedy strategies (e.g., whether it is preferable to introduce new nodes rather than revise earlier splits) and whether appropriate regularization drives convergence to compact trees, as Fig. 2 hints. This seems central to the value of non-greediness and deserves deeper analysis.
- Ambiguity at zero-weight decisions: What is the behavior when a node’s weight is exactly zero (or when wi^T x − bi = 0)? The text seems to imply taking the right branch by convention, which is unsatisfying. It may be preferable to predict at the node (analogous to pruning), with the option to later make the split active. This corner case is important, both theoretically and practically, and parallels standard pruning strategies.
- Clarity: The exposition could be tightened in places. For example:
  - Section 6.2 could be summarized more explicitly: first maximize over g consistent with each leaf, then maximize over leaves.
  - Section 6.4 could be more explicit about which bits differ between ĝ and ĥ and how this limits updates to a small number of nodes along the root-to-leaf path.
- Scope: The paper (and especially the abstract) mentions forests, but the method and experiments focus on single trees; the forest case is not addressed empirically.

Quality: The technical development is sound and well-motivated. The surrogate bound, the fast loss-augmented inference, and the SGD/SSGD algorithms are carefully derived. However, some practical edge cases (zero-weight decisions) and the breadth of baseline comparisons need attention.

Clarity: Generally well-written, but certain inference sections could be more direct, and experimental details (especially pruning protocols for baselines) should be expanded.

Originality: High. The latent structured prediction perspective and the tailored bound/inference procedure for trees are novel and interesting.

Significance: Promising but somewhat limited by the interpretability concern and the current experimental scope. Demonstrations with sparse regularization and a broader set of baselines would strengthen the case for practical impact.

Pros:
- Elegant reformulation and efficient optimization.
- Sparse updates and principled regularization that control model complexity.
- Empirical improvements over multiple greedy baselines.

Cons:
- Likely loss of interpretability; sparse variants not evaluated.
- Incomplete baseline/pruning comparisons.
- Ambiguity around zero-weight decisions and limited discussion of model-growth behavior.
- Forests not empirically addressed.

Post-rebuttal comments: I remain convinced the zero-weights case is critical and merits explicit treatment. The rebuttal suggests random initialization avoids this in practice, but the most compelling variant might initialize weights at zero and gradually activate them. I encourage the authors to explore this direction.

Overall: A clever and potentially impactful approach with solid technical contributions. Strengthening the empirical section (pruning baselines and sparse regularization), clarifying edge cases, and addressing interpretability would make the work more convincing as a practical alternative to greedy trees.
