- Paper casts global decision-tree optimization as latent structured prediction with sign-vector latents and proposes a non-differentiable loss approximated by a differentiable upper bound.
- Key technical contributions are the reformulation as structured prediction and efficient algorithms for loss-augmented inference used inside SGD.
- A notable property is that gradient updates are sparse: each SGD step changes only one node’s weight vector.
- The sparsity property suggests intriguing possibilities (e.g., growing an infinitely deep tree from zero weights) that the paper does not analyze.
- The paper does not clearly define behavior when a node’s weight is exactly zero; the current implied choice (take right branch) is questionable and needs precise treatment.
- The reviewer urges analysis of whether the method’s dynamics replicate or differ from known greedy splitting strategies and how regularization affects eventual tree size.
- Practical motivation is weak: if interpretability is sacrificed, other methods usually outperform decision trees, and L2 regularization will likely produce dense, non-interpretable splits.
- The authors should evaluate L1 regularization (or sparsity-inducing methods) and test if performance degrades when enforcing 1-sparse splits for interpretability.
- Experimental evaluation is insufficiently detailed: baseline greedy methods lack description of pruning strategies and comparable tuning.
- The reviewer recommends comparing to simple global baselines (e.g., train multiple greedy trees on random subsets and select by validation loss).
- Presentation could be clearer in inference sections (e.g., summarize Sec.6.2 as maximizing per-leaf then over leaves; clarify Sec.6.4).
- The paper’s title mentions forests but does not address random forests or multiple-tree cases.
- Post-rebuttal: the zero-weight initialization issue remains critical; random initialization may hide it, but starting from zero and growing weights would be an interesting direction to explore.
- Overall: the approach is clever and novel, but practical usefulness is unclear due to interpretability concerns and insufficient experimental validation.
