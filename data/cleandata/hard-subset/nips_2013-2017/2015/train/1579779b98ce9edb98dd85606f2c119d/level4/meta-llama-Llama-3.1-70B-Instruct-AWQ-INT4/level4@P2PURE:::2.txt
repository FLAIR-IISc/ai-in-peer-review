Summary: The authors propose a method for jointly training all parameters of decision trees, including decision splits and posterior probabilities. This is a significant topic, as current methods typically train trees in a greedy, layer-by-layer manner. The task is formulated as a single optimization problem, which is then upper-bounded and approximated to obtain a tractable formulation.

Quality: The paper is well-written, but there may be flaws in the reasoning. See below for details. Clarity: The derivation is clean, but the experiments and their presentation could be improved. Originality: Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important topic. Significance: The proposed solution is valuable, although there seem to be some shortcomings. See below for details.

Comments:
- A potential flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound is noted. Specifically, the argument in lines 216f is confusing, as it claims that for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right-hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$. However, a counterexample can be constructed: W\bx = [5;-5] --> \hat \bh = [1;-1] = \sgn(W\bx) --> \hat \bh^T W\bx = 10. Choosing g = [1;1] results in g^T W\bx = 0. Therefore, the right-hand side of Eq. (7) is g^T W\bx - \hat \bh^T W\bx + \ell(...) = -10 + \ell(...), which could be smaller than \ell(...) and hence not a valid upper bound. Furthermore, the proof of Prop. 1 in the supplementary material does not cover this case and is therefore incomplete.
- It is unclear why some leaves are not assigned any data points. This seems unusual and could indicate a problem with the objective or the initialization. The suggested heuristic of a fixed assignment of data-points to tree leaves appears suboptimal but could potentially be motivated via expectation-maximization or concave-convex procedures, similar to other clustering techniques.
- The importance of the efficient loss-augmented inference design is questioned. Given that the depth of the tree is at most 16, greedily checking 2^16 = 65536 values seems feasible. How much did the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball impact performance? While one result is provided in the supplementary material, it is unclear whether this generalizes. Additionally, the term 'leaves' is not clearly defined.
- A baseline that trains non-axis-aligned split functions of trees in a standard greedy manner, without any initialization, is missing.
- The depth of the tree needs to be specified ahead of time, which is a significant limitation. The authors should discuss this limitation more thoroughly and provide a more careful experimental evaluation regarding the depth parameter.
--------------- The authors are thanked for carefully explaining the reasoning behind the loss bound (Eq. 7). Replacing \arg\max with \max is suggested. As stated in the summary, the score has been adjusted.
A comparison of runtime could strengthen the paper. A more thorough evaluation of the method would be desirable for the reader. The submission considers an interesting problem of jointly training all parameters of decision trees. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and there could potentially be a flaw in the derivation. A careful explanation in the rebuttal would be appreciated, and the score may be adjusted accordingly.
