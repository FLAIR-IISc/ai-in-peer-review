

This paper presents a novel approach to decision tree optimization by formulating a global objective function that jointly optimizes split functions at all tree levels, rather than using the conventional greedy approach. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient optimization using stochastic gradient descent. This formulation reduces the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p), making deep tree training feasible. The work builds upon prior non-greedy approaches (Bennett, 2000; Murthy and Salzburg, 1994) while overcoming their limitations in computational complexity and applicability to general loss functions.

The paper excels in clarity, with a well-structured exposition that carefully develops the theoretical foundation and algorithmic details. The connection to structured prediction is elegantly explained, and the technical contributions regarding the complexity reduction are clearly articulated. The writing is precise and accessible to the machine learning community, making the paper a valuable contribution to the literature.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by framing decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. This perspective is novel and provides a principled framework for global tree optimization that goes beyond previous work. The approach differs substantially from recent NIPS papers on decision trees (e.g., Norouzi et al., 2015; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2014) by addressing the global optimization challenge rather than focusing on incremental improvements to greedy methods.

The quality of the work is strong, with rigorous theoretical development and careful analysis of the optimization problem. The authors provide a clear proof of the upper bound and demonstrate how the regularization parameter ν controls the trade-off between model complexity and generalization. The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims and appropriate experimental validation.

The significance of this work lies in its potential to inspire new research directions in tree-based learning methods. While the experimental results demonstrate improved generalization performance compared to greedy baselines, the gains are sometimes marginal, and the paper could have better explored the relationship between the regularization parameter ν and model complexity. The claim that "global optimization should generally lead to more overfitting" requires further investigation, as the experiments show the opposite trend.

Strengths: 
- Elegant theoretical formulation linking decision trees to structured prediction
- Clear and well-written exposition of a complex technical problem
- Significant reduction in computational complexity for deep tree training
- General approach applicable to any loss function

Weaknesses:
- Experimental results are somewhat disappointing, with marginal improvements over greedy baselines
- Limited discussion of overfitting concerns, contrary to the authors' claim
- The paper does not fully explore the potential of the approach to inspire future work
- The proposed method could be combined with standard post-processing techniques (e.g., pruning) to potentially improve results

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-written paper that makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to decision tree learning. While the experimental results could be strengthened, the elegance of the approach and its potential to stimulate future research justify acceptance. The paper would benefit from additional analysis of the regularization parameter's impact on overfitting and a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art tree-based methods.
