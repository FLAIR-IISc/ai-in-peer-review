The submission proposes an algorithm for global optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as structured prediction, utilizing latent structured variables as sign vectors that encode decisions made at each internal node in the tree. A non-differentiable and non-convex loss function is introduced, which is approximated by a differentiable yet non-convex upper bound. This loss is minimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD), requiring the solution of a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided. The experimental results compare this method to a standard greedy training approach.

The key contributions of this work lie in casting global decision tree (DT) optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms to optimize the objective. This novel approach raises interesting questions and has several advantages. By formulating the optimization as structured prediction, the method enables the use of SGD by solving a sequence of loss-augmented optimization problems that leverage the problem's special structure. Furthermore, the gradient updates are sparse, with each gradient step involving changes to only one node's weight vector. This property not only offers computational advantages but also presents intriguing possibilities, such as optimizing over an infinitely deep tree by gradually growing the tree and allowing the model's complexity to naturally adapt to the data.

However, several concerns arise regarding practical considerations, particularly the motivation behind the method and the significance of the performance gains over naive methods. As a method for globally optimizing DTs, the approach is intriguing but raises questions about its practical usefulness. The proposed method may produce non-interpretable results due to the use of L2 regularization, which could lead to dense weight vectors. Although switching to L1 regularization might help, it is uncertain whether performance would be affected if regularization were applied to the point of producing exactly 1-sparse splits.

The experimental results lack detail, particularly regarding baselines. The pruning strategies employed for the naive approach are not specified, and other trivial strategies for global optimization, such as training a greedy method with random subsets of the training data, are not considered. The paper is reasonably well-written, but some sections, such as those describing inference, could be clarified.

In summary, this approach is clever and raises interesting questions, but its practical usefulness is uncertain due to concerns about interpretability and the lack of experimental details. Further analysis is necessary to fully understand the method's potential.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS

The case of zero weights remains a critical issue that warrants further analysis. Although the rebuttal suggests that random initialization may avoid this issue in practice, the more interesting scenario would be to initialize weights to zero and gradually increase them. This direction could make the method more compelling, and the authors are encouraged to explore it.
