This paper introduces a novel framework for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The central idea is to formulate the problem of jointly learning all split functions and leaf parameters under a global objective by framing it as a structured prediction task with latent variables. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a tractable surrogate objective. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for the loss-augmented inference step, which reduces the computational complexity from exponential to polynomial in the tree's depth, thereby making the approach scalable to deep trees. This work provides a principled optimization-based alternative to traditional greedy, node-at-a-time induction methods (like CART and ID3) and builds upon prior work in structured prediction and non-greedy tree learning.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its elegant and principled formulation of a classic and difficult problem. Connecting global decision tree optimization to the well-established framework of structured prediction with latent variables is a significant conceptual contribution that is both insightful and novel. The derivation of the surrogate objective function is technically sound, providing a continuous and piecewise-smooth relaxation to an otherwise discrete and intractable optimization problem. The proposed method is general, applicable to various loss functions, and the development of an efficient, scalable training algorithm is a crucial contribution for practical relevance. The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. This approach is likely to inspire further research into global optimization methods for tree-based models.

**Weaknesses:**
The main weakness of the work lies in the experimental evaluation. While the proposed non-greedy trees are shown to outperform several greedy baselines, the improvements in classification accuracy are somewhat modest across the benchmark datasets. This might temper the practical significance of the approach when weighed against its increased complexity compared to standard greedy methods. Additionally, global optimization strategies are often more prone to overfitting than their greedy counterparts. While the authors incorporate regularization on the norm of the split function parameters, the paper would be stronger with a more detailed analysis of this potential for overfitting, perhaps by empirically comparing their regularization strategy against traditional methods like post-pruning or limiting the minimum number of samples per leaf.

**Constructive Suggestions:**
A potential way to enhance empirical performance could be to explore a hybrid approach. After running the proposed global optimization, the authors could discard the final layer of splits and re-optimize them and the leaf nodes using a standard greedy procedure. This might better capture the local data structure at the bottom of the tree and could potentially lead to better results.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   Presents a highly original and elegant formulation for the global optimization of decision trees.
    *   The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is a novel contribution that may open new research directions.
    *   The proposed optimization framework is technically sound, well-motivated, and includes an efficient algorithm that makes the method practical.
    *   The paper is very clearly written and easy to understand.

*   **Arguments Against Acceptance:**
    *   The empirical performance gains over strong, standard baselines are relatively small, which raises questions about the method's practical impact.
    *   The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion and analysis of overfitting, a key challenge in global optimization.
