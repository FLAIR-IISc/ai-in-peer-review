## Review

**Summary:** This paper introduces a method for the joint optimization of all parameters – decision splits and posterior probabilities – within decision trees. This is a significant contribution, as existing approaches typically rely on greedy, layer-wise training. The problem is elegantly formulated as a single optimization problem, subsequently upper-bounded and approximated to achieve a tractable solution.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong writing quality, though potential issues exist within the theoretical reasoning, as detailed below. 

**Clarity:** The derivation is presented clearly; however, the experimental section and its presentation would benefit from improvement.

**Originality:** Exploring joint training for decision trees represents an important and novel research direction.

**Significance:** The proposed solution holds considerable value, despite certain shortcomings outlined below.

**Comments:**

- There appears to be a potential flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound. Specifically, the argument presented in lines 216-217 is unclear. To illustrate: Consider the case where W\bx = [5;-5], leading to \hat \bh = [1;-1] = \sgn(W\bx) and \hat \bh^T W\bx = 10. If g = [1;1], then g^T W\bx = 0. Consequently, the right-hand side of Eq. (7) becomes g^T W\bx - \hat \bh^T W\bx + \ell(...) = -10 + \ell(...), which could be smaller than \ell(...) and thus invalidate the upper bound. The proof of Prop. 1 in the supplementary material does not address this scenario, indicating an incompleteness.

- The observation that some leaves are not assigned any data points is perplexing and may signal a problem with the objective function or the initialization process. The current heuristic of fixed data point assignment to leaves seems sub-optimal, but could potentially be justified through an expectation maximization or concave-convex procedure, analogous to other clustering techniques.

- The importance of the design choices in the efficient loss-augmented inference procedure warrants further discussion. Given a maximum tree depth of 16, a greedy search across 2^16 = 65536 values appears computationally feasible. Quantifying the performance impact of the Hamming ball restriction would be valuable. While a result is presented in the supplementary material, its generalizability is uncertain. Clarification on the meaning of 'leaves' in this context is also needed.

- The absence of a baseline employing standard greedy training with non-axis aligned split functions (i.e., without initialization) is a notable omission.

- The requirement to pre-specify the tree depth introduces a limitation that should be discussed more thoroughly. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation specifically addressing the influence of the depth parameter would be beneficial.

--------------- 

I appreciate the authors’ clarification regarding the reasoning behind the loss bound (Eq. 7). I suggest considering a replacement of \arg\max with \max. As mentioned in my summary, I have adjusted my score accordingly. Including an experiment comparing runtime would strengthen the paper, and a more thorough evaluation of the method would enhance its presentation. The submission addresses an interesting problem – the joint training of all decision tree parameters – but the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and a potential flaw exists in the derivation. I am willing to reassess my score based on a compelling rebuttal.
