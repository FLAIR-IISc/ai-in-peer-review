### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method for jointly training all parameters of a decision tree, including the decision splits and the posterior probabilities at the leaves. This is a significant and important departure from standard greedy, layer-by-layer training procedures. The authors formulate the training process as a single, global optimization problem. To make this problem tractable, they derive and optimize a convex upper bound on the empirical loss.

### **Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance**

*   **Quality & Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, and the mathematical derivations are presented clearly. However, as detailed below, there appears to be a potential flaw in a key derivation, and the experimental section could be improved for greater clarity and impact.
*   **Originality & Significance:** The problem of jointly optimizing all parameters of a decision tree is of considerable importance, as it has the potential to overcome the local optima associated with greedy training. The proposed solution is elegant and valuable, though its practical effectiveness is not yet fully demonstrated due to the concerns raised below.

### **Detailed Comments**

My primary concerns are related to the correctness of the loss bound and the thoroughness of the experimental evaluation.

**1. Potential Flaw in the Derivation of the Empirical Loss Bound**

My main concern regards a potential flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound in Equation (7). The argument in lines 216-217, which states that "... for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$ ...", appears to be incorrect.

To illustrate, consider a simple example where $W\bx = [5, -5]^T$. In this case, the argmax gives $\hat{\bh} = \sgn(W\bx) = [1, -1]^T$, and the term $\hat{\bh}^T W\bx = 10$. If we choose a different vector, for instance $\bg = [1, 1]^T$, the corresponding term is $\bg^T W\bx = 0$. The right-hand side of Equation (7) then becomes $\bg^T W\bx - \hat{\bh}^T W\bx + \ell(...) = 0 - 10 + \ell(...) = -10 + \ell(...)$, which could be smaller than the original loss $\ell(...)$ that would be obtained by setting $\bg$ to $\hat{\bh}$. This would violate the upper bound condition. The proof for Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not seem to account for this scenario, making it incomplete. As this bound is central to the proposed method, a clarification from the authors is essential.

**2. Empty Tree Leaves After Training**

The experiments reveal that some leaves are not assigned any data points after training. This is an unusual outcome for a decision tree and may indicate an underlying issue with the objective function or the initialization strategy. The authors suggest a heuristic of a fixed data-to-leaf assignment to address this. While this is a practical workaround, it seems suboptimal. Could the authors provide more intuition for this phenomenon? Perhaps this heuristic could be more formally motivated, for example, by drawing a connection to the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm or a concave-convex procedure (CCCP), where assignments are fixed while model parameters are updated.

**3. Necessity and Impact of Efficient Loss-Augmented Inference**

The paper introduces an efficient method for loss-augmented inference, but its practical necessity for the reported experiments is not entirely clear. With a maximum tree depth of 16, a full, greedy search over all $2^{16} \approx 65,000$ leaves seems computationally feasible on modern hardware. To better justify this contribution, it would be very helpful if the authors could provide:
*   An empirical runtime comparison between their efficient inference and a full search to quantify the speed-up.
*   A more comprehensive analysis of how restricting the search space to a Hamming ball affects classification performance. A single result is mentioned in the supplement, but it is unclear if this generalizes.

**4. Missing Experimental Baselines and Analysis**

The experimental comparison could be significantly strengthened in two ways:
*   **Missing Baseline:** A crucial baseline is missing: a decision tree with non-axis-aligned split functions trained using a standard, greedy, layer-by-layer procedure. This would allow for a direct comparison against the most relevant conventional method and would better isolate the performance gains attributable to the proposed joint optimization.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The proposed method requires the tree depth to be pre-specified, which is a practical limitation compared to greedy methods that can grow the tree dynamically. This limitation should be discussed more explicitly. Furthermore, an experimental evaluation showing the model's sensitivity to the depth parameter across a few datasets would be highly informative for potential users of this method.

### **Conclusion**

This paper addresses the interesting and important problem of jointly training all parameters of a decision tree. The approach of formulating a global optimization problem and solving a convex relaxation is compelling. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and more importantly, there appears to be a potential flaw in a core theoretical derivation. I am willing to raise my score if the authors can provide a careful and convincing explanation regarding the loss bound in their rebuttal.
