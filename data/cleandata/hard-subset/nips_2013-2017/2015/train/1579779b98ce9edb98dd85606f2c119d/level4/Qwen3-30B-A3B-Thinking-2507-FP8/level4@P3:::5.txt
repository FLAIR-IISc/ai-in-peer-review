

This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by jointly optimizing split functions at all tree levels with leaf parameters, rather than the standard greedy node-by-node procedure. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. They demonstrate that the gradient of their surrogate objective can be computed in O(d) time per example (where d is tree depth), enabling efficient training of deep trees using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The method's key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) through a clever reformulation of the loss upper bound.

The work builds on several important threads in the literature. While Bennett [1] previously proposed non-greedy tree optimization via multi-linear programming, their approach was limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss and high computational complexity. The paper also relates to the Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13] that uses soft splits for differentiable optimization, but the authors' approach maintains the computational efficiency of hard decision trees. Most relevantly, the paper extends the CO2 algorithm [18], which applies a similar upper bound approach but in a greedy manner. By optimizing the upper bound globally rather than sequentially, the authors aim to create more compact trees with better generalization.

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with a well-motivated formulation of the loss upper bound and a clear demonstration of its computational advantages. The theoretical analysis (Proposition 1) justifies the regularization approach, and the experimental results validate the method's feasibility. However, the performance gains over greedy baselines are modest across most datasets, with significant improvements observed only on a few benchmarks. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of when the non-greedy approach provides substantial advantages.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, though some sections would benefit from greater precision. The description of Stable SGD (SSGD) lacks sufficient detail—specifically, the paper does not clearly explain how data points are assigned to leaves or how these assignments are updated. A pseudocode for SSGD would substantially improve clarity. Additionally, the paper references "active leaves" in Figure 2 without defining the term, which may confuse readers. The projection step for Θ in Algorithm 1 (line 10) requires clarification on how the simplex projection is implemented in practice, though the paper correctly notes it ensures each row of Θ sums to one.

**Originality**: The connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is novel, and the efficient optimization method for deep trees represents a significant technical contribution. The reformulation of the loss upper bound to reduce computational complexity from exponential to O(d²p) is particularly valuable. While prior work has explored non-greedy tree learning [1, 17], this paper provides a more scalable and generalizable framework applicable to any loss function.

**Significance**: The method provides a principled approach to training better decision trees, which are widely used in practice. However, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by the modest performance gains across datasets and the computational complexity of the method (O(d²p) per gradient step). The authors note that the method is most beneficial when compared to CO2 [18], which applies their upper bound approach greedily. While the paper demonstrates that non-greedy optimization improves upon CO2 in most cases, it's unclear whether this would translate to substantial practical advantages over standard greedy approaches for most applications.

The paper contains several minor issues that should be addressed. The phrase "number of features evaluated" in lines 407-408 is confusing, as standard decision trees evaluate all features at each node. Equations (7), (8), and (9) incorrectly use "argmax" instead of "max" in the text. Figure 1 is referenced but never discussed in the main text, and line 141 contains a grammatical error ("leaf on by this path"). The supplementary material contains similar errors (line 29: "max{g ∈ sgn...}" should be "max{g = sgn...}"; line 30: the expression should reference sgn(Wx), not g).

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on decision tree learning. The non-greedy approach is well-motivated, the algorithm is efficiently implemented, and the results demonstrate the method's feasibility. While the performance gains are modest in some cases, the method provides a valuable alternative to greedy tree learning that could be particularly useful in applications where tree quality is paramount. The paper would be strengthened by more detailed explanations of the Stable SGD variant, clearer definitions of key terms, and a more comprehensive analysis of the method's advantages across different problem settings.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a solid technical contribution to decision tree learning with a well-motivated approach and sound experiments. While the performance improvements are not dramatic across all datasets, the method provides a principled framework for non-greedy tree optimization that advances the state of the art in this area. The minor issues noted above can be easily addressed in the camera-ready version.
