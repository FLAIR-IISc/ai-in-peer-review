Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a non-greedy algorithm for training decision trees with oblique (linear) splits by optimizing a global surrogate objective rather than fitting each split greedily. The key idea is to formulate tree inference as structured prediction with latent variables: an m-bit code h represents split decisions, and the empirical loss is upper-bounded by a convex–concave surrogate that combines a linear score h^T Wx with the leaf loss. The authors derive two bounds: a full loss-augmented inference bound and a fast variant that restricts g to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), enabling O(d^2 p) per-update complexity. Stochastic gradient descent (with a “Stable SGD” variant) is used to jointly optimize split weights W under per-row norm constraints and leaf parameters Θ. Experiments on several multiclass datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) show that single non-greedy trees generally outperform greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, and CO2) across depths.

This work builds on Bennett’s global optimization of trees, oblique split methods (OC1), max-margin trees, and probabilistic mixtures (HME). Its most direct connection is to structured SVMs with latent variables, margin-rescaling bounds, and the authors’ prior CO2 method (a depth-1 special case applied greedily). The contribution here is a principled global objective for joint split/leaf optimization with efficient loss-augmented inference.

Strengths
- Clear and principled formulation that links decision tree training to latent structured prediction.
- Well-motivated convex–concave upper bound with an insightful Hamming-ball restriction for efficient loss-augmented inference.
- Joint optimization of W and Θ under per-node norm constraints provides a natural regularization mechanism; the analysis of the effect of scaling W is helpful.
- Empirical comparisons against multiple greedy baselines show consistent improvements for single trees across depths, with reduced overfitting in several cases.
- Complexity analysis and the fast inference variant make deep-tree training computationally feasible.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical gains over strong greedy baselines are modest on some datasets, and the improvements are not universally significant. The paper would benefit from statistical significance analysis and error bars.
- Computational overhead is higher than greedy training. The paper shows scalability improvements with the fast bound, but a fair wall-clock comparison under consistent budgets (and across depths) would strengthen the case.
- The role and impact of initialization (axis-aligned tree → CO2 → non-greedy) is not fully disentangled; an ablation showing sensitivity to initialization would clarify how much the global optimization contributes beyond a good starting point.
- The Stable SGD (SSGD) variant is conceptually interesting but under-specified; its contribution to final accuracy and stability is not isolated in the experiments.
- The study focuses on single trees. While this choice is defensible for isolating algorithmic effects, some context on how the method scales to ensembles (or a small experiment) would help assess practical significance.

Quality
The technical development appears sound. The loss bounds are reasonable and correctly motivated by latent structured prediction. The fast loss-augmented inference based on a Hamming ball is a neat idea that aligns with the tree path structure. The complexity claims are plausible, and the projection onto row-wise L2 balls is straightforward. One point of confusion: given that Θ are unnormalized logits with a softmax at leaves, no simplex projection is required; please clarify if any projection is performed in practice.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. However, the description of SSGD is too brief:
- Please give pseudo-code or a more explicit description of how leaf assignments a(x) are fixed/updated, the stopping criterion between updates, and how often assignments are refreshed.
- Define “active” vs. “inactive” leaves precisely and explain how this affects training and the reported metrics.
- Clarify whether results in Figure 2 are obtained with standard SGD or SSGD.
- Be explicit about whether Θ is constrained; if a projection is used, specify the method. Otherwise, state that Θ are unconstrained logits and no projection is needed.

Originality
Framing tree learning as latent structured prediction with a convex–concave upper bound, together with the efficient Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference, appears novel and goes beyond prior oblique split and CO2 methods. The SSGD variant is a pragmatic addition.

Significance
If adopted, the approach could improve the accuracy of single oblique trees and potentially inform better tree ensembles. The main significance lies in the optimization framework and its efficient inference trick rather than in dramatic accuracy gains. The contribution is solid and relevant to NIPS’s core topics.

Questions and requests for clarification
- SSGD: Provide pseudo-code, update frequency for a(x), and the criterion for refreshing assignments. Quantify SSGD’s impact versus SGD in an ablation.
- Active leaves: Define formally and state how “inactivity” is detected and reported (e.g., fraction of training points per leaf).
- Figure 2: State explicitly whether curves use SGD or SSGD and whether hyperparameters (ν, η) differ across depths.
- Θ updates: Confirm whether any projection is performed. If so, detail the projection algorithm. If not, clarify that Θ are logits and softmax ensures valid probabilities.
- Initialization: Provide results from random W/Θ initialization or from axis-aligned only, to isolate the benefit of the non-greedy stage.
- Compute fairness: Report wall-clock training times and number of passes over data for all methods under comparable hardware and implementation.

Minor comments and typos
- “number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree”: by default, standard trees evaluate all features; please clarify what is tuned here (e.g., mtry-like feature subsampling).
- Equations (7–9): where a value is taken, use “max” rather than “argmax.”
- Figure references: ensure Figure 1 is referenced and explained where the navigation function is introduced.
- Line 141: “…to the index of the leaf on by this path.” → “…to the index of the leaf on this path.”
- Line 238: “the solution to loss-augmented inference” → “the solutions to …” (or “the result of …”).
- Line 301: “An key observation” → “A key observation.”
- Line 360: Define FSGD if used; otherwise replace with “fast loss-augmented inference” or “SGD.”
- Line 404: “a tree with minimum training errors.” Likely “minimum validation error.”
Supplementary:
- Line 29: “max{g ∈ sgn…}” → “max{g = sgn(…)}”.
- Line 30: “sgn(Wx)^T Wx + ℓ(Θ^T f(g), y)” → “sgn(Wx)^T Wx + ℓ(Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)), y)”.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled and novel optimization framework with a clear link to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient fast loss-augmented inference enabling deep trees.
- Consistent improvements over strong greedy baselines for single trees and reduced overfitting.

Arguments against acceptance
- Modest and dataset-dependent empirical gains; limited statistical analysis.
- Added computational complexity; limited fair timing comparisons.
- Insufficiently detailed description and ablation of SSGD and initialization effects.

Overall, this is a solid and well-motivated contribution; with clarifications and stronger empirical analysis, it would be a strong addition to the literature on decision tree optimization.
