This paper presents a novel framework for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The central idea is to address the inherent suboptimality of traditional greedy, node-by-node induction algorithms by formulating the joint optimization of all split functions and leaf parameters as a single global objective. The authors establish an insightful connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, where the path a data point takes through the tree is treated as a latent variable. To overcome the intractability of direct optimization, they derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is a proposed "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm, which reduces the per-update complexity from being exponential in the tree depth to polynomial, making the approach practical for training deep trees. The proposed method is evaluated on several benchmark classification tasks, showing improved performance over greedy baselines. This work builds upon the literature of global tree optimization (e.g., Bennett [1]) and structured output learning (e.g., Yu & Joachims [25]), offering a modern and scalable perspective on a classic machine learning problem.

**Strengths / Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Originality:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is highly novel and elegant. It provides a principled optimization framework for a problem that has been dominated by greedy heuristics for decades.
*   **Significance:** This work addresses a fundamental limitation in one of the most widely used classes of machine learning models. The proposed framework is general, supporting various loss functions, and naturally incorporates regularization (via norm constraints on the split parameters `W`), which provides a principled way to control model complexity and prevent overfitting, as suggested by the generalization performance shown in Figure 2.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the upper bound on the empirical loss is correct, and the use of SGD to optimize this surrogate is well-motivated. The analysis of the computational complexity is a major strength, and the development of the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm is a crucial contribution that makes the method computationally feasible for reasonably deep trees.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The connection between decision trees and latent variable structured prediction is explained effectively, and the motivation for each component of the method is well-argued.

**Weaknesses / Areas for improvement:**

*   **Significance of Empirical Gains:** While the experiments demonstrate that the proposed non-greedy trees consistently outperform the greedy baselines, the reported improvements in test accuracy are often modest. Given the increased complexity of the training procedure compared to standard greedy induction, a more thorough discussion of the practical significance of these gains would be valuable. For instance, do these smaller, globally-optimized trees offer other benefits like improved interpretability or faster inference time that might justify the training cost?
*   **Details on SSGD:** The paper introduces a "Stable SGD (SSGD)" variant, but its description is brief. The mechanism for deciding when to update the fixed data-to-leaf assignments (i.e., when "the improvement in the bound becomes negligible") is not specified. Providing a more precise algorithmic description or pseudo-code for this variant would improve the clarity and reproducibility of the work.
*   **Importance of Initialization:** The non-greedy optimization is initialized using the CO2 algorithm, which is itself a strong, greedily-trained baseline. This raises the question of how much of the final performance is attributable to the global fine-tuning versus the quality of the initialization. An ablation study comparing this initialization strategy to a simpler one (e.g., standard axis-aligned information gain, or random splits) would help isolate and quantify the benefit of the proposed global optimization procedure.
*   **Comparison to Ensembles:** The study rightly focuses on single trees to cleanly evaluate the optimization method. However, the state-of-the-art in many applications involving decision trees is dominated by ensembles like Random Forests or Gradient Boosted Trees. While a full empirical comparison is likely out of scope, a discussion on how this non-greedy approach could potentially benefit ensembles would strengthen the paper. For example, could an ensemble of a few non-greedy trees outperform an ensemble of many greedy trees? This would provide important context for the paper's contribution.
