This paper introduces a novel algorithm for learning decision trees with oblique (linear) splits. The central contribution is a non-greedy approach that optimizes all split functions and leaf parameters jointly, in contrast to standard methods that optimize nodes one at a time. The authors accomplish this by elegantly framing the decision tree optimization problem as one of structured prediction with latent variables. They derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective function. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for the loss-augmented inference step, which reduces the complexity from exponential to quadratic in the tree depth, making the training of deeper trees feasible.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a significant and elegant conceptual contribution. This novel perspective connects a classic machine learning model to a modern optimization framework and may open up new avenues for research in tree-based methods.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the surrogate objective via an upper bound on the empirical loss is well-founded. The development of a more efficient, `O(d^2p)`, algorithm for the loss-augmented inference step is a crucial and non-trivial contribution that makes the proposed method practical.
*   **Principled Optimization:** The proposed framework provides a principled way to perform global optimization for a problem that is known to be NP-complete. The use of a regularized objective (constraining the norm of the weight vectors) offers a natural way to control model complexity and prevent overfitting, which is a common issue in non-greedy tree learning.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

*   **Empirical Validation:** The primary weakness is that the experimental results are not entirely convincing. While the proposed non-greedy trees outperform baselines on some datasets, the improvement is not consistently significant across all experiments shown in Figure 2. Given the increased computational complexity compared to greedy methods, a stronger empirical demonstration of superiority would be necessary to motivate the adoption of this more complex algorithm. The paper would be strengthened by showing more substantial gains or by analyzing the cases where the method succeeds or fails.
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper suffers from several points of ambiguity that hinder both understanding and the ability to reproduce the results.
    *   The "Stable SGD" (SSGD) variant is not clearly described. The mechanism for fixing and updating the assignment of data points to leaves is vague. A more formal description or a pseudo-code block for SSGD would be highly beneficial.
    *   It is not specified whether the results in Figure 2 were obtained using the standard SGD (Algorithm 1) or the SSGD variant. This is a critical detail for interpreting the results.
    *   The term "active leaf" is used in the text (e.g., in the description of SSGD and Figure 3 caption) but is never formally defined.
    *   Algorithm 1, line 10, involves updating the leaf parameters `Θ`. For a classification setting with a softmax output, this update should be followed by a projection to ensure `p(y|j)` remains a valid probability distribution. The paper does not specify how this is handled, which is an important implementation detail.

**Detailed Comments and Typos:**

*   In equations (7), (8), and (9), the `argmax` operator over `g` and `h` should be `max`, as the objective value is being used, not the maximizing argument itself.
*   Figure 1 is illustrated but is never referenced or explained in the main text.
*   Line 141: The sentence "...to the index of the leaf on by this path" appears to be incomplete or contain a typo.
*   Line 238: "the solution to loss-augmented inference..." should likely be "the solutions to..." as there could be multiple.
*   Line 301: "An key observation..." should be "A key observation...".
*   Line 360: The acronym "FSGD" is used but was not previously defined. It seems to refer to SGD with fast loss-augmented inference, but this should be made explicit.
*   Lines 407-408: The text mentions tuning the "number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree." For standard decision tree algorithms like CART or ID3, all features are considered at each node. This phrase is confusing and may refer to a random forest-style feature subsampling, which should be clarified.
*   Line 404: "...a tree with minimum training errors." Given this is part of a hyper-parameter tuning process, it should almost certainly refer to validation error, not training error, to avoid overfitting.

**Supplementary Material Comments:**

*   Line 29: `max{g \in sgn...}` should probably be `max_{g = sgn...}` or simply state that `g` is fixed to `sgn(Wx)`.
*   Line 30: The loss term should be `l(Θ^Tf(sgn(Wx)), y)`, not `l(Θ^Tf(g), y)`, to match the argument in the previous line.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a well-motivated and technically interesting approach to non-greedy decision tree optimization. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is novel and powerful. However, the current manuscript is weakened by empirical results that do not consistently demonstrate a strong advantage over simpler greedy methods and by a lack of clarity on several key algorithmic and experimental details. While the core idea is promising, the paper would require significant revisions to improve its clarity and strengthen its empirical claims before it can be considered a strong contribution to the field.
