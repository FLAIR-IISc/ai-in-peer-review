Summary: This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters—decision splits and posterior probabilities—of decision trees. This is an important problem, as current methods typically train trees in a greedy, layer-by-layer fashion. The authors formulate the task as a single optimization problem, which they then upper-bound and approximate to obtain a tractable formulation.

Quality: The paper is generally well-written, but potential flaws exist in the reasoning, as detailed below. Clarity: The derivation is clean, but the presentation of experiments could be improved. Originality: Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important and novel direction. Significance: The proposed solution is valuable, although some shortcomings are apparent, which are discussed below.

Comments:
- I question the derivation of the empirical loss bound. At the very least, the argument in lines 216-217 is confusing: “…for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right-hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$…” Consider the following example to illustrate my concern: if $W\bx = [5;-5]$, then $\hat{\bh} = [1;-1] = \sgn(W\bx)$, and $\hat{\bh}^T W\bx = 10$. If we choose $\bg = [1;1]$, then $\bg^T W\bx = 0$. Therefore, the right-hand side of Eq. (7) becomes $\bg^T W\bx - \hat{\bh}^T W\bx + \ell(\dots) = -10 + \ell(\dots)$, which could be smaller than $\ell(\dots)$, and thus not a valid upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not address this case and is therefore incomplete.
- Why are some leaves not assigned any data points? This seems unusual and may indicate a problem with the objective function or the initialization procedure. The suggested heuristic of a fixed assignment of data points to tree leaves appears sub-optimal, but could potentially be motivated through an expectation maximization or concave-convex procedure, similar to other clustering techniques.
- I am curious about the necessity of the efficient loss-augmented inference design. Given that the tree depth is at most 16, a greedy search over the $2^{16} = 65536$ possible splits still appears feasible. How much did the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball impact performance? While one result is provided in the supplementary material, it is unclear whether this generalizes. Furthermore, the definition of ‘leaves’ in this context is unclear.
- The paper lacks a baseline that trains non-axis-aligned split functions in a standard, greedy manner (i.e., without any initialization). Including such a baseline would provide a more complete comparison.
- The depth of the tree must be specified *a priori*. This limitation should be discussed more thoroughly, and the authors should provide a more careful experimental evaluation regarding the choice of the depth parameter.

Thanks to the authors for carefully clarifying the reasoning behind the loss bound (Eq. 7). I suggest replacing $\arg\max$ with $\max$. As stated in my summary, I have adjusted my score accordingly.

An experiment comparing runtime could strengthen the paper. A more thorough evaluation of the method would be beneficial for the reader. Overall, the submission considers an interesting problem – the joint training of all parameters of decision trees. The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and a potential flaw exists in the derivation. I am happy to adjust my score further based on a comprehensive rebuttal.
