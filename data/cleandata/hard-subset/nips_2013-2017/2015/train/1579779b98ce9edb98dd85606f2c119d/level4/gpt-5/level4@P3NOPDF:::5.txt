Summary
The paper proposes a globally optimized decision tree with linear split functions, learned by minimizing an upper bound on the empirical loss. The approach connects tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables, yielding a smooth surrogate objective that combines an empirical-loss upper bound with L2 regularization on the split weights. The authors optimize this objective using (stochastic) gradient descent and introduce a “stable” SGD variant.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant formulation that bridges decision tree learning and latent structured prediction.
- Clear objective design with a principled upper bound and L2 regularization to stabilize optimization.
- Potentially broad applicability to settings where greedy split selection is suboptimal.

Weaknesses and empirical concerns
- The experimental evidence does not convincingly support the superiority of global optimization over standard greedy tree induction. The globally optimized trees significantly outperform the greedy baseline on only a few datasets, and often by modest margins.
- The proposed method appears substantially more computationally intensive than greedy training, but the paper does not quantify the trade-off (e.g., wall-clock time, number of epochs, convergence behavior).
- The presentation leaves several methodological details unclear, which makes it hard to assess reproducibility and to interpret some results.

Requests for clarification and suggestions
- Stable SGD: Please provide a precise description (ideally with pseudocode) of the “stable” SGD procedure. How are examples assigned to leaves during training? Are assignments updated after each step, mini-batch, or epoch? How does this differ from the standard SGD you consider?
- Figure 2: Clarify whether the results are from standard SGD or stable SGD. Define “active leaf” precisely (e.g., a leaf currently receiving at least one training sample under the current parameters, or a leaf with nonzero probability mass). Explain how “activity” is measured during training.
- Algorithm 1, line 10: After updating Θ, you project it onto a simplex. Please describe the projection routine used in practice (e.g., the standard Euclidean projection onto the probability simplex) and provide a citation. It would help to state explicitly that the projection enforces each row of Θ to be nonnegative and sum to one.
- Computational costs: Report wall-clock times or number of gradient updates for your method versus the greedy baseline, and specify hardware. If possible, include convergence plots of the objective and/or validation performance.
- Sensitivity analyses: Provide results varying tree depth, number of leaves, and regularization strength to show robustness. Also clarify the stopping criterion and learning-rate schedule.
- Baselines: Ensure a strong greedy baseline (with linear splits, if applicable) tuned comparably. If your baseline is axis-aligned only, discuss whether performance differences might be due to split function class rather than optimization strategy.

Minor comments and typos
- Lines 407–408: “number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree” is confusing. Standard CART-style trees typically consider all candidate features (or a specified subset in random forests). Please clarify what was tuned.
- Equations (7), (8), and (9): Replace “argmax” with “max” if you are referring to the value of the objective rather than the maximizer.
- Figure 1: It is never referenced in the main text. Please cite and explain its role.
- Line 141: “...to the index of the leaf on by this path.” The end of this sentence appears malformed—perhaps “...to the index of the leaf reached by this path.”
- Line 238: “the solution to loss-augmented inference...” should be “the solutions to...” if multiple solutions are possible.
- Line 301: “An key observation...” should be “A key observation...”
- Line 360: Define “FSGD” on first use (e.g., fast/stable stochastic gradient descent?).
- Line 404: “...a tree with minimum training errors.” If you mean model selection on a validation set, “minimum validation (or tuning) errors” would be clearer.
- Supplementary, line 29: “max{g ∈ sgn...}” should be “max{g = sgn...}”
- Supplementary, line 30: Replace “sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(Θ^T f(g), y)” with “sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)), y)”.

Overall assessment
The paper’s formulation is appealing and technically sound, and the connection to latent structured prediction for tree learning is a valuable contribution. However, the empirical results, as currently presented, do not convincingly demonstrate that global optimization yields consistent and practical gains over strong greedy baselines, especially given the added computational cost. Clarifying the training procedures (particularly stable SGD), tightening the exposition, and strengthening the empirical evaluation (including runtime, sensitivity, and stronger baselines) would substantially improve the paper. At present, I am not fully convinced by the empirical evidence, but with the requested clarifications and additional experiments, the contribution could be impactful.
