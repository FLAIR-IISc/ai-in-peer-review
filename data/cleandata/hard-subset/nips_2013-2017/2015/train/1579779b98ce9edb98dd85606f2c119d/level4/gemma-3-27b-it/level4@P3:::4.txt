The submission proposes an algorithm for global optimization of decision trees based on a reformulation of the problem as (latent) structured prediction. The latent structured variables are sign vectors encoding the decisions made at each internal node in the tree. A non-differentiable and non-convex loss is proposed, which is then approximated by a differentiable (but still non-convex) upper bound. Minimizing this loss via SGD entails solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are given. Experiments are shown comparing this method to a standard greedy training method. The key contributions of the work are casting the global DT optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms to optimize the objective.

The way this is done is novel and interesting, and raises interesting questions. However, I have some concerns about practical considerations—especially concerning the motivation for the method and how significant the performance gains over naive methods are. Considered purely as a method to globally optimize DTs, the method is intriguing. The advantage gained by casting the optimization as structured prediction is being able to perform SGD by solving a sequence of loss-augmented optimization problems that leverage the problem’s structure.

However, I believe the most interesting result is that the gradient updates are sparse—each gradient step involves changing only one node’s weight vector. The submission presents this as a computational advantage, but I believe this property raises more intriguing possibilities that are not fully acknowledged. One could imagine optimizing over an infinitely deep tree by initially setting all weights to zero and gradually growing the tree by making more weights nonzero, letting the model’s complexity grow naturally to fit the data. This raises questions: would such a method prove equivalent to, or a variation of, any known greedy strategy? Perhaps it's preferable to introduce a new node rather than revise an old node's weights. Or, with appropriate regularization, would it converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes (as Fig. 2 might suggest)? This issue cuts to the core of what makes the proposed method worthwhile compared to naive DTs and deserves further analysis.

For instance, a critical issue left ambiguous is: what happens if a node’s weight is zero? The discussion on page 3 implies taking the right branch, which seems dubious. It seems preferable to output a decision at this node, with the possibility of later making the weight nonzero, corresponding to splitting the node. Precisely defining this is important, as it directly relates to pruning strategies for greedy training.

I have a significant practical concern regarding the work’s motivation. Plain DTs are useful mainly to the extent that they are interpretable; if interpretability is dropped, other methods generally offer better performance. As stated, I expect the method to produce non-interpretable results, since the L2 regularization would produce dense weight vectors. Although L1 regularization might help, performance could drop if we were forced to regularize to the point where exactly 1-sparse splits were produced. This would definitely need to be tested.

My other main concern is that the experiments lack detail regarding baselines. No details are given as to which pruning strategies were employed for the naive approach. Since a range of regularization parameters were tried for the proposed method, I would expect the naive method to be similarly tried with different pruning strategies and/or parameters. It would also be fair to try other trivial strategies for global optimization, such as training a greedy method with random subsets of the training data and choosing the one that minimizes the desired loss on the full training or validation set.

Regarding clarity, the paper is reasonably well-written. The parts describing inference could be clearer. Section 6.2, for example, could be summarized by saying that the objective is first maximized for all *g* corresponding to a given leaf, then maximized over all leaves. Section 6.4 could also be more explicit.

In summary, this is a clever approach that raises interesting questions. However, it is unclear whether it is a practically useful method at this point, due to the aforementioned interpretability issue (why use plain DTs if they are not interpretable?) and a lack of detail in the experiments. The submission also doesn’t address the case of decision forests.

**Overall Assessment:**

**Quality:** 3 (Sound, but some technical aspects could be better fleshed out)
**Clarity:** 3 (Generally clear, but some sections could benefit from simplification)
**Originality:** 4 (Novel approach to a challenging problem)
**Significance:** 3 (Potentially significant, but the practical benefits need to be more convincingly demonstrated)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel reformulation of decision tree optimization as structured prediction.
*   Development of efficient algorithms for optimizing the proposed objective.
*   Empirical results show improvements over greedy baselines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Motivation for the method is not entirely convincing, particularly concerning interpretability.
*   Experiments lack sufficient detail regarding baseline methods and hyperparameter tuning.
*   The behavior of the method with zero-weighted nodes is not fully explored.
*   The potential for growing trees from zero-weighted initializations is an interesting avenue not fully explored.
*   Lack of discussion regarding extension to decision forests.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. While the paper presents a clever and technically sound approach, the concerns regarding motivation, experimental details, and unexplored potential limit its impact. Addressing these concerns in a revised version could significantly strengthen the submission.
