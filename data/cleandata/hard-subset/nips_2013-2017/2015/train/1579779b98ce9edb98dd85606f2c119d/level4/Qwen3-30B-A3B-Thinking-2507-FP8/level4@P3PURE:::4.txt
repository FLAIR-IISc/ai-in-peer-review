

The submission proposes a novel algorithm for global optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as latent structured prediction, where latent variables are sign vectors encoding node decisions. A non-differentiable, non-convex loss is approximated via a differentiable (yet non-convex) upper bound. Minimization via SGD requires solving sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided. Experiments compare the method against standard greedy training. The key contributions—casting global tree optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient optimization algorithms—are novel and raise intriguing theoretical questions.  

The work’s most compelling aspect is the sparsity of gradient updates: each step modifies only one node’s weight vector, enabling potential tree growth from a sparse initial state (all weights zero). This suggests a natural mechanism for complexity adaptation—gradually introducing nodes as weights become nonzero—though the paper insufficiently explores implications. Crucially, the method’s value hinges on whether this process aligns with or improves upon known greedy strategies (e.g., whether introducing new nodes always dominates revising existing weights) or converges to bounded tree sizes (as Fig. 2 implies). A critical ambiguity remains regarding zero-weight nodes: the paper assumes the right branch is taken (page 3), but this is problematic. A more principled approach would treat zero weights as pending splits (to be activated later), directly linking to pruning strategies. This issue warrants deeper analysis, as it fundamentally affects the method’s practical viability.  

A significant practical concern is the method’s interpretability. Decision trees are primarily valued for interpretability; if this requirement is relaxed, standard methods (e.g., ensembles) typically outperform trees. The proposed method uses L2 regularization, yielding dense weight vectors and compromising interpretability. While L1 regularization could enforce sparsity, it risks performance degradation if forced to produce exactly 1-sparse splits—a trade-off untested in the paper. The authors must address whether the method remains useful when interpretability is prioritized.  

Experimental evaluation lacks detail. The paper fails to specify pruning strategies for the greedy baseline, despite testing multiple regularization parameters for the proposed method. It would be fair to compare against: (1) the baseline with varied pruning, (2) trivial global baselines (e.g., training greedy trees on random data subsets and selecting the best-performing model on validation data).  

Clarity-wise, the paper is generally well-written, though Sections 6.2 and 6.4 could be more explicit. Section 6.2 should clarify that the objective is maximized over all leaves *after* maximizing over splits per leaf.  

In summary, the approach is clever and theoretically interesting, but its practical utility remains unclear due to interpretability concerns and insufficient experimental rigor. The zero-weight node issue and the potential of zero-initialization for natural tree growth deserve deeper exploration.  

PS: The title mentions "forests," yet the paper addresses only single trees.  

POST-REBUTTAL: The rebuttal notes random initialization avoids zero-weight issues in practice, but the method’s most compelling theoretical promise—starting from zero weights and growing the tree—remains unexplored. The authors should pursue this direction to unlock the method’s full potential. The work proposes a clever reformulation and efficient algorithm, but its practical motivation and experimental validation require strengthening.
