

The paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy optimization of decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, introducing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized using stochastic gradient descent. This approach enables joint optimization of split functions across all tree levels under a global objective, addressing a key limitation of traditional greedy tree induction methods.

The work builds upon previous efforts in decision tree optimization, such as Bennett's non-greedy multi-linear programming approach [1] and the CO2 method [18], but overcomes their limitations by enabling efficient learning of deep trees with O(d²p) complexity. Unlike soft-split approaches [13] that sacrifice computational efficiency, this method maintains the tree structure's O(dp) inference complexity while providing a principled way to regularize the optimization process.

The paper's main strengths include:
1. A theoretically grounded formulation that links decision trees to structured prediction with latent variables, which is a significant contribution to the literature.
2. An efficient optimization algorithm with practical computational complexity (O(d²p)) that enables training on large datasets.
3. Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple classification benchmarks, demonstrating consistent improvements over greedy baselines in terms of test accuracy and generalization.
4. Clear theoretical analysis of the upper bound and its relationship to the empirical loss, including a useful proposition on the effect of scaling W.

The main weaknesses are:
1. The improvements over greedy baselines are modest in absolute terms (typically 1-2% on most benchmarks), which may limit the paper's practical impact.
2. The method primarily addresses oblique decision trees, while many real-world applications use axis-aligned trees, potentially limiting immediate applicability.
3. The connection to structured prediction, while technically sound, may require some familiarity with that field for full appreciation.

Regarding the NIPS evaluation criteria:
Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and thorough experimental validation. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations, and the method is well-motivated. The experimental results support the claims, though the magnitude of improvements is modest.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized with clear exposition of the key ideas. The connection to structured prediction is well-explained, though some technical details may require careful reading. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are presented in a comprehensible manner.

Originality: The connection between decision trees and structured prediction with latent variables is novel. While there have been previous attempts at non-greedy tree optimization [1, 18], the authors' approach offers a fundamentally different perspective that leads to efficient optimization. The paper also properly references relevant prior work and distinguishes its contribution clearly.

Significance: The work addresses a well-known limitation in decision tree learning (greedy induction), but the improvements are relatively modest. The method could be valuable for applications where decision trees are used as building blocks (e.g., in ensembles), but the direct impact on state-of-the-art performance may be limited. The framework is general and could inspire future work on more efficient tree learning methods.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction
- Efficient optimization algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees
- Demonstrated improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets
- Clear, well-written paper with solid technical content
- Theoretical insights that could be extended to other structured prediction problems

Arguments against acceptance:
- Modest absolute improvements over existing methods
- Limited applicability to axis-aligned trees (common in practice)
- The connection to structured prediction, while interesting, may not be immediately compelling to the broader ML community
- The paper doesn't provide a strong argument for why the small improvements are significant enough to warrant acceptance over other more impactful contributions

Overall, this is a technically strong paper that makes a solid contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The theoretical framework is elegant and the optimization approach is efficient, making it a noteworthy contribution. While the improvements over greedy methods are modest, the paper's theoretical insights and framework are valuable to the community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better emphasize the significance of the work and clarify the relationship to prior art. The paper is well-written and makes a clear case for its contribution, demonstrating solid technical merit that aligns well with NIPS standards.
