**Summary**

This paper introduces a method for simultaneously optimizing all parameters of a decision tree, including both the decision splits and the leaf node posteriors. This is a significant research direction, as conventional methods rely on a sub-optimal, greedy, layer-wise training procedure. The authors formulate the task as a single, unified optimization problem, which is then made tractable by deriving and approximating an upper bound.

**Quality**

The manuscript is well-written, but there are potential issues in the theoretical justification of the proposed bound. These concerns are detailed in the comments below.

**Clarity**

While the mathematical derivations are presented clearly, the experimental section and the presentation of its results could be significantly improved to offer greater insight.

**Originality**

The investigation into a joint training paradigm for decision trees is a novel and important contribution to the field.

**Significance**

The proposed solution is a valuable step forward, though its impact is potentially constrained by several shortcomings that require further attention.

**Comments for Authors**

*   There appears to be a potential flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound. The argument on lines 216f is not convincing. Consider the following counterexample: Let $W\bx = [5; -5]$, which gives $\hat{\bh} = \sgn(W\bx) = [1; -1]$ and thus $\hat{\bh}^T W\bx = 10$. If we select an alternative vector $\bg = [1; 1]$, where $\bg \neq \hat{\bh}$, the right-hand side of Eq. (7) becomes $\bg^T W\bx - \hat{\bh}^T W\bx + \ell(...) = 0 - 10 + \ell(...) = -10 + \ell(...)$. This value could be smaller than $\ell(...)$, which would violate the condition for it to be a valid upper bound. The proof of Proposition 1 provided in the supplementary material does not seem to cover this case and is therefore incomplete. (A minor suggestion: consider replacing `\arg\max` with `\max` in the relevant equations for clarity).

*   The observation that some leaf nodes are not assigned any data points is puzzling. This behavior is unusual and may suggest an issue with the objective function or the initialization strategy. The proposed heuristic of a fixed data-to-leaf assignment seems sub-optimal. Could this approach be more rigorously motivated, perhaps by drawing parallels to Expectation-Maximization or concave-convex procedures used in related clustering algorithms?

*   The motivation for developing an efficient loss-augmented inference procedure is not fully established. Given that the maximum tree depth is 16, an exhaustive search over all $2^{16} = 65,536$ leaf assignments appears computationally feasible. How significantly did the Hamming ball restriction on the search space affect performance? The single result in the supplement is insufficient to assess the generalizability of this design choice. Furthermore, the term 'leaves' as used in the supplementary material requires clarification. An experiment comparing runtimes would help strengthen this claim.

*   The experimental evaluation is missing a crucial baseline: a standard greedy training method for trees with non-axis aligned (oblique) split functions, trained from scratch without any special initialization.

*   The requirement to specify the tree depth a priori is a notable limitation of the method. This constraint should be discussed more thoroughly. A more careful experimental evaluation, such as a sensitivity analysis with respect to the depth parameter, would be highly beneficial for the reader.

Overall, this submission addresses the interesting and important problem of jointly training decision tree parameters. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and there is a potential flaw in a core theoretical derivation. I am open to adjusting my score upon receiving a careful explanation and clarification in the author rebuttal.
