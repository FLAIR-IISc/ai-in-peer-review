The submission proposes a clever reformulation of global optimization of decision trees as structured prediction, along with an efficient algorithm to solve the optimization. The key contributions of the work are casting the global DT optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms to optimize the objective. The way this is done is novel and interesting, and raises interesting questions.

The submission presents a non-greedy method for learning decision trees, using stochastic gradient descent to optimize an upper bound on the empirical loss of the tree's predictions on the training set. This approach has several advantages, including the ability to perform SGD by solving a sequence of loss-augmented optimization problems that leverage the special structure of the problem. The gradient updates are sparse, which raises intriguing possibilities, such as optimizing over an infinitely deep tree by initially setting all weights to zero and then gradually growing the tree by making more weights nonzero.

However, there are some concerns regarding the motivation for the method and the significance of the performance gains over naive methods. Decision trees are mainly useful to the extent that they are interpretable, and if this requirement is dropped, other methods generally offer better performance. The proposed method may produce non-interpretable results, and it is unclear whether switching to L1 regularization would help.

The experiments are also lacking in detail, particularly regarding other baselines. No details are given as to which pruning strategies were employed for the naive approach, and it would be fair to try some other trivial strategies for global optimization. The paper is reasonably well-written, but some parts could be clearer, such as the sections describing inference.

Overall, this is a very clever approach that raises some very interesting questions. However, it is unclear whether this is a practically useful method at this point, both due to the aforementioned interpretability issue and due to a lack of details in the experiments.
