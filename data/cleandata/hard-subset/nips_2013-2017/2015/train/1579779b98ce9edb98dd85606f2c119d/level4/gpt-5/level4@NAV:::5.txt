This paper proposes an algorithm for learning a decision tree whose internal nodes use linear splitting functions. The method jointly optimizes the tree parameters (i.e., the split-function weights and the output distribution at each leaf) in a global manner, rather than via the standard greedy training of trees. The authors introduce an upper bound on the empirical loss of the tree and, to obtain a smoother optimization landscape, regularize this bound with an L2 penalty on the split-function weights. The algorithm then applies stochastic gradient descent to find the tree parameters that minimize this upper bound.

The optimization of the tree is elegantly formulated by drawing a connection to structured prediction with latent variables. Nonetheless, the empirical evidence does not convincingly demonstrate that global optimization is preferable to greedy tree learning. The globally optimized tree significantly outperforms the greedy baseline on only a few datasets, while incurring higher computational cost.

The paper is well organized, but several points require clarification:
- The “stable” variant of SGD is not described clearly. How are data points routed to leaves? Providing pseudocode would likely help.
- Figure 2: Are these results obtained using SGD or stable SGD? What precisely constitutes an “active” leaf?
- In line 10 of Algorithm 1, Theta is updated and then projected onto a simplex. How is this projection solved in practice? It may also be worth noting that this projection enforces that each row of Theta sums to one.
