Summary: This paper proposes a method for jointly training all parameters of decision trees, including decision splits and posterior probabilities. This is a significant contribution, as current methods typically train trees in a greedy, layer-by-layer manner. The authors frame the task as a single optimization problem, which they then upper-bound and approximate to obtain a tractable formulation.

Quality: While the paper is well-written, there may be flaws in the reasoning, as discussed below. Clarity: The derivation is clean, but the experiments and their presentation could be improved. Originality: Investigating joint training for decision trees is an important and timely topic. Significance: The proposed solution is valuable, although there appear to be some shortcomings, which are discussed below.

Comments:

1. Flaw in the derivation of the empirical loss bound: The argument in lines 216f is confusing. Specifically, the statement "... for all values of $\bg \neq \sgn(W\bx)$, the right-hand side can only become larger than when $\bg = \sgn(W\bx)$ ..." is problematic. Consider the following example: $W\bx = [5;-5]$, which yields $\hat \bh = [1;-1] = \sgn(W\bx)$. Then, $\hat \bh^T W\bx = 10$. However, choosing $\bg = [1;1]$ results in $\bg^T W\bx = 0$, making the right-hand side of Eq. (7) $-10 + \ell(...)$. This could potentially be smaller than $\ell(...)$, rendering the upper bound invalid. Furthermore, the proof of Prop. 1 in the supplementary material does not cover this case, making it incomplete.

2. Unassigned data points in leaves: It is unclear why some leaves are not assigned any data points. This seems unusual and may indicate a problem with the objective or initialization. The suggested heuristic of a fixed assignment of data points to tree leaves appears suboptimal but could potentially be motivated via expectation-maximization or concave-convex procedures, similar to other clustering techniques.

3. Importance of efficient loss-augmented inference: The depth of the tree is at most 16, and greedily checking $2^{16} = 65536$ values still seems feasible. The impact of the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball on performance is unclear. While one result is provided in the supplementary material, it is uncertain whether this generalizes. Additionally, the term "leaves" is not clearly defined.

4. Missing baseline: A baseline that trains non-axis aligned split functions of trees in a standard greedy manner (i.e., without any initialization) is lacking.

5. Tree depth limitation: The depth of the tree must be specified ahead of time, which is a significant limitation. The authors should discuss this limitation more thoroughly and provide a more careful experimental evaluation regarding the depth parameter.

The authors are thanked for carefully explaining the reasoning behind the loss bound (Eq. 7). Notably, "\arg\max" could be replaced with "\max". As stated in the summary, I adjusted my score accordingly. An experiment comparing runtime could strengthen the paper. A more careful evaluation of the method would be desirable for the reader. The submission considers an interesting problem of jointly training all parameters of decision trees. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and there could potentially be a flaw in the derivation. I am happy to adjust my score given a careful explanation in the rebuttal.
