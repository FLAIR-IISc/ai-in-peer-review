This paper proposes an algorithm for global optimization of decision trees by reformulating training as a (latent) structured prediction problem. The latent structured variables are sign vectors encoding the decisions at each internal node. The authors introduce a non-differentiable, non-convex loss and then replace it with a differentiable (but still non-convex) upper bound. Minimization via SGD reduces to a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are provided. Experiments compare the approach to a standard greedy training method. The main contributions are (i) casting global decision-tree optimization as structured prediction and (ii) devising efficient optimization procedures for the resulting objective. The approach is novel and interesting, and it raises several important questions.

Strengths and conceptual observations
- The structured-prediction view enables SGD updates by repeatedly solving loss-augmented inference problems that exploit the problem’s structure.
- If I understand correctly, a particularly interesting consequence is that the gradient updates are sparse: each step modifies only one node’s weight vector. While the paper frames this primarily as a computational benefit, I believe it opens up deeper modeling possibilities that are not discussed.

Potential implications and open questions
- One could imagine optimizing an infinitely deep tree initialized with all weights set to zero, and then gradually “growing” the tree by activating weights as needed so that model complexity increases with the data. This raises several questions:
  - Would such a procedure reduce to, or closely resemble, known greedy strategies? For example, might it always prefer to introduce a new node rather than revise an existing node’s weights?
  - With suitable regularization, would the procedure converge to a compact tree with a limited number of nodes (as Fig. 2 appears to suggest)?
- I believe this line of analysis goes to the heart of why the proposed method might be preferable to naive decision trees and warrants more careful treatment than is currently provided.

Ambiguity around zero-weight nodes
- A critical practical issue is what happens when a node’s weight vector is exactly zero. The discussion on page 3 seems to imply taking the right branch, which is an odd choice. It seems more natural to output a decision at that node; later, one could turn the weight nonzero to effect a split. Precisely specifying this behavior matters, both conceptually and because it closely parallels pruning strategies in greedy training.

Motivation and interpretability
- As a practical matter, plain decision trees are often chosen for interpretability. If interpretability is not preserved, other models typically offer better accuracy. As presented, L2 regularization will produce dense node weights, making splits non-interpretable. L1 regularization could help, but performance might degrade if we must enforce exactly 1-sparse splits to retain interpretability. This trade-off needs to be tested empirically.

Experimental evaluation
- The experimental section lacks sufficient detail about the baseline(s). Specifically:
  - The paper does not specify which pruning strategies were used for the naive approach. Given that the proposed method is tuned over a range of regularization parameters, the greedy baseline should be evaluated with multiple pruning strategies and parameter settings as well.
  - It would also be fair to include simple alternative “global” strategies, such as training several greedy trees on random subsets of the data and selecting the one minimizing the desired loss on the full training set or on a validation set.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written, but the inference sections could be clearer.
  - Section 6.2 can be succinctly described as: first maximize the objective over all g for a fixed leaf, then maximize the resulting value over leaves.
  - Section 6.4 could be made more explicit about the steps and assumptions involved.

Summary
- This is a clever and thought-provoking reformulation of global decision-tree optimization, with efficient algorithms and promising computational properties. However, its practical utility is currently unclear due to:
  - The interpretability concern (if interpretability is not preserved, why use decision trees rather than higher-performing alternatives?).
  - Insufficient experimental details and limited baselines.

PS: Although the title mentions forests, the paper does not actually address this case.

POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS
- I remain convinced that the zero-weight case is critical and merits deeper analysis, for the reasons outlined above. From the rebuttal, it appears weights may be initialized randomly, which sidesteps the issue in practice. Nonetheless, the more interesting regime, in my view, is the one where weights start at zero and are gradually increased, enabling principled, data-driven tree growth. I encourage the authors to explore this direction.

Overall, the submission presents a neat reformulation and an efficient algorithm, but some aspects of the motivation and the experimental evaluation are not yet fully convincing.
