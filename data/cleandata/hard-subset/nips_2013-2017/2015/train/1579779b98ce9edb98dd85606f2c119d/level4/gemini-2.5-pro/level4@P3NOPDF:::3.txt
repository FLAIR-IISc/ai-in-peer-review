This paper proposes a novel approach to decision tree induction by formulating the search for an optimal tree of a fixed depth as a structured prediction problem. This reframes the classic machine learning task, moving away from the standard greedy, top-down construction algorithms (e.g., CART, C4.5) towards a more globally-aware learning objective. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method can achieve higher test set accuracy than greedy baselines, although the improvements observed are modest.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** The primary strength of this work is its conceptual novelty. The idea of treating the entire decision tree as a structured output and learning its parameters within a global objective function is elegant and well-motivated. It directly addresses the well-known limitation of greedy approaches, which can get stuck in locally optimal solutions.

2.  **Principled Approach:** By grounding the problem in the established framework of structured prediction, the authors provide a principled mathematical basis for decision tree learning. This connection opens up the possibility of leveraging powerful optimization techniques and theoretical insights from the structured prediction literature.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1.  **Marginal Empirical Gains:** While the proposed method consistently outperforms greedy tree induction, the reported improvements in test accuracy are often small. This raises a critical question about the practical significance of the approach. Given that the proposed method is likely more computationally intensive than its greedy counterparts, a more substantial performance gain would be needed to justify its adoption in practice.

2.  **Lack of Computational Analysis:** A significant omission is the absence of a thorough analysis of the computational cost and scalability. Finding an optimal decision tree is an NP-hard problem, and any method that departs from a simple greedy search must carefully characterize its computational complexity. The review would be significantly strengthened by:
    *   Theoretical analysis of the method's complexity with respect to the number of data points, features, and desired tree depth.
    *   Empirical runtime comparisons against the greedy baselines on the reported datasets. Without this information, it is impossible to evaluate the trade-off between accuracy and training time.

3.  **Limited Baselines:** The experimental comparison is limited to standard greedy decision tree implementations. While this is a necessary baseline, the paper would be more impactful if it compared against other non-greedy or optimal tree induction algorithms from the literature (e.g., methods based on dynamic programming, SAT solvers, or other search techniques). Such a comparison would help to better position the paper's contribution and demonstrate its advantages over other state-of-the-art methods that also aim to overcome the limitations of greedy search.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the trade-off between the marginal accuracy gains and the increased computational complexity? Runtime comparisons would be highly informative. How does the training time scale as the tree depth increases?

2.  What is your hypothesis for why the performance gains are relatively small across the tested benchmarks? Does this suggest that the trees found by greedy methods are already near-optimal for these specific tasks, or is there a limitation in the proposed formulation that prevents it from finding significantly better solutions?

3.  Have you considered initializing your search or algorithm with a tree learned via a greedy method? It would be interesting to see if your method is primarily a fine-tuning approach or if it discovers fundamentally different tree structures.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a conceptually interesting and well-formulated idea for improving upon a classic algorithm. The connection to structured prediction is a valuable contribution that could inspire future research. However, the current manuscript is weakened by the modest empirical results and a lack of critical analysis regarding computational cost and scalability. While the novelty is appealing, the practical utility of the method is not yet clearly established. The paper is borderline, but I lean towards acceptance with the expectation that the authors will address the concerns about computational complexity and provide a more thorough discussion of the results in the final version.
