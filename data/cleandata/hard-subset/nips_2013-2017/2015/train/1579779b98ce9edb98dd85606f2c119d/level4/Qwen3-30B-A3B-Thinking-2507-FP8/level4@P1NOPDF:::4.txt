

The submission proposes a novel algorithm for globally optimizing decision trees by reformulating the problem as (latent) structured prediction, where latent variables are sign vectors encoding node decisions. A non-differentiable, non-convex loss is approximated via a differentiable (though still non-convex) upper bound. Minimization via SGD requires solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which the authors provide efficient algorithms. Experiments compare the method to standard greedy training.

The key contributions—casting global decision tree optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms for the objective—are novel and intriguing, raising compelling theoretical questions. The approach’s most compelling aspect is its inherent sparsity: each SGD update modifies only a single node’s weight vector, not the entire tree. While the paper frames this as a computational advantage, this property opens broader possibilities unexplored in the work. For instance, initializing all weights to zero could enable natural growth of tree complexity: nodes are progressively activated (weights become non-zero) as needed, potentially yielding a tree that adapts its structure to the data. This raises critical questions: Does this process align with known greedy strategies (e.g., is it always optimal to add a new node rather than revise an existing one)? Could regularization constrain the final tree size (as suggested by Fig. 2)? This issue cuts to the heart of the method’s value versus naive approaches and warrants deeper analysis than currently provided.

A specific ambiguity concerns nodes with zero weights. The discussion on page 3 implies taking the right branch when weights are zero—a questionable choice. It would be preferable to treat such nodes as unsplit (outputting a decision), with subsequent updates potentially activating them via non-zero weights. Precisely defining this behavior is essential, as it directly parallels pruning strategies in greedy training. The current treatment lacks clarity on this point.

My primary practical concern lies in the method’s motivation. Decision trees are valued primarily for interpretability; if this requirement is relaxed, other methods typically offer superior performance. The proposed approach likely produces non-interpretable results due to L2 regularization inducing dense weight vectors. Switching to L1 regularization to enforce sparse splits might degrade performance, as the paper does not test this trade-off. This critical limitation must be addressed through empirical validation.

Additionally, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail for fair comparison. The baseline (greedy training) does not specify pruning strategies, despite the proposed method testing multiple regularization parameters. The baseline should be evaluated with comparable pruning approaches or parameters. It would also be reasonable to compare against trivial global optimization baselines, such as training multiple greedy trees on random data subsets and selecting the best-performing one based on full-set loss.

The paper is generally well-written, but sections on inference (e.g., Section 6.2) could be clearer. For instance, Section 6.2 could succinctly state: "The objective is first maximized over all splits *g* for a fixed leaf, then maximized across all leaves." Section 6.4 also requires more explicit explanation.

In summary, the method presents a clever reformulation and efficient algorithm, raising interesting theoretical questions about tree growth and sparsity. However, its practical utility remains unclear due to unresolved interpretability concerns and insufficient experimental rigor. The core motivation—why use decision trees if interpretability is sacrificed?—is not convincingly addressed, and the experimental comparisons lack necessary detail to assess performance gains.

PS: The title mentions "forests," but the paper does not address this case.

POST-REBUTTAL: I maintain that the zero-weight case requires deeper analysis. The rebuttal suggests weights are initialized randomly, avoiding this issue in practice. However, the method’s most compelling potential lies in starting from zero weights and growing the tree—this direction merits exploration. The authors should consider it. While the reformulation is clever, the current work lacks sufficient justification for its practical value.
