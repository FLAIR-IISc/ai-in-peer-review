- Reformulates global decision-tree learning as a latent-variable structured prediction problem, encoding input paths as binary vectors.  
- Derives a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss as a differentiable surrogate and optimizes it with SGD.  
- Introduces an efficient "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm that makes training practical for deep trees.  
- Provides a principled, non-greedy alternative to greedy node-by-node methods like CART.  
- Original and elegant connection between decision trees and structured prediction.  
- Strong technical derivation and a significant algorithmic contribution reducing gradient-update complexity.  
- Framework suggests sparse SGD updates and opens avenues for novel tree-growing strategies.  
- Practical motivation is unclear because oblique, L2-regularized splits reduce interpretability and no clear performance advantage over strong non-interpretable baselines is shown.  
- Experiments lack rigorous comparisons and hyperparameter tuning for baselines (e.g., pruning parameters) and omit comparisons to simple global heuristics.  
- Method is ambiguous when a split weight vector is zero (sgn undefined); needs principled handling (e.g., treat zero node as leaf) and analysis of whether SGD can grow a tree from zeros.  
- Inference and Stable SGD descriptions lack clarity; loss-augmented inference and leaf-update schedules should be made more intuitive and explicit.  
- Minor mismatch: title references "forests" but experiments and methods focus on single trees.  
- Overall: technically interesting and original, but practical utility limited until interpretability/performance trade-offs, zero-weight behavior, and experimental rigor are addressed.
