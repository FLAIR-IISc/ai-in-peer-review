This paper presents an algorithm for learning a decision tree with linear split functions at the nodes, optimizing the tree parameters in a global way. The algorithm is based on an upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which is regularized via a L2 regularization of the split function weights. The tree optimization problem is elegantly formulated by establishing a link with structured prediction with latent variables.

The paper is well-structured, and the authors provide a clear and concise description of the proposed algorithm. However, the empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate that global optimization is a better alternative to greedy learning of the tree. The globally optimized tree outperforms the greedy tree on a couple of datasets, but this comes at the cost of increased computational intensity.

The main contributions of the paper are the formulation of the tree optimization problem and the proposal of an efficient algorithm for optimizing the upper bound. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm and demonstrate its scalability.

However, some points need to be clarified. The stable version of SGD is not described very clearly, and it would be helpful to have a pseudo-code. Additionally, some minor comments and typos were found throughout the paper.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is clearly written, and the organization is logical. However, the significance of the results is not entirely clear, and the authors could have done a better job of highlighting the impact of their work.

The originality of the paper is high, as it proposes a novel algorithm for learning decision trees. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm and demonstrate its scalability. However, the results could be more convincing, and the authors could have done a better job of comparing their method to other state-of-the-art approaches.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the organization is logical. However, some points need to be clarified, and the authors could have done a better job of explaining the stable version of SGD.

Overall, the paper is a good contribution to the field, but it could be improved by providing more convincing empirical results and clarifying some of the points mentioned above.
