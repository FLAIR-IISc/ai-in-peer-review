This paper proposes an algorithm for learning decision trees with linear split functions trained via global optimization rather than the standard greedy procedure. The method jointly optimizes the split weights and the leaf output distributions by minimizing an upper bound on the empirical loss. To smooth the objective, the bound is regularized with an L2 penalty on the split weights, and the parameters are learned with stochastic gradient descent (SGD).

The optimization is elegantly framed through a connection to structured prediction with latent variables. However, the empirical evidence does not clearly demonstrate that global optimization is preferable to greedy tree learning. The globally optimized trees significantly outperform greedy baselines on only a few datasets, while requiring substantially more computation.

Points that require clarification:
- Stable SGD: The “stable” version of SGD is not described clearly. How are examples assigned to leaves during training? A concise pseudocode description would help.
- Figure 2: Were these results obtained with standard SGD or the stable variant? Please define precisely what constitutes an “active” leaf.
- Algorithm 1, line 10: After updating Θ, you project it onto a simplex. How is this projection implemented in practice? It would also help to note explicitly that this projection enforces that each row of Θ sums to one.

Minor comments and typos:
- Lines 407–408: You state that you tune “the number of features evaluated when building an ordinary axis-aligned decision tree.” In standard trees, all features are considered at each split; please clarify what is tuned here (e.g., feature subsampling as in random forests?).
- Equations (7), (8), and (9): Replace “argmax” with “max.”
- Figure 1 is never referenced in the main text.
- Line 141: “...to the index of the leaf on by this path.” The ending appears incorrect; please revise.
- Line 238: “...the solution to loss-augmented inference...” should be “the solutions to...”
- Line 301: “An key observation...” should be “A key observation...”
- Line 360: What does FSGD stand for? Please define.
- Line 404: “...a tree with minimum training errors.” I assume you mean “tuning errors” or “validation errors.”

Supplementary material:
- Line 29: max{g ∈ sgn...} should be max{g = sgn...}
- Line 30: sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(Θ^T f(g), y) should be sgn(Wx)^T Wx + l(Θ^T f(sgn(Wx)), y)

Overall assessment:
The formulation is clean and the connection to structured prediction with latent variables is insightful. Nonetheless, the experimental results, as presented, are not compelling enough to justify the additional computational cost of global optimization over strong greedy baselines. Strengthening the empirical section—by expanding datasets, providing more rigorous comparisons (including runtime/accuracy trade-offs), and clarifying the training variants—would significantly improve the paper.
