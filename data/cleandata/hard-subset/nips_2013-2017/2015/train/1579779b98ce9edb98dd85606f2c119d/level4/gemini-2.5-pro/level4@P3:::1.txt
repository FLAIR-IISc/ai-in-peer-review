This paper presents a novel, non-greedy algorithm for learning the parameters of a decision tree. Standard induction algorithms, like CART and ID3, are greedy, optimizing split functions one node at a time based on a local criterion. This greedy procedure can lead to suboptimal trees. In contrast, this work proposes a global optimization framework that jointly learns all split functions and leaf node parameters by minimizing a global objective function.

The key conceptual contribution is an elegant formulation that connects decision tree optimization to the problem of structured prediction with latent variables. The authors associate a latent binary vector with the sequence of decisions made at the split nodes for a given input. Based on this, they derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a tractable surrogate objective. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A crucial technical contribution is a "fast loss-augmented inference" method that reduces the per-step complexity of SGD from being exponential in the tree depth to polynomial (O(d²p)), making the approach practical for deeper trees. Experiments on several classification benchmarks show that the resulting non-greedy trees outperform various greedy baselines.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Formulation:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and elegant approach. Casting the notoriously difficult problem of global decision tree optimization as a structured prediction task with latent variables is a significant conceptual contribution. It provides a fresh perspective and a principled optimization framework for a problem that has historically been tackled primarily with heuristics. This approach is likely to inspire further research in this direction.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated. The derivation of the surrogate objective via a loss-augmented inference formulation is rigorous. The use of SGD for optimization is appropriate, and the authors effectively address the significant computational challenges that arise. The development of the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm is a critical contribution that makes the entire framework practical and scalable.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and clear. The problem, motivation, and technical details are explained thoroughly, making the work easy for the reader to understand and appreciate. The connection between decision trees and latent structured models is laid out logically and convincingly.

4.  **Potential for Future Work:** The framework is general and opens up promising avenues for future research. As the authors note, the optimization problem could be extended with sparsity-inducing regularization to learn simpler split functions or by applying the kernel trick to learn non-linear splits, extending the model's applicability.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Modest Experimental Gains:** While the proposed non-greedy trees consistently outperform the greedy baselines in the experiments, the margin of improvement in test accuracy is somewhat modest across the tested datasets. Given the increased complexity and training time of the proposed algorithm compared to standard greedy methods, the practical gains may not be compelling enough to justify its adoption in all applications.

2.  **Potential for Overfitting:** The paper argues that norm regularization on the split weights (controlled by ν) helps prevent overfitting. However, the results in Figure 2 show a significantly larger gap between training and test accuracy for the non-greedy method compared to some baselines. This suggests that despite the regularization, the model's increased flexibility might make it more prone to overfitting the training data. A more detailed analysis of this phenomenon and the effectiveness of the regularization would strengthen the paper.

### Questions and Suggestions for the Authors

1.  Could you provide more insight into the trade-off between the tightness of the bound (controlled by ν) and generalization performance? The current results suggest the model may overfit more than baselines. Have other forms of regularization, such as L1 sparsity on the weight vectors `wi`, been considered to potentially create simpler splits and improve generalization?

2.  Have you considered a hybrid approach to potentially improve results? For example, after the global optimization finds a good configuration for the internal node splits, a final step could involve re-optimizing the leaf node parameters greedily on the resulting data partitions. This might refine the final predictions without altering the globally learned tree structure. A similar idea might be to "unfreeze" the final layer of splits and optimize them and their corresponding leaves greedily, which could potentially yield better performance.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper with a strong conceptual contribution. The elegance of the proposed framework and its potential to open up new research directions for non-greedy tree learning are compelling reasons for acceptance. While the experimental results are not groundbreaking, they demonstrate a consistent improvement over standard methods, validating the approach. The work represents a valuable and interesting advance in the field.

After discussion and considering the author rebuttal, my positive assessment stands. The elegance of the approach and its potential to stimulate future research are significant. The experimental results, while not overwhelming, do show a consistent advantage. Stronger results would have made this an exceptional paper, but as it stands, it is a solid and worthy contribution to the NIPS proceedings.
