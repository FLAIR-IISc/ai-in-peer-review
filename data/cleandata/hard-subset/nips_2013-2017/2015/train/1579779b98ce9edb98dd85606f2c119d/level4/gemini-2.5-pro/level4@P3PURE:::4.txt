This submission proposes a novel algorithm for the global optimization of decision trees by reformulating the problem as latent structured prediction. In this framework, the latent variables are sign vectors that encode the branching decisions at each internal node. The authors introduce a non-differentiable, non-convex loss function, which they approximate with a differentiable (though still non-convex) upper bound. This objective is then minimized using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), where each step requires solving a loss-augmented inference problem, for which the paper provides efficient algorithms. The primary contributions are this novel casting of decision tree optimization as structured prediction and the development of an efficient optimization procedure. Experimental results compare the proposed method against a standard greedy training algorithm.

The proposed approach is intriguing and conceptually elegant. Considered purely as a method to globally optimize decision trees, the method is compelling in several ways. A key advantage of casting the optimization as structured prediction is the ability to use SGD, which leverages the problem's specific structure by solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems.

Perhaps the most compelling consequence of this formulation, however, is the sparsity of the gradient updates: each gradient step modifies the weight vector of only a single node. While the authors present this primarily as a computational benefit, this property suggests more profound possibilities that are not fully explored. For instance, one could envision a procedure that optimizes an infinitely deep tree by initializing all weights to zero. The tree would then "grow" organically as the optimization makes specific weights non-zero, allowing the model's complexity to adapt to the data. This raises fascinating questions: Would such a method be equivalent to a known greedy strategy, particularly if it is always optimal to add a new node rather than update an existing one? Alternatively, could appropriate regularization lead the optimization to converge to a finite tree, as Figure 2 seems to suggest? A more thorough analysis of these points would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution, as it speaks directly to what makes this method fundamentally different from and potentially superior to standard greedy approaches.

Despite the novelty of the approach, I have several concerns regarding practical considerations, the motivation for the method, and the significance of the performance gains over naive methods.

A critical theoretical detail remains ambiguous: the behavior of nodes with a zero weight vector. The paper implies (page 3) that the right branch is taken by default in this case, which seems an arbitrary and potentially suboptimal choice. A more natural alternative would be to treat such a node as a leaf, making a final prediction. Precisely defining this behavior is important, as it would establish a direct connection to pruning strategies in greedy tree training and is crucial for understanding the "tree growing" process mentioned above.

A significant practical concern relates to the motivation for this work. Single decision trees are most often employed for their interpretability, which typically arises from simple, axis-aligned splits. The proposed method, using L2 regularization, is likely to produce dense weight vectors at each node, resulting in non-axis-aligned splits that are difficult to interpret. This undermines one of the primary reasons for using a decision tree over other, more powerful models like SVMs or neural networks. While switching to L1 regularization could potentially induce sparsity and recover interpretability, its impact on performance is not evaluated. It would be necessary to test whether interpretable (e.g., 1-sparse) splits can be achieved without a significant loss in accuracy.

The experimental evaluation is another area that requires improvement. The comparison to the "naive" greedy baseline lacks detail; for instance, the paper does not specify which pruning strategies or parameters were employed. For a fair comparison, the baseline should be tuned as thoroughly as the proposed method, for which a range of regularization parameters were tested. Furthermore, the experiments would be more convincing if they included other simple global optimization baselines, such as training multiple greedy trees on random subsets of the data and selecting the best-performing one on a validation set.

Regarding clarity, the paper is reasonably well-written. However, the sections describing the inference algorithms could be improved. For example, Section 6.2 could be more clearly summarized by stating that the objective is first maximized for all paths `g` corresponding to a given leaf, and this result is then maximized over all leaves. Similarly, Section 6.4 could be more explicit in its explanation.

Finally, it is worth noting that the paper's title mentions "forests," but this topic is not addressed in the text.

In summary, this paper presents a very clever and thought-provoking approach that raises some very interesting questions. However, it is unclear whether this is a practically useful method at this point, both due to the aforementioned interpretability issue and a lack of detail in the experiments.

**POST-REBUTTAL COMMENTS:**

I still believe that the case of zero weights is a critical point that deserves further analysis for the reasons mentioned in my review. The authors' rebuttal suggests that this issue does not arise in practice due to random weight initialization. However, I have a feeling that what would really make this method interesting is the case where weights are initialized to zero and the tree structure is learned via the sparse updates. I strongly encourage the authors to consider this direction. The submission proposes a clever reformulation for global optimization of decision trees, but some aspects of the motivation and experimental results are not yet convincing.
