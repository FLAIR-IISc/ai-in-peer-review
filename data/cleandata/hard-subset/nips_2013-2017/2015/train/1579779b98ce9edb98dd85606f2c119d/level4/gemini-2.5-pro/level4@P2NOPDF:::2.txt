### Review

**Summary**
This paper proposes a novel method for training decision trees by jointly optimizing all parameters, including the split functions and the leaf node posteriors. This approach contrasts with traditional greedy, layer-by-layer training procedures. The authors formulate this as a single, complex optimization problem and then derive a tractable surrogate objective by constructing a convex upper bound, which is then approximately optimized.

**Overall Assessment**
The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of non-greedy, end-to-end training for decision trees. A successful solution in this area would be a significant contribution. The formulation of the joint training task as a single optimization problem is elegant, and the derivation of the tractable objective is clearly presented.

However, my enthusiasm is tempered by a potential flaw in a core theoretical derivation and an experimental evaluation that could be strengthened with additional baselines and analysis. I am open to revising my assessment based on the authors' response, particularly regarding the correctness of the loss bound.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Potential Flaw in the Empirical Loss Bound:** My primary concern lies with the derivation of the upper bound in Equation (7). The argument that the right-hand side can only become larger for `g != sgn(Wx)` appears to be incorrect. Consider the following counter-example:
    *   Let `Wx = [5; -5]`.
    *   The argmax path is `ĥ = sgn(Wx) = [1; -1]`, which gives `ĥᵀWx = 10`.
    *   If we choose an alternative path `g = [1; 1]`, the term `gᵀWx - ĥᵀWx` becomes `0 - 10 = -10`.
    *   This negative value suggests that the right-hand side of Eq. (7), `gᵀWx - ĥᵀWx + ℓ(...)`, could be smaller than the original loss `ℓ(...)`, thus violating the upper bound property. The proof of Proposition 1 in the supplementary material does not seem to address this case, rendering it incomplete. I would appreciate a clarification from the authors on this critical point.

2.  **Empty Leaf Nodes:** The experiments show that some leaf nodes are not assigned any data points. This phenomenon seems counter-intuitive and may indicate an issue with the objective function or the optimization process (e.g., initialization). While the authors propose a heuristic of fixed assignments, this feels ad-hoc. Could the authors provide more intuition for why this occurs? Perhaps this heuristic could be more formally motivated, for example, through a connection to Expectation-Maximization or a concave-convex procedure (CCCP) as seen in some clustering algorithms.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Missing Baseline:** The experiments lack a crucial baseline: a decision tree with the same non-axis aligned split functions but trained using a standard, greedy, layer-by-layer approach. Including this baseline would help disentangle the benefits of the joint training procedure from the benefits of using more powerful split functions.

2.  **Necessity of Efficient Loss-Augmented Inference:** For the maximum tree depth reported (D=16), a brute-force search over all 2^16 ≈ 65,000 paths seems computationally feasible. Could the authors clarify the practical necessity of the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball? It would be helpful to quantify the performance impact of this restriction more broadly than the single result in the supplement. An experiment comparing runtimes would also strengthen this part of the paper.

3.  **Pre-specified Tree Depth:** A significant limitation of the proposed method is the requirement to fix the tree depth in advance. This constraint should be more prominently discussed, and a more thorough experimental evaluation of the model's sensitivity to this hyperparameter would be beneficial for understanding its practical applicability.

4.  **Clarity of Experiments:** The presentation of the experiments could be improved. For instance, in the discussion of loss-augmented inference, the meaning of the term 'leaves' in the supplementary material was unclear. A more careful evaluation and description would be desirable for the reader.
