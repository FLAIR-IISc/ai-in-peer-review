The submission proposes an algorithm for global optimization of decision trees based on a reformulation of the problem as (latent) structured prediction. The latent structured variables are sign vectors encoding the decisions made at each internal node in the tree. A non-differentiable and non-convex loss is proposed, which is then approximated by a differentiable (but still non-convex) upper bound. Minimizing this loss via SGD entails solving a sequence of loss-augmented inference problems, for which efficient algorithms are given. Experiments are shown comparing this method to a standard greedy training method.

The key contributions of the work are casting the global decision tree optimization as structured prediction and devising efficient algorithms to optimize the objective. This approach is novel and interesting, raising several compelling questions.

However, I have concerns about practical considerations, particularly the method’s motivation and the significance of its performance gains compared to simpler approaches. Considered as a method for global decision tree optimization, the work is intriguing. Casting the optimization as structured prediction allows for SGD using loss-augmented inference, leveraging the problem’s structure. 

More interestingly, the gradient updates are sparse – potentially involving changes to only one node’s weight vector per step. The submission highlights this as a computational advantage, but I believe it opens more intriguing possibilities, which aren’t fully explored. One could envision optimizing over an infinitely deep tree, starting with zero weights and gradually increasing them to allow the model complexity to grow naturally with the data. This raises questions about its relationship to greedy strategies – would it converge to an equivalent or variant? Or, with appropriate regularization, would it converge to a tree with a limited number of nodes (as suggested by Fig. 2)? This issue is central to assessing the method’s value compared to naive decision trees and warrants further analysis. 

Specifically, the handling of zero-weighted nodes requires clarification. The discussion on page 3 suggests taking the right branch, which seems questionable. Outputting a decision at such a node, potentially allowing the weight to be non-zero later, mimicking pruning strategies, seems preferable. Precisely defining this behavior is crucial.

A significant practical concern is the motivation for the work. Plain decision trees are primarily useful for their interpretability; when this isn't a requirement, other methods generally outperform them. This method, as presented, is likely to produce non-interpretable results due to L2 regularization leading to dense weight vectors. Exploring L1 regularization could help, but performance implications need investigation.

My other main concern is the lack of detail in the experimental comparisons. No details on the pruning strategies employed for the baseline greedy method are given. Given the parameter tuning for the proposed method, the baseline should also be evaluated with various pruning strategies/parameters. Comparing against trivial global optimization strategies, such as training multiple greedy methods with random data subsets, would also be valuable.

The paper is reasonably well-written, though the inference descriptions could be clearer. Section 6.2 could be summarized as maximizing the objective over all *g* for a given leaf, then over all leaves. Section 6.4 could also benefit from more explicit explanation.

In summary, this is a clever approach that raises interesting questions. However, its practical utility remains unclear due to interpretability concerns and a lack of experimental detail. The paper doesn’t address the case of decision forests.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:** I still believe the case of zero weights deserves further analysis. The rebuttal indicates weights are initialized randomly, which explains why this issue isn’t prominent in practice. However, I suspect the method would be more interesting if weights were initialized to zero and gradually increased. I encourage the authors to explore this direction. The submission presents a clever reformulation of global optimization of decision trees as structured prediction, along with an efficient algorithm to solve the optimization. Some aspects of the motivation for the work and experimental results are not that convincing, however.
