**Summary**
The authors propose a novel method for jointly training decision trees, addressing the limitations of current greedy layer-by-layer approaches. By formulating the task as a single optimization problem, they derive an upper-bound and approximated solution, yielding a tractable formulation.

**Quality**
While the paper is well-written, there are potential flaws in the reasoning that require attention. Specifically, the derivation of the empirical loss bound appears to be problematic.

**Clarity**
The derivation is clear, but the experimental presentation and results could be improved for better understanding.

**Originality**
Investigating joint training for decision trees is a significant and important topic.

**Significance**
Although the proposed solution has value, there are some shortcomings that need to be addressed.

**Comments**

* A potential flaw exists in the derivation of the empirical loss bound (line 216). The argument seems confusing, and a counterexample can be constructed to demonstrate the issue. For instance, when W\bx = [5;-5], choosing g = [1;1] results in a smaller right-hand side of Eq. (7), contradicting the upper-bound claim. The proof of Prop. 1 in the supplementary material does not cover this case and appears to be incomplete.
* The assignment of data points to leaves seems unusual, with some leaves not receiving any data points. This could indicate a problem with the objective or initialization. The suggested heuristic of fixed assignment to tree leaves may be suboptimal and could be improved using expectation maximization or concave-convex procedures, similar to other clustering techniques.
* The importance of efficient loss-augmented inference is unclear, given that the tree depth is at most 16 and greedy checking of 2^16 values is still feasible. The impact of the restricted search space introduced by the Hamming ball on performance is not well understood and requires further investigation. Additionally, the term 'leaves' is not clearly defined.
* A baseline comparison with standard greedy training of non-axis aligned split functions is missing and would provide valuable context.
* The need to specify the tree depth ahead of time is a significant limitation that requires more careful discussion. The authors should also provide a more thorough experimental evaluation of the depth parameter.
* The authors are thanked for clearly explaining the reasoning behind the loss bound (Eq. 7). However, \arg\max should be replaced with \max.
* An experiment comparing runtime performance would strengthen the paper. A more comprehensive evaluation of the method is desirable to provide readers with a better understanding of its capabilities. The submission addresses an interesting problem, but the experimental evaluation is limited, and potential flaws in the derivation need to be addressed. A careful explanation in the rebuttal may lead to an adjustment of the score.
