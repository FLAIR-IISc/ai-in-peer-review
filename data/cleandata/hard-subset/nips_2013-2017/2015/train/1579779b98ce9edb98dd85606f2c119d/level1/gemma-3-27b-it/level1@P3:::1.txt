This paper proposes a non-greedy algorithm for optimizing decision trees by jointly optimizing split functions and leaf parameters via a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. The core idea, linking decision tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables, is a promising direction. The authors demonstrate the feasibility of training deep trees with stochastic gradient descent and show improvements over greedy baselines on several benchmarks.

**Quality:** The paper is generally technically sound. The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound and the analysis of its gradient complexity are well-explained. The reduction of gradient complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a significant contribution, enabling the training of deeper trees. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines, validating the approach. However, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of the assumptions underlying the proposed upper bound and how violations of these assumptions might affect performance. The regularization parameter ν is introduced without a thorough theoretical justification beyond the observation in Proposition 1; a more rigorous analysis would be beneficial.  The experimental setup, while reasonably comprehensive, could benefit from a more detailed ablation study, specifically examining the impact of the SSGD (Stable SGD) algorithm and the initial greedy pre-training.

**Clarity:** The paper is reasonably well-written, but could benefit from some improvements in organization and clarity. The introduction provides adequate context but feels somewhat repetitive. The connection between the latent variable formulation and structured prediction could be explained more intuitively. The notation is consistent but dense, making it challenging to follow the derivations without careful attention.  Algorithm 1 is helpful, but lacks comments explaining the purpose of each step. The discussion of fast loss-augmented inference feels somewhat rushed and could be expanded with a visual example to aid understanding.

**Originality:** The idea of formulating decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is novel and a significant departure from traditional greedy approaches. While non-greedy tree optimization has been explored previously (e.g., Bennett [1]), the proposed formulation and optimization algorithm offer a distinct and efficient solution. The use of a convex-concave upper bound and stochastic gradient descent for joint optimization is also a novel contribution.  However, the paper does not sufficiently discuss the relationship to other structured prediction techniques beyond mentioning them in passing.  A more detailed comparison with methods like Conditional Random Fields (CRFs) and their applications to tree-structured data would strengthen the originality argument.  The connection to Mondrian Processes [15] could also be explored more deeply – are there similarities or differences in the underlying optimization principles?

**Significance:** The proposed method has the potential to significantly advance the field of decision tree learning. Overcoming the limitations of greedy algorithms is a long-standing challenge, and this work provides a promising solution. The ability to train deeper, non-greedy trees could lead to improved accuracy and generalization performance, particularly in complex datasets. The scalable optimization algorithm makes it feasible to apply this approach to large-scale problems. The work is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on decision trees, random forests, and related machine learning algorithms. The practical impact, however, hinges on the extent of the performance gains in real-world applications compared to highly optimized greedy methods and ensemble techniques.  A stronger demonstration of scalability to even larger datasets would further enhance the significance of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem.
*   Efficient optimization algorithm with O(d2p) gradient complexity.
*   Empirically demonstrated improvements over greedy baselines.
*   Addresses a long-standing challenge in decision tree learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed theoretical analysis of the upper bound assumptions.
*   Limited ablation study to isolate the impact of different components.
*   Insufficient discussion of the relationship to other structured prediction methods.
*   Regularization parameter justification is weak.




**Overall Assessment:** I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors have presented a novel and promising approach to non-greedy decision tree optimization. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly strengthening the theoretical justification, expanding the ablation study, and providing a more comprehensive comparison to related work – would significantly enhance the quality and impact of the paper. The current work represents a solid contribution to the field and has the potential to inspire further research in this area.
