**Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees. Traditional decision tree induction algorithms, such as ID3 and CART, operate greedily by optimizing split functions one node at a time. This can lead to suboptimal tree structures. The authors address this limitation by formulating the joint optimization of all split functions and leaf parameters as a global learning problem. The core idea is to frame decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, where the path an example takes through the tree is represented by a latent binary vector. As the resulting empirical loss function is discontinuous and difficult to optimize directly, the authors derive a convex-concave upper bound to serve as a surrogate objective. This approach is inspired by prior work in latent structured SVMs. A key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for the loss-augmented inference step required for optimization. The authors show how to reduce the complexity of this step from exponential in the tree depth (`O(2^d)`) to polynomial (`O(d^2)`), making the training of deep trees feasible. The surrogate objective is optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), allowing for training on large datasets. Experimental results on several classification benchmarks demonstrate that the proposed non-greedy trees outperform various greedy baselines, including axis-aligned and oblique decision trees.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance**: The paper's main strength lies in its novel formulation of decision tree optimization. Connecting this classical problem to the modern framework of structured prediction with latent variables is both elegant and powerful. It provides a principled way to perform global optimization, moving beyond the decades-old greedy paradigm. Given the widespread use of decision trees and forests, a method that demonstrably improves the quality of the base learner is a significant contribution with the potential for broad impact.

2.  **Technical Quality**: The work is technically sound. The derivation of the upper bound on the empirical loss is correct and well-motivated. The identification of the computational bottleneck in the loss-augmented inference step and the subsequent development of the "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm (by constraining the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1) is a crucial contribution that makes the approach practical. The optimization via SGD is appropriate for the problem structure and large-scale settings. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing the proposed method against multiple relevant and strong baselines on several datasets, and convincingly demonstrates the benefits of the non-greedy approach.

3.  **Clarity**: The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the limitations of greedy induction and motivate their global optimization approach. The mathematical formulation, particularly the link to structured prediction via the latent variable `h`, is presented logically and supplemented with helpful figures. The description of the optimization algorithm and the complexity analysis are clear and concise.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Complexity**: While the proposed "fast" algorithm reduces the complexity of the gradient update to `O(d^2*p)`, this is still considerably more expensive per-update than standard greedy training procedures. The experiments in Figure 4 effectively show the improvement of the fast version over the naive one, but a direct comparison of total training wall-clock time against the greedy baselines (e.g., OC1) would be valuable for practitioners to understand the performance-vs-computation trade-off.

2.  **Initialization and Local Minima**: The optimization problem is non-convex, and the SGD-based procedure is only guaranteed to find a local minimum. The method is initialized using a greedily trained tree. The paper does not investigate the sensitivity of the final result to this initialization. It would be useful to understand how much the final performance depends on the quality of the initial greedy tree versus the global refinement step. The introduction of "Stable SGD" appears to be a heuristic to manage optimization stability, but a deeper discussion of the optimization landscape and the potential impact of local minima would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Scope of Comparison**: The experiments rightly focus on single trees to isolate and highlight the contribution of the non-greedy learning algorithm. However, in practice, state-of-the-art performance is often achieved with ensembles of trees (e.g., Random Forests, Gradient Boosted Trees). A brief discussion or a small-scale experiment exploring how non-greedy trees perform within an ensemble framework would provide a more complete picture of the method's potential significance. It would be interesting to see if the gains from non-greedy training compound or diminish when trees are ensembled.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and principled framework for a fundamental and long-standing problem in machine learning.
*   The technical contribution of framing tree learning as latent structured prediction is insightful and opens up new research directions.
*   The proposed "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm is a key technical innovation that makes the approach computationally feasible.
*   The method is shown to be empirically superior to strong greedy baselines on multiple datasets.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The computational cost is significantly higher than for standard greedy methods, and the practical trade-offs are not fully explored.
*   The analysis of the non-convex optimization problem, including sensitivity to initialization and the nature of local minima, is limited.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a high-quality, novel, and significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed framework is technically sound and the empirical results are compelling.
