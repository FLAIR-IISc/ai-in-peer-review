**Summary of the Paper:**
This paper tackles the long-standing problem of greedy decision tree induction, where split functions are optimized locally at each node, potentially leading to a globally suboptimal tree. The authors propose a novel algorithm for non-greedy optimization of decision trees by jointly learning all split functions (for oblique trees) and leaf parameters under a global objective.

The central idea is to reframe the problem of learning a decision tree as structured prediction with latent variables. For any given input, the sequence of binary decisions that routes it to a leaf node is treated as a latent variable. Direct optimization of the empirical loss is intractable due to its discontinuous, piecewise-constant nature. To overcome this, the authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, inspired by latent structured SVMs. This surrogate objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD).

A key technical contribution addresses the computational complexity of this approach. A naive implementation of the required "loss-augmented inference" step has a complexity that is exponential in the depth of the tree (`d`), making it impractical. The authors introduce a "fast loss-augmented inference" method by constraining the search for the most violating path to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current best path. This reduces the complexity of each SGD step to O(d^2p) (where `p` is the feature dimension), enabling the efficient training of deep trees. The paper relates this work to prior art in non-greedy tree learning, noting that previous methods were often limited in scope or computationally prohibitive. The proposed method is a significant generalization of the authors' prior work (CO2), which applied a similar optimization greedily.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of decision tree learning as structured prediction is sound, and the derivation of the surrogate upper bound is correct. The proposed optimization algorithm, particularly the "fast loss-augmented inference," is a critical and well-executed contribution that makes the theory practical. Claims are well-supported by experimental results on several benchmark datasets. The experiments are thorough, comparing against multiple relevant baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, and the greedy CO2 variant). The results consistently demonstrate that the non-greedy trees achieve superior test accuracy and better generalization, validating the paper's core claims. The authors are also careful to include analyses of key hyperparameters and the runtime complexity, which strengthens the work.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly motivate the problem and build their solution step-by-step, from the initial intractable formulation to the final efficient algorithm. The connection to structured prediction is explained lucidly, and the mathematical derivations are presented with clarity. The use of figures and an algorithm box aids in understanding. The paper provides enough detail for an expert reader to grasp the key concepts and potentially reproduce the results.

**Originality:**
The paper is highly original. While non-greedy tree optimization has been explored before, the approach of framing it as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a novel and insightful contribution. This connection opens the door to applying a powerful set of tools from the structured prediction literature to a classic machine learning problem. The development of the efficient, constrained loss-augmented inference algorithm is also a novel technical element tailored to this specific problem structure. The work is clearly distinguished from previous contributions and represents a significant conceptual advance.

**Significance:**
The results are significant and likely to be impactful. Decision trees are a cornerstone of machine learning, yet their fundamental training algorithms have seen little change for decades. This paper presents a principled and practical method for global tree optimization that demonstrably improves performance. More accurate and compact single trees are valuable in their own right, especially in applications where interpretability or low latency are critical. Furthermore, this framework is general and opens up avenues for future research, such as incorporating different regularizers (e.g., L1 for sparse splits) or applying the kernel trick. The ideas presented here are likely to be used and built upon by other researchers in the field.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**
1.  **Role of Initialization:** The optimization is non-convex, and the paper notes that it is initialized using a greedy procedure. The performance of the final model might be sensitive to this initialization. While the results are strong, it would be interesting to see an analysis of how much the non-greedy refinement improves upon the initial greedy solution, and whether the method can recover from a weaker (e.g., random) initialization. This would help to fully disentangle the benefits of the global optimization from the benefits of a strong starting point.
2.  **Implications for Ensembles:** The experiments rightly focus on single trees to isolate the contribution of the non-greedy algorithm. However, in practice, the state-of-the-art performance of tree-based methods comes from ensembles like Random Forests and Gradient Boosted Trees. A brief discussion or a small-scale experiment showing how these non-greedily trained trees perform as base learners in an ensemble would greatly enhance the paper's practical significance. It is plausible that stronger, more diverse base learners could lead to more powerful and/or smaller ensembles.
3.  **Computational Trade-offs:** The O(d^2p) complexity is a substantial improvement over an exponential one, but it is still more expensive than the O(dp log N) complexity of standard greedy methods. A more direct comparison of the wall-clock time required to reach a certain level of accuracy versus greedy methods would be helpful for practitioners to understand the practical trade-offs.

Despite these minor points for improvement, the paper is a standout contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and elegant connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables.
*   Provides a technically sound and principled global optimization framework for a classic, notoriously difficult problem.
*   Introduces a crucial algorithmic innovation ("fast loss-augmented inference") that makes the approach computationally feasible for deep trees.
*   Demonstrates significant and consistent empirical improvements over strong baselines on multiple datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis lacks a study of the sensitivity to initialization, which is important for non-convex optimization problems.
*   The performance within ensemble methods, the most common use-case for decision trees, is not explored.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental and significant contribution to the literature on decision tree learning. The novel formulation, technical soundness, and strong empirical results make it a clear accept for NIPS.
