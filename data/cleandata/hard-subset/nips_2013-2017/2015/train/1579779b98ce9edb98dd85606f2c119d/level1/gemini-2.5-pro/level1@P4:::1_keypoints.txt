- Novel formulation: global non-greedy decision tree learning framed as structured prediction with latent binary path variables.  
- Surrogate objective: derives a convex-concave upper bound to handle the discontinuous empirical loss.  
- Fast inference: introduces "fast loss-augmented inference" reducing complexity from O(2^d) to O(d^2), making deep-tree training feasible.  
- Optimization: uses SGD to optimize the surrogate, enabling large-scale training.  
- Empirical gains: non-greedy trees outperform several strong greedy baselines (axis-aligned and oblique) on multiple datasets.  
- Strength — originality/significance: connects classical tree induction to latent structured prediction, offering a principled global alternative to greedy methods.  
- Strength — technical quality: sound derivations, key Hamming-ball constrained inference trick, appropriate SGD optimization, and thorough experiments.  
- Strength — clarity: paper is well-written, mathematically clear, and explains algorithms and complexity analyses concisely.  
- Weakness — computational cost: per-update complexity O(d^2 p) is substantially higher than greedy methods; lacks wall-clock training-time comparison to practitioners’ baselines.  
- Weakness — non-convexity/initialization: optimization is non-convex, initialized from greedy trees, and sensitivity to initialization/local minima is not thoroughly analyzed; "Stable SGD" is heuristic.  
- Weakness — scope of evaluation: experiments focus on single trees and do not assess performance within ensembles (Random Forests/Boosting), limiting practical relevance.  
- Pro-acceptance: presents a novel, principled framework, an important technical innovation (fast inference), empirical improvements, and excellent exposition.  
- Con-acceptance: higher computational overhead and limited analysis of non-convex optimization behavior/initialization sensitivity.  
- Overall judgment: strengths substantially outweigh weaknesses; a high-quality, significant contribution to decision tree learning.
