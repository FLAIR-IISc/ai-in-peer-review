

This paper proposes a non-greedy optimization framework for decision trees that jointly optimizes split functions across all tree levels with leaf parameters under a global objective. The authors establish a novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently using stochastic gradient descent. Their key technical contribution is reducing the gradient computation complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p), making deep tree training feasible. The paper builds on prior work like Bennett's non-greedy multi-linear programming approach [1] and structured prediction literature [25], but significantly advances the field by enabling efficient training with large datasets while handling general loss functions.

The paper excels in quality and originality. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear proofs of the upper bound and complexity analysis. The experimental results across multiple classification benchmarks consistently demonstrate superior performance over greedy baselines (including CO2, OC1, and axis-aligned trees), with better generalization and reduced overfitting. The approach is clearly differentiated from prior work through its efficient optimization method and general loss function support.

Clarity is excellent, with well-structured exposition and detailed algorithm descriptions. The significance is strong, as the method addresses a fundamental limitation of decision trees while maintaining their computational advantages. The connection to structured prediction opens new research avenues, and the paper's efficiency enables practical applications.

Weaknesses include limited comparison with recent deep learning-based tree methods (e.g., neural decision forests), and insufficient discussion of potential failure cases or scalability limits on extremely large datasets beyond the Connect4 benchmark. The paper could also better contextualize how their non-greedy approach relates to recent advances in tree-based ensemble methods.

Pro: Clear theoretical contribution, efficient algorithm, strong empirical results, well-written, addresses a fundamental limitation in decision trees.

Con: Limited comparison with recent tree-based deep learning methods, insufficient scalability discussion, no analysis of when non-greedy approach might not provide benefits.

The paper makes a significant contribution to decision tree learning that advances the state of the art while maintaining practical applicability. It meets all NIPS criteria for a high-quality scientific contribution with strong theoretical foundation and empirical validation. I recommend acceptance.
