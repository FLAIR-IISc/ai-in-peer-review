

# Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

This paper proposes a novel approach to decision tree learning that optimizes split functions at all levels of the tree jointly with leaf parameters, rather than using the standard greedy approach. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss. This surrogate objective can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent with computational complexity reduced from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p), enabling efficient training of deep trees. The method is evaluated on multiple classification benchmarks, demonstrating superior generalization performance compared to greedy baselines.

The paper builds upon previous work on non-greedy tree optimization (Bennett [1]) and structured prediction with latent variables (Yu and Joachims [25]), while extending the CO2 method [18] to a truly non-greedy approach. The authors effectively demonstrate how the decision tree learning problem can be reframed using latent variables, which is a significant theoretical contribution that bridges two distinct areas of machine learning.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a strong theoretical foundation. The authors provide a clear proof of the convex-concave upper bound (equation 7), justify the regularization through Proposition 1, and thoroughly analyze computational complexity. The experiments are well-designed, comparing against multiple relevant baselines (greedy axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2) across several datasets. The paper presents a complete solution, not just a position paper, with a clear algorithm (Algorithm 1) and comprehensive experimental validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, particularly regarding the need for hyperparameter tuning of ν and η.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation (Section 3) is clear and uses consistent notation. The connection to structured prediction (Section 4) is well-explained with helpful notation. The algorithm description (Section 6) is precise and includes pseudocode with detailed explanations of each step. The figures (particularly Figure 2 showing accuracy across tree depths) effectively communicate the key results. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the experiments.

**Originality:** The core contribution is the novel connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, which enables the development of the convex-concave upper bound. While previous work (Bennett [1]) explored non-greedy tree optimization, it was limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss and had prohibitive computational complexity. The authors' reduction of complexity to O(d^2p) is a significant technical contribution. The method generalizes beyond previous approaches by handling any loss function while maintaining the computational advantages of decision trees.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of decision tree learning that has persisted for decades (greedy induction). The results demonstrate clear improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets, which is significant for practitioners. The method provides a principled way to regularize tree training through the norm constraint on W, addressing the overfitting problem that has traditionally required post-pruning. The approach is applicable to large-scale problems (as demonstrated by the Microsoft Kinect example in the introduction), making it relevant to real-world applications.

**Strengths:** 
1. The theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction is insightful and well-executed.
2. The efficient algorithm with O(d^2p) complexity is a major practical contribution.
3. The empirical results consistently show superior performance over greedy baselines across multiple datasets.
4. The paper is well-written with clear explanations of technical concepts.
5. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the relationship between the regularization parameter ν and tree pruning.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't adequately compare with more recent tree-based methods like XGBoost or LightGBM, which have become the standard in many applications. This is a significant gap since these methods incorporate gradient boosting and other optimizations that might outperform both greedy and non-greedy trees.
2. The connection to structured prediction is interesting, but the paper could better explain why this specific formulation (equation 5) leads to the particular upper bound they propose, rather than other possible formulations.
3. The experiments are limited to classification tasks; demonstrating the method on regression benchmarks would strengthen the claim of generality.
4. While Figure 4 shows training time comparisons, the paper doesn't provide a clear breakdown of the speedup factor for larger datasets beyond the small Connect4 dataset (55,000 training points).

**Recommendation:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to decision tree learning. The theoretical insights are novel and well-justified, the algorithm is efficient and well-implemented, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over standard greedy approaches. While the lack of comparison with more recent tree-based methods is a notable limitation, the core contributions remain valuable to the field. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.

The paper meets all four criteria exceptionally well, with particularly strong contributions in originality and significance. The method addresses a long-standing limitation in decision tree learning with a theoretically sound and practically efficient solution, demonstrating clear advantages over existing approaches. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction.
