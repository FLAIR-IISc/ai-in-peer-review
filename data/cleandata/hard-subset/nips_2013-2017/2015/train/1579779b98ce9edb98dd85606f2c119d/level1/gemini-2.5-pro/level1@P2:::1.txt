### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel, non-greedy algorithm for training decision trees with oblique (linear-combination) splits. The central limitation of traditional decision tree induction algorithms (e.g., CART, ID3) is their greedy, node-by-node optimization, which can lead to globally suboptimal trees. The authors address this by formulating the joint optimization of all split functions and leaf parameters as a single global objective function.

The key conceptual contribution is to frame decision tree learning as a problem of structured prediction with latent variables. For a given input, the sequence of binary decisions that routes it to a leaf is treated as a latent variable. The empirical loss of the tree is then expressed in terms of this latent variable. Since this loss function is discontinuous and difficult to optimize directly, the authors derive a continuous, piecewise-smooth, convex-concave upper bound on the loss. This surrogate objective is then optimized using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD).

A critical technical challenge is that the naive formulation of the loss-augmented inference step required for optimization has a computational complexity that is exponential in the tree's depth (`O(2^d)`), making it impractical for deep trees. The authors overcome this by introducing a "fast loss-augmented inference" method. This involves a slightly looser but more efficient upper bound where the search for the optimal decision path is restricted to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current best path. This reduces the complexity of each SGD update to `O(d^2p)`, making the training of deep trees feasible. The authors demonstrate experimentally on several benchmark datasets that their non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines, achieving better generalization and being less prone to overfitting.

This work relates to a long line of research on decision tree optimization. While global optimization of trees is known to be NP-complete (Hyafil & Rivest, 1976), previous attempts like Bennett (1994) were computationally prohibitive. This work differs from methods like Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts (Jordan & Jacobs, 1994) by maintaining hard, efficient splits rather than soft, probabilistic ones. It provides a principled optimization framework that goes beyond the greedy heuristics that have dominated the field for decades.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables is sound and well-motivated. The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss provides a principled surrogate for the original intractable objective. The proposed "fast loss-augmented inference" is a clever and crucial contribution that makes the approach computationally practical. The claims of superior performance and better generalization are well-supported by experimental results on four different datasets (Figure 2), which consistently show the non-greedy approach outperforming several reasonable baselines. The authors are also careful to evaluate the scalability of their method, providing an empirical comparison of the slow and fast inference procedures (Figure 4).

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of building their argument, starting from the standard decision tree formulation, introducing the latent variable perspective, deriving the surrogate objective, identifying the computational bottleneck, and finally presenting the efficient solution. The notation is consistent, and Algorithm 1 provides a clear summary of the optimization procedure. The figures are informative and effectively illustrate the main claims of the paper.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The primary contribution—connecting decision tree induction to latent variable structured SVMs—is novel and provides a powerful new lens through which to view a classic machine learning problem. While non-greedy tree optimization is not a new idea, the proposed approach is fundamentally different from prior work. It is not a "look-ahead" heuristic but a true global optimization of a surrogate objective. The formulation itself is a significant and novel combination of familiar techniques from structured prediction applied to a domain where they have not been used before.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is high. Decision trees and forests are workhorse models in machine learning, but their induction has long been dominated by decades-old greedy algorithms. This paper offers a modern, principled, and effective alternative that advances the state of the art for learning single decision trees. The framework is general and could potentially be extended with different loss functions, sparsity-inducing regularizers on the split weights, or even the kernel trick, as the authors note in their conclusion. Researchers and practitioners working on tree-based models are likely to build upon these ideas.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Initialization:** The performance of non-convex optimization is often sensitive to initialization. The paper mentions initializing from a greedy procedure (CO2), but the details are brief. A more thorough discussion or ablation study on the effect of different initializations would strengthen the paper and aid in reproducibility. How much of the final performance is attributable to the good starting point versus the non-greedy refinement?

2.  **Comparison to Ensembles:** The experiments focus exclusively on single trees. In practice, the strongest baselines are ensembles like Random Forests and Gradient Boosted Trees. While the goal is to improve the base learner, it would be very interesting to see if these stronger non-greedy trees lead to stronger forests. It is possible that the randomization and diversity generated by simpler, greedy methods are more important for ensemble performance than the quality of any single tree. A preliminary experiment on this would significantly increase the paper's impact.

3.  **"Stable SGD" Heuristic:** The proposed "Stable SGD" (SSGD) method, designed to prevent leaves from becoming inactive, feels somewhat heuristic. While the motivation is clear, its introduction and justification could be expanded. It would be useful to see an empirical comparison between the standard SGD and SSGD to quantify its benefit and understand the failure modes it addresses.

4.  **Absolute Training Time:** While Figure 4 effectively demonstrates the *relative* speedup of the fast inference method, the paper lacks a comparison of the *absolute* training time against the fast, greedy baselines. This information is crucial for practitioners to assess the trade-off between improved accuracy and increased computational cost.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   Highly original and principled formulation of non-greedy tree learning via a connection to structured prediction.
*   Solves a key technical challenge (exponential complexity) with a clever and effective approximation, making the method practical.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating superior accuracy and generalization over several established greedy baselines.
*   The work opens up numerous avenues for future research (e.g., kernels, different regularizers) and could significantly influence how tree-based models are trained.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The evaluation is limited to single trees, leaving its utility within state-of-the-art ensemble methods unevaluated.
*   The initialization procedure and the "Stable SGD" method could be described and justified in more detail.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It tackles a fundamental and long-standing problem in machine learning with a novel, technically sound, and empirically successful approach. It is a complete and significant piece of work that is very likely to have a lasting impact on the field.
