## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper presents a novel approach to training decision trees by moving beyond the traditional greedy algorithms. The core idea is to jointly optimize split functions at all levels of the tree alongside the leaf parameters, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. The authors establish a connection to structured prediction with latent variables and develop an efficient gradient computation (O(d), where d is the tree depth) via a clever reformulation, enabling the training of deep trees with stochastic gradient descent.

The paper clearly builds upon the extensive history of decision tree learning (Quinlan ’93, Breiman ‘84), acknowledging the limitations of greedy approaches (Bennett ‘97, Murthy & Salzburg ‘95). It correctly positions itself against recent attempts to improve greedy algorithms with new objective functions while advocating for a fundamentally different, global optimization strategy. The connection to structured prediction (Taskar et al. ‘04, Yu & Joachims ‘05) is a strong contribution, providing a theoretical foundation and allowing the application of tools developed in that field. The presented speedup to gradient calculation is a key technical advancement, addressing a significant bottleneck in non-greedy tree optimization.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a long-standing problem in machine learning: the sub-optimality of greedy decision tree induction. The proposed method offers a compelling alternative with theoretical justification and promising empirical results. The efficient gradient computation is a significant contribution. The experimental validation on benchmark datasets demonstrates performance gains over several greedy baselines, including CO2 (Norouzi et al. ’15), highlighting the benefits of the non-greedy approach. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, with a detailed explanation of the technical contributions.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical derivations are presented, some of the justifications (e.g., Proposition 1) could benefit from further elaboration and intuition. The experimental section, while showing improvements, could be strengthened by a more detailed ablation study investigating the impact of the regularization parameter ν. The comparison to other methods is somewhat limited; comparing to more recent forest-based methods beyond the baselines considered would be valuable. The paper assumes binary classification. Extending the method to multi-class and regression settings would broaden its applicability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to decision tree optimization with strong theoretical grounding.
*   Significant improvement in performance over established greedy baselines.
*   Efficient gradient computation enabling training of deep trees.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited ablation study of the regularization parameter.
*   Relatively narrow experimental comparison.
*   Restricted to binary classification.




**Overall Assessment:** I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed method is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While some areas could be strengthened, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on expanding the experimental evaluation and providing further clarification of certain theoretical aspects.
