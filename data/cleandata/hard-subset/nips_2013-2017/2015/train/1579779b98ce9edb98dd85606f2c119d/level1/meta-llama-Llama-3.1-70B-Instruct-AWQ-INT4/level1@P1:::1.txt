**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper proposes a non-greedy algorithm for optimizing decision trees. The authors formulate the decision tree learning problem as a global optimization problem, where the goal is to minimize the empirical loss of the tree's predictions on the training set. They establish a link between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, and develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. The authors then use stochastic gradient descent (SGD) to optimize this upper bound, which enables efficient learning of deep trees.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The authors build upon previous work on decision tree induction, including greedy algorithms such as ID3 and CART. They also draw inspiration from structured prediction with latent variables, which has been applied to various problems in computer vision and machine learning. The authors' formulation of decision tree learning as a global optimization problem is novel, and their use of a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss is a key contribution.

**Strengths**

1. **Efficient optimization**: The authors' use of a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss enables efficient optimization of the decision tree parameters using SGD.
2. **Global optimization**: The authors' formulation of decision tree learning as a global optimization problem allows for joint optimization of the split functions and leaf parameters, which can lead to better generalization performance.
3. **Flexibility**: The authors' framework can handle any general loss function, and can be applied to various problems in computer vision and machine learning.
4. **Experimental results**: The authors' experiments demonstrate that their non-greedy decision trees outperform greedy decision tree baselines on several benchmark datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational complexity**: The authors' algorithm has a time complexity of O(d2p), which can be slow for very deep trees.
2. **Hyperparameter tuning**: The authors' method requires tuning of several hyperparameters, including the regularization constant ν and the learning rate η.
3. **Limited interpretability**: The authors' non-greedy decision trees may be less interpretable than traditional decision trees, since the split functions are optimized jointly.
