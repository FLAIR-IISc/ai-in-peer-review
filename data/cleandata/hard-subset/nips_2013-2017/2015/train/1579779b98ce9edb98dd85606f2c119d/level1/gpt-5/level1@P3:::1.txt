Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a non-greedy training algorithm for oblique decision trees that jointly optimizes all split parameters and leaf distributions under a global empirical loss. The key idea is to reinterpret the sequence of hard split decisions as a latent binary vector h ∈ {−1,+1}^m, reducing tree learning to a form of structured prediction with latent variables. This yields a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss akin to latent structural SVM objectives [23, 25]. The authors derive two surrogates: a full bound requiring loss-augmented inference over all h (exponential in depth), and an efficient variant restricting loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current path, reducing per-example computation to O(d^2 p). Training is performed by SGD with per-row norm constraints on W, and a “Stable SGD” variant that fixes leaf assignments intermittently to avoid collapsing leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets show consistent improvements over standard greedy axis-aligned trees (CART), OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and a greedy depth-1 CO2 variant [18], particularly at moderate-to-deep depths.

The paper connects to a long line of work on non-greedy or globally optimized trees (e.g., [1–3, 11]) and to soft/probabilistic trees and hierarchical mixtures of experts [13], while maintaining hard test-time routing. Relative to soft trees (which are trainable via smooth likelihoods but require evaluating many experts), the method retains O(dp) inference. The structured prediction framing parallels margin-based approaches for trees [2, 3] and leverages latent SSVM techniques [24, 25].

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity and novelty: The latent structured prediction view of hard decision trees is elegant and facilitates principled surrogate objectives and SGD training. The radius-1 Hamming-ball bound is a clever way to retain an upper bound while making loss-augmented inference tractable.
- Practicality: Per-example gradients are O(d^2 p) with hard routing preserved at test time; projection onto per-row L2 balls regularizes split scales and appears to control overfitting and implicit pruning.
- Empirical results: On multiple datasets and depths, non-greedy oblique trees outperform several strong greedy baselines. The sensitivity study over ν and analysis of training time helps substantiate the practicality and the role of regularization.
- Well-situated in the literature: The paper relates to classic tree induction (ID3/CART), oblique trees, margin-based trees, structural SVMs, and soft trees.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Surrogate tightness and design choices: Restricting loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 produces a different (looser) upper bound. The paper would benefit from an ablation varying the radius r (r = 0, 1, 2) to quantify the trade-off between tightness, accuracy, and runtime. The practical gap between the full bound (7) and the fast bound (13) remains unclear.
- Initialization dependence and nonconvexity: Training is initialized via a greedy procedure (axis-aligned then CO2). Please report sensitivity to initialization (e.g., random W, different greedy seeds) and whether multiple restarts help. Without such analysis, gains might partially stem from good initial trees rather than the non-greedy optimization per se.
- Baseline scope: While focusing on single trees clarifies the contribution, the practical competitiveness of trees often stems from ensembles. It would be informative to show that globally trained trees improve Random Forests or Gradient Boosting when used as base learners (e.g., replacing axis-aligned splits with learned oblique W). Likewise, comparisons to soft trees/HME [13] in accuracy vs. inference cost would contextualize advantages of hard routing. Boosting [8] is a relevant baseline even with axis-aligned splits.
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond Proposition 1 and the upper-bound construction, there is no analysis of generalization (e.g., margin-based or Rademacher bounds for trees with constrained split norms) or convergence (e.g., to stationary points under the convex–concave structure). Some discussion or preliminary bounds under the per-row L2 constraints would strengthen the paper’s theoretical contribution.
- SSGD details: The “Stable SGD” heuristic is plausible, but the paper lacks specifics (how long are assignments fixed, what constitutes “negligible” improvement, how sensitive are results?). An empirical comparison of SGD vs. SSGD accuracy and stability across datasets would clarify its necessity.
- Complexity claims and implementation: Algorithm 1 projects all rows, but only O(d) rows change per update. Please clarify whether you implement sparse projection (and how), since full projection is O(mp). Also, report wall-clock times on larger datasets to substantiate scalability claims.

Quality:
- The method is technically sound; the upper bounds and gradients are well-motivated by latent SSVMs. The fast inference trick is correct under the modified bound. Experiments reasonably support claims, though broader baselines and ablations would solidify the empirical case.

Clarity:
- Generally clear and well-organized. The navigation function f and path-constraint handling are explained well. A minor suggestion: add pseudocode or a schematic for fast loss-augmented inference and explicitly define the set of indices on a path to a leaf to help reproducibility. Provide more detail on SSGD scheduling.

Originality:
- The latent structured prediction formulation for hard oblique trees and the specific radius-1 bound for efficient loss-augmented inference are novel and nontrivial extensions beyond prior oblique/greedy and soft-tree methods.

Significance:
- Improving single-tree training with a principled non-greedy, scalable approach is valuable. If the approach can be shown to enhance forests/boosting, impact would be higher. As-is, the contribution advances the state of the art in training oblique trees and may spur further work on globally trained tree models.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, principled formulation with efficient training.
- Consistent empirical gains over strong greedy baselines.
- Maintains hard routing with O(dp) inference; practical regularization via per-row norm constraints.
- Clear connection to structured prediction and latent SSVMs.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited exploration of surrogate tightness (radius choices) and sensitivity to initialization.
- No evaluation within ensembles or against boosted trees; practical impact may be understated.
- Heuristic SSGD lacks detail and ablation.
- No generalization or convergence analysis.

Overall recommendation: Leaning accept. The conceptual advance and solid empirical improvements on multiple benchmarks justify inclusion, with the above suggestions aimed at strengthening the final version.
