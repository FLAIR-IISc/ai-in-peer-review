- Proposes a non-greedy method that jointly optimizes split functions and leaf parameters for decision trees using a global objective.  
- Links decision tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables and derives a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss.  
- Demonstrates efficient learning of deep trees and empirical improvement over greedy baselines on classification benchmarks.  

Strengths:
- Novel formulation enabling efficient learning of deep decision trees.  
- Establishes a meaningful connection to structured prediction with latent variables.  
- Empirical results show better performance than greedy decision tree baselines.  
- Provides a thorough computational complexity analysis and demonstrates efficiency.  

Weaknesses:
- Assumes a fixed tree structure and does not learn the tree topology.  
- Uses a simple weight-norm regularizer that may be insufficient to prevent overfitting.  
- Experiments are limited to classification and do not evaluate regression tasks.  
- Lacks detailed comparison with other non-greedy decision tree methods (e.g., Bennett).  

Originality:
- Introduces a novel decision-tree learning formulation for deep trees.  
- New contribution in connecting decision trees to structured prediction with latent variables.  
- Represents a significant departure from traditional greedy tree learning methods.  

Significance:
- Addresses the important problem of global optimization for decision trees.  
- Method has potential to improve decision-tree accuracy across tasks.  
- Offers a new perspective likely to stimulate further research.  

Quality:
- Paper is well-written and easy to follow.  
- Computational complexity analysis is thorough.  
- Experimental results are well-presented and supportive of claims.  

Clarity:
- Paper organization and exposition are clear.  
- Clear explanation of the method and its theoretical connection.  
- Notation is consistent and clear throughout.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Presents a novel, effective non-greedy method for learning decision trees.  
- Thorough complexity analysis supports practical viability.  
- Empirical results validate the method’s effectiveness.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Restricts attention to fixed tree structures, limiting generality.  
- Missing detailed comparisons to other non-greedy methods.  
- Evaluation limited to classification; lacks regression experiments.  

Recommendation:
- Recommend acceptance, with suggested revisions to address structure learning, stronger regularization, broader comparisons, and regression experiments.
