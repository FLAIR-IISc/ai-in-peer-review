This paper proposes a non-greedy algorithm for optimizing decision trees, formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and deriving a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. The core idea is to jointly optimize split functions at all levels of the tree, overcoming the limitations of traditional greedy approaches. The authors demonstrate the feasibility of training deep trees using stochastic gradient descent and show improved performance on several classification benchmarks.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the drawbacks of greedy decision tree induction and positioning the proposed method as a solution to these limitations. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is a novel and interesting perspective, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed approach. The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound and the efficient gradient computation (O(d)) are significant technical contributions. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, consistently outperforming greedy baselines. The analysis of the regularization parameter ν and its effect on tree pruning is insightful.

However, there are several areas that could be strengthened. The related work section, while covering relevant topics, lacks depth in discussing recent advances in non-greedy tree learning, specifically methods beyond CO2. For example, the discussion of [15] feels superficial, and other relevant works on non-parametric structured prediction or optimization techniques for tree structures are missing. The complexity analysis, while stated, could benefit from a more detailed breakdown, particularly regarding the constant factors involved. The empirical evaluation, while convincing, could be expanded to include more datasets, particularly those with higher dimensionality or different characteristics. A comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods, beyond those mentioned in the paper, would further strengthen the claims. The explanation of "Stable SGD" (SSGD) is brief; a clearer explanation of its rationale and implementation details would be helpful. Finally, the notation can be dense at times; more intuitive variable names or clearer explanations would improve readability.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem is a novel contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound and the efficient gradient computation are technically sound.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation and approach.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to greedy baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Related Work:** The related work section could be more comprehensive, especially regarding recent non-greedy methods.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** The complexity analysis lacks detailed breakdown of constant factors.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation could be expanded with more diverse datasets and comparisons to more recent state-of-the-art methods.
*   **SSGD Explanation:** The explanation of "Stable SGD" is brief and requires further clarification.
*   **Notation:** The notation is occasionally dense and could benefit from simplification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of traditional decision tree algorithms (greediness).
*   The proposed method offers a novel and theoretically sound approach to non-greedy decision tree learning.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement in performance over strong baselines.
*   The paper makes significant contributions to both the theoretical understanding and practical implementation of decision trees.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The related work section is not exhaustive, potentially overlooking relevant prior research.
*   The empirical evaluation, while positive, could be more comprehensive.
*   Certain aspects of the technical explanation (e.g., complexity analysis, SSGD) lack sufficient detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The novelty of the approach, combined with the solid theoretical foundation and promising experimental results, makes it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
