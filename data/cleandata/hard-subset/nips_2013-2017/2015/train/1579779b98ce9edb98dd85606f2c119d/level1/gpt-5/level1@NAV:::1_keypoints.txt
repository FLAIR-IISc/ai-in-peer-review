- Proposes a non-greedy training algorithm for oblique decision trees that jointly optimizes split functions and leaf parameters under a global surrogate objective.
- Reinterprets the vector of split decisions as latent variables, connecting tree learning to latent structured prediction.
- Derives a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss and optimizes it with SGD (with projection and SSGD stabilization).
- Introduces fast loss-augmented inference by restricting to a Hamming ball of radius one, reducing per-update complexity to O(d^2 p).
- Maintains standard O(dp) inference complexity at test time.
- Experiments on LibSVM multiclass datasets show consistent improvements over greedy axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique baselines, and a greedy depth-1 CO2 variant across depths.
- Formulation is technically sound; bounds and computational shortcuts are well motivated and algorithmic details are clear.
- Empirical scope is limited to single-tree settings and classic datasets; missing comparisons to modern strong baselines like gradient-boosted trees.
- Paper lacks stronger ablations (e.g., SSGD vs. SGD) and sensitivity analyses (initialization, ν).
- Clarity is generally good with helpful notation; some proofs are deferred and a schematic for the fast inference would help readers.
- Originality: latent-structured view plus convex-concave bound and Hamming-ball restriction is an elegant, novel combination in the literature on trees.
- Significance: offers a scalable, principled improvement for single-tree training but needs broader evaluations to confirm practical impact.
- Pros: principled non-greedy framework, efficient O(d^2 p) training with O(dp) inference, consistent empirical gains, loss-agnostic regularized formulation.
- Cons: limited empirical comparisons, light sensitivity/stability evaluation and SSGD assessment, and no theoretical generalization or convergence guarantees.
