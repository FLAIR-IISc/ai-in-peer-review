- Proposes a novel non-greedy decision tree training formulation by casting tree navigation as structured prediction with latent variables.  
- Derives a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss enabling joint optimization of all split functions instead of greedy, sequential splits.  
- Provides a proof (Proposition 1) about the effect of scaling on the bound, supporting the theoretical development.  
- Introduces an efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm reducing complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d² p) for deep trees.  
- Uses a Hamming-ball formulation to make loss-augmented inference tractable and well-justified.  
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) across multiple benchmarks.  
- Claims the method generalizes better and is less prone to overfitting than greedy approaches.  
- Builds on Bennett (1998) but extends to general losses and addresses computational intractability for global optimization.  
- Improves on CO2 (Norouzi et al., 2015) by optimizing tree structure non-greedily rather than node-wise greedy application.  
- Lacks thorough comparisons to other recent non-greedy tree optimization methods, limiting contextual novelty assessment.  
- Experimental section omits detailed evidence of scalability to very large datasets or very high-dimensional feature spaces.  
- Regularization parameter ν is tuned per dataset/depth with no discussion of automatic selection, reducing practical applicability.  
- Does not investigate the relationship between tree depth and number of leaves and its impact on utility.  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically sound, novel structured-prediction connection, significant efficient-inference contribution, and strong empirical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient scalability evidence, limited comparison to other non-greedy methods, and incomplete practical implementation details (ν tuning).  
- Overall recommendation: strong technical contribution, well-written, experimentally convincing, and recommended for acceptance.
