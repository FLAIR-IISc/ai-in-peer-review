

# Peer Review: "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper presents a novel approach to training decision trees by optimizing split functions at all levels of the tree jointly with leaf parameters, rather than using the traditional greedy approach. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is showing how to reduce the computational complexity of the gradient computation from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p), making the approach feasible for deep trees. The paper demonstrates that non-greedy decision trees outperform greedy baselines across multiple classification benchmarks.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors properly cite the foundational work on greedy decision trees (ID3, CART) and acknowledge prior attempts at non-greedy tree optimization (Bennett, 2003; Murthy & Salzburg, 1995), which were limited by computational complexity or restricted to specific loss functions. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables (Yu & Joachims, 2009; Taskar et al., 2003) is well-motivated and builds on recent advances in that area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with rigorous theoretical justification. The convex-concave upper bound is well-motivated and proven. The complexity analysis is thorough and convincing, showing how the proposed algorithm achieves O(d^2p) complexity, which is a significant improvement over the naive O(2^dp) approach. The authors provide a detailed explanation of the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm that enables efficient training.

2. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate clear improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST). The paper shows that non-greedy trees achieve better generalization performance and are less susceptible to overfitting. The ablation study on the regularization parameter ν provides valuable insights into how the method controls overfitting. The comparison with CO2 (a special case of their approach applied greedily) effectively isolates the contribution of non-greedy optimization.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The problem formulation, theoretical contributions, and algorithm description are clear and accessible to readers familiar with machine learning. The supplementary material provides additional details for interested readers.

4. **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of decision tree learning (greedy optimization) with a principled approach that can be applied to any loss function. The computational efficiency makes the method applicable to large-scale problems, and the consistent performance gains across multiple datasets demonstrate its practical value. The authors also identify promising directions for future work, such as learning sparse split functions and applying the kernel trick.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Connection to Structured Prediction:** While the connection to structured prediction is a key contribution, the paper could better explain why this connection is particularly useful for decision trees. The relationship to structured prediction is somewhat superficial, and the authors could have more explicitly connected their approach to specific structured prediction techniques.

2. **Comparison with Other Non-Greedy Methods:** The paper compares with several greedy baselines but doesn't adequately compare with other non-greedy methods beyond Bennett's work. While Bennett's method is limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss, it's not clear why the authors didn't explore more recent non-greedy approaches (e.g., Mondrian Forests by Lakshminarayanan et al., 2014, which also addresses non-greedy tree learning).

3. **Scalability with Dataset Size:** The paper shows that the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm scales better with tree depth, but doesn't provide a clear analysis of how the method scales with dataset size. The experiments are conducted on moderate-sized datasets (Connect4 has 55,000 training points), so it's unclear how the method would perform on very large datasets.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper makes a significant technical contribution by reducing the computational complexity of non-greedy tree optimization from exponential to polynomial.
- The method is general (works with any loss function) and demonstrates consistent performance improvements over greedy baselines.
- The connection to structured prediction is novel and well-motivated.
- The paper is well-written and thoroughly evaluated with experiments.

*Con:*
- The connection to structured prediction could be more deeply explored to better justify the approach.
- The comparison with other non-greedy methods is limited.
- The scalability analysis with dataset size is insufficient.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and significant contribution to decision tree learning. The authors successfully address a fundamental limitation of traditional decision tree induction by proposing a non-greedy optimization method that is both theoretically grounded and computationally efficient. The experimental results consistently demonstrate improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets. While there are some areas for improvement (particularly in the connection to structured prediction and comparison with other non-greedy methods), these do not undermine the paper's core contributions.

I recommend acceptance of this paper as it makes a clear and meaningful contribution to the field of machine learning, with potential applications in both academic research and industrial settings. The paper advances the state of the art in decision tree learning in a demonstrable way, and the results are likely to be of interest to a broad audience at NIPS.

**Rating: Strong Accept**
