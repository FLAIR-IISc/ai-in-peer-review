

# Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach for training decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently using stochastic gradient descent. The key insight is that the decision tree navigation process can be viewed as a latent variable problem, where the binary split decisions form a latent variable that determines the leaf node. The authors derive a surrogate objective that enables joint optimization of split functions across all tree levels, avoiding the sequential, greedy optimization of conventional decision tree algorithms.

The paper builds upon prior work on non-greedy tree optimization (Bennett, 1998), but makes significant improvements by addressing the computational intractability of global optimization. While Bennett's work is limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss and suffers from high computational complexity, this paper handles any general loss function while maintaining efficient O(d²p) complexity for deep trees through their fast loss-augmented inference algorithm. The authors also improve upon the CO2 method (Norouzi et al., 2015) by optimizing tree structure non-greedily rather than applying it greedily to each node.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is novel and well-motivated. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation showing how the decision tree navigation function can be expressed as a latent variable problem.
2. The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound (equation 7) is technically sound and provides a principled approach to optimizing the non-convex decision tree objective. The proof of Proposition 1 regarding the effect of scaling on the bound is particularly valuable.
3. The efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm (reducing complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p)) is a significant technical contribution that enables training of deep trees. The Hamming ball formulation (equation 13) is clever and well-justified.
4. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines (including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and random oblique trees) across multiple benchmark datasets. The non-greedy trees achieve better generalization performance and are less susceptible to overfitting.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper does not provide a thorough comparison with other non-greedy tree optimization methods beyond Bennett's work. While the authors cite related work, they don't clearly position their contribution against other recent approaches to non-greedy tree learning.
2. The experimental section lacks sufficient detail on computational scaling. While the paper claims O(d²p) complexity, the authors don't provide evidence of how the method scales with extremely large datasets or high-dimensional features.
3. The regularization parameter ν is tuned per dataset and depth, which may limit practical applicability. The paper doesn't discuss how to automatically select ν without validation data.
4. The paper doesn't explore the relationship between the depth of the tree and the number of leaves, which could affect the practical utility of the method for certain applications.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to a long-standing limitation in decision tree learning (greedy optimization).
- The technical contribution of the efficient inference algorithm (O(d²p) complexity) is significant and well-justified.
- The experimental results consistently show improvements over multiple greedy baselines across different datasets.
- The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and opens up new research directions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence of scalability to extremely large datasets (beyond the modest Connect4 dataset mentioned).
- The comparison with other non-greedy methods is limited, making it difficult to fully assess the novelty and significance of the contribution.
- The practical implementation details (particularly regarding the tuning of ν) could be more thoroughly discussed.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation of decision tree learning. The paper is well-written, the theoretical development is sound, and the experimental results are convincing. While the paper could benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with other non-greedy methods and better discussion of scalability, the core contribution represents a significant advance in decision tree optimization. The method's ability to jointly optimize all split functions with a global objective while maintaining computational efficiency is novel and valuable for the machine learning community. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is particularly insightful and could inspire further research in this area.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (quality), clearly written (clarity), presents a novel approach to decision tree learning (originality), and demonstrates significant improvements over baselines with potential for wide application (significance). I recommend acceptance.
