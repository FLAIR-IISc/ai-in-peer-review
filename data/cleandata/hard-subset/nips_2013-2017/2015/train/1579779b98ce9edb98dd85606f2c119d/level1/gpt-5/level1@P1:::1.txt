Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a non-greedy training method for decision trees with oblique (linear) splits by optimizing a global surrogate objective over all split and leaf parameters. The key idea is to recast the binary decisions at internal nodes as latent variables h ∈ {−1,+1}^m and to upper-bound the empirical loss with a convex–concave objective inspired by structured prediction with latent variables [24,25]. The loss-augmented inference term can be computed exactly. A naïve version would be O(2^d p), but the authors introduce a radius-1 Hamming-ball restriction that yields a tight surrogate and reduces per-example complexity to O(d^2 p), making the approach practical for deep trees. Optimization is carried out via SGD with per-row norm constraints on W (controlling the scale/“margin”), and a stable SGD (SSGD) variant keeps leaf assignments fixed for several iterations to prevent leaf inactivation. Experiments on multiple LibSVM multiclass datasets show consistent improvements over greedy axis-aligned trees (CART [5]), OC1 oblique trees [17], random oblique splits, and CO2 [18] (a depth-1 special case of the proposed bound applied greedily).

Relative to prior work: global tree optimization is known to be NP-complete [11]; previous non-greedy formulations (e.g., MILP, max-margin trees [1–3]) are either limited in scope (binary, 0–1 loss) or computationally heavy. Soft-split models (HME [13]) sacrifice the inference efficiency of hard trees. The paper’s contributions—latent-structured formulation, a tractable convex–concave upper bound with per-example O(d) gradient structure, and the fast loss-augmented inference—appear novel and connect tree induction to the structured SVM literature [23–25]. The empirical setup focuses on single trees, where the differences with greedy induction are most apparent.

Quality
- Soundness: The surrogate bound (Eq. 7) is standard in latent-structured formulations, and the paper carefully discusses its scale dependence (Proposition 1) and the need for norm constraints on W. The fast Hamming-ball bound (Eq. 13) is valid and yields the advertised complexity. The SGD updates and projection are correct; SSGD is a sensible stabilization. Some technical details are deferred to the supplement (proofs, exact loss-augmented inference). While the optimization is non-convex (as expected), the surrogate is piecewise linear and admits efficient per-example gradients.
- Empirics: Comparisons across four datasets and depths show consistent accuracy gains and reduced overfitting. The effect of ν is analyzed and tied to implicit pruning/regularization; runtime scaling of fast vs. full loss-augmented inference is reported. However, results lack variance/error bars and ablations (e.g., contribution of SSGD vs. SGD, effect of initialization). Comparisons are limited to single trees; ensembles are not explored.
- Limitations: No theoretical generalization guarantees beyond margin intuition; no convergence characterization beyond SGD practice; the method’s sensitivity to ν and learning-rate scheduling could be discussed more deeply.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The latent-variable framing, navigation function f, and the two bounds are clearly described; Algorithm 1 is helpful. Some minor points that could be clarified:
- Provide a concise worked example of loss-augmented inference on a small tree showing which bits differ and how the O(d^2 p) complexity arises.
- Make explicit how biases are handled (augmenting features with a constant −1 is mentioned, but restate in the algorithm for completeness).
- Specify the SSGD schedule (how many iterations leaf assignments are fixed; stopping criterion for “negligible improvement”).
- Discuss regularization on Θ (if any), and whether temperature scaling of the softmax was considered.

Originality
The connection of hard-decision trees to latent structured prediction with an efficiently computable convex–concave upper bound, together with the Hamming-ball restriction enabling O(d^2 p) training, is novel to my knowledge. CO2 [18] is a depth-1 special case; this work extends it to full trees with joint optimization and provides algorithmic advances (fast inference, SSGD).

Significance
Improving single-tree accuracy and stability matters for interpretability and for applications where ensembles are impractical. The method preserves O(d p) inference while enabling non-greedy global training, which is of broad interest. That said, many practitioners deploy forests/boosting; showing gains in an ensemble setting (e.g., as a drop-in for oblique splits in RF or as base learners in boosting) would strengthen practical impact. The approach could also inform learning of sparse or kernelized splits, as noted by the authors.

Pros
- Well-motivated and principled latent-structured formulation with clear ties to structural SVMs.
- Efficient and elegant fast loss-augmented inference; per-example gradient cost independent of number of leaves.
- Consistent empirical gains over strong greedy baselines; reduced overfitting via norm constraints.
- Maintains hard-split inference efficiency (O(d p)); scalable with SGD; applicable to general losses.

Cons
- Experiments limited to single trees on mid-scale datasets; no ensembles or comparison to modern boosted trees.
- Limited ablations (SSGD, initialization, ν sensitivity, effect of depth) and no statistical significance reporting.
- Theoretical guarantees limited to bounds and complexity; no convergence/generalization analysis.
- Some implementation details (SSGD schedule, Θ regularization, data preprocessing) are not fully specified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: (i) SGD vs. SSGD; (ii) different initializations (axis-aligned, random); (iii) ν sweep with test curves and active-leaf counts; (iv) impact of Hamming-ball radius (e.g., r=0,1,2) on accuracy vs. runtime.
- Report variance over multiple random splits and statistical significance.
- Compare to ensembles: (a) forests with oblique greedy vs. non-greedy trees as base learners; (b) boosted stumps vs. boosted non-greedy shallow trees; situate against GBRT/XGBoost where feasible.
- Provide runtime per epoch and total training time vs. baselines; memory footprint.
- Discuss or demonstrate sparse W via group-lasso to enhance interpretability and speed.
- Release code to enable reproducibility.

Overall recommendation
This is a strong paper with a principled, efficient non-greedy training method for oblique decision trees and solid empirical benefits. With expanded experiments (especially ensembles) it could be highly influential. I recommend acceptance.
