

# Peer Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

This paper presents an innovative approach to decision tree learning by formulating global optimization as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The authors establish a novel connection between decision tree induction and structured prediction, proposing a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss that can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity of gradient computation from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p), making deep tree learning feasible. The paper builds upon previous work on global tree optimization (Bennett, 1995) and structured prediction (Yu & Joachims, 2009), but significantly advances the state of the art by addressing the computational intractability of global optimization.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the connection between decision trees and structured prediction with latent variables. The convex-concave upper bound (Equation 7) is well-motivated and rigorously proven, providing a sound foundation for the optimization approach. The complexity reduction from exponential to polynomial time (O(d^2p)) is particularly impressive and addresses a fundamental barrier to non-greedy tree learning.

2. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments across multiple benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) consistently demonstrate superior performance over greedy baselines (including OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2). The paper's key finding—that non-greedy trees generalize better and are less prone to overfitting—holds across all datasets and tree depths.

3. **Practical Implementation:** The proposed Stable SGD (SSGD) variant effectively addresses a practical concern with non-greedy training (inactive leaves), making the method more robust for real-world applications. The algorithmic details are presented clearly with a well-structured pseudocode (Algorithm 1).

4. **Clear Differentiation from Prior Work:** The paper thoroughly contextualizes its contribution relative to previous approaches, particularly distinguishing itself from Bennett's (1995) work by addressing computational complexity and enabling deep tree learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison with Modern Alternatives:** The paper compares only with traditional greedy tree methods (OC1, axis-aligned trees) but omits comparison with more recent tree-based methods like XGBoost or LightGBM, which have become standard in machine learning practice. It would be valuable to see how the proposed method stacks up against these state-of-the-art ensemble methods.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** While the paper provides a solid optimization framework, it lacks theoretical guarantees on convergence or generalization bounds for the non-greedy approach. The paper acknowledges this limitation by noting that the optimization finds a "locally optimal configuration," but doesn't quantify the quality of this local optimum.

3. **Limited Discussion of Practical Limitations:** The paper doesn't thoroughly address when the non-greedy approach might be impractical (e.g., for extremely deep trees with many features). The computational complexity analysis focuses on the gradient computation, but doesn't discuss memory requirements or scaling with large feature spaces.

4. **Overlooked Related Work:** The paper references Murthy and Salzburg (1994) who argued against non-greedy approaches, but doesn't adequately address how their concerns about overfitting are mitigated by the proposed regularization scheme (the norm constraint on W).

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of decision tree learning by providing a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient approach to non-greedy tree optimization. The connection to structured prediction is novel and well-executed, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method over greedy alternatives.

The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The theoretical contribution is substantial, the implementation is practical, and the results are compelling. While the comparison with modern tree-based methods would strengthen the paper, the core contribution is valuable and addresses a long-standing limitation in decision tree learning.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction
- Significant computational complexity reduction (O(d^2p) vs O(2^dp))
- Consistent experimental improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets
- Practical algorithm with Stable SGD variant addressing implementation concerns
- Well-motivated regularization approach that improves generalization

**Arguments for Rejection (Minimal):**
- Limited comparison with modern tree-based methods (XGBoost, LightGBM)
- Lacks theoretical guarantees on convergence or generalization
- Doesn't fully address the overfitting concerns raised by Murthy and Salzburg

This paper advances the state of the art in decision tree learning in a meaningful way, and the method has clear potential for adoption in both research and practice. The authors have addressed a fundamental limitation of decision trees with a technically sound and computationally efficient approach.
