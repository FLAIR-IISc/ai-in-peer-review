Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a non-greedy training method for decision trees with oblique (linear) splits by optimizing a global surrogate objective over all internal split parameters and leaf distributions. The key idea is to recast tree routing decisions as a vector of binary latent variables and to upper bound the empirical loss via a convex-concave, structured-prediction–style loss with loss-augmented inference. The authors derive two related bounds: a full bound (requires maximizing over all binary code vectors) and a “fast” bound that restricts loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current routing, enabling O(d^2 p) per-example updates for depth d and feature dimension p. They optimize with (stable) stochastic gradient descent, using per-row norm constraints on the split weights to regularize and to prevent trivial tightening of the bound by scaling. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) show single-tree accuracy improvements over axis-aligned greedy trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique splits, and CO2 (a related greedy oblique approach). The work connects to: (a) classical greedy tree induction (ID3/CART), (b) non-greedy/MIQP or margin-based tree learning (Bennett et al.), (c) soft routing models (HME), and (d) structured SVMs with latent variables.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation linking sgn(Wx) to argmax over latent codes and the resulting structured upper bound are correct and well-motivated. The effect of scaling W and the need for norm constraints are clearly explained. The fast bound (restricting loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball) is an insightful compromise between tightness and tractability; the complexity analysis is careful.
- Empirical support: Results consistently favor the proposed non-greedy trees over the baselines across depths, with reduced overfitting. Training-time scaling with the fast bound is convincing. However, the experimental scope is limited to single trees on a handful of datasets; there is no statistical significance analysis, limited ablations (e.g., impact of Stable-SGD vs plain SGD, effect of initialization), and no regression tasks despite the general formulation.
- Baselines: Comparisons include strong greedy and oblique baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, CO2). That said, modern practice often deploys ensembles (random forests, boosted trees). While the authors justify focusing on single trees to reveal learning dynamics, it would be valuable to show that the method scales to forests and remains competitive against standard ensembles. Comparison to max-margin split-node methods (e.g., SVM-split trees) would also strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and generally clear. The latent-variable reformulation, the two bounds, and Algorithm 1 are easy to follow. Pointers to proofs are provided. Some areas for improvement:
- Provide more intuition for the navigation function f and explicit examples of how off-path bits cancel in Eq. (12).
- Explicitly report the accuracy impact of using the fast bound vs full bound (not just runtime).
- Detail all hyperparameters (learning rate schedule, momentum/minibatch sizes, ν search grid) and the Stable-SGD update schedule for reproducibility.

Originality
The work is original in combining: (1) a latent-structured upper bound tailored to tree routing, (2) a principled scale analysis and per-row norm constraints for oblique split learning, and (3) a fast loss-augmented inference restricted to a radius-1 Hamming neighborhood that retains O(d^2 p) complexity. Prior non-greedy approaches (e.g., MILP/LP relaxations, margin-based nodes) do not provide this particular surrogate or the scalable per-example gradient updates for deep trees. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is novel in the tree-learning context.

Significance
If one can train non-greedy oblique trees with per-example O(d^2 p) cost and improved generalization, this has practical value for large-scale classification where trees are favored for speed and interpretability. The improvements over strong greedy baselines are consistent, if sometimes modest. The approach could influence future work on globally training trees and forests, and the surrogate may be a foundation for kernelized or sparsity-regularized variants (as noted by the authors). Demonstrating benefits in forests and on larger vision tasks (e.g., pose estimation) would increase impact.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation tying decision tree learning to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient fast bound with rigorous complexity reduction; practical SGD algorithm with projection.
- Consistent empirical gains over several oblique and greedy baselines; reduced overfitting.
- Sensible regularization via per-node norm constraints; analysis of the bound’s scaling behavior.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited empirical scope: no ensembles, no regression, no significance tests. Suggest adding forests trained with the proposed objective (e.g., bagging with non-greedy trees) and comparing to random forests/gradient boosting.
- Ablations: quantify effects of Stable-SGD vs SGD; initialization sensitivity (axis-aligned vs random); impact of ν on accuracy vs pruning; accuracy vs runtime trade-off of full vs fast bound.
- Baseline breadth: include margin-based split-node trees and a modern boosted tree baseline (even if ensembles).
- Theory: beyond optimization properties, provide generalization or margin-based analysis (e.g., relating ν to margins or Rademacher complexity).
- Reproducibility: release code or provide detailed training protocols.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated surrogate and efficient solver for globally training oblique trees.
- Theoretical clarity on bound properties and practical speedups.
- Consistent improvements across datasets and depths; method reduces overfitting.
- Broad relevance to ML areas where trees are widely used.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is somewhat narrow; missing key comparisons to ensembles and some related non-greedy methods.
- Limited ablation and sensitivity analysis; fast vs full bound accuracy not quantified.
- No regression experiments despite claiming generality.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The methodological contribution is solid and, with modest extensions to the empirical section (especially ensembles and ablations), could be a strong accept. Confidence: medium.
