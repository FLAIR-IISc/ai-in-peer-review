This paper proposes a non-greedy training algorithm for oblique decision trees that jointly optimizes all split functions and leaf parameters under a global surrogate objective. The key idea is to reinterpret the vector of split decisions as latent variables, linking decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables (cf. Yu & Joachims, ICML’09; Tsochantaridis et al., ICML’04; Taskar et al., NIPS’03). The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and optimize it with SGD. A naïve loss-augmented inference would be exponential in tree depth; they introduce a fast alternative by restricting to a Hamming ball of radius one around the current path, reducing per-update complexity to O(d^2 p). Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets show consistent improvements over greedy axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique baselines, and a greedy depth-1 bound variant (CO2), with better generalization across depths. The approach preserves standard O(dp) inference at test time.

Quality: The formulation is technically sound, the bound is well motivated, and the computational shortcuts are carefully justified. The algorithmic details (SGD with projection and the SSGD stabilization) are clear, and complexity claims are plausible. Empirical results are positive, though limited to single-tree settings and classic datasets. The paper would benefit from stronger ablations (e.g., impact of SSGD vs. SGD, sensitivity to initialization and ν) and comparisons to modern strong baselines such as gradient-boosted trees.

Clarity: The manuscript is generally clear and well organized, with helpful notation and a concrete algorithm. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement; a brief intuition for Proposition 1 in the main text is appreciated. A schematic of the fast loss-augmented inference would aid readers.

Originality: The latent-structured view of tree splits and the convex-concave upper bound extend prior greedy CO2 work to joint non-greedy optimization, and the Hamming-ball restriction is an elegant, practical innovation. This combination appears novel in the NIPS literature on trees.

Significance: Improving single-tree training in a scalable, principled way is useful and may influence oblique trees and forest methods. Demonstrated gains across depths suggest practical impact, though broader comparisons and larger-scale benchmarks would strengthen claims.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled non-greedy framework with clear links to latent structured prediction
- Efficient O(d^2 p) training and preserved O(dp) inference
- Consistent empirical gains over strong greedy baselines
- Simple, general loss-agnostic formulation with regularization

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited empirical scope; missing comparisons to boosted trees and recent ensembles
- Sensitivity and stability analyses are light; SSGD heuristic not deeply evaluated
- No theoretical generalization guarantees or convergence analysis beyond heuristics
