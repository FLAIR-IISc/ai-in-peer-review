## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper tackles the well-known limitation of greedy algorithms in decision tree induction – their tendency to produce suboptimal trees. The authors propose a novel algorithm for jointly optimizing split functions at all levels of the tree, alongside leaf parameters, based on a global objective. They establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss. A key contribution is reducing the gradient computation complexity to O(d), enabling training of deep trees with SGD. Experiments on benchmark datasets demonstrate improved performance compared to greedy baselines.

The work builds upon decades of research in decision trees (Quinlan ‘93, Breiman et al. ‘84) and addresses a long-standing challenge – escaping local optima inherent in greedy approaches (Bennett ‘97, Murthy & Salzberg ‘95). The connection to structured prediction (Taskar et al. ‘04, Yu & Joachims ‘09) is insightful and allows leveraging techniques from that field. The authors appropriately position their work against related approaches like multi-linear programming (Bennett ‘01), Mondrian Processes (Lakshminarayanan et al. ‘15), and Hierarchical Mixture of Experts (Jordan & Jacobs ‘94), highlighting the advantages of their method in terms of efficiency and generality.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear motivation and a well-defined optimization problem. The reduction in gradient computation complexity is a significant contribution, making non-greedy optimization feasible for deeper trees. The experimental results consistently show improvements over greedy baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical analysis is present, the justification for certain design choices, such as the specific form of the convex-concave upper bound, could be further elaborated. The experiments, while demonstrating improvement, are somewhat limited in scope. Comparing against more recent and sophisticated tree-based methods (e.g., gradient boosted trees) would strengthen the evaluation. The discussion of hyperparameter tuning (specifically ν) feels somewhat brief, and a more detailed ablation study exploring its impact would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Addresses a fundamental limitation of standard decision tree algorithms.
* Presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach.
* Achieves demonstrable improvements in performance on benchmark datasets.
* Offers a significant efficiency improvement through gradient complexity reduction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
* Limited scope of experimental comparison.
* Justification for certain design choices could be more detailed.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis could be more thorough.

**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The technical novelty, combined with the empirical results, warrants acceptance. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
