This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree optimization, framing it as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The core idea is to jointly optimize split functions at all levels of the tree, using a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and stochastic gradient descent. The authors establish a connection to structured prediction, derive an efficient gradient computation (O(d) where d is tree depth), and demonstrate improved performance on several benchmark datasets compared to greedy baselines.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the surrogate objective and the analysis of its gradient computation are convincing. The claim of O(d) gradient computation (after the optimization to O(d2p) is a significant contribution enabling scalability to deeper trees. The experimental results support the claim that the non-greedy approach outperforms greedy baselines. However, the experiments could be strengthened with a more thorough ablation study (e.g., impact of the regularization parameter ν), and a comparison to more recent, sophisticated tree-based methods beyond those mentioned (e.g., gradient boosted trees). The authors acknowledge limitations regarding the computational complexity for very deep trees despite the optimization, which demonstrates honesty. The use of existing datasets (LibSVM) is appropriate. The paper isn’t just a position paper; it delivers a concrete algorithm and experimental validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the technical details are presented in a logical manner. However, some sections, particularly those detailing the mathematical derivations, can be dense and might benefit from more intuitive explanations. The notation is consistent, but the introduction of several variables requires careful reading. The algorithm is described clearly, but a pseudocode representation (like Algorithm 1) could have been placed earlier to aid comprehension.

**Originality:** The idea of jointly optimizing decision tree splits is not entirely new (referenced in the related work: Bennett [1]), but the formulation using structured prediction with latent variables and the efficient gradient computation represent a novel contribution. The connection to structured prediction is a smart move, enabling the application of techniques from that field to decision tree learning.  The paper appropriately references related work, although the breadth of the related work section could be expanded to include more recent advances in tree-based methods (e.g. deep learning based tree algorithms). The key innovation lies in making non-greedy optimization feasible for relatively deep trees through the proposed optimization of the surrogate objective.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate a clear improvement over greedy baselines, suggesting the potential for improved accuracy in decision tree-based models. The ability to train deeper, more complex trees efficiently is a significant advance. The approach is applicable to any general loss function, providing flexibility. While the performance gains may not be revolutionary, they are statistically significant and could be valuable in applications where accuracy is paramount. The approach addresses a longstanding limitation of decision trees – their susceptibility to suboptimal solutions due to greedy optimization – and offers a promising direction for future research. The contribution is likely to be of interest to researchers working on decision trees, structured prediction, and optimization.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem.
*   Efficient gradient computation allows for training deeper trees.
*   Empirically demonstrated improvement over greedy baselines.
*   Sound technical analysis and derivation of the surrogate objective.
*   Addresses a long-standing limitation of standard decision tree algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experiments could be more comprehensive (e.g., ablation study, comparison to more recent methods).
*   Some sections of the paper are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
*   The performance gains, while statistically significant, are not enormous.
*   Limited discussion of the potential impact of hyperparameter tuning.



**Overall Assessment:**  I believe this paper is a strong contribution and should be accepted. The technical novelty, coupled with the empirical results, justifies inclusion in NIPS.  The paper addresses a challenging problem with a well-motivated and technically sound approach. While there is room for improvement in the experimental analysis and clarity of certain sections, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses.
