This paper presents a novel approach to training decision trees by jointly optimizing split functions at all levels of the tree, moving away from the traditional greedy approach. The core idea is to establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss. The authors demonstrate that the gradient computation scales favorably with tree depth, enabling training of deep trees with SGD. Experimental results show improved performance compared to greedy baselines.

The paper tackles a long-standing problem in machine learning – the suboptimality of greedy decision tree induction. The connection to structured prediction is insightful and allows leveraging techniques from that field. The formulation of the convex-concave upper bound is a key contribution, as it enables efficient optimization via SGD. The reduction of gradient complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is crucial for scalability, and the experimental results, while not overwhelming, demonstrate the potential of the approach. The paper builds upon and references relevant work in decision tree learning, structured prediction, and related areas (e.g., [1, 2, 4, 5, 11, 21, 25]).  The inclusion of both theoretical analysis (gradient complexity) and empirical evaluation strengthens the paper.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The non-greedy approach to decision tree optimization is a significant departure from established methods.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The connection to structured prediction and the derivation of the convex-concave upper bound are well-motivated and theoretically justified. The analysis of gradient complexity is convincing.
* **Scalability:** The reduction in computational complexity to O(d2p) makes training deep trees feasible.
* **Empirical Validation:** Experiments demonstrate performance improvements over greedy baselines on several datasets.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and explains complex concepts in a reasonably accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Results:** While the paper demonstrates improvements, the gains are not dramatic. A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods (beyond the greedy baselines) would strengthen the conclusions.  The performance differences shown in Fig. 2 are relatively small.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The regularization parameter ν seems crucial for performance, and the sensitivity to its value (as shown in Fig. 3) raises concerns about practical usability. A more in-depth discussion of hyperparameter tuning and robustness would be beneficial.
* **Initialization:** The method relies on initializing with a greedy tree (CO2). The impact of this initialization on the final results is not fully explored. Would the performance be similar starting from random split functions?
* **Limited Scope:** The paper primarily focuses on binary classification trees.  While extending to N-ary trees is mentioned, the limitations for regression tasks are not explicitly discussed.
* **Algorithmic Details:** Some aspects of the SSGD algorithm are not fully detailed, making it difficult to fully reproduce the results. For example, how is the "negligible improvement" in SSGD quantitatively determined?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to decision tree learning.
* The computational analysis demonstrates the feasibility of scaling to deep trees.
* The empirical results, though modest, provide evidence of improved performance.
* The work addresses a significant challenge in machine learning – overcoming the limitations of greedy decision tree induction.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The experimental results are not particularly compelling and do not demonstrate a substantial breakthrough.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity raises concerns about practical applicability.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is limited.
* Some algorithmic details are lacking, hindering reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The approach is innovative, the theoretical analysis is strong, and the empirical results are promising. However, the relatively modest performance gains and the sensitivity to hyperparameters prevent me from giving it a strong recommendation. I would rate this paper as a **weak accept**.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly expanding the experimental evaluation and providing a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter tuning – would significantly strengthen the paper. A clear discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future work would also be beneficial.
