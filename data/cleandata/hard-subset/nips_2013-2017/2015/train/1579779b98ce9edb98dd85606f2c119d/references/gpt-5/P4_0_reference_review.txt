Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy method to train decision trees by jointly optimizing split functions under a global objective, rather than the conventional greedy, node-by-node approach. The key idea is to recast hard, oblique splits sgn(Wx) as latent binary variables h ∈ {−1,+1}^m, linking the problem to structured prediction with latent variables. The empirical loss is upper-bounded by a convex–concave surrogate (loss-augmented inference minus inference), which is optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with per-row norm constraints on W. A direct implementation would require O(2^d p) time per update; the paper introduces a modified bound restricting loss-augmented configurations to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing the per-step complexity to O(d^2 p), while keeping inference at test time O(dp). A “Stable SGD” variant freezes leaf assignments for several steps to prevent leaves from becoming inactive. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets and MNIST compare to axis-aligned CART, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and the CO2 method (a greedy depth-1 special case). The proposed method yields higher test accuracy across depths, appears less prone to overfitting, and scales more favorably in depth than the naive loss-augmented inference.

Relation to prior work
The approach contrasts with classical greedy induction (ID3, CART) and with soft-gated models (Hierarchical Mixture of Experts) that lose inference efficiency. It differs from earlier global optimization attempts (e.g., Bennett’s MILP) by being scalable and applicable beyond 0–1 loss. It relates to max-margin trees and structural SVMs with latent variables; the upper bound and loss-augmented inference mirror latent structural SVM formulations, and the representation sgn(Wx) = argmax_h h^T Wx has appeared in hashing. The CO2 method is positioned as a greedy special case; here the full tree is optimized jointly.

Strengths
- Principled connection to latent structured prediction with a clear surrogate objective and complexity analysis.
- Maintains hard splits and O(dp) inference while providing a non-greedy training framework.
- Efficient training via the Hamming ball bound yields O(d^2 p) updates; accompanied by an implementable SGD algorithm with projected per-row norm constraints.
- Empirical results across multiple datasets show consistent improvements over strong greedy baselines and reduced overfitting with increasing depth.
- The regularization via per-row norm constraints is well-motivated by the scale-sensitivity of the bound and provides a tunable capacity control.

Weaknesses
- The fast training uses a tighter neighborhood (radius-1 Hamming ball), which may substantially loosen the bound and could limit the benefits of joint optimization; no analysis of the trade-off is provided.
- Optimization remains non-convex; performance appears sensitive to initialization and heuristics (SSGD), with limited ablations quantifying these effects.
- Experimental scope is limited: mainly single-tree performance on standard datasets; no regression experiments despite claims of generality; no statistical significance testing; limited scalability evidence beyond moderate-depth trees and medium-sized datasets.
- Comparisons omit widely used modern baselines such as boosted trees/GBDT and random forests in terms of accuracy vs. compute, which are relevant for practical significance.
- Some proofs and details are deferred to supplementary material; SSGD schedule and hyperparameters are not fully specified, which may impair reproducibility.

Evaluation by criteria
Quality: The derivation of the surrogate bound and the complexity reduction are technically sound and aligned with latent structural SVM practice. The scale dependence of the bound and the choice of per-row norm constraints are correctly reasoned. The proposed SGD with projections is standard. However, theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence properties, generalization bounds) are not provided, and the impact of the radius-1 restriction on optimality is not quantified. The SSGD heuristic is plausible but lacks analysis.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized and clear. Notation is heavy but consistent; the navigation function f, bound, and algorithms are described sufficiently to reimplement. The fast loss-augmented inference could benefit from more explicit pseudo-code and a clearer explanation of which nodes are revisited during the depth-first search. Some references are missing years, and key proofs are deferred.

Originality: The non-greedy joint training via a latent structured prediction surrogate, combined with an efficient loss-augmented inference restricted to a Hamming ball, is a novel and nontrivial contribution beyond greedy oblique trees and beyond the CO2 depth-1 approach. The representation of splits via maximization over binary codes is known, but its use to globally train trees at scale appears original.

Significance: For single trees, the method offers measurable gains and a principled training framework that preserves inference efficiency, which is valuable in settings where small, fast trees are needed. The impact is tempered by the dominance of ensembles (forests/boosting) in applications and by the limited empirical scope. Nonetheless, the framework could inspire further advances (e.g., sparsity, kernels) and may be adopted in scenarios where compact models are critical.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide ablations on: initialization (axis-aligned vs random), effect of SSGD vs SGD, and sensitivity to ν and learning rate.
- Analyze the trade-off between Hamming radius and accuracy/runtime (e.g., radius 2).
- Include regression experiments to substantiate generality.
- Report statistical significance and confidence intervals; add wall-clock vs accuracy plots across methods.
- Clarify SSGD schedule (how many steps per assignment freeze) and batch/momentum settings; consider releasing code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, well-motivated formulation linking trees to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient algorithm with clear complexity improvements and preserved inference efficiency.
- Consistent empirical gains over multiple competitive greedy baselines.
- Potential to influence future work on globally training discrete hierarchical models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical scope and missing comparisons to strong modern baselines (e.g., boosted trees/forests).
- Heuristic components (SSGD) and sensitivity to initialization not fully analyzed.
- Theoretical guarantees and analysis of the bound looseness (radius-1) are lacking.
- Reproducibility could be improved with more implementation details.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
