## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for non-greedy learning of decision trees, addressing a long-standing limitation of traditional algorithms. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating the tree’s empirical loss using binary latent variables representing split decisions. They derive a convex-concave upper bound on this loss and propose optimizing it using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution lies in reducing the computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) for deep trees, enabling efficient learning. The work is accompanied by experimental validation on benchmark datasets, demonstrating improved performance and generalization compared to greedy baselines.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself within a body of work attempting to move beyond the inherent limitations of greedy decision tree induction (e.g., Bennett [1, 2, 3], Murthy & Salzburg [17]). It differentiates itself from previous non-greedy approaches like Bennett’s multi-linear programming by focusing on efficiency and scalability, handling general loss functions and maintaining the O(dp) inference complexity characteristic of trees. Unlike soft-split approaches (e.g., [13]), it preserves the computational advantages of hard splits.  The use of structured prediction with latent variables is inspired by recent advances in that field ([23, 24, 25]), but applied to the specific context of decision trees in a novel way. The work also relates to online learning of decision forests [15], but focuses on optimizing a single tree's structure rather than incremental growth.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a genuinely novel idea. This allows leveraging techniques from structured prediction to optimize the tree globally.
* **Efficiency:** The reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a significant technical achievement, making the proposed method applicable to deeper trees that would be intractable with the original formulation.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound provides a solid theoretical basis for the optimization process.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on multiple datasets demonstrate consistently better performance than greedy baselines, highlighting the benefits of non-greedy optimization. The ablation study investigating the regularization parameter ν is insightful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The explanations of the key concepts are clear, and the mathematical notation is well-defined.
* **Addressing Overfitting:** The inclusion of norm regularization provides a natural and effective mechanism to prevent overfitting, and the discussion around the impact of regularization strength is well-motivated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the authors achieve a reduction in computational complexity, the algorithm remains relatively complex to implement compared to standard greedy methods. The detailed explanation of the algorithm, specifically Alg 1, reveals a number of steps requiring careful consideration.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The paper mentions initializing the weights from a greedy tree (CO2 [18]). The dependence on the initial greedy tree might limit the exploration of the solution space. Investigating alternative initialization strategies could be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The effectiveness of the method appears sensitive to hyperparameter tuning (η and ν).  The paper mentions tuning these for each depth, but more discussion of how these parameters were chosen and their sensitivity would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to Ensemble Methods:** The comparison primarily focuses on single trees. While the authors acknowledge the benefits of ensembles (bagging, boosting), a comparison against a strong, well-tuned random forest baseline would provide a more complete evaluation of the method’s performance in a practical setting.
* **Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses on successes. A brief discussion of cases where the non-greedy approach *doesn't* outperform greedy methods, and potential reasons why, would strengthen the analysis.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a fundamentally new approach to decision tree learning with a strong theoretical foundation.
* The demonstrated improvements in accuracy and generalization performance are significant and consistent across multiple datasets.
* The reduction in computational complexity makes non-greedy optimization feasible for deeper trees.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The implementation complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity could hinder widespread adoption.
* The lack of comparison to strong ensemble methods leaves a gap in the evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The authors successfully address a fundamental limitation of traditional algorithms and demonstrate the benefits of non-greedy optimization through both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. While the complexity of implementation and hyperparameter tuning are potential drawbacks, the potential benefits in terms of accuracy and generalization warrant acceptance. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. I recommend the authors address the points raised regarding hyperparameter tuning sensitivity, initialization, and comparison to ensemble methods to further strengthen the paper.  A brief discussion of failure cases would also be valuable.
