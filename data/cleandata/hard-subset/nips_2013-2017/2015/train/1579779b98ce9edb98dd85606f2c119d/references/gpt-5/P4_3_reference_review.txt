Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees that jointly optimizes all split parameters under a global objective. The key idea is to recast hard split decisions as latent binary variables, linking tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables. The empirical loss is upper-bounded by a convex-concave surrogate involving a loss-augmented inference term. The authors show (i) a generic max-based upper bound on the per-example loss, (ii) a norm constraint on per-node split weights to regularize the bound and avoid degenerate scaling, (iii) an efficient loss-augmented inference scheme by restricting to a Hamming-1 neighborhood around current routing, which reduces the per-update complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p), and (iv) a “Stable SGD” variant that temporarily fixes leaf assignments to prevent collapse into inactive leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare single non-greedy oblique trees to greedy axis-aligned and oblique baselines (including OC1 and CO2), showing improved test accuracy and reduced overfitting across depths. Initialization uses a greedy procedure; hyperparameters are tuned on validation data.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on classical greedy tree induction (ID3/CART) and oblique splits (OC1), connects to max-margin/structured SVM formulations (MMMN, SSVM, and latent SSVM), and to prior attempts at non-greedy global tree optimization (e.g., multi-linear programming and margin-based trees). In contrast to soft trees/Hierarchical Mixture of Experts, the method preserves hard splits and O(d p) inference at test time. The CO2 method is a closely related special case applied greedily at depth-1; this paper’s contribution is global joint training and an efficient loss-augmented inference procedure.

Strengths
- Clear and principled formulation that connects hard-split tree training to latent structured prediction, allowing use of surrogate upper bounds and SGD.
- Sound derivation of an upper bound, with a computationally efficient variant via Hamming-1 restriction that makes deep trees tractable (O(d^2 p) per update).
- Practical training algorithm with projection-based per-node norm constraints; the regularization role of ν is motivated and empirically explored.
- Stable SGD variant addresses a real optimization pathology (inactive leaves).
- Empirical results on multiple datasets show consistent gains over strong greedy baselines across depths, with reduced overfitting.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical analysis is limited to bound validity and scaling behavior; there are no convergence guarantees for the non-convex optimization or generalization bounds.
- The fast bound (Hamming-1) is looser; the empirical accuracy-vs-speed trade-off between the exact and fast loss-augmented inference is only shown for runtime, not for final accuracy.
- Experimental scope is confined to single trees; practical significance would be clearer with ensembles (forests/boosting) or stronger modern baselines.
- Reproducibility details are sparse: exact hyperparameter search spaces for baselines, stopping criteria, momentum/minibatch settings, and code availability are not specified.
- Comparisons omit some relevant oblique or soft decision tree variants and modern optimized tree learners.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The max-based upper bounds (both global and Hamming-1 restricted) are correct, and the optimization procedure using subgradients/projections is standard for such non-convex surrogates. The complexity analysis is careful. The SSGD variant is reasonable, though lacking formal analysis.
- Empirics: Plots indicate consistent gains over baselines across depths. However, the paper would benefit from numerical tables, variance/error bars, and stronger/wider baselines (e.g., gradient boosting trees, extremely randomized trees, or modern oblique-tree methods). Ablations quantifying the impact of ν, initialization, SSGD, and the fast bound on accuracy are limited.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The notation for f, h, and the mapping to leaves is clear. Algorithm 1 is helpful.
- The fast loss-augmented inference procedure could use explicit pseudocode and a more detailed explanation of how the d+1 candidate leaves are enumerated and evaluated.
- Provide more guidance on setting ν, η, batch size, and momentum. Explain early stopping and convergence criteria for SGD/SSGD.
- Include a short discussion on the effect of initialization (axis-aligned + CO2) on final results.

Originality
- The latent-structured formulation for hard trees, the specific surrogate and its scalable Hamming-1 inference, and the SSGD heuristic are novel combinations. Prior work addressed global optimization or soft splits but did not provide this particular efficient bound and training scheme for hard oblique trees.

Significance
- Improving single-tree training is relevant; the method maintains fast inference and can, in principle, be extended to forests. The Hamming-1 inference makes deep-tree joint training computationally feasible, which is impactful. Demonstrated gains are promising, but broader comparisons and ensemble results would strengthen practical significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report accuracy impact of using exact vs fast loss-augmented inference to quantify the tightness-speed trade-off.
- Provide ablations: with/without SSGD; different ν; different initializations; Hamming radius >1 for intermediate trade-offs.
- Add variance/error bars over multiple random seeds/splits and numerical tables in addition to plots.
- Compare to stronger modern baselines (e.g., gradient boosting trees, extremely randomized trees), and consider a forest built from non-greedy trees to assess scalability/improvements in ensemble settings.
- Clarify hyperparameter tuning ranges and fairness for baselines; release code if possible.
- Discuss related soft/differentiable decision trees and neural decision forests, and contrast computational and accuracy trade-offs.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, well-motivated formulation with a concrete efficiency advance (O(d^2 p) training).
- Consistent empirical improvements over multiple baselines across depths.
- Maintains hard-split inference efficiency; broadly applicable to classification/regression losses.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond bound validity; no convergence or generalization theory.
- Experimental scope omits strong modern tree baselines and ensembles.
- Looser fast bound’s effect on final accuracy is not quantified; reproducibility details are limited.

Scope
- The paper is in scope for NeurIPS: learning algorithms, structured prediction, and scalable optimization for tree models.
