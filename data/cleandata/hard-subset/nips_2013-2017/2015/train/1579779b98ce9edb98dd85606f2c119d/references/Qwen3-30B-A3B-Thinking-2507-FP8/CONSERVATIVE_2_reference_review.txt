

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach for learning decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and developing an efficient optimization procedure using stochastic gradient descent. The authors claim their method jointly optimizes split functions across the entire tree rather than greedily optimizing node by node, potentially leading to better generalization and more compact trees. They introduce a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently with O(d²p) complexity, rather than the naive O(2ᵈp) complexity.

The paper's main strengths include a technically sound mathematical formulation that connects decision tree learning to structured prediction, a theoretically justified upper bound derivation, and a clever modification of loss-augmented inference that reduces computational complexity. The experimental evaluation on standard benchmark datasets shows modest performance improvements over greedy baselines, with the non-greedy trees demonstrating better generalization across tree depths.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's quality, significance, and validation that prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed method offers meaningful advantages over established greedy approaches for practical applications. The performance gains shown in Figure 2 are modest (typically 1-2% accuracy improvements), and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate why these small improvements are significant given the substantially increased methodological complexity. 

The experimental evaluation is inadequate for a NIPS paper. The authors test on only four benchmark datasets from LibSVM, without comparing against more recent state-of-the-art methods for decision tree learning. The paper claims to outperform CO2 [18], but CO2 is described as "a special case of our upper bound approach applied greedily," making this comparison less meaningful. More importantly, the paper lacks comparison with modern tree-based methods like XGBoost, LightGBM, or CatBoost, which have become the standard for tree-based learning in practice. The paper also fails to address scalability to larger datasets beyond the limited Connect4 dataset (55,000 training points).

The paper's claims about computational efficiency are misleading. While the authors reduce the complexity from O(2ᵈp) to O(d²p), they still require performing loss-augmented inference for each stochastic gradient update. The experimental results in Figure 4 show only a modest speedup factor, and the paper does not provide runtime comparisons with greedy methods on larger datasets or with more complex trees. The paper does not address whether the improved theoretical complexity translates to practical runtime improvements, which is essential for a method claiming to be "efficient."

The paper's theoretical contribution is also questionable. The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction is interesting but not novel. The authors cite Bennett [1] who proposed a non-greedy multi-linear programming approach for global tree optimization, though the paper dismisses it as limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss. The paper does not clearly differentiate their approach from Bennett's work or demonstrate why their method overcomes the limitations Bennett identified.

The paper's significance is limited. Decision trees remain popular for interpretability and efficiency, but the field has largely moved toward ensemble methods (random forests, gradient boosting) that inherently mitigate the limitations of greedy tree induction. The paper's focus on single trees without addressing how their method might integrate into ensembles is a missed opportunity. The modest performance gains shown do not justify the complexity of the proposed method for practitioners who would likely prefer established, well-validated ensemble approaches.

The paper lacks sufficient discussion of the method's limitations. It doesn't address how the regularization parameter ν affects performance across different datasets or how sensitive the method is to initialization. The paper also doesn't provide any analysis of the interpretability of the resulting trees, which is a key advantage of decision trees over black-box methods.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a technically sound mathematical formulation and makes an interesting connection to structured prediction, it fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence that the proposed method offers meaningful advantages over existing approaches for practical applications. The experimental validation is limited, the comparison with relevant baselines is inadequate, and the claimed benefits (better generalization, efficiency) are not convincingly demonstrated. The paper represents a incremental contribution at best, and the modest performance improvements do not justify the increased methodological complexity.

I recommend rejection. The paper would be significantly stronger if it included comparisons with modern tree-based methods, provided more detailed runtime analysis on larger datasets, and clearly demonstrated why the proposed method offers meaningful advantages over established greedy approaches for practical applications.
