This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy learning of decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and deriving a tractable surrogate objective. The core idea – moving beyond the inherently myopic nature of greedy tree induction by jointly optimizing splits at different levels – is a strong one and directly addresses a long-standing limitation in the field. The connection to structured prediction is insightful, and the complexity reduction from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a significant technical contribution enabling scalability to deeper trees.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The non-greedy approach to decision tree learning is a genuinely novel direction. While non-greedy methods have been explored (as acknowledged in the related work), the formulation via structured prediction with latent variables and the resulting optimization framework feel fresh and promising.
* **Theoretical Contribution:** The formulation and the complexity reduction result represent solid theoretical contributions. Linking decision trees to structured prediction is a non-trivial insight.
* **Potential Impact:** If successful, a truly effective non-greedy approach could lead to more compact, generalizable, and accurate decision trees, potentially revitalizing their use in areas where they’ve been superseded by more complex models. The demonstrated improvements over CO2 in the experiments hint at this potential.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, formulation, and algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Validation:** While the experiments demonstrate improvements over baselines, the gains are not overwhelming. The datasets used are relatively small and well-studied. The comparisons to strong, highly-optimized random forest implementations (beyond just the simple baselines) are missing. Deeper analysis into *why* the non-greedy approach works (e.g., visualization of learned splits, analysis of tree structure) would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The regularization parameter ν appears critical, and the paper doesn't fully address the sensitivity of the method to its tuning. The reliance on validation set performance for choosing ν raises concerns about potential overfitting to the validation data.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on classification. Exploring the performance on regression tasks, as mentioned in the paper, would broaden the impact.
* **Practical Considerations:** The algorithmic complexity, while improved, is still potentially significant for very high-dimensional data or extremely deep trees. A more detailed discussion of the practical limitations and scalability bottlenecks would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a fundamental limitation of decision tree learning with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The complexity reduction is a significant engineering achievement, making the non-greedy optimization more practical.
* The experiments, while not conclusive, provide initial evidence of improved performance.
* The connection to structured prediction opens up avenues for further research and potential integration with other structured prediction techniques. 

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The experimental results are not a dramatic improvement over existing methods.
* The method’s sensitivity to hyperparameters (particularly ν) could limit its practical applicability.
* The scope of experimental validation is somewhat limited.
* The potential scalability issues for very large datasets are not thoroughly addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a genuinely novel idea. While the experimental validation is not overwhelmingly convincing, the theoretical contributions and potential impact are significant. I lean towards acceptance, prioritizing the innovative nature of the work and the clear articulation of a promising new research direction. The authors should be encouraged to address the limitations identified in the review, particularly through more extensive experimentation and analysis, in future work.  I believe the potential benefits of this approach outweigh the current limitations.



**Rating:** Accept (with encouragement for future work)
