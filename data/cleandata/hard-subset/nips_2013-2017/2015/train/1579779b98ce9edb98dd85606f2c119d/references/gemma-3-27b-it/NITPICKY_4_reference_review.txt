This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framed as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The core idea is to introduce a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The authors address the computational complexity that arises from the exponential growth of latent variables with tree depth, reducing it from O(2dp) to O(d2p). They present experimental results on benchmark datasets, demonstrating improved performance compared to greedy approaches and a related method (CO2).

While the idea of framing decision tree learning as structured prediction is intriguing and potentially valuable, and the complexity reduction is a significant technical contribution, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings, particularly regarding clarity, detail, and justification of choices.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Formulation:** The connection to structured prediction with latent variables provides a fresh perspective on decision tree learning.
*   **Complexity Reduction:** The reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a non-trivial achievement and is crucial for scaling to deeper trees.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments suggest that the proposed method can outperform greedy approaches.
*   **Regularization:** The inclusion of norm regularization is a sensible approach to prevent overfitting.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Detail:** The paper lacks clarity in several crucial areas. For example, the initial motivation regarding the limitations of greedy algorithms feels somewhat overstated. It's not entirely clear *why* the inability to "fine-tune splits based on results at lower levels" is such a fundamental limitation without a more detailed explanation.
*   **Missing Justification:** The choice of the specific upper bound (Equation 7) is not adequately justified. Why is *this* bound superior to other possible bounds? A discussion of the trade-offs involved in choosing this particular form would be beneficial.
*   **Algorithm 1 Ambiguity:** Alg 1 is poorly detailed. “Initialize W (0) and Θ(0) using greedy procedure” lacks specificity. Which greedy procedure? What are the initialization parameters? Further, the update rule for W in Line 6, while presented, does not include information on how ĝ and ĥ are computed. It simply states that they are computed without detailing how.
*   **SSGD Implementation Details:** The description of Stable SGD (SSGD) is even more vague than the description of the standard SGD. The criteria for determining when the leaf assignment variables need updating is unclear. "When the improvement in the bound becomes negligible" is subjective and lacks a quantifiable definition.
*   **Experimental Setup Concerns:** The experiments are insufficient. The comparisons to baselines are not comprehensive enough. Specifically, the discussion on CO2, a simplification of the authors' own approach, lacks depth. The claim that non-greedy trees are “less susceptible to overfitting” is supported by figures but lacks statistical significance testing. The use of different random splits across experiments and datasets makes comparison difficult. The parameter search for ν and η is mentioned but not detailed.
*   **Proposition 1 Critique:** Proposition 1, while correct, feels almost trivially stated. It highlights a tension between tightness of the bound and stability, but doesn’t offer practical guidance on how to resolve it beyond simply including regularization.
*   **Related Work Gaps:** While the related work section covers some relevant papers, it misses critical recent advances in non-greedy tree learning, such as differentiable tree structures.
*   **Formatting Issues:** Minor formatting inconsistencies exist throughout the paper. For example, the variable 'd' is sometimes used to represent tree depth and other times is an exponent in complexity analysis.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty of the structured prediction formulation.
*   Significant complexity reduction.
*   Potential for improved performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity and detail.
*   Insufficient justification of key choices.
*   Incomplete experimental evaluation.
*   Vague algorithmic descriptions.
*   Numerous minor flaws throughout the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the core idea, the paper is currently unacceptable due to its lack of clarity, insufficient detail, and incomplete experimental evaluation. The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the work. The authors need to significantly revise the paper, providing more detailed explanations, rigorous justifications, a comprehensive experimental analysis, and address the specific concerns raised above. Even with these revisions, it’s uncertain whether the paper would reach the standard expected for NIPS. I recommend **rejection**.
