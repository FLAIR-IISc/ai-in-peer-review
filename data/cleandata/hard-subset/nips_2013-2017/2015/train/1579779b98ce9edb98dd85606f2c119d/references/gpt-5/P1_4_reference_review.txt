Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy method for training decision trees by jointly optimizing all split parameters under a global objective. The key idea is to cast the hard split decisions as latent variables h ∈ {−1,+1}^m and to upper bound the empirical loss with a convex-concave surrogate inspired by structured prediction with latent variables. The basic bound involves a loss-augmented inference over all binary codes (Eq. 7), which is exact but naïvely O(2^d p). The authors introduce a tighter computational formulation by restricting the loss-augmented search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx) (Eq. 13), yielding an O(d^2 p) procedure while preserving a valid upper bound. They regularize each split’s weight vector with per-row norm constraints ‖wi‖2 ≤ ν to control the scale of W, motivated by Proposition 1 showing that scaling W tightens the bound but harms optimizability. Optimization is via SGD with projection, plus a “Stable SGD” variant that freezes leaf assignments intermittently to avoid deactivating leaves.

Empirically, on several LibSVM multiclass datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST), their non-greedy oblique trees outperform axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique splits, and a greedy one-node-at-a-time variant (CO2). They analyze the role of ν (implicit pruning and generalization) and demonstrate the runtime advantage of fast loss-augmented inference vs. the exact version, especially for deeper trees.

This work relates to: (i) classical greedy tree induction (ID3/CART), (ii) oblique splits and margin-based tree methods (e.g., OC1; Bennett et al.), (iii) soft splits/HME (Jordan & Jacobs) which are probabilistic and differentiable but lose tree-time inference, and (iv) structured SVMs and latent structured SVMs (Taskar; Tsochantaridis; Yu & Joachims). The connection of hard trees to latent structured prediction and the Hamming-ball approximation for efficient loss-augmented inference appear novel in the tree literature.

Strengths
- Clear and principled formulation: casting hard-split trees as latent-structure problems and deriving a convex-concave upper bound is elegant and grounded in well-known theory.
- Efficient algorithmic contribution: the Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference reduces complexity from exponential to O(d^2 p), enabling practical training of deeper trees while retaining O(dp) inference at test time.
- Regularization and optimization insights: the analysis around the scale of W and the use of per-row norm constraints are well motivated; the Stable SGD heuristic addresses a practical pitfall (inactive leaves).
- Empirical results: consistent improvements over strong greedy baselines (including oblique OC1 and CO2) across multiple datasets and depths; informative exploration of ν and runtime scaling.

Weaknesses
- Bound tightness and search restriction: while Eq. 13 is a valid upper bound, restricting to a Hamming ball of radius 1 may be a crude approximation. The paper does not quantify how much this loosens the bound relative to Eq. 7 or how often the global loss-augmented maximizer lies outside the ball.
- Dependence on initialization and local minima: the method relies on initializing with a greedy CO2 procedure; no ablation is reported to assess sensitivity to initialization quality.
- Baseline fairness and capacity control: the method implicitly prunes via ν (inactive leaves). It is not fully clear whether greedy baselines were given comparable regularization/pruning opportunities tuned via validation to match effective capacity, beyond varying max depth.
- Limited scope of experiments: only single trees on a few datasets; no comparison to recent differentiable/soft decision tree variants or post-pruning baselines; no forest/ensemble results despite the method seeming composable into forests.
- Theoretical guarantees: beyond the surrogate bound and proposition on scaling, there are no generalization guarantees or convergence claims for (S)SGD on this nonconvex objective.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The derivations and complexity arguments are mostly convincing; the fast loss-augmented inference construction is sensible. Some algorithmic details (e.g., precise SSGD schedule, stopping criteria, step sizes, per-leaf parameter updates/regularization) could be expanded to ensure reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The connection to latent structured prediction is explained clearly. Figures are helpful. Suggestions:
- Clarify the exact mechanics and cost of fast loss-augmented inference, including how many node inner products w_i^T x must be computed per example (and how you avoid O(mp) recomputation).
- Provide the proof sketch for the bounds (7) and (13) in the main text or an intuition beyond the algebraic argument.
- Specify the SSGD schedule (number of steps between assignment updates, criterion for “negligible” improvement).

Originality
Moderately high. While leveraging structured prediction ideas for trees has precedents in spirit, the specific latent-variable formulation, the convex-concave upper bound for hard trees, and the Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference for efficiency appear novel contributions to non-greedy tree learning.

Significance
Moderate to high. Decision trees remain widely used; a practical, non-greedy training method with improved accuracy for single trees is valuable and could translate to better forests. The complexity reduction is an enabling contribution. That said, the empirical scope limits claims of broad impact; demonstrating gains in forests or on larger-scale datasets would strengthen significance.

Questions for the authors
- How often (empirically) does the true loss-augmented maximizer in Eq. 7 lie outside the Hamming-ball set? Can you report the bound gap or incidence statistics?
- How sensitive are results to initialization? What happens with random W/Θ initialization?
- Did you allow pruning or regularization for the greedy baselines matched via validation (e.g., cost-complexity pruning) to ensure comparable effective capacity?
- Do you regularize Θ to prevent overconfident leaves? Any temperature scaling or smoothing?
- Can the method be extended to forests efficiently (e.g., bagging with shared ν choices), and do improvements persist?

Pros for acceptance
- Principled and novel formulation with a practical, efficient algorithm.
- Consistent empirical improvements over multiple baselines.
- Insightful analysis of regularization and runtime.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth and baseline capacity matching.
- No analysis of bound looseness due to Hamming-ball restriction.
- Reliance on strong initialization; no convergence or generalization guarantees.

Recommendation: Accept (weak accept to accept). The paper provides a solid and novel contribution to non-greedy optimization of decision trees with a practical algorithmic advance and promising empirical results.

Confidence: 4/5.
