## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing problem of non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The authors present a novel framework linking decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables, allowing them to formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. This bound is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), with a clever technical contribution reducing the computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d²p) for trees of depth *d* and input dimension *p*. The paper demonstrates improved performance compared to greedy baselines on several benchmark datasets.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the context of decades of research on decision trees (Breiman et al., 1984; Quinlan, 1986). They accurately identify the limitations of greedy approaches and highlight previous attempts at global optimization (Bennett, 2000; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2012). The connection to structured prediction (Taskar et al., 2004; Yu & Joachims, 2009) and the use of upper bounds are well-motivated and clearly explained.  The comparison to CO2 (Norouzi et al., 2015) is particularly useful, demonstrating the benefits of the full non-greedy optimization. The paper acknowledges related work on soft splits (Jordan & Jacobs, 1994) and online learning (Murthy & Salzberg, 1995), appropriately noting their drawbacks in the context of efficient tree induction.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The framing of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a significant conceptual contribution. This allows the application of powerful techniques from that field.
* **Efficient Optimization:** The reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d²p) is crucial for scaling to deeper trees and larger datasets. This is a key technical achievement.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experiments consistently show improved performance compared to greedy baselines across multiple datasets, indicating a genuine benefit from the non-greedy approach. The ablation studies with the regularization parameter ν are particularly insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical derivations are clearly presented and explained.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of the existing literature and carefully position their work within the broader field.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed SGD algorithm, including the Stable SGD variant, is practical and provides concrete guidance for implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity Analysis Caveats:** The claim of O(d²p) complexity relies on the fast loss-augmented inference step. While explained clearly, it’s not immediately obvious how significant the constant factor hidden within this O-notation is, and if it might negate the advantage for certain parameter regimes. A more detailed discussion of this practical aspect would be valuable.
* **Initialization Dependency:** The performance appears to rely on initializing the non-greedy process with a greedy tree (CO2).  The sensitivity to this initialization could be further investigated.  What happens if the initialization is random or suboptimal?
* **Limited Comparison:**  While the comparisons to greedy baselines are comprehensive, a comparison to more recent and sophisticated gradient boosting methods (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM) would be beneficial to understand the relative performance of this approach in a more modern context. These methods don't strictly fit the 'decision tree' paradigm, but provide a strong benchmark for predictive accuracy.
* **Norm Regularization Choice:** The choice of L2 regularization on the weight vectors seems somewhat arbitrary.  A justification for this specific choice, or a comparison with other regularization strategies (e.g., L1 for sparsity), would strengthen the argument.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a long-standing problem in machine learning.
* The optimization framework and efficiency improvements are significant contributions.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical implications of the O(d²p) complexity need further scrutiny regarding constant factors.
* The reliance on greedy initialization and the limited comparison to more recent methods raise minor concerns.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The novel formulation, efficient optimization, and compelling empirical results outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and thoroughly researched.  I believe this work is a significant step forward in addressing the limitations of greedy decision tree algorithms and is suitable for acceptance to NIPS.

**Rating:**  Strong Accept (8/10)

**Recommendation:** Accept.
