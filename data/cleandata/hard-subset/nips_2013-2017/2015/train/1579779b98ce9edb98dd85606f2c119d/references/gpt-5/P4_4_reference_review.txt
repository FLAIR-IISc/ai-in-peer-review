Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees that jointly optimizes all split functions under a global objective, rather than optimizing one node at a time as in ID3/CART [21,5]. The key idea is to cast the tree’s routing decisions as latent binary variables h ∈ {−1,+1}m (one per internal node), linking decision trees to structured prediction with latent variables [25]. Using this view, the authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss (Eq. 7), analogous to margin-rescaled latent structural SVM objectives [23,24,25]. They then propose a stochastic gradient descent (SGD) procedure to minimize this bound with per-row norm constraints on W to regularize the scale (motivated by Proposition 1). A naive loss-augmented inference step over all binary codes is exponential in tree depth; the authors introduce an alternative bound that restricts the loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx) (Eq. 13), yielding an O(d^2 p) algorithm for depth d and p-dimensional inputs while preserving the upper-bound property because g = sgn(Wx) remains feasible. They also introduce a “Stable SGD” variant that temporarily fixes data-to-leaf assignments to prevent leaf inactivity.

Empirically, the method is evaluated on SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST (LibSVM [6]), comparing single non-greedy oblique trees against axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees [17], random oblique trees, and CO2 [18] (a depth-1 greedy instance of their bound). Across depths, the proposed method often achieves higher test accuracy and appears less prone to overfitting. The fast loss-augmented inference scales much better with depth than the naive variant. The regularization parameter ν controls both the tightness of the bound and the degree of implicit pruning; ν and the learning rate are tuned on validation data. Initialization uses an axis-aligned tree and greedy CO2 to set W, Θ.

Relative to prior work: (i) it avoids soft splits as in HME [13], retaining O(dp) inference; (ii) it differs from global MILP/MLP approaches [1] by handling multi-class and general losses with practical complexity; (iii) it operationalizes max-margin ideas for trees [2,3] in a joint, latent-variable optimization; and (iv) it extends CO2 [18] from greedy depth-1 optimization to a global non-greedy procedure with an efficient loss-augmented inference scheme.

Strengths
- Conceptual link between decision trees and latent structured prediction is clear and well-motivated; the bound-based formulation is principled.
- Efficient loss-augmented inference via the Hamming-ball restriction preserves an upper bound while reducing complexity to O(d^2 p), enabling deeper trees.
- Per-row norm constraints offer a practical and intuitive regularization mechanism, with a coherent discussion of the scale–smoothness trade-off (Prop. 1).
- Empirical results on multiple datasets show consistent improvements over strong greedy baselines, including OC1 and CO2, for single trees.
- The method maintains hard splits and O(dp) inference at test time, preserving core efficiency advantages of trees.

Weaknesses
- Theory: no convergence guarantees (e.g., to a stationary point) for the non-convex surrogate, nor generalization bounds connecting ν to predictive performance.
- Stability/SSGD: the stabilization heuristic is plausible but not theoretically analyzed; its empirical impact is not ablated in detail.
- Experimental scope: focuses on single trees. While this isolates effects of non-greedy training, comparisons to strong practical baselines (e.g., pruned trees [16] tuned to similar leaf counts, or ensembles trained to same compute budget) would better position significance.
- Limited quantitative detail: figures are referenced, but explicit numbers (means/variance over multiple runs/splits), standard errors, and significance tests are not reported.
- Initialization dependence: results may rely on CO2/axis-aligned initialization; sensitivity analyses are not shown.
- Minor clarity issues: precise definition of tie-breaking when wi^T x = 0; explicit pseudocode for loss-augmented inference; more details on the mapping f(·) beyond the figure.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations of the bounds (Eqs. 7 and 13) are standard and valid, relying on including g = sgn(Wx) in the feasible set. The complexity analysis and the per-row constraint projections are correct and practical. The approach is non-convex overall; optimization is heuristic SGD with projection and lacks theoretical convergence rates.
- Empirical support: Results are positive but would benefit from more rigorous reporting (replicates, CIs), ablations (effect of ν, initialization, SSGD vs SGD), and tests on regression to demonstrate loss generality.

Clarity
- Generally well-written with clear notation and motivation. Adding a concise algorithmic description of fast loss-augmented inference, tie-breaking policy, and full training hyperparameters would further improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The latent-structured formulation for hard-split trees, the specific convex–concave bound with per-row norm constraints, and the efficient Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference together appear novel beyond CO2 [18] and prior oblique/non-greedy works [1,17]. The contribution is a non-trivial extension and integration of structured SVM concepts into tree learning.

Significance
- Improving single-tree training in a principled, scalable way is valuable both scientifically and practically (e.g., when interpretability or low-latency inference precludes ensembles). The approach could inform future work on forests, kernels, and sparsity as suggested by the authors.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, novel framework connecting trees to latent structured prediction with an efficient optimization algorithm.
- Empirical gains over strong greedy/oblique baselines across datasets and depths.
- Maintains hard-split inference efficiency; practical and broadly applicable.
- Clear path to extensions (sparsity, kernels, ensembles).

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees and analysis of the optimization dynamics.
- Experimental evaluation omits stronger/practical baselines (e.g., carefully pruned trees or ensembles under matched resources) and lacks statistical rigor.
- Sensitivity to initialization and the role of SSGD not fully characterized.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide ablations: (a) SGD vs SSGD; (b) sensitivity to ν and learning rate; (c) different initializations (random, axis-aligned only).
- Report means/std over multiple seeds/splits, and include explicit accuracy tables.
- Compare against pruned greedy trees matched for active leaf count, and discuss extensions to small ensembles to assess scalability of the method within forests.
- Clarify tie-breaking when wi^T x = 0 and give pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference.
- Consider adding a brief convergence discussion (e.g., CCCP-style updates or monotonicity of the bound under certain schedules), even if only empirical.

Overall: In scope for NeurIPS; technically solid and novel with promising empirical results. Improvements to experimental rigor and analysis would strengthen the paper.
