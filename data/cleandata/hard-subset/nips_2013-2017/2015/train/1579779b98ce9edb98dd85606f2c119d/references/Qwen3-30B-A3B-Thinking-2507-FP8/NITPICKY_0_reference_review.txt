

# Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

This paper proposes a non-greedy framework for decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, introducing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity of gradient updates from O(2^dp) to O(d²p), enabling efficient learning of deep trees.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically sound connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables. The proposed surrogate objective is well-motivated, and the complexity analysis for the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm is a significant technical contribution. The experimental results demonstrate improved generalization performance compared to greedy baselines across multiple datasets, which is a promising empirical validation.

## Critical Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Gaps**: The paper claims Proposition 1 (inequality 8) holds but only references supplementary material for the proof. Without seeing the proof, it's impossible to verify the claim that "the upper bound on the loss becomes tighter as a constant multiple of W increases." This is a fundamental claim that should be verified in the main paper.

2. **Notational Inconsistency**: The paper switches between using "m" for the number of internal nodes (Section 3) and "d" for tree depth (Section 1) without clear explanation. In Section 6, the complexity analysis states "O(dp) where d is the tree depth," but earlier the paper uses "m" for internal nodes. This inconsistency makes it difficult to follow the complexity arguments.

3. **Algorithmic Error**: Algorithm 1, Line 6 has "W (tmp) ←W (t) − η ĝxT + η ĥxT" which appears to contain a typo. The correct update should be "W (tmp) ←W (t) − η (ĝ - ĥ)xT" to match the gradient calculation, as ∂/∂W (h^T Wx) = hx^T. This is a critical error that would prevent the algorithm from working correctly.

4. **Confusing Baseline Comparison**: The comparison with "CO2" [18] is problematic because CO2 is described as "a special case of our upper bound approach applied greedily to trees of depth 1." Comparing with a variant of their own method is not a fair baseline comparison, as it conflates the non-greedy aspect with the greedy implementation.

5. **Missing Statistical Significance**: The paper claims "non-greedy trees achieve the best test performance" but provides no statistical significance testing for these comparisons. Without p-values or confidence intervals, the claims about performance differences are not rigorously supported.

6. **Formatting and Presentation Issues**: The paper contains several grammatical errors (e.g., "the number of leaves is always one more than the number of internal (non-leaf) nodes" - this is only true for binary trees, not N-ary trees as the paper claims to extend to). The reference list has incomplete entries (e.g., missing publication years).

## Minor Issues

- The paper claims "our method can handle any general loss function" but doesn't provide specific examples or discuss limitations for non-smooth loss functions.
- The explanation of the Hamming ball B₁(sgn(Wx)) in Section 6 is unclear - it's not obvious why this restriction makes inference tractable.
- The paper mentions "we use the provided train; validation; test sets when available" but doesn't specify how they handled the validation set tuning process.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction, and the complexity reduction claim is potentially valuable, the paper contains several critical technical flaws that undermine its main contribution. The notational inconsistencies, algorithmic error in Algorithm 1, and lack of rigorous verification of Proposition 1 are particularly concerning. The baseline comparison with CO2 is misleading, and the experimental claims are not sufficiently supported by statistical evidence.

The paper is on the borderline of being acceptable, but the critical technical errors and theoretical gaps prevent it from being a strong contribution. A revised version addressing these issues would be significantly improved, but as presented, the paper does not meet NIPS standards for technical soundness.

**Recommendation**: Reject

**Pro**: Theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction is novel and well-motivated; complexity reduction claim is potentially significant.

**Con**: Critical algorithmic error in Algorithm 1; unverified theoretical claims; confusing baseline comparison; lack of statistical significance testing; notational inconsistencies.
