### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the long-standing problem of non-greedy decision tree optimization. Traditional methods like ID3 and CART build trees greedily, optimizing split functions one node at a time based only on local information. This can lead to globally suboptimal trees. The authors propose a novel framework for the joint, non-greedy optimization of all split functions in a tree under a global objective.

The core contribution is the reformulation of the decision tree learning problem as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The sequence of binary decisions made at the split nodes for a given input is treated as a latent variable. Direct optimization of the empirical loss under this formulation is intractable due to its discontinuous, piecewise-constant nature. To overcome this, the authors, inspired by latent structural SVMs [25], derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. This surrogate objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD).

A key challenge is that the complexity of evaluating this bound (specifically, the loss-augmented inference step) is exponential in the tree depth `d`, O(2^d*p), making it impractical for deep trees. The paper's second major technical contribution is a modified, more efficient formulation where the search space for the loss-augmented inference is restricted to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current optimal path. This clever modification reduces the complexity to a much more scalable O(d^2*p), enabling the efficient training of deep trees. Experiments on several benchmark datasets demonstrate that the proposed non-greedy trees consistently outperform various greedy baselines, including axis-aligned and oblique trees, showing better generalization and less susceptibility to overfitting.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation (Originality & Significance):** The paper presents a highly original and principled approach to the classic and difficult problem of global decision tree optimization. The connection established between decision tree learning and latent variable structured prediction is a significant conceptual contribution. This reframing moves the problem from the domain of combinatorial heuristics to a well-understood optimization framework, opening up new avenues for research (e.g., using kernels or different regularizers, as the authors suggest).

2.  **Strong Technical Contribution (Quality):** The paper's technical core is solid. The derivation of the surrogate objective based on latent structural SVMs is well-founded. More importantly, the identification and resolution of the exponential complexity bottleneck is a crucial practical contribution. The proposed "fast loss-augmented inference" by restricting the search to a Hamming ball is an effective and well-motivated approximation that makes the entire approach computationally viable for deep trees, which is critical for practical relevance.

3.  **Convincing Empirical Results (Quality & Significance):** The experimental evaluation is thorough and supports the paper's claims. The proposed non-greedy method consistently outperforms several strong greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, and a greedy version of their own approach, CO2) across multiple datasets. The results in Figure 2 compellingly show that the non-greedy trees achieve higher test accuracy and generalize better, particularly at greater depths where greedy methods tend to overfit. This demonstrates a clear advance in the state-of-the-art for learning a single, powerful decision tree.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly articulates the limitations of greedy methods, motivates the proposed formulation step-by-step, explains the technical challenges, and details the solutions. The connection between the latent variable `h`, the split function matrix `W`, and the tree navigation function `f` is explained effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Initialization:** The non-greedy optimization procedure is initialized with the parameters of a tree trained via a greedy procedure (CO2). The paper does not analyze the sensitivity of the final model to this initialization. It remains somewhat unclear to what extent the algorithm is merely fine-tuning a good greedy solution versus its ability to escape poor local minima and discover a fundamentally different, superior tree structure. An ablation study exploring different initializations (e.g., random, or a poorly performing greedy tree) would strengthen the claims.

2.  **Comparison to Ensembles:** The authors rightly state that they focus on single trees to isolate the effect of the non-greedy optimization. However, in practice, state-of-the-art performance for tree-based models is almost always achieved with ensembles like Random Forests or Gradient Boosted Trees. While a full comparison is beyond the scope, a brief discussion or a small-scale experiment comparing a single, powerful non-greedy tree against a small ensemble of greedy trees (e.g., a Random Forest with 10-20 trees) would provide valuable context regarding its practical utility and trade-offs.

3.  **Details on Optimization:** The paper mentions using "common SGD tricks, including the use of momentum and mini-batches" but does not provide specifics. Details regarding the learning rate schedule, momentum parameter, and mini-batch size would be essential for reproducibility. Similarly, the "Stable SGD" (SSGD) variant is an interesting heuristic to prevent leaf collapse, but its mechanism and impact on final performance could be elaborated on more.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, long-standing, and important problem in machine learning.
*   The formulation linking decision trees to latent structured prediction is highly novel and significant.
*   The technical contribution of the fast loss-augmented inference is critical for making the approach practical and is a key innovation.
*   The empirical results are strong, demonstrating a clear and consistent improvement over established greedy methods for single tree learning.
*   The work is well-presented, technically sound, and likely to inspire follow-up research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis of the algorithm's dependence on its greedy initialization is limited.
*   The practical context relative to powerful ensemble methods is not explored.
*   Some implementation details crucial for reproducibility are omitted.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on decision trees. The strengths—a novel and principled formulation for a classic problem, a key technical innovation to ensure computational feasibility, and strong empirical validation—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to avenues for future investigation and minor omissions in experimental details. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a problem of broad interest to the NIPS community. It clearly advances the state-of-the-art and provides a new and powerful framework for thinking about decision tree optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
