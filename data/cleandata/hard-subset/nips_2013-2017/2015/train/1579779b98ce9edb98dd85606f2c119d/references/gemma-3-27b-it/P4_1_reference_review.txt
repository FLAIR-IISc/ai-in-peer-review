This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The key idea is to introduce a latent variable representing split decisions and formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, which is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors address the computational complexity issue inherent in this formulation by showing how to reduce it from O(2dp) to O(d2p), enabling efficient learning of deep trees. The experimental results demonstrate that their approach outperforms several greedy baselines on benchmark datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly explaining the limitations of greedy decision tree induction and the potential benefits of a non-greedy approach. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is a novel and insightful contribution, allowing the application of techniques from that field to decision tree learning. The development of the convex-concave upper bound and the subsequent reduction in computational complexity are significant technical achievements. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed method against several relevant baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness on various datasets. The discussion of hyperparameter tuning, specifically the regularization parameter ν, adds to the practical relevance of the work. The writing is generally clear and concise, and the paper is well-organized.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the complexity reduction to O(d2p) is presented as a major contribution, the impact of this reduction on practical performance isn't thoroughly quantified. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between the original O(2dp) complexity and the reduced O(d2p) complexity, including scenarios where the reduction is most significant, would be valuable. The description of the "Stable SGD" (SSGD) algorithm feels somewhat cursory; more detail about its implementation and the rationale behind its design choices would be helpful. It is not entirely clear why fixed assignments for several iterations improve results. The experiments, while comprehensive, primarily focus on classification tasks. Exploring the performance of the proposed method on regression problems or other applications could broaden its appeal. The authors mention potential extensions involving kernel methods and sparse split functions but do not elaborate on the feasibility or potential challenges of these extensions. The initialization strategy—starting with a greedy tree—could potentially bias the results. Investigating the impact of different initialization schemes would be worthwhile.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables is a novel and promising approach.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The development of the convex-concave upper bound and the complexity reduction are mathematically sound and contribute to the practical feasibility of the method.
*   **Experimental Results:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to several established baselines.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a long-standing challenge in decision tree learning—the limitations of greedy algorithms—and offers a potentially significant improvement in performance and generalization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Analysis of Complexity Reduction:** The practical impact of the complexity reduction is not fully explored.
*   **Cursory Description of SSGD:** The Stable SGD algorithm is not described in sufficient detail.
*   **Narrow Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is primarily focused on classification tasks.
*   **Potential Bias from Initialization:** The greedy initialization strategy may introduce bias.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The novel formulation, technical soundness, and promising experimental results outweigh the identified weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised regarding the complexity analysis, SSGD algorithm description, and experimental scope. The paper aligns well with the interests of the NIPS community and has the potential to influence future research in this area.
