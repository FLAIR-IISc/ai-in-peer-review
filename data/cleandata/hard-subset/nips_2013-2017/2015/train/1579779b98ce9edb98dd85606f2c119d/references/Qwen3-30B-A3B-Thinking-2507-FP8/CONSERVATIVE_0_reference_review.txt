

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors introduce a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent. They claim their method avoids the greedy nature of conventional tree induction by jointly optimizing split functions across all tree levels, and they present a fast inference algorithm with O(d²p) complexity.

The paper builds on established structured prediction literature (citing Yu & Joachims, 2009; Taskar et al., 2005) and makes an interesting connection between decision trees and latent variable models. The technical approach is well-motivated and the mathematical formulation is sound. The authors show that their method can handle general loss functions and provide a principled way to regularize tree parameters.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the decision tree learning problem as structured prediction with latent variables.
- The technical contributions regarding complexity reduction from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) are sound and well-explained.
- The experimental results show modest improvements in generalization performance compared to greedy baselines across multiple datasets.
- The paper is well-written with clear mathematical exposition and organized presentation.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares only with basic greedy baselines (information gain, OC1, random oblique trees) and CO2. It fails to compare with modern, high-performance decision tree methods like XGBoost, LightGBM, or CatBoost, which have become standard in industry and research. This omission makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed approach.

2. **Marginal empirical gains**: The reported improvements in test accuracy are modest (typically 1-2% across datasets). The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that these marginal gains justify the significantly more complex optimization procedure compared to established methods.

3. **Lack of scalability analysis**: The paper claims O(d²p) complexity but doesn't provide evidence of practical scalability. Figure 4 shows only Connect4 (55,000 training points) and doesn't demonstrate performance on larger datasets where the complexity advantage would matter most.

4. **Inadequate theoretical justification**: While the upper bound is mathematically sound, the paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why this particular bound is practically useful. The relationship between the bound's tightness (controlled by ν) and actual performance improvements isn't thoroughly analyzed.

5. **No ablation study**: The paper doesn't perform an ablation study to determine which components of the proposed method contribute most to the performance gains. For example, it's unclear whether the non-greedy aspect or the specific optimization technique provides the main benefit.

6. **Limited experimental scope**: Experiments are restricted to four relatively small datasets from LibSVM. There's no evaluation on larger, more challenging datasets where decision trees are commonly applied in practice.

7. **Overstated novelty**: The paper positions its approach as a significant departure from existing methods, but the core idea is a straightforward application of structured prediction techniques to decision trees. The connection to existing work (e.g., Bennett's global tree optimization) isn't sufficiently discussed or contrasted.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound extension of structured prediction to decision tree learning, but it fails to demonstrate significant improvements over established methods that would justify its complexity. The marginal gains reported are not substantial enough to overcome the practical advantages of greedy tree induction, which has been the standard for decades due to its simplicity and efficiency.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) Comparing against modern tree-based methods (XGBoost, LightGBM, etc.)
2) Providing a thorough analysis of the trade-offs between the proposed method and established approaches
3) Demonstrating scalability on larger datasets
4) Including a proper ablation study to identify key contributors to performance

Given the marginal improvements reported and the lack of convincing evidence that the method provides substantial practical advantages, I cannot recommend acceptance at NeurIPS. The paper represents a modest extension of existing techniques rather than a significant advance in the field. The claims of "better generalization performance" are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this method would be preferred over existing, highly optimized tree learning approaches.

The authors should either substantially strengthen the experimental evaluation to show clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods or temper their claims to reflect the more modest nature of their contribution. As currently presented, the paper does not advance the state of the art sufficiently to warrant publication at NeurIPS.
