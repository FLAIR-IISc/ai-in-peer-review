

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a non-greedy framework for decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a link between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently via stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p), enabling efficient training of deep trees. The paper builds upon previous work on global tree optimization (Bennett [1]) and structured prediction (Yu & Joachims [25]), while addressing the limitations of prior non-greedy approaches that were computationally prohibitive.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical formulation of the problem, including a clear proof of the upper bound (Equation 7) and its properties. The authors carefully analyze the computational complexity and demonstrate how to efficiently compute loss-augmented inference using a novel fast inference algorithm. The experimental evaluation supports the theoretical claims, showing that non-greedy trees achieve better generalization performance than greedy baselines across multiple datasets.

2. **Novelty:** The formulation of decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables represents a significant conceptual advance. While Bennett [1] proposed a non-greedy approach, it was limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss and had high computational complexity. The authors' method handles any general loss function while maintaining O(dp) inference complexity, and the efficient optimization approach is novel.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithms (particularly Algorithm 1) are precisely described. The connection between decision trees and structured prediction is effectively explained, making the approach accessible to the NIPS audience.

4. **Significance:** The results demonstrate that non-greedy optimization leads to better generalization performance compared to greedy methods, addressing a fundamental limitation in decision tree learning. The efficient optimization approach makes this theoretically appealing method practically feasible, potentially benefiting real-world applications of decision trees.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to four datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) and lacks comparison with recent deep decision tree methods (e.g., [14], [22]). The paper would benefit from comparisons with more contemporary approaches to better establish the relative significance of their contribution.

2. **Implementation Details:** While the computational complexity is well-analyzed, the paper lacks specific details about how the method scales with extremely large datasets or very deep trees. The runtime analysis in Figure 4 is limited to Connect4 (55,000 training points), which doesn't demonstrate scalability to larger datasets.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper discusses the regularization parameter ν, but doesn't provide sufficient guidance on how to select it for new datasets. The validation process (using a separate validation set to tune ν and η) is reasonable but could be better justified.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel formulation linking decision trees to structured prediction with latent variables
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with clear proof of the upper bound
- Efficient optimization algorithm with O(d^2p) complexity, making non-greedy learning feasible
- Strong experimental results showing improved generalization over greedy methods
- Clear, well-organized presentation of a technically sound contribution

*Con:*
- Limited experimental comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods
- Insufficient demonstration of scalability to very large datasets
- Lack of detailed guidance on hyperparameter selection for new applications
- Relatively small number of benchmark datasets tested

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong technical contribution to the field of decision tree learning by providing a novel and efficient framework for non-greedy optimization. The theoretical foundation is solid, the algorithm is well-motivated and carefully analyzed, and the experimental results support the claims. The paper is well-written and clearly positioned within the literature. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons and scalability analysis, the core contribution represents a meaningful advancement over previous non-greedy approaches. The method has the potential to influence both theoretical understanding and practical applications of decision trees.

The paper meets all four review criteria: it is technically sound, clearly written, novel in its approach to the problem, and significant for advancing the state of the art in decision tree learning. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
