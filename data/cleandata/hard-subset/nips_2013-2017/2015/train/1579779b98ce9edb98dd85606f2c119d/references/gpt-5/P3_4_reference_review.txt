Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss using stochastic gradient descent. The key ideas are: (i) represent the vector of split outcomes h = sgn(Wx) as the maximizer of a linear score over binary codes (structured latent variable view), (ii) upper bound the loss with a loss-augmented inference term minus an inference term, in the spirit of latent structural SVMs, (iii) regularize via per-row norm constraints on W to prevent trivial tightening of the bound by scaling, (iv) derive an efficient loss-augmented inference procedure that, in its exact form, is O(2^d p) but can be reduced to O(d^2 p) by restricting to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), and (v) introduce a stabilized SGD variant that holds leaf assignments fixed intermittently to avoid deactivating leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare single trees trained with this non-greedy procedure to axis-aligned CART, OC1, random oblique trees, and a greedy depth-1 bound optimization (CO2), reporting consistent improvements in test accuracy as depth increases and reduced overfitting.

This line of work builds on and connects: (a) NP-hardness of global tree induction; (b) prior non-greedy/global formulations (e.g., MILP, max-margin nodes) that are limited in scale; (c) soft-split models (HME) that lose the computational benefits of hard trees; and (d) latent structural SVMs and loss-augmented inference. The Hamming-ball restriction and per-row norm projection provide a pragmatic way to make joint oblique-split optimization efficient for deep trees, and the method generalizes to arbitrary losses at leaves.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The latent-variable reformulation and the upper bound are standard and correctly adapted to trees. The inequality in Eq. (7) holds by selecting g = ĥ; the dependence of the bound on scaling W is correctly observed, and the constraint-based regularization is appropriate. The fast loss-augmented inference using a Hamming radius-1 set is well motivated: flipping one split on the path yields at most d alternative leaves, while off-path flips leave f(g) unchanged. The complexity claims O(d^2 p + k) per SGD step are plausible.
- Weaknesses in analysis: The optimization remains non-convex; no convergence guarantees beyond standard SGD heuristics are discussed. The statement that small ν “forces a large margin at each internal node” is imprecise; with hard constraints ||wi||2 ≤ ν, smaller ν limits capacity rather than explicitly maximizing margin. It would help to formalize how ν trades off the tightness of the bound and functional margin. The move from the exact bound (over all g) to the radius-1 bound is a key approximation; the paper does not analyze how this loosening affects optimization quality or provide conditions under which it finds good local optima. Stable SGD is interesting but under-evaluated; quantitative evidence on how it affects training dynamics and test error is missing.
- Experimental support: The empirical comparisons suggest consistent gains for single trees, but the presentation is qualitative (figures without numerical tables or confidence intervals). There is no statistical significance analysis, no ablation isolating the effect of the radius-1 restriction vs. exact loss-augmented inference, and no study of initialization sensitivity (the method is initialized via CO2; does performance degrade with weaker initializations?). Runtime plots for fast vs. exact inference are useful but limited to a modest dataset; wall-clock comparisons against OC1 and random oblique trees would strengthen the case for efficiency.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The latent-variable mapping, navigation function f, and the loss-augmented inference procedure are explained with sufficient detail to implement. Algorithm 1 is helpful. A few points could be improved:
- Clarify the role of ν with a more careful margin/regularization discussion, and provide guidance for tuning.
- Make the complexity breakdown explicit, including the dependence on k.
- Provide more details on the SSGD schedule: how often assignments are updated, stopping criteria, and its empirical effect.

Originality
Framing tree induction as latent structured prediction and optimizing a global surrogate is a novel and interesting perspective. Prior work has used related bounds for depth-1 nodes (CO2) and soft split models (HME), but the combination of: (i) a global latent-SSVM-style bound for hard trees, (ii) an efficient radius-1 loss-augmented inference tailored to tree structure, and (iii) row-wise norm constraints with projected SGD appears original. The link to hashing (using the same sgn-argmax trick) is appropriate and well acknowledged.

Significance
Improving single-tree training in a scalable, non-greedy way is a valuable contribution; oblique trees are competitive in settings where interpretability and fast inference matter. The method can, in principle, extend to forests and to other losses. That said, the empirical scope is limited to single trees on a handful of datasets; demonstrating impact on ensembles (e.g., plugging into random forests or boosting) and on larger, higher-dimensional problems would increase practical significance.

Pros
- Clear and principled formulation linking decision trees to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient loss-augmented inference via Hamming-ball restriction; sound exploitation of tree structure.
- Simple, scalable training with projected SGD; compatible with arbitrary leaf losses.
- Consistent empirical gains over strong greedy baselines for single trees and evidence of reduced overfitting.

Cons
- Non-convex optimization with no convergence analysis; performance may depend on initialization.
- Approximate bound (radius-1) not theoretically characterized; no ablation on its impact.
- Limited experimental scope: no statistical tests, no ensemble results, minimal runtime comparisons against baselines.
- Ambiguous discussion of the effect of ν; SSGD under-evaluated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations: exact vs. radius-1 loss-augmented inference on shallow trees; with/without SSGD; sensitivity to initialization.
- Clarify ν’s effect and report validation curves; consider sparsity-inducing regularization to handle large p.
- Add runtime and scaling comparisons to OC1 and random oblique baselines on larger datasets; report numerical accuracy with variability.
- Explore integration into forests/boosting to demonstrate broader impact.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a novel, well-motivated, and efficient approach to non-greedy training of hard decision trees, with promising empirical results, though the experimental evaluation could be strengthened and some theoretical aspects clarified.
