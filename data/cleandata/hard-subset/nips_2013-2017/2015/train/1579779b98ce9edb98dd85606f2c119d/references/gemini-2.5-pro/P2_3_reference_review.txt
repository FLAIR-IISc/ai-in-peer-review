### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the long-standing problem of greedy, one-node-at-a-time training for decision trees. The authors propose a novel, non-greedy framework for learning oblique decision trees by optimizing a global objective function over all split nodes simultaneously. The core contribution is an elegant reframing of the decision tree learning problem as one of structured prediction with latent variables. Specifically, the sequence of split decisions for a given input is treated as a latent structured variable. To overcome the non-differentiable and piecewise-constant nature of the true empirical loss, the authors derive a convex-concave upper bound, inspired by max-margin structured prediction, which serves as a surrogate objective. This objective is then optimized using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is a method for "fast loss-augmented inference," which reduces the per-step complexity of SGD from exponential in the tree depth, O(2^d*p), to polynomial, O(d^2*p), making the approach practical for deep trees. Experiments on several benchmark datasets show that the resulting non-greedy trees consistently outperform various greedy baselines, including axis-aligned and oblique trees, in terms of generalization performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel formulation. Connecting decision tree optimization to the well-established framework of structured prediction with latent variables is a significant conceptual contribution. This moves beyond decades of reliance on greedy, heuristic-driven methods (like information gain) and places tree learning on a more principled, global optimization footing. The potential impact is high, as it could inspire a new class of algorithms for training not just single trees, but also ensembles like random forests.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical development is sound and rigorous. The derivation of the surrogate objective (Eq. 9) via the upper bound in Eq. (7) is well-motivated and correctly draws from the structural SVM literature. The observation that the naive loss-augmented inference is intractable for deep trees, and the subsequent development of the "fast loss-augmented inference" method (Eq. 13) by constraining the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1, is a clever and crucial innovation that makes the entire approach computationally feasible. This demonstrates a deep understanding of both the problem and the optimization machinery.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing.
    *   **Strong Baselines:** The authors compare their method against a comprehensive set of relevant baselines, including standard axis-aligned trees, the classic oblique tree learner OC1, and a greedy version of their own approach (CO2). This comparison against CO2 is particularly insightful, as it isolates the benefit of the non-greedy, global optimization over a locally optimized version of the same objective.
    *   **Clear Results:** The results presented in Figure 2 consistently show that the proposed non-greedy trees achieve superior test accuracy and are less prone to overfitting than the greedy methods, especially as tree depth increases.
    *   **Ablation and Analysis:** The paper includes valuable analysis of the key hyperparameter `ν` (Figure 3) and empirically verifies the computational complexity claims of the fast inference method (Figure 4). This adds credibility to their contributions.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the notation is consistent, and the logical progression from the problem formulation to the final algorithm is easy to follow. The key insight linking `sgn(Wx)` to an `argmax` over latent variables (Eq. 5) is explained lucidly, making the bridge to structured prediction accessible.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on Initialization:** The optimization problem is non-convex, meaning the algorithm finds a local minimum. The paper states that initialization is performed using a greedy procedure (CO2). The quality of this initialization is likely critical to the quality of the final solution. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion or experiment on the sensitivity of the final model's performance to different initialization strategies (e.g., random initialization vs. greedy).

2.  **Clarity on "Stable SGD" (SSGD):** The SSGD variant is introduced as a practical improvement to prevent leaves from becoming inactive. However, its description is somewhat vague. The criterion for updating the leaf assignments ("when the improvement in the bound becomes negligible") is not precisely defined, which slightly hinders full reproducibility. While this is a minor point concerning an algorithmic variant, more detail would be beneficial.

3.  **Scope of "Global" Optimization:** The paper uses the term "global objective," which is accurate. However, the optimization itself is local (due to non-convexity). While this is an inherent challenge in this problem space and not a flaw of the paper, it's a nuance worth emphasizing. The method finds a locally optimal solution for a global objective, which is still a major step up from a sequence of locally optimal decisions made by greedy algorithms.

### Justification for Rating

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, technically sound, and significant contribution to a fundamental problem in machine learning. The elegant reframing of decision tree learning as structured prediction is a powerful idea, and the authors have executed it successfully with a practical and effective algorithm. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the value of the non-greedy approach, showing clear improvements over strong baselines. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to avenues for further exploration (initialization sensitivity) and small details in the presentation (SSGD) rather than fundamental flaws in the core methodology. The work is likely to have a substantial impact on the field and inspire future research into non-greedy tree and forest induction methods. It is a clear accept for NIPS.
