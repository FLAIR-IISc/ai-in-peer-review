

# Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

This paper proposes a novel non-greedy framework for optimizing decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent. A key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p), making deep tree learning feasible. The paper builds on prior work by Bennett [1], Murthy and Salzburg [17], and others who have explored non-greedy tree learning, but distinguishes itself through its structured prediction formulation and efficient optimization approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Soundness:** The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing a clear connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables. The upper bound formulation (Equation 7) is theoretically sound and well-justified. The complexity reduction from exponential to quadratic in tree depth is a major technical achievement that addresses a critical bottleneck in non-greedy tree learning.

2. **Experimental Validation:** The authors provide thorough experimental validation across multiple benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) showing that their non-greedy approach achieves better generalization performance than greedy baselines across various tree depths. The comparison with CO2 [18] as a special case of their method provides useful insights into the benefits of non-greedy optimization.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The mathematical formulations are precise, the algorithm descriptions are clear, and the connection between decision trees and structured prediction is well-motivated. The supplementary material provides additional proof details that strengthen the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Convergence Analysis:** While the paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for their upper bound, it lacks a thorough analysis of the convergence properties of their SGD-based optimization procedure. The authors claim "good training and test accuracy relatively quickly," but without theoretical guarantees on convergence rates or conditions for convergence, the practical applicability of their method remains somewhat uncertain.

2. **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to four datasets from LibSVM. While these are standard benchmarks, the paper would be significantly strengthened by including more diverse datasets (e.g., larger-scale or time-series data) and comparing with more recent state-of-the-art decision tree methods. The paper mentions "deep trees" but doesn't demonstrate significant performance gains on very deep trees where the complexity advantage would be most pronounced.

3. **Lack of Intuition for Performance Gains:** The paper shows that non-greedy trees generalize better, but doesn't provide sufficient insight into *why* this occurs. A more detailed analysis of how the non-greedy optimization changes the tree structure (e.g., leaf distribution, feature usage) compared to greedy trees would strengthen the contribution.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper references CO2 [18] as a baseline but doesn't clearly differentiate their approach from more recent non-greedy methods like those in [15, 13]. A more comprehensive comparison with the latest developments in decision tree learning would better position their contribution.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to non-greedy decision tree learning with significant potential impact. The theoretical contribution of reducing the complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth is particularly strong, and the experimental results demonstrate clear benefits over greedy baselines.

However, the paper would be substantially strengthened by:
1. Adding a theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the SGD optimization procedure
2. Expanding the experimental validation to include more diverse datasets and recent baselines
3. Providing deeper insights into why the non-greedy approach leads to better generalization (e.g., visualizations of tree structures, feature usage analysis)
4. More clearly differentiating from recent work on non-greedy tree learning

Given the strong theoretical contribution and clear experimental validation, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these concerns. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of decision tree learning that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. While the current version has some limitations in experimental scope and theoretical analysis, these are addressable with reasonable revisions that would further strengthen an already strong paper. The method's ability to handle any general loss function while maintaining the computational efficiency of decision trees is particularly valuable for practitioners.
