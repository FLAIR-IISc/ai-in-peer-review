This paper tackles a long-standing problem in decision tree learning – the limitations of greedy induction. The authors propose a non-greedy framework, cleverly linking decision tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables. This connection allows them to formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling optimization via stochastic gradient descent. A key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p), making deep trees tractable. The experimental results demonstrate improved performance over greedy baselines, and the analysis of the regularization parameter ν provides useful insight.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The non-greedy approach and the connection to structured prediction represent a genuinely novel direction for decision tree learning. This is a significant departure from decades of relying on greedy algorithms. The formulation is clever and opens up avenues for applying techniques from structured prediction.
* **Technical Contribution:** The reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is crucial for enabling the practical application of their method to deep trees. This demonstrates a strong understanding of the algorithmic challenges and a creative solution.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on multiple datasets consistently show improvement over strong greedy baselines. The ablation study regarding the regularization parameter is insightful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is clearly laid out, and the mathematical formulation is presented logically.
* **Significance:** While not revolutionary, moving beyond greedy tree induction is an important step. The potential to apply this framework with different loss functions and kernel methods is promising.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are solid, they primarily focus on standard datasets. Exploring more complex, high-dimensional data or real-world applications would strengthen the argument for the practical significance of the approach.
* **SGD Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to hyperparameter tuning (η and ν). The paper discusses this, but a more thorough exploration of the sensitivity and potential automated tuning methods would be beneficial.
* **Theoretical Justification:** While the authors provide a proposition about the effect of the norm of W, a deeper theoretical understanding of why their approach leads to better generalization would be valuable. A more rigorous analysis of the bias-variance tradeoff could be considered.
* **Comparison to Ensemble Methods:** The paper focuses on comparing to single trees. While this is a valid choice to highlight the benefits of non-greedy *individual* trees, a comparison to established ensemble methods (like Random Forests) with comparable computational cost would be informative.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to decision tree learning.
* The technical contribution regarding computational complexity is significant.
* The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating improved performance.
* The paper is well-written and addresses a long-standing problem in the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The experiments could be more comprehensive, exploring a wider range of datasets.
* The theoretical justification could be strengthened.
* The lack of comparison to ensemble methods is a minor weakness.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a bold and innovative approach to decision tree learning. While not perfect, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses. I believe the connection to structured prediction is a clever insight, and the reduction in computational complexity is a significant technical achievement. The experimental results are promising, and the paper is well-written. 

I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should consider expanding the experimental evaluation and exploring a more thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed method. Adding a comparison to ensemble methods would also be valuable. However, the core contribution is strong enough to warrant publication even without these additions. The willingness to move beyond the established paradigm of greedy tree induction is highly commendable and positions this work as a potentially influential contribution to the field.
