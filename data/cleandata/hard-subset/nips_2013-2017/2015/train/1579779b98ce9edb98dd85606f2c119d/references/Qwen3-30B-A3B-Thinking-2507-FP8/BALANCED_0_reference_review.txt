

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach for optimizing decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a link between decision tree learning and structured prediction, introducing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent. A key contribution is the development of an efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm with O(d²p) complexity instead of the naive O(2^dp) complexity, making non-greedy learning of deep trees computationally feasible.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is well-motivated and novel. The paper effectively bridges these two areas, providing a principled framework for global optimization.
2. The derivation of the upper bound (Equation 7) and its justification through Proposition 1 are technically sound and well-explained.
3. The fast loss-augmented inference algorithm (O(d²p)) represents a significant technical contribution that addresses a critical computational bottleneck in non-greedy tree learning.
4. The regularization approach using row-wise constraints on W is well-justified and leads to better generalization performance, as demonstrated in the experiments.
5. The paper provides a clear connection to prior work, particularly CO2 [18], which is a special case of their approach applied greedily.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The experimental evaluation is limited in scope. Only four benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) are used, and the comparison is primarily against older greedy baselines rather than recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the claims.
2. The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate the superiority of their method over existing non-greedy approaches. Bennett's [1] work on global tree optimization is mentioned but not thoroughly compared, despite being a direct predecessor to this work.
3. The theoretical analysis of the approximation error introduced by the upper bound is limited. It would be valuable to quantify how tight the bound is in practice and how it relates to the actual loss.
4. The paper lacks discussion of practical considerations: How does the method handle categorical features? What is the behavior with missing data? How does it scale to very large datasets (e.g., millions of samples)?
5. The paper claims "better generalization performance" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence. The results in Figure 2 show improved test accuracy, but the magnitude of improvement is modest across datasets (typically 1-3% better than best greedy baseline), and the statistical significance is not addressed.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Expand the experimental section to include comparisons with recent state-of-the-art tree-based methods (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost) as well as other non-greedy approaches like Bennett [1]. This would better establish the novelty and practical value of the approach.
2. Provide a more thorough comparison with Bennett's work [1], explaining why their method is superior in terms of computational efficiency and generalization performance.
3. Include analysis of the approximation error introduced by the upper bound. How does the tightness of the bound affect the final model performance?
4. Add experiments on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet or other large-scale datasets) to demonstrate scalability beyond the 55,000 training points in Connect4.
5. Discuss practical implementation details, particularly how the method handles categorical features and missing data, which are critical for real-world applications.
6. Provide statistical significance testing for the reported accuracy improvements to strengthen the empirical claims.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound approach to non-greedy decision tree learning with a novel connection to structured prediction. The theoretical contributions are solid, and the efficient inference algorithm is a significant technical advance. However, the experimental evaluation is insufficiently comprehensive to convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the method over existing approaches. The modest performance improvements shown in the current experiments do not strongly justify the additional computational complexity of the method compared to standard greedy tree algorithms.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough experimental evaluation that includes comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods, a deeper analysis of the approximation error, and discussion of practical implementation considerations. As currently written, the paper makes an incremental contribution to the field of decision tree learning rather than a substantial advance.

**Recommendation:** Weak reject. The paper has merit but requires significant improvements in the experimental evaluation and theoretical justification to meet NeurIPS standards.
