This paper proposes a novel method for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The central idea is to reframe the problem of learning the split functions of a tree as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The path a data point takes through the tree is represented by a latent binary vector. As direct optimization of the empirical loss is intractable, the authors introduce a convex-concave upper bound on this loss, which serves as a surrogate objective. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for the "loss-augmented inference" step, which reduces the complexity from exponential to polynomial in the tree depth, making the approach practical for deeper trees. Experiments on several benchmark datasets show that the proposed non-greedy trees outperform various greedy baselines, including axis-aligned and oblique trees.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** The paper's primary strength is its elegant and principled formulation of the notoriously difficult global decision tree optimization problem. Casting tree induction as learning a structured prediction model with latent variables provides a solid theoretical foundation and connects this classic problem to modern machine learning frameworks. This is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The derivation of the surrogate objective as a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss is technically sound. The optimization procedure via SGD is a standard and well-understood approach for such objectives. The paper also correctly identifies the computational bottleneck and proposes a sensible approximation (the "fast loss-augmented inference") to address it.

3.  **Addressing a Fundamental Problem:** Greedy decision tree induction is a cornerstone of machine learning, but its local, one-node-at-a-time optimization is a well-known limitation. This work makes a serious and credible attempt to address this fundamental issue, which has been a long-standing challenge in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Baseline Comparisons:** While the paper compares against several single-tree baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, CO2), it completely omits a comparison to the most dominant and established methods for tree-based learning: ensembles. In practice, single decision trees are rarely used for state-of-the-art performance; instead, methods like Random Forests and Gradient Boosted Trees (e.g., XGBoost) are the standard. A central question is whether a single, complex, globally-optimized tree can outperform or even match the performance of an ensemble of simpler, greedily-trained trees. Without this comparison, the practical significance of the proposed method is difficult to assess. The reported accuracy gains over greedy single trees might be marginal compared to the gains achieved by simply using a standard Random Forest.

2.  **Questions Regarding Generalization Claims:** The authors claim their method is "less susceptible to overfitting" due to norm regularization. However, regularization is a standard technique, and its benefits are not unique to this framework. More importantly, the training accuracy curves in Figure 2 for datasets like Protein show the non-greedy method achieving significantly lower training accuracy than baselines. This might not be evidence of better generalization, but could instead indicate that the optimization of the surrogate objective is struggling to find a good solution or that the bound is too loose, preventing the model from fitting the training data adequately. This requires a more nuanced discussion.

3.  **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The proposed non-greedy method is initialized using a greedily constructed tree. The optimization of the non-convex surrogate objective via SGD will find a local minimum. The quality of this local minimum is likely highly dependent on the initialization. The paper does not provide any analysis of this sensitivity. How does the final performance vary with different initializations (e.g., random initialization vs. greedy)? This is a critical aspect for understanding the robustness and reliability of the proposed algorithm.

4.  **Complexity and Practicality:** While the "fast" algorithm reduces the per-step complexity to O(d²p), this is still considerably more expensive than a single pass of a greedy algorithm. The overall training time, involving many SGD iterations, is likely much higher than that of the baselines. While Figure 4 shows the improvement of the fast variant, it does not compare the total training time against the greedy methods. For large-scale applications where greedy tree induction excels due to its efficiency, the practicality of this more complex optimization remains a concern. The "Stable SGD" variant also feels somewhat ad-hoc and detracts from the elegance of the initial formulation.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper presents a novel, theoretically-grounded, and elegant formulation for a classic and challenging problem. The connection to latent structured prediction is insightful, and the work to make the optimization computationally feasible is a solid technical contribution.

*   **Con-acceptance:** The experimental validation is not thorough enough to support the claims of advancing the state of the art. The lack of comparison to established and powerful ensemble methods is a major omission. Furthermore, questions regarding initialization sensitivity and the true nature of the generalization improvement remain unaddressed. The practical benefits over established, highly-efficient greedy methods are not convincingly demonstrated.

**Recommendation:**

While the theoretical contribution of this paper is interesting, its current empirical evaluation is insufficient for a conference of NIPS's caliber. The claims of superiority are made against relatively weak baselines (single greedy trees), while ignoring the methods that are actually used in practice (ensembles). To be a compelling contribution, the paper would need to either: a) demonstrate performance competitive with or superior to standard Random Forests or Gradient Boosting, or b) provide a more thorough analysis of the properties of the learned trees (e.g., interpretability, size, robustness) that would justify the use of this more complex optimization procedure over established methods. As it stands, the work presents a new idea but lacks the extensive evidence and thorough validation required to endorse it. Therefore, I lean towards rejection.
