## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing problem of non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The authors argue that the inherent limitation of greedy tree induction – optimizing splits locally without considering downstream effects – hinders performance. They propose a novel framework that casts decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, allowing for joint optimization of split functions via a convex-concave upper bound optimized with stochastic gradient descent (SGD).  A key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) for deep trees, making the approach more practical. The paper demonstrates empirically that this non-greedy approach outperforms greedy baselines on several benchmark datasets.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader landscape of decision tree optimization. It acknowledges the NP-completeness of global tree optimization (Bennett, 1995) and contrasts its approach with previous attempts like multi-linear programming (Bennett, 2000), Mondrian processes (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2012) and soft splits (Jordan & Jacobs, 1994).  Crucially, it highlights advantages over these methods: handling general loss functions (unlike Bennett, 2000), maintaining efficient inference complexity (O(dp) vs. the cost of evaluating all experts in soft split methods), and providing a natural way to regularize the joint optimization. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables (Taskar et al., 2004; Yu & Joachims, 2009) is well-articulated and provides a solid theoretical foundation.  The authors appropriately acknowledge and compare to CO2 (Norouzi et al., 2015), framing it as a greedy instantiation of their proposed framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a significant contribution, opening the door to leveraging techniques from that field.
* **Efficiency Improvement:** Reducing the computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is crucial for making the approach feasible for deeper trees, a common requirement for complex datasets.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound provides a solid theoretical basis for the optimization process and a means to introduce regularization.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate superior performance compared to various greedy baselines across multiple datasets.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear progression of ideas. The explanation of the algorithm and the complexity reduction is particularly commendable.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors address practical concerns like initialization with greedy trees and the importance of regularization (ν) and learning rate (η) tuning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the O(d2p) complexity is a significant improvement, the paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of scalability for extremely high-dimensional data (large *p*) or very deep trees (large *d*).  How does performance degrade as these parameters grow?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The regularization parameter ν seems critical. More details on how to choose an appropriate value for ν in practice, beyond simply selecting the one that minimizes validation error, would be beneficial.  A sensitivity analysis of ν could be insightful.
* **Comparison Depth:** The comparisons to baselines are good, but a deeper analysis of *why* the non-greedy approach performs better would be helpful. Is it simply finding better splits, or is it the interaction between splits at different levels?
* **SSGD details:** While SSGD is mentioned, the details of how the 'stability' is enforced are a bit vague. More details on the implementation and the criteria for updating leaf assignments would strengthen this contribution.
* **Alternative Bounds:** Are there alternative upper bounds that could further improve efficiency or tightness? The authors focus on the Hamming ball bound but do not discuss other possibilities.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*  The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a long-standing problem in machine learning.
*  The efficiency improvement makes non-greedy optimization more practical.
*  The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*  The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of scalability limitations and hyperparameter tuning.
*  A deeper analysis of the reasons behind the performance improvements would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed framework is novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper is **suitable for acceptance** to NIPS.

**Rating:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)
