

# Peer Review: "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary and Context

This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy training of decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors address the fundamental limitation of greedy tree induction—where each split is optimized in isolation without considering future splits—by developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized jointly across the entire tree. The key technical innovation is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) through a clever reformulation of the loss upper bound. This work builds on recent advances in structured prediction (Yu & Joachims [25], Taskar et al. [24]) and connects decision trees to a well-established optimization framework.

The paper makes a compelling case for non-greedy tree learning, noting that while prior work (Bennett [1], Murthy & Salzburg [17]) has explored this direction, it has been limited by computational intractability or restricted to specific loss functions. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach not only handles general loss functions but also achieves better generalization performance than greedy baselines across multiple datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Innovation**: The core contribution—reducing the computational complexity of non-greedy tree learning from exponential to quadratic in tree depth—is significant. The fast loss-augmented inference algorithm (Section 6.2) is elegant and well-motivated, making deep non-greedy trees computationally feasible.

2. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper establishes a clear connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, providing a principled theoretical foundation. The derivation of the upper bound (Equation 7) is rigorous, and Proposition 1 offers valuable insight into the relationship between the scale of W and the tightness of the bound.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments (Section 7) are thorough and well-designed. The authors compare against multiple strong baselines (OC1, random oblique trees, CO2 [18]) across several benchmark datasets. The consistent improvement in generalization performance (Fig. 2) across different tree depths is particularly convincing.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The Stable SGD variant (Section 6.3) addresses a practical concern about inactive leaves that many non-greedy methods overlook. This demonstrates the authors' understanding of real-world implementation challenges.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Complexity Claim**: The paper claims O(d²p) complexity for the fast loss-augmented inference, but the analysis in Section 6.2 is somewhat superficial. The "d+1 distinct values" argument for f(ĝ) is correct, but the O(d²p) complexity assumes that evaluating each of the d+1 candidates requires O(dp) time. However, the tree depth d is typically logarithmic in the number of training samples (n), so d = O(log n), making the complexity effectively O(p log² n) rather than O(d²p). This nuance should be clarified.

2. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper cites CO2 [18] as a baseline but doesn't adequately compare with the more recent "Co2 Forest" paper (Norouzi et al., 2015) which is an extension of CO2. The authors' method appears to generalize CO2 to non-greedy training, but this relationship isn't clearly articulated.

3. **Theoretical Limitations**: While the paper provides a convex-concave upper bound, it doesn't address whether the local optimum found by SGD corresponds to a global optimum for the non-convex problem. The authors should discuss the limitations of their local optimization approach more thoroughly.

4. **Practical Scalability**: The experiments are limited to moderate-sized datasets (Connect4 has 55K training points). The paper doesn't address how the method scales to very large datasets (e.g., millions of samples) where mini-batch SGD would be essential. The computational complexity analysis should be extended to include the cost of mini-batch processing.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical derivations and thorough empirical validation. The claims are well-supported by experiments. The authors are careful to discuss limitations (e.g., the need for hyperparameter tuning of ν), which enhances credibility.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and logically organized. The connection between decision trees and structured prediction is clearly explained. However, some technical details (e.g., the exact complexity analysis) could be clarified.

**Originality**: This work significantly advances the state of the art in non-greedy decision tree learning. While the connection to structured prediction is novel, the key innovation is the efficient algorithm for deep trees, which addresses a fundamental limitation of prior work. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work.

**Significance**: The implications are substantial—non-greedy tree learning has been limited to shallow trees due to computational constraints, but this work enables training deep trees efficiently. The method could be particularly valuable for applications requiring compact, high-accuracy trees (e.g., real-time systems like the Kinect example cited).

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The technical innovation in reducing the computational complexity of non-greedy tree learning is substantial, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate improved generalization performance. The paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and addresses a meaningful limitation in existing decision tree methods.

The weaknesses noted are important but not critical to the paper's core contribution. The computational complexity claim could be clarified, and the comparison with Co2 Forest should be strengthened. However, these are refinements rather than fundamental flaws.

I recommend acceptance. This work has the potential to influence both theoretical research and practical applications of decision trees, making it a strong fit for NeurIPS.

**Pro Acceptance**: Significant technical contribution, clear theoretical foundation, thorough experimental validation, and practical impact on a widely used machine learning method.

**Con Acceptance**: Minor gaps in complexity analysis and comparison with the most recent related work.
