Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy, joint optimization method for training oblique decision trees by casting the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. Each split decision vector h ∈ {−1,+1}^m is treated as latent; the empirical loss is upper-bounded by a convex–concave surrogate that resembles margin-rescaled latent structured SVM objectives. The authors derive an exact loss-augmented inference procedure for the bound but show it is exponential in depth; they then introduce a tighter computational scheme by restricting loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), yielding O(d^2 p) per update. They train with projected SGD, include a “Stable SGD” (SSGD) variant that conservatively updates leaf assignments to avoid inactive leaves, and regularize the norm per split to control bound tightness and generalization. Experiments on several LibSVM multi-class datasets demonstrate improved accuracy (across depths) compared to axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2 (a greedy depth-1 variant), and show better robustness to overfitting and more favorable training-time scaling for the fast inference scheme.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on classical greedy tree induction (ID3, CART), oblique trees (OC1), margin-based trees and SVM-at-nodes, and soft hierarchical mixture-of-experts (HME). It explicitly connects tree training to latent structured SVMs and margin-rescaling [Tsochantaridis; Yu & Joachims] and uses an argument akin to maximizing w^Tφ(h,x). The fast loss-augmented inference via localized Hamming neighborhoods seems novel for tree learning. The work also relates to CO2 (continuous optimization of oblique splits) by moving from greedy single-node optimization to joint global optimization; and it contrasts to Mondrian forests (online extension) and soft/probabilistic trees (HME), retaining hard splits and O(dp) inference.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation linking decision-tree training to latent structured prediction, enabling a global objective over all splits.
- Derivation of an upper bound that is optimizable by (projected) SGD; practical insight on the role of scale and norm constraints.
- Computational contribution: fast loss-augmented inference via Hamming-ball restriction, yielding O(d^2 p) updates with no change to test-time O(dp).
- Practical algorithmic refinements (initialization via greedy/CO2; SSGD to stabilize leaf usage).
- Empirical evidence across multiple datasets and depths showing consistent improvements over strong greedy baselines; useful analysis of the regularization parameter ν and its effect on pruning/overfitting.
- Good discussion of complexity, and a realistic initialization strategy that practitioners could adopt.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical depth is limited: no convergence analysis (beyond typical nonconvex SGD) or generalization bounds; the impact of the Hamming-ball relaxation on the tightness of the bound and final accuracy is not quantified.
- Experimental scope could be broader. Results focus on single trees; while this isolates the effect, many applications rely on forests or boosted ensembles. It would help to show that replacing greedy trees with the proposed trees as base learners brings net gains, or to compare to strong ensemble baselines.
- Ablations are incomplete: the paper introduces SSGD and two inference variants; however, quantitative comparisons of accuracy (not only runtime) for exact vs fast loss-augmentation, and with/without SSGD, are missing or underemphasized.
- Scalability evidence is modest: datasets are medium-sized; claims of applicability to deep trees or large-scale training (e.g., Kinect) would benefit from larger-scale experiments and memory/throughput profiling. The memory cost of W for deep trees (m ≈ 2^d−1) is not discussed.
- Some clarity gaps: details of SSGD (update cadence for assignments, stopping criteria), full training protocols, and hyperparameter search ranges could be more explicit for reproducibility.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the bound derivation is plausible, the optimization is standard and carefully regularized, and the complexity analysis is clear. Empirical validation supports the main claims, though additional ablations and larger-scale tests would strengthen the case.

Clarity
Generally well-written with precise notation. Algorithm 1 is helpful; however, a pseudocode block for fast loss-augmented inference and SSGD would improve reproducibility. The role of ν and its interaction with margin/bound tightness are nicely explained.

Originality
The latent-structured formulation for hard decision trees with an efficiently computable loss-augmented step appears original, as does the Hamming-ball relaxation for tractable joint optimization. The work goes beyond CO2’s greedy setting.

Significance
Potentially impactful: provides a principled alternative to greedy induction that improves single-tree accuracy and robustness to overfitting, and could serve as a drop-in upgrade for tree-based systems or ensembles. The lack of ensemble-scale demonstrations and limited theoretical guarantees temper the significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled formulation with a concrete, efficient algorithmic contribution.
- Consistent empirical gains across datasets and tree depths.
- Maintains O(dp) inference; practical initialization pathway.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited ablations and scalability experiments.
- No theory beyond the surrogate bound; unclear impact of fast inference on solution quality.
- Focused on single trees; limited evidence on integration into forests/boosting.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an ablation comparing: (i) exact vs fast loss-augmented inference on small trees (accuracy vs speed), (ii) SGD vs SSGD, and (iii) with/without per-row norm constraints.
- Report results for ensembles: e.g., bagging or gradient boosting using the proposed non-greedy trees as base learners.
- Add larger-scale benchmarks and discuss memory/compute trade-offs for deep trees; include wall-clock times, batch sizes, and hardware.
- Clarify SSGD details (update frequency, convergence criterion) and provide pseudocode.
- Explore sparsity-inducing regularization on W and/or feature subsampling to reduce memory and improve interpretability.
- Consider a brief discussion of generalization (e.g., margin-based intuition) and when the Hamming-ball relaxation is expected to be tight.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: Medium
