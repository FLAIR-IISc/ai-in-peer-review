

# Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The key insight is to represent the decision path through a tree as a latent binary variable and develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate that their approach reduces the computational complexity from exponential (O(2^dp)) to quadratic (O(d^2p)) in tree depth, making deep tree learning feasible while maintaining the computational advantages of decision trees.

The paper builds on connections between decision trees and structured prediction (citing [25]), extending prior work on non-greedy tree learning (e.g., Bennett [1]) by addressing computational scalability. Unlike Bennett's approach limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss, the authors' framework handles any loss function while preserving the O(dp) inference complexity of decision trees. The paper also relates to recent work on structured prediction with latent variables [23, 24, 25] and builds upon Norouzi and Fleet's reformulation of linear threshold functions [19].

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a significant technical contribution by reducing the computational complexity of non-greedy decision tree learning from exponential to quadratic in tree depth. This is a substantial improvement over previous non-greedy approaches.
2. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear justification for the upper bound and regularization strategy, including Proposition 1 on the relationship between the norm of W and bound tightness.
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, CO2) across four benchmark datasets. The results consistently show that non-greedy trees achieve better generalization performance across various tree depths.
4. The authors provide a principled way to control overfitting through norm regularization (ν), which implicitly controls leaf pruning during training, addressing a key limitation of greedy methods.
5. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper does not sufficiently contrast its approach with Bennett's earlier work [1], which also proposed a non-greedy approach. The authors acknowledge Bennett's work but don't clearly explain how their method improves upon it beyond computational complexity (e.g., handling general loss functions, scalability to deeper trees).
2. The computational trade-off analysis is incomplete. The authors demonstrate that their method scales better for deeper trees, but don't address how it compares to greedy methods for shallow trees (where greedy approaches might be faster due to simpler optimization).
3. The paper lacks a detailed discussion of how the method scales to extremely large datasets or high-dimensional features, which is crucial for real-world applications of decision trees.
4. The comparison with other recent non-greedy approaches (e.g., those using reinforcement learning) is limited, which weakens the claim of novelty.
5. The paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how the regularization parameter ν affects the tree structure (e.g., number of active leaves, tree depth) beyond showing it affects generalization performance.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong technical contribution to the field of decision tree learning by providing a computationally efficient framework for non-greedy tree optimization. The theoretical development is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the approach has clear potential for practical applications. While the paper could better contextualize its contribution relative to prior non-greedy approaches and provide more detailed scalability analysis, these are refinements rather than fundamental flaws.

The significance of this work lies in addressing a decades-old limitation of decision tree learning (greediness) in a computationally feasible way, with results showing consistent improvements in generalization performance. The framework could be extended to other tree-based methods (e.g., forests) and potentially inspire further research on global optimization of tree-structured models.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field with a technically sound approach, compelling experimental results, and clear potential for impact. The weaknesses identified are important but do not undermine the paper's core contributions, and the authors could easily address them in a revision.
