- Summary: Proposes a non-greedy decision tree optimization via structured prediction with latent variables, introduces a convex-concave upper bound optimized by SGD, and reduces complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p), showing empirical gains over greedy baselines.
- Strength: Addresses an important, long-standing limitation of greedy decision tree induction.
- Strength: Presents a novel, principled formulation linking decision trees to structured prediction with latent variables.
- Strength: Achieves a crucial computational complexity reduction (O(2dp) → O(d2p)) enabling deeper trees.
- Strength: Empirical results on multiple benchmarks show consistent improvement over greedy baselines, including CO2.
- Strength: Clear writing and useful algorithmic details (e.g., Alg 1) that aid reproducibility.
- Strength: Insightful discussion of the regularization parameter ν and its relation to pruning.
- Weakness: Core techniques (structured prediction, convex-concave optimization, SGD) are established; novelty is mainly in their combination and application.
- Weakness: Performance is sensitive to the regularization hyperparameter ν and requires careful tuning.
- Weakness: Scalability beyond the tested datasets is unclear; experiments are on relatively small datasets (e.g., 55k examples).
- Weakness: Dependence on greedy (CO2) initialization may bias results; sensitivity to initialization not fully explored.
- Weakness: Lacks theoretical generalization bounds or formal analysis of generalization error.
- Assessment: Quality 8/10 — technically sound with solid empirical support but missing theoretical guarantees.
- Assessment: Clarity 9/10 — well-organized and easy to follow.
- Assessment: Originality 7/10 — novel combination/application, but not fundamentally new techniques.
- Assessment: Significance 8/10 — potentially impactful if scalable to very large datasets.
- Arguments for acceptance: Tackles an important problem with a principled approach, demonstrates empirical improvements, offers significant efficiency gains, and is well-written.
- Arguments against acceptance: Relies on existing techniques, uncertain scalability, lacks theoretical guarantees, and is hyperparameter-sensitive.
- Overall recommendation: Accept.
