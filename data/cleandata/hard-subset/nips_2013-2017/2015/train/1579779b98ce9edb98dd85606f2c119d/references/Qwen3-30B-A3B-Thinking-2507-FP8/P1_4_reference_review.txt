

# Peer Review: "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy optimization of decision trees that addresses a fundamental limitation of conventional greedy tree induction methods. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating the tree's empirical loss in terms of a latent binary decision variable for each split node. They develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), with a key technical contribution being the reduction of computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) through a modified surrogate objective. The paper introduces Stable SGD (SSGD) to handle inactive leaves, and demonstrates that non-greedy trees achieve better generalization performance across multiple benchmark datasets compared to greedy baselines.

The work builds upon prior efforts in non-greedy tree optimization (e.g., Bennett [1]) but significantly advances the field by providing a scalable optimization method. It also relates to structured prediction literature [23-25], extending latent variable models to decision trees in a novel way.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The reduction of computational complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth is a substantial advancement over previous non-greedy approaches, making the method practically applicable to deep trees. This is a major technical contribution that addresses a critical barrier to non-greedy tree learning.

2. **Theoretical Foundation**: The connection to structured prediction with latent variables provides a strong theoretical foundation. The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound is well-justified and supported by a clear proof.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments consistently show that non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines in generalization performance across multiple datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) and tree depths. The ablation study on the regularization parameter ν demonstrates its meaningful impact on model complexity.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The SSGD algorithm effectively addresses the issue of inactive leaves, making the method more robust and usable in practice. The paper provides a clear, detailed algorithm with implementation considerations.

5. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to solution to experimental validation. The mathematical notation is precise and the figures are well-designed.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Methods**: The paper does not compare with more recent non-greedy or deep tree learning methods that have emerged since the cited references (e.g., neural decision forests, deep decision trees). This weakens the significance claim.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: While the paper provides a convex-concave upper bound, it does not establish convergence guarantees for the optimization procedure or theoretical bounds on the generalization error compared to greedy methods.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The O(d²p) complexity claim for fast loss-augmented inference requires more detailed justification. The paper states the complexity but doesn't fully explain how it's achieved.

4. **Scalability to Large Datasets**: Experiments are limited to moderate-sized datasets (Connect4 has 55,000 training points), without demonstrating scalability to very large datasets or discussing potential bottlenecks for extremely large-scale problems.

5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper mentions tuning ν and η but doesn't thoroughly analyze how sensitive the performance is to these hyperparameters across different datasets.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors carefully discuss limitations (exponential growth of latent space in naive formulation) and provide a solution. The experiments are thorough and show consistent improvements over baselines. The minor weakness is the lack of theoretical guarantees for the optimization procedure.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The key ideas are introduced gradually with precise mathematical notation. The algorithm description is detailed and easy to follow. No clarity issues were found.

**Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution by providing a scalable non-greedy approach that differs substantially from previous work. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is novel, and the technical contribution of reducing complexity is significant. The paper properly cites and distinguishes itself from related work.

**Significance**: The results demonstrate that non-greedy optimization leads to better generalization performance, which is a significant practical improvement. The method could be particularly valuable for applications where interpretability of tree structure is important. The approach has the potential to influence future work on tree-based methods, especially in contexts where model interpretability is valued alongside accuracy.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. The paper makes a significant technical contribution to decision tree learning, providing a scalable non-greedy optimization method that outperforms greedy baselines in generalization performance. The theoretical foundation is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the paper is clearly written.

## Specific Suggestions

1. Clarify the computational complexity analysis of the fast loss-augmented inference step, explaining why it's O(d²p) rather than O(2^dp).

2. Add a comparison with more recent non-greedy tree learning methods (e.g., neural decision forests) to strengthen the significance claim.

3. Provide more insight into the relationship between regularization parameter ν and tree structure (e.g., how it affects the number of active leaves).

4. Include a discussion of scalability to very large datasets, perhaps with runtime measurements on larger datasets.

5. Add a sensitivity analysis of the method to hyperparameters ν and η across different datasets.

## Final Assessment

This is a strong contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The non-greedy approach addresses a fundamental limitation of conventional tree induction methods, and the technical solution to the computational complexity problem is both elegant and practical. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method over greedy alternatives. I believe this work would be of significant interest to the NIPS community, particularly to researchers working on interpretable machine learning and tree-based methods. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission and should be accepted.
