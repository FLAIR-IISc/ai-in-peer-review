

# Review: "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach for decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and develop an efficient algorithm for optimizing this bound using stochastic gradient descent with fast loss-augmented inference, reducing complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p). The paper demonstrates that their method produces trees with better generalization performance than greedy approaches across multiple benchmark datasets.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with rigorous theoretical justification. The upper bound (Equation 7) is well-proven, and the complexity analysis is clear and convincing. The authors properly acknowledge limitations (e.g., the focus on binary trees rather than N-ary trees) and provide a principled way to regularize the model to prevent overfitting. The experimental results consistently show that their non-greedy trees achieve better test performance than greedy baselines across multiple datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) without overfitting. The comparison with CO2 [18] (a greedy variant of their method) is particularly compelling as it isolates the non-greedy aspect of their contribution.

However, the paper could have provided more thorough analysis of the limitations. While the authors mention that their method focuses on binary trees, they don't adequately address how this limitation might impact real-world applications where N-ary trees are common. Additionally, the hyperparameter selection process (ν and η) could be more detailed, as these parameters significantly affect the tree structure and performance.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with a clear logical flow from problem statement to solution to experiments. Key concepts like the tree navigation function and latent variable formulation are well-explained with appropriate notation. The algorithms (particularly Algorithm 1) are described precisely, and the complexity analysis is straightforward. The figures (especially Fig. 2 showing accuracy across tree depths) effectively illustrate the main findings. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by establishing a novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables. While prior work (e.g., Bennett [1] and CO2 [18]) has explored non-greedy tree optimization, this paper presents a fundamentally different formulation that enables efficient optimization. The key technical innovation—reducing the loss-augmented inference complexity from exponential to O(d^2p)—is both novel and impactful. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly by showing that their non-greedy approach outperforms CO2 (which is a greedy special case of their framework).

## Significance
The paper addresses a long-standing limitation of decision tree learning (greedy node optimization) that has persisted for decades. The proposed method demonstrates consistent improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets, which is significant for practitioners who rely on decision trees for interpretability and efficiency. The ability to handle any general loss function (not just classification or regression) while maintaining the O(dp) inference complexity is particularly valuable. The paper also opens promising avenues for future work (e.g., sparse split functions via sparsity-inducing regularization, kernel methods for higher-order splits).

## Strengths
- Novel theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction with latent variables
- Efficient algorithm with O(d^2p) complexity instead of exponential complexity
- Strong empirical results showing better generalization than greedy methods
- Clear and well-organized presentation with thorough theoretical justification
- Practical approach that can be integrated into existing decision tree frameworks

## Weaknesses
- Limited to binary trees (though the authors acknowledge this limitation)
- Hyperparameter selection process (ν and η) could be more thoroughly explained
- Limited comparison with more recent non-greedy approaches beyond greedy baselines
- The practical impact of the speedup (Fig. 4) on very large datasets isn't quantified

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of decision tree learning by addressing a fundamental limitation of the standard greedy approach. The theoretical insights are novel and well-justified, the algorithm is efficient and practical, and the empirical results consistently demonstrate superior generalization performance. While the focus on binary trees is a limitation, the method represents a significant step forward in non-greedy tree learning. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions (primarily to clarify the hyperparameter selection process and discuss the binary tree limitation more thoroughly).

## Overall Assessment
This paper meets all four NIPS criteria effectively. It is of high technical quality with sound theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. It is clearly written and well-organized. It makes a significant original contribution by connecting decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables and developing an efficient algorithm for this problem. The significance of the work is substantial, as it addresses a long-standing limitation in one of the most widely used machine learning models. The paper advances the state of the art in decision tree learning in a demonstrable way.
