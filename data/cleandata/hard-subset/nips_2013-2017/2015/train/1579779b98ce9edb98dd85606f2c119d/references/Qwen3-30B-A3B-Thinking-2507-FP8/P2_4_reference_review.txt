

# Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently using stochastic gradient descent, addressing the fundamental limitation of greedy tree induction that optimizes splits one node at a time without considering the global impact.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on the long-standing observation that conventional decision tree induction is greedy, optimizing splits in isolation rather than considering the global structure of the tree. Previous work like Bennett's multi-linear programming approach (1) was limited to binary classification and small trees, while Hierarchical Mixture of Experts (13) uses soft splits that lose the computational advantages of decision trees. The authors make a key connection to structured prediction with latent variables, which has been a fruitful area at NIPS (e.g., Yu and Joachims 2009, Taskar et al. 2003). Their formulation differs from prior work by providing a practical optimization method with theoretical guarantees.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The authors provide a proof of their upper bound (supplementary material) and demonstrate through Proposition 1 that increasing the norm of W tightens the bound. The complexity analysis is thorough, showing how the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm reduces complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p), making deep trees feasible. The experimental results on standard benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) convincingly demonstrate that non-greedy trees achieve better generalization performance across tree depths compared to greedy baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2). The paper acknowledges limitations, including the need to tune the regularization parameter ν, and provides a clear explanation of how it implicitly controls leaf pruning.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulation is clear and consistent, with well-chosen notation. The problem formulation section effectively sets up the decision tree learning problem, and the connection to structured prediction is well-motivated. The algorithms (especially Algorithm 1 and the fast loss-augmented inference) are clearly described with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental setup is transparent, with proper data splits and hyperparameter tuning procedures.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant original contribution by framing non-greedy decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. While previous work has attempted non-greedy optimization (Bennett 1), this paper provides the first efficient algorithm that scales to deep trees. The key insight is recognizing that the loss-augmented inference problem can be solved efficiently by restricting to Hamming balls of radius 1 around the current split decisions, which is novel and well-justified. This approach is substantially different from prior NIPS papers on decision trees (e.g., Norouzi et al. 2015 on CO2 forests).

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Decision trees have been dominated by greedy methods for decades, and this paper provides a theoretically sound, efficient alternative that improves generalization performance. The experiments show that non-greedy trees are less susceptible to overfitting and achieve better accuracy at various depths. The method can handle any general loss function while maintaining the O(dp) inference complexity of decision trees. The authors also identify promising future directions, including learning sparse split functions and applying the kernel trick.

## Strengths

1. Novel theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction with latent variables
2. Efficient algorithm (O(d^2p)) for loss-augmented inference, making deep trees feasible
3. Strong experimental results showing better generalization than greedy baselines
4. Clear writing and well-organized presentation
5. Comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines including CO2 (a greedy variant)

## Weaknesses

1. Limited comparison with recent, widely-used decision tree implementations (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM)
2. Does not address why practitioners would prefer this single-tree approach over ensemble methods (random forests, gradient boosting), which are the standard in practice
3. Limited discussion of sensitivity to the regularization parameter ν beyond tuning it on validation sets
4. Experiments are limited to standard benchmark datasets without scaling to larger-scale problems

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The theoretical insights are novel and well-supported, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The paper's main weakness is the limited discussion of how this approach fits into the broader landscape of tree-based methods used in practice, where ensembles dominate.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should consider adding a brief discussion comparing their approach to ensemble methods and explaining why single-tree non-greedy optimization might be preferable in certain scenarios (e.g., interpretability, computational constraints). They should also consider adding a comparison with XGBoost or LightGBM to better position their work against state-of-the-art.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria at a high level: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), offers a novel approach (Originality), and provides a meaningful advancement in decision tree learning (Significance). The work has the potential to influence both theoretical understanding and practical applications of decision trees.
