Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for binary decision trees by jointly optimizing all split parameters under a global surrogate objective. The key idea is to view the vector of split decisions as a latent binary variable h ∈ {−1,+1}^m and to upper bound the empirical loss with a latent-structured hinge–like objective: max_g(g^T W x + loss at leaf(g)) − max_h(h^T W x). This provides a difference-of-convex (DC) piecewise-linear surrogate that can be optimized with SGD under per-row norm constraints on W. A practical contribution is a “fast loss-augmented inference” variant restricting g to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing the per-step complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p). The method also introduces a “Stable SGD” variant that freezes data-to-leaf assignments for several updates to avoid leaf inactivity. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets indicate improved single-tree generalization vs. greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) and a greedy one-node-at-a-time variant (CO2).

Relation to prior work
The latent-variable formulation connects to structured prediction with latent variables (Yu & Joachims 2009) and margin-rescaled structured SVMs (Tsochantaridis et al.). The idea of oblique splits and max-margin trees links to Bennett & Blue and follow-ups on margins in trees. Soft probabilistic gating relates to Hierarchical Mixture of Experts (Jordan & Jacobs), with the distinction that here inference remains hard-routing and O(dp). The “CO2” baseline is a closely related greedy special case. The paper’s upper bound is akin to the structured hinge bound but adapted to tree routing via the f(g) navigation function.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: casting hard splits as latent decisions and building a DC surrogate that is optimizable with SGD is appealing and well motivated.
- Efficiency: the Hamming-1 restriction leading to O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference is a practical and nontrivial improvement that preserves hard-routing inference O(dp).
- Generality: the framework supports generic losses (log-loss, squared loss) and maintains the inference-time advantages of trees.
- Empirical trends: across multiple datasets and depths, non-greedy trees reportedly generalize better and overfit less than greedy baselines; ν acts as a principled regularizer.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and completeness
- Surrogate properties: The paper states L′(W,Θ) is “piecewise linear and convex-concave in W.” More precisely, L′ is a sum of convex terms minus convex terms (DC), not convex-concave in the usual sense. Please correct the terminology and, if possible, connect to standard DC programming or provide a rationale for using simple SGD on a DC objective.
- Proof/justification gaps: The crucial bound (7) is only sketched; the “algebraic proof” is deferred to the supplement. Similarly for (13). Given the centrality of these bounds, at least an outline with key steps and assumptions (e.g., properties of f) should be in the main paper.
- Scaling proposition: Proposition 1 shows the bound tightens as ‖W‖ grows, but the result is unsurprising (max of linear terms dominates loss) and raises a degeneracy that is only informally addressed with hard constraints. No generalization analysis or margin-based guarantees are provided to justify the chosen per-row constraints.
- SSGD objective mismatch: Freezing assignments yields a different bound (14) and effectively optimizes a surrogate under additional constraints. There is no discussion of what objective SSGD is optimizing overall, or convergence guarantees. An ablation quantifying SSGD’s contribution vs. plain SGD is missing.
- Complexity details: The claimed O(d^2 p) fast inference is plausible, but the algorithmic steps are not specified precisely. For example, do you compute all wix along both the original path and the d sibling subpaths? What is precomputed and reused per example to avoid O(mp) cost? A pseudocode and a proof/sketch of the d+1 candidate leaves argument would improve clarity and reproducibility.
- Initialization bias: The method initializes with an axis-aligned tree and CO2 refinement, whereas baselines may be trained from scratch. This could advantage the proposed method; please equalize initialization/training protocols or control for it.

Empirical evaluation
- Scope: Only four datasets, moderate scale, and single-tree evaluation. Modern baselines (e.g., gradient boosting, XGBoost/LightGBM oblique variants) are missing. Given that practitioners almost always use ensembles, evaluating whether the joint training still helps in forests/boosting is important for significance.
- Reporting: Figures only, no tables; numerical values and standard deviations are not reported. There is no statistical testing across random splits. Validation strategy is single random split for some datasets; no repeats.
- Ablations: No quantitative analysis of ν’s effect beyond a qualitative figure; no ablation of SSGD; no study of the Hamming-radius-1 vs. full loss-augmented inference tradeoff on accuracy; no sensitivity to η, batch size, momentum, projection frequency.
- Efficiency: Wall-clock comparisons vs. OC1 and greedy oblique methods (including CO2) are not shown; Figure 4 compares two variants of your own method only.

Clarity and presentation
- Notation/typos: Several grammatical errors (“by parameterized a weight vector”), inconsistent spacing (m+ 1), and occasional unclear statements (e.g., “O(mp) ⊆ O(2^d p)” needs careful qualification; m≈2^d−1 for full trees). Algorithm 1 uses both line-numbered and colon-prefixed steps inconsistently; the projection step (line 8) would benefit from clear notation and justification.
- Missing details: No mini-batch size, momentum coefficient, learning-rate schedule, number of epochs/steps, stopping criteria, or implementation details for maintaining active vs. inactive leaves.
- Figures and supplements are referenced but not described in sufficient detail to reconstruct the results.
- References: Many entries are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages), inconsistent, or malformed (e.g., “et al, J. Shotton, …”; “The elements of statistical learning (Ed, T. Hastie…”). Please fix to a consistent NeurIPS style.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and plausible framework for joint optimization of decision tree splits via latent structured surrogates.
- Practical inference-time efficiency is retained; training-time efficiency improved via Hamming-ball inference.
- Empirical indications of better generalization for single trees.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical development (DC nature, convergence/optimization guarantees, incomplete proofs).
- Limited and incomplete empirical evaluation; missing strong modern baselines, ablations, and statistical rigor.
- Clarity/reproducibility issues and numerous presentation/formatting problems.
- SSGD modifies the objective without principled analysis.

Recommendation
Reject. The core idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the current submission falls short on rigor, completeness, and presentation. Strengthening the theory (clear DC framing, proofs, and optimization discussion), providing precise fast-inference algorithms, expanding and systematizing experiments (including ensembles and modern baselines), and fixing the many clarity and reference issues would substantially improve the paper.
