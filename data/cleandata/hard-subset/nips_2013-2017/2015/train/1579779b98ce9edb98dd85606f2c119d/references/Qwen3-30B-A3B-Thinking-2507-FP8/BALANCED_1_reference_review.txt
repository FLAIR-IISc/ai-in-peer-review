

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning that addresses a fundamental limitation of conventional greedy methods (ID3, CART). The authors formulate decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, where the latent variables represent the binary decisions at each split node. They develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) through a clever reformulation of the loss upper bound, making deep trees feasible to train.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The connection between decision trees and structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and well-executed. The authors provide a rigorous proof for their upper bound and demonstrate how it can be efficiently optimized, addressing a significant limitation in the literature.

2. **Technical Innovation:** The complexity reduction from exponential to polynomial in tree depth (O(d^2p) vs O(2^dp)) is a substantial contribution that makes the approach practically applicable to deeper trees. The fast loss-augmented inference algorithm is particularly well-motivated and effective.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The paper includes thorough experiments across multiple datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) showing consistent improvements over greedy baselines. The results demonstrate better generalization performance and reduced overfitting compared to conventional methods.

4. **Practical Implementation:** The authors provide a clear algorithm (Algorithm 1) with implementation details, including the Stable SGD variant that addresses the issue of inactive leaves. The comparison with CO2 [18] effectively isolates the benefit of non-greedy optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Comparative Baselines:** The paper compares with relatively outdated methods (axis-aligned trees, OC1) but does not include comparisons with modern tree-based methods like XGBoost, LightGBM, or CatBoost. These represent the current state of the art in tree-based methods and would strengthen the significance claim.

2. **Computational Complexity Claim:** The paper claims "O(d^2p) complexity" but doesn't clarify that d (tree depth) is typically O(log n) for balanced trees with n nodes. The practical complexity should be described as O((log n)^2 p), which better reflects the scaling behavior for practical tree sizes.

3. **Loss Function Generality:** While the paper claims to handle "any general loss function," the experiments are limited to classification (log loss) and regression (squared loss). Additional experiments with other loss functions (e.g., hinge loss for SVMs) would better validate this claim.

4. **Training Time Comparison:** The paper shows the scalability of their fast loss-augmented inference algorithm but doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison of total training time with baseline methods. Without this, it's unclear whether the non-greedy approach is actually practical for large-scale applications.

5. **Regularization Sensitivity:** The paper tunes the regularization parameter ν on the validation set but doesn't analyze how sensitive the results are to this parameter. A sensitivity analysis would provide more insight into the robustness of the approach.

**Originality and Significance:**

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to a long-standing problem in decision tree learning. While non-greedy approaches to tree optimization have been attempted before (e.g., Bennett's work [1]), this paper provides a theoretically sound and computationally efficient solution that addresses the main limitations of prior methods. The connection to structured prediction is particularly novel and could inspire further research at the intersection of tree-based models and structured prediction.

The significance is somewhat limited by the lack of comparison with modern tree-based methods and the absence of large-scale experiments. However, the theoretical framework is strong and could lead to practical implementations for specific applications where non-greedy optimization provides clear benefits.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation is precise, and the technical development is logically presented. The algorithm description is sufficiently detailed for reproduction. However, some of the complexity claims could be clarified, and the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how the method scales with dataset size (not just tree depth).

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and technically innovative approach to non-greedy decision tree learning. While the core contribution is significant and well-executed, the paper would be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above. The lack of comparison with modern tree-based methods is a notable limitation for NIPS, where the state of the art in tree-based methods has evolved substantially since the cited works.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. Add comparisons with modern tree-based methods (XGBoost, LightGBM) to better establish significance.
2. Clarify the complexity claim to reflect that d is typically logarithmic in tree size.
3. Include experiments with additional loss functions to better validate the generality claim.
4. Provide a comprehensive training time comparison with baselines.
5. Add a sensitivity analysis for the regularization parameter ν.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning, and with the suggested improvements, it would be a strong candidate for NIPS. The theoretical insights and practical algorithm are both novel and well-executed, addressing a longstanding limitation in tree-based methods.
