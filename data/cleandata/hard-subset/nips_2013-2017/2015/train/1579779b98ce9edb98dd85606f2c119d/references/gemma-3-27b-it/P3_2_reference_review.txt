## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to non-greedy decision tree learning, addressing a long-standing limitation of traditional methods. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is insightful, and the proposed surrogate objective, along with the optimizations to reduce its complexity, are significant contributions. While the experimental results are promising, they are somewhat limited in scope and could be strengthened. I believe the paper is a strong submission to NIPS, offering a valuable addition to the field, but some revisions are needed to address the concerns outlined below.

**Summary:**

The authors tackle the inherent limitations of greedy decision tree induction – the inability to refine splits based on downstream tree structure – by formulating the problem as a structured prediction task. They introduce a latent variable representing split decisions, derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, and propose an efficient algorithm (with a complexity reduction from O(2dp) to O(d2p)) to optimize this bound using stochastic gradient descent. The paper establishes a link between decision trees and structured prediction, and presents a framework for jointly optimizing split functions across the tree, potentially leading to more compact and generalizable trees.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Theoretical Contribution:** The formulation of decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables is a clever and original idea. The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound and the complexity reduction are significant theoretical contributions.
* **Addressing a Fundamental Limitation:**  The paper directly addresses a core weakness of traditional decision tree algorithms - the lack of global optimization.  This has been a known challenge for decades, and the authors’ approach offers a promising solution.
* **Efficient Optimization:**  The reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is crucial for enabling the training of deeper trees.  This makes the approach more practical and scalable.
* **Regularization & Generalization:** The incorporation of a regularization term (controlling the norm of split weights) is a sound approach to prevent overfitting and improve generalization performance, as demonstrated in the experiments.
* **Clear Motivation & Connection to Existing Work:** The authors clearly articulate the problem, relate their work to prior attempts (Bennett’s work, Hierarchical Mixture of Experts, Mondrian Processes), and highlight the advantages of their approach. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Scope & Baselines:** While the experimental results demonstrate the potential of the proposed method, the scope is somewhat limited.  Comparing against more recent and state-of-the-art tree-based methods (e.g., gradient boosted trees like XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, or recent deep learning-based tree architectures) would strengthen the evaluation.  The baselines chosen are mostly older methods.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The results seem sensitive to the regularization parameter ν, requiring careful tuning.  A more detailed analysis of the sensitivity to this parameter and its impact on performance would be beneficial.  The explanation of how ν influences tree pruning is interesting but lacks deeper insight.
* **Practicality & Scalability:** While the complexity reduction is important, the O(d2p) complexity might still be a bottleneck for very high-dimensional data and extremely deep trees.  More discussion on the practical scalability of the method would be valuable.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** The paper does not perform a thorough ablation study to analyze the impact of different components of the proposed framework (e.g., the specific form of the surrogate objective, the regularization term).  This would help to understand the individual contributions of each component.
* **SSGD Explanation:** The motivation for Stable SGD (SSGD) is clear – preventing inactive leaves. However, the details of its implementation and the rationale for its improvement over vanilla SGD are not fully elaborated.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of decision tree learning.
* It addresses a long-standing problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The proposed method demonstrates promising empirical results, suggesting its potential for improved performance.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameters and scalability concerns need further investigation.
* The explanation of some of the algorithmic choices, like SSGD, could be more detailed.



**Recommendations & Questions for the Authors:**

* **Expand the experimental evaluation:**  Include comparisons with more recent and competitive tree-based methods (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost). Also, consider datasets with higher dimensionality.
* **Analyze hyperparameter sensitivity:**  Provide a more in-depth analysis of the impact of the regularization parameter ν on performance, and consider techniques for automatically tuning this parameter.
* **Discuss scalability limitations:**  Address the potential scalability issues associated with the O(d2p) complexity, and discuss strategies for mitigating these issues.
* **Conduct ablation studies:**  Perform ablation studies to evaluate the individual contributions of different components of the proposed framework.
* **Elaborate on SSGD:**  Provide a more detailed explanation of the SSGD algorithm and the rationale behind its improvements over vanilla SGD. Quantify the benefits of SSGD in the experimental section.
* **Discuss the choice of initializing W with a greedy tree:** How much does performance depend on this initialization? Is a random initialization viable?



**Overall, I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions.** The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning, and the proposed method has the potential to improve the performance and generalization capabilities of tree-based models. Addressing the concerns outlined above will further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
