- Proposes non-greedy decision tree training by treating split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a global surrogate objective.
- Encodes split decisions with latent code h ∈ {−1,+1}^m and derives a convex–concave upper bound inspired by latent-SSVMs for joint optimization of W and Θ.
- Introduces per-row norm constraints on W to regularize and prevent degenerate tightening of the bound.
- Develops a fast loss-augmented inference by restricting search to a Hamming ball (radius 1) around sgn(Wx), reducing complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p).
- Uses projected SGD and a “Stable SGD” (SSGD) variant that freezes leaf assignments temporarily to avoid inactive leaves.
- Experiments on LibSVM multiclass datasets show consistent test accuracy improvements over several single-tree baselines and reduced overfitting.
- Situates work relative to greedy tree induction, oblique trees, soft trees, and latent-structured SVMs; frames the explicit link to latent structured prediction as novel for trees.
- Fast Hamming-ball inference is a pragmatic contribution enabling deeper tree training.
- Derivation of the upper bound and the role of the norm constraint are sound and well-motivated (Proposition 1).
- The fast bound (Eq. 13) is valid and the observation that only d+1 leaves need consideration for one-bit flips is correct.
- Projected SGD optimization is reasonable and update sparsity justifies efficient per-step computation.
- Experimental results support the claim that joint non-greedy optimization improves generalization and compactness for single trees.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to single trees and lacks comparison to strong ensemble baselines like random forests and gradient boosting.
- Sensitivity to initialization and algorithm choices is not thoroughly analyzed; ablations on different initializations and SSGD vs. SGD are missing.
- The fast bound is looser than the full bound; no ablation quantifies the accuracy/time trade-off for different Hamming radii.
- No analysis of convergence or detailed training dynamics; training curves and leaf activation dynamics are not reported.
- Claims of generality to regression are unsupported by experiments, which are limited to classification.
- Paper is mostly clear and well-organized; notation is consistent and Algorithm 1 is helpful.
- Some proofs are deferred to the supplement; brief sketches in the main text would improve accessibility.
- The projection implementation detail (looping over rows vs. selective projection) needs clarification.
- SSGD requires more detail on the schedule/criterion for updating assignments to aid reproducibility.
- The combination of latent structured prediction formulation, per-row norm regularizer, and Hamming-ball inference is an original contribution.
- Framework is a meaningful step toward globally-optimized oblique trees and could enable further extensions (sparsity, kernels).
- Practical impact is moderate; stronger significance would come from larger-scale experiments and ensemble comparisons.
- Pros: principled formulation, efficient algorithm for deep oblique trees, empirical gains over single-tree baselines, and preserved fast inference.
- Cons: limited empirical scope, missing sensitivity/ablation studies, deferred proofs, and limited guidance on convergence and practical settings.
- Suggested improvements: add ablations (initialization, SSGD, Hamming radius), include small ensemble/regression comparisons, provide more implementation details, and summarize key proofs in the main text.
- Overall: technically sound and original; leans toward acceptance but would benefit from stronger empirical evaluation and additional ablations.
