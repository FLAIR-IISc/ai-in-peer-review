

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

The paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient joint optimization of split functions across the tree. The key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) through a modified surrogate objective, making deep tree learning feasible. The paper also introduces Stable SGD (SSGD) to address issues with inactive leaves during training.

This work relates to previous NIPS contributions in structured prediction (e.g., Yu and Joachims [25], Taskar et al. [24]) and builds upon recent advances in optimization for decision trees (e.g., Norouzi et al. [18] on CO2 forest). It extends the non-greedy optimization approach pioneered by Bennett [1] (which was limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss) to handle general loss functions while maintaining computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical justification for the upper bound (equation 7), including a proof of its validity and analysis of the regularization effect (Proposition 1). The complexity analysis of the fast loss-augmented inference is thorough and well-motivated.

2. **Novelty:** The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is a significant conceptual advance. The O(d²p) complexity reduction represents a meaningful technical contribution over the exponential complexity of naive approaches.

3. **Empirical Validation:** Experiments on multiple benchmark datasets demonstrate consistent improvements in test accuracy and generalization over greedy baselines (including CO2, OC1, and axis-aligned trees) across various tree depths. The analysis of the regularization parameter ν (Fig. 3) provides valuable insights into how the method controls model complexity.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and complexity analysis make the method accessible to the NIPS community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Loss Function Evaluation:** While the paper claims to handle "any general loss function," the experiments only evaluate log loss (classification) and squared loss (regression). It would strengthen the claim to demonstrate results with other loss functions (e.g., hinge loss for margin maximization).

2. **Incomplete Comparison with Prior Non-Greedy Methods:** The related work section mentions Bennett [1] but doesn't provide a direct comparison of computational complexity or performance with this prior work. The paper should clarify how their approach improves upon Bennett's method beyond the loss function scope.

3. **Scalability for Very Deep Trees:** The O(d²p) complexity remains challenging for very deep trees (large d), and the paper doesn't address how the method scales with extremely deep trees or large datasets beyond the Connect4 example.

4. **Overlooked Related Work:** The paper cites Murthy and Salzburg [17] who argue that non-greedy methods are unnecessary, but doesn't directly address their empirical findings or explain why their approach overcomes the limitations identified in that work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Significant theoretical contribution connecting decision trees to structured prediction
- Efficient optimization algorithm with rigorous complexity analysis
- Demonstrated empirical improvements in generalization performance
- Clear and well-written with sufficient detail for reproducibility
- Addresses a fundamental limitation of standard decision tree learning

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited evaluation of loss functions beyond classification and regression
- Incomplete comparison with key prior non-greedy approaches
- Insufficient discussion of scalability for very deep trees
- Overstatement of the generality of loss function applicability

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by providing a theoretically grounded, efficient method for non-greedy decision tree learning. While there are some limitations in the scope of evaluation and comparison, the technical soundness, novelty, and empirical results strongly support its significance to the NIPS community. The approach could enable more effective tree-based methods in applications where generalization is critical.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that advances the state of the art in decision tree learning. The theoretical insights and practical efficiency gains make it a valuable contribution to the field. The authors have addressed key challenges in non-greedy tree optimization with a well-motivated and rigorously analyzed solution.
