

# Peer Review: "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy learning of decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, introducing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized using stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity of loss-augmented inference from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) by restricting the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current decision path. The method is evaluated on several benchmark datasets, showing improved generalization performance compared to greedy baselines including axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant technical contribution in developing an efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm with O(d^2p) complexity, which is a substantial improvement over the naive O(2^dp) approach. This enables practical application to deeper trees.

2. **Novel Formulation**: The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is well-motivated and provides a principled framework for non-greedy optimization. This formulation enables the use of convex-concave upper bounds that facilitate efficient optimization.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets and tree depths compared to greedy baselines. The paper shows that non-greedy trees are less prone to overfitting, which aligns with the authors' regularization approach.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm design and experimental validation. The pseudocode for the SGD algorithm is clear and well-explained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: While the paper presents a theoretically sound upper bound, it lacks theoretical guarantees about convergence or generalization bounds for the proposed optimization approach. The connection to structured prediction is noted but not leveraged to derive theoretical properties.

2. **Limited Ablation Study**: The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of how the regularization parameter ν affects performance across different datasets. While Fig. 3 shows the effect of ν on leaf pruning, a more detailed ablation study would strengthen the claims about the regularization's role.

3. **Missing Inference Speed Comparison**: The paper focuses on training time improvements but does not compare inference speed with greedy trees. Given that decision trees are often used for inference in resource-constrained environments, this is an important practical consideration.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: While the paper references CO2 [18], it doesn't compare with more recent non-greedy tree methods like those in [15] or [17], which could provide better context for the contribution.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-justified claims. The theoretical derivation of the upper bound is rigorous, and the complexity analysis is correct. The experimental methodology is appropriate, using standard benchmark datasets and comparing with relevant baselines. However, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed optimization approach.

## Clarity

The paper is clearly written with a logical structure. The connection between decision trees and structured prediction is well-explained, though some readers unfamiliar with structured prediction might find this section challenging. The pseudocode for the SGD algorithm is well-presented, and the paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. Minor improvements could be made to clarify the relationship between the latent variables and the tree structure.

## Originality

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the decision tree literature. While the concept of non-greedy tree optimization has been explored before (e.g., Bennett [1]), this paper introduces a novel formulation that enables efficient optimization for deeper trees. The key technical novelty is the O(d^2p) loss-augmented inference algorithm, which represents a substantial improvement over previous approaches. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is particularly fresh.

## Significance

The work has significant practical implications as decision trees are widely used in industry and research. The improved generalization performance demonstrated in the experiments suggests that this approach could be valuable in real-world applications. The paper opens up new research directions, such as incorporating sparsity-inducing regularization or kernel methods, which could further enhance the approach.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The technical contribution is significant, the problem is well-motivated, and the experimental results are convincing. The paper addresses an important limitation of conventional greedy decision tree learning and provides a practical solution with demonstrated benefits.

## Pro/Con Summary

**Pros:**
- Novel formulation linking decision trees to structured prediction with latent variables
- Efficient O(d^2p) algorithm for loss-augmented inference (major technical contribution)
- Demonstrated improvement in generalization performance across multiple datasets
- Clear presentation and well-organized paper

**Cons:**
- Lack of theoretical guarantees for the optimization approach
- Limited analysis of the regularization parameter's effect
- Missing inference speed comparison (important for practical applications)
- Some comparisons with recent non-greedy tree methods could be strengthened

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of decision tree learning and provides a compelling alternative to conventional greedy approaches. The technical improvements are significant, and the experimental results support the claims. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
