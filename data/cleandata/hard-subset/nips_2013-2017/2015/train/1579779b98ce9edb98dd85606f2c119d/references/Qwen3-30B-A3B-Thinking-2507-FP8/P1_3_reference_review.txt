

# Peer Review: "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary

This paper presents a novel framework for non-greedy learning of decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently using stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is a fast algorithm for loss-augmented inference with complexity O(d²p) instead of the exponential O(2^dp) that would be required by the naive approach. This enables efficient learning of deep trees. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves better generalization performance than greedy baselines across multiple benchmark datasets while maintaining the computational efficiency of tree-based models.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions such as Yu and Joachims' work on learning structural SVMs with latent variables [25], and Norouzi and Fleet's use of similar reformulations for binary hashing [19]. It extends Bennett's earlier work on global tree optimization [1] by addressing the computational complexity limitations that prevented practical application to deeper trees.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-motivated theoretical framework that clearly connects decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables. The upper bound derivation is rigorous (with proof provided in supplementary material), and the complexity analysis is thorough.

2. **Novel Algorithmic Contribution**: The fast loss-augmented inference algorithm (O(d²p) vs. O(2^dp)) is a significant technical contribution that makes the non-greedy approach practically feasible. The authors clearly explain how this reduction is achieved by restricting the search to the Hamming ball of radius 1.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on multiple benchmark datasets demonstrate consistent improvements in generalization performance over greedy baselines. The results show that non-greedy trees are less susceptible to overfitting and achieve better test performance across different tree depths.

4. **Practical Utility**: The method is general enough to handle any loss function (not just classification) and maintains the O(dp) inference complexity of standard decision trees. The regularization parameter ν provides a principled way to control the tree's complexity.

5. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written, with clear mathematical formulations and logical flow. The algorithm descriptions are precise and implementable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Computational Comparison**: While the paper provides theoretical complexity analysis, it lacks a direct comparison of actual training times between their method and greedy approaches. The experimental section mentions "Fig. 4 shows training time" but doesn't provide quantitative results that would help assess the practical efficiency gains.

2. **Lack of Comparison to Modern Tree Methods**: The paper compares with standard greedy tree methods (axis-aligned trees, OC1) and CO2 [18], but doesn't include comparisons to recent state-of-the-art tree-based methods like XGBoost, LightGBM, or CatBoost, which have become standard in practice.

3. **Missing Analysis of Generalization Mechanism**: While the paper demonstrates that non-greedy trees generalize better, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of *why* this occurs beyond the regularization aspect. A more in-depth explanation of how the non-greedy optimization leads to better generalization would strengthen the paper.

4. **Scalability to Large Datasets**: The experiments are conducted on moderate-sized datasets (e.g., Connect4 with 55,000 training points), but the paper doesn't address how the method scales to datasets with millions of examples, which is where decision trees are often applied in industry.

5. **Prediction Time Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss whether their method affects prediction time, which is a critical consideration for tree-based models. Since the inference procedure remains similar (O(d) per example), this is likely not an issue, but it would be good to explicitly state this.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Presents a genuinely novel approach to a long-standing problem in decision tree learning
- Provides a theoretically sound framework with practical algorithmic improvements
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over greedy baselines
- The complexity reduction from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) is a significant technical contribution
- The paper is well-written and clearly structured

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Lacks comparison to recent state-of-the-art tree-based methods
- Limited experimental validation on large-scale datasets
- Doesn't fully explain why non-greedy optimization leads to better generalization
- Missing direct training time comparisons that would demonstrate practical efficiency

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of decision tree learning by providing a theoretically sound, efficient approach to non-greedy tree optimization. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is insightful, and the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm is a significant technical achievement that overcomes a major barrier to practical application of global tree optimization.

While the paper has some limitations in the experimental section (particularly regarding comparison to modern tree methods and scalability to very large datasets), these do not undermine the core technical contribution. The method addresses a fundamental limitation of current decision tree approaches and provides a practical solution that could be widely adopted.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. The technical content is sound, the problem is significant, the approach is novel, and the results demonstrate meaningful improvements over existing methods. The clarity of the writing and the thoroughness of the theoretical development make this a valuable contribution to the literature.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The technical contribution is significant, the approach is well-motivated and rigorously developed, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. With the minor limitations noted, this paper represents an important step toward more effective decision tree learning.
