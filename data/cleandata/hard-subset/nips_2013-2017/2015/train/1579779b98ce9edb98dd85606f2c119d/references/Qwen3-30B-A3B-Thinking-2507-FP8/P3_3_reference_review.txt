

# Peer Review: "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The key insight is to model the binary split decisions as latent variables, enabling the development of a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent. The authors make a significant technical contribution by reducing the computational complexity of loss-augmented inference from O(2^dp) to O(d²p), making deep tree learning feasible. The approach is evaluated on multiple benchmark datasets, showing improved generalization over greedy baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Innovation**: The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and well-executed. The formulation of the empirical loss as a latent variable problem is elegant and provides a principled foundation for non-greedy learning.

2. **Computational Breakthrough**: The reduction of computational complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth is a major contribution. The "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm is cleverly designed and effectively enables practical implementation for deep trees.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments are well-designed, comparing against multiple relevant baselines (including CO2, which is a special case of their method applied greedily). The results consistently show improved generalization performance across datasets and tree depths.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The paper provides clear algorithmic details and demonstrates the method's scalability (Fig. 4 shows clear time complexity benefits for deeper trees).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantee**: While the empirical results are promising, the paper lacks a theoretical guarantee that the non-greedy optimization will find better solutions than greedy approaches. The upper bound is convex-concave, but the paper doesn't establish how close the solution is to the global optimum or how it compares to the greedy solution.

2. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The comparison to prior work on non-greedy tree learning is incomplete. Bennett [1] developed a multi-linear programming approach for global tree optimization, which the authors cite but don't thoroughly compare against. The paper should clarify how their approach improves upon this earlier work in terms of both efficiency and performance.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper shows that the regularization parameter ν controls the tightness of the bound and implicitly controls pruning (Fig. 3), but doesn't discuss how sensitive the method is to this hyperparameter in practice. A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the empirical claims.

4. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide any analysis of the generalization properties of the proposed method. How does the norm regularization relate to the VC-dimension of the tree, and how does this compare to traditional pruning techniques?

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a significant technical contribution by enabling non-greedy learning of deep decision trees through an efficient optimization method. The reduction from exponential to quadratic complexity is particularly impressive and addresses a major barrier to non-greedy approaches.

However, the paper's main weakness is the lack of theoretical justification for why non-greedy learning should outperform greedy approaches. While the empirical results are compelling, a stronger theoretical foundation would make the contribution more impactful. The connection to structured prediction is well-established, but the paper doesn't leverage this to derive generalization bounds or convergence guarantees.

The comparison to Bennett's work [1] is particularly concerning. Bennett's method was specifically designed for global tree optimization and produced trees with higher classification accuracy than standard greedy trees. The authors don't adequately explain how their approach improves upon Bennett's in both computational efficiency and performance. The paper should clarify whether their method can solve the same problems as Bennett's (with higher classification accuracy) but with better scalability.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with a clear technical contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation of decision tree learning. The computational efficiency gains are substantial and the empirical results are convincing. However, the lack of theoretical justification for the non-greedy approach's superiority and the incomplete comparison to prior work on non-greedy tree learning are significant weaknesses.

I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:
1. Add a more thorough comparison to Bennett's work [1] and clarify how their approach improves upon it
2. Provide more theoretical analysis of the generalization properties
3. Include a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity

The paper's contribution is valuable for the machine learning community, as non-greedy decision tree learning has been a long-standing challenge. The computational efficiency achieved by the proposed method makes it practical for real-world applications, and the connection to structured prediction opens up potential for future work in this direction.

## Recommendation
Accept (with minor revisions)

The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to non-greedy decision tree learning with significant computational advantages. While the theoretical justification could be strengthened, the empirical results and computational efficiency make this a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed method has the potential to be widely adopted in practice, especially for applications requiring deep trees with good generalization.
