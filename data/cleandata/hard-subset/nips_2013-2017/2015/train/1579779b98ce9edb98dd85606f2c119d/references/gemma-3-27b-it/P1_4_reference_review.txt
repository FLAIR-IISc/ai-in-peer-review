## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, addressing a long-standing limitation of traditional greedy methods. The core idea is to formulate decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, enabling joint optimization of split functions across the tree. The authors develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and optimize it using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution lies in reducing the computational complexity of this optimization from O(2dp) to O(d²p), making it feasible for deeper trees. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through experiments on several benchmark datasets, showing improved test performance and generalization compared to greedy baselines.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions its contribution within the existing literature on decision tree learning. It acknowledges the NP-completeness of global tree optimization and discusses relevant work including multi-linear programming approaches [1], Mondrian processes [15], and hierarchical mixtures of experts [13]. The authors effectively differentiate their approach from these methods, highlighting the efficiency and generality of their formulation. The discussion of previous non-greedy methods and their limitations (e.g., [17]) is well-balanced. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables [25] is appropriately highlighted as a key inspiration.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a significant contribution. This allows for joint optimization of split functions, a departure from the inherently sequential nature of greedy methods.
* **Efficiency:**  The reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d²p) is crucial for scaling the method to deeper trees and larger datasets. This addresses a practical limitation of many non-greedy approaches.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The development of a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss provides a solid theoretical basis for the optimization procedure. The proposition regarding the effect of the norm of W is insightful.
* **Empirical Results:**  The experimental results on multiple datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improved test performance and generalization compared to established baselines. The ablation study exploring the effect of the regularization parameter ν is valuable.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, technical details, and experimental setup. The use of notation is consistent and aids in understanding the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:**  While the complexity reduction to O(d²p) is significant, the overall algorithm still appears complex to implement. The details of the fast loss-augmented inference and the SSGD variant could benefit from further clarification. 
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the regularization parameter ν. While the experiments explore this parameter, a more systematic analysis of the impact of other hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, momentum) would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the paper compares to several greedy baselines, it does not directly compare to more recent and sophisticated tree-based methods, such as extremely randomized trees or gradient boosted trees.  A comparison with these state-of-the-art techniques would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach. For example, the assumption of linear split functions may not be suitable for all datasets.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to non-greedy decision tree learning. The technical contributions are significant, and the experimental results are encouraging. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  This paper has the potential to advance the field of decision tree learning and is a worthwhile contribution to NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem.
* Significant reduction in computational complexity, enabling scalability.
* Strong empirical results demonstrating improved performance over greedy baselines.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Implementation complexity may be a barrier to adoption.
* Limited comparison to state-of-the-art tree-based methods.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity requires careful tuning.



**Rating:**  Strong Accept (8/10) – I believe this paper merits acceptance to NIPS, but minor revisions addressing the points above would further strengthen it. I would be happy to see this work presented at the conference.
