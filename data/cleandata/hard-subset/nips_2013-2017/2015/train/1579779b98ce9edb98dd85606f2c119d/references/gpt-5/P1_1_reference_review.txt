Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy, globally-trained method for learning decision trees that jointly optimizes all split functions and leaf parameters under a surrogate objective. The key idea is to cast binary split decisions as latent variables and connect tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss (Eq. 7), and a looser but computationally efficient bound restricted to a Hamming-1 neighborhood around the current hard routing (Eq. 13). This enables stochastic gradient descent (with per-row norm constraints on W) to update oblique splits and leaf parameters while preserving the O(dp) inference complexity of trees and reducing the per-update training cost from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p). A “Stable SGD” variant fixes leaf assignments for several steps to avoid premature deactivation of leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare against axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique splits, and CO2 (a greedy depth-1 variant of the proposed bound). The method generally yields better test accuracy across depths and appears less prone to overfitting. The paper discusses the role of a per-row norm constraint that both tightens the bound (Proposition 1) and regularizes the model.

Relation to prior work
The work draws an explicit connection to latent structural SVMs and loss-augmented inference, adapting these ideas to tree learning. Unlike soft probabilistic gating in Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts, the proposed approach preserves hard decisions and tree-time inference. It improves upon earlier global/non-greedy tree methods that were computationally heavy or limited in loss functions, and relates to margin-based trees by regularizing split norms. It also extends the authors’ previous CO2 work by enabling joint, non-greedy optimization across all nodes and deeper trees.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clean latent-variable reformulation of tree splits with a structured loss bound, allowing principled joint training while preserving fast inference.
- Technical contributions: two upper bounds (global and Hamming-1), an efficient loss-augmented inference procedure with O(d^2 p) updates, and a simple but effective per-row norm projection scheme.
- Practical algorithm: SGD with projections and a stabilizing SSGD variant; initialization via standard greedy trees/CO2 is sensible.
- Empirical results: consistent improvements over strong greedy baselines across depths; robustness to overfitting and a clear interpretation of the regularization parameter ν and its effect on pruning.
- Clarity of the main approach and motivation for regularization via Proposition 1.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope of evaluation: results focus on single trees. While this isolates the effect of non-greedy training, many practical applications use ensembles (RF/boosting). A comparison or discussion of how the method would integrate into forests/boosting would strengthen significance claims.
- Baseline coverage: no comparisons to strong modern baselines such as gradient boosting or strong oblique-tree learners beyond OC1; for multiclass, multiclass SVM/linear models as reference points would help contextualize performance.
- Ablations: limited analysis on (i) the gap between the global bound (Eq. 7) and the Hamming-1 bound (Eq. 13), (ii) the impact of SSGD vs. SGD, and (iii) sensitivity to initialization. These would clarify the optimization landscape and practical tradeoffs.
- Convergence/theory: no convergence guarantees (non-convex expected), but some empirical convergence diagnostics or bound values over time would improve the quality assessment.
- Generality claims: the framework is said to handle arbitrary losses and regression, but experiments demonstrate only multiclass log-loss. A small regression demo would support this claim.
- Reproducibility details: training schedules (e.g., SSGD update cadence), momentum/mini-batch sizes, and code availability are not specified.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The bound derivations and use of structured prediction machinery are appropriate, and the efficient loss-augmented inference for the Hamming-1 bound is convincing. The per-row norm constraint is justified both for generalization and for avoiding degenerate scaling of W. However, more thorough ablations and comparisons would strengthen empirical support, and some proofs are relegated to the supplement.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The notation for f(h) and the routing-to-leaf mapping is helpful; Algorithm 1 is readable. It would help to:
- Provide a short proof sketch of Eq. 7 in the main text.
- State explicitly how many steps SSGD freezes assignments and the criterion for updates.
- Clarify initialization details and hyperparameter search ranges in the main paper.

Originality
The latent variable formulation and structured-loss bounds for decision trees, together with efficient loss-augmented inference over a Hamming-1 ball, represent a novel combination. The work differs from soft-gating models, classical oblique trees, and the authors’ earlier CO2 method by enabling non-greedy joint training at scale.

Significance
Improving single-tree training with non-greedy global objectives is valuable and timely, especially for applications requiring fast inference. The ideas could transfer to ensembles and other hierarchical models. The empirical gains over greedy baselines suggest practical impact, though broader baseline comparisons would better establish state-of-the-art significance.

Questions for the authors
- How often do SSGD’s assignment freezes occur, and how sensitive are results to this schedule?
- What is the empirical gap between optimizing Eq. 7 vs. Eq. 13? Any cases where the looser bound hurts?
- Can you report ablations for ν and initialization strategy, and an SGD vs. SSGD comparison?
- Any plans or preliminary results for regression, or integration into forests/boosting?

Suggestions
- Add ablations on the bound choice, SSGD, and initialization.
- Include at least one comparison to a modern boosted tree baseline (single tree vs. shallow boosted trees) for context.
- Provide training/validation curves of the surrogate bound and accuracy.
- Release code and hyperparameter details to aid reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, principled formulation; efficient algorithm; clear empirical improvements on multiple datasets.
- Preserves tree inference efficiency while enabling non-greedy joint training.
- Potentially influential for future work on hierarchical models and tree ensembles.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baseline coverage and ablations; significance would be clearer with stronger comparisons.
- No regression experiments; some practical details missing.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept to accept.
Confidence: High.
