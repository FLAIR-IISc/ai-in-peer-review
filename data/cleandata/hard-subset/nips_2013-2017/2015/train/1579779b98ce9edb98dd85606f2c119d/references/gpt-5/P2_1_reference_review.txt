Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy method to train decision trees by jointly optimizing all split functions under a global objective. The key idea is to cast hard-split decision trees as a latent structured prediction problem: the binary decisions at internal nodes are treated as latent variables h ∈ {−1,+1}^m, and the empirical loss of the leaf prediction is upper-bounded by a convex-concave surrogate inspired by structural SVMs with latent variables. The authors derive (i) a tractable loss-augmented inference procedure that would naively be exponential in depth but is made efficient by restricting to a Hamming radius-1 neighborhood, yielding O(d^2 p) time per SGD step; (ii) a row-wise norm constraint on W to regularize and control the tightness of the bound; and (iii) two optimization variants (SGD and a “Stable SGD” that throttles leaf reassignments to avoid deactivating leaves). Experiments on several multiclass benchmarks show improved test accuracy for single trees across depths compared to axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and a greedy CO2 baseline. The method keeps the desirable O(dp) inference of hard trees at test time.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on: (a) classic greedy tree induction (ID3/CART) and oblique trees (OC1); (b) margin-based ideas for trees (SVMs at nodes); (c) soft trees (mixtures of experts) which enable smooth optimization but lose hard-tree inference efficiency; and (d) structured prediction and latent structural SVMs, from which the loss-augmented inference and margin-rescaling perspective are borrowed. The connection between hard tree training and latent structured prediction is clear and appears novel; CO2 is acknowledged as a closely related greedy special case for depth-1 nodes.

Quality
- Theory: The upper bound is clearly stated, with a simple, correct argument for tightness at sgn(Wx) and scale behavior (Proposition 1). The Hamming-ball variant is a valid looser bound enabling fast loss-augmented inference. The optimization problem is non-convex but well-posed; the use of projected SGD is appropriate.
- Algorithms: The fast loss-augmented inference and the per-row norm projection are sensible and efficient. The Stable SGD heuristic is reasonable for maintaining active leaves.
- Experiments: Multiple datasets, control of depth, hyperparameter tuning on validation, and comparisons to strong greedy baselines. The results support the claims that non-greedy joint optimization improves single-tree generalization and reduces overfitting. Runtime scaling with and without the fast bound is informative.

Main limitations in rigor/empirics:
- No experiments on regression despite claiming generality. 
- No ensembles (forests/boosting) where trees are most used; the paper argues single trees reveal differences, but demonstrating gains in forests would strengthen significance.
- Comparisons omit modern strong baselines like gradient-boosted trees; soft trees (HME) are discussed but not compared empirically.
- Sensitivity to initialization is not ablated (the method relies on initializing with greedy CO2/axis-aligned splits).
- Convergence behavior and stability of SSGD are not characterized beyond heuristics.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and readable. The latent variable reparameterization and the navigation function f are clearly explained. Algorithm 1 is helpful. A few suggestions:
- Provide pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference (radius-1) explicitly to make the O(d^2 p) pathway computation fully reproducible.
- Clarify the schedule/criteria for SSGD reassignment updates and its effect on the bound.
- State precisely the datasets’ sizes and feature dimensions in the main text for context on runtimes.

Originality
- The formulation of hard decision-tree training as latent structured prediction with a provable upper bound and efficient loss-augmented inference is novel and elegant.
- The Hamming-ball relaxation for tractable loss-augmented inference seems new in this context.
- The method differs meaningfully from CO2 by optimizing all nodes jointly under a global surrogate, and from OC1/greedy oblique methods by using a principled objective with regularization.

Significance
- Methodologically significant: provides a principled, scalable alternative to greedy tree induction while preserving hard-split inference complexity.
- Practical potential: could yield stronger single trees and, if extended, stronger forests with better depth/size-accuracy tradeoffs.
- Current empirical scope is moderate; demonstrating benefits in forests/boosting and on larger datasets would increase impact.

Pros
- Clear, principled objective connecting trees to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient training via a sound relaxation yielding O(d^2 p) updates.
- Empirical gains across datasets and depths for single trees; reduced overfitting.
- Retains hard-tree O(dp) test-time inference; supports general losses.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation omits ensembles and regression; limited comparison to state-of-the-art tree learners beyond classic baselines.
- Dependence on greedy initialization not thoroughly analyzed.
- No theoretical generalization guarantees beyond norm constraints; no convergence guarantees for SSGD.
- Some practical details (SSGD schedule, fast inference pseudocode) could be more explicit.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to the initialization (axis-aligned vs random vs CO2)? Can the method recover from poor initial trees?
- Can you report results for forests built from your non-greedy trees (bagging or random features) to assess ensemble-level benefits?
- Do you have regression results to support the general loss claim?
- How often does the radius-1 relaxation disagree with the full loss-augmented solution, and does increasing the radius materially help?

Overall assessment
This is a solid, technically sound, and novel contribution that addresses a longstanding limitation of greedy tree induction with a principled optimization framework and demonstrates meaningful empirical benefits for single trees. While the empirical scope could be broadened, the core ideas are strong and of clear interest to the NeurIPS community.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
Meets criteria: Quality (good), Clarity (good), Originality (strong), Significance (moderate-to-strong; could be bolstered with ensembles/regression).
