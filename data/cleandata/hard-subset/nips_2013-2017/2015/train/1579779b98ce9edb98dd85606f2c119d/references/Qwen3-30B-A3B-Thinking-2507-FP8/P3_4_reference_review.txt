

# Peer Review: "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary and Context

This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy decision tree learning by reformulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, introducing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p), making deep tree learning feasible. The work builds on prior structured prediction literature (e.g., Yu and Joachims, ICML 2009) but applies it to decision trees, a problem with significant practical relevance given the widespread use of decision trees in industry applications.

## Strengths

The paper makes several important contributions. First, the theoretical connection to structured prediction with latent variables is well-motivated and provides a principled framework for non-greedy tree learning. The convex-concave upper bound formulation (Equation 7) is elegant and well-justified with a clear proof sketch. Second, the computational complexity reduction to O(d^2p) is substantial and addresses a critical barrier to non-greedy tree learning. The fast loss-augmented inference algorithm (Section 6.2) is particularly clever and well-executed. Third, the experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets, with better generalization performance and reduced susceptibility to overfitting. The use of norm regularization (ν) to control tree complexity is well-justified and effectively demonstrated in Figure 3.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

Despite its strengths, the paper has several significant weaknesses. First, the claim of "non-greedy" optimization is misleading—while the method optimizes tree parameters jointly rather than sequentially, the loss-augmented inference step (Equation 10) still relies on a greedy path traversal through the tree. The authors should clarify this nuance in the paper. Second, the theoretical analysis of the upper bound is incomplete; the paper claims the bound becomes tighter as ||W|| increases (Proposition 1), but doesn't analyze how this affects convergence properties or the relationship between the bound and the true loss.

Third, the experimental evaluation lacks depth. The comparison is limited to standard benchmark datasets (LibSVM), with no analysis of how the method scales to larger datasets or more complex real-world problems. The runtime comparison in Figure 4 is based on Connect4 (55,000 training points), which is insufficient to demonstrate scalability for larger applications. Fourth, the paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from prior work like CO2 (Norouzi et al., 2015), which is described as a "special case" of their method but is presented as a separate baseline. The connection between their method and CO2 should be more thoroughly explained.

Finally, the paper lacks a comprehensive discussion of limitations. It doesn't address how the method handles categorical features (the paper assumes continuous inputs), nor does it analyze the sensitivity of performance to the choice of loss function beyond the specific examples provided. The claim that the method "can handle any general loss function" is overstated without sufficient evidence.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to decision tree learning by providing a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient method for non-greedy optimization. The complexity reduction from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) is significant and addresses a major practical limitation of previous non-greedy approaches. The experimental results demonstrate improved generalization performance over greedy methods, which is valuable for practitioners.

However, the paper's claims are somewhat overstated (particularly regarding the "non-greedy" nature of the approach), and the evaluation lacks depth and breadth. The connection to prior work could be strengthened, and the limitations of the approach are not sufficiently discussed. While the method is promising, the paper would benefit from more thorough analysis of the trade-offs between the upper bound tightness and generalization performance, and more comprehensive experimental validation on larger datasets.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field with a well-motivated approach and meaningful empirical results. The main concerns—particularly the misleading "non-greedy" claim and insufficient experimental validation—can be addressed with minor revisions. The authors should clarify the relationship between their method and CO2, provide a more nuanced discussion of the "non-greedy" aspect, and strengthen the experimental evaluation with additional dataset comparisons.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel theoretical approach, provides practical benefits for a widely used method, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing techniques. The connection to structured prediction literature is appropriate and well-executed, and the computational efficiency claims are substantiated by both theory and experiment.
