This paper introduces a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing it as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The core idea is to establish a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution lies in reducing the computational complexity of gradient updates from O(2dp) to O(d2p), crucial for scaling to deeper trees. The authors empirically demonstrate improved performance compared to greedy baselines on several benchmark datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is a novel and insightful perspective. This allows leveraging techniques from structured prediction to overcome limitations of traditional greedy methods.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The reduction in complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a significant technical achievement, making the proposed method practical for deeper trees. This addresses a key bottleneck in non-greedy tree learning.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently show that the non-greedy approach outperforms greedy baselines across different datasets and tree depths, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Regularization:** The incorporation of a norm regularization term addresses potential overfitting issues inherent in non-greedy optimization, leading to better generalization performance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Structured Prediction:** While the application to decision trees is novel, the use of convex-concave upper bounds and SGD for structured prediction is well-established. The paper does not introduce significant advancements in the structured prediction itself.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparison is primarily with relatively basic greedy methods. Including comparisons to more sophisticated non-greedy or ensemble methods (e.g., boosted trees, more recent oblique decision tree methods) would strengthen the evaluation. The comparison to CO2 is helpful, but a more thorough assessment against state-of-the-art methods is needed.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the regularization parameter ν, requiring careful tuning. The paper acknowledges this, but a more in-depth discussion of the hyperparameter tuning process and its impact on results would be beneficial.
* **Initialization Dependency:** The performance relies on initialization using a greedy tree (CO2). The influence of the initial greedy tree on the final solution is not adequately explored.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical analysis, while present, could be strengthened. Further exploration of the properties of the surrogate objective and its convergence guarantees would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to decision tree learning, addressing a long-standing limitation of greedy algorithms.
* The technical contribution of reducing computational complexity is significant and enables practical application to deeper trees.
* The empirical results are compelling, consistently demonstrating improved performance compared to baseline methods.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* The core techniques employed (convex-concave optimization, SGD) are not novel in themselves.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is limited.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning and initialization could be a practical concern.
* The theoretical analysis could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a solid contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The novelty lies in the clever application of structured prediction techniques and the efficient optimization strategy. The empirical results are promising, and the paper is well-written. While the paper does not revolutionize the field, it offers a valuable improvement over existing methods and opens up new avenues for research. I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
