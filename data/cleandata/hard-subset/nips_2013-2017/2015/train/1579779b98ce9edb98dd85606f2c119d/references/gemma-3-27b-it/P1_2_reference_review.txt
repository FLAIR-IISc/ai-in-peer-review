## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing limitation of greedy algorithms in decision tree induction – their inability to globally optimize split functions based on information learned at lower levels of the tree. The authors propose a non-greedy framework leveraging structured prediction with latent variables, specifically formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss.  This bound is optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD), with a key technical contribution being a reduction in computational complexity from O(2<sup>dp</sup>) to O(d<sup>2</sup>p) for deep trees. The work establishes a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, offering a principled way to regularize the joint optimization of tree parameters and encouraging more compact, generalizable trees.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on non-greedy decision tree optimization. It acknowledges and contrasts with approaches like Bennett’s multi-linear programming (limited scalability) and online forest learning via Mondrian Processes (incremental growth, differing focus).  It also differentiates itself from soft-split methods (loss of tree efficiency) and max-margin approaches (focus on regularization rather than joint optimization). The connection to structured prediction, drawing from works in [23, 24, 25], is clearly articulated and serves as a foundation for the proposed methodology.  The authors appropriately cite relevant prior work, demonstrating awareness of the field.  CO2 [18] is correctly positioned as a related greedy approach upon which their work builds.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The framing of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a clever and potentially impactful contribution. This provides a strong theoretical foundation for non-greedy optimization.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The reduction in complexity from O(2<sup>dp</sup>) to O(d<sup>2</sup>p) is crucial. It makes non-greedy optimization feasible for deeper trees, a significant practical advantage.
* **Regularization:** The introduction of a norm constraint on the split function weights (W) provides a principled way to prevent overfitting and control tree complexity. This is supported by the proposition linking regularization to the tightness of the upper bound.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms greedy baselines across multiple datasets, particularly in terms of test accuracy. The comparison to CO2 is also insightful, highlighting the benefit of the non-greedy approach.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the theoretical complexity reduction is significant, the actual implementation (SGD with careful projection and handling of latent variables) appears complex.  The paper does not fully convey the implementation effort required.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The reliance on initializing with a greedy tree (CO2) raises concerns about potential biases. It's unclear how sensitive the results are to different initialization strategies. Exploring alternative initializations would strengthen the work.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning (ν and η) is somewhat brief. More details on the tuning process, especially the validation set strategy and sensitivity analysis, would be beneficial.
* **Limited Ablation Study:**  While the experiments demonstrate overall performance gains, a more detailed ablation study could help isolate the contributions of different components (e.g., the norm regularization, the fast loss-augmented inference). For example, how much does the fast loss-augmented inference contribute to the performance compared to a standard loss-augmented inference?
* **Practical Scalability:** While the complexity is improved, the O(d<sup>2</sup>p) term still poses a potential bottleneck for very high-dimensional data (large *p*) and very deep trees (large *d*).  Discussion of scalability limitations and potential mitigations (e.g., feature selection) would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to non-greedy decision tree optimization.
* The computational efficiency improvements are substantial and address a key limitation of previous methods.
* The experimental results consistently demonstrate superior performance compared to strong baselines.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of decision tree learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The implementation complexity could limit widespread adoption.
* The reliance on greedy initialization introduces potential bias.
* The hyperparameter tuning details are insufficient.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The novel formulation, coupled with the efficient optimization algorithm, addresses a long-standing problem and delivers promising results. While there are some areas for improvement (implementation details, initialization sensitivity, hyperparameter tuning), the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

* Provide more details on the implementation process, including potential challenges and engineering considerations.
* Investigate the sensitivity of the results to different initialization strategies.
* Expand the discussion of hyperparameter tuning and provide more details on the validation set strategy.
* Consider including an ablation study to isolate the contributions of different components.
* Briefly discuss potential scalability limitations and possible mitigations.
