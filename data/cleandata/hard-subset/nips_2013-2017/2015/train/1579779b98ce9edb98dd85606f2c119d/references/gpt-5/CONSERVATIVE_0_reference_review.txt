Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to view the vector of split outcomes h = sgn(Wx) as a latent structured variable and to use a loss-augmented inference bound (analogous to latent structural SVM) to provide a surrogate objective. The authors further introduce a modified bound that restricts loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing per-update complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) for depth d and feature dimension p. They also propose a “Stable SGD” variant that freezes data-to-leaf assignments for several iterations to mitigate leaf inactivity. Experiments on several LibSVM multi-class datasets compare single trees against axis-aligned CART, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique splits, and a greedy depth-1 variant (CO2), suggesting improved test accuracy and reduced overfitting at comparable depths.

Strengths
- Conceptual link to structured prediction with latent variables is clear and technically interesting. The upper-bound formulation and the use of loss-augmented inference for tree training is well motivated.
- The fast loss-augmented inference via a radius-1 Hamming ball is a useful engineering contribution that materially reduces complexity and makes the approach plausible for deeper trees.
- The formulation supports general losses and keeps inference at test time O(dp), preserving a core advantage of trees.
- Practical algorithmic details (projection to per-node norm balls, momentum/minibatch SGD, SSGD heuristic) are presented, and the method appears to work across multiple datasets.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical validation is limited and largely focused on single trees. In practice, decision forests (bagging/boosting) dominate; the paper argues single trees isolate the training criterion, but significance to the community hinges on whether gains persist in ensembles. No results on forests (e.g., bagging this non-greedy learner or boosting shallow trees) are provided.
- Baselines omit several established and strong alternatives. In particular, no comparison to max-margin split learning (e.g., SVM-at-node variants) beyond citations, and no comparison to modern oblique tree methods that use global/regularized training. OC1 is quite dated; the paper would benefit from stronger baselines (and clear, equal hyperparameter tuning).
- Lack of statistical rigor in the results: no confidence intervals, variance across runs, or significance testing. The reported gains may be within noise on some datasets; it is difficult to assess robustness.
- Heavy reliance on initialization from a greedy tree/CO2. There is no ablation to quantify how much of the final performance is due to the proposed non-greedy optimization versus the initialization. Similarly, there is no ablation for SSGD vs. SGD, or for the radius-1 vs. full bound.
- Theoretical development stops at upper bounds; there are no generalization guarantees or margin-based analysis connecting the per-node norm constraints ν to test performance (beyond heuristic discussion). The tightness-vs-optimiziability trade-off (Proposition 1) is intuitive but does not yield actionable guidance for setting ν beyond validation tuning.
- The surrogate bound based on radius-1 Hamming ball is a looser objective; there is no analysis of the approximation gap relative to the full bound, nor evidence that optimizing it yields similar minima in practice.
- Scope claims outpace evidence: the method is stated to handle general losses and regression, but experiments cover only multi-class log loss; no regression results are shown.
- Efficiency evaluation is incomplete. The paper measures loss-augmented inference timing but not end-to-end wall-clock versus strong greedy baselines at matched accuracy. Without this, the practical value of the proposed efficiency improvements is unclear.
- Some clarity and referencing issues: several references are incomplete (missing years/pages); details on learning-rate schedules, batch sizes, number of epochs, and early-stopping criteria are scarce. Reproducibility would benefit from more comprehensive hyperparameter reporting and code availability.

Quality
Technically sound at the level of constructing valid upper bounds and deriving SGD updates. However, the paper lacks deeper theoretical guarantees and more rigorous empirical testing (variance, significance, ensembles, broader baselines) expected for a new training principle. SSGD appears heuristic and its effect is not quantified.

Clarity
Overall readable, but notation is dense and some definitions (e.g., exact construction of f(g) and path bit conventions) could be clarified with additional diagrams or pseudo-code. Experimental protocol details are insufficient for reproduction.

Originality
The connection to latent structural SVM for tree training is interesting but not entirely surprising; related ideas in soft splits (HME), margin-based trees, and latent-SVM style formulations exist. The main novelty is the specific upper bound application to decision trees and the computational shortcut via Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference.

Significance
Potentially useful if it consistently improves single-tree accuracy and scales. As presented, significance is limited by the narrow empirical scope and lack of ensemble results and stronger baselines. The community impact will hinge on demonstrating practical advantages in standard workflows (forests/boosting).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ensemble experiments: bagging and boosting with the proposed non-greedy learner, comparing to Random Forests and Gradient Boosted Trees at matched depth/leaf counts and wall-clock.
- Strengthen baselines: include SVM-at-node/max-margin oblique trees and other modern oblique/global training methods; ensure fair, well-documented hyperparameter tuning.
- Report variability (mean±std over multiple runs), statistical tests, and calibration metrics. Include regression datasets to support generality claims.
- Provide ablations: initialization (random vs. greedy), SSGD vs. SGD, full vs. radius-1 bound, effect of ν, and the impact of the per-node norm constraint vs. a global constraint.
- End-to-end efficiency: wall-clock training time vs. baselines for a target accuracy; memory footprint; scaling with depth and data size.
- Clarify and complete references; provide more implementation details or code for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting formulation linking tree learning to latent structured prediction.
- Practical and neat computational trick (Hamming-ball bound) enabling deeper trees.
- Promising results on several datasets for single trees.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evidence is not sufficiently thorough or rigorous; missing ensembles, stronger baselines, and statistical analysis.
- Heavy reliance on initialization and heuristics without ablations.
- Limited theoretical insight beyond the bound and its scaling behavior.

Recommendation: Weak Reject
Confidence: High (familiar with decision tree training, margin-based trees, and latent structural SVMs)
