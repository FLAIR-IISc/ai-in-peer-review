## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing limitation of greedy algorithms in decision tree induction – their inability to jointly optimize splits across the tree. The authors propose a novel framework for non-greedy learning based on structured prediction with latent variables. They formulate decision tree learning with a binary latent variable for each split, allowing for a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss.  A key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity of optimizing this bound from O(2dp) to O(d²p), enabling the training of deep trees. The paper demonstrates through experiments on standard datasets that this non-greedy approach outperforms greedy baselines in terms of generalization performance, while also offering insights into the regularization parameter's influence on tree complexity.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges the limitations of greedy methods (ID3, CART) and highlights the complexity of global optimization (Bennett, Murthy & Salzburg).  It contrasts its approach with prior non-greedy methods, pointing out limitations in scalability (Bennett’s multi-linear programming) or loss of tree efficiency (Hierarchical Mixture of Experts). The connection to structured prediction and the use of latent variables is clearly linked to related work in that area (Taskar et al., Yu & Joachims). The comparison to CO2 is also relevant, framing their work as a more general non-greedy optimization approach. The reference list is comprehensive and well-chosen.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a significant conceptual contribution. This allows the application of techniques developed in the structured prediction literature.
* **Computational Efficiency:** Reducing the complexity from O(2dp) to O(d²p) is a crucial practical achievement.  This makes non-greedy optimization feasible for deeper trees, which were previously inaccessible.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments consistently demonstrate improved generalization performance compared to greedy baselines across multiple datasets. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Regularization Analysis:**  The analysis of the regularization parameter (ν) and its effect on tree complexity and pruning is insightful and practical.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are reasonably accessible.  The inclusion of Algorithm 1 is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Baselines:** While comparisons to several greedy methods are included, the comparison is somewhat limited. More recent or sophisticated non-greedy techniques, if they exist, should be considered.  The description of the baselines could be more detailed (e.g., how hyperparameters are tuned).
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the regularization parameter ν. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of how to select an appropriate value for ν in practice, beyond simply tuning on the validation set.
* **Scalability beyond Depth:** The complexity analysis focuses on tree depth *d*.  The impact of the number of features *p* on scalability could be further investigated, particularly for very high-dimensional datasets. While O(d²p) is an improvement, *p* could still be a bottleneck.
* **SSGD Details:** The description of Stable SGD (SSGD) is a little brief. It would be beneficial to provide more details on the criteria for updating leaf assignments and the impact of this process on training time and performance.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of the upper bound in section 5 and its relation to the regularizer presented in section 6, and proposition 1, could benefit from more thorough justification.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to non-greedy decision tree learning.  The theoretical contributions – formulating the problem as structured prediction and reducing the computational complexity – are significant, and the empirical results are compelling.  While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*  Presents a genuinely new and valuable approach to a long-standing problem in machine learning.
*  Demonstrates clear improvements in generalization performance over established baselines.
*  Provides a crucial algorithmic improvement that makes non-greedy optimization practical for deeper trees.
*  Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The hyperparameter sensitivity to ν requires more detailed discussion and practical guidance.
* The comparison to other non-greedy approaches could be more comprehensive.
* Scalability concerns with high-dimensional data (large *p*) warrant further investigation.



**Rating:**

Strong Accept (7/10) - This paper represents a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of publication at NIPS. Addressing the minor weaknesses identified above would further strengthen the work.
