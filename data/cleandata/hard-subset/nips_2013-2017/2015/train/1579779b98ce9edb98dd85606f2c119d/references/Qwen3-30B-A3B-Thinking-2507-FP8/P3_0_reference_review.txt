

# Peer Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy decision tree learning by reformulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently using stochastic gradient descent, with a key technical contribution being the reduction of computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) through an efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm.

**Strengths:**

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, the connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and well-motivated. The authors successfully bridge a gap between two seemingly distinct areas of machine learning, providing a theoretically sound framework for global tree optimization. The derivation of the upper bound (equation 7) is mathematically rigorous, and the proof of Proposition 1 provides valuable intuition about the relationship between the scale of W and the tightness of the bound.

The computational efficiency of the proposed algorithm is particularly impressive. The fast loss-augmented inference method (Section 6.2) represents a major technical advance that makes non-greedy learning of deep trees feasible. The empirical results (Fig. 2) convincingly demonstrate that the non-greedy approach consistently achieves better generalization performance than greedy baselines across multiple datasets, including SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST.

The paper also thoughtfully addresses the overfitting problem through the norm regularization of W (equation 9), which provides a principled way to control the tightness of the tree's fit to training data. The SSGD variant (Section 6.4) is a practical and well-justified extension that prevents abrupt changes in leaf assignments.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

While the paper is technically strong, several aspects could be improved. The most significant gap is the insufficient contrast with Bennett's earlier work on global tree optimization [1], which also proposed a non-greedy approach. The authors cite this work but do not clearly articulate how their method improves upon it. Bennett's method handles binary classification with 0-1 loss and has high computational complexity, while the current approach handles general loss functions and achieves O(d²p) complexity. The paper should explicitly address this comparison to better establish the novelty.

The theoretical analysis could be strengthened. While the upper bound is proven, the paper lacks a theoretical guarantee on the quality of the solution found by the SGD approach compared to the optimal tree. Without such guarantees, it's difficult to assess the fundamental limits of the approach.

The experimental section would benefit from more detailed comparisons. The paper mentions that "non-greedy trees seem to be less susceptible to overfitting" but doesn't provide statistical significance tests to support this claim. The computational complexity comparison with greedy methods (beyond the theoretical O(d²p) vs O(2^dp) analysis) is also lacking. How much slower is the non-greedy approach compared to greedy training on the same datasets? The paper claims "efficient learning" but doesn't quantify this.

The paper briefly mentions "extension to N-ary trees is possible" but provides no details or evidence. This limitation should be acknowledged, and the paper should be clear about the scope of the approach.

**Significance and Originality:**

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to a well-established area (decision trees) by addressing a fundamental limitation of greedy approaches. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is novel and provides a fresh perspective on tree learning. The efficiency improvements make non-greedy learning practically feasible, which is a significant advancement over previous attempts at global tree optimization.

While the problem of non-greedy tree learning has been studied before (e.g., [1, 15, 17]), this paper's approach to making it computationally tractable is substantially different and more scalable than prior work. The method can handle general loss functions, unlike Bennett's [1] binary classification with 0-1 loss. The paper's approach is also distinct from soft-split methods like Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13], which lose the computational advantages of decision trees.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a technically sound and well-executed approach to non-greedy decision tree learning with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The key innovation—reducing the computational complexity of non-greedy learning from exponential to polynomial—makes the approach practically viable. The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements in generalization performance over greedy baselines.

The main limitations are the insufficient contrast with prior work (particularly Bennett [1]), the lack of statistical significance tests for the results, and the incomplete treatment of extensions to N-ary trees. However, these do not undermine the paper's core contributions.

I recommend acceptance at NeurIPS. The paper makes a strong contribution to a fundamental problem in machine learning with clear practical implications. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates significant improvements over the state of the art in generalization performance. With minor clarifications addressing the weaknesses noted above, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the conference.

**Recommendation:** Accept
