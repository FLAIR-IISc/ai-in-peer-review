

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a fundamentally novel approach to decision tree learning by formulating it as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The key insight is to treat the sequence of binary decisions in a tree as a latent variable and develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling joint optimization of split functions across the entire tree rather than the conventional greedy approach. The authors show how to reduce the computational complexity from exponential to O(d²p) (where d is tree depth and p is input dimension) through clever modifications to the surrogate objective, making non-greedy learning feasible for deep trees.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) establishing the connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, (2) developing a computationally efficient upper bound on the empirical loss, and (3) providing a practical optimization algorithm (SGD and SSGD variants) that achieves better generalization than greedy methods. The approach is general, working for any loss function (not just classification) and applicable to both classification and regression.

**Strengths:**

1. **High novelty:** This is a genuinely new approach to a long-standing problem in machine learning. The paper breaks from the decades-old greedy paradigm for decision tree learning, which is a bold move. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is particularly creative and has potential to inspire similar approaches for other sequential decision problems.

2. **Theoretical soundness:** The upper bound is rigorously justified with a proof, and the authors carefully analyze the relationship between the bound and the actual loss. The computational complexity analysis is thorough and convincing.

3. **Practical impact:** The method achieves better generalization performance across multiple datasets with comparable or better training accuracy than greedy methods. The paper shows that non-greedy trees are less prone to overfitting, which is a significant practical advantage.

4. **General framework:** The approach works for any loss function, not just classification with 0-1 loss as in previous non-greedy methods (e.g., Bennett [1]), making it much more broadly applicable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited experimental validation:** While the results on four datasets are promising, more comprehensive experiments would strengthen the case. The paper doesn't compare to state-of-the-art non-greedy methods beyond CO2 [18], and the dataset sizes are modest (Connect4 has only 55k training points). More thorough ablation studies on key parameters (like ν) would be valuable.

2. **Scalability concerns:** The paper claims the method scales well, but the experiments don't demonstrate performance on truly large-scale problems. The runtime analysis in Figure 4 shows improvement, but it's unclear how the method would perform on datasets with millions of samples.

3. **Connection to structured prediction:** While the connection is theoretically sound, the paper could make this link more accessible to readers unfamiliar with structured prediction, which would help more readers appreciate the novelty.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant conceptual advance in decision tree learning. The authors have identified a fundamental limitation in the standard greedy approach and proposed a novel, theoretically sound framework to address it. The connection to structured prediction is particularly elegant and could have broader implications for other sequential decision problems.

The paper excels in originality - it's not just another incremental improvement to decision trees but a fundamentally different approach to learning them. The technical contribution of reducing the complexity to O(d²p) is substantial, making non-greedy learning practically feasible. The results show clear advantages in generalization performance, which is a critical concern for practitioners.

While the experimental validation could be more comprehensive, the theoretical foundation and conceptual novelty are strong enough to outweigh these limitations. The paper represents a significant step toward more principled and effective decision tree learning, and it opens up new research directions at the intersection of structured prediction and tree-based models.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper makes a compelling case for a new paradigm in decision tree learning that is both theoretically grounded and practically useful. The novelty and conceptual contribution are substantial, and the method offers a clear alternative to the decades-old greedy approach. While the experimental section could be strengthened, the core idea is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS. The authors have successfully bridged two important areas (decision trees and structured prediction) in a way that could influence future work in both fields.
