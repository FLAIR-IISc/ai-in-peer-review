Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss using (stochastic) subgradient methods. The key technical ideas are: (i) reformulating split decisions sgn(Wx) as argmax over h ∈ {−1,+1}^m to link tree induction with latent structured prediction; (ii) an instance-wise upper bound on loss akin to margin-rescaled latent SSVM objectives; (iii) a per-row L2 constraint on W to regularize the scale that otherwise trivially tightens the bound; and (iv) a “fast” loss-augmented inference via restricting g to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around ĥ=sgn(Wx), yielding O(d^2 p) complexity instead of exponential in depth. The authors also propose a “Stable SGD” variant that freezes leaf assignments between updates to avoid inactive leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare non-greedy oblique trees to axis-aligned CART, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique splits, and a greedy depth-1 variant (CO2), showing better test accuracy and less overfitting across depths.

The work is closely related to structural SVMs with latent variables (Yu & Joachims’ LSVM), and to CO2 forests (Norouzi et al.), which can be seen as a depth-1 special case. It also connects to earlier non-greedy tree optimization (Bennett), max-margin trees, and soft probabilistic trees (HME).

Strengths
- Clear conceptual linkage between decision trees and latent structured prediction, enabling a principled global objective over split parameters.
- Technical contribution of a computationally tractable, per-example loss-augmented inference via a radius-1 Hamming ball, reducing the complexity to O(d^2 p) while preserving O(dp) test-time inference.
- Sensible regularization via per-row L2 constraints on W that directly controls the scale–tightness trade-off of the bound and implicitly prunes leaves.
- Empirical results (as described) suggest consistent improvements over several greedy baselines for single trees and reduced overfitting as depth increases.
- Practical algorithm with projection and simple updates; initialization via standard greedy procedures is pragmatic.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/methodological
- The fast bound (radius-1 Hamming ball) is strictly looser than the full bound. The paper does not quantify the empirical accuracy gap between optimizing the full vs. fast bound, only the runtime. An ablation is needed to justify that the approximation does not materially harm accuracy.
- Complexity accounting appears optimistic: loss-augmented inference over up to d+1 “nearby” leaves implies O(d^2 p) for scores plus O(d k) for computing d+1 softmax losses; the text states O(d^2 p + k). Please reconcile.
- No convergence analysis for SGD/SSGD on the non-convex objective is provided (even in the sense of subgradient convergence to stationary points). At minimum, state the subgradient being used at non-differentiable points and the step-size/momentum schedule.
- SSGD is underspecified: how long are assignments frozen, what metric defines “negligible improvement,” and what schedule is used? Without these details, reproducibility suffers, and the effect of SSGD vs. plain SGD on accuracy and leaf utilization is unclear.
- Initialization may bias results: the method is initialized via axis-aligned CART then CO2. Please include an ablation (random W, CART-only initialization) to show robustness and fairness against OC1/random oblique baselines that may not benefit from the same warm-start.
- Claims of generality (support for arbitrary losses and regression) are not substantiated with experiments on regression or non-logistic losses.
- Memory/time scalability with depth: m grows exponentially with d (m≈2^d−1), so storing W ∈ R^{m×p} becomes the bottleneck for deep trees. The paper does not discuss memory, nor do experiments quantify feasible depths/sizes in practice.

Empirical evaluation
- Only four multiclass datasets are used; no high-dimensional sparse tasks (e.g., text) or large-scale benchmarks where tree methods shine, nor any regression tasks.
- Focus solely on single trees, while many practical applications rely on ensembles. While isolating single-tree behavior is reasonable, a small ensemble comparison (e.g., boosted oblique trees, or random forests with oblique splits) would help establish practical significance.
- Results are only described qualitatively in the text; the paper does not report exact numbers, variances over runs, or statistical significance. Hyperparameter tuning ranges are minimal (ν ∈ {0.1,1,4,10,43,100} with an odd inclusion of 43) and search methodology is not detailed. Important training details (batch size, momentum, τ, early stopping) are missing.

Clarity and presentation
- Several notational and editorial issues hinder clarity:
  - “withm internal nodes,” “by parameterized a weight vector,” inconsistent spacing around math.
  - Hamming distance/norm is used but not explicitly defined; 1-of-(m+1) indicator space notation Im+1 is introduced without definition.
  - Tie-breaking when w_i^T x = 0 is not specified.
  - Proposition 1 and the bound inequalities (7), (13), (14) are deferred to the supplement. Given centrality, brief proofs or at least detailed sketches would help.
  - Algorithm 1 implies per-row projection onto an L2 ball of radius sqrt(ν); please explicitly denote ||·||_2 and make the projection step unambiguous.
- Figures are referenced for the main claims but not tabulated; precise values are necessary for readers and for reproducibility.

References and formatting
- The bibliography is in poor shape: many entries lack years, venues, or complete author lists (e.g., “et al, J. Shotton,...”), inconsistent capitalization, and typos (“John Hopkins” vs. “Johns Hopkins”; “Salzburg” vs. “Salzberg”). Some citations appear out of date (CO2 as arXiv 2015) relative to a NeurIPS submission timeline. Please normalize and complete all references.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide an ablation comparing full vs. fast loss-augmented inference: accuracy, training time, and the number of active leaves.
2. Detail SSGD (freezing schedule, thresholds) and quantify its impact on accuracy and leaf utilization.
3. Clarify complexity O(d^2 p + k) vs. O(d^2 p + d k) and include memory considerations as d grows.
4. Report exact test accuracies with standard deviations across seeds; include regression experiments and at least one high-dimensional sparse dataset.
5. Compare against max-margin oblique trees (e.g., Bennett & Blue) and logistic model trees, or explain why not feasible.
6. Describe tie-breaking for zero margins and how you handle subgradients at non-differentiable points.

Pros
- Conceptually elegant link to latent structured prediction.
- Practical algorithm with a clear computational improvement.
- Regularization that meaningfully trades off fit and pruning.
- Promising empirical trends on several datasets.

Cons
- Approximate bound not justified empirically; missing ablations.
- Insufficient experimental breadth, lack of exact numbers/variances.
- Incomplete methodological details (SSGD, optimization schedules).
- Poor reference formatting and several clarity/notation issues.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The ideas are interesting and potentially useful, but the current submission has too many methodological omissions, limited and insufficiently reported experiments, and substantial presentation/reference issues. With stronger empirical ablations, clearer optimization details, and cleaned-up writing/refs, this could be a solid contribution.
