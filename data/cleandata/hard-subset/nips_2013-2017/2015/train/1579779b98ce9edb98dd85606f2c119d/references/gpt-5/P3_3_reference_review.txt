Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for oblique decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables in a structured prediction formulation. Given an m-bit latent vector h = sgn(Wx), the authors define a global empirical loss and introduce a convex–concave upper bound that can be optimized via stochastic subgradient methods. The key technical step is an upper bound that involves loss-augmented inference over latent codes. A naive bound requires maximizing over all {−1,+1}^m, which is exponential in depth d; the paper introduces a modified “local” upper bound restricting g to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing per-update complexity to O(d^2 p) while keeping inference at test time O(dp). The method jointly updates split parameters W (with per-row L2 constraints) and leaf parameters Θ. A “Stable SGD” variant fixes data-to-leaf assignments for several steps to prevent collapse to inactive leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare single-tree performance against axis-aligned CART, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique, and a greedy CO2 variant, showing consistent gains and reduced overfitting across depths.

The approach builds on and connects several lines: non-greedy/”lookahead” trees (e.g., OC1), max-margin/tree hybrids (Bennett & Blue), soft/probabilistic trees such as HME (Jordan & Jacobs), and structured prediction with latent variables (latent SVM/SSVM). The latent-code reparameterization sgn(Wx) = argmax_h h^T W x echoes latent SVM formulations and prior use of this trick for hashing (Norouzi & Fleet). The CO2 bound is recovered as a depth-1 special case; the key novelty here is joint non-greedy optimization across depths with an efficient loss-augmented inference scheme that preserves hard routing and tree-time inference.

Quality:
- Theory: The upper bounds (global and Hamming-ball-restricted) are correct and standard latent-SSVM style arguments apply. Proposition 1 about scaling W and bound tightness is reasonable and motivates per-row norm constraints. The surrogate objective is a difference of convex terms in W; subgradient updates for W (ĝ−ĥ)x^T and Θ (gradient of the loss at the loss-augmented argmax) are consistent with subgradients of max functions. However, convergence guarantees are not discussed beyond stochastic subgradient heuristics; SSGD resembles block coordinate/CCCP but lacks analysis (e.g., monotonic bound decrease, conditions for leaf reactivation).
- Algorithms: The O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference is plausible given that only d+1 leaves are reachable by a single-bit flip; the complexity argument would benefit from a more explicit description of how inner products are reused/cached along paths to avoid O(mp) costs. The projection step per row is straightforward. The reliance on initialization (axis-aligned tree → CO2 → non-greedy) is sensible, but the paper does not show sensitivity to initialization or behavior from random starts.
- Experiments: The comparisons on multiple datasets and varying depths are informative. That said, results are reported primarily as plots; concrete numbers, variance across runs, and statistical tests would strengthen claims. Baseline tuning details (e.g., OC1 restarts, pruning criteria) are light; fairness of hyperparameter search across methods is unclear. Only single-tree results are shown; while the authors explain this choice, practitioners often deploy ensembles (RF/GBDT), and it would be valuable to see whether non-greedy optimization yields stronger base learners in forests or boosting, or enables shallower, more compact ensembles for a target accuracy. Runtime results compare slow vs fast loss-augmented inference but not wall-clock vs competing baselines.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The connection to latent structured prediction is clearly laid out; the notation for W, Θ, f, and h is consistent. Key proofs are deferred to the supplement; at least a sketch in the main text would help. Algorithm 1 is clear; a pseudocode block for the fast loss-augmented inference (radius-1 bound) would improve reproducibility. Some references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted.

Originality:
The core ideas synthesize known components (latent-SSVM bounds, oblique splits, prior CO2 work) into a non-greedy, globally trained hard tree with efficient training. The restriction to a Hamming ball around current routing to make loss-augmented inference tractable for deep trees is a neat, practical contribution. While the latent-code trick itself is known, its application to decision trees with an efficient training algorithm that preserves O(dp) inference is novel relative to soft/probabilistic trees.

Significance:
Improving single-tree performance and reducing overfitting via joint, globally regularized training is valuable, especially when inference-time efficiency and hard routing are required. The approach could influence how oblique trees are trained and potentially yield stronger base learners for ensembles. However, the impact would be greater with broader empirical validation (ensembles, larger/deeper trees, regression tasks), stronger runtime comparisons, and analysis of stability/initialization.

Specific suggestions/questions:
- Provide explicit pseudocode and complexity analysis for the fast loss-augmented inference, including reuse of dot products along paths.
- Report numerical accuracies with standard deviations over multiple seeds; clarify hyperparameter search spaces for all baselines.
- Ablations: effect of Hamming radius (0/1/2), of SSGD vs SGD, and of ν on accuracy and leaf utilization; train from random initialization to assess dependence on CO2 initialization.
- Compare to soft/probabilistic trees (HME) trained via EM in terms of accuracy and inference cost; discuss trade-offs more quantitatively.
- Explore integration into forests/boosting: do non-greedy trees as base learners translate into better ensembles or more compact ones?
- Minor: clean up and complete citations.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear, principled formulation connecting hard trees to latent structured prediction.
- Practical, efficient training via the Hamming-ball bound; preserves O(dp) inference.
- Consistent empirical gains across datasets and depths; reduced overfitting; interpretable regularization via ν.
- Addresses a longstanding limitation of greedy tree induction with a general loss framework.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited experimental breadth (single trees only, few baselines, no variance/ablations, modest runtime analysis).
- Nonconvex optimization without convergence analysis; sensitivity to initialization not explored.
- The fast bound is local; potential bias introduced by radius-1 restriction not analyzed theoretically.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The methodological contribution is sound and relevant, and the empirical results are promising, but the paper would benefit from stronger experimental validation and clarity on the efficient inference details and optimization behavior. Confidence: high.
