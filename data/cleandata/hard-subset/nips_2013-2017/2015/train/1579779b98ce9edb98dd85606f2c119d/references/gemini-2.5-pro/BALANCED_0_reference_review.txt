### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a novel framework for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The core idea is to move beyond the traditional node-by-node greedy training procedure, which is known to be suboptimal, by optimizing a global objective function over all split parameters in the tree simultaneously. To achieve this, the authors make an elegant connection between decision tree induction and structured prediction with latent variables. Specifically, the sequence of binary decisions that routes an input `x` to a leaf is modeled as a latent structured variable `h`. This reformulation allows the discontinuous empirical loss of the tree to be upper-bounded by a continuous, albeit non-convex, surrogate objective inspired by latent structural SVMs.

The main technical contributions are twofold. First, the paper derives a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which is amenable to optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). Second, to address the exponential complexity of the loss-augmented inference step required for gradient computation (which scales as `O(2^d)` with tree depth `d`), the authors propose a modified, slightly looser bound that enables a "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm. This clever modification reduces the per-step complexity to `O(d^2)`, making the non-greedy optimization of deep trees computationally feasible. The proposed method is evaluated on several benchmark classification datasets, where it is shown to outperform various greedy baselines, including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and a greedy version of the proposed optimization (CO2), demonstrating improved accuracy and better generalization.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The paper's main strength lies in its novel formulation of decision tree learning as a latent variable structured prediction problem. This is a significant conceptual contribution that recasts a classic problem in a modern optimization framework. It provides a principled way to define a global objective function and derive a surrogate that can be optimized effectively, moving beyond the local heuristics of greedy methods.

2.  **Efficient Optimization for a Hard Problem:** Global optimization of decision trees is NP-complete. The paper makes a crucial practical contribution by developing an efficient algorithm. The reduction of the loss-augmented inference complexity from exponential (`O(2^d p)`) to polynomial (`O(d^2 p)`) is key to the method's feasibility for non-trivial tree depths. The empirical runtime comparison in Figure 4 convincingly demonstrates the scalability of the proposed fast inference approach.

3.  **Strong and Consistent Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are thorough and compelling. The proposed non-greedy method consistently outperforms several strong baselines for single-tree performance across multiple datasets and a range of tree depths (Figure 2). The fact that it also improves upon CO2, a greedy variant of a similar optimization strategy, directly supports the central claim that joint, non-greedy optimization is beneficial. The results also suggest the method is less prone to overfitting than greedy alternatives.

4.  **Generality and Extensibility:** The framework is quite general. It is not tied to a specific loss function (the paper shows examples for both classification and regression) and naturally handles oblique splits. Furthermore, the formulation opens up clear avenues for future work, such as incorporating sparsity-inducing regularizers for feature selection or applying the kernel trick for non-linear splits, as noted by the authors in the conclusion.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity on Stable SGD (SSGD):** The SSGD variant is introduced very briefly in Section 6. While the motivation to prevent leaves from becoming "inactive" is clear, the description of the algorithm is insufficient for reproducibility. It is unclear how long the leaf assignments `a(x)` are held fixed, what criteria trigger their update, and how the constrained maximization term in Eq. (14) is computed. Expanding this section with more algorithmic details or pseudocode would significantly improve the paper's clarity.

2.  **Dependence on Initialization:** The method is initialized using a greedy procedure (axis-aligned tree followed by CO2). As the surrogate objective is non-convex, the final solution is likely sensitive to this initialization. The paper would be strengthened by an ablation study that explores the impact of different initialization strategies (e.g., random initialization, or only using an axis-aligned tree) on the final performance. This would provide valuable insight into the robustness of the optimization procedure.

3.  **Context within Ensembles:** The authors make a fair point that comparing single trees is the best way to isolate the benefits of the learning algorithm itself. However, the ultimate practical utility of a tree learner often lies in its performance within an ensemble like a Random Forest. A brief discussion or a small-scale experiment would be highly valuable. For instance, does the global optimization of each tree in a forest lead to stronger overall performance compared to an ensemble of greedily-built trees? Does it perhaps reduce the tree-to-tree variance, potentially requiring different bagging strategies? Addressing this would broaden the paper's impact.

4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:**
    *   In Figure 2, some of the lines are difficult to distinguish due to similar colors and the lack of distinct markers. Using a combination of colors and markers would enhance readability.
    *   The proof of Proposition 1 and the algebraic proof of the bound (Eq. 7) are mentioned as being in the supplementary material, which is appropriate. However, it would be helpful to include a brief, intuitive sketch of the proof for Proposition 1 in the main text to aid the reader's understanding of why a larger `||W||` tightens the bound.

### Decision

This is a strong paper that addresses a fundamental and long-standing problem in machine learning. The proposed connection to structured prediction is elegant and insightful, and the development of an efficient optimization algorithm makes the non-greedy approach practical. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate a clear advance over established greedy methods. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarifications and potential extensions rather than fundamental flaws in the method or evaluation. The work is of high quality, original, and significant, making it an excellent fit for NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance.
