

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary

This paper proposes a non-greedy framework for decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, which they optimize using stochastic gradient descent. The key technical claim is that their method reduces the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p), making non-greedy tree learning feasible for deep trees. The paper compares their method with several greedy baselines on benchmark datasets and reports improved generalization performance.

## Strengths

1. The paper presents a novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, which is a well-motivated approach.
2. The problem formulation is clear and well-structured, with proper mathematical notation.
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple relevant baselines across several datasets.
4. The authors provide a reasonable justification for their choice of regularization and hyperparameters.

## Weaknesses

### Critical Technical Flaw: Incorrect Complexity Claim

The paper's most significant flaw is a fundamental error in its complexity analysis. The authors claim that their "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm has a time complexity of O(d^2p), which is polynomial in tree depth d. However, this claim is incorrect.

The fast loss-augmented inference algorithm restricts g to be within a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), meaning g differs from sgn(Wx) in at most one bit. For a binary tree of depth d, the number of internal nodes m is Θ(2^d) (for a full tree). The number of possible g values within the Hamming ball is m+1 = Θ(2^d). For each candidate g, the algorithm must determine the corresponding leaf node, which requires traversing the tree from root to leaf, taking O(d) time. Therefore, the actual time complexity of the fast loss-augmented inference is Θ(d * 2^d * p), not O(d^2p) as claimed.

This is a critical error because it undermines the paper's main technical contribution. The paper's central claim is that their method makes non-greedy tree learning feasible for deep trees by reducing complexity from exponential to polynomial. However, the actual complexity remains exponential in tree depth (d), not polynomial. This is a severe methodological flaw that invalidates the paper's main technical contribution.

### Minor Technical Issues

1. The paper states "m internal (split) nodes, and m+1 leaf (terminal) nodes" without clarifying that this relationship holds for full binary trees. For incomplete trees, this relationship does not hold, which could confuse readers.

2. In Proposition 1, the paper claims "the upper bound on the loss becomes tighter as a constant multiple of W increases." However, the proof (referenced to supplementary material) is not provided, and the claim appears counterintuitive. If W is scaled up, the loss term becomes negligible compared to the score term, making the bound approach the loss itself, which is not "tighter" in a useful optimization sense.

3. The paper claims to "handle any general loss function," but only provides two specific examples (log loss and squared loss) without discussing how their method would handle other loss functions like hinge loss or absolute loss.

4. The paper cites Bennett [1] as a non-greedy approach but does not adequately compare with this work, which is a significant related method that the authors should address more thoroughly.

5. The paper states "the gradient for most rows of W are zero at each step in training," but the actual number of non-zero gradient updates is d (the tree depth), not "most rows" as claimed.

### Formatting and Clarity Issues

1. The reference for "Global tree optimization: A non-greedy decision tree algorithm" by K.P. Bennett is incomplete (missing year and page numbers).

2. The paper claims "extension to N-ary trees is possible" but does not provide any details or discussion about this extension.

3. The complexity claim in Section 6 is not properly explained. The paper states "O(2^dp)" without clarifying that the exponent is on the tree depth, not the number of features.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting idea for non-greedy decision tree learning, the critical error in the complexity claim fundamentally undermines the paper's main technical contribution. The claimed complexity reduction from exponential to polynomial in tree depth does not hold, as the actual complexity remains exponential in tree depth. This is a severe methodological flaw that cannot be overlooked.

The paper also has several minor technical issues and formatting inconsistencies that further weaken its technical contribution. While the experimental results appear promising, the methodological flaw is too significant to be overlooked.

Given the paper's fundamental technical error, I recommend rejection. The paper's main claim is invalidated by the complexity mistake, and this flaw is too substantial to be corrected through minor revisions. The authors would need to completely rework their complexity analysis and potentially their algorithm to address this issue.

This paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way due to the critical error in the core technical contribution. While the idea of non-greedy tree learning is interesting, the paper fails to deliver a technically sound solution with the claimed efficiency gains.
