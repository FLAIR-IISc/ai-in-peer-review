This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The core idea is to introduce a latent variable for each split, allowing for joint optimization of split functions across the tree to improve generalization. The authors establish a connection to structured prediction, derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, and propose an efficient stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm for optimization, reducing the complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p). Extensive experiments on benchmark datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to greedy baselines.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a novel and promising direction.  It moves beyond the limitations of traditional greedy algorithms.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss is mathematically sound. The reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a significant technical contribution, enabling the training of deeper trees.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on multiple datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) consistently show that the proposed non-greedy approach outperforms several strong greedy baselines, including OC1 and CO2. The ablation study highlighting the importance of the regularization parameter ν provides valuable insights.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The problem formulation and the derivation of the upper bound are presented in a clear and concise manner.
*   **Completeness:** The paper provides a complete description of the proposed method, including the optimization algorithm (SGD), the complexity analysis, and the experimental setup.  The inclusion of algorithmic details (Alg 1) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison to greedy methods is thorough, the paper lacks comparison to more recent and advanced decision tree techniques, such as gradient boosted trees (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM) or deep decision forests, which are current state-of-the-art in many applications. It would be beneficial to include these baselines to better assess the practical significance of the proposed approach.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance is shown to be sensitive to the regularization parameter ν. While the paper demonstrates how to tune this parameter using a validation set, the sensitivity itself could be a limitation in practical applications where obtaining a representative validation set may be difficult. More discussion about robustness to hyperparameter settings would be valuable.
*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the upper bound derivation is sound, a more detailed theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the SGD algorithm and the generalization bounds of the learned trees would strengthen the paper.
*    **Complexity discussion:** While the authors state the complexity is O(d2p), it is not immediately clear how this scales with the number of data points, n. Providing a discussion on this aspect will be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and technically sound approach to decision tree learning that addresses the limitations of greedy algorithms.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a clear and consistent improvement over strong greedy baselines.
*   The reduction in computational complexity enables the training of deeper trees.
*   The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides a complete description of the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., gradient boosted trees) limits the assessment of the practical significance of the proposed approach.
*   The sensitivity to the regularization parameter ν could be a limitation in practical applications.
*   Limited theoretical analysis of convergence and generalization.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and technically sound approach to decision tree learning. The experimental results are compelling, and the reduction in computational complexity is a significant contribution. While the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods and the hyperparameter sensitivity are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should include a comparison to at least one strong gradient boosted tree implementation (e.g., XGBoost) and provide a more detailed discussion about the robustness of the method to hyperparameter settings. Adding a paragraph on how the complexity scales with the number of data points, n, would be helpful.
