

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors introduce a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent, claiming to jointly optimize split functions across tree levels rather than sequentially (greedily). The key technical contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm with O(d²p) complexity instead of the naive O(2^dp) complexity. The paper demonstrates modest accuracy improvements over greedy baselines on several benchmark datasets.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a clear mathematical formulation of decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables. The connection to structured prediction literature (citing [25]) is well-motivated, and the theoretical development of the upper bound is sound. The fast loss-augmented inference algorithm with O(d²p) complexity is a technical contribution that addresses a genuine computational bottleneck. The experimental section is well-organized, with careful hyperparameter tuning and comparisons against multiple baselines including CO2 (a greedy variant of their method).

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's core claim of "non-greedy" optimization is questionable. As the authors themselves acknowledge, the problem of finding globally optimal decision trees is NP-complete [11], and prior work has attempted similar approaches. Bennett [1] proposed a non-greedy multi-linear programming approach for global tree optimization decades ago, though with limitations. The authors' method appears to be a refinement of existing structured prediction techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach to decision tree learning.

The computational complexity of the proposed method (O(d²p)) is significantly higher than standard greedy methods (O(dp)), which could be prohibitive for deep trees or large-scale applications. The paper's experiments (Fig. 4) show that the fast inference method only becomes advantageous for trees deeper than 10 nodes, but the authors don't demonstrate this on large-scale datasets where decision trees are commonly applied.

Most critically, the empirical improvements over greedy baselines are modest (typically 1-3% accuracy gain across datasets), and the paper fails to demonstrate whether these improvements justify the increased computational complexity. The paper compares against "axis-aligned trees," "OC1 oblique trees," and "random oblique trees," but doesn't compare against more recent state-of-the-art decision tree methods like XGBoost or LightGBM, which have been shown to substantially outperform traditional decision trees.

The paper also doesn't sufficiently address the practical limitations of their approach. For instance, they claim their method "can handle any general loss function," but don't provide evidence that it actually outperforms greedy methods on non-standard loss functions. The regularization parameter ν is shown to implicitly control pruning (Fig. 3), but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this provides a principled alternative to established pruning techniques.

**Critical Assessment:**
While the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of decision tree learning, it fails to establish significant practical advantages over established methods. The modest empirical gains do not sufficiently justify the increased computational complexity and implementation complexity. The "non-greedy" approach appears to be a refinement of existing structured prediction techniques rather than a breakthrough for decision tree learning.

The paper's main contribution seems to be a more efficient algorithm for implementing an existing non-greedy optimization approach, but it doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this approach is superior to the simpler greedy methods that have dominated the field for decades. The paper cites Murthy and Salzburg [17] who argued that "non-greedy tree learning methods that work by looking ahead are unnecessary and sometimes harmful," yet the paper fails to address this criticism or provide compelling evidence that their method avoids these pitfalls.

**Recommendation:**
This paper falls short of the high standards required for NIPS. While the theoretical formulation is sound, the paper fails to demonstrate significant improvements over established methods that would justify the increased complexity. The empirical results are not sufficiently compelling to overcome the skepticism toward unconventional approaches to decision tree learning. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods and a clearer justification for why their approach offers advantages that would make it preferable to practitioners.

The paper's contribution is incremental at best, and the modest empirical gains do not sufficiently justify the increased complexity of the proposed method. I recommend rejection.
