

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by formulating tree optimization as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient learning via SGD, claiming to reduce computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p). The approach is evaluated on several benchmark datasets, with results suggesting improved generalization over greedy baselines.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a theoretically sound connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables. The proposed upper bound on empirical loss is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how the complexity reduction is achieved. The experimental results generally support the claim that non-greedy optimization leads to better generalization performance than greedy alternatives.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Technical Inaccuracies and Ambiguities:**
   - The paper incorrectly states that "the number of latent decision variables grows exponentially with the tree depth d" (Section 5). The number of latent variables grows exponentially with the number of internal nodes (m), which is exponential in depth (m = 2^d - 1), but the phrasing is misleading.
   - The formulation in Section 4 ("sgn(Wx) = argmax h∈Hm (hTWx)") is technically incorrect. The sign function is a binary output, while the argmax produces a binary vector. The correct relationship should be that the sign vector is determined by the argmax, not equal to it.
   - The paper claims "W ≡ [w1, . . . ,wm]^T" for a matrix W ∈ R^{m×p} whose rows are weight vectors. This is incorrect; if W has rows w1, ..., wm, then W should be [w1; w2; ...; wm], not the transpose of the row vector.

2. **Unverified Claims and Missing Details:**
   - Proposition 1 claims the upper bound becomes tighter as the norm of W increases, but the proof is only referenced to supplementary material without providing the actual proof. Without verification, this critical claim cannot be assessed.
   - The paper states "the total time complexity of gradient update for both SGD and SSGD becomes O(d^2p + k)," but doesn't clarify whether this is per example or for the full dataset. The dependence on the number of training examples is missing.
   - The reference [18] (CO2) is cited as "a special case of our upper bound approach applied greedily to trees of depth 1," but the citation refers to "Co2 forest" (a forest method), not a single tree method. This creates confusion about what CO2 actually is.

3. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:**
   - Multiple grammatical errors: "by parameterized a weight vector" (Section 3), inconsistent notation (e.g., using both w_i and w for weight vectors), and inconsistent reference formatting (e.g., [1] is a conference abstract, [17] is a PhD thesis).
   - The paper incorrectly states "m internal (split) nodes, and m+1 leaf (terminal) nodes" when describing binary trees, which is confusing because standard terminology is that a binary tree with m internal nodes has m+1 leaves.
   - The reference list contains incomplete entries (e.g., "IEEE Trans. PAMI, , " for [22]) and inappropriate citation formats (e.g., citing a book [7] for a method that should be attributed to a specific paper).

4. **Experimental Weaknesses:**
   - The paper claims non-greedy trees are "more compact" but Figure 2 only shows accuracy vs. tree depth, not tree size. Without evidence of tree size, this claim cannot be substantiated.
   - Hyperparameter tuning details are insufficient: "hyper-parameters for each method are tuned for each depth independently" lacks specifics about the tuning procedure (e.g., grid size, range of values tested).
   - The paper cites "SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST" but doesn't clarify which specific versions of these datasets were used (e.g., MNIST has multiple standard splits).

**Conclusion:**
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction, several technical inaccuracies, unverified claims, and presentation issues undermine the paper's credibility. The complexity reduction claim is ambiguous, the key proposition lacks verification, and the experimental results don't fully support the main claims about tree compactness. These issues are significant enough to warrant rejection, as they prevent readers from properly evaluating the technical contribution. The paper would benefit from thorough technical revision, including correcting the mathematical formulations, providing the missing proof for Proposition 1, and clarifying the experimental methodology.
