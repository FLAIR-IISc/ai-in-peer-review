

# Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

## Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently using stochastic gradient descent. A key technical contribution is an O(d²p) algorithm for loss-augmented inference (compared to the naive O(2^dp) complexity), which enables efficient learning of deep trees. The method outperforms greedy baselines in generalization performance across multiple benchmark datasets while maintaining the computational advantages of decision trees.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a solid theoretical foundation. The authors successfully establish a link between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, which is the paper's core insight. The convex-concave upper bound (Equation 7) is rigorously derived and validated, with a proof provided in the supplementary material. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, and the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm (O(d²p)) represents a significant improvement over the naive approach. 

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple greedy baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2) across four benchmark datasets. The results consistently show that the non-greedy approach achieves better generalization performance at various tree depths, with the method being less prone to overfitting. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the focus on binary trees and the need for careful tuning of the regularization parameter ν.

One minor weakness is that the paper doesn't provide detailed runtime comparisons with greedy methods, which would help contextualize the computational efficiency claim. While the complexity analysis shows O(d²p) versus O(2^dp), concrete runtime measurements would strengthen the argument.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation in Section 3 is clear and precise, with well-defined mathematical notation. The connection to structured prediction (Section 4) is explained logically, and the technical contributions (Sections 5-6) are presented with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 1 and the fast loss-augmented inference) are particularly clear.

The experimental section (Section 7) is well-structured, with appropriate visualizations (Figures 2-4) that effectively communicate the key results. The authors provide sufficient detail about experimental setup, including hyperparameter tuning procedures and dataset splits.

## Originality
This work makes a significant original contribution by establishing a novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables. While previous work has attempted non-greedy tree optimization (e.g., Bennett [1]), this paper provides the first efficient approach that maintains the computational advantages of decision trees while addressing their greedy induction limitation.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work. It explicitly acknowledges Bennett's earlier work [1] and explains how their approach differs (efficient learning, handling of general loss functions, maintaining O(dp) inference complexity). The connection to structured prediction literature (e.g., Yu and Joachims [25]) is well-established, and the paper properly cites and references relevant work.

The most original technical contribution is the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm with O(d²p) complexity, which makes non-greedy learning of deep trees computationally feasible. This represents a meaningful advance over previous non-greedy approaches that suffered from exponential complexity.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Decision trees are one of the most widely used machine learning models, yet they have been limited by greedy induction for decades. This paper provides a theoretically sound and computationally efficient approach to non-greedy tree learning that achieves better generalization performance across multiple datasets.

The method's ability to handle any general loss function (not just classification) while maintaining the O(dp) inference complexity of standard decision trees is particularly valuable. The regularization approach through the norm constraint on W provides a principled way to tune generalization, addressing a key limitation of previous non-greedy methods.

The paper also opens promising avenues for future work, such as learning sparse split functions via sparsity-inducing regularization or applying the kernel trick to the linear split parameters. These extensions could further enhance the method's applicability.

## Strengths and Weaknesses Summary

**Strengths:**
- Novel connection between decision trees and structured prediction with latent variables
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with well-justified upper bound
- Efficient algorithm (O(d²p) complexity) enabling practical use
- Strong experimental results showing better generalization than greedy baselines
- Clear and well-organized presentation

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited to binary trees (extension to N-ary trees is mentioned but not explored)
- Lacks detailed runtime comparisons with greedy methods
- Could provide more detailed analysis of how ν affects tree structure
- Doesn't compare with the most recent state-of-the-art decision tree methods

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of decision tree learning by providing an efficient non-greedy optimization approach that maintains the computational advantages of decision trees while improving generalization performance. The theoretical foundation is sound, the algorithmic contributions are significant, and the experimental results are compelling.

The paper meets all four NeurIPS review criteria:
- **Quality**: Technically sound with strong theoretical and empirical validation
- **Clarity**: Well-written and well-organized
- **Originality**: Makes a novel contribution that builds on but significantly advances prior work
- **Significance**: Addresses a fundamental limitation in a widely used machine learning method with practical impact

The paper would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS, as it advances the state of the art in a core machine learning topic with both theoretical and practical significance. The method has the potential to be adopted in real-world applications where decision trees are currently used.

I recommend acceptance, as this paper represents a meaningful advance in decision tree learning that would be of interest to the NeurIPS community. The authors have addressed the key limitations of previous non-greedy approaches while maintaining the computational efficiency that makes decision trees so appealing.
