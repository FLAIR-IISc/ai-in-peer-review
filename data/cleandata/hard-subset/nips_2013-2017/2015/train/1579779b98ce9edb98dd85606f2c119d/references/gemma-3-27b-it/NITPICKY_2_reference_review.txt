This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning, framed as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The core idea is to formulate an upper bound on the empirical loss and optimize it using stochastic gradient descent. The authors claim improvements in generalization performance and computational efficiency (reducing complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p)). While the motivation is sound – addressing the limitations of greedy tree induction – the paper is riddled with minor issues that collectively detract from its impact and raise concerns about the rigor of the work.

The introduction is reasonable, but the asterisked footnote regarding affiliation feels somewhat haphazardly placed. The justification for non-greedy approaches is clearly stated, but the transition between highlighting the limitations of greedy methods and introducing the latent variable formulation feels abrupt. The claim of handling “any general loss function” is overstated; while the framework *allows* for different loss functions, the presented derivations and algorithm focus specifically on softmax and squared loss. 

The related work section is comprehensive but lacks critical analysis. While mentioning relevant papers, it often dismisses them too quickly. For example, the criticism of Murthy and Salzburg [17] relies on the assumption that regularization is *always* necessary, which isn't universally true. The claim that their formulation improves on prior art “in a number of ways” is vague and needs more specific quantification, especially given the acknowledged NP-completeness of global tree optimization.

Section 3 is dense and notation-heavy. While mathematically precise, it could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The constant appending of "-1" to the input feature vector to incorporate the offset parameter is mentioned almost as an afterthought and might confuse readers unfamiliar with this convention. The formulation of the empirical loss is clear, but the justification for introducing latent variables feels somewhat contrived. 

Section 4’s link to structured prediction is logical, but the benefits of this connection aren’t fully realized. The discussion lacks a deeper engagement with the nuances of structured prediction beyond simply drawing an analogy.

The upper bound derivation in Section 5 is the paper's technical core, and here, the lack of detail is most problematic. The algebraic proof is relegated to the supplementary material, which is unacceptable for a core claim. While the bound itself is plausible, the justification for its tightness is weak. Proposition 1 is trivial and doesn't offer substantial insight. The regularization discussion is reasonable, but the motivation for hard constraints (rather than soft regularization with a Lagrange multiplier) isn't adequately explained. 

Section 6 details the optimization algorithm. The discussion of fast loss-augmented inference is convincing, but the claim of O(d2p) complexity feels optimistic given the potential for nested loops within the search for ĝ. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is concise but lacks sufficient detail. For instance, the learning rate schedule isn't specified, and the use of momentum and mini-batches is only mentioned in passing.

The experimental setup (Section 7) is reasonable, but the results presentation is underwhelming. Figures 2, 3, and 4 are presented without sufficient explanation. The axis labels are sometimes unclear, and the error bars are missing. The discussion of the results is superficial, focusing on broad trends rather than specific comparisons. The choice of initializing with a greedy tree (CO2) is sensible, but the rationale for this specific initialization isn't fully explained. The claim of "much larger speedup factors for larger datasets" is unsubstantiated.

The conclusion reiterates the main contributions but doesn't acknowledge the limitations of the work. The discussion of future work (sparsity and kernel methods) feels tacked on and lacks concrete details.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of conventional decision trees – the lack of non-greedy optimization.
* The formulation of the problem as a structured prediction task is a novel and potentially fruitful direction.
* The proposed algorithm achieves reasonable performance on benchmark datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Numerous minor flaws in notation, clarity, and presentation.
* Lack of detailed proofs and justifications for key claims.
* Superficial experimental evaluation and analysis.
* Overstated claims of originality and significance.
* Insufficient detail in the algorithmic description.
* Reliance on supplementary material for core technical derivations.

**Overall:**

Despite a potentially interesting idea, the paper suffers from a lack of polish and rigor. The numerous minor flaws, combined with the superficial experimental evaluation, raise serious concerns about the validity and impact of the work. While the core idea of a non-greedy tree induction method is appealing, the current presentation is insufficient to warrant acceptance at NIPS. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the points raised in this review and resubmit to a future conference. The potential is there, but significant revisions are required.
