This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and developing a convex-concave upper bound for optimization via stochastic gradient descent. While the motivation – addressing the limitations of greedy tree induction – is reasonable, and the connection to structured prediction is formally presented, the paper falls short of convincing me of its practical significance due to a lack of compelling evidence demonstrating a substantial advantage over established, and often simpler, methods.

The core idea of jointly optimizing split functions is not entirely new. Bennett’s work (cited as [1, 2, 3]) explored similar non-greedy approaches, albeit with limitations acknowledged by the authors. The paper correctly points out those limitations, but fails to convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method overcomes them sufficiently to warrant the added complexity. The algorithmic improvements to reduce complexity to O(d2p) are welcome, but the constant factors involved and the scalability to genuinely large datasets remain unclear. The empirical evaluation, while thorough in comparing against several baselines, doesn't demonstrate a consistently large or statistically significant improvement justifying the increased methodological complexity. The gains appear modest, and the performance variations across datasets are not fully explained.

My primary concern revolves around the reliance on a surrogate objective. While theoretically sound, the authors don't provide sufficient justification for why this specific upper bound is particularly well-suited to decision tree learning. The regularization parameter ν is critical, and the explanation of its effect, while present, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The connection between ν and pruning is observed empirically rather than theoretically motivated. Furthermore, the initialization procedure, relying on a greedy tree, introduces a bias that might influence the final results and isn’t adequately discussed.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, although the mathematical notation can be dense at times. The organization is logical, progressing from problem formulation to optimization and experiments. However, the presentation of the optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) could be improved with more detailed explanations of each step. The connection between the theoretical contributions (surrogate objective, complexity reduction) and the practical implementation isn't always clear.

The originality lies primarily in the specific formulation of the decision tree problem within a structured prediction framework and the development of the tailored surrogate objective. However, the core concepts – structured prediction, surrogate optimization – are well-established. The novelty is incremental rather than revolutionary.

The significance is questionable. While the paper addresses an important problem – improving decision tree learning – the demonstrated improvements over existing methods are not substantial enough to suggest a paradigm shift. The method appears computationally more expensive, and the benefits are not consistently realized across different datasets. The claim that the method can handle any general loss function is true, but not particularly groundbreaking, as many existing methods can also be adapted to different loss functions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Formal connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction.
*   Development of a tractable surrogate objective for non-greedy optimization.
*   Algorithmic improvements reducing computational complexity.
*   Empirical validation demonstrating competitive performance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Modest performance improvements over established baselines.
*   Lack of strong theoretical justification for the chosen surrogate objective.
*   Computational complexity remains a concern, particularly for large datasets.
*   Limited novelty beyond the specific formulation within a known framework.
*   The dependence on a greedy initialization.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the work is technically sound and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the field, the limited practical improvements and the added complexity do not justify acceptance to NIPS. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a significant advance. Further work is needed to demonstrate the scalability, robustness, and practical benefits of this approach before it can be considered a valuable contribution to the field. I encourage the authors to address the concerns raised above and to provide more compelling evidence of the method's advantages in future work.
