

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for loss-augmented inference that reduces computational complexity from exponential in tree depth to O(d²p), enabling practical training of deep trees. The approach differs from prior work by optimizing all split functions simultaneously under a global objective rather than using the traditional greedy node-by-node approach.

## Strengths
1. **Novel formulation**: The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is well-motivated and represents a significant conceptual contribution. The paper clearly articulates how the latent variable formulation enables joint optimization of split functions.

2. **Computational efficiency**: The development of the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm (reducing complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p)) is a major technical contribution that makes non-greedy tree learning practical for deep trees.

3. **Strong experimental results**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets compared to greedy baselines, including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and random oblique trees. The results support the claim that non-greedy optimization leads to better generalization.

4. **Theoretical grounding**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the upper bound and regularization approach, with Proposition 1 offering important insights into the relationship between the bound's tightness and the norm of W.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient novelty justification**: While the paper makes a clear connection to structured prediction, it doesn't adequately address whether this specific formulation for decision trees has been previously explored. Yu and Joachims (ICML 2009) already established connections between structured prediction and similar problems, and the paper should clarify how this work differs from or builds upon that foundation.

2. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art**: The experimental section compares only with traditional greedy methods (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees) and CO2 (a greedy variant of their method). The paper misses an opportunity to compare with more recent, high-performing decision tree methods, such as those using gradient boosting or other ensemble techniques that have become standard in practice.

3. **Incomplete theoretical analysis**: The paper lacks a thorough convergence analysis for the proposed SGD algorithm. While the surrogate objective is shown to be convex-concave, the paper doesn't address whether the algorithm converges to a global optimum or provide bounds on convergence rates.

4. **Ambiguous motivation for Hamming ball restriction**: The justification for restricting loss-augmented inference to Hamming balls of radius 1 (rather than the full set H^m) is somewhat vague. The paper states that this "makes the optimization tractable" but doesn't sufficiently explain why this approximation is valid or how it affects the quality of the solution.

## Recommendations for Improvement
1. **Clarify novelty**: The authors should explicitly compare their work with structured prediction approaches to decision trees in prior literature, particularly Yu and Joachims (2009), and clearly articulate how their formulation differs and why it's more suitable for decision tree learning.

2. **Expand experimental comparisons**: Include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art methods for decision trees, such as those using gradient boosting or other ensemble approaches, to better demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed method.

3. **Add theoretical analysis**: Provide a convergence analysis for the SGD algorithm, discussing whether it converges to a local optimum and under what conditions. This would significantly strengthen the theoretical foundation of the work.

4. **Improve explanation of key algorithmic choice**: Provide a more detailed justification for the Hamming ball restriction in the loss-augmented inference, including theoretical or empirical evidence that this approximation doesn't significantly degrade performance.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a technically sound approach to non-greedy decision tree learning with a clear connection to structured prediction. The computational efficiency improvements are significant and well-justified. However, the paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of its novelty relative to existing work, more comprehensive experimental comparisons, and a more thorough theoretical analysis. The current presentation makes the contribution somewhat less compelling than it could be.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing an efficient non-greedy optimization framework for decision trees, but it needs to better position itself against prior work and provide stronger theoretical grounding. With the suggested revisions, this could be a strong NIPS submission.

**Decision**: Weak Accept (with significant revisions required)

**Pro**: Strong technical contribution with clear computational efficiency gains, good experimental results demonstrating improved generalization, well-motivated connection to structured prediction.

**Con**: Insufficient novelty justification, limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods, lack of convergence analysis, ambiguous motivation for key algorithmic choice.
