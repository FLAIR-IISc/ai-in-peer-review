Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy, jointly optimized approach for learning oblique decision trees. The key idea is to recast split decisions as latent binary variables and to optimize a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss inspired by structured prediction with latent variables. The resulting surrogate objective can be minimized via SGD. A central technical contribution is reducing loss-augmented inference from exponential in tree depth (O(2^d p)) to O(d^2 p) by restricting the loss-augmented search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current split decisions. The method keeps standard O(dp) test-time inference, introduces per-node norm constraints to regularize W, and proposes a Stable SGD variant to avoid collapse of leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multi-class datasets show consistent improvements over axis-aligned CART, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2 (a depth-1 greedy version of the bound), with better generalization and scalability.

Relation to prior work
- Greedy tree induction (ID3/CART) remains the default; prior global optimization is NP-complete. The paper connects tree learning to latent structured prediction (e.g., M3N, structural SVM with latent variables), an original angle for decision trees.
- Compared to soft splits (Hierarchical Mixture of Experts), this retains hard splits and the computational efficiency of trees; authors argue soft models sacrifice the runtime advantage.
- Prior max-margin trees and SVM-at-node approaches regularize local decisions; here the entire set of split functions is optimized under a single surrogate objective.
- CO2 applies a similar bound greedily one node at a time; this work demonstrates the benefits of joint non-greedy optimization and introduces an efficient loss-augmented inference scheme.

Strengths
- Novel formulation: Casting hard split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a structured-loss upper bound is a bold, principled departure from standard greedy induction.
- Efficiency: The Hamming-ball restriction for loss-augmented inference is a clever, well-motivated trick that cuts complexity to O(d^2 p), making global optimization feasible for deeper trees.
- Practicality: Maintains O(dp) inference at test time, uses simple per-node norm constraints, and adds SSGD to mitigate inactive leaves.
- Empirical gains: Consistent improvements across datasets and depths versus strong greedy baselines; reduced overfitting and clear sensitivity analysis for the regularization parameter.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The objective, algorithms (SGD and SSGD), complexity analysis, and initialization pipeline are clearly described.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond an upper bound and a discussion of scale, there are no convergence or consistency guarantees, nor generalization bounds tied to the per-node constraints. Some theoretical insight (even limited) on generalization would strengthen the work.
- Scope of inference restriction: The Hamming radius-1 bound raises the question of how much non-greedy cooperation is actually captured. Some ablation assessing performance vs. radius (e.g., 0, 1, 2) would be informative.
- Baselines: While the single-tree focus is reasonable, the paper omits comparisons to strong modern oblique/differentiable trees and soft-split models in accuracy (even if slower). A brief empirical point or citation-supported argument would help.
- Dataset breadth: Experiments are limited to classification on standard benchmarks; a regression task would substantiate the claim of generality. Also, scaling behavior on larger datasets or higher-dimensional inputs would further validate the O(d^2 p) advantage.
- Initialization dependence: The method relies on greedy trees and CO2 for initialization; it would be helpful to report sensitivity to initialization quality.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The bound (7) and its radius-1 variant (13) are reasonable, the optimization is straightforward, and the complexity analysis is careful. The SSGD heuristic is plausible but could be better justified empirically (e.g., effect on accuracy and leaf utilization). Claims are generally supported by experiments, though broader baselines and tasks would improve completeness.

Clarity
Well written and organized. The connection to latent structured prediction and the navigation function are explained clearly. Algorithmic steps are explicit. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement, which is acceptable. Figures reportedly show consistent trends; including exact numerical gains or confidence intervals would further aid interpretation.

Originality
High. The latent-variable structured prediction view of hard decision trees and the attendant efficient loss-augmented inference are novel and impactful. The work goes beyond prior local-margin or greedy upper-bound methods (e.g., CO2) by enabling joint optimization at scale.

Significance
High. A principled, efficient, non-greedy training method for decision trees is a meaningful contribution with practical implications. The approach should stimulate follow-up work (extensions to forests, sparsity-inducing regularization, kernelization as noted by the authors).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a small ablation on Hamming radius (0 vs. 1 vs. 2) to calibrate the trade-off between tractability and tightness.
- Include at least one regression experiment.
- Report sensitivity to initialization and to the SSGD schedule; quantify inactive leaves and their impact.
- Add a brief discussion or experiment contrasting with a representative soft-split baseline (e.g., HME), acknowledging runtime differences.
- Where possible, provide numerical test accuracies and training times in the main paper for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and bold reframing of tree induction.
- Elegant and efficient loss-augmented inference enabling deep trees.
- Consistent empirical gains with less overfitting.
- Clear, practical algorithm that preserves tree inference efficiency.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical generalization story.
- Restricted baselines and tasks; no regression results.
- Dependence on initialization and heuristic SSGD not thoroughly analyzed.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new direction for decision tree optimization with a well-motivated surrogate and an efficient algorithm, and delivers solid empirical benefits. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the relatively minor shortcomings in theoretical depth and experimental breadth.

Confidence
High.
