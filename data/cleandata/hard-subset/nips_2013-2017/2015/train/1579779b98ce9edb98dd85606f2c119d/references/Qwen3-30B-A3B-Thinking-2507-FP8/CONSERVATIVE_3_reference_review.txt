

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a novel approach to decision tree learning by reformulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors argue that conventional greedy tree induction methods are suboptimal because they optimize split functions one node at a time without considering the global structure of the tree. Their approach formulates the decision tree learning problem as a latent variable structured prediction problem, develops a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, and optimizes this bound using stochastic gradient descent with a reduced complexity of O(d²p) compared to the naive O(2^dp) approach.

The paper makes a reasonable connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, and the theoretical development of the upper bound is technically sound. The authors provide a clear mathematical derivation of their approach and demonstrate how their method can be applied to both classification and regression tasks. The connection to previous work on non-greedy tree learning (Bennett [1], Murthy and Salzburg [17]) is acknowledged, though the paper could better differentiate its contributions from these earlier approaches.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical foundation linking decision tree learning to structured prediction is well-motivated and the mathematical derivation of the upper bound appears correct.
2. The computational complexity reduction from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) is a genuine technical contribution that addresses a significant practical limitation.
3. The paper provides a clear algorithmic description and implementation details for their approach.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient experimental validation:** The paper tests on only four relatively small datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) with Connect4 having just 55,000 training examples. This is inadequate to demonstrate the practical benefits of the method for large-scale applications where decision trees are commonly used. The paper should have included results on larger datasets or at least provided a more thorough analysis of scalability.

2. **Lack of rigorous comparison:** The comparison with baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2) is limited. The authors don't compare with more recent state-of-the-art decision tree methods such as Extremely Randomized Trees, or modern ensemble methods like XGBoost or LightGBM. The claim that "non-greedy trees achieve the best test performance" is not convincingly supported by the experiments, particularly since the performance gains shown in Figure 2 appear marginal (typically 1-2% improvement).

3. **Inadequate analysis of trade-offs:** The paper fails to thoroughly analyze the trade-offs between their method and established approaches. For example, they don't provide a comprehensive analysis of how the regularization parameter ν affects the tree structure, generalization, and computational cost. The claim that their method is "less susceptible to overfitting" is not statistically validated.

4. **Lack of statistical significance testing:** The results don't include statistical significance tests for the performance differences between the proposed method and baselines, making it difficult to assess whether the observed improvements are meaningful.

5. **Overstated significance:** The paper claims that their method "promotes cooperation between the split nodes to create more compact trees" and "leads to better generalization performance," but the experimental evidence provided is insufficient to support these strong claims. The marginal performance improvements shown in the experiments don't justify the significantly more complex methodology.

6. **Limited discussion of limitations:** The authors don't adequately address when their method might fail or where greedy approaches would be preferable. They don't discuss scenarios where the computational overhead of their method would outweigh the marginal performance gains.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to decision tree learning with a plausible complexity reduction, the experimental validation is insufficient to demonstrate the practical significance of the method. The marginal performance improvements on small datasets don't justify the complexity of the proposed approach compared to well-established, efficient decision tree methods that are widely used in practice. The paper would need to demonstrate substantially better performance on larger datasets and provide more thorough comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods to be compelling.

The authors should address the following before reconsideration:
1. Conduct experiments on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet, large-scale tabular data) to demonstrate scalability and real-world applicability.
2. Provide a more comprehensive comparison with recent state-of-the-art decision tree methods (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost).
3. Include statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons.
4. Provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between their method and established approaches, including computational cost versus performance gains.

Given the current evidence, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate that the proposed method offers a significant improvement over existing approaches to justify its publication in NIPS. The methodological contribution is interesting but lacks the experimental rigor and practical significance required for acceptance at this venue.
