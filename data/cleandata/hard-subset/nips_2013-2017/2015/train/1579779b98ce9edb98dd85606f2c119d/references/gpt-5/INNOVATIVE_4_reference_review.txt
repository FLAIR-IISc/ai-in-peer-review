Summary
This paper proposes a principled, non-greedy approach to learning decision trees by framing split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a global surrogate objective. The key idea is to view the vector of internal-node decisions h = sgn(Wx) as a latent structured output, and to upper-bound the empirical loss by a margin-style convex–concave surrogate inspired by latent structural SVMs. The naive loss-augmented inference would be exponential in depth; the paper introduces a tighter, computationally feasible variant by restricting loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), yielding O(d^2 p) time per SGD step while retaining O(dp) inference at test time. The method supports general losses, uses per-node norm constraints to regularize and control bound tightness, and introduces a “Stable SGD” variant to prevent leaf collapse. Experiments on several LibSVM multi-class datasets show that, for single trees across varying depths, the proposed method consistently improves test accuracy over axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and a greedy CO2 variant; runtime scaling with depth also supports the efficiency claim.

Strengths
- Novel formulation: Clear and elegant link between decision tree training and latent structured prediction. Casting the split vector as a latent code and optimizing a global surrogate is a fresh and bold direction for tree learning.
- Efficient optimization: The Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference is a clever compromise, dropping exponential complexity to O(d^2 p) while keeping the spirit of non-greedy joint optimization.
- Generality: Works with generic losses (log-loss, squared loss), keeps fast inference, and is compatible with per-node norm constraints that provide a principled capacity control (and implicit pruning).
- Practical refinements: Stable SGD to avoid inactive leaves addresses a real optimization pathology in joint training of trees.
- Empirical signal: Across multiple datasets and depths, the method yields better generalization than strong greedy baselines; ablation on ν illustrates how norm constraints regulate both fit and effective tree size.
- Clarity of contributions: The paper is well-organized, with a sound justification for regularization and a reasonable complexity analysis.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Experimental scope and baselines: The focus on single trees is justified to isolate the algorithmic effect, but adding at least one ensemble baseline (e.g., bagging/boosting with comparable tree budgets) in the supplement would strengthen claims of practical impact. Similarly, including standard post-pruning for greedy trees would make the comparison fairer.
- Missing comparisons: No empirical comparison to soft decision trees / hierarchical mixture of experts (even a timing/accuracy trade-off) or more recent oblique-tree learners with explicit margin regularization beyond OC1.
- Limited ablations: The benefits of SSGD are asserted but not quantified. An ablation showing training dynamics, fraction of inactive leaves, and final accuracy with/without SSGD would clarify its importance. Likewise, exploring Hamming radius >1 (or adaptive radius) could show whether the fast bound unduly restricts optimization.
- Reporting: Results are mostly via plots; tabulated numbers with variance would help. Include wall-clock training times vs baselines (not just scaling vs depth) to support efficiency claims on larger datasets.
- Theoretical details: Some key proofs are deferred; it would help to move the proof of the main bound (Eq. 7) or a sharpened sketch into the main text, and to more explicitly characterize the convex–concave structure and any convergence guarantees under projected SGD.
- Scope: Regression is discussed but not evaluated. Even one regression dataset would substantiate generality.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to initialization? Does initialization from an axis-aligned greedy tree materially affect outcomes vs random or OC1-initialized trees?
- Can you quantify how often loss-augmented solutions differ by exactly one bit from sgn(Wx)? Does a larger Hamming radius improve results on deeper trees?
- What schedule/criterion do you use to update assignments in SSGD? How many steps are assignments held fixed, and how is “improvement negligible” defined?
- Could you report the number of active leaves at convergence as a function of ν and depth?
- Any preliminary results on sparse W (e.g., per-node L1 or group sparsity) as hinted in the conclusion?

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with a plausible bound and an efficient solver. The objective is non-convex (as expected); the use of projected SGD with momentum/minibatches is reasonable. Some theoretical points could be elaborated, but the methodology is coherent and the empirical results support main claims.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-structured. A concise pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference and SSGD would improve reproducibility. Minor notational clarifications around f(·) and path indices would help.

Originality
- High. The latent-structured perspective and the Hamming-ball relaxation for loss-augmented inference in trees are novel and represent a meaningful shift from classical greedy induction.

Significance
- High potential. If broadly adopted, this could reset the baseline for training single (and possibly ensemble) trees by enabling non-greedy, globally trained oblique trees with competitive efficiency. The approach is also extensible (kernelization, sparsity).

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel conceptual contribution with a practical, efficient algorithm.
- Consistent empirical gains for single trees across datasets and depths.
- Clear path for extensions and impact on how trees are trained.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing stronger baselines (ensembles, pruning, soft trees) and some ablations/reporting details.
- Limited theoretical exposition in the main text.

Recommendation
- Weak accept (leaning accept). The conceptual novelty and the efficiency trick are compelling and, in my view, outweigh the moderate experimental gaps. With a bit more empirical breadth and clarity, this could be a strong contribution.

Confidence: 4/5
