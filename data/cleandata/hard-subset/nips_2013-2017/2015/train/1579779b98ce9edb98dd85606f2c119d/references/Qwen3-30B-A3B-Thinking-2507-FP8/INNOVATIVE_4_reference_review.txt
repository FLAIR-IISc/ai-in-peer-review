

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to decision tree learning, challenging the decades-old paradigm of greedy tree induction. The authors propose a non-greedy framework that jointly optimizes split functions at all tree levels under a global objective, establishing a crucial connection between decision trees and structured prediction with latent variables. The key innovation lies in their convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, which enables efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent while maintaining the O(dp) inference complexity of standard decision trees.

The paper's most significant contribution is its non-greedy optimization framework, which directly addresses the fundamental limitation of greedy tree induction: the inability to fine-tune split functions based on results from lower tree levels. By formulating decision trees as structured prediction with latent variables (where the latent variable represents the path through the tree), the authors develop a surrogate objective that can be optimized efficiently. Their technical breakthrough is reducing the computational complexity from exponential (O(2^dp)) to O(d²p) through a clever reformulation of the loss-augmented inference problem, making deep tree learning feasible.

The paper makes a strong connection to structured prediction literature (e.g., Yu and Joachims [25]), which is particularly valuable as it places their work in a well-established theoretical framework. This connection is not merely superficial but enables the development of a principled optimization approach that could inspire future work in structured prediction. The authors also provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including proofs of their upper bound and the effect of the regularization parameter.

The experimental results demonstrate the practical value of the approach. On multiple benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST), the non-greedy trees consistently outperform greedy baselines, including conventional axis-aligned trees, OC1, and random oblique trees. Crucially, the non-greedy approach shows better generalization performance and reduced overfitting across various tree depths, with the norm regularization parameter (ν) providing a principled way to control the tightness of the tree's fit to training data. The computational efficiency of their fast loss-augmented inference algorithm (O(d²p) vs. exponential complexity) is convincingly demonstrated in Figure 4.

Strengths:
1. **High Originality**: The non-greedy approach represents a fundamental shift from the standard greedy paradigm in decision tree learning, which has remained largely unchanged for decades. This is a significant conceptual advance.
2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The connection to structured prediction with latent variables provides a rigorous framework for the problem, with well-supported theoretical analysis.
3. **Practical Implementation**: The authors successfully address the computational challenges of non-greedy optimization, making their approach feasible for real-world applications.
4. **Clear Experimental Validation**: The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over multiple baselines across diverse datasets.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could provide more detailed comparison to recent methods that have attempted to improve decision trees (e.g., recent work on oblique decision trees or tree ensembles).
2. While the computational complexity is reduced to O(d²p), the paper doesn't provide extensive analysis of how the approach scales to extremely large datasets (beyond the benchmark datasets used).
3. The relationship between the regularization parameter ν and the tree's structure could be explored more deeply.

The paper excels in originality and significance, which are the most important criteria for NIPS. While the methodological details are sound, the paper's true value lies in its bold rethinking of a fundamental ML technique. The authors have successfully bridged a theoretical gap between decision trees and structured prediction, creating a new direction for future research.

This work is not merely an incremental improvement to existing methods but introduces a fundamentally new approach to decision tree learning that could have broad implications. The connection to structured prediction opens doors for future work that could extend this framework to more complex structured prediction tasks. The practical benefits demonstrated in the experiments further validate the significance of the contribution.

Given the high novelty and the potential to influence how decision trees are learned in the future, I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper represents a significant conceptual advance that aligns perfectly with NIPS's focus on innovative and impactful research. While the computational complexity analysis could be expanded, this is a minor concern compared to the paper's bold new direction.

The authors have made a compelling case for rethinking decision tree learning, and their approach is both theoretically grounded and practically viable. This is the kind of paper that could reshape how decision trees are used in machine learning applications across the field.
