Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy method to train oblique decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a global surrogate objective. The key idea is to upper-bound the empirical loss with a convex-concave objective inspired by latent structured prediction. The bound involves a loss-augmented inference over binary split codes; exact loss-augmented inference is exponential in depth, so the authors introduce a tractable variant that restricts candidate codes to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current routing, yielding O(d^2 p) per update for tree depth d and feature dimension p. Training uses projected SGD with row-wise norm constraints on W (split weights), and a “Stable SGD” variant that freezes leaf assignments intermittently to avoid deactivating leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multi-class datasets compare single trees against greedy baselines (axis-aligned CART, OC1, random oblique, CO2) and show improved test accuracy and reduced overfitting; runtime scaling of the fast loss-augmented inference is also demonstrated.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reformulation sgn(Wx) = argmaxh h^T Wx is standard and permits a latent-structured view. The inequality defining the upper bound (Eq. 7) is correct by construction (tight at ĥ) and the projected constraint on each row of W is a reasonable way to control the bound’s scale sensitivity (Proposition 1 explains the scaling effect). The surrogate objective is difference-of-convex, and subgradient steps are appropriate. The fast variant (Eq. 13) is also a valid upper bound and drastically reduces inference cost.
- Correctness and completeness: The optimization algorithm is fully specified (Alg. 1), including projection and gradient expressions. The complexity analysis is clear: per step O(d^2 p + k). The SSGD heuristic is sensible to stabilize leaf usage.
- Empirics: Results show consistent gains over several greedy baselines across depth settings, and a clear scaling benefit of fast loss-augmented inference. However, the empirical evaluation has gaps:
  - No ablation showing the effect on accuracy of full vs fast (radius-1) loss-augmented inference.
  - Limited baselines: single-tree comparisons are fair for isolating the training objective, but modern strong baselines (e.g., gradient boosting, random forests) are omitted even as a point of reference; the authors explain the focus, but it weakens claims of practical significance.
  - No statistical significance or variance across runs; sensitivity to initialization (greedy/CO2) is not analyzed.
  - No regression experiments despite claiming generality.
Overall, the methodology appears sound; the main weaknesses are empirical breadth and ablations.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The mapping from split decisions to latent variables and the definition of the navigation function f are well explained. The role of ν (row-norm constraint) is described both theoretically (scale-bias of the bound) and empirically (implicit pruning). Algorithmic details (e.g., projection, gradients) are provided. Suggestions:
- Provide a concise pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference to complement the high-level description.
- Include a small illustrative example to show how g differs from ĥ along a path and how Eq. 12 zeroes out off-path bits.
- Report exact dataset stats and hyperparameter ranges in an appendix to aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The connection between tree induction and latent-structured prediction via h = sgn(Wx) and the use of a global loss upper bound are novel in this specific form for decision trees. CO2 is acknowledged as a greedy depth-1 special case; this paper advances it by joint non-greedy optimization across the whole tree, with a principled surrogate and efficient inference.
- The “radius-1 Hamming ball” restriction is a simple but effective idea to trade tightness for efficiency; I have not seen this specific device used for tree training.
- Related work is reasonably covered (oblique trees, SVM-at-node, soft/EM trees, Mondrian forests, global MIP formulations), though a short discussion contrasting with differentiable soft-routing trees would help contextualize design choices.

Significance
- Scientifically, the paper provides a clear, optimization-grounded alternative to greedy induction, with general losses and explicit regularization, and an efficiency trick that makes non-greedy training practical for deeper trees.
- Practically, single trees trained by this method outperform greedy oblique and axis-aligned baselines across depths and are less prone to overfitting. That said, for many applications ensembles dominate; the paper does not evaluate forests/boosting built from the proposed trees, which limits immediate impact claims. Still, as a building block, the contribution is promising.
- The lack of ablations and broader baselines reduces the strength of the empirical case, but the idea is likely to influence subsequent work on non-greedy trees and structured optimization.

Pros
- Clear and principled formulation linking tree learning to latent structured prediction.
- Valid upper bounds; careful analysis of scaling/regularization and efficient inference (O(d^2 p)).
- Consistent empirical improvements over strong greedy single-tree baselines; reduced overfitting.
- Practical training algorithm with projected SGD and stabilization.

Cons
- Empirical scope limited: no ablations (full vs fast bound, with/without SSGD), no regression tasks, no ensembles, no variance analysis.
- Theoretical guarantees limited to surrogate properties; no generalization bounds or convergence guarantees beyond standard SGD heuristics.
- Dependence on initialization is not studied.
- The Hamming-radius-1 restriction may over-loosen the bound; its effect on final accuracy is not quantified.

Questions/requests for authors
- Provide an ablation comparing full loss-augmented inference vs the radius-1 variant on a smaller tree to quantify the trade-off in tightness vs accuracy.
- Analyze sensitivity to initialization (greedy/CO2), ν, and learning rate; report variance over multiple runs.
- Can the fast inference be extended to “flip one bit along the path only” (d+1 candidates) vs anywhere in the code? Is that what you implement in practice?
- Any results on regression tasks or large-k settings?
- Discuss prospects for ensembles (e.g., bagging) with your non-greedy trees.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper is technically solid, clearly written, and proposes a novel, efficient non-greedy training framework with encouraging results on single trees. Strengthen the empirical section with ablations and additional baselines if space permits.
