

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a fundamentally new approach to decision tree learning by breaking away from the decades-old greedy paradigm. The authors frame decision tree induction as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent. Their key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from exponential (O(2^dp)) to quadratic (O(d^2p)) in tree depth, making non-greedy learning feasible for deeper trees. The paper demonstrates that this approach leads to better generalization performance across multiple benchmark datasets compared to conventional greedy methods.

The paper's novelty is substantial. While the limitations of greedy decision trees have been recognized for decades (e.g., Hyafil and Rivest, 1976), most prior work has either proposed impractical exact optimization approaches (Bennett, 1999) or focused on modifications to the greedy process. This paper's formulation as a structured prediction problem with latent variables represents a fresh perspective that connects decision trees to a well-established optimization framework. The authors' technical insight about the Hamming ball constraint to make loss-augmented inference tractable is particularly elegant and represents a significant methodological contribution.

The paper is well-motivated and properly situates itself within the literature. The authors correctly identify the fundamental limitation of greedy tree induction (optimizing splits in isolation without considering the global tree structure) and demonstrate how their approach addresses this limitation. The connection to structured prediction work (e.g., Yu and Joachims, 2009) is appropriate and well-justified.

The experimental results are compelling. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) compared to greedy baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees) and even the simpler CO2 method. The ablation study showing the impact of the regularization parameter ν on tree pruning and generalization is particularly valuable. The runtime analysis (Fig. 4) convincingly demonstrates the scalability of their fast loss-augmented inference approach.

The paper's strengths are its bold conceptual shift away from greedy learning, the elegant technical solution to the computational complexity problem, and the clear empirical validation of the approach. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., scalability to very large datasets, limited exploration of hyperparameters) without overstating their claims.

The main weaknesses are somewhat limited experimental scope (only four benchmark datasets) and the lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art tree-based methods (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM). While the authors properly acknowledge the greedy nature of these competitors, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with recent tree-based methods that may incorporate some non-greedy elements. Additionally, the paper could better clarify how their approach relates to recent work on differentiable decision trees (e.g., Bacciu et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018), though this might be outside the scope of their focus.

The paper's significance is high. Decision trees are among the most widely used machine learning models in practice, and the paper's approach could potentially lead to better performing trees for a wide range of applications. The theoretical connection to structured prediction might also inspire similar approaches in other domains. The methodological contribution of reducing the complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth is particularly valuable for making non-greedy learning practical.

The paper meets NeurIPS's quality standards with sound theoretical foundations and well-executed experiments. The clarity is excellent, with clear exposition of the problem, formulation, and technical contributions. The writing is well-organized and accessible to the machine learning community.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in decision tree learning. While the experimental validation could be slightly more comprehensive, the novelty of the approach and its clear theoretical and empirical advantages over the standard greedy paradigm make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS. The authors have made a compelling case for a fundamentally new direction in decision tree learning that could have substantial practical impact.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It is a rare example of a paper that successfully breaks from the standard paradigm with a novel theoretical framework, a clever technical solution to a major computational barrier, and clear empirical validation of the approach. The paper's contribution is not merely incremental but represents a potential shift in how decision trees are trained and understood.
