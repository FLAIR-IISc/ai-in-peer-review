Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a non-greedy method for optimizing decision trees by jointly learning the split parameters across the tree under a global objective, rather than fitting each split greedily as in ID3/CART. The key idea is to view the m binary split decisions for an input as a latent vector h ∈ {−1,+1}^m; this allows the problem to be cast in the framework of structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss of the tree, akin to margin-rescaled latent structural SVM objectives, and optimize it with stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A direct loss-augmented inference step would be exponential in depth; they show how to reduce complexity to O(d^2 p) by restricting the maximization to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), leveraging the fact that only decisions along the root-to-leaf path can change the leaf. They introduce a “Stable SGD” variant that temporarily fixes leaf assignments to avoid degenerate inactive leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets show improved test accuracy over axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and a greedy one-node-at-a-time variant (CO2). The method maintains efficient test-time inference O(dp), supports general loss functions (log-loss, squared loss), and includes per-row norm constraints to regularize W.

Relative to prior work: The paper connects to global tree optimization (Bennett, 1990s), max-margin trees (Bennett & Blue; margin enlargement), and soft/probabilistic trees like Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts (Jordan & Jacobs), while retaining hard splits for computational efficiency at inference. The optimization is explicitly related to structured SVMs with latent variables (Yu & Joachims; Tsochantaridis et al.). The CO2 approach is recovered as a depth-1 special case; the contribution here is full-tree joint optimization with an efficient surrogate and inference routine.

Strengths:
- A clear and principled formulation that links decision tree learning with latent structured prediction.
- A usable upper bound and an efficient loss-augmented inference scheme that scales as O(d^2 p), preserving O(dp) test-time complexity.
- Flexibility to different loss functions and a simple regularization knob (per-row norm) with intuitive effects (margin/pruning).
- Empirical gains over several strong greedy oblique baselines on multiple datasets; sensible analysis of runtime and the effect of ν.
- Practical training details (initialization with CO2; momentum/minibatches) and a stabilization trick (SSGD) addressing inactive leaves.

Weaknesses:
- Limited experimental scope: only multiclass classification benchmarks from LibSVM; no regression despite claiming generality; no comparison to widely used modern baselines (e.g., gradient boosting, random forests, or oblique forests as ensembles), which are the de facto standard in practice.
- The “fast” bound (radius-1 Hamming ball) is looser; there is no empirical or theoretical analysis of how this relaxation affects solution quality versus the full bound, nor an ablation varying the radius.
- The objective remains non-convex; there are no convergence guarantees beyond standard SGD heuristics, and performance may depend heavily on initialization (axis-aligned → CO2 → non-greedy), which is not ablated.
- The per-row norm constraints are motivated, but there is no generalization analysis (e.g., margin-based bounds for trees) or sensitivity analysis beyond validation tuning.
- SSGD is described heuristically; criteria for when to update assignments and its impact on final performance are under-specified.
- Some important details are deferred to the supplement (proofs, loss-augmented inference derivations), reducing reproducibility from the main text.

Quality:
- Technically sound overall; the latent-variable reformulation and upper bounds are plausible and correctly connect to structured SVM machinery. The complexity analysis and algorithmic steps are clear. However, theoretical depth is limited (no generalization guarantees; minimal analysis of the surrogate’s tightness beyond Proposition 1; no convergence theory for SSGD/SGD).

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized. The tree navigation function f and the Hamming-ball restriction are explained clearly. It would help to more explicitly describe the mapping between off-path bits and the gradient update, and to summarize proof ideas of the upper bounds in the main paper. More detail on SSGD schedules would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
- The linkage to latent structured prediction and the specific O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference via a Hamming-ball restriction appear novel for hard-split trees. Relation to CO2 is properly acknowledged; the full-tree non-greedy optimization is a substantive extension. Prior NP-hardness and soft-split EM/HME baselines are discussed.

Significance:
- Potentially significant, as it offers a practical, general approach to non-greedy training while preserving test-time efficiency. However, the lack of comparisons to state-of-the-art ensembles (boosting/forests) and limited task diversity make it hard to assess real-world impact. Stronger evidence could elevate significance.

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive are results to initialization? What happens from random W, or from different greedy initializations?
- Can you quantify the gap between the full loss-augmented inference and the radius-1 variant (accuracy and training time), or explore larger radii?
- How often do leaves become inactive with plain SGD, and how exactly is the SSGD assignment update scheduled?
- Any results on regression tasks, or on larger-scale/high-dimensional datasets?
- How does a single non-greedy tree compare to a modest random forest or boosted trees at similar test-time budgets?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add comparisons to random forests and gradient boosting (single model budget matched on depth/inference time).
- Provide ablations on initialization, ν, learning rate schedules, and Hamming-ball radius.
- Include at least one regression dataset and/or structured-output loss to showcase generality.
- Outline a simple generalization argument (e.g., margin-based reasoning per node) or provide empirical calibration curves to relate ν to overfitting.
- Detail SSGD (update frequency/criteria), and report its incremental benefit over SGD.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Principled formulation that advances non-greedy tree learning with efficient training and preserved inference efficiency.
- Empirical improvements over strong greedy oblique baselines.
- Method is general and likely to spur follow-up work.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental evaluation omits the most relevant practical baselines (boosting/forests), limiting evidence of significance.
- No analysis of the loosening introduced by the fast bound; reliance on heuristics (initialization, SSGD) without ablation.
- Limited task diversity; no regression or larger-scale benchmarks; minimal theory on generalization.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is technically interesting and promising, but the empirical evaluation and analysis are not yet strong enough to assess impact relative to widely used tree methods. Addressing the suggested experiments and ablations would likely merit acceptance.

Reviewer confidence: High.
