- Proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees that jointly optimizes all split functions under a global objective.  
- Models internal binary routing decisions as a latent vector h ∈ {−1,+1}^m and derives a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss inspired by latent structured prediction.  
- Uses the derived surrogate objective minimized by SGD with projection and loss-augmented inference over h.  
- Introduces a fast loss-augmented inference restricting g to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing complexity to O(d^2 p) per update and keeping test-time inference at O(d p).  
- Adds per-row norm constraints on W to control bound tightness and prevent trivial scaling, and introduces “Stable SGD” that fixes leaf assignments for several steps to avoid inactive leaves.  
- Experiments on LibSVM multiclass datasets show consistent improvements over axis-aligned, OC1 oblique, random oblique splits, and greedy CO2, with less overfitting at larger depths.  
- Initialization is done via a greedy tree and CO2 warm start.

- Latent-variable formulation is well grounded in latent structured SVM techniques and the upper bound is correct by construction.  
- The fast inference bound (Eq. 13) is looser but carefully justified and makes training practical for deeper trees.  
- SGD with projection is appropriate; gradients and complexity analysis are clear.

- Optimization remains nonconvex with no convergence or generalization guarantees beyond standard SGD behavior.  
- Restricting loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball changes the objective and may bias learning; no ablation isolates this impact.  
- Proposition 1 motivates norm control, but no theoretical generalization bounds for ν are provided.  
- Stable SGD is heuristic and not systematically analyzed.

- Baselines are relevant and results favor the proposed method across depths.  
- Experimental scope is limited to a handful of datasets; lacking high-dimensional settings and comparisons to strong modern baselines like boosted trees.  
- Wall-clock training time vs accuracy and ablations on ν, SSGD, and fast vs exact inference are missing.

- Paper is generally clear and well organized; mapping from h to leaves and Algorithm 1 are helpful.  
- Some critical details are deferred to the supplement; a brief pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference would improve reproducibility.  
- Need to explicitly define relationship between d and m and what is cached per node to justify O(d^2 p).  
- Experimental hyperparameter grids (beyond ν and η), batch sizes, momentum, number of passes, and run variability are not fully specified.

- The connection between non-greedy tree optimization and latent structured prediction is insightful and appears novel.  
- The combination of a latent-SSVM-style bound with fast, tree-structured loss-augmented inference and per-row constraints is a novel contribution.  
- CO2 is correctly positioned as a greedy special case and the joint optimization advantage is clearly articulated.

- Addresses global optimization of decision trees with a principled, scalable approach that maintains efficient inference.  
- Fast inference trick enabling O(d^2 p) per update is practically important.  
- Impact would increase with demonstrations as base learners in ensembles and with theoretical generalization results.

- Pros: principled latent-SSVM formulation; practical and scalable training algorithm; consistent empirical gains; reduced overfitting at larger depths; retains hard splits and efficient test-time inference; loss-agnostic.  
- Cons: nonconvex optimization with heuristic components (SSGD); approximate fast bound alters objective without ablation; limited experimental scope (few datasets, no ensembles, limited runtime comparisons); some implementation details and proofs deferred, affecting reproducibility.

- Request ablation comparing exact vs fast loss-augmented inference on accuracy and runtime.  
- Request quantification of SSGD vs plain SGD on stability, active leaves, and accuracy.  
- Ask for wall-clock training time vs depth for all baselines and variability across random seeds.  
- Suggest experiments/extensions to ensembles (bagging/boosting) and adding pseudocode plus more hyperparameter details.

- Overall: solid, timely, technically sound and novel contribution with practical scalability improvements; leans toward acceptance despite missing ablations, broader experiments, and deeper theory.
