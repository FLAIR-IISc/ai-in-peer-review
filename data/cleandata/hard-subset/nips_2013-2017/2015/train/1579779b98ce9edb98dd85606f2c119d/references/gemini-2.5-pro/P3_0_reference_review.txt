This paper presents a novel framework for non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The central idea is to re-cast the tree learning problem as one of structured prediction with latent variables. Specifically, the path a data point takes through the tree is represented by a latent binary vector, and the overall tree optimization is framed as finding split parameters that, along with leaf parameters, minimize a global empirical loss. Recognizing that this objective is discontinuous and hard to optimize directly, the authors derive a convex-concave upper bound inspired by the structural SVM literature (e.g., Yu & Joachims, 2009). This surrogate objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is a method to reduce the complexity of the gradient calculation from exponential to polynomial in tree depth, `O(d^2p)`, by restricting the loss-augmented inference step to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current optimal path. Experiments on several benchmark datasets show that the proposed non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines, including axis-aligned, oblique (OC1), and a greedy version of their own split optimization (CO2).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The connection between decision tree learning and latent variable structured prediction is elegant and novel. It places the notoriously difficult problem of global tree optimization into a well-understood optimization framework. This provides a principled alternative to purely heuristic or greedy approaches and opens the door to leveraging the rich toolkit of structured prediction for tree-based models.

2.  **Significant Technical Contribution for Efficiency:** The primary obstacle to such a global formulation is computational complexity. The naive loss-augmented inference step is exponential in tree depth. The paper's proposal to approximate this by searching within a Hamming ball of radius 1 is a crucial contribution that makes the method practical for reasonably deep trees. This is a clever and effective trade-off between optimization quality and computational feasibility, which is well-supported by the timing experiments in Figure 4.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing. The proposed method consistently outperforms a strong set of baselines across multiple datasets and tree depths. The comparison with CO2 is particularly insightful, as it isolates the benefit of the non-greedy, global optimization over a greedy application of a similar objective. The results in Figure 2 suggest that the non-greedy approach leads to better generalization and is less prone to overfitting, likely due to the global objective and the explicit norm regularization on the split parameters.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly motivate the problem, walk the reader through the mathematical formulation step-by-step, and justify their design choices. The connection to structured prediction is explained lucidly, making the work accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Impact of the Hamming Ball Approximation:** The main efficiency gain comes at the cost of approximating the loss-augmented inference. This means the gradient is calculated based on only considering single-bit flips from the current path. This might re-introduce a form of local or "pseudo-greedy" behavior into the optimization. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the implications of this approximation. Does it prevent the model from escaping certain local minima that the full inference step might? Could a larger Hamming radius (e.g., radius 2) be feasible and offer better performance?

2.  **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The method is non-convex, and the optimization is initialized using a greedy procedure. This raises the question of how sensitive the final model is to this specific initialization. An ablation study exploring different initializations (e.g., random hyperplanes, or a different greedy algorithm) would provide valuable insight into the robustness of the SGD optimization and how much of the final performance is owed to the global fine-tuning versus the quality of the initial tree.

3.  **Lack of Comparison with Ensembles:** The authors rightly state that comparing single trees is the best way to isolate the effectiveness of the learning algorithm. However, in practice, the state-of-the-art is dominated by ensembles like Random Forests and Gradient Boosted Trees (GBTs). While a full comparison is likely out of scope, a brief discussion or a small experiment could provide crucial context. For instance, how does a single, globally optimized tree of depth `d` compare in accuracy and, importantly, interpretability and inference speed to a small ensemble of greedy trees (e.g., a Random Forest of 5-10 trees) with a similar total number of nodes? A key selling point for this work could be achieving near-ensemble accuracy with a single, more interpretable model.

4.  **Details of "Stable SGD" (SSGD):** The SSGD variant is introduced as a practical improvement to prevent leaves from becoming inactive. However, its description is brief and feels somewhat ad-hoc. It would be beneficial to provide more detail on the criteria for "when the improvement in the bound becomes negligible" and to include an ablation in the experiments that directly compares the performance of standard SGD with SSGD to quantify its impact.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on decision tree learning. The formulation is novel, the technical solution to the complexity problem is clever and practical, and the empirical results are strong. It successfully bridges two important areas of machine learning and provides a new, principled, and effective tool for training decision trees. The weaknesses identified are primarily avenues for further exploration and do not undermine the core contributions of the work. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, original, and addresses an important problem. It is very likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community and to inspire follow-up work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
