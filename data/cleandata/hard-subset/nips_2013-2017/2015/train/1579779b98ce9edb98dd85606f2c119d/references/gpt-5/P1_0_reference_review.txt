Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy, joint optimization framework for training decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). Each internal split is a linear threshold si(x)=sgn(wi^T x), and each leaf j carries logits θj for a softmax over labels. The key reformulation is to view the vector of split outcomes h∈{−1,+1}^m as a latent structure with score h^T W x (equivalently w^T φ(h,x)), linking tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables. This yields an upper bound on the loss that involves a loss-augmented inference over g∈{−1,+1}^m. The naive inference is exponential in depth; the paper introduces a modified bound restricting g to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), which limits loss-augmented inference to O(d^2 p), where d is depth and p feature dimension, making joint training of deep trees feasible. The method regularizes each row of W (‖wi‖2≤ν) to control the scale-induced tightness of the bound and overfitting. Two optimization variants are proposed: SGD and a “Stable SGD” (SSGD) that temporarily fixes example-to-leaf assignments to avoid deactivating leaves too aggressively.

Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare single non-greedy oblique trees against axis-aligned CART, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique splits, and CO2 (a greedy one-node-at-a-time variant of the same upper-bound idea). Results show consistent test-accuracy improvements across depths, better resistance to overfitting, and large speedups of fast loss-augmented inference over the exponential alternative. The regularizer ν controls both bound tightness and implicit pruning.

The work relates to: greedy tree induction (ID3/CART), global tree optimization via MILP (Bennett), SVM-at-nodes and margin-based trees, soft splits and HME (EM-training but expensive at test time), online Mondrian forests, and structural SVMs and latent SVMs (Taskar, Tsochantaridis, Yu & Joachims). The paper’s structured-prediction formulation and the Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference are the main novelties compared to prior oblique-tree methods and to CO2.

Quality
- The derivation of the loss upper bounds and the structured-prediction mapping is sound and standard; the proofs are deferred to the supplement. The observation that scale of W tightens the bound and the choice to regularize per-row norms are well-motivated.
- The fast inference bound (restricting g to a Hamming ball of radius 1) is a clean, computationally driven modification. While the change preserves an upper bound, the paper does not analyze how much looser it becomes relative to the full bound or how this affects optimization bias.
- Optimization is non-convex; the paper provides clear SGD updates, projection steps, and a practical SSGD heuristic. There are no convergence guarantees beyond standard SGD intuition.
- Experiments are reasonable for single-tree comparisons and support the claims (better test accuracy across depths, scalability of fast inference), though quantitative details are mostly in figures; ablations quantifying the impact of ν, the Hamming-ball restriction, and initialization are only partially discussed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. The structured-prediction view, navigation function f, and the two bounds ((7) and (13)) are explained clearly, with an explicit algorithm box for SGD.
- Some details could be expanded for reproducibility: exact hyperparameter grids (η, momentum, batch sizes), stopping criteria, and initialization via CO2. A brief schematic explaining how off-path bits cancel in loss-augmented inference would aid intuition.

Originality
- Framing tree induction as latent structured prediction and optimizing a margin-rescaled upper bound end-to-end across the tree is novel relative to greedy splits and to prior oblique-tree algorithms. The fast loss-augmented inference via a Hamming-ball restriction is a clever and, to my knowledge, new device in this context.
- The approach is clearly distinguished from HME (soft/probabilistic splits), MILP global optimization, and margin-at-node methods. Relation to CO2 (depth-1 greedy application of a related bound) is explicit.

Significance
- Decision trees are widely used; enabling scalable, joint, non-greedy optimization that improves generalization of single trees is impactful. The method retains O(dp) test-time inference and supports arbitrary differentiable losses.
- The demonstrated gains are on standard datasets and single-tree settings; extensions to ensembles (bagging/boosting), regression, or kernels are discussed as future work but not evaluated. A comparison to strong modern tree baselines (e.g., gradient boosting) is deliberately omitted to isolate single-tree behavior but limits practical impact claims.

Pros
- Elegant, principled formulation linking decision trees to latent structured prediction.
- Tractable training via a novel fast loss-augmented inference with O(d^2 p) complexity.
- Regularization that controls both bound tightness and overfitting, with empirical evidence it implicitly prunes.
- Consistent empirical improvements over strong greedy baselines, including OC1 and CO2, and reduced overfitting across depths.
- Clear algorithmic description and complexity analysis.

Cons
- No analysis of the suboptimality introduced by the Hamming-ball restriction; potential optimization bias not quantified.
- No convergence guarantees for SGD/SSGD; heavy reliance on initialization (CO2) not ablated.
- Experimental scope limited: classification only, few datasets, single-tree setting; no regression or large-scale benchmarks; limited runtime numbers beyond a plot.
- Baseline tuning and exact numeric results are not fully specified; reproducibility details are sparse.
- Theoretical generalization benefits of the per-row norm constraint are argued intuitively but not formalized.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you quantify the gap between the full bound (7) and the Hamming-ball bound (13) empirically (e.g., average difference per sample during training) and its effect on final accuracy?
- How sensitive is performance to initialization? Please include an ablation (random W, axis-aligned only, CO2) and report final accuracy and active-leaf counts.
- Provide more details on hyperparameters, batch sizes, momentum, and stopping criteria, and clarify baseline tuning to aid reproducibility.
- Include regression experiments (the framework supports squared loss) and at least a brief comparison to a soft-tree/HME baseline to contextualize tradeoffs.
- Report absolute training times and memory use for varying depths and p, in addition to the speedup plot.

Recommendation
Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution with a principled formulation and a practical algorithm that advances non-greedy decision tree learning. Despite some empirical and theoretical gaps, the paper is well within NIPS scope and likely to influence subsequent work. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying experiments and adding ablations as suggested.
