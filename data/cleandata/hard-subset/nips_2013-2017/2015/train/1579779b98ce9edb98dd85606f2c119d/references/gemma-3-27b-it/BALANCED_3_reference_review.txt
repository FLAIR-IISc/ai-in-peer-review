This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and leveraging convex-concave optimization techniques. The core idea is to introduce latent variables representing split decisions, allowing for joint optimization of split functions across the tree, addressing the limitations of traditional greedy methods. The authors establish a link to structured prediction, present a surrogate objective, and introduce algorithmic optimizations to reduce computational complexity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a novel and interesting perspective. This allows the application of techniques from structured prediction to improve tree learning.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The development of a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss is a significant theoretical contribution. The reduction in complexity from O(2dp) to O(d²p) is crucial for practical application to deeper trees.
*   **Potential for Improvement:** Non-greedy optimization promises better generalization and more compact trees compared to traditional greedy approaches, as evidenced by the experiments. The method offers a pathway to overcoming the inherent limitations of greedy tree induction.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate improved performance compared to several baselines, including CO2, across multiple datasets. The ablation study highlighting the effect of the regularization parameter ν is informative.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors clearly articulate the problem, their proposed solution, and the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Implementation:** The proposed method is significantly more complex to implement compared to standard greedy algorithms. The reliance on stochastic gradient descent and the need for careful tuning of hyperparameters (ν, η) adds to the practical challenges.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** Despite the optimization to O(d²p), the method may still face scalability issues with extremely high-dimensional data (large p) and very deep trees (large d). While the paper acknowledges this, a more detailed analysis of the scalability limitations would be beneficial.
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to baselines is solid, it lacks a comparison to more recent and advanced tree-based methods, such as gradient boosted trees (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM). These methods often achieve state-of-the-art results and would provide a more challenging benchmark.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears to be sensitive to the regularization parameter ν. A more thorough investigation of the robustness of the method to different hyperparameter settings is needed. The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but lacks details on the search strategy.
*   **Limited Discussion of Latent Variable Interpretation:** The paper introduces latent variables but doesn't deeply explore their interpretation or provide insights into what these variables represent in terms of the learned splits. A discussion of the learned latent representations could enhance the understanding of the model.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to decision tree learning.
*   The proposed method demonstrates improvements over existing greedy algorithms in empirical evaluations.
*   The technical contributions, particularly the complexity reduction, are significant.
*   The work addresses a long-standing challenge in the field – overcoming the limitations of greedy tree induction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The method's complexity and potential scalability issues may limit its practical applicability.
*   The lack of comparison to state-of-the-art gradient boosted trees weakens the evaluation.
*   The sensitivity to hyperparameters and the limited discussion of latent variable interpretation are areas for improvement.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel approach to a fundamental problem in machine learning. The theoretical contributions are valuable, and the empirical results are encouraging. However, the complexity of the method and the lack of comparison to stronger baselines raise some concerns.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by:

1.  **Adding a comparison to state-of-the-art gradient boosted trees.**
2.  **Providing a more detailed analysis of scalability limitations and potential mitigation strategies.**
3.  **Conducting a more thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity and robustness.**
4.  **Expanding the discussion of the learned latent variables and their interpretation.**
5.  **Providing more implementation details to aid reproducibility.**

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential for acceptance in a future review cycle.
