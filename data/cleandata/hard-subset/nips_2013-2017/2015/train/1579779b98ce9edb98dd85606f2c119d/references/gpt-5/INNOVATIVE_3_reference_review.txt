Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy framework for training decision trees by jointly optimizing all split parameters under a global surrogate objective inspired by structured prediction with latent variables. The key idea is to view the m split decisions as a latent binary vector h = sgn(Wx), and to optimize an upper bound on the empirical loss that resembles loss-augmented inference in structural SVMs. The authors derive a convex-concave, piecewise linear upper bound and optimize it via SGD with per-row norm constraints on W. A central technical contribution is reducing the naively exponential loss-augmented inference to O(d^2 p) per step by tightening the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current decisions, which yields a fast, still-valid upper bound. They also introduce a Stable SGD variant to reduce leaf inactivity. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets show that single non-greedy trees outperform several greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) and a greedy depth-1 variant (CO2), with better generalization and runtime scalability as depth grows.

Relation to prior work
- Global decision tree optimization is NP-complete; most practice remains greedy (ID3/CART). Prior non-greedy methods include MILP-style global optimization (limited scale), SVM-at-node margin methods, and soft/probabilistic trees (HME) that sacrifice decision-tree inference cost.
- The paper’s formulation connects tree training to latent structured prediction (Tsochantaridis; Yu & Joachims) via sgn(Wx) = argmax_h h^T Wx, and uses loss-augmented inference. This link is, to my knowledge, novel in the context of hard-split trees with preserved O(dp) inference.
- CO2 can be seen as a special case applied greedily to depth-1 subproblems; this work moves to joint, non-greedy optimization with an efficient surrogate and SGD.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The latent-structured prediction perspective on hard decision trees is elegant and potentially influential. It provides a principled global objective and tools (loss-augmented inference, per-row norm constraints) that align with well-understood frameworks.
- Technical contribution: The fast loss-augmented inference using a radius-1 Hamming ball is a clever modification that preserves an upper bound while cutting the per-step cost to O(d^2 p), making deep trees trainable.
- Practicality: The method retains O(dp) inference at test time, unlike soft trees, and introduces SSGD to stabilize training.
- Empirical indications: Across multiple datasets and depths, the non-greedy trees outperform strong greedy baselines and appear less prone to overfitting. The runtime scaling plots convincingly show the advantage of the fast bound.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope versus modern practice: Evaluation focuses on single trees and excludes widely used strong baselines such as gradient boosted trees (e.g., Friedman’s boosting/XGBoost) and modern oblique/differentiable trees. While the paper argues the single-tree setting clarifies differences, practitioners will ask whether the proposed training also yields forests that outperform off-the-shelf ensembles.
- Bound quality vs. speed trade-off: The paper shows runtime benefits for the radius-1 Hamming bound, but does not report the effect on accuracy vs. the full bound across depths/datasets. A short study (e.g., accuracy vs. radius r) would strengthen the case.
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond being an upper bound and piecewise convex-concave, there are no convergence or generalization guarantees tailored to the surrogate (e.g., margin-based bounds under the per-row constraints). Some discussion or a simple bound would be helpful.
- Sensitivity to initialization and ν: Training starts from a greedy CO2-initialized tree, and ν strongly influences pruning. The method’s dependence on initialization and its robustness to ν (and η) could be better quantified. What happens from random W? How often do leaves go inactive, and how much does SSGD help?
- Comparisons to soft/probabilistic splits: While the paper cites HME, there is no empirical comparison. Given soft trees’ smooth objectives, a brief comparison (even on one dataset) would contextualize the trade-off between training efficiency/test-time cost and accuracy.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The surrogate is well motivated, the fast inference trick is correct (and still an upper bound), and the optimization is implementable with standard SGD. Some proofs are deferred; that’s fine. The empirical section supports the main claims but could be broadened (see above).

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The bound derivations and the loss-augmented inference procedure are explained at a useful level of detail, and Algorithm 1 helps. Suggestions:
- Briefly quantify the number of candidate leaves considered under the radius-1 bound as a function of depth and branching to make O(d^2 p) intuitive.
- Add a short schematic on which bits differ between ĝ and ĥ, clarifying why only path bits matter.

Originality
High. The latent-structured prediction formulation for hard-split trees with efficient loss-augmented inference and a computable fast bound appears novel and opens a path to non-greedy tree learning without giving up test-time efficiency.

Significance
Potentially significant. If extended to forests and larger datasets, this could change how oblique trees are trained and spur follow-on work on regularization, kernels, and sparsity in W. The Hamming-ball trick is a neat idea with likely reuse in other latent combinatorial models.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Report accuracy differences between the full bound and the fast radius-1 bound; if possible, include results for radius r > 1 to show the trade-off.
- Include at least one comparison to a strong boosted tree baseline; or train a small forest of your non-greedy trees to demonstrate practical impact.
- Provide an ablation on SSGD vs. SGD (leaf utilization, accuracy).
- Discuss sensitivity to initialization; can the method recover from poor initial trees?
- Consider adding sparsity-inducing penalties on W rows to validate the “higher-order/sparse split” claim empirically.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled, and bold reformulation of tree training as latent structured prediction.
- Effective and efficient training via a new fast upper bound; preserves test-time speed.
- Empirical gains across depths; clearer path for future work (kernels, sparsity).

Cons for acceptance
- Missing comparisons to modern strong baselines and ensembles.
- Limited analysis of the bound–accuracy trade-off and sensitivity to hyperparameters/initialization.
- No theoretical generalization/convergence results beyond the surrogate properties.

Recommendation
Weak Accept. The conceptual novelty and efficient algorithmic contribution outweigh the empirical and comparative limitations. With minor extensions/clarifications, this could be a strong contribution.

Confidence
High.
