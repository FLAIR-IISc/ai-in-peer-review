## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing problem of non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The authors propose a novel framework that leverages concepts from structured prediction with latent variables to jointly optimize split functions across the tree. They formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and employ stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for optimization, addressing the computational complexity issue with a reduction from O(2dp) to O(d²p). The work is well-motivated, pointing out the limitations of traditional greedy approaches and the lack of methods that can effectively optimize trees globally.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is a novel and insightful idea.  It provides a principled framework for global optimization.
* **Technical Contribution:**  Reducing the computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d²p) is a significant technical achievement, making the method applicable to deeper trees, a common requirement in practice.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a clear theoretical grounding for the proposed approach, including the derivation of the upper bound and its relationship to regularization. Proposition 1 provides nice intuition on the role of regularization.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on benchmark datasets consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over greedy baselines, suggesting effective generalization and reduced overfitting.  The ablation study regarding ν (regularization parameter) is helpful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The introduction clearly outlines the problem and the authors’ contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the authors address the computational complexity, the proposed method appears considerably more complex to implement compared to standard greedy algorithms. This could hinder its adoption by practitioners.  The description of the SGD algorithm (Alg 1) is a little dense.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison is primarily with greedy methods and one older, less scalable non-greedy method (CO2). A more thorough comparison to more recent, competitive methods for tree-based models (e.g., extremely randomized trees, gradient boosted trees) would strengthen the claims. While these are ensembles, the focus is on single tree performance here.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the regularization parameter ν. More details on how this parameter was tuned and its impact on different datasets would be valuable.
* **Scalability Discussion:** While the authors report runtime comparison in Figure 4, a more detailed discussion about the scalability of the method to very large datasets (e.g., millions of samples) would be useful.  The Connect4 dataset seems relatively small, and the scalability benefits may become more pronounced with larger datasets.
* **Missing Proofs:** References to "supplementary material" for proofs (Proposition 1) are not ideal. Including essential proofs within the main text increases clarity and accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed non-greedy optimization framework, grounded in structured prediction, offers a promising alternative to traditional greedy methods. The reduction in computational complexity is a key achievement, enabling the application of this method to deeper trees. While the implementation complexity and limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods are drawbacks, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and promising empirical results outweigh these concerns.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Strong. The paper is technically sound, and the claims are supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results.  It's a complete piece of work addressing a well-defined problem.
* **Clarity:** Good. The paper is generally well-written, although some sections could benefit from further simplification.
* **Originality:** High.  The formulation of the problem as structured prediction with latent variables is a novel contribution.
* **Significance:**  Good. The results are important and address a longstanding challenge in decision tree learning.  The method has the potential to improve the accuracy and generalization performance of decision trees, which could be valuable in various applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to non-greedy decision tree optimization.
* Significant reduction in computational complexity.
* Demonstrably superior performance compared to greedy baselines.
* Addresses a challenging and important problem.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Implementation complexity might limit adoption.
* Limited comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity requires careful tuning.




**Recommendation:**

**Accept.**  I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses related to the comparison with state-of-the-art methods, provide more details on hyperparameter tuning, and ideally include the proofs in the main text.  This is a solid contribution that deserves to be presented at NIPS.
