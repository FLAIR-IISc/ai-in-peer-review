

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy framework for learning decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. It develops a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent, claiming to reduce computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) for deep trees. The authors demonstrate improved generalization performance over greedy baselines on standard classification datasets.

## Strengths
The paper establishes an interesting connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, which provides a theoretically sound framework for non-greedy optimization. The upper bound derivation appears correct, and the experimental results do show that the proposed method achieves better generalization performance than greedy alternatives across multiple datasets. The complexity reduction claim is potentially significant for deep tree learning.

## Weaknesses

1. **Notational inconsistencies and ambiguities**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For example, it refers to "Hm" as both the set of m-bit vectors and the set {−1,+1}^m without clarification. The relationship between m (number of internal nodes) and d (tree depth) is unclear, with the paper stating "for a binary tree the number of leaves is always one more than the number of internal (non-leaf) nodes" without explaining how m relates to d (for a perfect binary tree, m = 2^d - 1). This is confusing for readers trying to understand the complexity claims.

2. **Mischaracterization of latent variable growth**: The paper states "the number of latent decision variables grows exponentially with the tree depth d," but this is incorrect. For a tree with m internal nodes (where m = 2^d - 1 for a perfect binary tree), the number of latent decision variables is 2^m = 2^(2^d - 1), which is double exponential in d, not exponential. This fundamental error undermines the complexity claims.

3. **Incomplete experimental validation**: The paper lacks statistical significance tests for the performance improvements over baselines. The claim that "non-greedy trees achieve the best test performance" is not supported by proper statistical analysis. Additionally, the paper compares with CO2 [18] but doesn't explain why CO2 is considered a baseline rather than a special case of their method.

4. **Unjustified complexity claims**: The paper claims the complexity is reduced to O(d^2p) but provides insufficient justification for this claim. The statement "we need to evaluate (12) for at most d+1 values of j, requiring a running time of O(d^2p)" is not adequately explained. Why is the complexity d^2p rather than dp? The paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of the constant factors or how the d^2 term arises.

5. **Incomplete references and formatting**: The reference for Bennett [1] is incomplete (missing page numbers and publication details). The citation [3] is missing the publication year. These minor formatting issues are inconsistent with the high standards expected at NIPS.

6. **Ambiguous parameter selection**: The paper states they "tune the choice of the SGD learning rate, η" and "pick the value of ν that produces the tree with minimum validation error," but provides no details on how this tuning was performed or how robust the results are to these hyperparameters. This lack of detail makes it difficult to reproduce the experiments.

7. **Incomplete treatment of general loss functions**: The paper claims "our formulation can handle any general loss function" but only provides examples for log loss and squared loss. It doesn't explain how the method would handle other loss functions, such as hinge loss for margin maximization, which would be relevant for comparison with SVM-based approaches.

8. **Inaccurate technical claims**: The paper states "the intrinsic limitation of this procedure is that the optimization of si is solely conditioned on Di" but this is not entirely accurate—greedy algorithms can be modified to look ahead (e.g., with lookahead methods), though these are computationally expensive. This overstates the limitations of greedy approaches.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction, the numerous technical inconsistencies, notational ambiguities, and unjustified complexity claims significantly undermine its credibility. The minor flaws in the paper's technical details are substantial enough to warrant rejection, especially given NIPS's high standards for precision and rigor.

The paper's central claim of reducing complexity to O(d^2p) is undermined by the double exponential growth of latent variables, and the experimental validation lacks statistical significance testing. The combination of these issues, along with the inconsistent notation and incomplete references, makes it difficult to trust the paper's claims.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial technical inconsistencies that overshadow the paper's potential contributions. The authors would need to thoroughly revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
