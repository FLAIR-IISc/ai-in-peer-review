Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for binary decision trees with oblique splits. The key idea is to view the vector of split decisions as a latent variable and upper-bound the empirical loss with a convex-concave surrogate inspired by structured prediction with latent variables. The authors optimize this bound via SGD with per-row norm constraints on W, and introduce a “fast” loss-augmented inference by restricting to a Hamming-1 neighborhood of the current routing. They further propose a Stable SGD variant that fixes leaf assignments temporarily to mitigate inactive leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare single trees trained by the proposed method against axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique baselines, and CO2 (a greedy depth-1 variant), showing improved test accuracy and reduced overfitting across tree depths. They also provide a runtime comparison between exact and fast loss-augmented inference.

Relation to prior work
The formulation directly builds on structured SVMs with latent variables and margin rescaling, and connects naturally to the long line of decision tree induction methods (ID3/CART), oblique trees (OC1), and max-margin decision trees (e.g., Bennett & Blue). The CO2 approach is a close baseline; the contribution here is to optimize split parameters jointly under a global surrogate instead of greedily node-by-node. The Hamming-1 restriction resembles a one-bit flip search and trades exactness for computational tractability. The paper positions itself as maintaining the test-time efficiency of hard trees while enabling global training, as opposed to soft trees like HME which sacrifice inference efficiency.

Strengths
- Clear and principled connection between decision tree training and latent structured prediction; the bound is sensible and easy to implement with SGD.
- The fast loss-augmented inference reducing complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) is a practical contribution and appears correct (the upper bound remains valid since ĥ ∈ B1).
- The per-row norm constraint and analysis of the effect of scaling W are reasonable and align with max-margin intuitions.
- Empirical results indicate consistent improvements over several greedy baselines for single trees, with reduced overfitting as tree depth increases.
- The method supports general losses and preserves O(dp) test-time inference with hard splits.

Weaknesses
- Experimental validation is limited and omits the most relevant strong baselines used in practice: random forests, extremely randomized trees, and gradient boosted trees (e.g., Friedman 2001). Since single trees are rarely used in isolation, demonstrating improvements for ensembles (e.g., as base learners in bagging/boosting) is crucial for significance.
- Comparison to existing oblique/max-margin approaches is incomplete. For example, modern oblique-node formulations with SVMs or logistic splits with pruning are not comprehensively benchmarked. It is unclear if the improvements persist under stronger, tuned oblique baselines.
- The gains vs. CO2 (the closest method) are not quantified rigorously (no confidence intervals or statistical tests), and appear modest on some datasets. Initialization with CO2 may advantage the proposed method; fairness of hyperparameter tuning across methods is not fully specified.
- Ablations are missing: effect of the Hamming-1 restriction vs. the exact bound, effect of SSGD vs. vanilla SGD, role of initialization, and sensitivity to ν and learning schedules beyond a handful of values. The observed leaf inactivation issue is handled heuristically; it would be useful to quantify its prevalence and the impact of SSGD.
- Scalability claims are only partially supported. The runtime figure is shown for a single dataset (Connect4); there is no end-to-end training time comparison against baselines, nor large-scale experiments commensurate with stated applications (e.g., Kinect-scale).
- Theoretical support remains limited to an upper bound and a simple scaling proposition; there are no generalization bounds or convergence guarantees beyond standard SGD heuristics, and no discussion of the tightness of the Hamming-1 bound in practice.
- Reproducibility details are sparse: preprocessing, feature normalization, stopping criteria, momentum and mini-batch sizes, and code availability are not specified. Some references are incomplete, and several citations lack publication details.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; the core inequalities are plausible, and the algorithm is coherent. However, important proofs are relegated to the supplement, the SSGD heuristic lacks analysis, and the empirical section falls short of the rigor expected for a global training claim (missing strong baselines, statistical rigor, and ablations).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Algorithm 1 helps. However, several details (optimization hyperparameters, SSGD schedule, initialization sensitivity) should be specified. The references need careful cleanup.

Originality
The joint non-greedy optimization under a latent-structured surrogate for decision trees is a natural extension of existing structured SVM ideas. The main novelty is applying this globally to trees and introducing the Hamming-1 fast inference trick. Relative to CO2, the conceptual step is incremental.

Significance
Potentially useful if it demonstrably improves widely used tree ensembles or yields substantial single-tree gains. As is, the scope is narrow (single trees on a few datasets), making it difficult to assess broader impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments with random forests and gradient boosted trees using your method as base learners; report accuracy and training time vs. standard greedy splits.
- Include strong oblique baselines (e.g., SVM/logistic nodes with pruning) and ensure identical hyperparameter budgets.
- Provide ablations: exact vs. Hamming-1, SGD vs. SSGD, effect of initialization, ν sensitivity, and depth sensitivity. Report statistical significance.
- Report end-to-end training time and memory usage across datasets.
- Clarify implementation details (normalization, batching, momentum, early stopping) and release code if possible.
- Improve references and fix citation formatting.

Reasons to accept
- Principled formulation; simple algorithm; preserves tree inference efficiency.
- Consistent empirical improvements over several greedy baselines for single trees.
- Practical fast inference trick with clear complexity benefits.

Reasons to reject
- Insufficient experimental validation against strong, established baselines and in ensemble settings.
- Incremental advance over prior CO2/latent SVM ideas; limited novelty.
- Lack of ablations and statistical rigor; unclear practical significance at scale.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Confidence: High
