Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for hard (oblique) decision trees by casting tree induction as structured prediction with latent variables. The key idea is to view the vector of split decisions h = sgn(Wx) across all internal nodes as a latent binary vector, and to optimize a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss L(W, Θ) that resembles loss-augmented inference in structural SVMs with latent variables (Tsochantaridis et al.; Yu & Joachims). The bound depends on a score h^T Wx and a choice of leaf distribution via a navigation function f(h). Because naïve loss-augmented inference scales as O(2^d p), the authors introduce a tractable variant that restricts the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), yielding O(d^2 p) complexity per SGD step. They regularize via per-row ℓ2 constraints on W to control the scale sensitivity of the bound, and propose a “Stable SGD” variant that temporarily freezes leaf assignments to avoid collapse of active leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM datasets compare against axis-aligned CART, OC1, random oblique splits, and CO2 (a greedy depth-1 variant of their bound), showing improved test accuracy across tree depths and better resistance to overfitting. The approach maintains O(d p) inference at test time.

This connects several strands of prior work: (i) global (non-greedy) tree optimization (Bennett et al.), (ii) margin-based trees (Bennett & Blue), (iii) soft-split probabilistic trees (HME; later neural decision trees), and (iv) structural SVMs with latent variables. The CO2 baseline (Norouzi et al.) is a special-case greedy application of the same upper bound to individual nodes; this work performs joint optimization across all splits under a single global surrogate.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: A neat, principled reformulation of hard tree learning as latent-structured prediction, with a clear link to loss-augmented inference and margin-rescaling.
- Efficient optimization: The Hamming-ball restriction is a clever device to keep loss-augmented inference tractable while still providing an honest upper bound, reducing updates to O(d^2 p).
- Practical algorithm: SGD with projection onto per-row ℓ2 balls is simple and implementable; SSGD is a pragmatic fix to stabilize leaf utilization.
- Preserves the test-time advantage of trees: Hard splits and O(d p) inference are retained, unlike soft-split models (HME) that require evaluating many experts.
- Empirical improvements: Consistent gains over strong greedy baselines (including OC1 and CO2) across several datasets and a range of depths, with reduced overfitting.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Fixed topology: The method jointly optimizes split parameters W and leaf parameters Θ for a fixed tree structure (initially obtained greedily). The paper sometimes reads as if it is “non-greedy tree learning” generally, but it does not learn the topology. Please clarify the scope (parameter optimization vs. topology optimization) and discuss whether/how structure could be updated.
- Theory limited to an upper bound: Apart from the scale monotonicity proposition, there are no generalization guarantees (e.g., margin-based or Rademacher-style bounds for the constrained W), nor convergence guarantees for the non-convex optimization. Some analysis of how ν trades off tightness vs. optimization difficulty would strengthen the contribution.
- Complexity accounting: Algorithm 1 uses ĥ = sgn(Wx). Computing the full m-bit vector appears to require O(m p), which is exponential in d. The text later argues only path bits matter. Please make the implementation explicit: how do you compute ĥ and the needed components in O(d p) without evaluating all m inner products? A precise description of the data structures and reuse of path computations would improve clarity.
- Experimental scope: Results are on standard tabular benchmarks with single trees. While the focus on single-tree accuracy is justified, it would be valuable to include:
  - Variants without the Hamming-ball speedup (to quantify accuracy gap vs. efficiency gain).
  - Ablations for SSGD vs. vanilla SGD, and sensitivity to ν and η (beyond one figure).
  - Wall-clock comparisons against OC1 and CO2 on larger datasets.
  - An assessment of leaf inactivity dynamics and whether SSGD recovers leaves that would otherwise go unused.
- Baselines and related work: The positioning vs. margin-based oblique trees is good, but it would help to clarify differences from other global or near-global oblique optimization methods and from later soft/hard hybrid approaches (even if soft models are less efficient). Also, please ensure the CO2 comparison uses identical initialization/tuning, as the method depends strongly on initialization quality.
- Objective mismatch and calibration: Leaves use log-loss; the surrogate bound is not a calibrated upper bound on misclassification. Some discussion on whether the method improves calibration or margins at leaves would be useful.

Quality
- Technically sound overall: The latent-variable reformulation and the two bounds ((7) and the Hamming-ball variant (13)) are correct; the Hamming ball still contains ĥ(x), preserving an upper bound. The projection-based regularization is standard and appropriate.
- Some theoretical aspects (generalization, convergence) are underdeveloped. The empirical section is solid but could be broader and include more ablations.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The navigation function f and loss-augmented inference are clearly presented.
- Please clarify the complexity claims and the practical computation of sgn(Wx) and ĝ on a path without touching all nodes. Also, explicitly state that topology is fixed and initialized by a greedy tree.

Originality
- The combination of latent-structured prediction with hard decision trees under a global surrogate, plus the Hamming-ball speedup, is novel and interesting. The relation to CO2 (depth-1 greedy) is well positioned.

Significance
- The work addresses a long-standing limitation of greedy tree induction and offers a practical, scalable alternative that improves accuracy of single trees while preserving fast inference. It is likely to influence both research on oblique trees and practice in domains where tree interpretability and inference speed matter.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to the initialization tree? Can random initial topology plus your optimization recover comparable accuracy?
2) What is the empirical accuracy gap between using the full loss-augmented inference (exponential) and the Hamming-ball approximation on small trees where both are feasible?
3) How often does SSGD change leaf assignments, and what criterion do you use to trigger reassignments?
4) Can you provide confidence intervals over multiple random splits to quantify variance?

Pros
- Principled formulation; efficient optimization; preserves test-time efficiency; consistent empirical wins; practical algorithm.

Cons
- Fixed topology; limited theory; some ambiguity in complexity claims; relatively narrow empirical scope and ablations.

Overall assessment
A strong, well-motivated paper that advances non-greedy training of hard decision trees with a principled surrogate and efficient optimization. With clarifications on complexity, broader ablations, and explicit acknowledgment of the fixed topology, I lean toward acceptance.
