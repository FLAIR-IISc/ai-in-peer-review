Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss. The key steps are: (i) re-express tree splits via h = sgn(Wx) = argmaxh∈{±1}m h^T Wx to connect to latent structured prediction; (ii) derive an example-wise upper bound on the loss that enables stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with per-row norm constraints on W; (iii) address the exponential cost of loss-augmented inference by restricting the maximization to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing per-update complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) (plus label-cost); and (iv) introduce a “Stable SGD” variant that freezes leaf assignments for several updates to avoid leaf collapse. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare single trees against axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, and a greedy special case (CO2), reporting improved test accuracy and reduced overfitting across depths.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual link between non-greedy tree optimization and latent structured prediction; the bound in Eq. (7) is standard and sound.
- Practical computational contribution: restricting loss-augmented inference to B1(sgn(Wx)) yields a scalable O(d^2 p) update with competitive empirical performance.
- The per-row norm projection and use of SGD are reasonable, simple to implement, and maintain O(dp) inference at test time.
- The empirical comparisons focus on single-tree accuracy versus depth, which isolates learning quality rather than ensembling.
- The method supports generic losses (e.g., log loss), not only 0–1 loss.

Weaknesses and issues
Technical/methodological
- Complexity accounting: in the “fast” loss-augmented inference, evaluating at most d+1 candidate leaves requires computing ` for each, so the dependence should be O(d^2 p + d·k) for log loss (not O(d^2 p + k) as claimed).
- Norm constraint inconsistency: the constraint is written as ‖wi‖2 ≤ ν, which typically denotes the L2 norm (not the squared norm). However, the projection in Alg. 1 uses scaling by √ν/‖·‖2, implying a ball of radius √ν (i.e., a squared-norm constraint ‖wi‖2^2 ≤ ν). This should be clarified and made consistent.
- Margin/scale discussion: Proposition 1 correctly shows the bound tightens with scaling W, but the text claims “a small ν forces a large margin at each internal node,” which is at best imprecise in this setting (there is no explicit per-node margin objective; the role of ν is to prevent trivial bound tightening by scaling W, not to increase margin per se). Please clarify the intended margin interpretation.
- Tie handling at sgn(·) = 0 and subgradient issues near Wx ≈ 0 are not specified; the behavior of ĥ and ĝ under ties (and its effect on stability) should be defined.
- SSGD is underspecified: criteria for when to update assignments a(x), how many SGD steps between updates, and whether this procedure optimizes a well-defined surrogate (Eq. 14) with monotonic improvements are not clear. Provide a schedule and stopping conditions.
- Θ is unregularized. With log loss per leaf and potentially few examples per leaf, overfitting is likely; smoothing (e.g., L2 or Dirichlet) or explicit priors on θj should be discussed. Current text suggests only W is regularized.
- Claims about “more compact trees” and cooperation among splits are not substantiated empirically. All comparisons fix depth; no results on actual pruning/leaf counts under a common performance target are shown.
- Regression is mentioned, but all experiments are multiclass classification; no evidence the approach works for regression is provided.

Empirical
- Comparisons: OC1 is quite dated; more recent oblique/soft-tree baselines (e.g., soft decision trees, probabilistic splits) are not compared. The CO2 comparison is helpful, but the non-greedy method is initialized by axis-aligned + CO2, which might favor it; please ensure fair initialization or report sensitivity to initialization (e.g., random or axis-aligned only).
- Reporting: numerical results (means, std, seeds) are not provided in text; figures alone are insufficient to assess effect sizes or statistical significance. Provide tables with test accuracies, standard deviations, and train/test times.
- Ablations: effects of ν, initialization, fast vs full loss-augmented inference, and SSGD vs SGD are only partially explored. For SSGD, show its impact on inactive leaves and accuracy.
- Reproducibility: key details are missing (batch size, momentum values, epochs/steps, learning rate schedules, data preprocessing, tie-breaking, stopping criteria), preventing a faithful reproduction.

Clarity/notation/presentation
- Several notational definitions are incomplete or inconsistent: Im+1 is not defined; ‖·‖H (Hamming norm) is used but not defined; f(·) is only described informally; “⊆ O(2^d p)” is odd phrasing (use O(mp) with m = 2^d − 1).
- Algorithm 1 line numbering/formatting has typos (e.g., “: for i = 1 to m do”; vector norm superscripts).
- References are highly inconsistent and often incomplete (missing years/venues/pages; “John Hopkins” instead of “Johns Hopkins”; “et al” used as an author; extraneous words in titles; many entries lack bibliographic details). This needs a thorough cleanup to meet NeurIPS standards.

Originality and significance
- The latent structured formulation and the bound are conceptually aligned with prior structured SVM/latent SVM work. The novel part is the specific instantiation for trees and the B1 restriction for tractability. This is a reasonable, incremental advance with practical implications. However, the experimental scope and rigor are currently insufficient to demonstrate clear state-of-the-art improvements or robustness beyond the selected datasets and single-tree setting.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and principled formulation connecting trees to latent structured prediction.
- Practical algorithmic idea (Hamming ball restriction) that reduces complexity and seems effective.
- Broad applicability to different losses, maintaining efficient inference.

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological and clarity issues (norm constraint, complexity count, SSGD specification, tie-handling).
- Limited and partially unfair experimental evaluation; lack of ablations and statistical rigor.
- Unregularized Θ and missing implementation details hinder reproducibility and may affect robustness.
- Numerous formatting and citation problems that fall below conference standards.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper presents a promising and principled approach with an interesting computational trick, but the current version has too many methodological ambiguities, insufficient experimental rigor, and significant presentation/citation issues. I encourage the authors to address the technical clarifications, expand and tighten the empirical evaluation (with full details and ablations), regularize Θ, and thoroughly fix the writing and references.
