- Proposes a non-greedy framework that jointly optimizes split functions across the whole decision tree under a global objective.  
- Connects decision tree learning to structured prediction with binary latent variables, framing tree paths as latent assignments.  
- Derives a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss and optimizes it with stochastic gradient descent.  
- Key technical contribution: reduces gradient-update complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) for loss-augmented inference.  
- Introduces Stable SGD (SSGD) to prevent inactive leaves during training.  
- Builds on prior non-greedy methods (e.g., Bennett’s MLP and CO2), with CO2 as a greedy special case of their bound.  
- Technical quality and novelty rated high; proofs (inequality (7), Proposition 1) and complexity analysis are sound.  
- Experimental validation strong: consistent generalization improvements on SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST versus greedy and CO2 baselines.  
- Regularization via norm constraint on W is theoretically grounded and shown to control pruning/generalization.  
- Theoretical foundation is solid: principled handling of discrete tree structure via structured prediction viewpoint.  
- Clarity issues: tree navigation f(h), Hamming ball B_1(sgn(Wx)), and latent variable notation need clearer examples/diagrams.  
- Insufficient comparison to some recent work (e.g., [23], differentiable decision trees [24],[25])—relationship to those methods should be clarified.  
- Complexity claim caveat: O(d^2 p) may omit constant factors and depth-first search costs for very deep trees; more concrete analysis requested.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, practically efficient, with strong empirical support—clear contribution to decision tree learning.  
- Recommendation: accept.  
- Suggested improvements: add diagram for f(h)/Hamming ball, clarify relation to differentiable trees, analyze constant factors in complexity, discuss extensions to missing/categorical data.  
- Pro: novel structured-prediction connection, large complexity improvement, strong empirical gains, justified regularization.  
- Con: some concepts need better illustration, incomplete distinction from recent differentiable-tree work, complexity analysis could be more precise.
