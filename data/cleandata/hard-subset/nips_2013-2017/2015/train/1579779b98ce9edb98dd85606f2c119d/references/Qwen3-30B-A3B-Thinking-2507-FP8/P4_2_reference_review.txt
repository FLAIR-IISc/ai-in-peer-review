

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy approach for training decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) through a modified surrogate objective, making non-greedy learning feasible for deep trees. The paper builds upon prior work on non-greedy tree optimization (e.g., Bennett [1]) but addresses the computational limitations that previously restricted such approaches to shallow trees.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical foundations. The authors rigorously derive an upper bound on the empirical loss (equation 7) and prove its validity, along with Proposition 1 demonstrating how the bound tightens with increasing W norm. The complexity analysis is thorough, showing how the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm reduces the computational bottleneck from exponential to polynomial in tree depth. The experimental evaluation on multiple benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) demonstrates consistent improvements over greedy baselines (including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and random oblique trees) in terms of generalization performance. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, particularly regarding the choice of regularization parameter ν, which implicitly controls the degree of pruning during training. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproduction, with clear algorithmic descriptions and implementation details.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The problem formulation is logically developed, and the connection to structured prediction is effectively motivated. The mathematical derivations are presented in a readable manner, with the supplementary material providing additional proofs. The experimental setup is clearly described, with appropriate baselines and hyperparameter tuning procedures. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 1 and the fast inference variant) are detailed and well-organized. One minor weakness is the explanation of why the fast loss-augmented inference achieves O(d^2p) complexity; while the paper states this, a slightly more detailed justification would improve clarity.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant original contribution by providing an efficient solution to the long-standing problem of non-greedy decision tree learning. Previous non-greedy approaches (Bennett [1]) were limited to shallow trees due to computational complexity, while this work overcomes this limitation through a novel reformulation and complexity reduction. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is particularly novel and provides a principled framework for the problem. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from prior work, including the Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13] (which uses soft splits) and the CO2 method [18] (which is a special case of their approach applied greedily). The technical innovation in reducing the complexity from exponential to polynomial in tree depth represents a meaningful advancement over previous non-greedy methods.

## Significance
The significance of this work lies in its practical contribution to making non-greedy decision tree learning feasible for deep trees, addressing a fundamental limitation of conventional decision tree algorithms. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets, with the non-greedy trees showing less susceptibility to overfitting than greedy baselines. The approach has the potential to improve decision tree performance in applications where tree depth is important, such as the Microsoft Kinect example cited in the introduction. The paper also identifies promising future directions, including learning sparse split functions and applying the kernel trick, which could further extend the applicability of the approach.

## Strengths
- Novel reformulation of decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables
- Significant computational improvement (O(d^2p) vs O(2^dp)) enabling non-greedy learning for deep trees
- Comprehensive experimental validation showing consistent improvements over greedy baselines
- Clear theoretical foundation with rigorous proofs and complexity analysis
- Practical implementation details that enable reproduction

## Weaknesses
- The explanation of the fast loss-augmented inference complexity (O(d^2p)) could be slightly more detailed to fully justify the claim
- Limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity beyond the main regularization parameter ν
- Comparison with more recent non-greedy methods (e.g., those using deep learning for decision trees) could be expanded

## Recommendation
This paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The authors successfully address a fundamental limitation of conventional decision tree algorithms (greedy induction) by providing an efficient non-greedy optimization approach. The theoretical foundation is solid, the experimental results are compelling, and the approach has clear practical implications. While the explanation of the complexity reduction could be slightly more detailed, this does not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is of high quality with rigorous theoretical and empirical validation, it is clearly written and well-organized, it makes a novel contribution that advances the state of the art, and it has significant practical implications for a widely used machine learning method. The work represents a meaningful advancement over previous non-greedy approaches and provides a framework for future extensions.

I recommend acceptance.
