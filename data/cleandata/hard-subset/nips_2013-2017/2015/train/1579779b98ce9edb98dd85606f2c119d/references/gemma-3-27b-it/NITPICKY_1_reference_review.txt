This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and developing a surrogate objective optimized via stochastic gradient descent. The core idea of jointly optimizing split functions across tree levels to improve generalization is interesting, and the reduction in complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a valuable technical contribution. However, despite the novelty and potential significance, I find numerous flaws – ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to substantial methodological concerns – that lead me to recommend rejection.

My primary concern lies with the presentation and justification of the proposed method. The introduction promises a “general framework” but quickly focuses on binary classification, limiting the perceived applicability. While acknowledging extension to N-ary trees, the subsequent formulation and experiments are exclusively binary. This narrowing isn’t inherently problematic, but the introduction's framing is misleading. 

The connection to structured prediction, while present, feels somewhat forced. The latent variable formulation (h) is a natural consequence of the oblique splits, but the benefits of framing it as such aren’t convincingly demonstrated beyond providing a pathway to utilize existing structured prediction techniques. The assertion that this connection is a “key contribution” is a stretch.

Several methodological details require clarification. The regularization parameter ν is introduced without sufficient motivation beyond Proposition 1, which, while mathematically correct, doesn’t intuitively explain *why* regulating the norm of W improves generalization. The authors claim this implicitly controls pruning, but this is not shown, merely asserted. Furthermore, the choice of initializing with a greedy tree (CO2) and subsequent tuning of ν feels ad-hoc and lacks theoretical justification. Why CO2 specifically? How sensitive are the results to different initialization schemes? These questions remain unanswered.

The experimental section suffers from a lack of rigor. The hyperparameter tuning process is vaguely described ("tuned for each depth independently"). What metric was used for validation? What range of values were considered for η and ν? A more detailed explanation is necessary for reproducibility. The comparison to baselines, while present, isn't comprehensive enough. Several state-of-the-art oblique decision tree algorithms ([26, 27]) are missing. Furthermore, the claim that non-greedy trees are “less susceptible to overfitting” isn’t fully supported. The provided accuracy plots, while generally favorable, don't show statistical significance tests. Are the observed differences statistically meaningful or merely due to random fluctuations?

Numerous minor issues further detract from the paper's quality. There are notational inconsistencies (e.g., the definition of Θ and its elements). The explanation of Algorithm 1 is convoluted and difficult to follow. The claim in line 6 of Alg 1,  “∂∂W hTWx = hxT”, is a standard result, but its inclusion feels unnecessary and disrupts the flow. Equation (13) is stated without explanation or transition from equation (7), leaving the reader to infer the connection. The references are missing formatting (e.g. [6] is missing details on the source).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem.
*   Reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p).
*   Empirically promising results on several benchmark datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of rigorous experimental validation (no statistical significance tests, incomplete baseline comparisons).
*   Insufficient justification for methodological choices (initialization, regularization).
*   Vague description of hyperparameter tuning.
*   Numerous minor notational inconsistencies and clarity issues.
*   Overstated claims of originality and significance.
*   Limited scope (exclusively binary classification despite initial framing).

**Overall:**

While the core idea of this paper is interesting, the numerous flaws – both major and minor – outweigh its strengths. The lack of methodological rigor, incomplete experimental evaluation, and clarity issues make it unsuitable for acceptance at NIPS. A major revision addressing these concerns is necessary before reconsideration.
