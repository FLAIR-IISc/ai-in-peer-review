This paper tackles a long-standing limitation of decision trees – their greedy nature – by proposing a non-greedy optimization framework. The core idea, framing decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables and utilizing a convex-concave upper bound for optimization, is a genuinely novel and promising approach. The connection to structured prediction is cleverly exploited, and the reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a significant technical contribution, opening the door to deeper trees. While the paper is dense and could benefit from clearer exposition in places, the boldness of the idea and its potential impact are substantial.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The non-greedy approach to decision tree learning is a significant departure from decades of established practice. Framing it as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is insightful.
* **Technical Contribution:** The complexity reduction from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is crucial for scalability and demonstrates a strong understanding of the optimization challenges.
* **Potential Impact:** If successful, this approach could lead to more accurate and compact decision trees, potentially revitalizing their use in areas where they’ve been surpassed by other methods. The ability to handle general loss functions is a significant advantage.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results, while not overwhelming, consistently show improved performance over greedy baselines and related work (CO2), especially as tree depth increases. The analysis of the regularization parameter ν is particularly valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity:** The paper is quite dense and requires significant effort to follow. The notation is heavy, and some explanations could be more intuitive. More illustrative examples would be helpful.
* **Validation Scope:** While experiments are conducted on several datasets, the comparisons are somewhat limited. A more thorough evaluation against state-of-the-art methods (e.g., gradient boosted trees) would strengthen the claims. The sensitivity to the initialization strategy (greedy tree) isn’t fully explored.
* **Theoretical Gaps:** While the upper bound is presented, a more rigorous justification for its tightness and the impact of the regularization term would be valuable. The paper relies on Proposition 1 without a fully detailed proof in the main text.
* **Practical Considerations:** The implementation details, while mentioned, could be expanded. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of learning rate and other SGD hyperparameters?



**Pro-Acceptance Arguments:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely new and significant idea to a core machine learning problem.
* The technical contributions (complexity reduction, formulation as structured prediction) are substantial.
* The empirical results, though not dramatic, support the claims of improved performance.
* The potential impact on the field is considerable – a successful non-greedy approach could revitalize decision trees.

**Con-Acceptance Arguments:**

* The paper is dense and could be difficult for some readers to follow.
* The experimental validation is not exhaustive.
* The theoretical justification could be strengthened.
* The practical considerations of implementation and hyperparameter tuning are not fully addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor flaws in clarity and validation scope, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and potential impact of the proposed approach outweigh the limitations. This work represents a bold and promising direction for decision tree learning, and even if the current implementation has room for improvement, the fundamental idea is compelling. The connection to structured prediction is clever, and the technical contributions are valuable. The paper addresses a significant limitation of a widely used algorithm, and I believe it has the potential to inspire further research in this area. I anticipate this work will be well-received by the NIPS community.
