Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees that jointly optimizes all split parameters under a global objective, rather than fitting splits greedily node-by-node. The key idea is to view the sequence of split decisions along a path as a binary latent variable h ∈ {−1,+1}^m, recasting tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized by SGD. Naively, loss-augmented inference over all binary codes is exponential in tree depth, O(2^d p), but the paper introduces a tighter, efficiently computable surrogate that restricts loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), yielding O(d^2 p) complexity per step. The method supports general losses (e.g., log loss, squared loss), enforces per-row norm constraints on W to regularize and control bound tightness, and introduces a Stable SGD (SSGD) variant that stabilizes leaf assignments. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets show improved test accuracy for single trees over axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and the greedy CO2 variant; the fast inference yields substantial speedups for deeper trees.

Relation to prior work
- Greedy CART/ID3-style induction remains the dominant approach in applications; prior non-greedy global optimization (e.g., Bennett) is computationally prohibitive and limited in scope.
- Soft decision trees/HME enable smooth optimization but sacrifice the computational advantages of hard splits at test time.
- Max-margin decision trees and margin enlargement relate to per-node margins but still typically fit greedily.
- Structured prediction with latent variables and structural SVMs motivate the loss-augmented inference and margin-rescaling perspective; the paper adapts these ideas to the tree setting via the latent code h = sgn(Wx) and an efficiently computable bound.
- CO2 can be seen as the depth-1 (greedy) special case; this work provides a joint, non-greedy extension with an efficient inference routine.

Strengths
- Novel formulation: Casting hard-split decision tree training as latent structured prediction with an explicit convex–concave upper bound is a compelling conceptual contribution.
- Algorithmic advance: The Hamming-ball (radius-1) loss-augmented inference is a neat, principled device that turns an exponential step into O(d^2 p), enabling practical training of deep trees while retaining hard-split inference at O(dp).
- Generality: The framework handles general losses and both classification and regression, unlike several earlier non-greedy approaches.
- Practicality: The SGD/SSGD procedures, per-row norm constraints, and initialization strategies are sensible and easy to implement; fast inference shows clear wall-clock advantages on deeper trees.
- Empirical results: Across multiple datasets, single non-greedy trees consistently outperform strong greedy baselines and the CO2 variant, with less overfitting at larger depths. The role of ν as both regularizer and implicit pruning knob is clearly demonstrated.

Weaknesses and questions
- Bound tightness vs. bias: Restricting loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 produces a looser bound than the full max; while the inequality is correct, the impact on optimization quality is not deeply analyzed. A small empirical ablation comparing radius-1 vs. full (for small d) would strengthen the case.
- Baselines and scope: The focus on single trees is reasonable for isolating algorithmic effects, but many practitioners use forests/boosting. A short experiment showing gains transfer when the method is used as a base learner in a forest (or at least discussion of training time/accuracy tradeoffs) would broaden significance.
- Comparisons to modern soft/differentiable trees: HME is cited, but a brief empirical or conceptual comparison to recent differentiable/hardening-splits methods would help situate the contribution.
- Initialization dependence: The method initializes from an axis-aligned tree and CO2. It would be good to quantify sensitivity to initialization (e.g., random W vs. CO2) and whether SSGD mitigates poor starts.
- Statistical reporting: The plots suggest consistent gains, but it’s unclear if differences are statistically significant. Reporting mean±std over multiple runs and a small ablation on SSGD vs. SGD would improve the rigor.
- Regularization theory: The per-row norm constraint is motivated and effective empirically, but some theoretical insight (e.g., margin/generalization discussion tailored to trees) would be valuable.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The derivations are plausible and well-motivated; proofs are deferred to the supplement. Complexity claims are clear, and the algorithm is implementable. Empirics are solid for single-tree evaluation, though additional ablations and statistical reporting would strengthen the evaluation.

Clarity
Well-written and organized. The mapping from trees to latent structured prediction is explained cleanly; notation is consistent; algorithmic steps are explicit. Minor suggestions: include a proof sketch of Proposition 1 in the main text; add a figure clarifying which bits differ under the Hamming-ball inference; explicitly state constants in the O(d^2 p) step.

Originality
High. The latent-structured perspective on hard-split trees with an efficient loss-augmented inference restricted to a small Hamming neighborhood feels novel and nontrivial, going beyond prior greedy or MILP-based approaches and beyond soft-split models.

Significance
High for the decision tree literature. The method offers a practical route to globally trained, compact, hard-split trees with better generalization, and it opens several promising directions (sparse W, kernels, ensembles). It is likely to influence both research and practice.

Pros
- Bold, conceptually clean formulation linking trees to latent structured prediction
- Efficient and elegant radius-1 loss-augmented inference with O(d^2 p) complexity
- Consistent empirical gains over strong greedy baselines and CO2
- Maintains hard-split inference-time efficiency and supports general losses

Cons
- Limited analysis of the tradeoff introduced by the radius-1 restriction
- Single-tree focus; lack of forest/boosting experiments
- Sensitivity to initialization and to SSGD not thoroughly quantified
- Limited statistical reporting

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a fresh and practical non-greedy paradigm for training decision trees, with a novel and efficient optimization scheme. Despite some empirical gaps and ablations that could be added, the conceptual and algorithmic contributions are significant and likely to inspire follow-up work.

Confidence: 4/5
