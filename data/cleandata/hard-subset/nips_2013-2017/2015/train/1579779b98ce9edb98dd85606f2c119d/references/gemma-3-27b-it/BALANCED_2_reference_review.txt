This paper proposes a novel framework for non-greedy learning of decision trees, addressing the limitations of traditional greedy approaches. The core idea is to establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution lies in reducing the computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p), enabling efficient learning of deeper trees.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the drawbacks of greedy tree induction and the potential benefits of a non-greedy approach. The connection to structured prediction is insightful, and the proposed formulation provides a principled way to jointly optimize split functions at different levels of the tree. The reduction in computational complexity is also significant, making the method more practical for larger datasets and deeper trees. The experiments on benchmark datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing improvements in test accuracy compared to several greedy baselines and a related CO2 method. The ablation studies exploring the effect of the regularization parameter ν are also valuable.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, while the authors claim generality to different loss functions, the experimental evaluation focuses primarily on classification tasks with cross-entropy loss. Expanding the evaluation to regression tasks, or other loss functions, would strengthen the claim of generality. Second, the explanation of the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm, while described as O(d2p), lacks sufficient detail to fully understand its implementation and benefits. A more detailed explanation, perhaps with pseudocode, would be helpful. Third, the comparison to related work could be more thorough. For example, the discussion of Hierarchical Mixture of Experts (HME) focuses on the computational cost of evaluating experts, but doesn't adequately address how the proposed approach tackles the issues of soft splits. Further, the benefit of SSGD over vanilla SGD isn’t thoroughly explained. Why does fixing assignments prevent the underutilization of leaves? A more detailed discussion of the intuitive benefits would be helpful. Fourth, the impact of the initialization strategy (using axis-aligned trees and CO2) on the final results isn’t clearly articulated. Is the performance sensitive to this initialization, or is it a reliable starting point? Fifth, the discussion of the regularization parameter ν could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of its role in controlling the trade-off between bias and variance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The non-greedy approach to decision tree learning, framed within structured prediction, is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The formulation of the convex-concave upper bound and the complexity reduction are well-justified.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on benchmark datasets.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Loss Function Evaluation:** Primarily focuses on classification with cross-entropy.
*   **Lack of Detail in Fast Inference:** Insufficient explanation of the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm.
*   **Incomplete Related Work Discussion:** HME and SSGD aren’t fully explored.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** Impact of initialization strategy is unclear.
*   **Regularization Parameter Analysis:**  A deeper analysis of ν’s impact would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to decision tree learning.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*   The technical contributions, particularly the complexity reduction, are significant.
*   The paper addresses a long-standing problem in machine learning – the limitations of greedy tree induction.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The evaluation is somewhat limited in its scope, particularly regarding loss functions.
*   The details of the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm could be more clear.
*   The related work discussion could be more thorough.
*   The discussion of the regularization parameter isn’t detailed enough.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed non-greedy approach offers a promising alternative to traditional greedy methods, and the theoretical and empirical results are encouraging. However, the weaknesses mentioned above prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance in its current form.  I recommend **rejection** with a request for a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, a clearer explanation of the fast inference algorithm, and a more detailed discussion of the related work and regularization parameter. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
