### **Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"**

#### **Summary**

This paper addresses the long-standing problem of greedy decision tree induction, where splits are optimized locally at each node without regard for their impact on subsequent splits. The authors propose a novel, non-greedy framework for globally optimizing all split parameters in a decision tree. The core contribution is to re-frame the tree learning problem as one of structured prediction with latent variables. Specifically, the sequence of binary decisions that routes an example to a leaf is treated as a latent variable. This formulation allows the authors to derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective function. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for the loss-augmented inference step required for gradient computation, reducing the complexity from exponential in the tree depth (`O(2^d p)`) to polynomial (`O(d^2 p)`), making the approach practical for deep trees. The method is evaluated on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating superior accuracy and generalization compared to greedy methods, including both axis-aligned and oblique tree variants.

#### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical formulation is sound, building upon established work in structured prediction (specifically, latent structural SVMs) and adapting it creatively to the decision tree problem. The claims are well-supported by both the mathematical derivations and the experimental results. The derivation of the surrogate objective and the justification for regularization are clear and principled. The experiments are thorough, using relevant benchmark datasets and a strong set of baselines that effectively isolate the contribution of the non-greedy optimization (e.g., the comparison with CO2, a greedy version of their optimization strategy). The empirical validation of the "fast" loss-augmented inference algorithm's complexity (Fig. 4) is a crucial and convincing piece of evidence for the paper's claims of efficiency. The work is a complete and self-contained contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of building up their formulation step-by-step, starting from the standard decision tree model, introducing the latent variable perspective, connecting it to structured prediction, and finally deriving the optimization algorithm. The notation is consistent and the mathematical arguments are easy to follow. Figure 1 provides a helpful illustration of the tree navigation function. The description of the SGD algorithm and its computationally efficient variant is clear enough for an expert to understand the implementation details.

**Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is highly original. While non-greedy tree optimization is not a new problem, the approach taken here is novel and powerful. Casting tree induction as latent structured prediction is, to my knowledge, a new and insightful connection that opens up a rich toolbox of optimization techniques. Previous non-greedy methods were often limited by computational complexity (e.g., Bennett [1]) or changed the fundamental nature of the tree (e.g., Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts [13]). This work provides a method that optimizes the standard hard-split tree structure in a globally-aware manner while remaining computationally feasible. The development of the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm is a key piece of original technical work that makes the entire framework practical.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. Decision trees and random forests are among the most widely used models in machine learning, yet their core induction algorithms have remained largely unchanged for decades. This paper presents one of the first truly practical and effective methods for breaking away from the greedy paradigm. The demonstrated improvements in accuracy and generalization suggest that this approach can advance the state of the art in tree-based models. The framework is general, supporting various loss functions, and as the authors note, could be extended with techniques like kernelization or sparsity-inducing regularization. Researchers and practitioners working with tree-based models are very likely to build upon or use these ideas.

#### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to make it even better:

1.  **Dependence on Initialization:** The method is initialized using a greedily trained tree. Since the overall optimization problem is non-convex, the quality of this initialization seems critical. A discussion of the sensitivity of the final model's performance to different initializations would be valuable. For instance, does the method always improve upon its initialization, or can it get stuck? What happens if initialized randomly?

2.  **Analysis of the "Stable SGD" Heuristic:** The introduction of Stable SGD (SSGD) to prevent leaves from becoming inactive is a practical and reasonable heuristic. However, it would be beneficial to provide a more detailed analysis or an ablation study. How much of the final performance gain is attributable to SSGD versus the standard SGD on the surrogate? Quantifying its impact would strengthen the justification for its inclusion.

3.  **Relationship between the Two Bounds:** The paper first introduces a general upper bound (Eq. 7) and then a second, more constrained bound for the fast algorithm (Eq. 13), where the search space for `g` is limited to a Hamming ball of radius 1. It should be explicitly stated that the second bound is looser than the first. While the computational benefits are clear and the empirical results are strong, a brief comment on any potential theoretical or practical downsides of using a looser surrogate would be helpful for completeness.

4.  **Contextualization with Gradient Boosting:** Gradient Boosting Machines (GBMs) can also be viewed as a form of non-greedy tree learning, where each new tree is fit to the residual errors of the existing ensemble, thus acting in a globally-aware, additive fashion. While mechanistically different, a brief discussion in the related work section contextualizing the proposed joint-optimization approach against the stage-wise functional gradient approach of boosting would be insightful for the reader.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant advance in a fundamental area of machine learning. The novel formulation, principled optimization, and strong empirical results make it a clear contribution to the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
