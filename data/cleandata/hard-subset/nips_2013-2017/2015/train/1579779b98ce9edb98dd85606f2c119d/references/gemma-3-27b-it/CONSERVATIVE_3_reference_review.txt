This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and developing an efficient stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm for optimization. While the motivation – overcoming the limitations of greedy tree induction – is reasonable, and the connection to structured prediction is interesting, the paper falls short of convincing me of its significant contribution to the field. The core issue is a lack of compelling evidence demonstrating a substantial advantage over well-established, greedy methods, particularly given the added complexity.

The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional greedy algorithms. However, the proposed solution, while theoretically sound, feels overly complex. The introduction of latent variables and the resulting upper bound optimization, while potentially allowing for improved trees, introduces substantial computational overhead. The authors do address this with a reduced complexity gradient update, but the experimental results don’t clearly demonstrate a performance gain commensurate with this complexity. The claim of handling “any general loss function” is not particularly impactful; existing greedy methods can readily accommodate different loss functions at the leaf nodes. 

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, acknowledging prior attempts at non-greedy tree learning. However, the dismissal of Murthy and Salzburg’s argument regarding the necessity of non-greedy methods feels somewhat dismissive, particularly given the limited empirical evidence presented here. The comparison to CO2 is a positive step, but the gains shown in the experiments are not dramatic enough to justify the increased complexity of the full proposed method.

The experimental setup appears reasonable, utilizing standard datasets and comparison to relevant baselines. However, the performance gains shown in Fig. 2 are often modest and not consistently observed across all datasets. The regularization parameter analysis (Fig. 3) is helpful, but it doesn't fundamentally alter the conclusion that the performance improvements, while present, are not substantial. The runtime comparison in Fig. 4 highlights the efficiency gains of the fast loss-augmented inference, but it doesn’t account for the overall increased complexity of the method—including the overhead of latent variable handling. Furthermore, the initialization using a greedy tree (CO2) suggests an inherent reliance on greedy methods for good performance.

The mathematical formulation is generally clear, and the proofs (referenced to supplementary material) are crucial for assessing the validity of the proposed method. However, the exposition is dense, and a more intuitive explanation of the latent variable formulation would improve clarity. The justification for the norm regularization (Proposition 1) is reasonable, but the explanation of its impact on the tightness of the bound feels somewhat hand-wavy.

**Strengths:**

*   Interesting connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction.
*   Development of an efficient SGD algorithm for optimizing the proposed upper bound.
*   Addresses a valid limitation of traditional greedy decision tree algorithms.
*   Clear mathematical formulation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited and modest performance gains compared to established greedy methods.
*   Increased complexity and computational overhead.
*   Reliance on greedy initialization (CO2).
*   Lack of a truly compelling demonstration of generalization improvement.
*   Dense exposition, making the latent variable formulation difficult to grasp intuitively.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel theoretical framework for non-greedy decision tree learning.
*   The proposed algorithm is efficient and scalable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant performance improvement over existing methods.
*   The added complexity of the approach is not justified by the observed gains.
*   The paper lacks a strong, compelling narrative regarding the practical benefits of the proposed method.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the technical work is sound and the ideas are interesting, the lack of a substantial performance gain and the increased complexity make it difficult to justify its contribution to the field. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a significant advancement. The authors should consider significantly strengthening the experimental evaluation, exploring more challenging datasets, and providing a more convincing demonstration of the practical benefits of their approach before resubmitting. Alternatively, focusing on a very specific niche where the non-greedy approach demonstrably outperforms existing methods might strengthen the paper's impact.
