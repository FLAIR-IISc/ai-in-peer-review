Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy method for learning oblique decision trees by jointly optimizing all split parameters under a global objective. The key idea is to recast split decisions as binary latent variables h ∈ {−1,+1}^m and to upper-bound the empirical loss with a convex–concave surrogate reminiscent of latent-structured SVMs. Loss-augmented inference is used within SGD to update W (split parameters) and Θ (leaf parameters), with per-row norm constraints on W. A “fast” variant restricts loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing per-step complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p). An SSGD variant fixes leaf assignments intermittently to stabilize training. Experiments on LibSVM multiclass datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) compare against axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2 (a depth-1 greedy special case). The method reportedly improves test accuracy and reduces overfitting across depths; ν controls regularization and implicit pruning.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual link between decision trees and latent-structured prediction; the reformulation sgn(Wx) = argmax_h h^T W x is elegant and useful.
- A principled, optimizable surrogate bound enabling non-greedy joint training; per-row norm constraints provide a tunable regularizer.
- Practical efficiency improvement via restricting loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball; complexity analysis is plausible and aligns with empirical timings.
- Simple SGD algorithm with projection and a stabilizing SSGD heuristic; initialization via CO2 aligns with best practices for non-convex optimization.
- Empirical results suggest consistent gains over several greedy baselines for single trees across depths.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Quality and correctness
- The fast bound (Eq. 13): While the bound remains valid because the ball includes g = sgn(Wx), this key reasoning is not stated; as written (“identical proof”), the justification is incomplete. Please make explicit that the restricted maximization set contains sgn(Wx), guaranteeing the bound.
- Tie handling for sgn(·): The method’s correctness and the tightness/behavior of the bounds depend on ĥ(x) = sgn(Wx). No tie-breaking rule is specified for Wx elements equal to 0; this matters for correctness and stability (and is referenced qualitatively in Proposition 1 but never formalized).
- Notation for norms is inconsistent: the paper uses ‖·‖2 to denote squared norm in Eq. (3) and in (9), but the projection step uses √ν, implying the constraint is ‖wi‖^2 ≤ ν. Please standardize (e.g., use ‖·‖2 for Euclidean norm and ‖·‖2^2 for squared norm).
- Algorithm 1 uses full loss-augmented inference over Hm (Line 5), whereas experiments use the fast Hamming-ball variant; this discrepancy should be reflected in pseudocode or clarified.
- SSGD is only described qualitatively; there are no concrete schedules, stopping criteria, or hyperparameters for when to freeze/unfreeze assignments. It’s unclear whether SSGD or SGD is used to produce each figure.
- Kernelization claim in the conclusion is speculative: applying the kernel trick to w^T φ(h, x) requires a kernel over (h, x) with feasible inference; this is nontrivial and not discussed.

Experimental rigor and reproducibility
- Experimental details are insufficient to reproduce results: no seeds, batch sizes, momentum values, number of epochs/steps, stopping criteria, or exact train/val/test splits (when randomized). No error bars, standard deviations, or multiple runs reported.
- Initialization is crucial, yet there is no ablation showing sensitivity to initialization quality (e.g., random vs. CO2 vs. axis-aligned). Similarly, no ablation for the fast bound vs. full bound, or for SSGD vs. SGD.
- The hyperparameter grid contains an odd value “ν ∈ {0.1, 1, 4, 10, 43, 100}”; “43” seems arbitrary—clarify whether this is a typo (e.g., 40 or 4^3) or motivated choice.
- Baselines omit widely used strong learners for single trees and ensembles (e.g., soft decision trees, max-margin trees, gradient-boosted trees). While the paper argues for analyzing single trees, comparisons to strong single-tree oblique baselines and a discussion of how the approach extends to forests are missing.
- Dataset preprocessing (especially for MNIST), feature normalization, and class distributions are not described. It’s unclear whether oblique splits are disadvantaged/advantaged by preprocessing.

Clarity and organization
- Numerous typos/grammar issues impede readability (e.g., “withm internal,” “by parameterized a weight vector,” missing hyphens in “axisaligned”). Some sentences are hard to parse.
- Figures are referenced but not described in detail; axes, scales, and absolute accuracies are not discussed. Without error bars, robustness is unclear.
- References are poorly formatted, with missing years/venues/pages; at least one entry is malformed (“et al, J. Shotton…”). This is below the bar for NeurIPS formatting standards.

Originality and significance
- The non-greedy joint optimization of oblique splits with a latent-structured upper bound and a fast Hamming-ball approximation is novel and interesting. However, the paper positions itself primarily against older baselines; the lack of stronger comparisons and deeper analysis of when/why it helps limits the demonstrated impact.
- The idea likely matters for practitioners who want better single trees, but its broader significance would be clearer with forest-level results or stronger baselines.

Questions for the authors
- Please formalize tie-breaking in sgn(Wx) and discuss its impact on the bound and training.
- Provide precise SSGD schedule and hyperparameters used in experiments. Which figures use SSGD vs. SGD?
- Report ablations: initialization strategies; fast vs. full bound; effect of ν beyond leaf pruning; sensitivity to η; with/without projection; training stability (inactive leaves).
- Add multiple runs with variances on test accuracy; include error bars.
- Clarify whether the per-step complexity can be reduced to O(dp) by caching w_i^T x along the root-to-leaf path for all candidate leaves in the Hamming ball.

Pros
- Principled non-greedy framework with a workable surrogate.
- Clever and effective fast loss-augmented inference.
- Empirical improvements across datasets and depths.
- Regularization interpretation is intuitive (controls implicit pruning).

Cons
- Many missing details and notational inconsistencies; limited reproducibility.
- Insufficient ablations and statistical rigor.
- Baselines are dated; practical significance underexplored.
- Presentation and references require substantial polishing.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The core idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the accumulation of methodological, clarity, and experimental shortcomings prevents confident acceptance in its current form.

Confidence
High.
