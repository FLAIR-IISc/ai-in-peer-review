

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a non-greedy framework for learning decision trees by jointly optimizing split functions across the entire tree structure rather than using the traditional greedy approach. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating the problem as optimizing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. Key technical contributions include showing how to reduce the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) for loss-augmented inference, and demonstrating improved generalization performance on multiple benchmark datasets compared to greedy methods. The work builds on prior efforts like Bennett's non-greedy tree optimization [1] and the Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13], but offers a more efficient and general approach that handles any loss function while maintaining the computational advantages of tree structures.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical contributions. The authors provide a rigorous proof of the upper bound (Proposition 1) and demonstrate its relationship to structured prediction. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, and the authors carefully address the exponential growth problem in loss-augmented inference. The experimental section compares the proposed method with multiple baselines (greedy axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and CO2) across several benchmark datasets. The results consistently show improved generalization performance across different tree depths, with less susceptibility to overfitting. The authors acknowledge limitations, particularly the need to tune the regularization parameter ν, and provide clear explanations for their design choices.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation is precise, with clear mathematical notation and definitions. The connection to structured prediction is elegantly explained, and the algorithms (particularly Algorithm 1 and the fast loss-augmented inference) are described with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are presented with appropriate visualizations (Figures 2-4) that effectively communicate the key findings. The supplementary material appears to contain necessary mathematical proofs, supporting the claims made in the main text.

**Originality:** The paper makes several significant contributions to the field. The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is novel and provides a fresh perspective on a longstanding problem. The key technical innovation of reducing the computational complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth is substantial and addresses a major practical limitation of prior non-greedy approaches. While prior work has explored non-greedy tree learning (Bennett [1], Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13]), this paper offers a more efficient, generalizable framework that handles any loss function while maintaining the computational advantages of tree structures. The comparison with CO2 [18] (a greedy variant of their approach) effectively demonstrates the value of the non-greedy optimization.

**Significance:** Decision trees are widely used in practice, making this work highly significant for both theoretical and practical applications. The demonstrated improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets show that the method can produce better models without increasing computational complexity in a way that scales to larger trees. The approach enables learning more compact trees (as shown in the experiments), which is valuable for resource-constrained applications. The paper also identifies promising directions for future work, including learning sparse split functions and applying the kernel trick to handle higher-order split functions.

**Strengths:** 
- Novel theoretical connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction.
- Significant computational complexity reduction from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p).
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets and baselines.
- Clear demonstration of improved generalization performance with less overfitting.
- Well-written and technically sound with clear mathematical derivations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The comparison with recent non-greedy methods (post-2015) could be more extensive, particularly given the rapid pace of development in tree-based methods.
- The computational complexity analysis for the fast loss-augmented inference could be more detailed, especially regarding practical implementation considerations.
- The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss potential limitations for very large datasets (beyond the Connect4 example) or extremely deep trees.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

Pro:
- Novel theoretical framework connecting decision tree learning to structured prediction.
- Significant technical contribution in making non-greedy tree learning computationally feasible.
- Demonstrated improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets.
- Well-written with clear experimental validation.
- General approach applicable to any loss function.

Con:
- Limited comparison with the most recent non-greedy tree learning methods.
- Some aspects of computational complexity analysis could be more detailed.
- Potential limitations for extremely large-scale applications are not thoroughly addressed.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning with a theoretically sound, computationally efficient approach that demonstrates clear improvements over existing greedy methods. The work is well-executed, well-written, and addresses a fundamental limitation in decision tree induction. While the comparison with the most recent non-greedy methods could be expanded, the paper's core contributions are substantial and well-supported by both theory and experiments. The significance of the work is clear for both practitioners and researchers working with tree-based models.
