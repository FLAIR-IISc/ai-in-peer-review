

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a fundamentally novel approach to decision tree learning by breaking away from the decades-old greedy paradigm. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating the problem as a latent variable structured prediction task where the binary split decisions serve as latent variables. Their key insight is to develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent, while introducing a clever computational trick to reduce the complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth.

The paper's main strength lies in its bold theoretical shift - challenging the foundational greedy approach to decision tree induction. This is a significant departure from conventional wisdom that has dominated the field since ID3 and CART. The authors successfully demonstrate how this non-greedy framework can lead to better generalization performance and more compact trees, which is a compelling theoretical and practical contribution. The connection to structured prediction is particularly elegant, as it leverages well-established techniques from that field to solve a long-standing problem in decision trees.

The technical contribution of reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) is substantial and makes the approach practically feasible for deeper trees. The authors provide a clear explanation of how this complexity reduction is achieved through a carefully constructed loss-augmented inference procedure. The experimental results on standard benchmark datasets consistently show that the non-greedy approach outperforms greedy baselines across multiple tree depths, with improved generalization and reduced overfitting.

I'm particularly impressed by the paper's conceptual novelty. While previous work on non-greedy tree optimization (e.g., Bennett [1]) has been limited by computational complexity, this paper provides a scalable solution that could potentially reshape how decision trees are trained. The authors successfully address the key challenge of non-greedy tree learning - the exponential number of latent variables - in a way that's both theoretically sound and computationally efficient.

The paper does have some limitations that could be addressed in future work. The experimental section is somewhat limited to standard benchmark datasets without exploring scaling to very large datasets or more complex real-world applications. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper could benefit from more discussion about how the approach might integrate with modern ensemble methods or deep learning frameworks. The comparison with recent non-greedy approaches beyond Bennett's work is somewhat limited, though the authors do provide a good discussion of related work.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual shifts over methodological perfection, I find the core contribution of this paper to be exceptionally strong. The paper's central idea - that decision trees can be trained non-greedily by leveraging structured prediction techniques - is genuinely novel and has the potential to influence both theory and practice in machine learning. The authors have made a compelling case for why this approach is superior to greedy methods, both theoretically and empirically.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The connection between decision trees and structured prediction is presented as a natural extension of the field, making the contribution feel both innovative and grounded in established theory.

In summary, this paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by challenging the greedy paradigm in decision tree learning. The authors present a theoretically sound framework that addresses a fundamental limitation of traditional decision tree induction, with practical computational efficiency. While the experimental validation could be expanded, the novelty of the approach and the elegance of its theoretical foundation make this a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The paper represents a genuinely new direction in decision tree learning that could inspire future research and applications.

**Recommendation: Accept**
