This paper tackles a long-standing problem in machine learning: the limitations of greedy decision tree induction. The authors propose a non-greedy approach framed as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, offering a compelling link between these two areas. The core idea—jointly optimizing split functions across the tree under a global objective—is genuinely novel and has the potential to significantly improve decision tree performance. The formulation leveraging a convex-concave upper bound and the reduction of computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) are clever and important contributions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The non-greedy approach, structured prediction framing, and the proposed optimization strategy are highly novel. This isn’t just another tweak to CART; it's a fundamental shift in how decision trees are learned.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The connection to structured prediction and the derivation of the upper bound provide a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Computational efficiency:** Addressing the exponential complexity with the O(d2p) reduction is critical for practical applicability and demonstrates insightful technical work.
*   **Empirical results:** The experiments, while not extensive, clearly demonstrate the benefits of the non-greedy approach, consistently outperforming greedy baselines across various datasets and tree depths. The regularization parameter analysis (Fig. 3) is a nice touch.
*   **Clear writing:** Despite the technical depth, the paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the authors achieve O(d2p) complexity, this could still be prohibitive for extremely deep trees and high-dimensional data. A more detailed discussion of scalability limitations and potential future optimizations would be valuable.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to existing methods is somewhat limited. While CO2 is included, comparing against more recent and sophisticated ensemble methods (beyond just greedy baselines) would strengthen the evaluation. It's not clear how this method compares to gradient boosting or modern random forest implementations.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on tuning the regularization parameter *ν* suggests potential sensitivity to this parameter. A more systematic study of its impact and potential automatic tuning strategies would be beneficial.
*   **Initialization:** The dependence on initializing with an axis-aligned tree and CO2 is a potential weakness. The performance could be sensitive to this initialization, and it would be beneficial to explore alternative initialization strategies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is exceptionally innovative and addresses a fundamental limitation of decision trees.
*   The theoretical formulation is rigorous and the complexity reduction is a significant technical achievement.
*   The empirical results, while not exhaustive, strongly support the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   The paper introduces a new perspective on decision tree learning that has the potential to inspire further research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limited scalability discussion and potential hyperparameter sensitivity could hinder practical adoption.
*   The comparison to the state-of-the-art is not comprehensive enough to definitively establish the method's superiority.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a highly promising paper that presents a significant advance in decision tree learning. While there are some areas for improvement, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper deserves acceptance to NIPS. The authors have taken a bold approach to a long-standing problem, and even if the method isn’t immediately applicable to all scenarios, it opens up exciting new avenues for research. I’d encourage the authors to address the scalability concerns and explore more extensive comparisons in future work.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
