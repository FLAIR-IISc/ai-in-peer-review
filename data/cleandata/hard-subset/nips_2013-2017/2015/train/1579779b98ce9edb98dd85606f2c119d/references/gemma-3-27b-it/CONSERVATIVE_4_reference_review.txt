This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the optimization as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The core idea—jointly optimizing split functions across the tree to improve generalization—is not entirely novel (as evidenced by the related work section), but the formulation and proposed optimization strategy utilizing a convex-concave upper bound and stochastic gradient descent are presented as contributions. The authors attempt to address the computational complexity inherent in their formulation with a reduction from O(2dp) to O(d2p).

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation and technical details. The connection to structured prediction is made explicit, and the derivation of the upper bound is presented with reasonable clarity. However, my overall assessment is skeptical. While the approach *could* be beneficial, the presented evidence is not compelling enough to warrant acceptance to NIPS, particularly given the established effectiveness of existing, simpler methods. 

The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. The reported results show improvement over baselines, but the gains are not substantial and, critically, the experimental setup feels incomplete. The choice of initializing the non-greedy method with a greedy tree (CO2) introduces a potential bias. A fairer comparison would involve randomly initialized split functions. Furthermore, the datasets used (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) are standard, but the depth range explored (6-18) feels somewhat arbitrary without a more systematic analysis of optimal tree depth for each dataset and baseline. The impact of the regularization parameter ν is discussed, but the justification for the chosen values isn't thoroughly presented. 

A significant concern is the complexity analysis. While reducing the complexity to O(d2p) is a technical contribution, the constant factors hidden within the big-O notation are likely substantial. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion of runtime (Fig. 4), but the observed speedups aren’t dramatically different from the greedy approaches. The claim of applicability to "deep trees" feels overstated without demonstrating significant scalability to substantially larger datasets or deeper trees. The reliance on SGD also introduces the usual challenges of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity to initialization. 

The related work section is adequate but could be more comprehensive. The authors dismiss Murthy and Salzberg’s work somewhat flippantly, claiming their methods lack regularization. However, depth limiting and ensemble methods *are* forms of regularization. A more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between non-greedy and greedy approaches would strengthen the paper. The comparison to CO2 is useful, but the authors don’t sufficiently explore why their method provides a benefit *beyond* simply enabling joint optimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem.
*   Convex-concave upper bound and SGD optimization provide a potentially scalable approach to non-greedy learning.
*   Demonstrated improvement in accuracy over baselines on several datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental results are not overwhelmingly convincing. Gains are modest, and the experimental setup is potentially biased.
*   Complexity analysis doesn't fully address practical scalability concerns.
*   The benefits of non-greediness over simpler methods are not sufficiently justified.
*   Related work discussion could be more comprehensive and nuanced.



**Overall, I recommend rejection.** The paper presents a technically sound approach, but it lacks the significant impact and rigorous validation necessary for acceptance to NIPS. The improvements over existing methods are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity, and the experimental evaluation needs to be more thorough and convincing. I encourage the authors to address the concerns raised above and potentially resubmit to a future conference after conducting more extensive experiments and providing a more compelling demonstration of the benefits of their approach.
