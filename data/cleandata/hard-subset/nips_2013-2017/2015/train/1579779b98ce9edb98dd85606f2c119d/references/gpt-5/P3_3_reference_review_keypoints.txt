- Proposal: Non-greedy training for oblique decision trees by treating split decisions as latent variables h = sgn(Wx) and optimizing a convex–concave upper bound via stochastic subgradient methods.  
- Key technical idea: Use a Hamming-ball (radius-1) restriction for loss-augmented inference to reduce per-update complexity to O(d^2 p) while preserving O(dp) test-time inference.  
- Optimization details: Jointly update split weights W (per-row L2 constraints) and leaf parameters Θ; introduce “Stable SGD” that fixes data-to-leaf assignments to avoid inactive leaves.  
- Relation to prior work: Synthesizes non-greedy/lookahead trees (OC1), latent SVM/SSVM formulations, CO2 (depth-1 special case), soft/probabilistic trees (HME), and hashing-style latent-code reparameterizations.  
- Theoretical assessment: Upper bounds and surrogate objective are standard and correct; subgradient updates are consistent, but convergence guarantees and analysis of SSGD/leaf reactivation are missing.  
- Algorithmic assessment: O(d^2 p) inference claim is plausible but needs explicit description of dot-product reuse/caching to justify practical costs; projection per row is straightforward.  
- Experimental assessment: Empirical gains vs several baselines across depths are shown, but results lack numeric tables, run variance, statistical tests, thorough baseline tuning, ensemble experiments, and full runtime comparisons.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well-written with consistent notation; however, key proof sketches should appear in main text, and pseudocode for the fast loss-augmented inference plus citation fixes are needed.  
- Originality: Novel in combining latent-SSVM-style training with a practical Hamming-ball bound to enable globally trained hard oblique trees with efficient inference, though components are known.  
- Significance: Potentially valuable for improving single-tree accuracy and regularization while preserving hard-routing efficiency, but impact limited by narrow empirical validation.  
- Suggested experiments/improvements: Provide pseudocode and complexity details, report means/std and hyperparameter search for all baselines, ablations on Hamming radius/SSGD/ν/random init, compare to soft trees, and evaluate integration into ensembles.  
- Pros: Principled formulation linking hard trees to structured prediction, practical efficient training scheme, consistent empirical gains, and interpretable regularization via ν.  
- Cons: Limited experimental breadth, no convergence/initialization analysis, and lack of theoretical analysis of bias introduced by the radius-1 restriction.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — solid and relevant methodological contribution with promising results but needs stronger experiments and clarity on inference/optimization behavior.
