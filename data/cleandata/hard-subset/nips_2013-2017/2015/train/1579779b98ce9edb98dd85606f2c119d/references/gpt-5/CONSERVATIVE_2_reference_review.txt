Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy framework for training decision trees with oblique splits by jointly optimizing all split parameters under a global surrogate objective. The key idea is to reinterpret the path decisions as latent binary variables h ∈ {−1, +1}^m and to upper bound the empirical loss with a convex–concave surrogate inspired by latent structured prediction. Optimization is carried out by SGD with per-row norm constraints on W, and the authors derive a fast “loss-augmented inference” step by restricting to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing per-update complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p). A “Stable SGD” variant fixes leaf assignments for several steps to avoid deactivating leaves. On four LibSVM multiclass datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST), they report that single trees trained with their non-greedy method outperform several greedy baselines across depths and appear less prone to overfitting.

Relation to prior work
The paper is grounded in established ideas: global (non-greedy) tree optimization (Bennett et al.), oblique splits (OC1), margin-based trees (SVM at nodes), soft-split trees (HME), and latent structured SVM formulations. The link sgn(Wx) = argmax_h h^T Wx and the use of a loss-augmented upper bound follow the latent-SSVM paradigm; the Hamming-ball relaxation is a computational device to keep inference tractable. The work CO2 (a greedy depth-1 upper-bound approach) is used for initialization and as a baseline.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean latent-variable formulation that unifies tree training with structured prediction ideas and explicitly exposes the scale dependence of the bound.
- Algorithmic practicality: the fast loss-augmented inference reduces complexity to O(d^2 p), enabling deeper trees than naive loss-augmented inference.
- Generality: the framework can, in principle, handle different losses (classification and regression) while preserving O(dp) inference at test time.
- Empirical indications: for single trees, non-greedy optimization improves test accuracy vs. axis-aligned greedy trees, OC1, random oblique splits, and CO2 across depths on several datasets.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theory and guarantees: The main result is an upper bound that depends critically on the scale of W; the choice of per-row norm constraints is reasonable but ad hoc. There are no generalization bounds or convergence guarantees beyond standard SGD heuristics for a non-convex objective. The Hamming-ball relaxation yields a looser bound; its effect on optimization quality is not analyzed. It is unclear to what extent minimizing this bound correlates with true empirical loss or test performance.
- Limited empirical validation versus strong baselines: The paper explicitly focuses on single trees. However, in practice, decision trees are predominantly used in ensembles (Random Forests, Gradient Boosted Trees). There is no comparison to strong, established baselines such as RF and gradient boosting at matched depth/leaf budgets, nor any evidence of improvements when the proposed non-greedy trees are used as base learners in ensembles. Without this, it is hard to assess significance.
- Experimental scope and rigor: Only four datasets are used, with no large-scale setting despite claims of efficiency for deep trees. There are no statistical significance tests (error bars, multiple seeds). The sensitivity to ν and learning rate is only qualitatively discussed; no systematic ablations (e.g., effect of initialization, SSGD vs. SGD, depth scaling, impact of the Hamming-ball radius) are presented. Wall-clock comparisons are limited to the two inference variants, not against baselines like OC1.
- Algorithmic details: 
  - The method heavily relies on greedy/CO2 initialization; behavior from random initialization is not reported.
  - The fast bound seems to change at most one bit relative to sgn(Wx), which implies gradients typically update only nodes on the current path where g differs from ĥ. How do off-path rows receive meaningful updates? If most rows are inactive most of the time, learning may be slow or biased.
  - SSGD (fixing leaf assignments) is introduced to stabilize training, but its schedule and hyperparameters are not specified, and no quantitative benefit is shown.
- Clarity points: Some critical proofs are deferred to the supplement. The argument around subtracting sgn(Wx)^T Wx in (12) and the claim that off-path bits “zero out” would benefit from a more formal, self-contained derivation. The assertion that the approach “handles any general loss” is not backed by experiments beyond log-loss.

Quality: The method is technically plausible and sound at a high level, but the theoretical development stops at a scale-sensitive upper bound and lacks guarantees or a characterization of the fast relaxation’s impact. The empirical evaluation does not yet meet the rigor expected for a new training paradigm (strong baselines, significance, ablations).

Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Some derivations and algorithmic choices (SSGD, initialization dependence) need more detail.

Originality: Moderate. The mapping to latent structured prediction and loss-augmented inference are established ideas; the Hamming-ball relaxation for fast inference within trees is a reasonable, if incremental, twist.

Significance: Currently limited. Improvements for single trees are interesting but of modest practical impact without demonstrating gains in commonly used ensembles or against strong baselines.

Questions for the authors
1) How does the method perform as base learners in Random Forests or Gradient Boosted Trees? Do gains persist?
2) Can you provide comparisons to RF and GBDT with matched depth/leaf counts and report wall-clock times?
3) How sensitive are results to ν and learning rate? Please include error bars over multiple random seeds.
4) What happens from random initialization? How crucial is CO2/greedy initialization?
5) Under the fast Hamming-ball bound, how often do off-path split nodes receive gradients? Do they effectively learn?
6) Can you quantify the benefit of SSGD and describe its schedule and hyperparameters?

Pros
- Clear formulation tying trees to latent structured prediction
- Practical complexity reduction to O(d^2 p)
- Consistent single-tree improvements over older greedy baselines

Cons
- No comparison to standard strong ensemble methods (RF, GBDT)
- No theoretical guarantees beyond a loose, scale-dependent bound
- Limited datasets, no statistical tests, few ablations
- Heavy reliance on initialization and ad hoc stabilization (SSGD)

Recommendation
Weak reject. The formulation is neat and potentially useful, but the empirical evidence is not yet sufficient against established baselines, and the theoretical contribution is limited to a heuristic surrogate with incomplete analysis. Strengthening the experiments (ensembles, stronger baselines, significance, ablations) or providing tighter theory would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
