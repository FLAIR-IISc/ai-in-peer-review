This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning, framing the optimization problem as structured prediction with latent variables and developing a convex-concave upper bound for efficient learning via stochastic gradient descent. The core idea of jointly optimizing split functions at different tree levels is novel and addresses a long-standing limitation of traditional greedy methods. The paper establishes a connection to structured prediction, introduces a surrogate objective, and provides a complexity reduction from O(2dp) to O(d2p) for deep trees, a significant technical contribution.

However, despite the novel ideas, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to significant concerns about the experimental validation and the practical impact of the proposed method. The writing quality, while generally understandable, is often imprecise, leading to ambiguity and requiring careful parsing.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The non-greedy approach and its formulation as a structured prediction problem are genuinely novel.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The complexity reduction to O(d2p) is a valuable technical contribution, enabling efficient learning of deeper trees.
*   **Potential for Improvement:** The framework provides a foundation for incorporating regularization and kernel methods, suggesting potential for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Precision:** The writing is often verbose and lacks precision. For instance, the discussion on Proposition 1 is convoluted and doesn’t clearly explain the intuition behind the regularization. The connection between the regularization parameter ν and the generalization of the learned trees isn’t sufficiently explained.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** There are minor but pervasive notational inconsistencies. For example, ‘m’ is used for both the number of internal nodes *and* to index them. While not critical, this makes the paper harder to follow. Similarly, the use of ‘d’ to represent both tree depth and a dimension in some contexts is confusing.
*   **Experimental Setup Concerns:** The experimental comparison lacks sufficient detail. While the authors mention tuning hyperparameters, the specific ranges and search strategies are not described. The initialization with a greedy tree (CO2) is reasonable, but the influence of this initialization on the final results isn’t adequately discussed. The datasets are fairly standard, but the ablation studies are lacking. For example, the effect of the proposed regularization is not systematically explored. 
*   **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The performance gains shown in Fig. 2, while present, are not dramatic. The authors claim "best test performance across tree depths," but the improvements are often marginal and the error bars are not shown, making it difficult to assess statistical significance. The comparison to CO2, while important, doesn’t demonstrate a substantial advantage.
*   **Algorithm 1 Issues:** Line 7 in Algorithm 1 contains a typo ("W (tmp)i, ."). More critically, the description of the projection step (Line 8) is vague.  It’s unclear if this is a hard threshold or a soft projection.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claim that the method can handle "any general loss function" is an overstatement. While theoretically possible, the computational cost might be prohibitive for some loss functions.
*   **Missing Details:** The supplementary material, referenced several times, is not provided with the submission. This makes verifying claims about proofs and derivations impossible.
*   **Lack of Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn't adequately address practical considerations like memory usage or scalability to very large datasets.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is novel and potentially impactful.
*   The theoretical contribution regarding complexity reduction is significant.
*   The paper opens up new avenues for research in non-greedy decision tree learning.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The writing is often unclear and imprecise.
*   The experimental validation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.
*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies detract from the overall quality of the paper.
*   The practical impact of the method is not well-established.
*   Lack of access to supplementary material hinders verification of key claims.



**Overall Assessment:**

I find it difficult to recommend acceptance of this paper in its current form. While the ideas are promising, the numerous weaknesses significantly outweigh the strengths. The paper reads as a preliminary exploration of a complex topic rather than a polished, conclusive contribution. The lack of clarity, insufficient experimental validation, and minor flaws undermine the potential impact of the work.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. I would encourage the authors to address the concerns raised in this review, focusing on improving the clarity of the writing, providing a more thorough experimental evaluation, and addressing the notational inconsistencies. Revising the paper with these improvements could potentially lead to a stronger submission in the future.
