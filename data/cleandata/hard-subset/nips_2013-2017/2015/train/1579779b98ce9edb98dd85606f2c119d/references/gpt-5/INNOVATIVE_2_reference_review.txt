Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy framework for training decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss via (stochastic) gradient descent. The key conceptual link is to structured prediction with latent variables: the split pattern h ∈ {−1,+1}^m is treated as latent, yielding a loss-augmented inference problem. The authors derive a general bound that would naively make each update O(2^d p), and then introduce a tighter computational approach by restricting loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing per-update cost to O(d^2 p) while preserving O(dp) inference at test time. They also include practical ingredients (per-row norm constraints with projection, SGD with momentum/minibatches, and a “Stable SGD” variant that freezes leaf assignments for several steps to avoid collapsing leaves). Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass benchmarks show consistent improvements over axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique splits, and a greedy variant (CO2), with better generalization and scalability for deeper trees.

Relation to prior work
- Global tree optimization is NP-complete (Hyafil & Rivest). Prior non-greedy attempts (Bennett; Bennett & Blue; Bennett et al.) are either computationally heavy or limited in scope. Soft trees (HME) trade accuracy for speed due to evaluating many experts. Greedy oblique trees (OC1) optimize local splits only.
- The paper’s core novelty is the structured-prediction view with latent split assignments, adopting a loss-augmented inference bound akin to latent structural SVMs (Tsochantaridis; Yu & Joachims). The Hamming-ball restriction is an algorithmic innovation that retains the spirit of global optimization while making it tractable for deep trees.
- CO2 is positioned as a special greedy case (depth-1 optimization), and the non-greedy joint optimization improves upon it.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual bridge: decision tree training as latent structured prediction with a clear, general surrogate objective that supports arbitrary losses and preserves fast O(dp) test-time inference.
- Efficient algorithmic contribution: from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) per SGD step via the Hamming-ball bound; this is a crucial step for practical deep trees.
- Principled regularization: analysis of scale effects on the bound motivates per-row norm constraints with simple projection; ν doubles as a knob to control model capacity/implicit pruning.
- Well-motivated practical variant (SSGD) to stabilize leaf usage without ad hoc heuristics.
- Empirical results across multiple datasets show consistent gains over strong greedy baselines and reduced overfitting, with a runtime study highlighting the scalability benefits of fast loss-augmented inference.

Weaknesses and questions
- Bound tightness vs. efficiency: Restricting g to B1(sgn(Wx)) trades optimality for speed. How much performance is lost relative to the full bound? A small ablation (e.g., radius r=1,2 vs. full) on shallower trees could quantify this.
- Initialization reliance: The method initializes W, Θ via a greedy axis-aligned tree followed by CO2. How sensitive are final results to initialization quality? Are there cases where poor initialization leads to many inactive leaves or local minima?
- SSGD specifics: Freezing leaf assignments improves stability, but adds another schedule/hyperparameter. A brief ablation (SGD vs. SSGD) on accuracy and leaf utilization would help understand its necessity and trade-offs.
- Scope of experiments: All results are multiclass classification; the framework supports general losses (e.g., regression), and the paper mentions kernels/sparsity as promising directions. A small regression benchmark or a kernelized split experiment (even shallow trees) would strengthen the case for generality.
- Comparisons: Single-tree comparisons are fair to isolate learning effects, but practitioners commonly use ensembles. A brief study (e.g., small forests) or at least a discussion of how the proposed training scales in forests would increase practical relevance.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameter ranges and training details are partly described; it would be helpful to release code or provide fuller training protocols (batch sizes, momentum, early stopping, SSGD schedule) to reproduce figures.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a clean derivation of the latent-structured bound and a compelling complexity reduction. Claims are supported by experiments on several datasets. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement; the main bound and scale proposition are plausible. The empirical section could be expanded with ablations and broader tasks, but the core claims are substantiated.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well-organized. The navigation function f and the loss-augmented inference mechanics are explained carefully. A schematic of the Hamming-ball restriction and which nodes are re-evaluated would aid intuition. Minor editorial issues aside, the exposition should be accessible to the NeurIPS audience.

Originality
High. The latent structured prediction formulation for hard-decision trees, the specific convex–concave surrogate, and the Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference for O(d^2 p) learning constitute a novel and bold approach relative to long-standing greedy paradigms and prior non-greedy attempts.

Significance
High. If widely adopted, this could shift how decision trees (and by extension forests) are trained, enabling non-greedy global optimization with practical complexity. The generality to arbitrary losses and potential to combine with kernels/sparsity make it a promising foundation for future work.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel conceptual contribution with practical algorithmic instantiation.
- Significant efficiency improvement enabling deep non-greedy trees.
- Consistent empirical gains over competitive baselines.
- Clear path to extensions (sparsity, kernels, ensembles).

Cons against acceptance
- Limited ablations on key design choices (radius-1 bound, SSGD).
- Narrow experimental scope (no regression, no ensembles).
- Dependence on initialization not fully analyzed.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a substantial new direction for decision tree learning with a compelling blend of theory, algorithm, and empirical evidence. Some experimental breadth and ablations are missing, but given the novelty and potential impact, these are minor and fixable. Confidence: high.
