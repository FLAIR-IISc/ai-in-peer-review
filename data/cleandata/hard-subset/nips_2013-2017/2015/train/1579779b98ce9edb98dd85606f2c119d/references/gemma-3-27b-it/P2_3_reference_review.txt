## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing problem of efficiently learning decision trees beyond the limitations of greedy algorithms. The authors propose a novel framework for non-greedy learning by formulating the decision tree learning problem as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. They derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and employ stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for optimization. A key technical contribution is a reduction of the computational complexity from O(2<sup>dp</sup>) to O(d<sup>2</sup>p), making the approach scalable to deeper trees. The paper includes experimental validation on benchmark datasets demonstrating improved performance compared to greedy baselines.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** The limitations of greedy decision tree algorithms are well-known.  Moving beyond greedy approaches is a significant goal in the field.
* **Novel Formulation:** Framing decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a clever and insightful connection. This allows leveraging tools from structured prediction for optimization.
* **Technical Contributions:** The development of the convex-concave upper bound and, crucially, the complexity reduction to O(d<sup>2</sup>p) are significant technical achievements. The latter is essential for practical applicability to reasonably deep trees.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments demonstrate improved performance compared to several established greedy baselines (OC1, random forests, CO2).  The experiments on different datasets show reasonable generalization of the proposed approach.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the technical details are presented in a reasonably accessible manner.
* **Consideration of Regularization:**  The inclusion of a norm regularization term (ν) and its impact on generalization are well-discussed and empirically evaluated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the O(d<sup>2</sup>p) complexity is a significant improvement, the practical scalability to very large datasets with high dimensionality (p) is not thoroughly discussed.  The experimental datasets are relatively modest in size.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the regularization parameter ν, and tuning this parameter requires a validation set. The paper briefly mentions this but does not delve into the challenges of selecting an appropriate value in a real-world scenario.
* **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison with greedy baselines is solid, a comparison with more recent and sophisticated tree-based methods (e.g., gradient boosted trees like XGBoost, LightGBM) would strengthen the evaluation.  These methods are often the benchmark in many practical applications.
* **Lack of Theoretical Justification for SSGD:** The Stable SGD (SSGD) variant is presented as empirically effective, but lacks a strong theoretical justification for why it improves performance. The rationale feels somewhat heuristic.
* **Initialization Dependency:** The method relies on initializing W with a greedy tree. The influence of this initialization on the final solution is not investigated.  Exploring different initializations might be beneficial.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations are clear, and the empirical results support the claims. It presents a complete, albeit initial, exploration of the proposed framework. The authors acknowledge the limitations (e.g., hyperparameter tuning).
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it reasonably easy to understand the core ideas and technical details.  However, some sections (e.g., the derivation of the upper bound) could benefit from further simplification.
* **Originality:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is novel. The complexity reduction is also a significant original contribution. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but a deeper dive into recent advancements in gradient boosted trees would be beneficial.
* **Significance:**  The work addresses a significant problem and offers a promising new approach. While it doesn't immediately surpass state-of-the-art methods like XGBoost, it lays a valuable foundation for future research in non-greedy decision tree learning. The potential to improve generalization performance and create more compact trees is important.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental limitation of standard decision trees.
* Presents a novel and technically sound approach.
* Demonstrates promising empirical results.
* The O(d<sup>2</sup>p) complexity reduction is a key contribution.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Scalability to very large datasets isn’t fully explored.
* Lack of comparison to current state-of-the-art methods (e.g., XGBoost).
* SSGD lacks a strong theoretical foundation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a **strong contribution** to the field of decision tree learning.  The proposed framework is novel and technically well-grounded, and the empirical results are encouraging. While there are some areas for improvement (scalability discussion, comparison to stronger baselines, justification of SSGD), the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors address the scalability concerns, provide a comparison with XGBoost (or similar), and strengthen the justification for SSGD.
