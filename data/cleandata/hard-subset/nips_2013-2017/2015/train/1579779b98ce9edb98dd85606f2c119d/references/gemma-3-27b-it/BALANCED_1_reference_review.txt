This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The core idea is to introduce binary latent variables representing split decisions and formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors address the computational complexity issue arising from the exponential growth of latent variables with tree depth, reducing it from O(2dp) to O(d²p). The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, experiments on benchmark datasets, and comparisons with greedy baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formulation of decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables is a significant and innovative contribution. It provides a fresh perspective and opens up new avenues for optimization.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including the derivation of the convex-concave upper bound and the complexity reduction. The proofs are well-structured and contribute to the understanding of the method.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  Addressing the computational bottleneck associated with the naive implementation is critical, and the reduction to O(d²p) complexity makes the approach practical for deeper trees.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on multiple datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, consistently outperforming greedy baselines and showing improved generalization performance. The ablation studies (e.g., varying ν) provide useful insights.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details, making it accessible to readers familiar with machine learning and structured prediction.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately positions the work within the context of existing decision tree algorithms and structured prediction techniques, highlighting the advantages of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the theoretical complexity is reduced to O(d²p), the implementation details appear intricate, potentially making it difficult for others to reproduce and build upon. The code is not available, which hinders reproducibility.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the regularization parameter ν, requiring careful tuning. A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity analysis and guidelines for selecting appropriate values of ν would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons to greedy algorithms are thorough, the paper lacks a comparison to more recent and sophisticated tree-based methods, such as Extremely Randomized Trees or gradient boosting machines (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM).
* **Scalability Concerns (beyond d²p):** While reducing complexity to d²p is a significant improvement, the scalability to very large datasets with high dimensionality (p) might still be a concern, especially for very deep trees (d). This should be discussed.
* **Detailed Discussion of SSGD:** The Stable SGD (SSGD) algorithm is mentioned, but the benefits and differences from standard SGD are not fully elucidated. Providing a more detailed explanation of its rationale and experimental results would strengthen the paper.
* **Assumption of Linear Split Functions:** The reliance on linear split functions (sgn(wiTx)) may limit the expressiveness of the trees. Exploring non-linear splits, or methods to approximate non-linear splits with linear ones, would be a valuable extension.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to decision tree learning.
* The method consistently outperforms existing greedy baselines in empirical evaluations.
* The computational complexity reduction makes the approach practical for deeper trees.
* The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the technical details.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of decision tree learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The implementation complexity and lack of available code hinder reproducibility.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is limited.
* Scalability to very large datasets remains a potential concern.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameters requires further investigation and guidance.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The authors present a novel and theoretically well-grounded approach to non-greedy decision tree learning. The empirical results are promising, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, the complexity of implementation, limited comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods, and the lack of code availability are concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1. **Provide code:** Making the code publicly available will greatly enhance reproducibility and facilitate further research.
2. **Expand comparisons:** Include comparisons to more recent and sophisticated tree-based methods (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM).
3. **Discuss scalability:**  Provide a more detailed discussion of the scalability concerns, especially with respect to high-dimensional data.
4. **Elaborate on SSGD:** Provide a more in-depth explanation of the SSGD algorithm, including its rationale and experimental results.
5. **Guidance on ν:** Offer more practical guidance on selecting appropriate values for the regularization parameter ν.
