Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy method to train oblique decision trees by jointly optimizing all split parameters and leaf predictors under a global objective. The key idea is to reinterpret the vector of split outcomes at internal nodes as a latent binary variable h ∈ {−1,+1}^m and to upper-bound the empirical loss with a convex-concave surrogate inspired by structured prediction with latent variables (e.g., latent structural SVMs). The surrogate involves loss-augmented inference over h. A naïve implementation is exponential in depth, so the authors introduce a radius-1 Hamming-ball restriction that yields a tractable O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference per SGD step, while inference at test time remains O(dp). They regularize each split’s weight vector via per-row norm constraints, and propose a “Stable SGD” that freezes data-to-leaf assignments for several updates to avoid leaf inactivation. Experiments on several LibSVM multi-class datasets compare single non-greedy trees to axis-aligned greedy trees (information gain), OC1-style oblique trees, random oblique trees, and CO2 (a greedy special case of their bound). The non-greedy method shows improved test accuracy across depths, reduced overfitting, and substantially better training scalability relative to the exact (exponential) loss-augmented inference.

This work relates to: classic greedy tree induction (ID3/CART), oblique trees (OC1), SVM-based split learning and margin-based trees (Bennett et al.), soft trees/HME (Jordan & Jacobs), structured prediction and margin-rescaling (Taskar; Tsochantaridis; Yu & Joachims), and CO2 (a per-node continuous optimization). The contribution is to cast hard-split trees into the latent structured framework with an efficiently optimizable surrogate that preserves O(dp) inference.

Quality
- Theory: The formulation of hard splits via argmax over latent h and the upper bound on the loss are sensible and consistent with latent structural SVMs. The proposition about tightening with scale is standard; the use of per-row norm constraints to avoid trivial minima and to regularize is reasonable. The fast (radius-1) bound remains a valid upper bound; however, the induced approximation error relative to the full latent problem is not analyzed. No generalization guarantees are provided.
- Algorithms: The SGD and projection steps are straightforward; the loss-augmented inference procedure is clearly described, and the computational savings are well-motivated. SSGD is a practical heuristic to prevent leaf collapse, but its schedule and convergence behavior are not theoretically characterized.
- Empirics: The study focuses on single-tree performance across depths and shows consistent gains and less overfitting vs. greedy baselines and CO2. The runtime comparison between exact and fast loss-augmented inference convincingly demonstrates scalability. However, the experimental scope is limited: datasets are moderate in size, hyperparameter/search details are brief, and stronger baselines (e.g., boosted trees, modern oblique/regularized trees, or even forests) are not included. Ablations (e.g., effect of ν, radius-1 vs. full bound, SGD vs. SSGD) are only partially shown.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The latent-variable formulation, navigation function f, and the two bounds (global and Hamming-ball) are presented cleanly. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement; that is fine, but adding pseudocode for loss-augmented inference and a clearer description of SSGD’s update schedule would aid reproducibility. Hyperparameter tuning and initialization choices are described but could be more detailed.

Originality
Framing hard decision trees within a latent structured prediction objective and deriving an efficiently optimizable upper bound that preserves O(dp) inference is novel and elegant. The fast loss-augmented inference via Hamming balls is a neat idea. Connections to CO2 and latent SVMs are explicit; the contribution goes beyond prior greedy per-node continuous optimization by enabling joint non-greedy training.

Significance
The ability to train non-greedy oblique trees efficiently is potentially impactful, especially for compact, accurate single trees. The formulation could catalyze extensions (sparsity, kernels, multiway splits). That said, many applications rely on ensembles (RF/GBDT); without experiments showing benefits in forests or boosted settings, the practical impact is somewhat constrained. The empirical improvements, while consistent, appear modest on the tested datasets.

Strengths
- Clear and principled latent-structured formulation for hard decision trees.
- Sound surrogate objective with an efficient O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference.
- Maintains O(dp) test-time complexity.
- Per-row norm regularization provides a principled control of fit; SSGD is a useful practical addition.
- Consistent gains over greedy baselines and CO2 across depths; evidence of reduced overfitting.

Weaknesses
- No theoretical guarantees (e.g., generalization bounds, convergence properties of SSGD, or analysis of the approximation gap between full and radius-1 bounds).
- Experimental scope limited: mostly single trees on standard datasets; lacks comparisons to strong ensemble baselines (RF/GBDT), and to more recent oblique-tree methods with regularization.
- Sensitivity to initialization and to ν not deeply explored; limited ablations (e.g., full vs. fast bound).
- Reproducibility details (SGD schedule, mini-batches, momentum, exact hyperparameter grids, code availability) are sparse.

Questions and suggestions
- Please provide an ablation of the radius-1 fast bound vs. the full bound: effect on training time, final objective value, and test accuracy across depths.
- Analyze sensitivity to initialization (e.g., random, axis-aligned, CO2) and ν; report leaf activation statistics and how SSGD affects them; include convergence plots of the surrogate objective.
- Compare to strong ensemble baselines (e.g., RF, GBDT) under matched model size (number of leaf parameters) and to oblique forests (e.g., CO2 forests), even if your emphasis is single trees.
- Discuss or experiment with sparsity-inducing regularizers on W (e.g., per-row l1 or group lasso) to yield interpretable and fast splits.
- Clarify SSGD schedule: how long are assignments frozen, what triggers reassignment, and what is the overhead?
- Report wall-clock training/inference times and memory across depths and datasets; include class-count scaling.
- Consider evaluating regression tasks and/or multiway splits to demonstrate generality.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, original formulation that bridges decision tree learning with latent structured prediction.
- Efficient algorithm with a clear computational advantage and preserved inference efficiency.
- Consistent empirical improvements over relevant greedy baselines; reduced overfitting.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical scope and missing comparisons to widely used strong baselines (RF/GBDT).
- Lack of theoretical analysis of approximation gap and generalization.
- Practical heuristics (SSGD) not well-analyzed; some reproducibility details missing.

Recommendation: Weak reject
This is a thoughtful and promising approach with solid technical ideas and encouraging results. However, to meet the NeurIPS standard, the paper should broaden and deepen the empirical evaluation (including stronger baselines and ablations) and improve analysis and clarity around the fast bound and SSGD. I encourage resubmission after addressing these points.
