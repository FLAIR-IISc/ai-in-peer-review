

# Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

This paper presents a fundamentally novel approach to decision tree learning by breaking away from the long-standing greedy paradigm. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, proposing a framework for joint optimization of split functions across the entire tree under a global objective. Their key technical contribution is a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient non-greedy learning, with computational complexity reduced from exponential O(2^dp) to O(d²p) through a clever reformulation.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, clearly distinguishing itself from prior non-greedy approaches like Bennett's multi-linear programming method [1] (which is limited to binary classification and small trees) and Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13] (which sacrifices computational efficiency for smoothness). The authors properly acknowledge these limitations while demonstrating how their approach overcomes them.

**Strengths:**

1. **High novelty and boldness:** This is a genuinely new direction for decision tree learning, challenging the decades-old greedy paradigm that has dominated the field. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is elegant and insightful, offering a fresh perspective on a classic problem.

2. **Technical contribution:** The development of the efficient upper bound and the O(d²p) algorithm represents a significant technical achievement. The proof of the bound and analysis of the regularization effect (Proposition 1) are particularly strong.

3. **Empirical validation:** The experiments across multiple benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) consistently show that non-greedy trees achieve better generalization performance than greedy baselines, with less susceptibility to overfitting. The ablation study of the regularization parameter ν (Fig. 3) provides useful insights into how the method tunes tree complexity.

4. **Clear presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to solution to evaluation. The algorithm descriptions are precise and the complexity analysis is thorough.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The focus on binary trees, while justified as a starting point, limits the immediate applicability to some real-world scenarios where multi-way splits might be beneficial.

2. The comparison with state-of-the-art tree ensembles (like random forests or gradient boosted trees) is limited, though the authors note that the key differences between non-greedy and greedy trees are most apparent in single trees.

3. While the paper demonstrates efficiency gains for moderate tree depths, the scalability to extremely large trees or massive datasets (beyond Connect4's 55k samples) isn't fully explored.

**Recommendation:**

This paper should be accepted. It represents a significant conceptual advance in decision tree learning that is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The authors have successfully overcome a fundamental limitation of traditional decision trees through a novel approach that connects to broader machine learning frameworks. While the experimental scope could be expanded, the core contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

The paper's novelty is particularly compelling because it doesn't merely tweak existing methods but proposes a fundamentally different learning paradigm. As a reviewer who prioritizes bold new directions over incremental improvements, I find this paper to be exactly the kind of contribution that NIPS should celebrate.

The technical content is rigorous, the writing is clear, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over the standard greedy approach. The authors have also appropriately acknowledged limitations and positioned their work within the broader literature.

This paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. It offers a genuinely new perspective on a foundational machine learning problem with the potential to influence future work in tree-based methods. The non-greedy approach opens up new avenues for research that could lead to better-performing, more interpretable tree-based models across various applications.
