Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a global surrogate objective via stochastic gradient descent. The key technical idea is to upper-bound the empirical loss with a convex-concave (piecewise linear) surrogate inspired by structured prediction with latent variables. Each input induces a latent binary vector h over internal nodes; the loss is upper-bounded by a loss-augmented inference term minus the inference score. The naive loss-augmented inference is exponential in depth; the paper shows (i) how to reduce it to O(mp) by exploiting the tree structure and (ii) a modified surrogate that restricts loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), yielding O(d^2 p). The model uses per-row norm constraints on W to regularize, motivated by a proposition that the bound tightens with larger ‖W‖ but becomes uninformative in the limit. Training uses SGD with projection; a “Stable SGD” variant fixes leaf assignments for several steps to avoid leaf inactivity. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare single-tree performance against axis-aligned CART, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and CO2 (a greedy depth-1 continuous optimization baseline). The proposed method generally yields higher test accuracy across depths and is less prone to overfitting; the fast loss-augmented inference scales better with depth.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects decision tree learning to latent structured prediction (Tsochantaridis et al.; Yu & Joachims) and margin-based learning. It contrasts with soft-split HMEs (Jordan & Jacobs), which lose test-time efficiency, and with prior non-greedy/global tree optimizations (Bennett et al.) that are computationally prohibitive. It also relates to max-margin tree splits and the authors’ CO2 method; here CO2 is positioned as a depth-1 special case while this work provides joint non-greedy optimization over the full tree.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clean latent-variable structured prediction formulation for hard-split decision trees, with a principled surrogate objective and regularization.
- Technical contributions: (a) a simple yet effective upper bound for decision-tree loss; (b) a fast, radius-1 Hamming-ball variant enabling O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference; (c) practical SGD with projection and a stabilizing SSGD heuristic.
- Maintains tree inference efficiency O(dp) while enabling joint training.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over strong greedy baselines across depths and datasets; method appears less prone to overfitting.
- Clear path to extensions (sparsity-inducing regularization, kernels).

Weaknesses
- The final training objective remains non-convex; no convergence guarantees beyond standard SGD behavior. The effect of the radius-1 restriction on optimization quality versus the full bound is not quantified.
- Experimental evaluation focuses on single trees. While justified to isolate learning effects, a comparison to widely used ensembles (random forests, gradient boosting) would contextualize significance and practical impact.
- Limited ablations: the contribution of SSGD vs. vanilla SGD, and sensitivity to ν and other hyperparameters, are discussed but not rigorously quantified (e.g., variance across runs, stability of active leaves).
- Some complexity claims are presented sequentially (O(2^d p) → O(mp) → O(d^2 p)); the narrative could be streamlined to avoid confusion and to clarify when each bound applies.
- Reproducibility details (mini-batch size, momentum parameters, stopping criteria, number of epochs, initialization variance) are sparse; figures rather than tables limit precise comparisons.

Quality
- The surrogate bound (Eq. 7) is sound and standard in latent structured prediction; the radius-1 variant (Eq. 13) remains a valid upper bound because ĥ is within the ball. Proposition 1 is intuitive and motivates per-row norm constraints. The algorithmic derivations for loss-augmented inference along root-to-leaf paths are correct and lead to the claimed complexities.
- The method is complete and implementable. The main caveat is the non-convexity and the introduction of SSGD (a heuristic) without a deeper analysis; nonetheless, the empirical evidence supports effectiveness.
- Experimental claims (accuracy improvements and reduced overfitting) are plausible and supported by plots, but numeric tables and statistical significance would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- Overall well-written and organized; the latent-variable viewpoint and f(g) navigation function are clearly explained. Algorithm 1 is helpful.
- Suggestions: clarify early that O(2^d p) is the naive complexity before structural constraints, then present O(mp) and the O(d^2 p) variant. Provide pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference. Include more training details for reproducibility and a short discussion of how often leaves become inactive and how SSGD schedules updates.

Originality
- The non-greedy, global training of hard trees via a latent structured surrogate and the radius-1 loss-augmented inference are novel and distinct from prior oblique/greedy or soft-split approaches. The connection to CO2 is appropriate; this work is a meaningful generalization.

Significance
- Addressing non-greedy, globally trained decision trees is important; the method advances the state of the art for single-tree learning and may benefit tree ensembles when used as a base learner. The fast inference trick makes the approach practical for deeper trees. Impact would be higher with evidence on ensembles and larger-scale/high-dimensional settings.

Constructive suggestions
- Provide ablations: (i) full vs radius-1 bound; (ii) SGD vs SSGD; (iii) effect of ν and projection radius on generalization and leaf activity; (iv) initialization sensitivity.
- Add numeric tables with means/standard deviations; include wall-clock training times and memory for all baselines.
- Compare against gradient-boosted trees or at least report where single-tree accuracy stands relative to small ensembles.
- Explore sparsity-inducing regularization on W to improve interpretability and speed.
- Discuss potential convergence diagnostics and criteria for SSGD’s reassignment schedule.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled formulation; non-trivial technical contribution enabling efficient joint training.
- Consistent empirical gains and reduced overfitting across datasets and depths.
- Maintains test-time efficiency and opens avenues for further research (sparse/kernalized splits).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited breadth of empirical evaluation (no ensembles; few ablations; lack of statistical rigor).
- Heuristic elements (SSGD) without theoretical backing.
- Reproducibility details and numerical reporting could be improved.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
Reviewer confidence: High on methodology/learning theory; medium on empirical breadth due to limited reporting.
