Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees that jointly optimizes all split functions under a global objective. The key idea is to reinterpret the vector of split decisions as latent variables h ∈ {−1,+1}^m, linking tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that serves as a surrogate objective, enabling stochastic gradient descent (SGD) updates of the split parameters W and leaf parameters Θ. They further introduce a computationally efficient variant by restricting loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current split decisions, reducing per-update complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) for depth d and input dimension p. A “Stable SGD” (SSGD) variant freezes data-to-leaf assignments for several steps to avoid deactivating leaves prematurely.

This work connects classical greedy decision tree induction (ID3, CART) and oblique trees (e.g., OC1) to max-margin and structured prediction methods (Taskar et al., Tsochantaridis et al., Yu & Joachims), and contrasts with probabilistic soft trees such as Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts (Jordan & Jacobs), preserving O(dp) inference with hard routing. It also relates to margin-based trees (Bennett et al.) and to CO2 (Norouzi et al.), which can be seen as a depth-1 special case. The method accommodates general losses (e.g., log loss, squared loss), uses per-row norm constraints on W to regularize and control the tightness of the bound, and demonstrates empirical improvements over greedy baselines on several LibSVM datasets.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The latent-variable reformulation sgn(Wx) = argmax_h h^T Wx is correct and aligns with prior latent SVM formulations. The derivation of an upper bound on the loss via loss-augmented inference is standard in structural SVMs; the bound in Eq. (7) is plausible, and the restricted bound in Eq. (13) remains an upper bound, trading tightness for efficiency. Proposition 1 formally clarifies the effect of scaling W on bound tightness, justifying norm constraints. The loss-augmented inference exploits tree structure to achieve O(d^2 p), which is technically appealing.
- Algorithmic details: The SGD update is straightforward, with projection enforcing per-node norm constraints; the gradient structure is sparse along the root-to-leaf path. The SSGD variant is intuitive, but the schedule for freezing/updating leaf assignments and stopping criteria need clearer specification for reproducibility.
- Experiments: The comparisons cover axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, and CO2 baselines, with per-depth hyperparameter tuning. Results (as described) show improved test accuracy across depths and datasets, reduced overfitting via ν-regularization, and favorable runtime scaling for fast loss-augmented inference. However, quantitative results are summarized via figures; numeric tables, statistical variability, and wall-clock times are not provided in the text excerpt.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized: problem formulation, connection to latent structured prediction, derivation of the surrogate, and optimization are clearly delineated. The intuition behind the bounds and the effect of scaling are well motivated. The description of loss-augmented inference leveraging tree paths is helpful. Areas for improved clarity:
- Provide explicit pseudocode and complexity for fast loss-augmented inference (Eq. (13)) and how the d+1 candidate leaves are enumerated from a single DFS.
- Elaborate the SSGD schedule: how long assignments are fixed, how “negligible improvement” is defined, and how assignment updates affect convergence.
- Include explicit hyperparameters (batch size, momentum), number of epochs/steps, and initialization details beyond “CO2” for full reproducibility.

Originality
The central conceptual novelty is the explicit latent-structure formulation of decision-tree training with a structural SVM-style upper bound, combined with an efficient loss-augmented inference scheme that exploits the tree’s path structure and a Hamming-1 relaxation. Prior non-greedy global tree optimization methods (e.g., MILP, OC1, max-margin nodes) did not provide this latent-variable perspective with general losses and efficient joint optimization. CO2 is identified as a depth-1 special case; the joint, non-greedy optimization across depths appears to be new. The SSGD stabilization is a practical contribution.

Significance
Decision trees and forests are widely used; improving single-tree training via a principled, scalable, non-greedy optimization is potentially impactful. The method preserves hard routing and inference efficiency, supports general losses, and empirically improves test accuracy and generalization across depths. The efficiency gain from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) for the learning inner loop is significant for deeper trees. That said, the experimental scope focuses on single trees and medium-scale datasets; demonstrating benefits in ensembles (forests/boosting), or on larger-scale tasks, would further strengthen significance.

Strengths
- Clear and principled formulation via latent structured prediction and upper bounds.
- Efficient loss-augmented inference exploiting tree paths; scalable O(d^2 p) updates.
- Regularization via per-node norm constraints with intuitive effect on overfitting and pruning.
- Supports general losses and maintains O(dp) inference at test time.
- Empirical improvements over multiple greedy baselines across depths; runtime scaling analysis.

Weaknesses
- Some theoretical elements deferred to the supplement; no convergence guarantees beyond standard SGD heuristics.
- SSGD algorithmic details (schedules/criteria) under-specified; potential sensitivity not analyzed.
- Experimental reporting lacks numeric tables, variance, and statistical tests; limited discussion of wall-clock times and memory.
- Baselines do not include strong modern tree ensembles (e.g., boosted trees), though the authors justify focusing on single trees.
- The Hamming-1 bound may be a relatively loose surrogate; ablation comparing full vs. restricted loss-augmented inference on small trees would be informative.

Questions for the authors
- Please detail the SSGD schedule and criteria, and report sensitivity analyses.
- Provide numeric accuracy and runtime tables (with standard deviations) and significance tests.
- Can you compare against a simple joint fine-tuning baseline (e.g., alternating optimization of nodes on fixed assignments) to isolate the benefit of the bound?
- How sensitive are results to ν and η across datasets? Any principled guidance for ν selection?
- Can you report the fraction of inactive leaves over training and its correlation with generalization?

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, novel formulation connecting trees to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient and scalable optimization with hard routing preserved.
- Broad applicability to different losses; practical SSGD variant.
- Consistent empirical gains over standard greedy baselines.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited quantitative experimental depth and lack of variance/statistical analysis.
- Missing comparisons to strong ensemble baselines; impact on forest/boosting settings not shown.
- Some implementation details and theoretical justifications left to supplement; SSGD specifics unclear.
- Potential looseness of the Hamming-1 bound not empirically ablated.
