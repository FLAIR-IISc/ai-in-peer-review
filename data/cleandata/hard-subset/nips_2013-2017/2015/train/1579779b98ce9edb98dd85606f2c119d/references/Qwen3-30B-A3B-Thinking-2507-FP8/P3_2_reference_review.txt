

# Peer Review: "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary and Context
This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a novel connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, introducing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The key innovation is an algorithm with O(d²p) complexity (compared to the naive O(2^dp)), making deep tree learning feasible while achieving better generalization than greedy methods. The work builds on prior efforts like Bennett's global optimization (1) and hierarchical mixture of experts (13), but addresses their limitations in computational complexity and applicability to general loss functions.

## Strengths

**Technical Quality and Originality (9/10):** The paper makes a strong technical contribution with a well-motivated formulation linking decision trees to structured prediction. The upper bound derivation is theoretically sound, and the efficient algorithm with O(d²p) complexity represents a significant improvement over previous approaches. The connection to structured prediction is novel and well-executed, with clear mathematical justification. The authors properly contextualize their work against related literature, including Bennett's NP-hardness results (11) and the limitations of soft-split approaches (13).

**Clarity (8/10):** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation, technical derivations, and algorithmic details are presented with precision. The supplementary material appears to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The connection between decision trees and structured prediction is particularly well-explained.

**Significance (8/10):** Decision trees are fundamental to machine learning, with widespread applications in industry (e.g., Microsoft Kinect as cited in the paper). The proposed method addresses a long-standing limitation of greedy tree induction while maintaining the computational efficiency of tree structures. The empirical results across multiple benchmark datasets demonstrate consistent improvements in generalization performance, making this work practically significant.

## Weaknesses

**Experimental Scope (6/10):** While the experiments show promise, they are limited to classification tasks and binary trees. The paper mentions regression but doesn't provide substantive results for this important application. More importantly, the comparison with state-of-the-art methods is incomplete. The authors compare primarily with greedy baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees) but omit recent approaches like gradient-boosted trees (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM), which have become the de facto standard for tree-based learning. A comparison with these methods would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about generalization performance.

**Theoretical Justification (7/10):** The paper convincingly shows that the non-greedy approach generalizes better, but the theoretical explanation for why this occurs is somewhat superficial. The claim that "cooperation between split nodes" leads to better generalization lacks concrete theoretical analysis. A deeper investigation into how the non-greedy optimization affects the bias-variance tradeoff would be valuable.

**Computational Considerations (7/10):** While the O(d²p) complexity is an improvement, the paper doesn't sufficiently address how this scales to extremely large datasets or very deep trees. The computational complexity analysis assumes d is small, but in practice, tree depth can grow significantly. The paper mentions Microsoft Kinect as an application, but doesn't provide concrete runtime metrics for large-scale applications.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to decision tree learning. The core idea of using structured prediction with latent variables to formulate non-greedy tree learning is novel and well-executed. The O(d²p) complexity improvement is significant, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over greedy methods.

However, the paper would be substantially stronger with a more comprehensive experimental comparison against modern tree-based methods (particularly gradient-boosted trees), expanded to include regression tasks, and with deeper theoretical justification for the observed improvements in generalization.

The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS as it presents a novel algorithmic approach to a fundamental machine learning problem with clear theoretical and practical significance. The technical quality is excellent, and the contribution advances the state of the art in tree-based learning.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper's core contribution is strong and well-executed, but the experimental section requires expansion to address the limitations noted above. The authors should:

1. Add comparison with gradient-boosted tree methods (XGBoost, LightGBM) to demonstrate the relative advantage of their approach
2. Include regression experiments to broaden the scope
3. Provide deeper theoretical analysis of why the non-greedy approach improves generalization
4. Discuss scalability to very large datasets in more detail

The paper has significant potential to influence both theoretical and practical approaches to decision tree learning, making it a valuable contribution to the NeurIPS community.
