

# Peer Review: "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a link between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, introducing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity of loss-augmented inference from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) through a novel Hamming ball constraint. The method outperforms greedy baselines in generalization performance across multiple benchmark datasets while maintaining the computational efficiency of tree-based methods. This work extends prior art like CO2 [18] (a greedy variant of their method) and Bennett's multi-linear programming approach [1], while addressing its computational limitations.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation, including a proof of the upper bound and an analysis of how the regularization parameter ν affects the solution. The complexity analysis is thorough, clearly demonstrating how the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm achieves O(d²p) complexity. The experimental section is comprehensive, comparing against multiple relevant baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2) across several benchmark datasets. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the need for careful tuning of ν, and demonstrate how it implicitly controls pruning.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction is clearly established, and the technical details are presented with appropriate mathematical precision. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear and well-structured. The supplementary material appears to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The relationship between tree depth d and the number of internal nodes m (which is exponential in d) is properly addressed.

**Originality:** The formulation of decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables represents a significant conceptual advance. The authors' key technical contribution—reducing the computational complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth—demonstrates a novel solution to a long-standing challenge in non-greedy tree learning. While CO2 [18] is a special case of their approach applied greedily, this paper's non-greedy optimization framework is substantially different and more general. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, distinguishing from prior work on non-greedy tree learning.

**Significance:** This work addresses a fundamental limitation of decision trees—their greedy nature—that has persisted for decades despite their widespread use. The results demonstrate improved generalization across multiple datasets, suggesting practical value for practitioners. The framework's flexibility (handling any loss function) and potential for future extensions (sparse learning, kernel methods) enhance its significance. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient method for non-greedy tree learning.

## Weaknesses

**Experimental Comparison:** While the paper compares against several baselines, it does not include a direct comparison with the most recent non-greedy tree methods, such as the Mondrian Forests [15] or Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13]. The comparison with CO2 is valuable for highlighting the non-greedy aspect, but additional baselines would strengthen the claims.

**Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper acknowledges that ν controls the tightness of the upper bound and implicitly controls pruning, but it doesn't provide sufficient evidence on how sensitive the performance is to this parameter. A more thorough exploration of the hyperparameter space would be beneficial.

**Scalability on Very Large Datasets:** While the computational complexity analysis is strong, the experimental section doesn't demonstrate scalability on extremely large datasets (beyond the 55,000 examples in Connect4). The paper claims "much larger speedup factors for larger datasets," but this would be more convincing with empirical evidence.

**Theoretical Limitations:** The paper focuses on binary trees, and while it mentions extensions to N-ary trees are possible, it doesn't provide a clear path or analysis for how the complexity would scale with N. For some applications, N-ary trees might be preferable.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. It successfully addresses a fundamental limitation of conventional decision trees through a novel theoretical framework that is both computationally efficient and empirically effective. The technical contributions are substantial, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the writing is clear and precise.

The paper advances the state of the art by providing the first efficient non-greedy optimization method for decision trees that can handle general loss functions while maintaining computational efficiency. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is both elegant and fruitful, opening up new avenues for future research.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field. The method is novel, well-justified theoretically, and demonstrates superior empirical performance compared to relevant baselines. The authors have addressed important limitations of previous approaches while maintaining the computational advantages of decision trees. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a substantial contribution that will likely influence future work on decision tree learning.

The minor weaknesses noted (particularly regarding additional baselines and hyperparameter sensitivity) do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. The authors could address these in the camera-ready version, but they are not sufficient to warrant rejection.

This work aligns well with NIPS's focus on theoretical and practical advances in machine learning, and it would be a valuable contribution to the conference proceedings.
