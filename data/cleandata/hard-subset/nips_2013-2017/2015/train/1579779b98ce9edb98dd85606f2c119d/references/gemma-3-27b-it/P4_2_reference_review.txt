This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The key idea is to introduce a latent variable for each split, allowing for joint optimization of split functions across the tree, potentially leading to more compact and generalizable trees. The authors develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss and utilize stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for optimization, addressing computational complexity with a reduction from O(2dp) to O(d2p).

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of greedy decision tree induction and positioning their work as a solution. The connection to structured prediction is insightful and provides a strong theoretical foundation. The proposed upper bound and the reduction in computational complexity are significant technical contributions. The experimental evaluation on benchmark datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach, showcasing improved test performance and reduced overfitting compared to greedy baselines. The ablation study showing the impact of the regularization parameter ν is particularly valuable.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. While the paper establishes a link to structured prediction, a more detailed discussion of existing structured prediction methods and their applicability to decision trees (beyond the cited references) would be beneficial. The experiments, while comprehensive, are somewhat limited in scope. Exploring different datasets, loss functions, or tree structures (e.g., varying maximum depth more drastically) could provide further insights. The description of the initialization procedure (using a greedy tree to initialize W and Θ) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more principled initialization method, or a discussion of its impact on performance, would be helpful.  The algorithm presented in the supplementary material (Alg 1) and described in the main text needs better referencing and clearer explanation.  The explanation of Stable SGD (SSGD) is also relatively brief; elaborating on the intuition behind its effectiveness would strengthen the paper.  The discussion of related work, while covering key papers, is sometimes cursory. For example, the work of Caruana and Nicoloau on greedy randomized growing decision trees [1] is absent, despite being a relevant and influential contribution. The authors claim their formulation can handle any general loss function, yet the experiments only showcase binary cross-entropy and squared loss. Expanding the experimental evaluation to other loss functions would bolster this claim.  Finally, while the authors mention potential future work involving kernel methods, a more concrete discussion of the challenges and benefits of such an approach would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The non-greedy approach to decision tree learning, framed as a structured prediction problem, is a novel and potentially impactful contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed upper bound and the complexity reduction from O(2dp) to O(d2p) are technically sound and well-justified.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to established greedy baselines.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the main ideas and contributions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, exploring a wider range of datasets, loss functions, and tree structures.
*   **Lack of Detailed Comparison to Structured Prediction:** A more detailed discussion of existing structured prediction methods is needed.
*   **Somewhat Ad-hoc Initialization:** The initialization procedure is not fully justified.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed non-greedy approach, grounded in structured prediction, is both novel and technically sound. While the experiments could be more extensive, the results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the method. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, I suggest expanding the experimental section to include additional datasets and loss functions, and providing a more detailed discussion of the connection to existing structured prediction methods.
