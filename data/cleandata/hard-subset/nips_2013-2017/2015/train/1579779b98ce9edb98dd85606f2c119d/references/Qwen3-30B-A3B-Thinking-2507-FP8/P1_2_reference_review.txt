

# Peer Review: "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

## Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy framework for decision tree learning that jointly optimizes split functions at all levels of the tree under a global objective, rather than the conventional greedy approach that optimizes one node at a time. The authors establish a novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating the problem using binary latent decision variables at each split node. They develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), with a key technical contribution being the reduction of computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) for gradient updates. The paper also introduces Stable SGD (SSGD) to prevent inactive leaves during training. This work builds on but significantly extends prior non-greedy approaches like Bennett's multi-linear programming method [1] and the CO2 algorithm [18], which is a special case of their upper bound approach applied greedily.

## Strengths

**Technical Quality and Novelty (High):** The paper makes a significant technical contribution by establishing a theoretical connection to structured prediction with latent variables and developing an efficient optimization algorithm. The proof of the upper bound inequality (7) and Proposition 1 are sound, and the complexity analysis is thorough. The key innovation of reducing complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth (O(d^2p) vs O(2^dp)) is well-justified and represents a major practical advancement over previous non-greedy approaches.

**Experimental Validation (High):** The experimental section provides strong empirical evidence supporting the theoretical claims. The comparison across multiple benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) demonstrates consistent improvements in generalization performance over greedy baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees) and the CO2 baseline. The authors carefully analyze the effect of the regularization parameter ν on tree structure and generalization, showing how it implicitly controls pruning.

**Theoretical Foundation (High):** The paper successfully places decision tree learning within a well-characterized optimization framework. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and provides a principled way to handle the discrete nature of decision trees. The regularization approach through the norm constraint on W is theoretically sound and practically effective.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity of Key Concepts (Moderate):** While the paper is generally well-written, some key concepts could be clarified. The tree navigation function f(h) and the Hamming ball concept (B_1(sgn(Wx))) are not sufficiently illustrated with concrete examples. A simple diagram showing how the Hamming ball relates to the tree structure would greatly improve understanding. Additionally, the notation for the latent variable h and its relationship to the tree path could be made more intuitive.

**Comparison with Recent Work (Moderate):** The paper cites relevant prior work but could better distinguish itself from recent advances in decision tree learning. For example, it does not discuss the work of [23] (which also uses structured prediction for tree learning) or the more recent methods like [24] (which uses differentiable decision trees). The relationship to differentiable decision trees [25] is not addressed, though the authors' method is fundamentally different.

**Computational Complexity Claims (Moderate):** The paper claims O(d^2p) complexity for the fast loss-augmented inference, but the analysis doesn't fully account for the depth-first search required to solve for all possible leaf paths. The actual complexity might be higher for very deep trees, and the authors should provide more concrete analysis of the constant factors involved.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The non-greedy framework addresses a fundamental limitation of conventional greedy approaches, and the computational efficiency achieved through the fast loss-augmented inference makes the method practically applicable to deeper trees. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the experimental results consistently demonstrate improved generalization performance across multiple datasets.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in decision tree learning. While the clarity of some key concepts could be improved, the technical content is rigorous and well-supported. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is particularly elegant and opens up potential for future work in this direction.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper's contributions are significant, well-supported by both theory and experiments, and address a fundamental limitation in a widely used machine learning method. The proposed framework provides a principled approach to non-greedy decision tree learning that outperforms existing methods in terms of generalization performance while maintaining computational efficiency.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a simple diagram illustrating the tree navigation function f(h) and the Hamming ball concept.
2. Clarify the relationship between this work and recent differentiable decision tree approaches [25].
3. Provide more detailed analysis of the constant factors in the O(d^2p) complexity claim.
4. Add a brief discussion of how the method might be extended to handle missing data or categorical features.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Summary

**Pro:**
- Novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables
- Significant computational complexity improvement (O(d^2p) vs O(2^dp))
- Strong experimental results demonstrating better generalization than greedy methods
- Well-justified regularization approach through norm constraints on W

**Con:**
- Some key concepts could be better illustrated with examples/diagrams
- Could better distinguish from recent work in differentiable decision trees
- Complexity analysis could be more precise regarding constant factors

The paper is an excellent contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning and would be of significant interest to the NIPS community. The combination of theoretical insight, practical efficiency, and empirical validation makes this a strong candidate for acceptance.
