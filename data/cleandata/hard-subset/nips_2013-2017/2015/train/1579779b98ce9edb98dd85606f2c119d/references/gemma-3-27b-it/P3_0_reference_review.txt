## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing problem of non-greedy optimization of decision trees, a limitation of traditional algorithms like ID3 and CART. The authors propose a novel framework that casts decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, allowing for joint optimization of split functions across the tree. They derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is reducing the gradient complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p), crucial for deep trees. The paper provides empirical validation on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating improved performance compared to greedy baselines and a related greedy approach (CO2).

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges the NP-completeness of optimal tree construction [11] and reviews relevant approaches like Bennett's multi-linear programming [1], Mondrian Processes [15], and Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13].  It distinguishes itself by offering a potentially scalable, general-purpose solution applicable to any loss function while maintaining a reasonable inference complexity. The link to structured prediction with latent variables [25] is a strong contribution, bringing techniques from that field to bear on decision tree learning. The connection to CO2 [18] is also well-defined, allowing for a focused comparison of the benefits of non-greedy optimization. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:**  Framing decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a clever and theoretically sound approach. This unlocks the potential for applying powerful optimization techniques developed in the structured prediction community.
* **Scalability:** The reduction in gradient complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a significant practical contribution. It directly addresses a major bottleneck in applying non-greedy methods to deeper trees.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements in test accuracy compared to several strong baselines across multiple datasets.  The ablation study highlighting the effect of the regularization parameter ν is particularly informative.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical derivations are presented clearly, and the algorithm description (Alg 1) is easy to follow.
* **Thorough Related Work:**  The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature and accurately position their work within it.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the theoretical complexity reduction is significant, the implementation appears quite involved, especially the fast loss-augmented inference. The paper doesn't fully convey the practical challenges of implementing this algorithm, and more details on the implementation would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Ensemble Methods:**  The paper primarily compares against single-tree methods.  While the authors acknowledge the effectiveness of ensembles (bagging, boosting) in the introduction, a direct comparison with a strong, well-tuned random forest would be crucial to establish the true value of the proposed method.  The argument that comparing single trees highlights the non-greediness benefit feels somewhat weak; ultimately, practitioners care about overall performance.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the regularization parameter ν.  The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of how to choose an appropriate value for ν in practice and whether the optimal value varies significantly across datasets.
* **Assumption of Linearity:**  The split functions are limited to linear thresholds (si(x) = sgn(wiTx)). While this keeps the problem tractable, it’s a potentially limiting assumption.  The discussion of extending the approach to kernel methods is promising but remains high-level.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to non-greedy decision tree learning. The technical contributions, particularly the complexity reduction and the formulation as a structured prediction problem, are significant. The empirical results are encouraging and demonstrate the potential of the method. While the implementation complexity and limited comparison to ensemble methods are weaknesses, they don't fundamentally detract from the quality of the work. 

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* Offers a new and theoretically grounded approach to a long-standing problem in machine learning.
* Achieves significant performance gains over existing methods.
* Presents a practical optimization technique (SGD with a convex-concave bound) and addresses a key scalability concern.
*  Demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*  Implementation complexity might limit its widespread adoption.
*  Lacks a direct comparison to strong ensemble methods (e.g., Random Forests).
*  Hyperparameter sensitivity requires further investigation.




**Rating:**  Strong Accept.  

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. I recommend the authors address the reviewer concerns regarding implementation details and comparisons to ensemble methods in a final version.
