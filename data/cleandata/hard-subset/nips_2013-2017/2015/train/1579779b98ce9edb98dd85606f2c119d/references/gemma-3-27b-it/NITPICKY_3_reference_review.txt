This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and developing a surrogate objective function optimized via stochastic gradient descent. While the core idea of moving beyond greedy tree induction is valuable, and the connection to structured prediction is interesting, the paper is riddled with minor flaws and inconsistencies that, cumulatively, detract from its overall impact and raise concerns about the thoroughness of the work.

The introduction establishes the motivation well, highlighting the limitations of greedy algorithms. However, the phrasing is often imprecise. For instance, the claim that recent work has “proposed new objective functions to guide greedy algorithms” (citing [20, 12]) feels understated. These papers often propose entirely different learning *frameworks*, not just objective functions *for* greedy algorithms. Similarly, the statement that the proposed method's complexity is reduced to O(d²p) is presented as a technical contribution, but the justification for this reduction isn’t sufficiently clear in the introduction itself, and requires digging into section 6.

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive but lacks critical assessment. The dismissal of Murthy and Salzburg [17] as focusing on empirical loss without regularization feels dismissive; their argument isn't simply about the lack of regularization, but the potential harms of *looking ahead* in decision tree induction. The comparison to CO2 [18] is mentioned only briefly, but a more detailed discussion of the relationship – given that CO2 is a special case of the presented approach – is warranted.

Section 3, the problem formulation, is generally clear, but several points are concerning. The assumption of linear threshold functions feels limiting given the stated goal of a “general framework”. The appended "-1" to the input feature vector to incorporate the offset parameter is a standard trick, but its presentation feels somewhat abrupt. Furthermore, the notation is occasionally inconsistent; for instance, the use of both 'j' for leaf index and 'j' within summations is confusing. The indexing starting at 1 instead of 0 is also an odd choice that adds minor cognitive load.

Section 4, linking decision trees to structured prediction, is conceptually interesting, but the mathematical justification feels somewhat hand-wavy. The explanation of the latent variable 'h' is clear, but the connection to existing structured prediction literature isn't fully explored. The statement that "one can re-express sgn(Wx) in terms of a latent variable h" feels like a re-framing of a well-understood concept without significant new insight.

Section 5, the upper bound derivation, is where the paper begins to unravel. The justification for the bound in equation (7) is asserted rather than rigorously demonstrated. The proof promised in the supplementary material is crucial, and its absence in the main text is a significant omission. Proposition 1, while intuitively reasonable, requires a more detailed proof. The move to the Hamming ball constraint in (13) is presented without sufficient motivation – why is this a tighter and more efficient bound? The algorithmic description following the derivation is brief and lacks sufficient detail.

Section 6, detailing the optimization, suffers from similar issues. The explanation of fast loss-augmented inference is confusing and relies heavily on “observations” without formal justification. The claim of O(d²p) complexity feels unsubstantiated without a clearer breakdown of the computational steps. The jump to SGD and SSGD without a more detailed discussion of parameter tuning (learning rate, momentum, etc.) feels incomplete.

Section 7, the experiments, provides some evidence of the method's effectiveness, but the experimental setup lacks detail. The specific hyperparameter settings, beyond mentioning the tuning of ν, are not reported. The comparison to baselines is reasonable, but the choice of baselines could be more comprehensive (e.g., gradient boosting trees). The discussion of the results is superficial and lacks statistical significance testing. The figures are small and difficult to read. Furthermore, claiming "empirical comparison of training time" when the x-axis of Fig. 4 is not labeled and thus the comparison is impossible is frankly unacceptable.

Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor formatting issues: inconsistent use of mathematical notation (e.g., boldface vs. non-boldface), typos (e.g., "l ∈ {1, . . . , k}" without defining what 'l' represents earlier), and a general lack of polish.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The idea of non-greedy decision tree learning is a potentially valuable direction.
*   The connection to structured prediction provides a novel perspective.
*   The experimental results suggest some improvement over greedy baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The technical details are often unclear, imprecise, or unsubstantiated.
*   The proofs are incomplete (relying on supplementary material not included).
*   The experimental setup and analysis are lacking in detail.
*   The paper is riddled with minor flaws and inconsistencies that detract from its overall quality.
*   The claims of complexity reduction (O(d²p)) aren't adequately justified.

**Overall Assessment:**

Reject. Despite the potentially interesting core idea, the numerous flaws and lack of rigor make this paper unsuitable for NIPS. The authors need to significantly revise the technical details, provide complete proofs, and conduct a more thorough experimental evaluation before resubmission. A substantial rewrite is needed to address the clarity and precision issues throughout the manuscript. The level of polish expected for a NIPS submission is simply not met here.
