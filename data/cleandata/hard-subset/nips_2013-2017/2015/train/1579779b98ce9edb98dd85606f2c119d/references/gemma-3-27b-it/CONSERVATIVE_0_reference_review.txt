This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and introducing a convex-concave upper bound optimized via stochastic gradient descent. While the motivation – overcoming the limitations of greedy tree induction – is sound and broadly relevant, the paper presents a significant amount of technical complexity without demonstrating convincingly that the gains outweigh the added difficulty, or that the method represents a substantial improvement over existing, well-established techniques.

The core idea of formulating decision tree learning as structured prediction is interesting, linking it to a broader area of research. However, the proposed optimization strategy, while theoretically motivated, feels overly complicated. The reduction of the gradient complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a welcome improvement, but the resulting O(d2p) complexity remains a potential bottleneck, particularly for deeper trees and higher-dimensional data. The authors acknowledge this, but the experimental results do not fully address the practical implications of this complexity for larger problems.

The experiments themselves are a primary weakness. While comparisons are made to several baselines, including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and CO2, the reported results are not decisively in favor of the proposed method. The improvements shown in Figure 2 appear modest, and the narrative emphasizes potential advantages (less overfitting) rather than clear, consistent performance gains. Furthermore, the dataset selection is somewhat limited; while standard benchmarks are used, the lack of evaluation on truly large-scale datasets is a concern. The parameter tuning discussion, focusing on ν, is helpful, but doesn't fully address the sensitivity of the method to other hyperparameters like the learning rate.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, but the sheer volume of technical detail can be overwhelming. The multiple propositions and algorithmic details, while rigorous, arguably detract from the overall message. A more streamlined presentation, perhaps focusing on the key insights and experimental results, would enhance clarity. Related work is adequately covered, though the dismissal of Murthy & Salzburg’s arguments as stemming from “minimizing empirical loss without regularization” feels somewhat dismissive; regularization is now standard practice even with greedy methods. 

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a long-standing limitation of greedy decision tree learning.
*   Novel formulation linking decision trees to structured prediction.
*   Technical contributions reducing gradient complexity.
*   Clear writing and organization (despite complexity).

**Weaknesses:**

*   The proposed method is complex and difficult to implement.
*   Experimental results show only modest improvements over strong baselines.
*   Limited evaluation on large-scale datasets.
*   The sensitivity of performance to hyperparameter choices (beyond ν) is not thoroughly investigated.
*   Overly dismissive of some related work.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel perspective on decision tree learning.
*   The technical contributions related to gradient complexity reduction are valuable.
*   The paper has the potential to stimulate further research in non-greedy tree induction.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The gains in performance do not clearly justify the added complexity.
*   The experimental validation is not sufficiently compelling.
*   The method may not scale well to large datasets due to the O(d2p) complexity.
*   The improvements are not significant enough to displace well-established greedy methods in practical applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. The paper presents a technically sound but ultimately incremental contribution. While the ideas are interesting, the practical benefits remain unclear, and the complexity of the proposed approach is a significant barrier to adoption. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the advantages of non-greedy optimization outweigh the added computational cost and implementation effort. More substantial experimental validation, especially on larger datasets, would be required to warrant acceptance. I believe this work would be more appropriate for a venue less focused on demonstrated practical impact.
