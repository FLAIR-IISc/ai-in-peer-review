### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the long-standing problem of the greedy, node-by-node training procedure for decision trees. The authors propose a novel, non-greedy framework for optimizing the split parameters of a decision tree under a global objective function. The core idea is to reframe the tree learning problem as one of structured prediction with latent variables. Specifically, the sequence of binary decisions that routes an input sample `x` to a leaf is treated as a latent variable. As direct optimization of the empirical loss is intractable due to its discontinuous, piecewise-constant nature, the authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on this loss, inspired by latent structured SVMs. This surrogate objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is an efficiency improvement that reduces the complexity of each SGD update. A naive implementation of the required loss-augmented inference step is exponential in the tree depth `d` (O(2^d p)). The authors propose a "fast" version by restricting the search space, reducing the complexity to a practical O(d^2 p). Experiments on several benchmark datasets demonstrate that the resulting non-greedy trees achieve better generalization and are less prone to overfitting compared to traditional greedy methods, including axis-aligned and oblique tree variants.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a highly original and elegant formulation for a classic machine learning problem. Connecting non-greedy decision tree optimization to the well-established framework of latent structured prediction is a significant conceptual contribution. This moves beyond heuristic or locally-scoped lookahead methods and provides a principled global objective for training. If this approach proves scalable and robust, it could meaningfully advance how decision trees are trained, leading to more compact and accurate models.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The derivation of the surrogate upper bound is well-founded and correctly links the problem to structural SVMs. The insight to reduce the complexity of loss-augmented inference by constraining the search to a Hamming ball of radius one is clever and crucial for making the method practical for reasonably deep trees. The experimental validation of this complexity reduction is convincing (Fig. 4).

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the authors do an excellent job of building the formulation step-by-step, starting from the standard decision tree, introducing the latent variable perspective, and finally deriving the surrogate objective. The notation is consistent, and Algorithm 1 provides a clear summary of the optimization procedure.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The comparison against multiple relevant baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, and a greedy version of their own framework, CO2) provides strong evidence for the benefits of the non-greedy global optimization. The results in Figure 2 consistently show that the proposed method yields higher test accuracy and better generalization across different tree depths and datasets. The analysis of the hyperparameter `ν` and its connection to regularization and leaf pruning is insightful.

### **Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions**

While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas that could be clarified or strengthened.

1.  **Details of the SSGD Algorithm:** The Stable SGD (SSGD) algorithm is an interesting practical heuristic to improve training stability, but its description is too brief. For reproducibility, the paper should provide more specific details on its implementation. For example:
    *   How are the leaf assignments `a(x)` fixed and updated? Is it for a fixed number of epochs, or based on a specific convergence criterion?
    *   The criterion mentioned—"when the improvement in the bound becomes negligible"—is vague. A more precise definition (e.g., improvement below a certain threshold `ε` over `k` iterations) would be helpful.
    *   The paper states that the bound in Eq. (14) for SSGD is looser than that in Eq. (13). A brief discussion on the potential impact of using this looser bound would be welcome.

2.  **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The method is initialized using a greedily trained tree. This is a reasonable choice, but it raises the question of how dependent the final performance is on this specific initialization. Would the non-greedy optimization be able to recover from a poorer (e.g., random) initialization, or is it primarily a fine-tuning procedure? An ablation study comparing the greedy initialization to a random one would provide valuable insight into the convexity of the optimization landscape and the robustness of the method.

3.  **Comparison with Ensemble Methods:** The authors fairly argue that they focus on single trees to isolate the effect of the non-greedy algorithm. However, state-of-the-art performance on tabular data is dominated by ensembles like Random Forests and Gradient Boosted Trees. To better position the practical significance of this work, it would be highly beneficial to include a comparison against these methods. For instance, how does a single non-greedy tree compare to a Random Forest with a similar total number of parameters or inference time? This would help readers understand the trade-offs and determine in which scenarios this more complex single-tree training is advantageous.

4.  **Trade-off in "Fast" Inference:** The fast loss-augmented inference relies on a different, potentially looser upper bound (Eq. 13) than the original one (Eq. 7). While the speedup is essential, there is an implicit trade-off between computational cost and the tightness of the surrogate objective. The paper could benefit from a short discussion on this trade-off. Does this approximation ever lead to a degradation in performance compared to what might be achieved with the full, albeit intractable, inference step?

### **Decision**

This is an excellent paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the field. The proposed framework for non-greedy decision tree optimization is elegant, technically sound, and empirically validated. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to requests for additional clarification and experiments that would further strengthen the paper but do not undermine its core contributions. This work is of high quality and is likely to be impactful, inspiring future research on global optimization of tree-based models. I recommend acceptance.
