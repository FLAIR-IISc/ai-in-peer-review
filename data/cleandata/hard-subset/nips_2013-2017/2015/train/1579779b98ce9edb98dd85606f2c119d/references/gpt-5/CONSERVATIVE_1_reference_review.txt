Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training procedure for decision trees with oblique (linear) splits by casting the training problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The key idea is to interpret the vector of split decisions h = sgn(Wx) as a latent variable and to upper bound the empirical loss with a convex–concave surrogate that admits stochastic subgradient optimization. The loss-augmented inference term is first formulated exactly (but exponential in depth in the naive worst case), then replaced with a restricted “Hamming ball radius 1” variant that reduces per-update cost to O(d^2 p), where d is depth and p is feature dimension. The authors also propose a “Stable SGD” variant that freezes leaf assignments for several steps to avoid degenerate inactive leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare single trees trained by the proposed method against standard axis-aligned CART, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique baselines, and a greedy depth-1 CO2 variant. Reported results show improved test accuracy across depths and reduced overfitting.

Relation to prior work
- The reduction to latent structured prediction and the use of a margin-style upper bound closely follow structural SVM ideas, including margin-rescaling and loss-augmented inference (Taskar et al., Tsochantaridis et al., Yu & Joachims).
- The representation sgn(Wx) = argmax_h h^T Wx has been used in previous hashing work (Norouzi & Fleet).
- Non-greedy/global optimization of trees is a long-standing goal; prior work includes MILP formulations (Bennett) and large-margin trees (Bennett et al.). Soft decision trees/HME train with EM but lose the computational advantages of hard routing.
- The paper positions itself as preserving hard splits and O(dp) inference while enabling joint optimization via an upper bound.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing conceptual link between decision tree training and latent structured prediction, enabling a principled surrogate objective.
- Practical algorithmic contributions: a tractable loss-augmented inference via the Hamming-1 relaxation (yielding O(d^2 p) per step), and a stabilization heuristic (SSGD) for avoiding inactive leaves.
- The method accommodates general losses (e.g., log loss), and the per-row norm constraints provide an interpretable regularizer connecting to margin control at splits.
- Empirical results on several datasets indicate consistent improvements over standard greedy single-tree baselines and the CO2 greedy depth-1 variant.
- Inference remains fast (hard routing), which is crucial for deployment scenarios where trees are favored.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and strength of baselines. The comparisons focus on single trees; however, in practice, state-of-the-art performance with trees is achieved by ensembles (random forests, gradient boosting). Even if the paper aims to isolate single-tree behavior, at least a small set of results showing that the proposed non-greedy trees improve forest/boosted ensembles would substantially strengthen the case for significance.
- Missing comparisons to stronger oblique/sparse oblique tree methods beyond OC1, e.g., recent L1/L2-regularized oblique split learning, rotation forests, or modern oblique optimization variants. It is unclear how much benefit stems from non-greediness vs. simply allowing oblique splits with good regularization.
- The optimization changes the objective twice: from the original loss to an upper bound (7), then to a looser Hamming-1 bound (13), and further to the SSGD bound (14). There is no theoretical characterization of the gap introduced by these relaxations or any convergence guarantees beyond standard SGD heuristics. Proposition 1 is correct but mostly qualitative; there is no generalization analysis or bounds on the tightness of the surrogate under the norm constraints.
- Initialization uses a greedy axis-aligned tree refined by CO2; ablations on initialization are missing. It is possible the gains depend heavily on this warm start.
- Runtime evaluation is thin (one dataset) and does not convincingly demonstrate the practical scalability of the O(d^2 p) approach on deeper trees or higher-dimensional data. Memory and wall-clock training comparisons to baselines are not systematically reported.
- Several bibliographic entries are incomplete; some citations appear as arXiv or missing years, and the placement of CO2 (as both a prior and a baseline) should be more clearly delineated to establish novelty.
- Reproducibility details (code, exact hyperparameter ranges, random seeds, statistical significance/error bars) are lacking.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the initialization (axis-aligned vs. OC1 vs. random W)? Can the method recover from poor starts?
2. How does performance vary with larger Hamming ball radii (r > 1), trading computation for a potentially tighter bound?
3. Can you report results on ensembles built from your non-greedy trees (bagging/boosting), at fixed test-time compute, to demonstrate practical significance?
4. Do you enforce any sparsity in W? Sparse oblique splits could improve interpretability and speed; any results?
5. Are improvements statistically significant across runs? Please provide means/standard deviations and consistent validation protocols for all baselines.

Minor comments and clarity
- Provide full proofs in the supplementary and a brief intuition for (7) and (13) in the main text.
- Clarify the relationship to CO2: what exactly is reused, and what is substantively new here?
- Improve references (complete bibliographic details).

Pros
- Principled surrogate objective for non-greedy tree training with hard splits
- Efficient loss-augmented inference via Hamming-1 relaxation
- Consistent empirical gains over standard single-tree baselines
- Straightforward to implement with SGD and norm constraints

Cons
- Limited baselines and datasets; no ensembles
- No theoretical guarantees on the relaxed objectives or generalization
- Dependence on initialization not studied
- Sparse runtime analysis; no statistical significance

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper has interesting ideas and promising empirical signals, but given the heavy reliance on relaxations without theoretical guarantees and the limited experimental validation against strong, established baselines (especially ensembles), I am not convinced of sufficient significance for NeurIPS at this time. Strengthening the experimental section (including ensembles, stronger oblique baselines, ablations, and statistical analyses) and clarifying novelty relative to CO2 would likely change my assessment.

Confidence
High on the assessment of empirical and methodological rigor; medium on the completeness of related work coverage in oblique tree literature.
