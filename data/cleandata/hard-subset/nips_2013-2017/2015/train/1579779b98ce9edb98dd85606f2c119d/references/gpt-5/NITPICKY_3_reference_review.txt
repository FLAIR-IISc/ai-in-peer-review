Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy method for training oblique decision trees by jointly optimizing all split parameters under a global surrogate objective inspired by structured prediction with latent variables. The key idea is to treat the m split decisions as a latent vector h ∈ {−1,+1}^m, rewrite the discrete routing as an argmax over h, and upper bound the empirical loss with a convex-concave surrogate that can be optimized via (stochastic) gradient descent (SGD). A “loss-augmented inference” step is required; a naive version has O(2^d p) cost (d = depth, p = input dimension). The paper derives an alternative bound restricting g to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), which reduces loss-augmented inference to O(d^2 p). A Stable SGD variant is introduced to prevent leaves becoming inactive. Experiments on several LibSVM multi-class datasets suggest improved test accuracy versus axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, and CO2 baselines, and show reduced overfitting and better scalability than naive loss-augmented inference.

Relation to prior work
The connection to latent structured prediction and the use of loss-augmented inference parallels latent SVMs and structural SVMs (Tsochantaridis et al.; Yu & Joachims). Prior global tree optimization (Bennett) and margin-based node training (Bennett & Blue) are discussed. The paper claims advantages over HME (Jordan & Jacobs) by retaining hard routing and fast O(dp) inference. CO2 (Norouzi et al.) is treated as the greedy special case.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clean latent-variable formulation of tree training, yielding a principled global objective and enabling end-to-end joint optimization with general losses.
- Technical contribution: a tractable upper bound and a fast loss-augmented inference scheme with O(d^2 p) complexity; insightful restriction to a Hamming ball to keep only path-relevant flips.
- Practicality: SGD-based training with per-row norm constraints; Stable SGD to discourage collapsing capacity; retains O(dp) test-time inference.
- Empirical promise: on multiple datasets, single trees trained non-greedily outperform standard greedy oblique/axis-aligned baselines at comparable depths.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and clarity
- Inconsistent norm constraint and projection. Eq. (9) uses “‖wi‖2 ≤ ν”, which typically denotes L2 norm ≤ ν, but Algorithm 1 projects with factor min{1, sqrt(ν)/‖w‖2}, which corresponds to constraint ‖wi‖2 ≤ sqrt(ν) if ‖·‖2 is the L2 norm, or to ‖wi‖^2 ≤ ν if ‖·‖2 denotes squared norm. Please disambiguate and fix the projection accordingly.
- Complexity vs. algorithm steps. Algorithm 1 sets ĥ ← sgn(Wx), which naively requires all m inner products (O(mp) = O(2^d p)), conflicting with the O(d^2 p) complexity claim. The fast bound relies on never computing off-path signs or inner products; the presented algorithm and complexity discussion are inconsistent. Provide explicit pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference and show how ĥ, ĝ, and the needed deltas are computed using only O(d) nodes per candidate.
- Loss-augmented inference derivation. The key step subtracting sgn(Wx)^T Wx to argue off-path bits are irrelevant is correct only if the algorithm never needs their signs numerically. The current exposition mixes “conceptual subtraction” with actual computations; clarify exactly which node dot products are computed.
- Proposition 1 and discussion: The text says the bound tightens as ‖W‖ scales up (consistent), and that in the limit the bound “approaches the loss itself,” which is correct. However, earlier phrasing could be misread as the difference of maxima approaching zero; please sharpen the explanation to state explicitly that the RHS tends to `(Θ^T f(ĥ), y)`, making the surrogate nearly piecewise-constant in W and hard to optimize.
- SSGD is underspecified. The schedule for freezing and updating assignments a(x), the stopping criterion (“improvement becomes negligible”), and how often to refresh are not defined, impacting reproducibility and potentially the results.

Algorithmic scope and empirical evaluation
- Baselines and fairness. The comparison omits strong margin-based oblique trees using SVMs at nodes (Bennett & Blue) or other modern oblique split learners; only OC1, random oblique, and axis-aligned are included. Also unclear whether baselines received comparable hyperparameter tuning (e.g., min samples/leaf, pruning strategies) beyond max depth.
- Lack of statistical rigor. No confidence intervals, multiple runs, or significance testing; sensitivity to random seeds is not reported, although non-convex training and random initializations matter.
- Claim of generality vs. experiments. The framework claims broad applicability (e.g., regression with squared loss), but experiments are limited to multi-class classification with log loss. No ablations on ν and η beyond one figure, no report on SSGD impact vs. SGD, and no quantitative measure of inactive leaves over training.
- Scalability claims. The complexity analysis emphasizes per-update O(d^2 p), but empirical runtime measurements are limited (e.g., Connect4; relatively small n). Provide wall-clock comparisons versus baselines across depths and sample sizes, and report the number of epochs/updates to reach best validation performance.

Presentation and formatting issues
- Numerous typographical/formatting errors (e.g., “withm internal nodes,” inconsistent spacing and punctuation, “John Hopkins” vs. “Johns Hopkins”).
- Notational ambiguities: I_{m+1} or Im+1 is not defined; appending a constant “−1” to x to model bias is unconventional and confusing—clarify sign conventions.
- References are incomplete or malformed (missing years/venues/pages; “et al, J. Shotton, … tracking, and action recognition” contains stray text; CO2 arXiv ref without year; several others lack detail). For NeurIPS, complete and consistent bibliographic info is required.
- Figures are heavily referenced but not visible here; ensure captions quantify the axes, include seeds/variances, and define all hyperparameters.
- Some claims (kernelization, sparsity-inducing regularization) are listed as future work; fine, but do not overstate applicability without evidence.

Pros
- Clear, interesting connection between hard tree routing and latent structured prediction.
- A principled surrogate objective with a computationally efficient variant.
- Demonstrated improvements over standard greedy oblique/axis-aligned trees on several datasets.
- Maintains fast test-time inference characteristic of decision trees.

Cons
- Core algorithmic details (projection, fast inference, SSGD) are underspecified or inconsistent with the stated complexity.
- Baselines are limited; lack of statistical rigor.
- Reproducibility concerns due to missing details.
- Numerous editorial/formatting/reference issues suggest lack of polish.

Recommendation: Reject
While the paper contains a promising and potentially impactful idea with nontrivial technical components, the accumulation of methodological inconsistencies (especially around complexity vs. algorithm steps), underspecified procedures (SSGD), limited baselines/statistics, and significant presentation issues outweigh the current strengths. I encourage the authors to (1) fix the norm constraint/projection and fast inference algorithm with precise pseudocode and complexity, (2) expand and fairly tune baselines (including margin-based oblique trees), add statistical rigor, and (3) thoroughly polish the writing and references. With these addressed, this could become a strong submission.
