

Review:

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent. The authors claim their method reduces the computational complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth, enabling efficient learning of deep trees.

The theoretical connection between decision trees and structured prediction with latent variables is interesting, but the novelty of this connection is questionable. The paper cites relevant work (e.g., [25]) but doesn't clearly establish how this formulation differs from prior attempts to address non-greedy tree learning. The technical contribution of reducing the complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) is mathematically sound, but the paper fails to demonstrate how this translates to practical benefits on real-world problems.

The experimental validation is notably weak. The authors evaluate on only four benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST), which are relatively small and not representative of the large-scale applications where decision trees are commonly used (e.g., the Microsoft Kinect example cited in the introduction). The improvements over greedy baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees) are marginal across most datasets (Fig. 2), and the paper lacks statistical significance tests to confirm these improvements are meaningful.

The paper claims "non-greedy trees achieve the best test performance across tree depths across multiple datasets," but this claim is not supported by sufficient evidence. For instance, on the Protein dataset, the non-greedy approach only marginally outperforms the greedy baselines, and the differences in accuracy are often less than 1%. The paper also doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art decision tree methods like XGBoost, LightGBM, or CatBoost, which have become the standard in the field.

The regularization parameter ν is tuned via validation sets, but the paper doesn't investigate how sensitive the results are to this hyperparameter or provide guidance on how to choose it in practice. The paper's claim that "the norm regularization provides a principled way to tune the tightness of the tree's fit to the training data" is not substantiated with sufficient empirical evidence.

The computational complexity claim (O(d^2p)) is supported by the theoretical analysis, but Fig. 4 only shows a modest speedup on the Connect4 dataset (55,000 training points), which is far from the scale of the Microsoft Kinect application mentioned in the introduction. The paper doesn't provide evidence that their method can scale to the millions of data points mentioned in the introduction.

The paper also fails to address a critical concern: why would non-greedy optimization of tree parameters improve generalization? The paper claims it "promotes cooperation between the split nodes to create more compact trees," but doesn't provide evidence that their trees are actually more compact or that this compactness leads to better generalization.

The authors cite Bennett's earlier work [1] on non-greedy tree optimization, but don't provide a thorough comparison of their method with Bennett's. Bennett's method was limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss and had high computational complexity, but the paper doesn't explain how their method overcomes these limitations in a way that Bennett's couldn't.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework but lacks sufficient experimental validation to support its claims of improved performance and practical utility. The marginal gains over greedy baselines on small datasets do not convincingly demonstrate that this method would be useful for real-world applications where decision trees are commonly used.

The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors:
1. Expanded the experimental evaluation to include larger datasets and more recent decision tree methods
2. Provided statistical significance tests for the reported improvements
3. Demonstrated the method's scalability to large-scale problems (e.g., millions of data points)
4. Provided a clearer explanation of why non-greedy optimization improves generalization

Without these improvements, the paper's contributions appear to be incremental at best, and the claims of improved performance are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented.

Given my skeptical stance as a reviewer who prefers established methods and requires substantial validation for new approaches, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper in its current form. While the theoretical framework is sound, the empirical validation is insufficient to support the claims made in the paper, and the improvements over existing methods are marginal at best. The paper would need significantly more rigorous experimental validation to justify its claims before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
