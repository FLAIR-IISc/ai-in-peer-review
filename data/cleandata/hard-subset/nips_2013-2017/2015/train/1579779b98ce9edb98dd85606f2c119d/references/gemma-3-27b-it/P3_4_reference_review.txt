## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing problem of efficiently learning decision trees beyond the limitations of greedy algorithms. The authors propose a novel framework for non-greedy learning, establishing a connection to structured prediction with latent variables. They formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution lies in reducing the computational complexity of this optimization from O(2dp) to O(d²p), making it feasible for deeper trees. The paper presents experimental results demonstrating improved performance compared to greedy baselines across several datasets.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of decision tree learning. It acknowledges the limitations of greedy approaches (ID3, CART) and references recent efforts to improve objective functions within a greedy framework. The authors appropriately contrast their work with previous non-greedy methods like Bennett's multi-linear programming approach (limited scalability) and Mondrian Processes (online learning, different focus). They also discuss related work on hierarchical mixture of experts and regularization techniques for decision trees, highlighting the advantages of their proposed formulation. The connection drawn to structured prediction with latent variables, citing works by Taskar, Joachims, and Yu, is particularly relevant and well-justified.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The latent variable formulation is a clever way to frame decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem, opening the door to leveraging techniques developed in that field.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The reduction in complexity from O(2dp) to O(d²p) is a significant contribution. This makes non-greedy optimization practical for trees of reasonable depth, a crucial step forward.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently show improvement over greedy baselines, suggesting the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparison to CO2 is particularly insightful, demonstrating the benefits of the full non-greedy optimization.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains complex concepts in a relatively accessible manner. The connection between the upper bound, loss-augmented inference, and the optimization algorithm is clearly articulated.
* **Thorough Ablation Study:** The exploration of the regularization parameter ν and the introduction of Stable SGD demonstrate a careful consideration of the practical aspects of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Justification of Regularization:** While the authors provide an intuitive explanation for the regularization term (Proposition 1), a more rigorous theoretical analysis of its impact on generalization would be beneficial. Why is the L2 norm specifically chosen? Are there alternative regularizers that could be more effective?
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison to greedy baselines is solid, the paper lacks a comparison to more recent and sophisticated tree-based methods, such as gradient boosting machines (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM) or very deep decision trees trained with more advanced regularization techniques. These represent the current state-of-the-art and would provide a more compelling benchmark.
* **Dependency on Initial Greedy Tree:** The reliance on initializing the non-greedy optimization with a greedy tree (CO2) is a potential limitation. It's unclear how sensitive the performance is to this initialization. Exploring random initializations or alternative starting points might be valuable.
* **Scalability to Extremely Large Datasets:** While the complexity reduction is significant, the O(d²p) term could still be a bottleneck for extremely high-dimensional datasets (large *p*) and very deep trees (large *d*).  A discussion of potential scalability challenges and future work in this direction is warranted.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The need to carefully tune the regularization parameter ν suggests potential sensitivity to hyperparameters. A more robust analysis of hyperparameter selection and its impact on performance would be helpful.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to non-greedy decision tree learning.
* The computational efficiency improvements are significant and practical.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate clear benefits over existing greedy methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed framework.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is limited.
* The theoretical justification for the regularization term could be strengthened.
* The reliance on greedy initialization and the potential sensitivity to hyperparameters are concerns.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed non-greedy optimization framework, coupled with the computational efficiency improvements, represents a valuable step forward. While some areas could be strengthened (e.g., comparison to SOTA, theoretical justification of regularization), the novelty, soundness, and empirical results justify acceptance.

**Rating:**  **Accept (Strongly Consider)**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider:

1.  Adding a comparison to a state-of-the-art gradient boosting machine.
2.  Providing a more rigorous justification for the choice of the L2 regularization term.
3.  Exploring the sensitivity of the algorithm to the initial greedy tree and investigating alternative initialization strategies.
4.  Discussing the scalability limitations for very large datasets and potential future directions to address them.
