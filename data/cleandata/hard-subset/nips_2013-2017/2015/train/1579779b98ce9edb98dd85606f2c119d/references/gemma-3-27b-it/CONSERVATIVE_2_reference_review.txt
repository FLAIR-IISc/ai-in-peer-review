This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning, framing it as a structured prediction problem with latent variables and developing a surrogate objective optimized via stochastic gradient descent. While the motivation – addressing the limitations of greedy tree induction – is valid and the problem statement is clearly articulated, the paper falls short of convincing me of the practical significance of the proposed method. My primary concern is the lack of compelling evidence demonstrating a substantial advantage over well-established, greedy baselines, despite the increased complexity of the approach.

The connection to structured prediction is a potentially interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical benefit remains unclear. The authors acknowledge the NP-completeness of global tree optimization and present a surrogate objective to circumvent this difficulty. However, the proposed solution introduces its own complexities – namely, the O(d2p) computational cost, which, while an improvement over the initial O(2dp), could still be prohibitive for high-dimensional data or deep trees. The paper does not sufficiently address the trade-offs between this complexity and the purported gains in accuracy. 

The experimental evaluation, while reasonably thorough in terms of datasets, lacks a clear and decisive demonstration of superiority. While the authors report better test performance, the improvements appear marginal, and the gains do not consistently outweigh the added computational cost. The comparison against CO2, a greedy method informed by the same theoretical framework, is helpful, but further comparisons against optimized implementations of classic algorithms (e.g., highly tuned CART or C4.5) are needed. Furthermore, the ablation study to understand the role of the regularization parameter ν, while informative, doesn't demonstrate a consistent advantage across all datasets. The choice of initializing the non-greedy method with a greedy tree (CO2) is also not sufficiently justified; a random initialization might provide a fairer assessment of the non-greedy optimization’s capabilities.

The clarity of the presentation is adequate, but the mathematical notation is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations, especially regarding the latent variable formulation and the derivation of the upper bound. The paper's organization is logical, but the constant references to supplementary material for proofs and details detract from readability. Key insights should be presented more directly within the main body of the paper.

The originality lies in the formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem and the development of the convex-concave surrogate objective. However, the core idea of optimizing a global objective for decision trees is not entirely novel (as acknowledged in the related work, with citations to Bennett’s work). The contribution, therefore, is more incremental than groundbreaking. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but it misses recent advances in differentiable tree structures which represent a different approach to non-greedy tree learning.

The significance of the work is questionable. While the proposed method *can* achieve better accuracy, the improvements are not substantial enough to warrant the increased complexity. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach will be widely adopted by practitioners or serve as a foundation for significant future research. The practical impact seems limited, especially given the computational demands and the availability of efficient, well-established greedy algorithms.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem.
*   Development of a surrogate objective enabling non-greedy optimization.
*   Demonstrated, albeit marginal, improvements in accuracy over some baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of substantial and consistent improvement over established greedy algorithms.
*   Increased computational complexity compared to greedy approaches.
*   Limited practical significance and potential for real-world impact.
*   Dense mathematical notation and reliance on supplementary material.
*   Incomplete comparison against highly optimized greedy baselines.



Overall, I find this paper to be technically sound but lacking in significant novelty and practical impact. The theoretical contributions are interesting, but the empirical results do not convincingly justify the added complexity. I would lean towards **reject** with the suggestion that the authors focus on further improving the efficiency of the algorithm, conducting more comprehensive comparisons against state-of-the-art greedy methods, and clearly demonstrating a substantial advantage in terms of accuracy or scalability before resubmitting.
