- Proposes non-greedy training for decision trees by treating split outcomes as latent variables and optimizing a convex–concave upper bound with SGD.  
- Key technical ideas: structured latent-variable view h = sgn(Wx), latent-SSVM-style bound, per-row norm constraints, Hamming-ball radius-1 loss-augmented inference, and stabilized SGD (SSGD).  
- Fast loss-augmented inference reduces exact O(2^d p) search to O(d^2 p) by restricting to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx).  
- Per-row norm constraints prevent trivial tightening of the bound by scaling W and act as practical regularization.  
- The latent-variable reformulation and the upper bound are standard adaptations and appear technically sound.  
- Complexity claims O(d^2 p + k) per SGD step are plausible given the Hamming-ball approximation.  
- Optimization remains non-convex with no convergence guarantees beyond standard SGD heuristics.  
- The claim that small ν “forces a large margin” is imprecise; ν limits capacity but does not formally maximize margin.  
- Moving from exact to radius-1 bound is a key approximation with no theoretical analysis of its impact on optimization quality.  
- Stabilized SGD is an interesting practical idea but is under-evaluated with no quantitative evidence of its effects.  
- Experiments show consistent accuracy gains for single trees versus greedy baselines, especially as depth increases.  
- Empirical presentation is qualitative: figures lack numerical tables, confidence intervals, and statistical significance tests.  
- No ablation studies isolating the radius-1 restriction, SSGD effect, or initialization sensitivity (initialized with CO2) are provided.  
- Runtime comparisons are limited (small dataset) and lack wall-clock baselines against OC1 and random oblique trees.  
- Paper is generally clear and implementable; Algorithm 1 and explanations are helpful.  
- Clarity could be improved on ν’s role, explicit complexity breakdown (including k), and SSGD scheduling/details.  
- Framing tree induction as latent structured prediction with radius-1 inference and per-row projection is original and well-motivated.  
- The contribution is potentially significant for scalable non-greedy oblique trees, but practical impact is limited by narrow empirical scope (single trees, few datasets).  
- Pros: principled formulation, efficient Hamming-ball inference, scalable projected SGD, and consistent empirical gains.  
- Cons: non-convex optimization without convergence analysis, uncharacterized radius-1 approximation, limited experiments (no stats/ensembles), and ambiguous ν/SSGD discussion.  
- Suggested improvements: add ablations (exact vs. radius-1, with/without SSGD, initialization sensitivity), clarify ν’s effect and tuning, report numerical accuracy with variability, expand runtime/scaling baselines, and explore ensembles.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — novel and promising method but requires stronger empirical evaluation and some theoretical clarification.
