Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy learning framework for decision trees that jointly optimizes all internal split functions and leaf parameters under a global objective. The key idea is to cast hard-split decision trees as a latent structured prediction problem: for each input, a latent binary vector h encodes split outcomes across internal nodes; the tree loss can be upper-bounded by a convex–concave surrogate akin to the latent structural SVM “loss-augmented inference” bound. Optimizing this surrogate with SGD yields cooperative split decisions across levels, in contrast to classical greedy training (ID3/CART). A central technical contribution is an efficient loss-augmented inference procedure: the naive bound would require O(2^d p) per update, but the authors derive a looser bound that restricts the maximization to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing complexity to O(d^2 p) while preserving the upper-bound property. They also use per-row L2-norm constraints on W to regularize and stabilize optimization, and introduce a “Stable SGD” variant that holds leaf assignments fixed for several steps to prevent collapse to inactive leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets show higher test accuracy and reduced overfitting versus axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique splits, and CO2 (a greedy, depth-1 special case of their bound).

The work connects to: (i) non-greedy tree optimization via MILP/LP and margin-based splits [1–3], which are limited in scale or scope; (ii) soft trees/HME [13], which allow smooth optimization but lose the O(d p) inference advantage; and (iii) CO2 [18], which optimizes a similar bound per node greedily. The latent-structured view and the efficient radius-1 loss-augmented inference appear novel in the context of hard-split, non-greedy trees.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reformulation sgn(Wx)=argmax_h h^T Wx is standard; the bound in Eq. (7) follows the latent structural SVM template and is valid. Importantly, the radius-1 restriction in Eq. (13) still upper-bounds the loss because it includes g = sgn(Wx). The per-row norm constraints and projected SGD are reasonable choices; the complexity analysis of O(d^2 p) per update (dominated by loss-augmented inference over d+1 candidate leaves and O(d p) path computations) is plausible. The SSGD heuristic is sensible given the observed inactive-leaf phenomenon.
- Weak points: There is no generalization analysis tying ν (row-norm constraint) to error bounds; claims about “less overfitting” are empirical only. Optimization-wise, the objective is non-convex; convergence guarantees are not discussed (beyond standard SGD practice). Some implementation details (mini-batch sizes, momentum, stopping criteria) are not specified enough for exact reproduction. The empirical evaluation focuses on single trees on a handful of datasets; no regression experiments despite proposing squared loss; and no ensembles (where trees are most impactful) or wall-clock comparisons against strong greedy baselines. Ablations isolating the effects of SSGD vs. SGD, and of the radius-1 bound vs. full loss-augmented inference, are limited.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mapping f from binary decisions to leaf indicators is clear; Algorithm 1 is straightforward. The discussion around the scaling of W and Proposition 1 is helpful, though the practical trade-off (tighter bound vs. degeneracy to piecewise-constant behavior) could be explained more crisply. It would help to explicitly state that test-time inference retains O(d p) complexity (hard-split evaluation), and to clarify that the projection in Alg. 1 can be applied only to rows with nonzero gradients to retain O(d p) per update in practice (the pseudocode loops over all rows).

Originality
The latent-structured perspective for hard-split trees, the particular convex–concave upper bound tailored to tree navigation, and especially the efficient radius-1 loss-augmented inference that preserves an upper bound while reducing complexity, are novel and non-trivial. Prior non-greedy or margin-based trees either optimize per node, require soft splits, or are computationally heavy; this work hits a useful algorithmic sweet spot.

Significance
If robust, the proposed framework could influence how single trees and tree ensembles are trained, enabling deeper trees with coordinated splits and retaining inference speed. The empirical gains over strong greedy baselines are promising. However, significance would be much stronger with: (i) demonstrations in ensembles (bagging/boosting) where trees shine; (ii) broader datasets; and (iii) more thorough ablations and runtime comparisons.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation linking decision trees to latent structured prediction.
- A valid, computable upper bound and an efficient radius-1 variant with O(d^2 p) updates.
- Joint optimization across levels reduces overfitting and improves test accuracy over multiple baselines, including oblique and CO2 trees.
- Maintains hard-split inference time O(d p).
- Practical touches: per-row norm constraints, projected SGD, SSGD to prevent leaf inactivity.

Cons
- Limited empirical scope: only single trees, few datasets; no regression experiments; no ensembles; limited runtime comparisons versus strong baselines.
- No theory on generalization vs. ν or on optimization convergence.
- Sensitivity to initialization (from CO2) and ν is acknowledged but not deeply analyzed; improvements over CO2 are “in most cases” without statistical analysis.
- Some implementation details and ablations (effect of SSGD, choice of radius, possible beam search vs. radius-1) are missing.

Questions and suggestions
- Can the authors report results with ensembles (e.g., bagging) to substantiate practical significance and runtime trade-offs?
- Provide ablations: SGD vs. SSGD; full vs. radius-1 loss-augmented inference where feasible; sensitivity curves for ν and learning rate; multiple random seeds for statistical confidence.
- Clarify whether projection is applied only to rows touched by (ĝ − ĥ); otherwise the per-step cost could be O(m p).
- Consider adaptive neighborhoods (e.g., small beam over g differing on a few path bits) to trade tightness vs. efficiency.
- Any margin- or Rademacher-style generalization bound under per-row norm constraints?
- Report wall-clock comparisons vs. OC1 and CO2 for equal accuracy.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a principled and efficient non-greedy training framework for hard-split trees with novel insights and solid empirical improvements on single-tree benchmarks. While the experimental scope could be broader and the analysis deeper, the methodological contribution is likely to be of interest to the NIPS community and to spur follow-up work on scalable, globally trained trees and forests.

Confidence: High.
