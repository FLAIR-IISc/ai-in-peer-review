## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing limitation of greedy algorithms in decision tree induction – their inability to consider downstream effects of splits. The authors propose a non-greedy framework leveraging structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p), enabling learning of deeper trees. The authors demonstrate improved performance compared to greedy baselines on several benchmark datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Fundamental Limitation:** The paper directly addresses a well-known weakness of traditional decision tree learning. Moving beyond greedy algorithms has the potential for significant improvement in accuracy and tree structure.
* **Novel Formulation:** The connection to structured prediction with latent variables provides a solid theoretical foundation and opens up opportunities to leverage advancements in that field.  The formulation is a clever way to reframe the problem.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a crucial contribution, making the approach feasible for deeper trees and larger datasets. This is a key practical advancement.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines across multiple datasets, supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparison to CO2 highlights the benefit of non-greedy optimization.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction provides good motivation and context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the authors achieve O(d2p) complexity, the implementation appears intricate. The use of multiple techniques (surrogate objective, latent variables, SGD, stable SGD) adds to the overall complexity, potentially hindering reproducibility.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the regularization parameter ν. While tuning is performed, the sensitivity itself suggests potential instability or the need for more robust hyperparameter selection strategies.  The discussion around ν feels a bit ad-hoc.
* **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparisons are primarily against simpler greedy baselines.  More comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art methods like Extremely Randomized Trees or Gradient Boosted Trees (even if these are ensembles) would provide a stronger evaluation.  How does this compare to Random Forests in terms of training *and* inference time?
* **Scalability Discussion:**  While the authors demonstrate scalability improvements, the experiments are conducted on relatively modest datasets.  A more detailed discussion of the scalability to truly large-scale datasets (e.g., millions of samples) would be valuable. The Connect4 dataset is notably small in comparison to modern applications.
* **Impact of Stable SGD:** While SSGD is presented as an improvement, the explanation of *why* it improves performance is a bit vague. More insight into the behavior of the optimization process would be helpful.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the experimental results are well-supported. However, the complexity of the method introduces a potential risk of implementation errors.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized. However, the detailed algorithmic description and the interplay between the different components (latent variables, surrogate objective, SGD, SSGD) could be made even more accessible.
* **Originality:** The combination of structured prediction with decision trees and the complexity reduction technique are novel contributions. The paper builds on existing work but presents a significant advancement in the field.
* **Significance:** The potential to overcome the limitations of greedy decision tree learning is significant. If the method proves scalable and robust, it could have a substantial impact on various applications. The improvement in generalization performance is a promising result.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental problem in decision tree learning.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* Achieves promising empirical results.
* Offers a crucial complexity reduction, making the method practical.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Implementation complexity may hinder reproducibility.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity raises concerns about robustness.
* Limited comparison to stronger baselines.
* Scalability to extremely large datasets needs further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed non-greedy framework is a promising approach to overcome the limitations of traditional algorithms. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding implementation complexity and the need for more extensive comparisons, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should:

*   Provide more details regarding the implementation to facilitate reproducibility.
*   Include comparisons to stronger baseline methods (e.g., Random Forests, Gradient Boosting).
*   Expand the discussion on scalability to large datasets.
*   Provide a more detailed explanation of the benefits of Stable SGD.
* Clarify the choice of ν and its influence on the tree structure.
