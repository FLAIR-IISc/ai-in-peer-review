This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. The core idea is to jointly optimize split functions at different levels of the tree, potentially leading to more compact trees and improved generalization. The authors also present a complexity reduction from O(2dp) to O(d2p) for efficient learning of deep trees.

The paper builds upon a rich history of decision tree learning [5, 21, 4], addressing a long-standing limitation of greedy algorithms [21, 5] – the inability to refine splits based on lower-level training. It correctly identifies the NP-completeness of global tree optimization [11] and positions itself within the landscape of previous non-greedy attempts [1, 15, 13]. The connection to structured prediction [25] is a novel and potentially valuable perspective.  The authors differentiate their approach from existing methods like Bennett’s multi-linear programming [1], Mondrian Processes [15], and Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13], highlighting the efficiency and generality of their formulation.  The paper thoroughly references relevant work, including discussions on overfitting and regularization [17, 2, 3, 4].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a significant contribution.
* **Efficiency Improvement:** The reduction of complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is crucial for scaling to deep trees.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss appears well-motivated and justified.  The propositions regarding the regularization are clearly stated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on multiple benchmark datasets, consistently achieving better test performance than baseline greedy approaches. The ablation study with varying regularization parameters is valuable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Depth of Theoretical Analysis:** While the upper bound is presented, a more detailed analysis of its tightness and the implications of the regularization term (ν) would be beneficial. The connection to margin-based approaches [2,3] could be further elaborated.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The reliance on an initial greedy tree (CO2 [18]) for initialization might introduce bias. The sensitivity of the results to different initialization strategies isn't thoroughly investigated.
* **Computational Details:** While the complexity analysis is presented, a more detailed breakdown of the runtime for each component (loss-augmented inference, gradient update, etc.) would improve reproducibility.  The mini-batch size used in the experiments is not mentioned.
* **Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** While the comparisons to greedy methods are informative, a comparison to more recent and advanced decision tree/forest algorithms (e.g., extremely randomized trees, gradient boosted trees) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  The description of hyperparameter tuning (especially for ν) feels somewhat brief. More details on the validation strategy and search space would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to non-greedy decision tree learning.
* The efficiency improvement is significant and enables learning deeper trees.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding the tightness of the upper bound.
* The sensitivity to initialization and hyperparameter tuning requires further investigation.
* The comparison to stronger baselines would strengthen the empirical evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed method offers a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning by addressing a long-standing limitation of greedy algorithms. The technical soundness, novelty, and empirical validation are all commendable. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly concerning theoretical analysis and a more detailed discussion of experimental setup.
