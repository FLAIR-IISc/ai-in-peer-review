**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel, non-greedy algorithm for learning the parameters of a decision tree. The authors address the primary limitation of traditional decision tree induction methods (like ID3 and CART), which optimize splitting criteria at each node locally and greedily. The proposed approach formulates the entire tree learning problem as a global optimization objective. The key insight is to reframe decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, where the latent variables represent the path a data point takes through the tree. As the resulting empirical loss function is non-convex and piecewise-constant, the authors derive a convex-concave upper bound, inspired by latent structural SVMs, which serves as a tractable surrogate objective. This objective is then optimized using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). A significant technical contribution is a method to reduce the complexity of the gradient update (specifically, the loss-augmented inference step) from exponential in the tree depth, O(2^d * p), to polynomial, O(d^2 * p), by constraining the search space to a Hamming ball of radius 1. Experiments on several benchmark datasets demonstrate that the proposed non-greedy trees outperform various greedy baselines, including oblique tree methods, and show less susceptibility to overfitting.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The primary strength of this work is its reformulation of decision tree optimization. Moving from greedy, heuristic-based criteria (like information gain) to a global, regularized empirical risk minimization framework is a significant conceptual advance. The connection drawn to structured prediction with latent variables is both elegant and powerful, placing decision tree learning on a more solid theoretical footing common to other modern machine learning models.

2.  **Technically Sound and Clever:** The methodology is well-developed. The derivation of the surrogate upper bound on the loss (Eq. 7) is correct and provides a viable path for optimization. More impressively, the paper identifies the critical computational bottleneck of the loss-augmented inference step and proposes a well-motivated and effective solution (the "fast loss-augmented inference" in Eq. 13). This trick is crucial for making the algorithm practical for reasonably deep trees and is a strong technical contribution in its own right.

3.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method not just to simple axis-aligned trees but also to stronger baselines like OC1 and random oblique trees. The consistent outperformance of the non-greedy approach across multiple datasets and tree depths strongly supports the paper's central claim. The comparison with CO2 is particularly insightful, as it effectively serves as an ablation study, isolating the benefit of the global (non-greedy) optimization over a greedy application of a similar local objective.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, systematically building up their formulation from the basic tree structure to the final surrogate objective, and clearly explaining the technical challenges and their solutions.

**Weaknesses and Questions**

1.  **Dependence on Initialization:** The optimization problem is non-convex, meaning the SGD-based solution finds a local minimum. The paper states that initialization is performed using a greedy procedure (axis-aligned -> CO2). This raises a question about the sensitivity of the final model to this initialization. How much of the performance gain is attributable to the non-greedy fine-tuning versus simply starting from a very strong, greedily-trained oblique tree? An experiment starting from a random (or weaker) initialization would help clarify the robustness and power of the global optimization procedure itself.

2.  **Details of the SSGD Algorithm:** The "Stable SGD" (SSGD) variant is introduced as a practical improvement to prevent leaves from becoming inactive. However, its description is brief. It would be beneficial to have more details on the criteria for "when the improvement in the bound becomes negligible" and how often the leaf assignments `a(x)` are updated. Furthermore, the experimental results do not seem to differentiate between the performance of standard SGD and SSGD, making it difficult to assess the practical impact of this modification.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has key hyperparameters, notably the norm constraint `ν`. Figure 3 provides some insight, showing that `ν` controls model complexity (and effectively, pruning). However, a more detailed discussion on the sensitivity to `ν` and the learning rate `η` would strengthen the paper. For practitioners looking to apply this method, understanding the tuning process and the robustness to hyperparameter choices is critical.

4.  **Comparison to State-of-the-Art Ensembles:** The authors rightly focus on single trees to cleanly demonstrate the benefits of their method over greedy induction. However, in practice, the highest performance on tabular data often comes from ensembles like Gradient Boosted Decision Trees (GBDT) and Random Forests. A brief discussion on the potential implications of this work for ensembles would be valuable. For instance, could a forest of smaller, more powerful non-greedy trees outperform a forest of larger, greedy trees? Could this method serve as a stronger base learner in a boosting framework?

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to a classic area of machine learning. The novel formulation, strong technical execution, and compelling empirical results make a clear case for its acceptance. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and the results are likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. It advances the state of the art in decision tree learning and opens up several avenues for future research. The identified weaknesses are minor and relate more to requests for additional clarification and analysis rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology.

**Final Verdict: Accept**
