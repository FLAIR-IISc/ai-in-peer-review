

# Review: Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach to decision tree learning that optimizes split functions across the entire tree structure under a global objective, rather than the traditional greedy approach that optimizes nodes sequentially. The authors establish a connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating the problem as optimizing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. A key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) for the optimization procedure. The paper demonstrates improved generalization performance compared to greedy baselines across multiple datasets.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation. The authors establish a rigorous mathematical framework for non-greedy decision tree learning, proving the validity of their upper bound (equation 7) and providing a detailed complexity analysis. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with careful hyperparameter tuning (particularly for the regularization parameter ν) and comparisons against multiple relevant baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2). The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the need for careful tuning of ν and the fact that their method doesn't directly address inference speed. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including clear algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 1) and implementation details.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem, highlighting the limitations of greedy decision tree induction. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is well-motivated and explained with appropriate mathematical notation. The technical contributions (particularly the complexity reduction) are clearly articulated. While some readers might need to reread certain sections (e.g., the tree navigation function), the overall presentation is logical and accessible. The supplementary material appears to contain the necessary proofs, which is a good practice for theoretical papers.

## Originality
This work makes significant original contributions. The connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables is novel and well-executed. The specific formulation of the convex-concave upper bound (equation 7) represents a meaningful advance over previous approaches. The key technical contribution of reducing the computational complexity from exponential to polynomial is particularly valuable and original. While Bennett's earlier work on global tree optimization is cited, the authors clearly differentiate their approach through the structured prediction perspective and the complexity reduction. The paper adequately references related work, including recent advances in structured prediction and decision tree learning.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Decision trees are among the most widely used machine learning models in practice, with applications spanning numerous domains. The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of the standard greedy approach that has persisted for decades. The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets, suggesting this could have practical impact. The method's ability to handle any loss function while maintaining O(dp) inference complexity makes it broadly applicable. The authors also suggest promising extensions (e.g., sparse split functions, kernel methods) that could further enhance the approach.

## Strengths
1. The theoretical connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction is elegant and well-executed.
2. The technical contribution of reducing computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p) is substantial and well-justified.
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, demonstrating clear advantages over greedy baselines.
4. The paper provides a complete framework that can be applied to both classification and regression tasks.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the scalability of the method for extremely large datasets beyond the benchmark datasets shown (Connect4 with 55,000 training points).
2. The comparison with Bennett's earlier work on global tree optimization (Reference [1]) could be more detailed, as their method also addresses non-greedy tree learning.
3. The paper doesn't discuss the impact of the method on inference speed or memory requirements compared to greedy trees, which is an important practical consideration.
4. The "stable SGD" variant (Section 6.3) could be better motivated and explained, as its distinction from standard SGD is somewhat unclear.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of decision tree learning by providing a theoretically sound, computationally efficient non-greedy optimization framework. The technical innovation is significant, the experimental validation is thorough, and the potential impact on a widely used machine learning method is substantial. The minor weaknesses noted above are not sufficient to outweigh the paper's strengths.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The work advances the state of the art in decision tree learning in a demonstrable way, addresses a fundamental limitation of traditional methods, and provides a framework that could be widely adopted in practice. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field.

The authors have successfully bridged a gap between two areas (structured prediction and decision trees) that have not been previously connected in this way, and their approach offers a principled alternative to the decades-old greedy tree induction methods that remain dominant in practice. This work has the potential to influence both the theoretical understanding and practical implementation of decision tree methods.
