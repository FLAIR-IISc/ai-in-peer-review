This paper introduces a novel approach to non-greedy optimization of decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The core idea of using a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, combined with a clever reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) for deep trees, is a significant contribution. The authors connect decision tree learning to established techniques in structured prediction, providing a solid theoretical foundation. The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of their method, showing improved test performance compared to greedy baselines and a related CO2 approach, and highlight the benefits of the proposed regularization scheme.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a novel and insightful approach. This bridges the gap between decision tree induction and a well-studied area of machine learning.
* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound and the complexity reduction from O(2dp) to O(d2p) are mathematically rigorous and contribute significantly to the practicality of the proposed method for deeper trees.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on multiple datasets consistently demonstrate the superior performance of the non-greedy approach compared to greedy baselines. The analysis of the regularization parameter (ν) and its impact on overfitting is particularly valuable.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and technical details. The motivation for each step is well-articulated.
* **Significance:** The work addresses a long-standing limitation of traditional decision tree algorithms – their greedy nature – and offers a promising path towards building more accurate and robust tree-based models. The efficiency improvement makes this approach viable for larger datasets and deeper trees.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the authors achieve a complexity reduction, the algorithm remains significantly more complex than traditional greedy methods. The implementation details, particularly for the fast loss-augmented inference, could benefit from further clarification.  The reliance on initial greedy tree initialization is also a point to consider, and the sensitivity to this initialization is not fully explored.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison is primarily focused on greedy baselines and CO2. A more thorough comparison with more recent and sophisticated non-greedy methods (if they exist) would strengthen the paper.  For example, recent work on differentiable trees might be relevant.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The regularization parameter ν seems crucial for performance. The paper discusses tuning this parameter, but a more systematic exploration of its sensitivity and guidelines for selecting an appropriate value would be helpful.
* **Scalability to Extremely Large Datasets:** While the complexity reduction is significant, the O(d2p) complexity might still pose challenges for very large datasets with high dimensionality and deep trees. Further scalability improvements could be a direction for future work.
* **Discussion of Latent Variable Interpretation:** The paper introduces latent variables representing split decisions, but doesn’t delve into a deeper interpretation of what these latent variables learn or how they contribute to the improved performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a long-standing problem in machine learning.
* The empirical results demonstrate clear and consistent performance improvements over established baselines.
* The complexity reduction makes the method practical for deeper trees.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of decision tree learning and related areas.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The implementation complexity is relatively high, and the scalability to extremely large datasets might be limited.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is not comprehensive.
* The sensitivity to the regularization parameter requires careful tuning and could be a practical concern.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and technically sound solution. The empirical results are compelling, and the potential impact of this work is significant. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding implementation complexity and a limited comparison to state-of-the-art, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Clarify Implementation Details:** Provide more detailed explanations of the implementation, particularly for the fast loss-augmented inference algorithm. Consider including pseudo-code snippets.
* **Expand Comparative Evaluation:** Include a comparison with more recent and sophisticated non-greedy methods, if available.
* **Hyperparameter Analysis:** Conduct a more systematic analysis of the regularization parameter (ν) and provide guidelines for selecting an appropriate value.
* **Scalability Discussion:** Discuss the limitations of the method in terms of scalability and potential avenues for further improvement.
* **Latent Variable Interpretation:** Provide a more insightful discussion of the learned latent variables and their contribution to improved performance.
* **Consider providing code:** Sharing code would greatly benefit the community and facilitate further research in this area.
