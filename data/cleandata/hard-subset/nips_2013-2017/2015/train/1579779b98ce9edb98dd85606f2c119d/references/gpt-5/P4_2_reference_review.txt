Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a global surrogate objective with stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to represent the binary split outcomes at internal nodes as a latent code h = sgn(Wx) ∈ {−1,+1}^m and to define an empirical loss in terms of the leaf reached by h. The authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss that resembles margin-rescaling in structured prediction with latent variables [25, 24], and optimize this surrogate via SGD with per-row norm constraints on W to control the scale and regularize the model. They further introduce an efficient “fast loss-augmented inference” by restricting to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing per-update time from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) for depth d and p-dimensional inputs. An SSGD variant stabilizes training by temporarily fixing data-to-leaf assignments. Empirically, on several LibSVM multi-class datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST), the method yields higher test accuracy than greedy baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1 [17], random oblique splits) and improves over the depth-1 continuous optimization approach CO2 [18], while retaining O(dp) inference.

Relation to prior work includes: global tree optimization (NP-complete [11], early non-greedy MILP [1]), max-margin trees [2,3], soft/probabilistic trees (HME [13]), and recent objectives for greedy trees [20,12]. The novelty here is a latent-structured-prediction formulation for decision trees with a tractable, efficiently optimizable surrogate.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The loss upper bound is standard in form for latent structured prediction; the “fast” variant remains an upper bound since the Hamming ball includes the current code ĥ. The subgradient updates for W are well motivated (ignoring argmax dependence as in structured SVM), and the per-row projection enforces the stated constraints. The complexity analysis of both full and fast loss-augmented inference is plausible, and the O(dp) inference property of trees is preserved. The SSGD heuristic is reasonable for preventing leaf inactivity.
- Empirical support: The experiments cover multiple datasets and depths with hyperparameter tuning. The trends (better test accuracy, reduced overfitting, and improved depth scalability) are consistent. However, quantitative uncertainty (e.g., variance over random seeds) is not reported, and statistical significance is not assessed. The impact of initialization and the SSGD variant is not extensively ablated. The paper evaluates single trees rather than ensembles, which limits practical comparisons.
- Limitations: No generalization bounds or convergence guarantees are provided; the method is non-convex and initialization-dependent. The “fast” bound trades tightness for efficiency; the effect on final accuracy is not analyzed beyond runtime.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a concrete algorithm (Alg. 1), clear definitions of f(·), loss functions, and constraints. The connection to latent structured prediction is well explained. Some points could be clarified:
  - Explicitly state that m ≈ 2^d − 1 for full binary trees when relating O(mp) to O(2^d p).
  - Provide pseudocode or a brief description for the fast loss-augmented inference procedure (Hamming ball analysis, path-based leaf candidates).
  - More detail on SSGD (update schedule, stopping criteria for assignment updates) would aid reproducibility.
  - Clarify whether the fast bound is used throughout training and quantify any accuracy gap relative to the full bound.

Originality
- The mapping of tree training to latent structured prediction with a global surrogate optimized by SGD appears novel relative to prior oblique and max-margin tree work. The fast loss-augmented inference via a radius-1 Hamming ball and per-row norm constraints are also practical contributions. The relationship to CO2 [18] as a special case (depth-1 greedy) is clearly delineated.

Significance
- For single-tree learning, the approach provides a principled and scalable non-greedy alternative that improves accuracy and controls overfitting via a regularized global objective, with retained inference efficiency. This is relevant for scenarios where compact trees are desirable. The broader impact would be stronger with evaluation against widely used ensemble methods (e.g., random forests, gradient boosting) and on larger-scale or regression tasks, but the methodological contribution itself is likely to be of interest and utility to the community.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation connecting tree learning to latent structured prediction with an optimizable upper bound.
- Efficient training via fast loss-augmented inference with O(d^2 p) updates; preserves O(dp) inference.
- Empirical improvements over multiple greedy baselines and over CO2 across depths; reduced overfitting.
- Regularization via per-row norm constraints offers an interpretable trade-off and implicit pruning.
- Practical training heuristics (SSGD) to stabilize leaf usage.

Cons
- No theoretical generalization or convergence guarantees; non-convex and initialization-dependent.
- Limited empirical scope: mainly single-tree comparisons on standard datasets; no ensembles or statistical tests.
- Limited ablations: effect of fast vs full bound on accuracy; impact of SSGD; sensitivity to initialization.
- Some clarity gaps on algorithmic details and complexity statements.

Suggestions for improvement
- Quantify the tightness/impact of the fast bound: compare training loss curves and final accuracy vs the full bound on smaller trees where full inference is feasible.
- Provide ablations on initialization (e.g., random, OC1, CO2) and on SSGD vs SGD, including the fraction of active leaves over training.
- Report variability over multiple random splits/seeds and statistical significance of improvements.
- Include comparisons to strong baselines beyond single trees (e.g., boosted trees, random forests) to contextualize significance, even if the focus remains single-tree training.
- Extend experiments to regression datasets and/or show that the approach generalizes to other losses as claimed.
- Add more details on the fast inference procedure and SSGD schedule for reproducibility.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, clearly written, and contributes a novel and efficient framework for non-greedy decision tree optimization with encouraging empirical results. The work fits well within NeurIPS’s scope at the intersection of optimization and machine learning.
