## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing limitation of greedy algorithms in decision tree induction – the inability to jointly optimize split functions across the tree. The authors propose a novel framework for non-greedy learning based on structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2<sup>dp</sup>) to O(d<sup>2</sup>p), enabling efficient learning of deeper trees. The paper presents experimental results on benchmark datasets demonstrating improved performance compared to greedy baselines and a related greedy approach (CO2).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Core Limitation:** The paper directly confronts a fundamental issue in decision tree learning – the sub-optimality of greedy approaches.
* **Novel Formulation:**  The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and provides a strong theoretical foundation for the non-greedy approach.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The reduction in complexity from O(2<sup>dp</sup>) to O(d<sup>2</sup>p) is a significant contribution, making non-greedy learning feasible for deeper trees. This is crucial for practical applicability.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over greedy baselines across several datasets, suggesting the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the proposed method. The explanation of the surrogate objective and optimization process is clear.
* **Regularization Insight:** The analysis of the effect of the norm of W and the regularization parameter ν is helpful and provides a practical guideline for hyperparameter tuning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Structured Prediction Link:** While the application to decision trees is novel, the use of structured prediction techniques (latent variables, convex-concave bounds, SGD) is well-established in other areas of machine learning. The paper doesn't deeply explore novel contributions *within* structured prediction itself.
* **Complexity Analysis Caveats:** The O(d<sup>2</sup>p) complexity reduction is significant, but it relies on the "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm.  The paper doesn't fully discuss the practical implications of this simplification and potential performance trade-offs.  Could the constant factor hidden within O(d<sup>2</sup>p) be substantial, impacting performance in certain scenarios?
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The baselines, while reasonable, aren’t the *state-of-the-art* in decision tree learning (e.g., gradient boosting machines like XGBoost or LightGBM are not considered).  A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the claims of significance.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The results seem to be sensitive to the regularization parameter ν. The paper demonstrates how to tune this parameter, but doesn’t fully discuss the robustness of the method to different parameter settings.
* **Initialization Dependency:** Initializing with a greedy tree (CO2) is a good starting point, but the paper doesn't explore alternative initialization strategies or their impact on performance.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and well-supported by experimental results. The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation, and the complexity reduction is a valuable contribution. However, the reliance on established structured prediction techniques and the potential limitations of the simplified complexity analysis prevent a higher score.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors do a good job of explaining complex concepts.
* **Originality (7/10):** The application of structured prediction to non-greedy decision tree learning is novel, but the core techniques are not entirely new. The primary originality lies in adapting and optimizing these techniques for this specific problem.
* **Significance (7/10):** The potential to improve decision tree performance through non-greedy learning is significant. However, the practical impact is somewhat limited by the comparison to relatively simple baselines and the need for careful hyperparameter tuning.  The impact would be higher if the method could demonstrably outperform more sophisticated ensemble methods.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a long-standing limitation in decision tree learning.
* Presents a theoretically sound and computationally efficient approach.
* Demonstrates clear improvements over relevant baselines.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Doesn’t present a revolutionary advancement in structured prediction itself.
*  Comparisons to state-of-the-art methods are lacking.
*  Sensitivity to hyperparameters and potential limitations of the complexity simplification.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The non-greedy approach and complexity reduction are valuable additions to the existing toolbox. However, the authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by including comparisons to stronger baselines and further discussing the practical implications of the complexity simplification and hyperparameter tuning.  A brief discussion on potential future directions, such as exploring sparsity-inducing regularization or kernel methods, would also be beneficial.
