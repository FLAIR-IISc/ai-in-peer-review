Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees by casting the sequence of split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a global surrogate objective. The key technical ideas are: (i) formulate the tree’s empirical loss via a latent code h ∈ {−1,+1}^m that encodes split decisions; (ii) derive a convex–concave upper bound on the loss inspired by latent-structured SVMs, enabling joint optimization over all split parameters W and leaf parameters Θ; (iii) introduce a norm constraint on each row of W to regularize and avoid degenerate tightening of the bound; (iv) develop a fast loss-augmented inference procedure by restricting to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing per-step complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p); (v) use SGD with projected updates and a “Stable SGD” variant that freezes leaf assignments for several steps to avoid inactive leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare single non-greedy oblique trees against axis-aligned CART, OC1, random oblique trees, and a greedy depth-1 upper-bound method (CO2). The non-greedy method achieves consistently better test accuracy across depths and appears less prone to overfitting; the regularization parameter ν controls both bound tightness and implicit pruning.

Relation to prior work
The work is well-situated relative to classic greedy tree induction (ID3/CART), oblique trees (OC1), margin-based trees (Bennett et al.), soft trees/HME, and structured SVMs with latent variables. The explicit link between decision tree training and latent structured prediction (with loss-augmented inference and bound optimization) is novel in the tree literature and distinct from probabilistic soft trees and from node-wise greedy oblique optimization (e.g., OC1, CO2). The fast inference trick via a Hamming ball is a pragmatic contribution enabling deep-tree training.

Quality
Strengths:
- The derivation of the upper bound (Eq. 7) is sound and follows the latent-SSVM template; the scale dependence and the role of the norm constraint are well-motivated (Proposition 1).
- The fast variant (Eq. 13) is a valid upper bound; the observation that only d+1 leaves need consideration when flipping at most one bit is correct and leads to the stated O(d^2 p) complexity for loss-augmented inference.
- The optimization recipe (projected SGD) is reasonable for the non-convex objective; the update sparsity argument (only path bits change) justifies efficient per-step cost.
- Experiments show consistent improvements over several reasonable single-tree baselines across depths, supporting the claim that joint non-greedy optimization improves generalization and compactness.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evaluation is limited to single trees; there is no comparison to standard strong baselines that practitioners use (random forests, gradient boosting). While the decision to analyze single-tree regimes is understandable to isolate the effect of non-greedy training, the absence of even a brief ensemble comparison weakens claims of practical significance.
- Sensitivity to initialization and algorithmic choices is not thoroughly examined. The method initializes with an axis-aligned tree and a greedy CO2 pass; ablations on different inits (random, OC1) and on SSGD vs plain SGD would clarify robustness.
- The fast bound is looser than Eq. (7); an ablation quantifying the accuracy/time trade-off (Hamming radius 0/1/2) would strengthen the justification for the chosen radius.
- Convergence/stability: beyond heuristics (momentum, mini-batches, SSGD), there is no analysis of convergence behavior; reporting training curves for loss bounds and leaf activation dynamics would help.
- Claims of generality to regression are not supported by experiments; only classification datasets are presented.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well-organized. Notation (W, Θ, f, h) is consistent, and Algorithm 1 is helpful.
- Some important proofs are deferred to the supplement (bound correctness, Proposition 1). A brief sketch in the main text would help readers assess correctness without the supplement.
- The projection step (line 8) is written as looping over all rows, but the text suggests only rows with nonzero gradients need projection; clarifying the implemented variant would avoid confusion.
- SSGD is introduced succinctly; more detail on the schedule/criterion for updating assignments a(x) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The formulation of decision tree training as latent structured prediction with a convex–concave upper bound, the per-row norm constraint as a principled regularizer, and the efficient Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference constitute a novel and nontrivial combination in this domain.
- The work is a clear extension beyond CO2 (depth-1, greedy optimization) to joint non-greedy training, with both conceptual and algorithmic advances.

Significance
- For researchers on tree learning, the framework is a meaningful step toward globally-optimized oblique trees while preserving O(dp) inference, and could serve as a basis for further developments (sparsity, kernels).
- Practical impact would be stronger with demonstrations on larger-scale or higher-dimensional settings and with comparisons to strong ensemble baselines; as presented, significance is moderate but credible.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled formulation linking trees to latent structured prediction.
- Solid algorithmic contribution enabling efficient training of deep oblique trees.
- Empirical gains over multiple single-tree baselines and insights into regularization as implicit pruning.
- Preserves fast inference at test time.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited empirical scope: no ensembles, no regression, modest dataset sizes.
- Sensitivity/ablation studies (initialization, SSGD, Hamming radius) are missing.
- Proofs deferred; convergence behavior and practical guidance less developed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: initialization strategies; with/without SSGD; Hamming radius 0/1/2 to show bound tightness vs speed.
- Include at least a small-scale comparison to random forests and gradient boosting (even if single-tree focus remains primary), and a regression dataset if claiming generality.
- Provide more implementation details (assignment update schedule in SSGD; stopping criteria; epoch counts) and release code if possible.
- Summarize key proofs in the main text.

Overall assessment
A technically sound and original contribution that advances non-greedy optimization for decision trees with a principled, efficient framework. Despite some empirical limitations and missing ablations, the paper meets the NeurIPS criteria reasonably well. I lean toward acceptance, with the caveat that strengthening the empirical section would materially improve the paper.
