- Proposes non-greedy training for oblique decision trees by treating split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a global surrogate upper-bound on empirical loss.  
- Derives a convex-concave upper bound inspired by latent structured prediction requiring loss-augmented inference over binary split codes.  
- Exact loss-augmented inference is exponential in tree depth; authors introduce a tractable radius-1 Hamming-ball approximation reducing cost to O(d^2 p) per update.  
- Training uses projected SGD with row-wise norm constraint on split weights and a “Stable SGD” variant that intermittently freezes leaf assignments.  
- Reformulation sgn(Wx)=argmax_h h^T Wx and the derived upper bound are technically sound; surrogate is difference-of-convex and subgradient steps are appropriate.  
- The fast radius-1 variant (Eq. 13) is a valid upper bound and greatly reduces inference cost.  
- Algorithm and complexity (per step O(d^2 p + k)) are fully specified; SSGD heuristic for stabilizing leaf usage is sensible.  
- Empirical results show consistent gains over greedy single-tree baselines and demonstrate scaling benefits of fast inference.  
- No ablation comparing full loss-augmented inference vs the radius-1 approximation to quantify the accuracy vs tightness trade-off.  
- Limited baselines: omits ensemble methods (random forests, gradient boosting), weakening practical significance claims.  
- No statistical significance testing, run variance, or sensitivity analysis to initialization reported.  
- No regression experiments despite claimed generality of the method.  
- Paper is generally clear; mapping from splits to latent variables and role of ν are well explained and algorithmic details are provided.  
- Suggested clarity improvements: include pseudocode for fast inference, a small illustrative example, and dataset/hyperparameter details in an appendix.  
- Originality: novel joint non-greedy formulation linking tree learning to latent structured prediction; radius-1 Hamming-ball trick appears new for tree training.  
- Related work coverage is reasonable but should contrast more with differentiable/soft-routing tree approaches.  
- Scientific significance: offers an optimization-grounded alternative to greedy induction with a practical efficiency trick for deeper trees.  
- Practical significance limited because ensembles are not evaluated and may dominate single-tree performance in applications.  
- Pros: principled formulation, valid upper bounds, efficient inference, clear analysis, and consistent empirical improvements over greedy single-tree baselines.  
- Cons: empirical scope limited (missing ablations, regression, ensembles, variance analysis), theoretical guarantees limited to surrogate properties, and initialization dependence unstudied.  
- Concerns that the radius-1 restriction may overly loosen the bound; its effect on final accuracy is not quantified.  
- Requests for authors: provide ablation full vs radius-1, sensitivity to initialization/ν/learning rate, clarify candidate-flip implementation, report regression/large-k results, and discuss ensemble prospects.  
- Overall recommendation: Lean accept — technically solid and promising but would benefit from stronger empirical ablations and broader baselines.
