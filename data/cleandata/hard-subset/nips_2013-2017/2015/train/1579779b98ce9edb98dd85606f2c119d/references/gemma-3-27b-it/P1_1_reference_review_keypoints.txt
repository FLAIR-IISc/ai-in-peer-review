- Proposes a novel non-greedy decision tree learning framework by casting tree optimization as structured prediction with binary latent split variables.  
- Derives a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss and optimizes it with stochastic gradient descent.  
- Presents a key technical improvement reducing complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p), enabling efficient learning for deep trees.  
- Empirical results on benchmarks show consistent improvements in accuracy and generalization over greedy baselines.  
- Ablation study on the regularization parameter ν provides insight into overfitting control via norm regularization.  
- Paper is generally clear, well-organized, and mathematically well-defined.  
- The approach preserves hard-split computational advantages and handles general loss functions, differentiating it from soft-split methods.  
- Connects to prior work in non-greedy tree learning and structured prediction, but emphasizes efficiency and scalability.  
- Implementation remains relatively complex compared to standard greedy methods, with Alg 1 requiring careful handling.  
- Method appears sensitive to initialization (uses a greedy CO2 tree) which may limit exploration of solutions.  
- Performance depends on careful hyperparameter tuning (η and ν) with limited discussion of selection and sensitivity.  
- Evaluation compares mainly to single-tree baselines; lacks strong comparisons to well-tuned ensemble methods (e.g., random forests).  
- Paper omits discussion of failure cases where non-greedy optimization may not outperform greedy methods.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically solid, experimentally validated, and computationally feasible for deeper trees.  
- Arguments against acceptance: implementation complexity, hyperparameter sensitivity, and incomplete comparison to ensemble baselines.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution that addresses a key limitation of greedy trees; recommend accept with minor revisions.  
- Recommendation: Accept, with requested revisions addressing hyperparameter tuning, initialization, ensemble comparisons, and failure-case discussion.
