Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss via stochastic gradient descent. Each internal split is a linear threshold (oblique) function; leaves hold softmax parameters. The key technical steps are: (i) reformulating split decisions using latent binary codes h and deriving a structural-SVM-like upper bound on the loss; (ii) introducing a per-row norm constraint on W to regularize and stabilize training; (iii) reducing the complexity of loss-augmented inference from exponential in depth to O(d^2 p) by restricting to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around current split decisions; and (iv) a “Stable SGD” variant that fixes leaf assignments for several iterations to avoid deactivating leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets compare single trees against axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and a greedy depth-1 CO2 variant, showing improved test accuracy, reduced overfitting with depth, and better scalability of the fast inference routine.

Relation to prior work
The connection to latent structured prediction follows established lines (structural SVM with latent variables), and the max-margin tree literature (e.g., Bennett and colleagues) is acknowledged. The paper builds on prior oblique-split work (OC1) and the CO2 formulation, positioning this as a joint, non-greedy optimization rather than node-wise. The Hamming-ball approximation is a pragmatic variant akin to loss-augmented inference pruning.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean latent-variable view of split decisions ties tree induction to well-studied optimization frameworks.
- Algorithmic practicality: the Hamming-ball restriction yields a tractable O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference, preserving test-time O(dp).
- Regularization: per-row norm constraints provide a principled knob that empirically mitigates overfitting; the paper connects bound tightness with scale.
- Empirical gains for single trees: across several datasets and depths, the non-greedy approach outperforms the chosen greedy baselines.
- Implementation details: SGD with projection, and a stability trick (SSGD), are described, and runtime trends for the fast inference variant are shown.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evidence scope and baselines: The evaluation is limited to single-tree baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, CO2). In practice, decision trees are rarely used alone; random forests and gradient boosting (already cited) are standard. The paper argues single-tree focus to isolate training effects, but to justify significance, at least (i) ensembles built from the proposed non-greedy trees vs. strong ensemble baselines and (ii) comparisons to modern oblique ensemble methods should be included. Without this, the practical impact remains unclear.
- Novelty vs. prior art: The core idea—optimize a margin-style surrogate with latent split decisions—feels incremental relative to structural SVMs with latent variables and to CO2 (which the authors acknowledge as a special case). The restriction to a radius-1 Hamming ball is a heuristic that changes the surrogate. The paper does not analyze how this modified bound deviates from the original objective beyond computational benefits.
- Theoretical guarantees: The bound is an upper bound; however, no generalization guarantees, convergence analysis, or approximation guarantees for the radius-1 surrogate are provided. Proposition 1 about scale is intuitive but offers limited guidance for choosing ν beyond tuning. The work would benefit from a deeper theoretical treatment.
- Stability/SSGD: SSGD is introduced to prevent leaf deactivation, but it is ad hoc and not analyzed. How often does it improve test accuracy? What are the failure modes? An ablation comparing SGD vs. SSGD across datasets and depths is missing.
- Experimental rigor: 
  - Only multiclass classification is tested; the method claims applicability to arbitrary losses and regression but provides no evidence.
  - No statistical significance tests, variance across runs, or sensitivity to initialization (notably, initialization uses a greedy pipeline). Are gains robust to different seeds/initializations?
  - Hyperparameter tuning details are sparse (e.g., schedule for η, momentum, mini-batch size), and retraining on train+val after model selection may bias results.
  - Runtime comparisons focus on the two variants of the authors’ inference, not on wall-clock vs. standard greedy training or vs. OC1.
- Missing/unclear details: Some derivations are deferred to the supplement (e.g., bound proofs). The algorithmic description of fast loss-augmented inference could be made more explicit (e.g., pseudocode for enumerating the d+1 alternatives). The projection step (per-row L2 constraints) should be carefully notated.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the initialization from CO2/axis-aligned trees? Can you report performance with random W initialization?
2. How often does SSGD help relative to vanilla SGD? Please provide quantitative ablations on accuracy and number of inactive leaves.
3. How do training and test wall-clock times compare to OC1 and standard greedy trees for the same depth and accuracy?
4. Can you provide experiments on regression or with non-log-loss to support the claimed generality?
5. What is the effect of increasing the Hamming-ball radius beyond 1 on accuracy/runtime? Any evidence that radius 1 is sufficient?
6. How does the method fare when used as base learners in bagging/boosting, compared to Random Forests and Gradient Boosted Trees?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ensemble experiments and compare to strong baselines (Random Forests, Gradient Boosted Trees). Also consider Mondrian forests in streaming setups.
- Include regression tasks and/or alternative losses.
- Provide ablations: (i) SGD vs. SSGD; (ii) with/without per-row norm constraint; (iii) different Hamming radii; (iv) different initializations.
- Report variance over multiple runs and statistical significance.
- Expand runtime analysis to wall-clock comparisons against standard training baselines for matched accuracy.
- Clarify algorithmic details (pseudo-code for fast inference; explicit projection formula); move essential proofs from supplement if space allows.

Pros
- Clear formulation linking tree induction with latent structured prediction.
- Practical, scalable loss-augmented inference via Hamming-ball restriction.
- Empirical improvements for single trees; good control of overfitting through ν.
- Maintains O(dp) test-time complexity.

Cons
- Limited baselines; no ensemble comparisons; unclear practical impact.
- Incremental novelty; heuristic surrogate without guarantees.
- Ad hoc stability trick without analysis.
- Narrow experimental scope (classification only), limited reporting of variance and robustness.

Overall assessment
Technically competent and clearly written, with a reasonable algorithmic advance, but the empirical evidence is not yet sufficient to justify acceptance at NeurIPS given the lack of comparisons to widely-used ensemble baselines and limited analysis of the proposed heuristics (Hamming-ball bound, SSGD). I recommend rejection in the current form, with the above additions likely to strengthen the case significantly.
