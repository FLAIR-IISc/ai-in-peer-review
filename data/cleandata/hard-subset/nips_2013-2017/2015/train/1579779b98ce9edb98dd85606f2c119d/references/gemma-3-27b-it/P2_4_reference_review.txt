## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing problem of non-greedy optimization of decision trees, arguing that the inherent limitations of greedy approaches hinder generalization performance. The authors propose a novel framework leveraging structured prediction with latent variables to formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, which can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent. A key technical contribution lies in reducing the computational complexity of this optimization from O(2dp) to O(d2p), enabling the training of deeper trees. The paper demonstrates empirically that this non-greedy approach outperforms greedy baselines on several benchmark datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:**  The limitations of greedy decision tree induction are well-recognized, and finding efficient non-greedy alternatives is a significant challenge in machine learning. This work directly tackles this challenge.
* **Novel Formulation:**  The connection to structured prediction with latent variables provides a fresh perspective and a principled framework for non-greedy optimization. The formulation is mathematically sound and well-motivated.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The reduction in complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is crucial for scaling the method to reasonably deep trees, making it more practical.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on multiple datasets consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over various greedy baselines, supporting the claims of improved generalization. The comparison to CO2, a related greedy approach, is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, formulation, and optimization procedure.  The inclusion of Alg 1 aids in understanding the implementation details.
* **Regularization Insight:** The discussion of the regularization parameter ν and its connection to pruning is a valuable observation and demonstrates a good understanding of the trade-offs involved.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Core Ideas:** While the *combination* of techniques is novel, the individual components (structured prediction, convex-concave optimization, SGD) are well-established in the literature. The core novelty lies in the application to decision trees and the complexity reduction. 
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the choice of the regularization parameter ν, requiring careful tuning. The paper mentions this but doesn't delve into the challenges of choosing a good value for ν in practice.
* **Scalability Concerns (Beyond Complexity):**  While the complexity reduction to O(d2p) is significant, the experiments are performed on relatively small datasets. It’s unclear how well the method will scale to truly massive datasets (e.g., millions of features or training examples). The Connect4 dataset is mentioned as having only 55,000 training points, which limits the demonstration of scalability.
* **Initialization Dependency:** The use of a greedy tree (specifically, CO2) for initialization could potentially bias the results.  It would be valuable to explore the sensitivity of the method to different initialization strategies.
* **Lack of Theoretical Generalization Bounds:**  While the paper demonstrates empirical improvements, it lacks theoretical guarantees regarding generalization performance. A formal analysis of the generalization error would strengthen the contribution.



**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):**  The paper is technically sound and the claims are supported by experimental results. The optimization framework is well-defined and the complexity reduction is a significant technical achievement. However, the lack of theoretical generalization bounds lowers the score slightly.
* **Clarity (9/10):**  The paper is generally very clear and well-organized. The explanations are easy to follow, and the inclusion of Alg 1 is helpful. 
* **Originality (7/10):** The combination of existing techniques to address decision tree learning is novel, but the individual techniques are not groundbreaking.  The major originality comes from the application and complexity reduction.
* **Significance (8/10):**  The potential to improve decision tree performance, a widely used technique, is significant. The method could be particularly valuable in applications where generalization is paramount. The impact hinges on its scalability to truly large datasets.



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a long-standing problem with a novel and principled approach.
* Empirically demonstrates improved performance over strong baselines.
* Presents a significant technical contribution in terms of computational efficiency.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on existing techniques without introducing fundamentally new theoretical ideas.
* Scalability to very large datasets remains a concern.
* Lacks theoretical guarantees regarding generalization.
* Sensitive to hyperparameter tuning.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept.**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed non-greedy optimization framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates promising empirical results.  The computational efficiency improvements are particularly significant. While theoretical analysis and scalability testing with larger datasets would be beneficial for future work, the current paper provides a strong foundation for further research in this area.  I believe this work is of sufficient quality and significance to be included in NIPS.
