## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a long-standing problem in machine learning: the inherent limitations of greedy decision tree induction. The authors propose a novel framework for non-greedy learning of split parameters by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. They introduce a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, enabling optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution lies in reducing the computational complexity from O(2<sup>dp</sup>) to O(d<sup>2</sup>p), making the approach feasible for deeper trees. The paper includes experimental validation on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating improved performance and generalization compared to greedy baselines.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing landscape of decision tree optimization. It acknowledges the NP-completeness of global tree optimization and references relevant work like Bennett's multi-linear programming approach [1], Mondrian Processes [15], and Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13]. The authors effectively differentiate their work by highlighting its ability to handle general loss functions, maintain the O(dp) inference complexity of trees, and provide natural regularization. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables [25] is a novel and insightful contribution.  It's good that the authors acknowledge Murthy and Salzburg's [17] arguments against non-greedy methods, and address the potential for overfitting through regularization, referencing the margin-based approaches of Bennett & Blue [2].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The framing of decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables is a strong conceptual contribution. This unlocks the potential to leverage techniques from structured prediction to improve tree learning.
* **Efficiency Improvement:**  Reducing the computational complexity from O(2<sup>dp</sup>) to O(d<sup>2</sup>p) is a significant technical achievement. This makes non-greedy optimization practical for trees with moderate depth, opening up possibilities for more complex models.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements in test performance over various greedy baselines across multiple datasets. The analysis of the regularization parameter (ν) and its effect on tree complexity is insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are reasonably clear, and the algorithm is presented in a digestible manner.
* **Addresses Overfitting:** The inclusion of a norm regularization term effectively tackles the potential overfitting issues associated with non-greedy methods, as previously highlighted by Murthy and Salzburg [17].

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the theoretical complexity reduction is significant, the implementation of the surrogate objective, loss-augmented inference, and the SSGD algorithm appears complex. The practical overhead beyond the theoretical complexity isn’t fully discussed.
* **Scalability Concerns:**  While O(d<sup>2</sup>p) is an improvement, it still limits scalability for very deep trees or high-dimensional data.  The experiments don’t explore particularly large datasets, and the runtime comparison (Fig. 4) is limited to a single dataset (Connect4).  More extensive scaling experiments would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the regularization parameter ν.  The paper doesn’t thoroughly investigate the robustness of the method to different values of ν or explore automated hyperparameter tuning strategies.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison to greedy methods is thorough, the paper lacks a comparison to more recent and sophisticated decision tree ensemble methods like gradient boosting machines (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM) or modern random forest implementations.  A comparison to these would better contextualize the benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Dependence on Greedy Initialization:** The method relies on a greedy initialization (CO2 [18]). While the authors justify this, the potential impact of the initialization on the final solution isn’t fully explored.



**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to non-greedy decision tree learning.
* The complexity reduction is a significant contribution, enabling practical application of non-greedy optimization.
* The empirical results demonstrate consistent performance gains over strong greedy baselines.
* The inclusion of regularization addresses a key challenge in non-greedy learning.

**Con Acceptance:**

* The implementation complexity and scalability concerns may limit the practical applicability of the method.
* The lack of comparison to state-of-the-art ensemble methods weakens the assessment of the significance of the results.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity requires further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to decision tree learning. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the experimental results are encouraging. However, the scalability concerns and limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods prevent me from giving it a strongly positive recommendation. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the following points:

* **Scaling Experiments:**  Conduct more extensive scaling experiments on larger datasets to demonstrate the practical scalability of the proposed method.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  Include a comparison to modern gradient boosting machines and random forest implementations.
* **Hyperparameter Robustness:** Investigate the robustness of the method to different values of ν and explore automated hyperparameter tuning strategies.
* **Discussion of Implementation Overhead:**  Provide a more detailed discussion of the implementation challenges and overhead associated with the surrogate objective and the optimized algorithms.
