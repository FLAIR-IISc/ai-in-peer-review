Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss using stochastic gradient descent. Each internal node uses an oblique linear threshold, and each leaf carries a parametric distribution (softmax) over labels. The key idea is to rewrite sgn(Wx) as the argmax of a linear score over binary codes (latent h), enabling a latent structured prediction view. The authors derive an upper bound on loss that involves a loss-augmented inference term max_g g^T W x + loss(Θ^T f(g), y) minus an inference term max_h h^T W x. To make training efficient for deep trees, they introduce a restricted “Hamming ball” bound where g is constrained to be within Hamming distance 1 of sgn(Wx), reducing the complexity of loss-augmented inference from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) while remaining an upper bound. They further regularize the per-row norms of W to counteract trivial tightening of the bound via scaling. Optimization is via SGD with projection and a “Stable SGD” variant that freezes leaf assignments intermittently. Experiments on several multiclass datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) show that the proposed non-greedy trees outperform various greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique, and CO2) across depths, with better generalization and manageable training time using the fast bound.

Relation to prior work
- Greedy tree induction (ID3/CART) and regularization via depth/pruning/bagging/boosting are standard; this paper addresses the long-standing challenge of global optimization of tree splits (NP-complete) by a tractable surrogate.
- It connects decision trees to latent structured SVMs (e.g., Yu & Joachims), with a CCCP-like upper bound and loss-augmented inference, and improves upon prior non-greedy oblique split training such as CO2 (which is greedy, depth-1).
- OC1 and SVM-at-nodes approaches are relevant but do not jointly optimize all splits under a global objective with an explicit, efficiently optimizable surrogate; soft trees (HME) trade accuracy for higher inference cost, which this work avoids.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a clear and principled link between tree induction and latent structured prediction, yielding an explicit, optimizable upper bound on empirical loss.
- Technical contribution: fast loss-augmented inference via Hamming-ball restriction that preserves the upper-bound property while reducing complexity to O(d^2 p), enabling deep trees.
- Practical training procedure: SGD with per-row norm projection, and a “Stable SGD” innovation to avoid premature leaf inactivity.
- Empirical evidence: consistent test-accuracy gains over strong greedy baselines across multiple datasets and tree depths; good analysis of the role of the norm constraint ν and its effect on pruning/overfitting; scaling study comparing fast vs full loss-augmented inference.
- Preserves tree inference efficiency (O(d p)) at test time.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope of experiments: focuses on single trees; while the authors justify this to isolate split learning effects, comparisons to standard ensembles (RF, gradient boosting, or oblique forests including CO2 in ensemble form) would better demonstrate practical significance.
- Initialization sensitivity: the method initializes via axis-aligned and CO2 training. How sensitive are final results to initialization quality? An ablation with random initialization or weaker initialization would clarify robustness.
- Ablations: it would help to separate the effects of (a) global non-greedy joint optimization, (b) the upper-bound surrogate (full vs Hamming-ball), (c) row-norm constraints, and (d) SSGD. Some of these are discussed qualitatively, but quantitative ablations would strengthen the paper.
- Theory: while the upper bound is well motivated and the scaling regularization is justified, there are no generalization bounds or convergence guarantees (the landscape remains non-convex). Even a discussion of conditions under which SSGD improves stability would be useful.
- Implementation details: SSGD update schedule (when to refresh assignments), mini-batch sizes, and momentum specifics are only briefly mentioned; reproducibility could benefit from more specifics or code.

Quality
- The derivations are sound, with correct use of latent structured prediction machinery. The fast bound remains a valid upper bound due to inclusion of ĥ within the Hamming-1 ball. The regularization argument (Proposition 1) is intuitive and aligns with margin-rescaling behavior.
- Empirical claims are supported by multiple datasets and comparisons. However, absence of ensemble comparisons and limited ablations slightly weaken the empirical case for broad impact.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. The problem setup, notation (W, Θ, f, h), and algorithms are explained cleanly. The complexity analysis is explicit. Some additional pseudo-code for fast loss-augmented inference and more detail on SSGD scheduling would help reproducibility. Minor reference formatting issues exist but are not critical.

Originality
- The latent-structured formulation for decision trees and the fast upper-bound variant are novel in this context. The work goes beyond CO2 by jointly optimizing all splits with a global surrogate and by enabling deep-tree training efficiently. To my knowledge, this combination and analysis are original.

Significance
- Improving single-tree training is impactful both scientifically and practically: better trees improve interpretability and can feed into ensembles. The method likely will influence follow-on work on non-greedy oblique trees, structured surrogates for discrete models, and efficient inference-bounded training. Demonstrating competitive performance versus strong ensembles would further elevate significance.

Pros
- Principled non-greedy joint optimization via latent structured prediction.
- Efficient loss-augmented inference enabling deep trees (O(d^2 p)).
- Consistent empirical improvements over several greedy baselines.
- Clear role for regularization; preserves fast inference.

Cons
- No comparison to ensemble methods; practical impact not fully quantified.
- Limited ablations (init sensitivity, SSGD effect, fast vs full bound).
- No theoretical generalization/convergence guarantees.
- Some implementation details omitted.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper makes a solid, well-justified methodological contribution with clear technical ideas and demonstrable empirical benefits. While additional experiments (ensembles and ablations) would strengthen the case, the novelty and quality meet NeurIPS standards, and the approach is likely to be used and extended by the community.

Minor comments
- Please clarify SSGD schedule (number of steps between assignment updates, stopping criteria).
- Report wall-clock comparisons to greedy training, not just scaling trends.
- Consider adding an ablation on initialization and an ensemble comparison in supplementary material.
- Fix reference formatting inconsistencies.
