This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to learning decision trees, framing the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and developing an efficient optimization scheme based on a convex-concave upper bound. The core idea – jointly optimizing split functions across the tree rather than greedily – is a compelling and potentially significant departure from decades of established practice. The link to structured prediction and the resulting optimization framework are well-motivated and presented.

The paper's strength lies in its novelty. While non-greedy tree learning isn't entirely unexplored (as acknowledged in the related work), this formulation, leveraging structured prediction and developing the efficient O(d2p) complexity algorithm, represents a genuinely fresh approach. The potential benefits – more compact trees and improved generalization – are intuitively plausible and supported by the experimental results. The introduction of the surrogate objective and the careful analysis to reduce computational complexity are significant contributions. The explicit connection to regularization via the norm constraint on W is a clever addition, providing a tunable lever for controlling model complexity.

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses. While the experimental validation is encouraging, it feels somewhat limited. The comparisons are primarily against other tree-based methods, and it's not entirely clear how this approach stacks up against more modern machine learning techniques like gradient boosting or deep learning, *especially* considering the datasets chosen are relatively small. A more rigorous comparison, perhaps with a hyperparameter sweep for competing methods, would strengthen the claims.  The discussion of the "Stable SGD" variant feels somewhat cursory; the benefits aren’t fully elucidated with a clear performance comparison against standard SGD. Further, while the theoretical justification for the upper bound is present, the practical impact of the tightness of this bound on performance isn't deeply explored. Finally, the code is not available, which hinders reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **High novelty:** The approach represents a significant departure from standard greedy tree induction.
* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The connection to structured prediction and the derived optimization framework are well-motivated and analyzed.
* **Potential for impact:**  The promise of more compact and generalizable trees is attractive.
* **Efficient algorithm:** The reduction of complexity to O(d2p) is crucial for practical applicability.
* **Promising empirical results:** Demonstrates improved performance compared to relevant baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited experimental scope:** Comparison is largely within the tree-based methods paradigm, lacking comparisons with more recent techniques.
* **Superficial discussion of SSGD:** The benefits of the Stable SGD variant are not clearly demonstrated.
* **Lack of code availability:** Hampers reproducibility and further exploration by the research community.
* **Incomplete validation:** While the results are promising, a more thorough investigation of the method’s robustness and scalability is needed.



Overall, despite the minor flaws, the paper’s novelty and potential impact are substantial. The authors have identified a critical limitation of conventional decision tree learning and proposed a clever solution. While the experimental validation could be more comprehensive, the core ideas are compelling and warrant acceptance. I would lean towards accepting this paper, with a strong recommendation that the authors release their code and consider expanding the experimental evaluation in a future version. The paper has the potential to open up a new direction in decision tree research.
