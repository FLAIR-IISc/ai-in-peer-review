Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for hard-decision trees by casting the optimization of all split parameters under a single global objective. The key idea is to view the vector of split outcomes across internal nodes as a latent binary variable, linking decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables. The empirical loss of a tree is upper-bounded by a convex-concave surrogate that combines a score term (g^T W x) with a loss term on the leaf selected by g. This yields a loss-augmented inference problem similar to structural SVMs and latent SVMs. Direct loss-augmented inference over all 2^m binary codes is intractable, so the authors introduce an efficient variant that restricts the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current code sgn(Wx), reducing the per-update complexity to O(d^2 p) for depth d and input dimension p, while preserving O(d p) inference at test-time. The method is trained with SGD, with per-row norm constraints on W acting as regularization that also controls bound tightness; a stable-SGD variant (SSGD) freezes leaf assignments intermittently to reduce instability (leaf inactivity). Experiments on four LibSVM multi-class datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) show that single non-greedy trees outperform several greedy baselines, including axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2 (a greedy depth-1 variant of the bound).

This work relates to: global/non-greedy tree optimization (Bennett), max-margin splits (Bennett and Blue; margin enlargement), soft/deep gating models (HME) where inference is expensive, online Mondrian forests, and CO2 (the authors’ prior greedy oblique split optimization). The structural SVM lineage (Taskar; Tsochantaridis; Yu & Joachims) motivates the bound and loss-augmented inference perspective.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean and principled reformulation of hard-split tree training as latent structured prediction, with a practical, optimizable upper bound on empirical loss.
- Technical contributions: 
  - A surrogate objective with per-row norm constraints on W, including analysis of how scaling affects bound tightness (Proposition 1).
  - An efficient loss-augmented inference via a radius-1 Hamming ball, reducing updates to O(d^2 p) while maintaining hard decision paths at inference.
  - SGD/SSGD procedures with simple projection and sparse updates aligned to the tree structure.
- Practicality: Maintains the test-time efficiency of trees; applicable to general losses (classification and regression).
- Empirical evidence: Single-tree improvements over multiple greedy baselines across depths; analysis of the effect of the regularization parameter ν; runtime comparison showing exponential vs. quadratic scaling in depth for the two inference strategies.
- Clarity of main ideas and algorithms; the relationship to prior work is explained and well-cited.

Weaknesses
- Scope and breadth of experiments: Only single-tree results on four datasets; no regression tasks despite the framework supporting them; limited comparisons to strong modern baselines (e.g., boosted/gradient-boosted trees, soft/differentiable trees), and no ensembles to assess whether the gains persist when bagging/boosting is used.
- Limited ablations: The dependence on initialization (axis-aligned + CO2) is strong; there is little analysis of sensitivity to initialization or of the relative contributions of fast loss-augmented inference vs. full inference vs. SSGD. ν is tuned but its mechanism on pruning and generalization could be analyzed more systematically.
- Theory: Aside from Proposition 1 and complexity arguments, there are no generalization guarantees or margin-based bounds tailored to the proposed objective, nor convergence analysis for SSGD with frozen assignments.
- Practical considerations: Wall-clock training costs and memory are only partially reported; constant factors for O(d^2 p) could be discussed. The projection-based per-row hard constraints may complicate hyperparameter selection compared to standard L2 regularization.
- Clarity nits: Some notation (navigation function f, path-consistency constraints) could benefit from a more explicit pseudo-code for fast loss-augmented inference; details of momentum/minibatching are deferred.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the bound and optimization are well motivated and implemented. Claims are supported by experiments, though broader evaluations would strengthen the case. Proof details in the supplement should be checked.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Adding explicit pseudo-code for the fast loss-augmented inference and SSGD would improve reproducibility. A schematic illustrating which bits can flip under the Hamming ball bound would help.

Originality
- The latent structured prediction view and the specific upper-bound plus efficient radius-1 inference for hard-split trees are original and non-trivial advances over greedy split optimization and prior CO2.

Significance
- For researchers and practitioners who rely on decision trees for fast inference, enabling non-greedy global optimization with retained test-time efficiency is valuable. The approach could influence oblique tree design and hybrid tree-neural models. Broader impact would be clearer with stronger baselines and regression/ensemble results.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include regression experiments and at least one ensemble experiment (bagging/boosting) to assess whether non-greedy optimization yields additive gains in common usage.
- Compare against soft probabilistic trees (e.g., HME) and recent differentiable tree/forest methods, reporting both accuracy and inference cost.
- Provide ablations: effect of initialization (random vs. axis-aligned vs. CO2), effect of SSGD vs. SGD, and accuracy gap between full and fast loss-augmented inference.
- Offer more guidance on selecting ν, perhaps via a margin proxy; consider L2 regularization as an alternative to hard constraints and report differences.
- Report wall-clock training times and memory across depths and datasets; add a high-dimensional sparse dataset to probe scalability.
- Discuss potential generalization analyses (e.g., margin-based guarantees per path).

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, well-motivated formulation that bridges decision trees and latent structured prediction.
- Non-trivial algorithmic advance (fast loss-augmented inference) preserving test-time efficiency.
- Consistent empirical gains for single trees and insight into regularization controlling pruning/generalization.
- Clear relevance to NeurIPS audience.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental scope limited; missing comparisons to some strong baselines and to regression/ensembles.
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond the optimization surrogate and scaling proposition.
- Dependence on initialization not fully characterized.

Recommendation
- Accept.

Confidence
- High.
