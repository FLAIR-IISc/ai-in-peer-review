Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for oblique decision trees by casting split decisions as latent variables and optimizing a global surrogate objective inspired by structured prediction with latent variables. Each internal node’s binary decision is treated as a latent bit h ∈ {−1,+1}^m, with a navigation function mapping h to a leaf; leaf parameters produce class probabilities via a softmax (or regression outputs). The empirical loss is upper-bounded by a convex-concave surrogate consisting of a loss-augmented inference term minus an inference term. Optimizing this bound with SGD jointly updates split parameters W and leaf parameters Θ, with per-row norm constraints on W to regularize and control the bound’s tightness.

A key technical contribution is an efficient loss-augmented inference scheme. The naive maximization over g ∈ {−1,+1}^m is O(2^d p). The paper introduces a restricted bound that only considers g within Hamming distance 1 of sgn(Wx), yielding loss-augmented inference over at most d+1 candidate leaves and an overall O(d^2 p) per-step complexity. The authors also propose a “Stable SGD” variant that temporarily fixes assignments of examples to leaves to prevent many leaves from becoming inactive during training.

Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) compare the proposed non-greedy trees to axis-aligned greedy trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and CO2 (a greedy depth-1 continuous optimization baseline). The proposed method generally improves test accuracy across depths, overfits less, and trains efficiently with the fast loss-augmented inference.

Relation to prior work
- Classical tree induction (ID3/CART) is greedy; OC1 [17] optimizes oblique splits locally; structured prediction (MMNs, structural SVMs with latent variables) has similar margin-rescaling bounds and loss-augmented inference [23–25]. The paper’s formulation imports these ideas into decision tree training while retaining hard-split inference at test time.
- Hierarchical mixtures of experts [13] use soft splits and EM but lose tree inference efficiency. The present work preserves O(dp) inference and uses hard splits.
- Prior attempts at global tree optimization (e.g., MIPs [1]) do not scale; max-margin trees [2,3] regularize margins but do not offer a joint latent-variable bound with efficient training.
- CO2 [18] is a related greedy, depth-1 upper-bound approach; here the authors optimize jointly over the whole tree and show empirical gains.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: an elegant bridge from decision trees to latent structured prediction, with a justified surrogate objective and regularization.
- Technical contribution: the Hamming ball restriction leads to a practical O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference while still upper-bounding the loss.
- Practicality: SGD with per-row projection is simple and efficient; inference remains O(dp) at test time.
- Empirical results: consistent improvements over multiple strong greedy baselines across depths, and an informative analysis of the regularization parameter ν and its effect on pruning/overfitting.
- SSGD is a pragmatic fix to a real instability (inactive leaves), improving training stability.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Bound tightness vs efficiency: The Hamming radius-1 restriction loosens the bound. The paper shows runtime benefits but does not quantify how much this affects final accuracy vs the full bound on small trees/datasets; an ablation would strengthen the case.
- Experimental scope: Only single trees are evaluated. While the focus is justified to isolate training effects, modern practice often uses forests/boosting; demonstrating gains in forests (e.g., replacing greedy splits in Random Forests or boosting) would increase significance.
- Baseline coverage: OC1 and axis-aligned trees are dated; comparisons to more recent oblique split methods or neuralized/differentiable trees would be valuable (though HME [13] is discussed).
- Reproducibility details: Important training hyperparameters (learning rate schedule, momentum, minibatch sizes, number of passes, initialization sensitivity) are not fully specified; no code availability is mentioned.
- Statistical rigor: No error bars or multiple random seeds are reported; it’s unclear how sensitive results are to initialization (CO2 warm start seems important).

Quality
- The derivations are sound; the upper bounds follow cleanly from max inequalities, and Proposition 1’s monotonicity is plausible (proof in supplement). The algorithm is coherent, and complexity analysis is convincing.
- The empirical methodology is reasonable, with per-depth hyperparameter tuning and appropriate baselines, but could be strengthened with more rigorous statistical reporting and ablations.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The mapping from h to leaves via f, the role of Θ, and the SGD updates (Algorithm 1) are clear. The fast inference derivation is easy to follow. Minor suggestions:
  - Provide a concise pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference with Hamming ball restriction.
  - Clarify how inactive leaves are handled in SSGD and the schedule for updating assignments.
  - Report exact optimization settings to aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The formulation of tree training as latent structured prediction with a tractable loss-augmented inference via Hamming-ball restriction is novel and nontrivial. While it builds on established structural SVM concepts, the adaptation to trees with hard splits and the efficient inference design are original contributions.

Significance
- Improving single-tree training in a non-greedy, scalable way is valuable; trees remain widely used, and better training can reduce tree size and improve generalization. The method seems broadly applicable (any loss; potential for kernels/sparse W as noted). Demonstrated gains over strong baselines suggest real practical impact, though showing benefits in ensembles would elevate significance.

Questions and suggestions
- How sensitive are results to initialization? What happens with random W/Θ?
- Can you report accuracy when using the full loss-augmented inference (no Hamming restriction) on shallow trees to assess the trade-off?
- Provide training time comparisons to baselines (OC1, CO2) at matched accuracy.
- Do you observe leaf probability calibration improvements vs greedy trees?
- Could ν be adapted per level or node to better control pruning?
- Any convergence diagnostics for SSGD (e.g., number of assignment updates until stabilization)?

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear and well-motivated formulation bringing latent structured prediction to decision trees.
- Solid technical contribution enabling efficient non-greedy training.
- Consistent empirical gains and good practical scalability.
- Likely to inspire follow-up work on forests and regularized oblique splits.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth (no ensembles, few modern baselines) and lack of ablations on the tightness-speed trade-off.
- Reproducibility details could be improved; statistical analyses are limited.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution with clear novelty and practical impact. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement to include additional ablations and training details.
