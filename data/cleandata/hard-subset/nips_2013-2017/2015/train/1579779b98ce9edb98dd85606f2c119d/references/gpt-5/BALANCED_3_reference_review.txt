Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a non-greedy method for training oblique decision trees by jointly optimizing all split functions under a global surrogate objective. The key idea is to view the vector of split decisions along the tree as a latent binary variable h, linking decision tree training to structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A naive loss-augmented inference step is exponential in tree depth d; the paper introduces a tighter, tractable variant that restricts loss-augmented search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current split decisions, reducing per-iteration complexity to O(d^2 p) for p-dimensional inputs. They also propose a “Stable SGD” variant that updates leaf assignments less frequently to avoid deactivating leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multi-class datasets compare the method against greedy axis-aligned trees (CART), OC1 oblique trees, random oblique splits, and CO2 (a depth-1 continuous optimization approach), demonstrating improved test accuracy across a range of depths and better robustness to overfitting.

This work relates to classical greedy tree induction (ID3, CART), oblique split optimization (OC1), max-margin trees and SVM-based splits, and soft decision trees such as Hierarchical Mixture of Experts. Unlike soft trees, the proposed method keeps hard splits and O(dp) inference. It also connects to structured SVMs with latent variables (margin-rescaling), using loss-augmented inference to define and optimize an upper bound. CO2 can be seen as a depth-1 instance of the proposed upper bound; this paper extends it to joint, non-greedy optimization over entire trees with an efficient inference scheme.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean and compelling reduction of hard-split tree training to latent structured prediction, with an analyzable surrogate objective and principled regularization.
- Technical contribution: An efficient loss-augmented inference via a radius-1 Hamming ball that yields O(d^2 p) per SGD step, enabling deeper trees.
- Practicality: Maintains hard, fast splits at inference; simple SGD implementation with projection, compatible with standard losses (log loss; squared loss).
- Empirical evidence: Consistent improvements over multiple strong greedy baselines across tree depths; non-greedy optimization improves upon CO2 in most cases.
- Insight on regularization: Row-wise norm constraints control bound tightness and implicitly prune/deactivate leaves, helping generalization.

Weaknesses
- Theory is limited to an upper bound and a monotonicity result w.r.t. scaling W; there are no generalization guarantees or convergence analyses for the non-convex optimization.
- The fast bound (Hamming radius 1) is looser; the practical trade-off between tightness and speed is not quantified (e.g., radius >1 ablation).
- Experimental scope is modest: four datasets, no statistical tests, limited metrics, and no regression tasks despite claiming generality. Comparisons to widely used ensemble methods (boosted trees/GBDT), modern oblique tree learners, or soft-tree EM methods are absent; the focus on single trees is understandable but limits significance claims for practice.
- Sensitivity to initialization and hyperparameters (ν, η) is not deeply analyzed; heavy reliance on greedy initialization may be critical.
- The SSGD variant is heuristic; its impact and cost versus standard SGD are not systematically evaluated.
- Some implementation details are sparse (e.g., how ties/near-zero margins are handled; mini-batch sizes; momentum; stopping criteria; per-dataset ν/η selections), which may affect reproducibility.

Quality
Overall technically sound: the surrogate is correctly derived, the efficient inference is valid for the modified bound, and the algorithm is well-motivated. Empirical results are positive but not exhaustive; stronger baselines and ablations would bolster claims. No apparent errors, but more diagnostics (e.g., active leaf counts, margin distributions) would help.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The structured prediction connection is clearly explained; the algorithm is specified with pseudocode. Some notational density remains (e.g., f(·), Hamming ball constraints) and could benefit from an illustrative figure for the radius-1 inference and a concrete walk-through on a small tree. Explicit pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference would help.

Originality
The latent structured prediction view of hard decision trees and the efficient radius-1 loss-augmented inference are novel in this context. The work extends CO2 beyond depth-1 in a principled way.

Significance
For single-tree learning, the contribution is meaningful: a practical, non-greedy alternative with better generalization across depths. The impact for real-world practice may be limited unless integrated into forests/boosting; however, the formulation could influence future non-greedy or differentiable tree methods.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Quantify the effect of the radius-1 restriction: compare r=0/1/2 (when feasible) to show the accuracy/speed trade-off.
- Provide ablations on initialization (random vs. greedy vs. CO2), ν sensitivity curves, and the impact of SSGD vs. SGD on accuracy and number of active leaves.
- Add comparisons to a strong boosted-tree baseline (e.g., gradient boosting) and, if possible, a soft-tree method (HME or recent differentiable trees) to contextualize accuracy vs. compute at inference.
- Report wall-clock to reach a target accuracy and the number of epochs; include standard deviations over multiple runs.
- Include at least one regression dataset to substantiate generality.
- Discuss handling of near-zero margins (stability) and any tie-breaking; report margin distributions and how ν affects them.
- Consider L1 or group-sparse regularization on W for interpretability/sparsity; even a small experiment would be informative.
- Release code and exact hyperparameters to aid reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, principled formulation with efficient algorithmic innovations.
- Clear empirical gains over strong greedy/oblique baselines across depths.
- Maintains fast, hard-split inference; broadly applicable losses.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth and missing comparisons to dominant ensemble methods.
- No theoretical generalization guarantees; optimization is heuristic and non-convex.
- The key efficiency trick relies on loosening the bound; limited analysis of the trade-off.

Recommendation
Weak Accept. The paper offers a well-motivated and practical non-greedy training paradigm for hard decision trees with novel insights and consistent empirical improvements. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and adding ablations would solidify the contribution. Confidence: medium.
