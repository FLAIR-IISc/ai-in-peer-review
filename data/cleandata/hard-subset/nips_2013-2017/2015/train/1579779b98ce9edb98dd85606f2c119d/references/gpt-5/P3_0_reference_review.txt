Summary
This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to training decision trees by jointly optimizing all split functions under a global objective. The key idea is to view the m binary split decisions for an input as a latent vector h ∈ {−1,+1}^m and to express the tree’s prediction loss as a function of the leaf selected by h. The authors introduce an upper bound on the empirical loss, structurally akin to latent structural SVM objectives, yielding a piecewise linear convex–concave surrogate. They optimize this surrogate with projected SGD, constraining each split’s L2 norm to control the tightness of the bound and regularization. A naïve loss-augmented inference step would be exponential in tree depth; they derive a modified bound restricting the loss-augmented search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), which keeps the bound valid while reducing per-update complexity to O(d^2 p). They further propose a “Stable SGD” variant that temporarily fixes leaf assignments to avoid deactivating leaves. Empirically, on several LibSVM multiclass datasets, their non-greedy oblique trees outperform standard greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) and a greedy one-node-at-a-time variant (CO2), particularly at larger depths, and show improved resistance to overfitting.

Relation to prior work
- NP-hardness of global tree optimization is well known (Hyafil & Rivest). Earlier non-greedy attempts (Bennett) had high complexity or limited losses. OC1 offered heuristic oblique splits; SVM trees and large-margin trees improved margins but still largely greedy.
- Soft/probabilistic trees (e.g., HME) enable smooth optimization but sacrifice tree-time efficiency at inference. The proposed model preserves hard splits and O(dp) test-time inference while enabling joint training via a latent-variable surrogate.
- The structured-prediction perspective and latent SVM-like upper bounds are well-established; the paper adapts these ideas to decision trees, including a practically important fast loss-augmented inference via a restricted search around sgn(Wx).

Strengths
- Clear and principled connection between hard-split trees and latent structured prediction, with a valid upper bound surrogate and a sensible regularization scheme.
- Efficient training algorithm: the Hamming-ball restriction yields O(d^2 p) loss-augmented inference, enabling deeper trees than naïve formulations.
- Practical SGD with per-row projection; SSGD is a reasonable heuristic to stabilize leaf usage.
- Empirical gains across multiple datasets and depths; reduced overfitting compared to greedy baselines.
- Maintains O(dp) inference at test time and accommodates general losses, not just 0-1.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theory: While the bound is valid and Proposition 1 clarifies scaling effects, there is no generalization analysis (e.g., margin-based bounds for trees under per-node norm constraints) or conditions under which the surrogate meaningfully correlates with test error. The discussion of how ν trades off tightness vs optimization smoothness is qualitative; sharper theory would strengthen the contribution.
- Initialization and ablations: Training starts from a CO2-initialized oblique tree. It would help to quantify how much of the gain stems from the non-greedy joint optimization vs the initialization. An ablation comparing full loss-augmented inference vs the Hamming-ball approximation on shallow trees would also be informative.
- Experimental scope: Only multiclass classification is evaluated, despite claims of generality to arbitrary loss and regression. Comparisons to strong modern baselines (e.g., gradient boosting, extremely randomized trees) are missing; while the single-tree focus is defensible to isolate the contribution, some ensemble results would contextualize practical impact.
- Practical details: The SSGD schedule (frequency of assignment updates, stopping criteria), feature preprocessing, and hyperparameters (momentum, batch sizes, epochs) are not fully specified, limiting reproducibility. The effect of ν on implicit pruning is interesting but would benefit from quantitative analysis (e.g., leaves used vs accuracy).
- Complexity details: The fast loss-augmented inference relies on evaluating up to d+1 candidate leaves; an explicit algorithmic description and discussion of constants would help. Memory and time for storing/updating assignments in SSGD are not analyzed.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The surrogate bound is correct (and remains valid under the Hamming-ball restriction since it contains ĥ = sgn(Wx)). The optimization is a reasonable projected SGD for a DC-like objective. The empirical methodology tunes ν and η per depth and uses standard baselines. However, some claims (generality to any loss; kernelization) are not empirically validated, and theoretical guarantees are limited.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. The reformulation sgn(Wx) = argmax_h h^T Wx and the navigation function f are clearly explained. The roles of ν and the projection are intuitive. Suggested improvements:
- Provide pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference and SSGD, specifying the assignment update schedule.
- Clarify whether sgn(Wx) requires evaluating all m splits or only those on the current path during training, and how inner products are cached to achieve O(d^2 p).
- Include more implementation details for reproducibility.

Originality
The latent-structured view of tree training and the specific surrogate/optimization are a novel and interesting synthesis. Prior non-greedy oblique tree methods did not provide this particular bound with an efficient loss-augmented inference strategy for deep trees. The SSGD stabilization is a pragmatic addition.

Significance
Improving single-tree training with principled non-greedy optimization is valuable, as trees remain widely used and are often components of larger systems. The method appears to advance the state of the art for oblique trees, and the fast inference idea is likely to be adopted. Broader impact would be stronger with either ensemble results or regression tasks, and with more comprehensive baselines.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide ablations: (a) effect of initialization (random/axis-aligned vs CO2); (b) full vs Hamming-ball loss-augmented inference on shallow trees; (c) SSGD vs SGD.
- Report training wall-clock times and memory, including SSGD overhead, and per-depth scaling.
- Add at least one regression experiment to support generality claims.
- Discuss feature scaling: are inputs standardized? Oblique splits can be sensitive to scaling; how does ν interact with this?
- Explore sparsity-inducing per-node penalties (L1 or group lasso) to yield more interpretable splits and faster inference.
- Consider a brief comparison to boosted trees to contextualize practical relevance, even if ensembles are not the main focus.

Overall
This is a solid and well-motivated contribution that brings modern latent-structured optimization to decision tree training with a practical algorithmic twist enabling deep trees. The empirical results are promising, though the scope is somewhat limited and more ablations would bolster the case. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved clarity around the fast inference and SSGD procedures and some additional empirical analysis.
