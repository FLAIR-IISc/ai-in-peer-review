**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel, non-greedy algorithm for training decision trees with oblique splits. The central idea is to reframe the problem of finding the optimal split functions as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The sequence of binary decisions that directs an input sample to a leaf node is treated as the latent variable. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective function. This objective is then optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical contribution is a modification to the surrogate objective that reduces the computational complexity of each SGD step from exponential in the tree's depth (O(2^d*p)) to polynomial (O(d^2*p)), making the approach viable for deeper trees. The method is evaluated on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating superior performance and better generalization compared to standard greedy methods for training single decision trees.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables is elegant and well-justified. The derivation of the upper bound on the empirical loss (Eq. 7) and the subsequent proof of its properties (Proposition 1) appear correct. The main technical hurdle—the exponential complexity of the loss-augmented inference step—is addressed with a clever and practical solution by constraining the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 (Eq. 13). This simplification is critical for the method's feasibility and is a strong point of the paper.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare their method against a sensible set of baselines, including standard axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, and, crucially, a greedy variant of their own method (CO2). This last comparison effectively isolates the benefit of the global, non-greedy optimization. The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms the baselines in terms of test accuracy and is less prone to overfitting, which supports the central claims of the paper. The analysis of the regularization hyperparameter `ν` and the empirical validation of the computational complexity reduction are also valuable additions.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of building the argument, starting from the limitations of greedy induction and progressively introducing the latent variable formulation, the surrogate objective, and the efficient optimization algorithm. The notation is consistent and the mathematical developments are easy to follow. Algorithm 1 provides a clear summary of the optimization procedure. The figures are informative, clearly illustrating the performance gains and the computational trade-offs.

One minor point of confusion is the description of the "Stable SGD" (SSGD) algorithm. While the motivation to prevent inactive leaves is clear, the mechanism for updating the leaf assignments `a(x)` is not fully detailed. The paper states that assignments are fixed for a number of updates and then re-evaluated, but the precise criteria for this re-evaluation are not specified. A little more detail here would improve reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While non-greedy tree optimization has been explored before (e.g., Bennett [1]), previous methods were often computationally prohibitive or limited in scope. The primary novelty here is the connection to latent variable structured prediction models. This is a powerful and non-obvious framing of the problem that opens up a new avenue for research. It allows the authors to leverage well-understood optimization techniques (loss-augmented inference, SGD on a surrogate objective) from the structured prediction literature and apply them to a classic machine learning problem. The efficiency improvement via the Hamming ball constraint is also a novel and important contribution within this framework.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is significant. Greedy decision tree induction has been the dominant paradigm for decades due to its simplicity and speed, despite its known sub-optimality. This paper presents one of the most convincing and practical alternatives to date. The demonstrable improvements in accuracy and generalization for single trees are important, as single, interpretable models are often preferred in domains like medicine or finance. Furthermore, the framework itself is general and could inspire future work. The authors' suggestions of incorporating sparsity-inducing regularizers or applying the kernel trick are promising directions. This work could plausibly influence how practitioners and researchers approach decision tree training, shifting focus from purely greedy methods to more principled global optimization, especially now that an efficient method is available.

A potential limitation not discussed is how this method would perform within an ensemble context like Random Forests or Gradient Boosted Trees. The benefits of global optimization might be diminished when hundreds of diverse, sub-optimal trees are aggregated. However, investigating single-tree performance is a valid and important research question in its own right, and this paper addresses it convincingly.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novel Formulation:** The connection between decision tree learning and latent variable structured prediction is a highly original and insightful contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed surrogate objective and the efficient SGD-based optimization algorithm are well-motivated and technically solid.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The method consistently outperforms strong baselines across multiple datasets, demonstrating clear improvements in both accuracy and generalization.
*   **Practicality:** The reduction in computational complexity from exponential to polynomial in tree depth makes the non-greedy approach computationally feasible for real-world problems.
*   **High Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible and easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The "Stable SGD" variant could be described in more detail to improve clarity and reproducibility.
*   The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the method to the greedy initialization, which could be an important practical factor.
*   The impact of this non-greedy approach in the context of tree ensembles is not discussed.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, principled, and effective method for non-greedy decision tree optimization. The strengths, particularly the originality of the formulation and the strong empirical evidence, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It addresses a long-standing problem in machine learning with a sophisticated and practical solution. This work is of high quality and is likely to be of significant interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
