

# Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach to learning decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction, developing a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key innovation is reducing the computational complexity from exponential to O(d²p) for a tree of depth d and p-dimensional inputs, making efficient learning of deep trees possible. The paper addresses the fundamental limitation of greedy tree induction (lack of cooperation between split nodes) by optimizing split functions jointly under a global objective.

The paper relates its work to prior attempts at non-greedy tree learning, particularly noting Bennett's work on multi-linear programming [1] which was limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss and high computational complexity. It contrasts with soft-split approaches like Hierarchical Mixture of Experts [13] that lose the computational advantages of decision trees, and with Murthy and Salzburg's skepticism about non-greedy methods [17] which argued against lookahead methods without regularization. The authors position their work as addressing these limitations by enabling efficient learning of deep trees with general loss functions while maintaining computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, establishing the connection to structured prediction with latent variables and proving the validity of their convex-concave upper bound. The complexity analysis is thorough, showing how the exponential complexity of naive approaches is reduced to O(d²p).

2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation, connection to structured prediction, and optimization algorithm are clearly presented. The supplementary material provides additional mathematical proofs that support the main claims.

3. **Novelty:** The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by framing decision tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables. The specific development of the upper bound and the efficient loss-augmented inference algorithm represent novel technical contributions that distinguish this work from prior approaches.

4. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on standard benchmark datasets (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) demonstrate consistent improvements in generalization performance across multiple tree depths compared to greedy baselines (including OC1, random oblique trees, and CO2). The results support the theoretical claims about reduced overfitting.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Experimental Scope:** While the paper compares to several greedy baselines, it does not include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art tree-based methods like XGBoost or LightGBM, which are widely used in practice. The paper's focus on individual trees rather than ensembles is understandable, but the lack of comparison to modern tree-based methods is notable.

2. **Computational Efficiency Claims:** The paper claims "efficient learning" but doesn't provide comprehensive wall-clock time comparisons between their method and greedy approaches on large-scale datasets. The computational complexity analysis is strong, but the actual runtime benefits on large datasets remain somewhat speculative.

3. **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper mentions using a greedy initialization but doesn't elaborate on how sensitive the method is to initialization. The Stable SGD variant is introduced to address leaf inactivity, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how often this is needed or how it affects the final model.

4. **Comparison to Deep Learning:** The paper doesn't address how this approach compares to deep learning methods that might achieve similar or better performance on the same tasks, which would help contextualize the significance of the contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The theoretical connection to structured prediction is insightful, and the development of the efficient optimization algorithm is well-executed. The experimental results provide compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness in improving generalization performance.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS due to its theoretical contribution to a fundamental machine learning problem, its technical soundness, and its clear presentation. The novelty is substantial, as the paper presents a fundamentally different approach to tree learning that addresses a long-standing limitation of greedy methods. The significance is high, as decision trees are widely used in practice, and improvements in their generalization performance could have broad impact.

While the paper could be strengthened by more comprehensive experimental comparisons with modern tree-based methods and additional runtime analysis on large datasets, these are not critical flaws. The paper makes a clear, novel contribution to the field with solid theoretical and experimental support.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of decision tree learning with strong theoretical and empirical validation. The technical soundness, clarity, and novelty are all excellent, and the significance of the work is high. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor compared to the paper's strengths.
