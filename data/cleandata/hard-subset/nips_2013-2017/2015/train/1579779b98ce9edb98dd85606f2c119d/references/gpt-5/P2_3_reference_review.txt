Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training framework for decision trees that jointly optimizes all split functions under a global objective. The key idea is to view the binary routing decisions at internal nodes as a latent vector h ∈ {−1,+1}^m and to derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss inspired by latent structured prediction. This yields a surrogate objective that can be minimized by SGD with projection, using “loss-augmented inference” over h. A central technical contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference scheme that restricts g to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around sgn(Wx), reducing a naive O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) per update while keeping hard-split inference at O(d p). The method includes per-row norm constraints on W to control the tightness of the bound and prevent trivial scaling, and introduces a “Stable SGD” variant that fixes leaf assignments for several steps to avoid inactive leaves. Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass datasets show consistent improvements over axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique splits, and a greedy one-node-at-a-time variant (CO2). The method appears less prone to overfitting and achieves better test accuracy for comparable depths. Initialization is done via a greedy tree and a CO2 warm start.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The latent-variable formulation is well grounded in latent structured SVM techniques. The upper bound (Eq. 7) is correct by construction; the tighter-but-harder behavior with scaling and the use of per-row norm constraints are sensible. The fast inference bound (Eq. 13) is looser but makes training practical for deep trees and is carefully justified. SGD with projection is appropriate; gradients are straightforward. The complexity analysis is clear.
- Limitations: The optimization remains nonconvex; there are no convergence or generalization guarantees beyond standard SGD behavior. The choice to restrict loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball modifies the objective, potentially biasing learning; the empirical impact of this approximation is not isolated in ablations. While Proposition 1 motivates norm control, the paper provides no theoretical generalization bounds for ν. Stable SGD is heuristic; its effectiveness is illustrated qualitatively, not systematically analyzed.
- Empirics: The baselines are relevant for single-tree comparisons. Results consistently favor the proposed method across depths. However, the scope is limited to a handful of datasets; it would be useful to see higher-dimensional settings or comparisons to strong modern baselines (e.g., boosted trees) even if the focus is single trees. Reporting wall-clock training time versus accuracy for all methods would further support the practicality claims. An ablation on ν, SSGD, and fast vs exact loss-augmented inference would strengthen causal claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The mapping from h to leaves via f(·) is helpful. Algorithm 1 is explicit; the fast inference explanation is intuitive (only path bits matter; the radius-1 restriction reduces candidate leaves to d+1).
- Some critical details are deferred to the supplement (proofs of bounds). Including a brief pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference would improve reproducibility. It would also help to explicitly define how d relates to m (e.g., balanced tree vs general tree) and to more explicitly state what is computed and cached per node to reach O(d^2 p).
- Experimental details are mostly sufficient (datasets, depth ranges, ν grid, train/val/test splits), but hyperparameter grids beyond ν and η, batch sizes, momentum, and number of passes should be specified for reproducibility. Reporting standard deviations over multiple runs would help quantify robustness to initialization.

Originality
- The link between non-greedy decision tree optimization and latent structured prediction is insightful and, to my knowledge, not previously developed in this form for hard-split trees with efficient training. While related ideas exist (OC1, SVM-at-nodes, HME with soft splits, Mondrian forests), the combination of a latent-SSVM-style bound with a fast loss-augmented inference tailored to the tree structure, and per-row constraints enabling sparse updates, appears novel. The CO2 method is positioned as a greedy special case; the paper clearly articulates how joint non-greedy optimization improves on it.

Significance
- The work addresses a long-standing problem—global optimization of decision trees—with a principled, scalable approach that maintains the efficiency of tree inference. If adopted, it could improve single-tree performance and inform new training strategies for oblique forests. The fast inference trick enabling O(d^2 p) per update is practically important. That said, the community often uses ensembles; demonstrating benefits as base learners in forests or boosted settings would increase impact. Theoretical advances (e.g., generalization guarantees under the norm constraints) would also raise significance.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Principled formulation connecting decision trees to latent structured prediction; clear, sound upper bounds.
- Practical training algorithm with a clever fast loss-augmented inference that scales to deeper trees.
- Consistent empirical gains over strong greedy baselines; reduced overfitting at larger depths.
- Retains hard splits and O(d p) test-time inference; method-agnostic to loss functions.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Nonconvex optimization with heuristic components (SSGD); lacks convergence or generalization theory.
- Approximate fast bound changes the objective; no ablation to quantify its effect on accuracy.
- Experimental scope is limited; no evaluation within ensembles, few datasets, limited runtime comparisons.
- Some implementation details and proofs deferred; reproducibility could be improved.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please provide an ablation comparing exact vs fast loss-augmented inference on accuracy and runtime.
- Quantify the effect of SSGD vs plain SGD on stability, number of active leaves, and final accuracy.
- Report wall-clock training time vs depth for all baselines, and add variability across random seeds.
- Discuss or experiment with extending the method to ensembles (e.g., as base learners in bagging/boosting).
- Consider adding a short pseudocode for fast loss-augmented inference and more hyperparameter details.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution: technically sound, reasonably clear, with a novel and practical approach to non-greedy tree optimization that yields empirical benefits. While some experiments and ablations are missing and the theory could be deeper, the core idea and scalability improvement are strong. I lean toward acceptance.
