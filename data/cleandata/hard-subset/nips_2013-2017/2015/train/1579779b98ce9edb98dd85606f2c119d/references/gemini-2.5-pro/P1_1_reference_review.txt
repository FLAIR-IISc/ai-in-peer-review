**Summary**

This paper presents a novel, non-greedy algorithm for learning the parameters of a decision tree. The authors address the fundamental limitation of traditional greedy induction methods (like CART or ID3), where splits are optimized locally and cannot be adjusted based on the structure of the lower parts of the tree. The core idea is to formulate the decision tree learning problem as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The sequence of binary decisions that routes an example to a leaf is treated as a latent variable. The authors then derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective function. This objective is optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) to jointly learn all split parameters (oblique splits) and leaf parameters under a global objective. A key technical contribution is an efficient "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm that reduces the complexity of each SGD update from being exponential in the tree depth `d` (O(2^d*p)) to polynomial (O(d^2*p)), making the approach practical for deep trees. The experimental results on several benchmark datasets demonstrate that the proposed non-greedy trees consistently outperform various greedy baselines, including axis-aligned and oblique trees, showing better accuracy and reduced overfitting.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is a significant and elegant conceptual contribution. It moves the problem of tree learning away from heuristic, greedy procedures and into a well-understood global optimization framework. This formulation provides a fresh perspective on a classic machine learning problem.

2.  **Technical Depth and Soundness:** The paper is technically strong. The derivation of the surrogate objective as an upper bound on the empirical loss is sound and well-motivated by prior work in structural SVMs. The insight to reduce the computational complexity of the loss-augmented inference step by restricting the search to a Hamming ball of radius 1 is particularly clever and critical for the method's practicality. The complexity analysis is clear and is empirically verified.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against a solid set of relevant baselines, including both axis-aligned and oblique greedy methods. The inclusion of CO2 as a baseline is particularly effective, as it helps isolate the performance gains attributable to the non-greedy global optimization versus the oblique split optimization alone. The results consistently show that the proposed method achieves higher test accuracy and generalizes better, especially for deeper trees where greedy methods are more prone to overfitting.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for moving beyond greedy methods is clearly articulated. The technical development is presented logically, starting from the intractable formulation and progressively building towards the final, efficient algorithm. The figures and algorithms effectively support the text.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on Initialization:** The non-greedy optimization procedure is initialized using a greedily trained tree. Since the surrogate objective is non-convex, the final solution is likely sensitive to this initialization. The paper does not explore this dependency. An ablation study showing performance with different initializations (e.g., random, or a simpler axis-aligned tree) would strengthen the claims and provide a better understanding of the optimization landscape.

2.  **Comparison with State-of-the-Art Ensembles:** The paper deliberately focuses on the performance of single trees to highlight the benefits of the non-greedy approach. While this is a valid methodological choice, the state-of-the-art in many practical applications relies on ensembles like Random Forests and Gradient Boosted Trees. A discussion or even a preliminary experiment on how a forest of these "globally optimized" trees would perform against standard ensembles would significantly increase the paper's impact and situate the contribution within the broader landscape of tree-based methods.

3.  **Heuristic Nature of Stable SGD (SSGD):** The SSGD variant is introduced to solve a practical problem of "inactive leaves," but it is presented as a somewhat ad-hoc heuristic. While effective, a more formal justification for this procedure or a deeper analysis of the stability issues in the standard SGD updates would be beneficial. It feels like a patch to an underlying optimization instability that isn't fully explored.

**Critique based on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, the claims are well-supported by both theoretical reasoning (the upper bound, the complexity analysis) and strong empirical results. It is a complete and polished piece of work.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is very clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work. The motivation and technical contributions are easy to grasp.
*   **Originality:** High. The core idea of framing tree learning as latent structured prediction is novel and powerful. While it builds on established concepts from the structured prediction literature, its application to globally optimizing decision trees is new and significant. The work is clearly differentiated from prior art.
*   **Significance:** High. This work addresses a long-standing, fundamental problem in machine learning. By providing a practical and principled method for non-greedy tree optimization, it has the potential to influence how decision trees are trained. Researchers and practitioners are likely to build upon these ideas, for instance, by applying them to ensembles or by exploring the proposed extensions like kernelized splits. It represents a demonstrable advance over decades-old greedy induction techniques for single trees.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a novel, elegant, and technically sound solution to the important problem of non-greedy decision tree induction. The connection to structured prediction is insightful, the efficient optimization algorithm is a key practical contribution, and the empirical results are compelling. The weaknesses are minor and represent opportunities for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution. This paper is a high-quality submission that would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
