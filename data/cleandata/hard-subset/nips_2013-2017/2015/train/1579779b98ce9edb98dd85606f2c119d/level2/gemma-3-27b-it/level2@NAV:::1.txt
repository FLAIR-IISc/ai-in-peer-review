## Review of "Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees"

This paper addresses the well-known limitation of greedy algorithms in decision tree induction – their tendency to produce suboptimal trees. The authors propose a novel approach for jointly optimizing split functions at all levels of the tree, leveraging connections to structured prediction with latent variables and formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. The core contribution lies in reducing the computational complexity of gradient computation to O(d), enabling training of deep trees with stochastic gradient descent.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the shortcomings of conventional greedy methods and highlighting the potential benefits of global optimization. The connection drawn between decision tree optimization and structured prediction is insightful and provides a solid theoretical foundation for their approach. The proposed convex-concave upper bound and the method for reducing its gradient complexity are significant technical achievements. Experimental results on benchmark datasets demonstrate the superiority of the non-greedy approach over several greedy baselines, including CO2, confirming the practical benefits of their method.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarity in certain areas. While the link to structured prediction is established, a more intuitive explanation of *why* this connection facilitates efficient optimization would be valuable. The explanation of Proposition 1 and its justification of the regularization term feels somewhat hand-wavy and could be expanded.  Furthermore, while the experiments demonstrate improved accuracy, a more detailed ablation study examining the impact of different regularization parameters (ν) and the effectiveness of the Stable SGD (SSGD) variant would strengthen the analysis.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a long-standing problem in machine learning.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and significant reduction in computational complexity.
*   Empirically validated improvement over established baseline methods.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Some theoretical explanations could be more intuitive and detailed.
*   Ablation study of key hyperparameters would benefit the analysis.
*   Limited discussion of potential limitations and future work beyond the mentioned extensions. 

**Overall:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. While minor clarifications and additional analysis could further strengthen the work, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical results justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions, encouraging the authors to address the points mentioned above to enhance the clarity and impact of their work.
