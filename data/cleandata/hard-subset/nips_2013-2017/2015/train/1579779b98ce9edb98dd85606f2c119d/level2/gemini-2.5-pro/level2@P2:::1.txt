### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the long-standing problem of greedy optimization in decision tree induction. Standard algorithms like CART and ID3 build trees by making a sequence of locally optimal decisions, optimizing the split at each node independently, which can lead to globally suboptimal trees. The authors propose a novel, non-greedy framework for jointly optimizing all split functions and leaf parameters of a decision tree with respect to a global objective.

The core contribution is to re-frame the decision tree learning problem as one of structured prediction with latent variables. For a given input, the path it takes through the tree is modeled as a latent variable. Based on this formulation, the authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, inspired by latent structural SVMs. This surrogate objective is piecewise linear and much better behaved than the original discontinuous loss function, enabling optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical challenge is that a naive implementation of the required loss-augmented inference step is exponential in the tree's depth. The authors propose a crucial and clever approximation, restricting the search space to a Hamming ball of radius one around the current best path, which reduces the complexity from exponential to polynomial (`O(d^2p)`), making the approach practical for deep trees. Experiments on several benchmark datasets show that the resulting non-greedy trees outperform various greedy baselines, achieving higher accuracy and demonstrating better generalization.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The connection drawn between global decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables is highly original and insightful. It moves beyond heuristic or look-ahead strategies and places the problem within a well-understood optimization framework (minimizing a surrogate loss). This is a significant conceptual contribution that could open up new avenues for research in tree-based models.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Practicality:** The derivation of the surrogate objective is sound. More importantly, the paper successfully addresses the computational challenge inherent in the formulation. The "fast loss-augmented inference" method is a critical contribution that makes the algorithm computationally feasible for reasonably deep trees, transforming an elegant but impractical idea into a usable algorithm. The complexity analysis is clear and the empirical runtime comparison in Figure 4 validates the theoretical gains.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough. The proposed method is compared against several relevant and sensible baselines, including axis-aligned trees, greedy oblique trees (OC1), and a greedy variant of their own method (CO2). The results across multiple datasets consistently demonstrate that the non-greedy approach yields trees with superior test accuracy and is less prone to overfitting, especially at greater depths. This provides strong evidence for the paper's main claim that joint, non-greedy optimization leads to better models.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. It clearly motivates the problem, builds the theoretical framework step-by-step, and explains the technical challenges and their solutions in an accessible manner. The connection to structured prediction is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Details on Stable SGD (SSGD):** The description of the SSGD variant is somewhat vague. The paper states that leaf assignments are updated "when the improvement in the bound becomes negligible," but does not specify the criterion. Is this based on a fixed number of iterations, a threshold on the objective function's change, or performance on a validation set? Providing a more precise description of this procedure would be essential for reproducibility.

2.  **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The optimization problem is non-convex, and the algorithm is initialized using a greedy procedure. This is a reasonable choice, but it would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion or a small experiment on the sensitivity of the final results to this initialization. For instance, how does the performance compare when starting from a random initialization versus the greedy one? This would help clarify whether the method is primarily a fine-tuning procedure or if it can discover good solutions from arbitrary starting points.

3.  **Context with Ensemble Methods:** The paper focuses on single trees to isolate the benefits of non-greedy training, which is a valid experimental design choice. However, in practice, the state-of-the-art for tree-based methods relies heavily on ensembles like Random Forests and Gradient Boosted Trees. The paper would have significantly greater perceived impact if it included a discussion on how this non-greedy training could be integrated into ensemble methods. For example, could these stronger, more compact base learners lead to more powerful or efficient ensembles? While a full experimental comparison might be beyond the scope, acknowledging this context and potential future direction would be valuable.

4.  **Minor Clarification on Regularization:** The paper argues for regularizing the norm of `W` to prevent the bound from becoming trivially tight but hard to optimize. This is justified by Proposition 1. It would be helpful to explicitly connect this to the margin concept in SVMs, as the underlying mechanism is very similar: controlling model complexity by encouraging a larger margin at each split node. This would make the role of the `ν` parameter more intuitive to readers familiar with margin-based methods.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, theoretically principled, and empirically successful approach to a classic and important problem in machine learning. The framing of tree learning as latent structured prediction is a powerful idea, and the authors have done the necessary technical work to turn it into a practical algorithm. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to requests for clarification or discussion of broader context, which do not detract from the core contribution. The paper is a clear advancement over prior work and is very likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
