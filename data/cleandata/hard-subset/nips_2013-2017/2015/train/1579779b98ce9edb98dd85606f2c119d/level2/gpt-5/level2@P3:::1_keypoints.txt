- Proposes a non-greedy training framework that jointly optimizes all internal oblique splits and leaf parameters using a global surrogate objective.  
- Treats the vector of split decisions as a latent variable and derives convex–concave upper bounds inspired by latent-SSVMs.  
- Presents two bounds: a general latent-SSVM-style bound and a fast restricted bound that limits loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1.  
- The radius-1 bound yields efficient per-example complexity O(d^2 p) for trees of depth d and input dimension p.  
- Optimization uses SGD with per-row L2-norm constraints on split parameters and a “stable SGD” (SSGD) variant to prevent leaf inactivation.  
- Experiments on SensIT, Connect4, Protein, and MNIST show consistent single-tree accuracy gains over strong greedy baselines and reduced overfitting.  
- Connects to prior work on structured prediction with latent variables and extends CO2 from node-wise to global joint training.  
- Retains hard routing and O(d p) test-time inference while providing a smooth training surrogate, unlike soft/HME trees.  
- Technical formulation, upper bounds, and regularization rationale (including scale effects) are well motivated and sound.  
- The fast Hamming-ball approximation is a pragmatic and theoretically justified strategy to keep updates tractable.  
- The optimization problem is non-convex but the surrogate is piecewise linear and amenable to SGD; convergence guarantees are not claimed.  
- Empirical evaluation uses standard baselines and demonstrates scalability benefits of the fast bound.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized; derivations and the navigation function are explained clearly.  
- Clarify fast loss-augmented inference pseudocode, how off-path bits are handled, and enumeration of the d+1 candidates.  
- Clarify the relationship between m (internal nodes) and d in complexity claims and assume balanced trees when stating O(2^d p).  
- Provide details on Θ updates (smoothing/regularization), and minibatch/momentum settings used in experiments.  
- Novelty lies in joint non-greedy training of hard-split oblique trees via a latent structured prediction bound and the fast Hamming-ball bound.  
- Significance: improves single-tree accuracy and robustness while preserving test-time efficiency; potential for meaningful impact if gains extend to ensembles.  
- Pros: principled formulation, efficient training via fast bound, consistent empirical improvements, practical tricks (CO2 init, norm constraints, SSGD).  
- Cons: limited theoretical guarantees (generalization/convergence), focus on single trees without ensemble results, missing baselines (max-margin, HME), and limited sensitivity analysis of ν and radius-1.  
- Essential improvements: add ablation comparing full vs. radius-1 bound on accuracy/runtime and include at least one ensemble result (e.g., bagging 10–50 trees).  
- Nice-to-have: broaden baselines to max-margin and soft trees, report sensitivity curves for ν and η and inactive-leaf frequency, discuss memory/parameter growth and sparsity strategies, and release code/hyperparameters.  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed contribution that convincingly advances non-greedy tree training for single trees and is suitable for NIPS with modest experimental extensions.
