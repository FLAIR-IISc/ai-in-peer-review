This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach to learning decision trees by jointly optimizing split functions and leaf parameters under a global objective. The authors establish a connection to structured prediction with latent variables and formulate a convex-concave upper bound on the tree’s empirical loss, allowing for efficient stochastic gradient descent (SGD) training, even for deep trees. The key innovation appears to be a method to reduce the computational complexity of gradient computation to O(d), where d is the tree depth. Experimental results on benchmark datasets demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms greedy tree baselines.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a long-standing limitation of greedy decision tree induction. The connection to structured prediction is insightful, and the proposed convex-concave upper bound is a clever technique to enable optimization. The reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is crucial for scaling to deeper trees, and the empirical results provide evidence of the effectiveness of the approach. The comparison to CO2 is also valuable, as it isolates the benefit of non-greedy optimization. The inclusion of training time comparisons reinforces the practical impact of the efficiency improvements.

However, there are several areas that could be strengthened. First, the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the motivation behind the hard constraints on the norm of W in the surrogate objective. While the paper mentions it discourages overfitting, a more intuitive explanation of how it affects the learning process would be beneficial. Second, the discussion of the Stable SGD (SSGD) algorithm is relatively brief. More details on its implementation and the rationale behind its design choices (e.g., how many iterations assignments are fixed for) would be helpful. A more thorough ablation study, comparing SGD and SSGD with different configurations, would strengthen the claims about SSGD’s effectiveness.

The related work section is comprehensive, but could be further improved by discussing more recent advances in decision tree learning, especially those focusing on regularization techniques or alternative split criteria. For example, recent work on differentiable trees or neural decision forests could be relevant. While the paper acknowledges potential extensions (kernel trick, sparsity regularization), these are only briefly mentioned and would benefit from further elaboration.

Regarding clarity, the notation is generally clear, but some equations (particularly those involving the latent variables and the upper bound) could be more clearly presented. A visual illustration of the tree navigation function (Fig. 1) would also enhance understanding.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The non-greedy approach to decision tree learning is a significant departure from traditional methods.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation based on structured prediction and the convex-concave upper bound appear theoretically well-founded.
*   **Efficiency:** The reduction in computational complexity allows for training deeper trees with SGD.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms greedy baselines.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental limitation of decision trees and has the potential to improve performance in a variety of applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The paper is technically dense and may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with structured prediction.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:**  A more thorough evaluation of the different components of the proposed method (e.g., SSGD) is needed.
*   **Missing Context:**  The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work, including recent advances in decision tree learning.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. While there are some areas for improvement, the novelty, technical soundness, and empirical results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity and completeness concerns mentioned above. Specifically, expanding the explanation of the regularization term, providing more details about SSGD, and strengthening the related work section would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.
