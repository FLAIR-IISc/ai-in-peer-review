- Proposes a novel non-greedy decision tree learning method that jointly optimizes split functions across all tree levels.  
- Frames decision tree optimization as structured prediction with latent variables.  
- Derives a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss that can be optimized with stochastic gradient descent.  
- Key technical advance: reduces loss-augmented inference complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p) using a Hamming ball constraint.  
- Enables efficient training of deep trees while keeping O(dp) inference complexity.  
- Empirical results show non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1 oblique, random oblique) on multiple benchmarks.  
- Demonstrates reduced overfitting and improved generalization compared to greedy methods.  
- Method supports any general loss function in principle, though experiments use only log loss and squared loss.  
- Strength: strong theoretical connection to structured prediction and clear technical development.  
- Strength: major practical contribution by making non-greedy training computationally feasible.  
- Strength: thorough experimental evaluation and analysis of regularization parameter ν and initialization.  
- Weakness: limited comparison to prior non-greedy work (e.g., Bennett); lacks direct empirical or complexity comparisons on same datasets.  
- Weakness: experiments do not demonstrate flexibility across a wider range of loss functions.  
- Weakness: insufficient quantification of training time vs. model quality and runtime speedup relative to greedy methods beyond one dataset.  
- Suggested improvement: add a detailed comparison with Bennett’s approach (accuracy and computational complexity on same datasets).  
- Suggested improvement: include experiments with a non-standard loss function to showcase flexibility.  
- Suggested improvement: provide a more thorough analysis of training time versus model quality for large datasets.  
- Suggested improvement: clarify the impact and necessity of CO2 initialization on final model quality.  
- Overall: well-written, technically sound, high-significance contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
