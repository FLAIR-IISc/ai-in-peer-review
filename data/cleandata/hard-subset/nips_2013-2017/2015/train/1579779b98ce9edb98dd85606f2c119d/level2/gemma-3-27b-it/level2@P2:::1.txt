This paper presents a novel non-greedy algorithm for optimizing decision trees, tackling a long-standing limitation of traditional greedy approaches. The core idea is to formulate the problem as a structured prediction task with latent variables, allowing for joint optimization of split functions and leaf parameters via a convex-concave upper bound and stochastic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate promising results on benchmark datasets, outperforming greedy baselines.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem with latent variables is a significant contribution. This allows for a global optimization approach, which is a departure from the standard greedy methods.
* **Theoretical Justification:**  The derivation of a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithm. The reduction of gradient complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) is a crucial practical improvement, enabling training of deeper trees.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that the non-greedy trees consistently outperform greedy baselines on several benchmark datasets. The analysis of the impact of the regularization parameter ν is insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and experimental setup. The connection to related work in structured prediction is well-established.
* **Practical Considerations:** Addressing the computational complexity issue and proposing a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm demonstrates a focus on practical applicability. The discussion of Stable SGD (SSGD) highlights a careful consideration of optimization challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Related Work Coverage:** While the paper cites relevant work, the connection to recent advances in differentiable tree structures (e.g., [1, 2]) is missing.  These methods also offer end-to-end differentiable training of trees, albeit with different approaches.  Specifically, the work of Popescu et al. (NeurIPS 2017) on differentiable decision trees, and similar work exploring continuous relaxations of decision boundaries, would be valuable comparisons.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments show that the performance is sensitive to the regularization parameter ν. A more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter tuning process and its impact on performance would be beneficial. The sensitivity analysis lacks a more systematic exploration of the parameter space.
* **Scalability:** While the authors address computational complexity, the scalability to extremely large datasets (e.g., millions of samples) is not thoroughly demonstrated. Further investigation into distributed training or approximation techniques would strengthen the paper. The runtime comparison (Fig. 4) is limited to a single dataset (Connect4) and doesn’t provide a comprehensive picture.
* **Explanation of SSGD:** The motivation behind the Stable SGD algorithm is presented, but the practical benefits and details of its implementation could be more clearly articulated.  A comparison of the convergence rate and performance of SGD vs. SSGD on different datasets would be useful.
* **Loss Function Generalization:** While the paper claims to handle any general loss function, the experiments only focus on cross-entropy loss. Exploring other loss functions, such as hinge loss or squared loss, would demonstrate the generality of the approach more convincingly.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a well-substantiated contribution. The theoretical analysis and experimental validation support the claims made by the authors.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized. However, some sections (e.g., the derivation of the upper bound) could benefit from more detailed explanations.

**Originality:** The formulation of decision tree learning as a structured prediction problem is novel and represents a significant departure from traditional approaches. 

**Significance:** The results demonstrate the potential for improved accuracy and generalization performance compared to standard greedy decision tree algorithms.  This has the potential to impact applications where decision trees are widely used, such as computer vision and machine learning. The work advances the state of the art in decision tree learning by providing a framework for global optimization.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to non-greedy decision tree learning.
* Consistently outperforms greedy baselines on benchmark datasets.
* Addresses the computational complexity issue with an efficient optimization algorithm.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited discussion of recent related work in differentiable tree structures.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity requires further investigation.
* Scalability to extremely large datasets is not thoroughly demonstrated.
