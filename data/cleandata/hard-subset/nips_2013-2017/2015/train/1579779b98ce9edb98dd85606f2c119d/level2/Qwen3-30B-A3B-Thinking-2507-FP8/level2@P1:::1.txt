

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach to decision tree learning that jointly optimizes split functions across all tree levels rather than using the standard greedy node-by-node procedure. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that enables efficient training via stochastic gradient descent. Their key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p), making deep tree training feasible. Experiments on multiple classification benchmarks show consistent improvements over greedy baselines, including axis-aligned trees, OC1, and random oblique trees.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, the connection to structured prediction with latent variables is insightful and well-motivated, providing a theoretically sound framework for non-greedy tree learning. Second, the convex-concave upper bound formulation is novel and well-justified, with Proposition 1 providing a clear explanation of how regularization affects the bound's tightness. Third, the efficient O(d²p) algorithm for loss-augmented inference represents a significant technical achievement that enables practical application to deep trees. The experimental results are thorough, with clear comparisons across multiple datasets and tree depths, demonstrating consistent performance improvements over greedy methods, particularly in generalization.

Several aspects could be strengthened for improved clarity and impact. The paper mentions that "the use of stochastic gradient descent for optimization enables effective training with large datasets," but doesn't provide concrete evidence of scalability on very large datasets (e.g., millions of samples). While the Connect4 dataset is mentioned as having 55,000 training points, this is not particularly large by modern standards, and the paper could better demonstrate scalability. Additionally, the comparison to CO2 [18] is somewhat limited; CO2 is described as "a special case of our upper bound approach applied greedily," but it would be helpful to have a more detailed explanation of how CO2 relates to the proposed method and why non-greedy optimization specifically improves upon it.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter ν (the regularization constant controlling the tightness of the upper bound). While Figure 3 shows that ν implicitly controls leaf pruning, it would be valuable to have a more systematic analysis of how ν affects tree structure and generalization. The authors mention that "with a small ν, the norm constraints force the method to choose a W with a large margin at each internal node," but a more concrete explanation of the trade-off between margin size and tree complexity would strengthen the paper.

Regarding related work, the paper properly addresses Bennett's non-greedy approach [1] but could better distinguish their method from other recent non-greedy tree learning approaches. For instance, the paper could more explicitly discuss how their latent variable formulation differs from the Mondrian Processes approach in [15] or the hierarchical mixture of experts in [13].

The writing is generally clear, though some technical passages (particularly in Section 5) could be made more accessible. The proof of Proposition 1 would be more convincing with a more intuitive explanation alongside the algebraic proof. The authors could also better highlight the practical implications of their method for real-world applications, such as the Microsoft Kinect example mentioned in the introduction.

The paper excels in quality, with a theoretically sound approach that is well-supported by experimental results. The clarity is good overall, though some technical details could be presented more accessibly. The originality is strong, with the connection to structured prediction providing a fresh perspective on a longstanding problem in decision tree learning. The significance is high, as decision trees remain widely used in practice, and the proposed method addresses a fundamental limitation of current approaches.

Strengths:
- Novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction
- Theoretical justification for the proposed upper bound
- Efficient O(d²p) algorithm enabling deep tree training
- Consistent empirical improvements over greedy baselines
- General applicability to any loss function

Weaknesses:
- Limited scalability demonstration on very large datasets
- Insufficient discussion of hyperparameter ν's impact
- Incomplete comparison to other non-greedy approaches
- Some technical sections could be made more accessible

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field that addresses a fundamental limitation of decision tree learning. The paper's technical novelty, theoretical grounding, and empirical results make it a compelling candidate for NIPS. The authors have made a significant step toward making non-greedy tree learning practical and effective. With minor clarifications to the hyperparameter discussion and scalability claims, this paper would be even stronger. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
