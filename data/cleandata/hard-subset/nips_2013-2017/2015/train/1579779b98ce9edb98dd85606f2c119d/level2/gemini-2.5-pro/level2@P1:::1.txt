### **Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a novel framework for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The standard approach to training decision trees is a greedy, recursive procedure where splits are optimized one node at a time. This paper argues that this greedy approach leads to suboptimal trees and proposes a method to jointly optimize all split functions (oblique splits) and leaf node parameters under a global objective. The key insight is to formulate the decision tree learning problem as one of structured prediction with latent variables, where the path an example takes through the tree is represented by a latent binary vector. Building on this formulation, the authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective function. A naive optimization of this objective would be exponential in the tree's depth. The main technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for loss-augmented inference that reduces the complexity of each stochastic gradient descent (SGD) update to be quadratic in the tree depth, making the approach practical for deep trees. Experiments on several benchmark classification datasets show that the proposed non-greedy trees consistently outperform greedy baselines, including axis-aligned, OC1, and a greedy version of their own approach (CO2).

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of decision tree optimization as structured prediction with latent variables is both elegant and powerful, providing a principled foundation for global optimization. The derivation of the surrogate upper bound is sound and well-motivated by prior work in structured SVMs. The development of the "fast loss-augmented inference" algorithm is a crucial contribution that makes the method computationally feasible, moving from an exponential complexity in tree depth to a much more manageable polynomial one. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against several relevant and strong baselines on multiple datasets, and the results clearly demonstrate the benefits of the non-greedy approach in terms of improved generalization and reduced overfitting, especially for deeper trees.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for moving beyond greedy optimization is clearly stated. The authors do an excellent job of building up their formulation, starting from the basic decision tree setup and carefully introducing the notation and concepts that link it to latent structured prediction. The figures are informative, particularly Figure 2 which clearly visualizes the performance gains over baselines across different tree depths, and Figure 4 which empirically validates the theoretical improvement in computational complexity of the fast inference algorithm. The overall organization is logical and guides the reader effectively through the complex material.

**Originality:**
The paper is highly original. While non-greedy tree optimization is a long-standing problem, previous attempts have often been limited by computational complexity or restricted problem settings. The primary conceptual contribution here is the novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables. This reframing of the problem is a significant departure from prior work and opens up a new avenue for research, allowing powerful optimization tools from the structured prediction literature to be applied to tree induction. The resulting algorithm is a novel and practical contribution to the field.

**Significance:**
The potential impact of this work is significant. Decision trees and forests are among the most widely used models in machine learning and computer vision due to their efficiency and interpretability. This paper addresses a fundamental limitation—their greedy training procedure—that has been present since their inception. By providing a practical and scalable algorithm for non-greedy training that demonstrably improves performance, this work could influence how decision trees are trained in the future. Researchers and practitioners are likely to build on these ideas, for instance, by incorporating this non-greedy training into ensemble methods like Random Forests or Gradient Boosted Trees, which could potentially lead to more powerful models or similar performance with smaller ensembles.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and insightful formulation of global decision tree optimization as a latent variable structured prediction problem.
*   A technically sound and practical algorithm (based on SGD with fast loss-augmented inference) that scales polynomially with tree depth.
*   Strong empirical results on several benchmark datasets demonstrating superior performance over well-established greedy baselines.
*   The paper is very well-written, clear, and addresses a fundamental problem of high importance to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance / Points for improvement:**
*   The method requires initialization from a greedily trained tree. The paper does not analyze the sensitivity of the final model to this initialization. It would be valuable to understand if the method can recover from a poor initialization or if its success is highly dependent on a good starting point.
*   The "Stable SGD" (SSGD) variant feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the motivation of preventing leaves from becoming inactive is understandable, the mechanism of fixing assignments seems heuristic. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of why this phenomenon occurs and perhaps a more principled regularization-based solution. The experiments do not clearly separate the performance of SGD vs. SSGD.
*   The experiments are focused on single trees to highlight the core contribution, which is a reasonable choice. However, given the prevalence of tree ensembles, a discussion or even a preliminary experiment on how this non-greedy approach could benefit forest-based methods would significantly increase the paper's practical impact.

In summary, the strengths of this paper—its novelty, technical quality, and potential significance—far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which mostly represent interesting avenues for future work. The paper presents a compelling solution to a classic and important problem in machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
