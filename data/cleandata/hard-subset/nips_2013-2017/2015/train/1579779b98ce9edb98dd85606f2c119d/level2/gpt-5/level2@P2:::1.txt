Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy training algorithm for decision trees with oblique (linear) splits. The key idea is to formulate training as structured prediction with latent binary variables indicating split decisions, and to optimize a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss. The surrogate bound supports general losses and is optimized by (stochastic) gradient descent under per-node norm constraints on split weights. A central contribution is an efficient loss-augmented inference procedure: replacing an exponentially large latent search with a Hamming-ball radius-1 variant reduces each SGD step to O(d^2 p + k), where d is tree depth, p input dimensionality, and k classes, while retaining an upper bound. Additional practical elements include initialization via a greedy/CO2 procedure and a “Stable SGD” schedule to prevent leaf collapse. Experiments on four multiclass benchmarks (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) show consistent improvements over greedy axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique trees, and CO2 (nodewise bound optimization), with better generalization and scalability.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on classical greedy tree induction (ID3, CART), oblique split induction (OC1), and max-margin tree approaches, connects decision trees to latent structured SVMs (e.g., Tsochantaridis et al.; Yu & Joachims), and contrasts with soft-split hierarchies such as HME. The CO2 method (Norouzi et al.) is a closely related “local” variant; here the objective is global and optimized jointly over all internal nodes and leaves. The paper clearly positions contributions within these lines of work.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The latent-variable reformulation, bound (Eq. 7/13), and the role of norm constraints are well-motivated. The radius-1 Hamming-ball bound remains valid and yields an efficient loss-augmented inference. Complexity analysis is clear. Non-convexity remains (as expected), but the algorithmic choices (initialization, projection, SSGD) are reasonable.
- Empirical support: Results across datasets and depths indicate consistent test accuracy gains over strong greedy baselines and CO2; overfitting is reduced via norm constraints. Runtime scaling with fast loss-augmented inference is demonstrated.
- Missing analyses: The impact of using the radius-1 bound vs. the exact bound on accuracy is not quantified (only runtime shown). Ablations for SSGD vs. vanilla SGD and for initialization quality are not reported. Comparisons to max-margin trees/SVM-at-node methods or soft trees (HME) are missing experimentally.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and structured. The latent-variable perspective and inference procedures are explained clearly, with intuitive arguments and algorithmic pseudocode. Notation is heavy but consistent. Some small presentation issues remain (see Minor comments).

Originality
The combination of (i) a global, latent-structured objective for decision trees, (ii) a tractable upper-bound with efficient loss-augmented inference via a Hamming-ball restriction, and (iii) a practical SGD/SSGD training scheme is novel relative to prior non-greedy or oblique tree training. The link to structured prediction is elegant and potentially enabling for further extensions (e.g., kernels, sparsity).

Significance
Improving single-tree induction in a non-greedy, scalable way is impactful: trees remain important for efficiency and interpretability, and non-greedy training has been an open challenge. While most applications use ensembles, better single trees can strengthen forests and boosting as base learners. The method supports general losses and offers a clear optimization framework likely useful beyond the specific instantiation.

Pros
- Clear, principled reformulation of tree learning as latent structured prediction with a valid and efficiently optimizable bound.
- Efficient training of deep oblique trees with O(d^2 p) per step; good engineering choices (projection, momentum, stabilization).
- Consistent empirical gains over relevant greedy baselines and CO2 across multiple datasets and depths; reduced overfitting via per-row norm constraints.
- Generality to arbitrary loss functions and potential for extensions (kernels, sparsity).

Cons
- No empirical comparison between the exact loss-augmented inference (Eq. 7) and the Hamming-ball approximation (Eq. 13) in terms of accuracy/optimality.
- Limited ablations: effect of SSGD, initialization, and ν not thoroughly quantified beyond illustrative plots.
- Experimental scope focuses on single trees; no evidence on forests/boosting where practical gains would matter most.
- Missing baselines: margin-based trees (SVM-at-nodes) and soft decision trees (HME) are cited but not compared empirically.
- Reproducibility details (stopping, momentum/minibatch sizes, regularization on biases) are sparse; no code availability noted.

Questions for the authors
1. How does the fast (radius-1) bound affect final accuracy vs. the exact bound across datasets? Is the difference negligible relative to runtime gains?
2. Can you provide ablations quantifying SSGD’s effect on accuracy and active leaves, and sensitivity to the ν constraint?
3. How sensitive is performance to initialization (axis-aligned vs. random; with/without CO2 warm start)?
4. Have you tried integrating your trees into bagging or boosting to assess gains in standard ensemble settings?
5. Do you regularize the bias terms explicitly? Any effect of feature scaling on stability?
6. For regression (squared loss), can you show at least one experiment to support generality?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: (i) fast vs. exact loss-augmented inference (accuracy vs. time), (ii) SSGD vs. SGD, (iii) different initializations. Report leaf activity over training.
- Include at least one experiment on a random forest or boosted ensemble to demonstrate practical utility beyond single trees.
- Compare with max-margin tree approaches and, if feasible, a soft-split baseline (HME or a modern soft decision tree), noting trade-offs in inference cost.
- Provide more implementation details (learning-rate schedule, momentum, minibatch sizes, stopping, bias regularization) and release code.
- Discuss theory beyond Proposition 1: any margin-based generalization insight tied to per-row norm constraints?
- Minor typos/formatting: “Murthy and Salzburg” → “Salzberg”; odd formatting in some references (e.g., Elements of Statistical Learning; Shotton et al.).

Overall assessment
A strong, well-motivated contribution that advances non-greedy tree learning with an elegant structured-prediction perspective and practical, efficient optimization. With broader experiments (ensembles, ablations) and a clearer assessment of the fast bound’s impact, this could be a solid addition. I lean toward acceptance.
