This paper presents a novel approach to decision tree induction by moving beyond the standard greedy algorithms. The core idea is to jointly optimize split functions at all levels of the tree, combined with leaf parameters, using a global objective. This is framed as a structured prediction problem with latent variables, and a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss is derived, allowing for efficient optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The work demonstrates improved performance compared to greedy baselines on several benchmark datasets.

The strengths of this work are significant. The problem addressed – the suboptimality of greedy decision tree induction – is well-motivated and important, especially given the continued widespread use of decision trees and forests. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables is a clever insight, providing a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed approach. The derivation of the convex-concave upper bound is technically sound, and the resulting optimization algorithm with O(d2p) complexity is a key contribution, enabling training of reasonably deep trees. The experimental results provide compelling evidence that the non-greedy approach outperforms greedy baselines, consistently achieving better test accuracy across different datasets and tree depths. The analysis of the regularization parameter ν and its impact on tree pruning is insightful. The inclusion of a detailed complexity analysis is commendable.

However, there are areas where the paper could be improved. While the paper provides a good overview of related work, it could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of recent advances in differentiable tree structures. Several papers have explored making decision trees fully differentiable, enabling end-to-end learning with gradient descent, and a comparison to these methods would be valuable (e.g., differentiable decision trees using softmax approximations of the step function). Although the paper mentions the potential for kernelization, it does not elaborate on its practical implementation or benefits. Providing even a brief discussion or preliminary results would strengthen this aspect. The experimental section is well-executed but could be expanded.  Ablation studies, examining the impact of different components of the proposed method (e.g., the regularization term, the fast loss-augmented inference) would provide greater insight into their individual contributions. A more thorough analysis of the sensitivity to the learning rate η and the regularization parameter ν would also be useful. The justification for initializing W using a greedy approach could be more explicitly explained; why is a greedy initialization helpful in this context? While the paper addresses the issue of inactive leaves with SSGD, the details of the implementation and the criteria for switching between updates could be clarified.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded approach to a long-standing problem in machine learning.
*   Efficient optimization algorithm with a reasonable complexity.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating improved performance over established baselines.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the technical details.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of recent differentiable tree approaches.
*   Lack of detailed ablation studies and sensitivity analysis.
*   Initialization strategy is not fully justified.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed non-greedy approach is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically effective. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses.  A revision addressing the suggestions above would further strengthen the paper.  This reviewer recommends acceptance with minor revisions.
