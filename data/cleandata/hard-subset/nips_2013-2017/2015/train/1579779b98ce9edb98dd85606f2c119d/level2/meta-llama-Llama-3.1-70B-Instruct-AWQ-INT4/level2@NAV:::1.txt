This paper proposes a non-greedy algorithm for learning decision trees using stochastic gradient descent to optimize an upper bound on the empirical loss of the tree's predictions on the training set. The main claim of the paper is that their algorithm outperforms traditional greedy decision tree induction methods and achieves better generalization performance.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the problem of decision tree optimization and its relation to structured prediction with latent variables. The authors propose a novel formulation of the decision tree learning problem, which associates a binary latent decision variable with each split node in the tree and uses such latent variables to formulate the tree's empirical loss. They also develop a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, which is optimized using stochastic gradient descent to find a locally optimal configuration of the split functions.

The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed non-greedy decision tree algorithm outperforms several greedy baselines, including conventional axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, and random oblique trees. The results also show that the algorithm is less susceptible to overfitting and achieves better generalization performance at various tree depths.

The strengths of the paper include:

* A thorough analysis of the problem of decision tree optimization and its relation to structured prediction with latent variables.
* A novel formulation of the decision tree learning problem, which associates a binary latent decision variable with each split node in the tree.
* A convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss, which is optimized using stochastic gradient descent to find a locally optimal configuration of the split functions.
* Experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The algorithm requires a large number of hyper-parameters to be tuned, which can be time-consuming and may not always lead to optimal results.
* The computational complexity of the algorithm can be high, especially for deep trees.
* The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the time complexity of the algorithm and its scalability to large datasets.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of decision tree learning and provides a new perspective on the problem of decision tree optimization. The proposed algorithm has the potential to improve the accuracy and generalization performance of decision trees, and its application to real-world problems can be beneficial.
