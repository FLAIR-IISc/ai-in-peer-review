**Summary**

This paper presents a novel method for the non-greedy optimization of decision trees. The standard approach to training decision trees involves a greedy, recursive procedure where splits are optimized one node at a time. This can lead to suboptimal trees. To address this, the work formulates the training of an entire tree as a single global optimization problem. The core idea is to frame decision tree learning as a form of structured prediction with latent variables, where the path taken by an example through the tree is determined by a set of latent binary decisions.

Based on this formulation, the paper derives a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a surrogate objective for optimization. This objective allows for the joint optimization of all oblique split functions and the leaf node parameters. A significant technical contribution is the development of an efficient algorithm for the loss-augmented inference step required for optimization. By restricting the search space of the inference, the complexity of a stochastic gradient descent (SGD) update is reduced from being exponential in the tree's depth to being polynomial (quadratic), making the training of deep trees computationally feasible. The proposed method is evaluated on several benchmark classification tasks, where it is shown to outperform greedy baselines, including both axis-aligned and oblique tree induction methods.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of decision tree learning as a latent structured prediction problem is sound and provides a principled foundation for global optimization. The derivation of the surrogate objective function via a loss-augmented inference framework is well-established in the structured prediction literature and is correctly applied here.

Claims are well-supported by the experiments. The results consistently demonstrate that the non-greedy approach leads to trees with better generalization performance compared to several relevant baselines across multiple datasets. The inclusion of an ablation-like comparison with the "CO2" method, which applies a similar objective but in a greedy fashion, effectively highlights the benefits of the proposed joint optimization scheme. The empirical validation of the computational complexity claims (Fig. 4) further strengthens the work. The paper presents a complete piece of work, from theoretical formulation to a practical and effective algorithm.

**Clarity**

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The logical flow from the limitations of greedy methods, through the novel problem formulation, to the derivation of the surrogate objective and the final optimization algorithm is easy to follow. The notation is consistent and the use of figures effectively illustrates the experimental results.

For potential improvement, the presentation of the different upper bounds could be slightly clarified. The paper introduces an initial bound (Eq. 7), a more efficient variant for "fast loss-augmented inference" (Eq. 13), and another for "Stable SGD" (Eq. 14). While the motivation for each is given, a more explicit summary of the trade-offs between bound tightness and computational efficiency would be helpful for the reader. It should also be made explicit which bound and algorithm variant (e.g., SGD or SSGD) were used to generate the main experimental results in Figure 2.

**Originality**

The work is highly original. While the problem of non-greedy tree optimization is a classic one, the approach of casting it as a latent structured prediction problem is novel. This reframing connects two previously disparate areas of machine learning and provides a new, principled lens through which to view tree induction. This contrasts with previous non-greedy methods that were either computationally intractable for non-trivial trees or relied on more heuristic lookahead strategies. The paper appropriately cites and discusses relevant prior work, clearly positioning its unique contributions.

**Significance**

The results presented are significant and likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners. Decision trees and forests remain fundamental tools in machine learning, and a practical method that improves the quality of the base learner is an important contribution. The proposed framework is general and could be extended to other loss functions or types of splits. The demonstration that a global, non-greedy optimization can be performed efficiently for reasonably deep trees may encourage a shift away from purely greedy induction methods. Furthermore, the ideas presented could inspire similar non-greedy approaches for other hierarchical models. The potential for improved base learners to lead to stronger ensemble models further underscores the work's potential impact.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The connection between decision tree optimization and latent structured prediction is a key strength, moving the problem from heuristics to a well-defined global optimization framework.
*   **Efficient and Scalable Algorithm:** The development of the "fast loss-augmented inference" method is a crucial contribution that makes the approach practical for deep trees, overcoming a major hurdle for non-greedy methods.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed non-greedy trees consistently outperform strong greedy baselines on multiple datasets, providing clear evidence of the method's effectiveness.
*   **Clear Demonstration of Non-Greedy Benefit:** The comparison against the greedy CO2 variant effectively isolates and validates the advantage of joint global optimization.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The algorithm is initialized using a greedy procedure. It would be valuable to include a brief discussion or analysis of the method's sensitivity to this initialization. Understanding whether the optimization is simply fine-tuning a good greedy solution or is capable of finding fundamentally different and better solutions from various starting points would strengthen the paper. This is a suggestion for added context, not an essential revision.
*   **Analysis of SSGD:** The Stable SGD (SSGD) variant is introduced as a practical way to manage inactive leaves. The motivation is clear, but the mechanism is presented as a heuristic. A more detailed characterization of its behavior or sensitivity to its own scheduling parameters would be a useful addition.
*   **Potential in Ensembles:** The experiments rightly focus on single trees to isolate the contribution. However, a brief discussion on the potential implications for ensemble methods like Random Forests would be welcome. An optional, small-scale experiment showing how these trees perform as base learners in an ensemble would significantly increase the perceived impact of the work.
