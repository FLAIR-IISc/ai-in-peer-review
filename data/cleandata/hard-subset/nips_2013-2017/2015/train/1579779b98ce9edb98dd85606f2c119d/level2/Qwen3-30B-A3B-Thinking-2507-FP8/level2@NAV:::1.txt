

This paper proposes a non-greedy approach for optimizing decision trees by jointly optimizing split functions across all tree levels with a global objective, rather than the standard greedy one-node-at-a-time procedure. The authors establish a novel connection between decision tree learning and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized using stochastic gradient descent. The key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p), making deep tree training feasible. Experiments on multiple classification benchmarks demonstrate that the non-greedy trees consistently outperform greedy baselines in test accuracy and generalization.

The paper's main strengths include its strong theoretical foundation, with rigorous proofs of the upper bound and complexity analysis. The connection to structured prediction is insightful and well-motivated, and the experimental results convincingly show the superiority of the non-greedy approach across multiple datasets and tree depths. The authors also provide a clear implementation of the algorithm with practical considerations like the Stable SGD variant to handle inactive leaves.

However, the paper has some limitations. While it claims to handle any general loss function, the experiments only evaluate classification tasks with log loss. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the sensitivity to the regularization parameter ν, particularly how it affects tree structure and performance. Additionally, the comparison with the CO2 method (a greedy variant of their approach) is somewhat limited, as it doesn't directly compare with other non-greedy approaches like Bennett's work [1].

The paper is technically sound and well-written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. It makes a significant contribution by providing a principled framework for non-greedy decision tree optimization that addresses a fundamental limitation of standard methods. The approach has clear practical utility for applications where decision tree quality matters, and the computational efficiency enables training on large datasets.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in decision tree learning. The paper should be accepted for presentation at NIPS. The main suggestion for improvement would be to expand the experimental evaluation to include more loss functions and provide deeper analysis of the regularization parameter's impact on tree structure.
