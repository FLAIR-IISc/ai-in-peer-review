Summary
The paper proposes a non-greedy algorithm for training oblique decision trees by jointly optimizing all split functions and leaf parameters under a global objective. The key idea is to re-express hard split decisions as latent binary variables, linking tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that serves as a surrogate objective and can be optimized by stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with row-wise norm constraints on split weights. A central technical contribution is a “fast” loss-augmented inference scheme: restricting the loss-augmented maximization to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current sgn(Wx) yields per-sample gradient updates in O(d^2 p) time (d = depth, p = input dimension), making deep trees feasible. A “Stable SGD” variant reduces leaf inactivity by fixing assignments for several updates. Experiments on multi-class benchmarks (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) show single trees trained by the proposed method outperform several greedy baselines (axis-aligned CART, OC1, random oblique) and a greedy depth-1 upper-bound method (CO2), with better generalization across depths. The regularization parameter ν (row-norm constraint) is shown to control pruning and overfitting.

Relation to prior work
- Greedy tree induction (ID3/CART) and typical regularization (depth/leaf-size limits, pruning, bagging/boosting) are contrasted with the proposed global, non-greedy objective.
- Oblique trees (e.g., OC1) and max-margin trees (SVM at nodes) are discussed as alternatives; the proposed approach differs by optimizing a single joint objective over all nodes.
- Soft hierarchical models (mixtures of experts) enable smooth optimization but lose the O(d) inference advantage of hard trees; this work retains hard splits and O(d) inference.
- The formulation draws directly on structured prediction with latent variables and margin-rescaling ideas, but tailors loss-augmented inference to tree structure.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: the latent-variable view unifies hard-split trees with structured prediction, enabling principled surrogate optimization.
- Technical novelty: the convex-concave upper bound and the Hamming-ball (radius-1) loss-augmented inference give an elegant O(d^2 p) update scheme that scales to deeper trees.
- Practical training recipe: SGD with row-norm projection and an SSGD variant addresses inactive leaves, with reasonable implementation details.
- Empirical evidence: consistent gains over multiple greedy baselines across depths; analysis of ν highlights a principled regularization lever.
- Maintains fast inference: unlike soft trees, the model uses hard routing at test time with O(d p) cost.

Weaknesses and questions
- Optimization objective: The fast bound (radius-1) is looser than the original bound. Although justified by an inequality, the potential optimization gap is not quantified. An empirical ablation comparing optimization with the full bound vs. fast bound (on shallow trees where full is feasible) would clarify trade-offs in accuracy and runtime.
- Convergence and generalization: No theoretical guarantees are provided (e.g., convergence to a stationary point under SGD/SSGD, generalization bounds under the norm constraint). While not necessary for acceptance, even informal discussion or references would strengthen the claims.
- Sensitivity and ablations: More detail is needed on:
  - The SSGD schedule (how often are assignments updated? sensitivity to this hyperparameter?).
  - The effect of initialization (axis-aligned + CO2) vs. random weight initialization on final performance and stability.
  - The contribution of each component (norm projection, fast inference, SSGD) to accuracy.
- Baselines: Focusing on single trees is reasonable to isolate training effects, but limited in scope given the prevalence of ensembles. It would help to show that non-greedy trees also improve as base learners in forests (bagging/boosting) or at least compare to strong single-tree baselines like CART with strong pruning or max-margin oblique splits trained globally at each node (if available).
- Runtime analysis: Timing comparisons are shown for one dataset; broader profiling across datasets, d, p, and k would substantiate scalability claims. Consider reporting wall-clock time to reach a fixed validation accuracy.
- Large-k settings: Leaf softmax updates cost O(k); in datasets with large label spaces, efficiency may degrade. Are there approximations (e.g., sampled softmax) compatible with the surrogate?
- Over-regularization behavior: Proposition 1 implies trivial tightening via scaling W; the paper counters with row-norm constraints. A short discussion of alternative regularization (e.g., L1/group-sparsity to encourage sparse splits, path-wise constraints, or annealing ν) and their practical behavior would be useful.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound, with a clear derivation of the bound, sensible regularization, and efficient inference/training procedures. The experimental methodology is generally careful (per-depth tuning, multiple datasets), though additional ablations and broader baselines would improve confidence. Claims are appropriately scoped to single-tree settings.

Clarity
The paper is well-written and organized. The latent-variable formulation, bounds, and algorithms are explained clearly. A small addition of pseudo-code for fast loss-augmented inference and more explicit SSGD scheduling details would further aid reproducibility.

Originality
The link between oblique trees and latent structured prediction, the specific bound construction, and the efficient Hamming-ball inference scheme represent a novel and useful combination. This goes beyond prior non-greedy tree and oblique split methods.

Significance
Decision trees remain important both as interpretable models and as base learners. A practical, scalable non-greedy training method is of clear interest. The potential to plug this method into forests/boosted ensembles increases its impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, novel formulation connecting trees and latent structured prediction.
- Efficient algorithm with strong practical relevance (O(d^2 p) training, O(d p) inference).
- Consistent empirical improvements over multiple strong greedy baselines.
- Clear exposition and accessible implementation details.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees and characterization of the gap introduced by the fast bound.
- Missing ablations (SSGD schedule, initialization) and broader runtime profiling.
- Lack of evaluation within ensembles or against the strongest single-tree baselines used in practice.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations on the fast vs. full bound (where feasible), SSGD scheduling, and initialization.
- Report training time vs. accuracy curves across datasets and d.
- Explore use as a base learner in forests/boosting, or compare to stronger oblique single-tree baselines.
- Add a brief discussion of generalization under row-norm constraints and possible extensions (e.g., sparsity-inducing regularization, annealing ν).

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution that advances non-greedy training of decision trees with a well-motivated and efficient approach. I lean positive, contingent on clarifying ablations and runtime characterization.
