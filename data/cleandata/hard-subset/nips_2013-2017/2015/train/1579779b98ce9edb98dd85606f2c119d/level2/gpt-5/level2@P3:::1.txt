Summary
The paper addresses the suboptimality of standard greedy decision tree induction by proposing a non-greedy training framework that jointly optimizes all internal oblique splits and leaf parameters under a global surrogate objective. The central idea is to view the vector of split decisions as a latent variable and to derive a convex–concave upper bound on the empirical loss inspired by structured prediction with latent variables (e.g., latent structural SVMs). Two bounds are presented: (i) a general latent-SSVM-style bound and (ii) a restricted version that limits loss-augmented inference to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around the current hard routing. The latter yields an efficient O(d^2 p) per-example complexity for trees of depth d and p-dimensional inputs. Optimization uses stochastic gradient descent with per-row L2-norm constraints on split parameters and a “stable SGD” variant to mitigate leaf inactivation. Experiments on several multi-class benchmarks (SensIT, Connect4, Protein, MNIST) show consistent single-tree accuracy improvements over greedy baselines (axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, random oblique, and a greedy one-node-at-a-time variant, CO2), with reduced overfitting and practical scalability.

Relation to prior work
The work connects decision tree learning to structured prediction with latent variables (e.g., Yu & Joachims, 2009) and margin-rescaling ideas (Taskar et al., Tsochantaridis et al.). It builds on and extends CO2 (Norouzi et al., 2015) from node-wise to joint, global training. Compared to soft decision trees/HME (Jordan & Jacobs), this approach retains hard routing and O(dp) inference while providing a smooth training surrogate. Prior non-greedy or max-margin trees (Bennett; Bennett & Blue; Bennett et al.) are either limited in scope or computationally expensive; the presented method scales via SGD and a fast loss-augmented inference.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The latent-variable formulation, upper bounds (Eq. 7; Eq. 13), and per-row norm constraints are well motivated. The discussion of scale effects (Proposition 1) and the need for regularization is appropriate. The complexity analysis is careful; the fast inference strategy that restricts to Hamming radius 1 is a useful and sound approximation to keep updates tractable.
- Optimization: The objective remains non-convex, but the surrogate is piecewise linear and amenable to SGD with projections. The SSGD variant addresses a practical pathology (inactive leaves). Convergence guarantees are not claimed; this is typical for non-convex tree training.
- Empirics: Benchmarks and baselines are standard and relevant. Results consistently favor the proposed non-greedy trees in the single-tree setting. Ablations on ν illustrate the role of regularization and implicit pruning; a runtime study demonstrates the scalability advantage of the fast bound.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and well organized. The latent-variable perspective, the role of the navigation function f, and the derivation of the fast inference bound are explained clearly. Minor suggestions for clarity:
- Provide explicit pseudocode for the fast loss-augmented inference (radius-1) routine, including how bits off the selected path are handled and how the d + 1 candidates are enumerated.
- Clarify the relationship between m and d in complexity claims (m is the number of internal nodes, which is O(2^d) for a balanced tree). When stating O(mp) ⊆ O(2^d p), explicitly assume balanced trees to avoid confusion.
- Detail the update of Θ (e.g., whether smoothing or regularization on leaf distributions is used) and mini-batch sizes/momentum settings used in experiments.

Originality
Joint, non-greedy training of hard-split oblique decision trees via a latent structured prediction upper bound appears novel. The fast Hamming-ball restricted bound is a pragmatic and original contribution that makes deep-tree training feasible while preserving hard routing. The link to structured prediction is a conceptually clean bridge that may inspire further work on globally trained trees.

Significance
Improving single-tree accuracy and robustness is valuable given the continued use of decision trees and forests in large-scale applications (e.g., vision). The method retains the computational advantages of hard decisions at test time and suggests clear paths to extensions (sparsity, kernels, ensembles). Assuming the gains translate to ensembles, the approach could have meaningful impact.

Pros
- Clear and principled formulation connecting decision trees to latent structured prediction.
- Efficient training with O(d^2 p) per-example updates using the fast bound.
- Consistent empirical improvements over strong greedy baselines, with reduced overfitting.
- Practical considerations: initialization via CO2, per-row norm constraints, SSGD to stabilize training.
- Insightful analysis of the role of parameter scaling and regularization.

Cons
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond the correctness of the bound; no generalization or convergence guarantees provided.
- Experimental scope focuses on single trees; an ensemble evaluation (bagging/boosting) would strengthen the significance claims.
- Baselines could be broadened to include max-margin trees (e.g., Bennett & Blue) and soft/probabilistic trees (HME) to contextualize gains.
- Sensitivity to the regularization ν and the choice of the radius-1 approximation is not extensively quantified; ablations comparing the full vs. fast bound in accuracy would be useful.
- Memory/parameter growth with depth (m × p) is not discussed; practical implications for very deep trees could be clarified.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Add an ablation comparing the general bound (Eq. 7) vs. the fast radius-1 bound (Eq. 13) on accuracy and runtime across a few depths to quantify the trade-off introduced by the approximation.
- Include at least one ensemble result (e.g., bagging 10–50 trees) to demonstrate that the single-tree gains translate to standard practice.

Nice-to-have:
- Broaden baselines with a representative max-margin decision tree method and a soft tree/HME baseline trained by EM, to strengthen positioning.
- Report sensitivity curves for ν and η, and frequency of inactive leaves under SGD vs. SSGD; consider an automated schedule or penalty to discourage premature leaf death.
- Discuss memory footprint/parameter count and any sharing strategies across nodes; consider adding sparsity-inducing regularization on W as hinted in the conclusion.
- Release code or provide more detailed hyperparameter and training settings to improve reproducibility.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-executed contribution that advances non-greedy training of decision trees with a principled surrogate and efficient optimization. The empirical improvements and scalability are convincing in the single-tree regime. With modest extensions to experiments and baselines, this work is well suited for NIPS.
