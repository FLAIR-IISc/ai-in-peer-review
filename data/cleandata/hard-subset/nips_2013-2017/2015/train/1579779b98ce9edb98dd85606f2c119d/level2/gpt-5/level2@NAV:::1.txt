Summary and relation to prior work: The paper tackles the well-known suboptimality of greedy decision tree induction by proposing a non-greedy, joint optimization of oblique split functions and leaf parameters under a global objective. The key idea is to cast tree routing decisions as latent variables and optimize a convex–concave upper bound on empirical loss using SGD. A practical contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference scheme that restricts to a Hamming ball of radius 1 around current decisions, reducing per-step complexity to O(d^2 p). Experiments on several LibSVM multiclass benchmarks show consistent improvements over axis-aligned trees, OC1, random oblique baselines, and a greedy variant (CO2). The work connects to structural SVMs with latent variables (Taskar; Tsochantaridis; Yu & Joachims) and prior non-greedy/large-margin trees (Bennett et al.), and discusses soft trees/HME and online forests.

Quality: The formulation is technically sound and well-motivated. The bound is correct, differentiable almost everywhere, and paired with sensible per-row norm constraints. The complexity analysis and the fast inference variant are carefully reasoned. However, empirical support, while positive, is limited to medium-scale tabular datasets; statistical significance, calibration, and robustness analyses are missing. The impact of the fast bound versus the full bound on accuracy is not quantified.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized, with helpful algorithmic details (SGD/SSGD). Some notation is dense (e.g., f(g) and path-consistency) and could benefit from a worked example. Proofs are deferred to the supplement.

Originality: The latent structured prediction view of tree induction, the specific convex–concave upper bound, and the Hamming-ball inference trick constitute a novel and coherent approach beyond prior greedy/EM or max-margin node-local methods.

Significance and usefulness: If reproducible, the method is likely useful for building stronger single trees and potentially improving forests via better base learners. The O(d^2 p) per-step cost and SGD friendliness are appealing.

Limitations and suggestions:
- Compare against strong practitioners’ baselines (Random Forests, gradient boosting) and soft/probabilistic trees to contextualize single-tree gains.
- Provide ablations: effect of ν, SSGD vs SGD, initialization, and fast vs full bound on accuracy and convergence.
- Report wall-clock vs accuracy scaling with d, n, and p; include memory footprint.
- Add significance tests and fairness details for hyperparameter tuning of baselines.
- Evaluate regression (claimed) and larger or high-dimensional vision datasets; release code for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance: solid theory–algorithm link; efficient training; consistent empirical gains; clear path to broader impact.

Arguments against acceptance: limited experimental scope and baselines; unquantified trade-off introduced by the fast bound; missing statistical rigor.
