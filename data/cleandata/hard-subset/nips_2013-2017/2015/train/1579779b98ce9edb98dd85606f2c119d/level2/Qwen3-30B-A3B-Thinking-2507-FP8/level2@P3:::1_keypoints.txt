- Proposes a non-greedy decision tree optimization by formulating tree learning as structured prediction with latent variables.  
- Connects decision tree learning to structured prediction, enabling a convex-concave upper bound on empirical loss that is efficiently optimizable.  
- Shows optimal linear-combination split finding can be transformed into a latent variable problem with theoretical justification.  
- Main technical contribution: a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm reducing complexity from O(2^d p) to O(d^2 p), making deeper tree training feasible.  
- Uses stochastic gradient descent with row-wise norm constraints on split parameters to ensure efficient optimization and avoid overfitting.  
- Empirical results: non-greedy trees consistently outperform greedy baselines (axis-aligned, OC1, random oblique) across various depths.  
- Demonstrates better generalization and reduced overfitting, with regularization parameter ν implicitly controlling pruning.  
- Comparison to CO2 and other baselines validates benefits of the non-greedy optimization.  
- Theoretical foundations are sound: rigorous upper bound derivation, thorough complexity analysis, and supporting proofs in supplementary material.  
- Experimental design is appropriate with careful hyperparameter tuning and relevant baseline comparisons.  
- Clarity is good: logical organization, consistent notation, precise algorithm descriptions, and effective figures.  
- Originality is strong: provides a more efficient, general non-greedy framework handling arbitrary loss functions and reducing complexity from exponential to quadratic in depth.  
- Significance is substantial: addresses a long-standing limitation of greedy tree training with practical performance improvements on benchmarks.  
- Minor weaknesses: lacks deeper analysis of why joint optimization improves generalization, more study of how optimization changes tree structure, limited comparison to neural/tree hybrids, and no demonstration on very deep trees (e.g., depth > 20).  
- Overall recommendation: well-suited for NIPS—technically sound, clear, novel, and significant, with only minor addressable weaknesses.
