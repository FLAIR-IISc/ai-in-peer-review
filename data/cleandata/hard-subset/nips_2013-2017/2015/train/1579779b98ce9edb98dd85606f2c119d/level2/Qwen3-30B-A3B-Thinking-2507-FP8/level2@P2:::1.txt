

This paper proposes a novel non-greedy approach to decision tree learning that jointly optimizes split functions across all tree levels rather than using the standard greedy node-by-node procedure. The authors establish a connection between decision tree optimization and structured prediction with latent variables, formulating a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be efficiently optimized via stochastic gradient descent. A key technical contribution is reducing the computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d^2p) through a Hamming ball constraint for loss-augmented inference, enabling efficient training of deep trees.

The paper demonstrates strong experimental results, showing that non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines (including axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, and random oblique trees) across multiple benchmark datasets. The authors also show that their approach is less susceptible to overfitting and achieves better generalization performance. The method's ability to handle any general loss function while maintaining O(dp) inference complexity is a significant advantage over previous work.

Strengths: The theoretical foundation connecting decision trees to structured prediction is compelling and well-developed. The complexity reduction from exponential to polynomial is a major technical contribution that makes the approach practically feasible. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with comparisons across multiple datasets and tree depths, and the authors carefully analyze the impact of the regularization parameter ν on tree pruning and generalization. The paper also provides a clear explanation of the initialization procedure (using CO2 as a greedy baseline).

Weaknesses: The comparison with prior non-greedy methods is limited. While Bennett's work is mentioned, the paper doesn't directly compare with his approach on the same datasets or with the same experimental setup, making it difficult to assess how much better the proposed method is. The paper claims to handle any general loss function, but the experiments only use log loss and squared loss, without demonstrating the flexibility with other loss functions. The paper also doesn't sufficiently address the computational trade-off between training time and model quality - while the fast loss-augmented inference makes training feasible, the paper doesn't quantify the speedup compared to greedy methods for large datasets beyond the Connect4 example.

The paper would benefit from several improvements: 1) A more detailed comparison with Bennett's non-greedy approach, including computational complexity and accuracy on the same datasets; 2) Demonstration of the method with a non-standard loss function to showcase its flexibility; 3) More thorough analysis of the training time versus model quality trade-off; 4) Clarification on how the initialization procedure (using CO2) affects the final model quality and whether it's necessary for good performance.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in machine learning. The theoretical insights are valuable, and the practical implementation enables efficient training of deep decision trees. While the experimental results are promising, the paper could strengthen its claims by providing more direct comparisons with previous non-greedy methods and demonstrating the flexibility of the approach with additional loss functions.

The significance of this work is high, as decision trees are widely used in practice, and a method that improves their accuracy without sacrificing computational efficiency could have broad impact. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by providing a theoretically grounded and computationally efficient approach to non-greedy tree learning.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The main weakness is the insufficient comparison with prior non-greedy methods, which is critical for establishing the novelty and impact of the approach. With the suggested improvements, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
