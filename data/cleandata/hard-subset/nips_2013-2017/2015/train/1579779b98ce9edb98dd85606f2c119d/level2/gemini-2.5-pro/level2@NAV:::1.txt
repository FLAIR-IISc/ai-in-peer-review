This paper introduces a novel framework for non-greedy learning of decision trees, addressing the sub-optimality of conventional greedy induction algorithms like ID3 and CART. The core contribution is to formulate the joint optimization of all split functions and leaf parameters as a structured prediction problem with latent variables. The authors derive a convex-concave upper bound on the tree's empirical loss, which serves as a tractable surrogate objective. This objective is optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key technical innovation is a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm that reduces the complexity of gradient updates from exponential to polynomial in the tree depth, making the approach practical for deep trees.

The paper’s strengths are numerous and significant:

**Pros:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The formulation connecting global tree optimization to structured prediction with latent variables is novel, elegant, and provides a principled path away from the local, greedy choices that have dominated tree induction for decades. Given the widespread use of tree-based models, an effective non-greedy training method represents a substantial advance.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The derivation of the surrogate objective and, critically, the efficient algorithm for its optimization are well-executed contributions. The claims of superiority over greedy methods are convincingly supported by experiments on multiple benchmark classification datasets, showing improved generalization and less susceptibility to overfitting.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly motivates the problem, explains the theoretical framework, and presents the empirical results in an accessible manner.

While the paper is strong, there are areas for potential improvement:

**Cons:**
*   **Evaluation Scope:** The experiments focus exclusively on single trees. While this is justified to isolate the impact of the non-greedy algorithm, the most common and powerful applications of decision trees are in ensembles (e.g., Random Forests). An experiment demonstrating the benefit of using these non-greedy trees as base learners in a forest would significantly bolster the paper’s practical impact.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** The method is initialized using a greedily trained tree. A short discussion or ablation study on the sensitivity of the final model to the quality of this initialization would be insightful.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and effective method for a fundamental machine learning problem. The theoretical contribution is strong, and the empirical results are compelling. This work is likely to inspire further research into global optimization for tree-based models. I strongly recommend acceptance.
