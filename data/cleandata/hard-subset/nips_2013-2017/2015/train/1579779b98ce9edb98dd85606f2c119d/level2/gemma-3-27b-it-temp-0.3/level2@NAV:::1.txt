## Review of “Efficient Non-greedy Optimization of Decision Trees”

This paper addresses the well-known limitation of greedy algorithms in decision tree induction – their tendency to produce suboptimal trees. The authors propose a non-greedy approach, formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables and deriving a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss. This allows for optimization via stochastic gradient descent (SGD), enabling training of deep trees with large datasets. 

The core idea of linking decision tree optimization to structured prediction is novel and potentially impactful. The formulation, leveraging latent variables and a surrogate objective, is a clever way to tackle the non-convexity inherent in global tree optimization. The authors demonstrate a reduction in computational complexity from O(2dp) to O(d2p) which is a significant improvement for deeper trees, and crucial for scalability. The experimental results on benchmark datasets show that the proposed non-greedy trees outperform greedy baselines, validating the approach. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the experimental section feels somewhat limited. The comparison is primarily against relatively simple baselines (OC1, random trees), and the gains, while statistically significant, aren’t always dramatic. A comparison against more sophisticated methods like those utilizing ensemble techniques (e.g., boosted trees) would strengthen the claims. The discussion of hyperparameter tuning (specifically ν) is brief and lacks detail on the search process. Furthermore, the motivation for the hard constraints on the norm of W could be more thoroughly explained.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation linking decision trees to structured prediction.
*   Significant reduction in computational complexity for deep trees.
*   Empirical evidence of improved performance over greedy baselines.
*   Clear and well-defined problem statement and proposed solution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., ensemble techniques).
*   Insufficient detail on hyperparameter tuning.
*   Motivation for certain design choices (e.g., hard constraints) could be stronger.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of decision tree learning. The proposed non-greedy optimization approach is theoretically sound and empirically promising. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the novelty and potential impact of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and a comparison to stronger baseline methods.
