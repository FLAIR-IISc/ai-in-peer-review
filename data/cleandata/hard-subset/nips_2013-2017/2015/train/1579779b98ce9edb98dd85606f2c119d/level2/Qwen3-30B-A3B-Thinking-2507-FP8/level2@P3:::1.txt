

The paper proposes a non-greedy optimization framework for decision trees by formulating the problem as structured prediction with latent variables. The key insight connects decision tree learning to structured prediction, enabling the development of a convex-concave upper bound on the empirical loss that can be optimized efficiently. The authors establish a theoretical connection between the decision tree optimization problem and structured prediction, showing that the problem of finding optimal linear-combination splits can be transformed into a latent variable problem. The main technical contribution is a fast loss-augmented inference algorithm that reduces computational complexity from O(2^dp) to O(d²p), where d is tree depth and p is feature dimension, making deep tree training feasible. The approach uses stochastic gradient descent with row-wise norm constraints on the split parameters to ensure efficient optimization and prevent overfitting.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on multiple classification benchmarks, showing that non-greedy trees consistently outperform greedy baselines (including axis-aligned trees, OC1 oblique trees, and random oblique trees) across various tree depths. The non-greedy approach achieves better generalization performance and is less susceptible to overfitting, with the regularization parameter ν implicitly controlling pruning during training. The comparison with CO2 [18], a greedy variant of their approach, further validates the benefits of the non-greedy optimization.

The work is technically sound with clear theoretical foundations. The upper bound derivation is rigorous, and the complexity analysis is thorough. The experimental design is appropriate, with careful hyperparameter tuning and comparison against relevant baselines. The connection to structured prediction is well-motivated and provides a novel perspective on decision tree optimization. The paper's approach handles any general loss function while maintaining the computational efficiency of decision trees.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with logical organization and well-constructed figures. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithm descriptions are precise. The supplementary material provides additional proofs that support the main claims. The paper effectively explains how the proposed method differs from previous non-greedy approaches, particularly Bennett's [1] work which was limited to binary classification with 0-1 loss and high computational complexity.

The originality of the work is strong. While prior research has attempted non-greedy tree optimization, this paper provides a more efficient and general framework that handles any loss function while maintaining the computational advantages of decision trees. The reduction of computational complexity from exponential to quadratic in tree depth is a significant contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation of previous non-greedy approaches.

The significance of the work is substantial. Decision trees remain widely used in practice due to their interpretability and efficiency, yet their standard greedy training procedure has persisted for decades without fundamental improvement. The proposed method offers a principled way to optimize tree structures globally, potentially improving performance across numerous applications. The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over standard greedy baselines on multiple benchmarks, suggesting practical impact.

A few areas could be strengthened. The paper could provide more insight into why the non-greedy approach leads to better generalization beyond the regularization argument. A more detailed analysis of how the joint optimization changes tree structure would be valuable. The comparison with recent deep learning approaches to decision trees (e.g., neural network-based tree learning) would strengthen the significance argument, though this may be outside the scope of NIPS 2015. The paper claims "training deep trees is feasible" but doesn't demonstrate results on very deep trees (e.g., depth > 20) to fully validate this claim.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel approach that is both theoretically grounded and practically applicable. The technical contributions are significant, the experiments are comprehensive, and the writing is clear. The work advances the state of the art in decision tree learning by providing a computationally feasible non-greedy optimization framework that outperforms standard greedy methods. The connection to structured prediction with latent variables provides a new perspective that could inspire further research in this area.

The paper meets all four criteria for NIPS acceptance: it is technically sound with strong theoretical and experimental support (quality); it is well-written and clearly organized (clarity); it presents a novel formulation that differs significantly from previous work (originality); and it addresses a fundamental limitation in a widely used machine learning method with practical implications (significance). The minor weaknesses identified are reasonable to address in revision but are not critical to the paper's core contributions.
