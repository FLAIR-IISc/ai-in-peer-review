- Introduces a top-k extension of multiclass SVM using a convex top-k hinge loss and a projection onto the top-k simplex, optimized via Prox-SDCA, with empirical gains on large datasets (ImageNet, Places205).
- Novelty: Extends Crammer and Singer SVM to top-k with a top-k hinge loss and top-k simplex connection as a significant theoretical contribution.
- Technical soundness: Rigorous derivations and proofs using Fenchel duality and Prox-SDCA provide convergence guarantees.
- Scalability: Demonstrates scalability to large-scale datasets with millions of examples.
- Empirical validation: Consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across five datasets, especially where many classes make top-k metrics relevant.
- Reproducibility: Public implementation provided, enhancing impact and reproducibility.
- Clarity: Presentation is dense and may be hard to follow for readers unfamiliar with convex optimization or duality.
- Comparisons: Related work discussion is limited; needs deeper analysis vs. ranking-based losses (e.g., Usunier et al. 2009) on tightness and efficiency.
- Top-1 trade-off: Occasional drops in top-1 accuracy (e.g., MIT Indoor 67) are observed but not thoroughly discussed.
- Argument for acceptance: Addresses an important large-scale classification problem with a theoretically sound, practically effective solution.
- Argument for acceptance: Consistent empirical improvements and demonstrated scalability make it relevant to the community.
- Argument for acceptance: Technical contributions (top-k hinge loss and projection algorithm) are novel and impactful.
- Argument against acceptance: Dense presentation and limited related-work discussion may reduce accessibility and contextual understanding.
- Argument against acceptance: Incomplete exploration of the trade-off between top-k and top-1 accuracy could limit practical applicability.
- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance, with suggestions to improve exposition clarity and expand related-work discussion in the final version.
