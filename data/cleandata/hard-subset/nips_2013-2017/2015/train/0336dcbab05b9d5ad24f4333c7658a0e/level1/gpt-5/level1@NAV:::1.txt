This paper proposes a principled extension of multiclass SVM to directly optimize top-k performance. The key idea is a convex upper bound on the top-k zero-one loss, the “top-k hinge,” which averages the k largest margin violations and recovers Crammer–Singer [5] at k=1. Via Fenchel duality, the authors derive a conjugate whose effective domain is a new “top-k simplex,” a contraction of the standard simplex with adaptive upper bounds. They develop an efficient algorithm for (biased) Euclidean projection onto this set—of independent interest—and embed the loss within Prox-SDCA [24], yielding a scalable solver with a computable duality gap. Experiments on five image datasets (including SUN397, Places205, and ILSVRC12) report consistent top-k accuracy gains over baselines (Crammer–Singer, SVMPerf [11], TopPush [16], WSABIE-like methods [9]) and show the proposed φk can be tighter than ranking-based surrogates φ̃k from Usunier et al. [27].

Quality: The formulation is technically sound. The convexity of φk follows from the convexity of sums of order statistics; the conjugate and j-compatibility arguments are carefully tied to the optimization framework. The projection algorithm is well motivated, distinguishes knapsack vs. cone regimes, and gives clear complexity bounds (sorting + O(kd)). Empirically, the method scales and improves top-k metrics. However, the paper lacks statistical guarantees (e.g., calibration/consistency for top-k risk), sensitivity analyses (k at train vs. test, λ), and ablations on the biased projection (ρ) and projection runtime vs. standard simplex solvers.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. Some derivations are dense and deferred; adding intuition for the top-k simplex geometry and a small worked example would help. A few reference/typo issues should be cleaned up. The relation between φk and φ̃k could be summarized more crisply in the main text.

Originality: The top-k simplex and its projection algorithm are novel and useful; the surrogate is a tighter variant relative to [27]. Integrating this with Prox-SDCA yields a practical contribution beyond prior LaRank/structured-SVM approaches.

Significance: Top-k metrics are central in large-class recognition; consistent gains and scalability suggest impact. Broader relevance extends beyond vision to any high-m multiclass setting.

Pros for acceptance:
- New convex surrogate and geometric construct (top-k simplex) with efficient projection.
- Sound dual derivation and scalable Prox-SDCA implementation with stopping criterion.
- Consistent empirical improvements on diverse, large-scale datasets; code release.

Cons for acceptance:
- No generalization/calibration theory for top-k; limited analysis of trade-offs with top-1.
- Missing comparisons to strong cross-entropy/softmax baselines optimized end-to-end.
- Limited ablations and clarity on the bias term and projection regimes.
