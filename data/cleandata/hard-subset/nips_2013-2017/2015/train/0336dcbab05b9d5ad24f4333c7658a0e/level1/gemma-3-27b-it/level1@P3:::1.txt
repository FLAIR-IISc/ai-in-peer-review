This paper introduces a top-k multiclass SVM, motivated by the ambiguity often present in high-class image classification. The core idea is to directly optimize for top-k accuracy using a convex upper bound on the top-k error. A novel projection onto the top-k simplex is also presented, with its own theoretical interest. The paper is well-structured and addresses a relevant problem.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The problem of top-k classification is well-recognized (e.g., [27, 16, 11]), offering a more robust evaluation metric than zero-one loss in scenarios with many classes. Existing approaches often rely on reductions to binary classification or approximations of ranking losses. This paper differs by proposing a direct, convex optimization formulation tailored for top-k multiclass SVM. The connection to Crammer and Singer's multiclass SVM [5] is clearly established, and the work builds upon their framework. The relationship to Usunier et al.’s [27] general ranking loss framework is also well-explained, positioning their approach as a specific instance within that broader context. The derivation of the conjugate function for the top-k loss and the formulation of the top-k simplex (∆k) are new contributions.  The extension to the standard simplex, while interesting, feels less central to the main contribution. The use of Prox-SDCA ([24]) for optimization is a sensible choice, given its efficiency and scalability.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound. The derivations of the primal-conjugate pair for the top-k hinge loss (Proposition 2) and the theoretical foundation for the top-k simplex are well-presented. The proofs are relegated to the supplement, which is standard practice. The use of Fenchel duality and the connection to Prox-SDCA are appropriate and well-justified. The claim of convexity for the proposed loss function is crucial and appears to hold, based on the presented analysis. The optimization framework appears robust and leverages existing, well-established algorithms. However, the justification for choosing the specific convex upper bound (3) over the more natural (5) – and the subsequent analysis of their differences – feels slightly underdeveloped. A more detailed comparison of the properties of the two loss functions would strengthen the argument for their choice.  The update rule and its connection to projection onto the top-k simplex are correctly derived.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. The notation is consistent, and the key concepts are explained in a straightforward manner. However, the section on the projection onto the top-k simplex (4.1 and 4.2) is dense and requires careful reading.  The explanation of the continuous quadratic knapsack problem and its relation to the projection could benefit from a more intuitive explanation or a clarifying example. A visual aid illustrating the projection process would be very helpful.  The use of Lemma 5 to determine whether to apply the knapsack or cone projection could also be made more explicit.

**Originality:**

The paper presents a novel approach to top-k multiclass SVM. While the problem itself is not new, the direct optimization of top-k accuracy via a convex upper bound, along with the derived top-k simplex and efficient projection algorithm, constitutes a significant contribution. The careful connection to existing work (Crammer & Singer, Usunier et al.) positions the work well within the existing literature. The efficient projection algorithm is particularly noteworthy and demonstrates a good understanding of optimization techniques.

**Significance:**

The results demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy compared to baselines on five datasets. The scalability of the method to large datasets (Places 205, ImageNet) is a significant advantage.  While the improvements in accuracy are not always dramatic, they are consistent and statistically significant. This work addresses a practical problem in image classification, where class ambiguity is common, and offers a valuable tool for improving performance in these scenarios. The potential impact is moderate-to-high, as practitioners and researchers working with large-scale image classification problems are likely to find this method useful.  The theoretical contribution of the top-k simplex and projection algorithm also adds to the broader body of knowledge in convex optimization.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to top-k multiclass SVM.
*   Clear theoretical justification for the proposed loss function and optimization framework.
*   Scalable implementation based on Prox-SDCA.
*   Consistent improvements in top-k accuracy on multiple datasets.
*   Interesting theoretical contribution of the top-k simplex.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The justification for the specific convex upper bound (3) could be stronger.
*   The section on the projection algorithm is dense and could benefit from more clarity and visual aids.
*   The magnitude of improvements, while consistent, isn't always substantial.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work is technically sound, original, and addresses a relevant problem. While there are some areas where the clarity could be improved, the overall contribution is significant and warrants inclusion in NIPS. The authors have convincingly demonstrated the value of their approach through both theoretical analysis and experimental results.
