Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles multiclass classification when evaluation uses top-k error rather than strict top-1. It proposes a direct surrogate tailored to top-k: a “top-k hinge” loss defined as the positive part of the average of the k largest margin violations. This loss upper-bounds the discrete top-k error and tightens the standard multiclass hinge (Crammer-Singer) for k>1. Using Fenchel duality, the authors derive the conjugate and identify a new feasible set, the “top-k simplex,” which generalizes the standard simplex by coupling per-coordinate upper bounds to the sum of coordinates. Building on this, they design a Prox-SDCA solver whose inner step reduces to (biased) Euclidean projection onto the top-k simplex. They present algorithms and structural lemmas for this projection (including the top-k cone case), show computational complexity comparable to classic simplex projection, and demonstrate empirical gains in top-k accuracy on five vision datasets over baselines including multiclass SVM, ranking surrogates (Usunier’s weighted losses via LaRank/SVMPerf), and TopPush.

The work connects to prior multiclass SVMs (Crammer-Singer), ranking-based surrogates for top-k/recall@k (Usunier et al., Joachims), and large-scale SDCA optimization (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang). The new surrogate sharpens the link to top-k error beyond Lβ formulations by summing the k largest raw violations before thresholding, yielding a tighter convex upper bound than averaging k individual hinges. The projection onto the top-k simplex appears novel and of independent interest.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The surrogate is convex (sum of k largest is convex), upper-bounds the discrete top-k error, and reduces to Crammer-Singer at k=1. The primal-dual derivation leverages a compatibility condition and corrects a subtlety in prior SDCA formulations. The conjugate characterization with the top-k simplex is coherent, and the equivalence of the dual update to a (biased) projection problem is well-justified. While full proofs are deferred to the supplement, the statements are standard-appearing and internally consistent.
- Optimization: The Prox-SDCA framework is appropriate, provides convergence guarantees, and gives a principled stopping criterion via duality gap. The projection algorithm is carefully reasoned, splitting between a knapsack case (sum-active) and a top-k cone case, with worst-case O(d log d + k d) time. This is a substantive algorithmic contribution.
- Empirics: Experiments on five datasets (including large-scale Places205 and ILSVRC; details partly in the supplement) show consistent top-k gains over multiclass SVM and several ranking baselines. Implementation details (features, cross-validation ranges, use of BLAS, released code) support reproducibility. However, statistical significance and ablations are limited, and comparisons to multinomial logistic regression (softmax) baselines are missing despite their prevalence in top-k evaluation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized: motivation → loss → conjugate/top-k simplex → dual/SDCA → projection → experiments. Notation is dense but consistent, and the geometric intuition (Figure of the top-k simplex) helps. Algorithm 1 and the explicit lemmas for projection improve readability. Some results critical to understanding (proofs, large-scale results) are pushed to the supplement; summarizing key statements and including a compact main-table for large datasets in the paper would improve accessibility. Minor typographic issues are acknowledged (compatibility correction).

Originality
The top-k hinge as a tighter convex upper bound than Lβ’s averaged hinge, its conjugate, and the associated top-k simplex/projection are, to my knowledge, novel. While the idea of summing k largest components is known in optimization, the specific integration into a multiclass surrogate with a full primal-dual derivation and scalable solver is new. The projection algorithm seems original and may be useful beyond this application.

Significance
Top-k metrics are ubiquitous in large-vocabulary recognition. A direct, convex, scalable surrogate that improves top-k accuracy and integrates cleanly with linear models is practically valuable. The top-k simplex projection is a reusable primitive. Empirical gains (~1–3% absolute in top-5 across datasets) are modest but consistent, and the method scales to million-example datasets. Theoretical significance would be strengthened by statistical guarantees (e.g., calibration/consistency w.r.t. top-k error), which are absent.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for top-k optimization and a principled, convex surrogate tighter than prior ranking-based bounds.
- Sound primal-dual analysis; compatibility with Prox-SDCA and a rigorous stopping criterion.
- Novel and well-reasoned projection onto the top-k simplex/top-k cone with practical complexity.
- Consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets; released code aids reproducibility.
- Scalable to large datasets; implementation leverages BLAS and supports kernelization in principle.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Missing statistical analysis: no calibration or consistency results w.r.t. top-k Bayes optimality. Adding even partial guarantees or discussion would strengthen the contribution.
- Baselines: absence of multinomial logistic regression/softmax (a dominant baseline for top-k) on the same features; adding this would contextualize gains.
- Experimental reporting: lack of confidence intervals/significance tests; limited ablation on k-mismatch (e.g., training with k’ vs evaluating with k), and on the effect of the biased vs unbiased projection (ρ).
- Scope of models: only linear models on fixed CNN features are evaluated; showing at least kernelized or shallow nonlinear variants would test generality.
- Some key results are in the supplement; include a concise main-text table for large-scale datasets and a brief runtime comparison for the projection vs standard simplex projection.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, well-founded surrogate and associated optimization primitive (top-k simplex projection).
- Solid duality-based optimization with convergence guarantees and practical stopping criterion.
- Consistent, scalable empirical gains on relevant tasks; code release enhances impact.
- Good fit to NIPS topics (learning theory, optimization, large-scale classification).

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of statistical guarantees relating the surrogate to the target top-k risk.
- Missing strong softmax baseline and limited ablation/significance analysis.
- Improvements are moderate; top-1 can degrade on some datasets.

Overall evaluation
This is a strong paper with a clear problem, a principled and novel convex formulation, a nontrivial optimization component of independent interest, and solid empirical evidence. Despite some missing analyses (softmax baseline, statistical guarantees), the contribution is of sufficient originality and practical significance to merit acceptance.
