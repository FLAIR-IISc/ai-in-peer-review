**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel extension to the multiclass Support Vector Machine (SVM) framework, called top-k multiclass SVM, which optimizes for top-k performance instead of the standard zero-one loss. The authors introduce a convex upper bound on the top-k error, called the top-k hinge loss, and derive its convex conjugate. They also propose an efficient optimization scheme based on the proximal stochastic dual coordinate ascent (Prox-SDCA) framework. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method on five image recognition datasets, showing consistent improvements in top-k accuracy.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon the multiclass SVM framework of Crammer and Singer [5], which is a well-established method for multiclass classification. The authors also relate their work to ranking-based losses, such as the one proposed by Usunier et al. [27]. The top-k hinge loss is shown to be a tighter upper bound on the actual top-k zero-one loss compared to the loss function proposed in [27].

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough derivation of the top-k hinge loss and its convex conjugate. The optimization scheme is also well-motivated and efficient. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on a variety of datasets.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and its relation to previous work. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow.

**Originality**

The paper proposes a novel extension to the multiclass SVM framework, which is a significant contribution to the field of machine learning. The top-k hinge loss and its convex conjugate are new and interesting results.

**Significance**

The paper addresses a significant problem in multiclass classification, namely, the need to optimize for top-k performance instead of the standard zero-one loss. The proposed method has the potential to improve the performance of multiclass classification systems in a variety of applications.
