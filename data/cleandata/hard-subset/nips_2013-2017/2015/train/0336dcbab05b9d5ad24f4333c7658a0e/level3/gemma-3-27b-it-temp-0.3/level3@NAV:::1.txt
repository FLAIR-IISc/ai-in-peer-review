## Review of “Top-k Multiclass SVM”

This paper proposes a novel formulation for top-k multiclass classification based on a hinge loss, generalizing the standard multiclass SVM. The core idea is to use the sum of the k largest score components as a convex approximation to the top-k loss, enabling efficient optimization via a proximal stochastic dual coordinate ascent (Prox-SDCA) algorithm. The paper is well-written and clearly presents the mathematical derivation of the proposed loss function and its conjugate, alongside a detailed optimization framework. It appropriately connects the work to existing literature on multiclass SVMs, ranking-based losses, and convex optimization techniques. 

A key strength of this work lies in its theoretical soundness. The authors convincingly demonstrate the convexity of their proposed approximation and provide a practical algorithm for efficient optimization, including a discussion of projection onto the top-k simplex. The experimental results on five datasets consistently show improvements in top-k accuracy compared to several baselines, validating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors also acknowledge limitations and discuss potential future work, demonstrating a thoughtful understanding of the research. 

However, a notable weakness is the lack of comparison with methods [25, 8], which the authors attribute to unavailable implementations. Including these comparisons, even with approximations or re-implementations, would significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's performance relative to the state-of-the-art. Furthermore, while the paper discusses scalability, more detailed computational complexity analysis, particularly concerning the projection step, would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound formulation of top-k multiclass SVM.
*   Efficient optimization algorithm with practical implementation details.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Consistent empirical improvements over strong baselines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Missing comparisons with relevant prior work ([25, 8]).
*   Limited discussion of computational complexity.

**Overall:** This is a neat and valuable contribution to the field. The proposed formulation is well-motivated, theoretically justified, and empirically validated. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the reviewer's request to include comparisons with [25, 8] and making the code publicly available upon acceptance, as this will greatly benefit the research community.
