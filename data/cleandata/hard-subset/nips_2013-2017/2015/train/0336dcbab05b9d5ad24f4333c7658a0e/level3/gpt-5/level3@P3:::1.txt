Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Top-k Multiclass SVM, a direct large-margin method for optimizing top-k classification accuracy. The core idea is to replace the standard multiclass hinge with a convex upper bound tailored to the top-k error: the “top-k hinge” uses the average of the k largest score residuals as a surrogate. This yields a principled generalization of the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM (recovered at k=1), and ties to Ky-Fan norms via the sum of k largest components. The authors derive the convex conjugate of the loss and introduce the “top-k simplex,” a constrained polytope that plays the role of the feasible set in the dual. They then develop an efficient Prox-SDCA optimization scheme whose per-example dual update reduces to a (biased) Euclidean projection onto the top-k simplex. A dedicated algorithm for this projection is presented and analyzed.

The paper clearly positions itself with respect to prior multiclass and ranking-based approaches. It relates to Usunier et al.’s ordered weighted hinge losses and shows that a more common “truncated” top-k hinge variant is looser than the proposed surrogate, while remaining optimizable within the same Prox-SDCA framework. It also compares conceptually to Joachims’ structured SVM for multivariate measures and to reduction-based ranking methods (e.g., TopPush), clarifying when recall@k aligns with top-k error and the practical scaling limits of those formulations. Experiments on five image datasets (including large-scale Places205 and ILSVRC12 with CNN features) demonstrate consistent gains in top-k accuracy over strong baselines.

Strengths
- Neat formulation: a tight, convex, and interpretable surrogate for top-k error that smoothly generalizes Crammer–Singer.
- Solid theory: clear Fenchel-duality derivations, yi-compatibility conditions, and the introduction/characterization of the top-k simplex; the conjugate pairs are clean and informative.
- Efficient optimization: smart use of Prox-SDCA with a well-defined duality-gap stopping criterion; the dual update reduces to a structured projection problem with a practically efficient algorithm (sorting-based, O(d log d + k d)).
- Clarity: the paper is well-organized and readable; the geometry and optimization details are explained with helpful lemmas and cases.
- Empirical efficacy: consistent improvements in top-k metrics across datasets; scalability to millions of examples; careful use of strong baselines (LibLinear OVA, SVMPerf for recall@k, TopPush, Wsabie++).

Weaknesses and questions
- Missing comparisons: the paper notes the lack of results for [25, 8] due to unavailable implementations. These seem relevant to top-k/ranking performance; please add comparisons if possible (re-implement core variants, use reported hyperparameters, or at least provide a careful quantitative/qualitative discussion with matched protocols).
- Trade-off with top-1: while often acceptable in high-class-count settings, the method can reduce top-1 accuracy on some datasets. A more systematic analysis of the top-1/top-k trade-off (e.g., varying k at training vs evaluation) would strengthen the empirical narrative.
- Complexity details: the per-update cost depends on m (number of classes) via the projection dimension. A brief runtime/memory analysis versus Crammer–Singer and OVA for large m would help practitioners anticipate scaling.
- Practicality of the projection: while the projection algorithm is elegant, it includes a worst-case O(k d) exhaustive search over partitions. Empirical timing comparisons to standard simplex projections are mentioned (supplement); it would help to summarize these in the main text and outline heuristics that prune partitions in practice.
- Sensitivity/robustness: more ablations on λ, choice of k during training, and the two loss variants (φk vs φ̃k) would add confidence about hyperparameter sensitivity.

Quality
- Technically sound and careful. The derivations are correct and well-motivated; proofs (in the supplement) appear to close the necessary gaps. The optimization algorithm is principled, with convergence guarantees inherited from Prox-SDCA and a verifiable stopping criterion. Experiments are convincing and use competitive baselines; however, the missing comparisons with [25, 8] should be addressed.

Clarity
- Very clearly written and well-structured. Notation is consistent. The role of the bias term ρ in the projection and its default choice (ρ=1 in the dual update) are explained, though a brief intuition in the main text on why the bias enforces orthogonality and helps numerics would be valuable. Consider moving a short summary of the projection timing results from the supplement into the main paper.

Originality
- The top-k hinge based on the sum of the k largest components (and its conjugate/top-k simplex) is a crisp and novel surrogate tailored to top-k error, distinct from prior ordered-weighted or structured surrogates. The dual characterization and projection algorithm are original and of independent interest.

Significance
- High practical significance: top-k metrics are the norm in modern large-scale classification. The method is scalable, simple to implement with standard BLAS, and yields consistent improvements. The top-k simplex projection may be reused in other models (e.g., metric learning, multilabel with cardinality priors). Likely to be adopted in practice and to inspire follow-ups (e.g., deep integration, smoother surrogates).

Requested additions before final decision
- Please include empirical comparisons with [25, 8] (or closest re-implementations) and discuss any discrepancies in datasets/settings. If code is unavailable, provide a reasoned reproduction or a robust cross-paper comparison under matched features/splits.
- The authors state that code is (or will be) released. Please commit to making the full training/testing code and the top-k simplex projection library publicly available upon acceptance, with scripts to reproduce main tables/figures.

Pros
- Clear, principled extension of multiclass SVM to top-k loss
- Strong theory (conjugate, top-k simplex) and efficient algorithm (Prox-SDCA + projection)
- Consistent top-k gains and large-scale results
- Clean writing and useful connections to prior work

Cons
- Missing comparisons with [25, 8]
- Limited analysis of top-1 vs top-k trade-offs and hyperparameter sensitivity
- Projection complexity/practicality could be summarized more clearly in the main text

Overall recommendation
A neat and well-executed formulation of top-k multiclass SVM with solid theoretical grounding, an efficient solver, and compelling empirical gains. I recommend acceptance, contingent on adding comparisons with [25, 8] and committing to public code release.
