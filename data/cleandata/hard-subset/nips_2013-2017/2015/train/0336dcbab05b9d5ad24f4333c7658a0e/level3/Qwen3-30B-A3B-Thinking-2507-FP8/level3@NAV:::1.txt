

This paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM formulation that generalizes the standard multiclass SVM to optimize for top-k classification performance. The key innovation is the top-k hinge loss, which uses the sum of the k largest score components as a convex approximation to the target top-k loss, providing a tight convex upper bound that extends the standard multiclass SVM when k=1. The authors derive the convex conjugate of this loss, introducing the top-k simplex as a natural extension of the standard simplex, and develop an efficient optimization algorithm based on Prox-SDCA with a novel projection onto the top-k simplex. The paper demonstrates strong theoretical connections to ranking-based losses and provides clear experimental results showing consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across five image classification datasets compared to standard multiclass SVM and other baselines.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured mathematical derivations and helpful illustrations (like Figure 2). The technical quality is strong, with rigorous theoretical development and effective optimization. The work is original in its formulation of the top-k hinge loss and the associated top-k simplex, making a meaningful contribution to multiclass classification literature. The significance is evident as top-k classification addresses a real-world challenge in large-scale image recognition where class ambiguity is common.

The main weakness is the omission of comparisons with [25, 8] due to unavailable implementations, which the reviewer requests the authors to address before acceptance. While the paper's code will be made public upon acceptance (a positive requirement), the missing comparisons limit the completeness of the experimental validation.

Strengths: Novel convex formulation, clear theoretical development, efficient optimization, consistent experimental improvements across datasets, excellent writing.

Weaknesses: Missing comparisons with [25, 8], though this is understandable given implementation constraints.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in multiclass classification with a clean, theoretically sound approach that demonstrates practical value. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale classification.
