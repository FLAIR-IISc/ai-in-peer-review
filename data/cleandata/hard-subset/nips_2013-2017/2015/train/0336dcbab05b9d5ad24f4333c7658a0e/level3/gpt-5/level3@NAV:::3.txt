Summary: The paper tackles multiclass classification in the presence of class ambiguity by optimizing top-k accuracy rather than top-1. The core contribution is a top-k multiclass SVM derived from a tight convex upper bound on the top-k zero-one loss: φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑j=1..k (a+c)[j]}, which relaxes the penalty when the true class appears among the top-k predicted scores. The authors derive the Fenchel conjugate of this top-k hinge and identify a new feasible set—the “top-k simplex” Δk—leading to a proximal SDCA optimization with an inner step reducible to a (biased) projection onto Δk. They design an efficient projection algorithm by decomposing cases (knapsack when the sum constraint is active, and a “top-k cone” otherwise). The method relates to prior multiclass SVMs (Crammer–Singer), ranking-based ordered-weighted hinge losses (Usunier et al.), and structured SVM optimization of recall@k (Joachims), but offers a tighter, instance-decomposable surrogate with scalable optimization. Experiments on five image datasets show consistent improvements in top-k accuracy.

Quality: The technical development is sound; the convex upper bound, dual derivation, and Δk projection are carefully argued and integrated into a convergent Prox-SDCA framework with a principled stopping criterion. Empirical results are strong and cover moderate to large scales. A limitation is the absence of statistical analysis (e.g., calibration/consistency for top-k, generalization guarantees tied to φk), and more ablations on the projection runtime vs. standard simplex projection would help.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and organized; proofs are available in the supplement. Minor issue: page 3 typo “globally optimal leads to” should remove “optimal.”

Originality: Extends multiclass SVM to top-k via a novel hinge and introduces the top-k simplex with efficient projection—both non-trivial and, to my knowledge, not previously explored in this exact form. The comparison to φ̃k from ordered-weighted losses is insightful.

Significance: Optimizing top-k is highly relevant for large-class recognition; the method is practical and likely to be adopted. The Δk projection may be of independent interest.

Suggestions:
- Further motivate the choice of φk versus φ̃k: discuss tightness, optimization stability, and empirical trade-offs; clarify whether φk is top-k calibrated or offers margin-based generalization benefits.
- Provide more guidance on selecting k and sensitivity to λ and data scale; discuss behavior as k→1 and k→m.
- Expand runtime comparisons and memory considerations on the largest datasets.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, non-trivial optimization and modeling contributions
- Strong, consistent empirical gains on relevant benchmarks
- Good positioning vs. prior NIPS/ICML work; scalable implementation

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical guarantees for the surrogate beyond convexity/tightness
- Some empirical analyses (sensitivity, ablations) could be deepened
