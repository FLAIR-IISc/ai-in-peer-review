Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses large-scale multiclass classification where evaluation commonly uses top-k error. The authors propose a direct, convex surrogate for optimizing top-k performance: a “top-k hinge” loss that averages the k largest margin violations and upper bounds the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss. The formulation recovers the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM when k=1 and yields a tighter bound than the ordered-weighted hinge losses of Usunier et al. for the specific choice of β (they also discuss an alternative φ̃k that corresponds to that family and empirically compare).

The paper develops a convex-analytic view via Fenchel duality and introduces the “top-k simplex,” a contraction of the standard simplex that arises as the effective domain of the conjugate. This geometry leads to an efficient optimization scheme using Prox-SDCA with block updates, where each coordinate block update reduces to a (biased) projection onto the top-k simplex. A key technical contribution is an algorithm for this projection, handling two regimes: (i) when the sum constraint is tight (reduces to a continuous quadratic knapsack), and (ii) otherwise, a projection onto a “top-k cone,” for which the authors derive thresholding conditions and complexity O(d log d + kd). The approach scales to linear models and can be kernelized.

Experiments on five image classification datasets (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN397, Places205, ImageNet2012) using CNN features show consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over strong baselines: Crammer–Singer SVM, ranking-based approaches (SVMPerf for recall@k, TopPush), one-vs-all SVM, and Wsabie++. Code is released.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The convex surrogate is well motivated, with clear inequalities ψk ≤ φk ≤ φ̃k ≤ φ and equality conditions. The derivation of the conjugate and top-k simplex appears correct and insightful, and the dual derivation for l2-regularized multiclass objectives is careful (including the j-compatibility condition). The projection algorithm is a substantive contribution; the case analysis and complexity claims are reasonable.
- Empirical support: The experiments cover varied scales and provide consistent top-k gains; the inclusion of both φk and φ̃k helps attribute where the tighter bound matters. The use of Prox-SDCA with a duality-gap stopping criterion is appropriate, and the method scales to million-example datasets. Providing code enhances reproducibility.
- Areas to strengthen: While the surrogate is intuitive, it would help to further motivate why averaging the top-k violations (versus, e.g., soft top-k variants or smoothed max-k) is the preferred choice theoretically. A statistical perspective (e.g., top-k calibration/consistency or excess-risk bounds) would raise confidence that optimizing φk yields improvements in population top-k error beyond empirical evidence. Sensitivity analyses would also help: training with k different from evaluation k, effect of ρ in the biased projection (even if fixed to 1 here), and ablations on the projection solver’s components. Finally, including a multiclass logistic regression baseline (and, where feasible, an end-to-end softmax model) would contextualize improvements relative to widely used cross-entropy training for top-k.

Clarity
The paper is well written, logically organized, and sets up notation clearly. The geometric intuition for the top-k simplex is helpful; additional intuition (or a small toy example) showing how φk relaxes constraints on the top-(k−1) competitors while separating the rest would be valuable to readers. Minor issue: Typo on page 3: “globally optimal leads to” — remove “optimal.” The discussion contrasting CQKP and the top-k simplex is clear about why existing projection methods don’t apply.

Originality
The top-k hinge φk and its conjugate with the associated top-k simplex seem novel, as is the efficient projection algorithm tailored to this geometry. The integration with Prox-SDCA for block updates in multiclass is natural but well executed. Relative to prior work on ranking-based surrogates (Usunier et al., LaRank, Wsabie) and structured SVM for recall@k (Joachims), this work offers a distinct convex surrogate that is instance-decomposable and scalable.

Significance
Top-k metrics are central in vision and other large-label applications. A principled, scalable method that directly improves top-k accuracy is impactful, and the projection algorithm may be useful beyond this setting. The empirical gains, while sometimes modest, are consistent across datasets and k, and the method’s scalability is a practical strength.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation of a top-k hinge loss with tightness relative to alternatives.
- Novel convex-analytic insight (top-k simplex) and an efficient projection algorithm of independent interest.
- Solid optimization framework with convergence diagnostics and scalability to large datasets.
- Consistent top-k accuracy improvements across multiple benchmarks; code release aids adoption.
- Good positioning relative to multiclass SVMs and ranking-based surrogates.

Cons
- Limited theoretical guarantees on statistical properties (e.g., calibration/consistency for top-k).
- Motivation for choosing this specific top-k surrogate over alternatives could be strengthened.
- Experimental baselines could include multiclass logistic/softmax to better benchmark against common practice; more detailed runtime/memory comparisons would be useful.
- Some results deferred to the supplement; include key large-scale numbers in the main text if space permits.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you comment on top-k calibration of φk or provide bounds relating excess φk-risk to excess top-k error?
- How sensitive is performance to training k versus evaluation k? Any guidance on selecting k?
- Did you consider smoothed versions of φk to facilitate faster optimization or improved generalization?
- Please add a brief complexity comparison (wall-clock, epochs to a fixed duality gap) versus LibLinear and ranking baselines on at least one dataset.
- Minor: fix the typo noted above and consider adding more intuition/visualization for the top-k simplex geometry.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and well-executed paper on an important problem, with both theoretical and algorithmic contributions and convincing empirical results. I recommend acceptance after minor revisions to strengthen motivation and clarify empirical comparisons.
