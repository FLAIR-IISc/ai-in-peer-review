This paper introduces a novel multiclass Support Vector Machine (SVM) formulation, dubbed top-k multiclass SVM, designed to directly optimize for top-k performance, a common evaluation metric in tasks like image classification where class ambiguity is prevalent. The authors address the challenge of allowing ‘k’ guesses and propose a convex upper bound of the top-k error for optimization. A key contribution is an efficient projection onto the top-k simplex, offering computational advantages. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across five datasets through comprehensive experimentation.

The work builds upon the well-established multiclass SVM framework of Crammer and Singer [5] and addresses a limitation of standard zero-one loss by explicitly modeling the relaxed penalty for errors within the top-k predictions. Related work in ranking-based losses, particularly that of Usunier et al. [27], is thoughtfully discussed, and the paper highlights the differences and connections between their approach and the proposed top-k hinge loss. The connection to the continuous quadratic knapsack problem and existing algorithms for the simplex projection (e.g., [13, 18, 19]) is also well-acknowledged. 

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the top-k hinge loss and its conjugate are rigorously presented and justified. The derivation of the optimization procedure, based on Fenchel duality and Prox-SDCA [24], appears solid. The careful analysis regarding the convexity of the proposed loss is a significant strength.
* **Originality:** Addressing top-k accuracy directly within an SVM framework is a novel and practical contribution. While ranking-based losses exist, the specific formulation and efficient optimization for top-k SVM offer a distinct advantage. The derivation of the top-k simplex and its properties is also a noteworthy contribution.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the technical details are presented logically, and the connection to existing work is thoroughly explained. The notation is consistent and accessible.
* **Significance:** The problem of optimizing for top-k accuracy is highly relevant in many real-world applications, particularly in large-scale image classification where discriminating between similar classes is difficult. The demonstrated improvements on five datasets, including large-scale benchmarks like Places 205 and ImageNet, suggest that this approach could have a practical impact. The release of the C++ library is a commendable move for reproducibility and broader adoption.
* **Scalability:**  The authors highlight the scalability of the method to very large datasets, which is a crucial factor for its potential application to real-world problems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Convergence:** While the optimization utilizes the well-established Prox-SDCA framework with known convergence guarantees, the paper does not provide a detailed theoretical analysis of the convergence rate specifically for the proposed top-k hinge loss and its associated projections.  
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The experiments show cross-validation is used to tune the regularization parameter, but a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the choice of 'k' itself would be beneficial. How does performance vary across different values of k, and what guidelines can be provided for selecting an appropriate value?
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons to several methods are presented (SVMPerf, TopPush), it might be valuable to compare against more recent and state-of-the-art deep learning approaches specifically designed for large-scale image classification, even if it’s just to contextualize the gains achieved.
* **Minor Typo/Inconsistency:** The statement in Section 3.3 "This is opposed to SGD updates with data-independent step sizes, as well as to maximal but scalar updates in other SDCA variants" is somewhat vague and could be clarified.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in multiclass classification.
* The proposed approach is technically sound, novel, and well-motivated.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements on multiple datasets.
* The code release promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research.
* The writing quality is excellent, making the paper easy to understand and appreciate.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed convergence analysis specific to the proposed method is a minor weakness.
* The sensitivity analysis regarding the 'k' parameter could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multiclass classification. The proposed top-k multiclass SVM offers a principled and efficient approach to optimizing for top-k performance, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is well-suited for acceptance to NIPS 2017.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
