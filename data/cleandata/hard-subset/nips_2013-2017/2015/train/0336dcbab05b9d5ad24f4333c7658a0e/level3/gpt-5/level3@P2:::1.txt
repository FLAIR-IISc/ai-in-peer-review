Summary
This paper proposes a principled top-k multiclass SVM that directly optimizes a convex surrogate for top-k error. The key idea is to replace the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss with a tight convex upper bound, the top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑j=1..k (a + c)[j]}, which averages the top k largest margin violations. The formulation recovers the Crammer-Singer SVM at k=1 and yields a conjugate whose effective domain is a novel “top-k simplex” ∆k, a proper subset of the standard simplex. The authors derive primal-dual formulations via Fenchel duality and optimize with Prox-SDCA, relying on an efficient (biased) projection onto ∆k(r). They also position their loss against ranking-based surrogates (notably Usunier et al.’s weighted losses), showing that the alternative φ̃k (the average of k hinge terms) is a looser bound than φk except in edge cases. Experiments on five vision datasets (Caltech-101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN397, Places-205, ImageNet-2012) demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over baselines, including Crammer-Singer (k=1), SVMPerf for recall@k, TopPush, and Wsabie++.

Relation to prior work
- Multiclass SVM: Extends Crammer-Singer [5] by replacing the max-of-margins with the average of the top k margins; recovers [5] at k=1.
- Ranking losses and top-k metrics: Connects to the ordered-weighted hinge of Usunier et al. [27], contrasts φk with φ̃k and with structured SVM for multivariate measures [11]. The reduction from multiclass to binary for ranking losses is correctly described.
- Optimization: Builds on Prox-SDCA [24] and classical CQKP/knapsack projection literature [18,19,13], while introducing the top-k simplex geometry and a dedicated projection routine that existing simplex projection algorithms do not support.

Strengths
- Clear, tight convex surrogate: φk is a demonstrably tighter upper bound on top-k error than φ̃k in nontrivial regimes, with a transparent relation to Crammer-Singer.
- Solid convex analysis: Clean conjugate derivation, introduction of the top-k simplex ∆k, and yi-compatibility for dual derivations; the formulation is mathematically sound.
- Algorithmic contribution: Efficient (biased) projection onto ∆k(r) with complexity O(d log d + kd), covering both cone and simplex cases; of independent interest.
- Practical optimization: Prox-SDCA with batch-wise dual updates, explicit duality gap, BLAS-friendly operations, and kernelizability.
- Empirical results: Consistent top-k gains across multiple datasets, including large-scale (Places-205, ImageNet-2012), with comparisons to strong baselines.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Missing comparisons: The paper mentions the lack of comparisons to some related methods (e.g., [25], and another cited as [8]) due to unavailable implementations. Even partial reimplementations or controlled approximations would strengthen the case. At minimum, please provide detailed justification and a discussion of expected behavior relative to those methods.
- Limited theoretical guarantees on statistical properties: No analysis of calibration/consistency w.r.t. top-k error. A short discussion or known results (even negative) on Bayes consistency for top-k would be valuable.
- Trade-off with top-1 accuracy: The paper notes possible decreases in top-1 for some datasets. An ablation (varying k at training time, evaluating multiple k at test time) and a discussion of when top-1 degrades would guide practitioners.
- Empirical detail: For large-scale datasets, more explicit training-time curves, convergence plots (duality gaps), and a breakdown of time spent in projection vs. other steps would help quantify scalability.
- Projection algorithm clarity: The exhaustive search over partitions in the general cone case is elegant but could benefit from clearer pseudo-code and stopping/optimality guarantees, plus average-case complexity in practice.

Quality
The technical development is careful and sound: convexity and conjugates are correctly derived; the dual and SDCA updates follow from standard theory; the projection routine is justified with appropriate lemmas and case analyses. Experiments are well-chosen and show consistent improvements in top-k metrics. Some theoretical aspects (statistical consistency) are not addressed, and certain comparisons are missing, but overall the work is solid.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The connections between φk, φ̃k, and prior ranking losses are explained cleanly. The optimization section is detailed, and the supplement reportedly contains proofs and additional experiments. Minor suggestion: include concise pseudo-code for the projection algorithm in the main paper for readability.

Originality
The top-k hinge φk and its top-k simplex conjugate domain appear novel, as does the efficient (biased) projection onto ∆k(r). The Prox-SDCA adaptation is a judicious application rather than a novelty, but the overall package—surrogate, dual, projection, and solver—is a meaningful and original contribution.

Significance
Top-k metrics are central in modern high-class-count recognition problems. A principled, scalable convex surrogate that improves top-k accuracy is practically important. The top-k simplex projection could find broader use beyond this specific SVM. The empirical gains are consistent (though sometimes modest), and the method scales to millions of examples, increasing potential impact.

Specific questions and requests for the authors
- Please include empirical comparisons (even partial) or at least stronger analytical positioning versus [25] (probabilistic n-choose-k) and the other omitted baseline [8], and clarify why these methods are impractical to run under your setting.
- Provide training-time and convergence plots, and a breakdown of time per epoch for your solver vs. SVMPerf/TopPush/Wsabie++.
- Discuss whether φk is classification-calibrated for top-k error, or provide counterexamples/references.
- Analyze the effect of choosing k in training vs. evaluation time; e.g., train with k=5 and test for k=1,3,5,10 to quantify trade-offs.
- Please make the code and projection routines publicly available upon acceptance (preferably with scripts to reproduce key tables/figures).

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, principled extension of multiclass SVM to top-k with a tight convex surrogate.
- Novel and useful projection algorithm onto the top-k simplex.
- Strong empirical evidence across diverse datasets; scalable optimization with clear stopping criteria.
- Well-written and likely to be used and extended by the community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing comparisons to closely related approaches (e.g., [25] and another cited baseline), leaving some uncertainty about relative performance.
- Lack of theoretical guarantees regarding statistical consistency for top-k.
- Modest improvements in some settings and occasional top-1 degradation without a systematic analysis.

Overall recommendation
A neat and technically solid formulation of top-k multiclass SVM with an efficient and scalable solver and a novel projection routine. Despite the missing comparisons and limited statistical analysis, the paper is clear, well-motivated, and shows consistent empirical gains on important benchmarks. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the missing comparisons and releasing code.
