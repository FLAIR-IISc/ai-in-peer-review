This paper proposes a direct method for optimizing top-k classification performance, a relevant problem for tasks with many classes where class ambiguity is common. The authors introduce a "top-k hinge loss," which serves as a convex upper bound on the non-convex top-k zero-one loss. This formulation is presented as a natural generalization of the well-known multiclass SVM by Crammer and Singer. The core theoretical contribution lies in showing that this new loss function's convex conjugate has an effective domain that defines a "top-k simplex," a contraction of the standard simplex. The paper further relates its proposed loss to the family of ranking-based losses from Usunier et al. [27], arguing that their formulation provides a tighter convex upper bound. To make the method practical, the authors develop an efficient optimization algorithm based on the Proximal Stochastic Dual Coordinate Ascent (Prox-SDCA) framework. A key component of this is a novel and efficient algorithm for projection onto the top-k simplex, which is a significant technical contribution in its own right.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical development is elegant, starting from the motivation for a new loss function, deriving its convex formulation and conjugate, and establishing the connection to the top-k simplex. The proposed optimization algorithm is well-justified and its most complex step—the projection onto the top-k simplex—is addressed with a novel, efficient algorithm. The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on five image classification datasets, including large-scale ones like Places 205 and ImageNet, demonstrating the method's scalability and effectiveness.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for optimizing top-k error directly is clear, and the mathematical development is easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of relating their formulation to the standard multiclass SVM, which helps the reader understand the proposed generalization. Figures are used effectively to illustrate concepts like the top-k simplex. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the contributions and potentially reproduce the work.

*   **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the top-k multiclass SVM is novel and provides an elegant, direct approach to a practical problem. While related to prior work on ranking losses, the proposed top-k hinge loss is a tighter bound on the target loss, and the resulting optimization framework is distinct. The development of an efficient projection algorithm for the top-k simplex is a valuable and non-trivial contribution that could be of independent interest. Given the increasing prevalence of large-scale classification problems where top-k accuracy is a primary metric, this work is significant and likely to be used and built upon by both researchers and practitioners. It advances the state of the art by providing a principled and effective method that outperforms standard baselines.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The main weakness of the paper lies in the experimental comparison. The authors note that they did not compare against the methods in [25] (Sun and Ponce, 2013) and [8] (Gong et al., 2014) due to the unavailability of public implementations. While this is an understandable difficulty, these methods represent relevant and strong baselines in the image classification domain, particularly for approaches using deep features. Including these comparisons would provide a more complete picture of the proposed method's standing relative to the state of the art at the time. I would encourage the authors to make a stronger effort to implement or find comparable recent baselines to strengthen their empirical evaluation.

Finally, the authors state their intention to release the code, which is commendable. I strongly recommend that they make the code and experiment setups publicly available upon acceptance. This would be a great service to the community, ensuring reproducibility and fostering future research in this area.

**Summary:**

Overall, this is a very strong paper presenting a neat and principled formulation for a top-k multiclass SVM. The work is theoretically sound, the proposed algorithm is efficient, and the experimental results are convincing. The paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale classification. The strengths clearly outweigh the minor weakness regarding the experimental comparisons.
