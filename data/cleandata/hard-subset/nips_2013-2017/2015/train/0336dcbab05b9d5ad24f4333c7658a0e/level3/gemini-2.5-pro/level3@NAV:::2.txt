This paper addresses the problem of multiclass classification in scenarios where, due to semantic similarity between classes, top-k accuracy is a more appropriate evaluation metric than the standard zero-one loss. The authors propose a novel "top-k Multiclass SVM" that directly optimizes a convex surrogate for the top-k error. This work builds directly upon the well-known multiclass SVM of Crammer and Singer, extending its hinge loss formulation to a new "top-k hinge loss," which is shown to be a tight, convex upper bound on the non-convex top-k objective. The work also situates itself in relation to general ranking-based losses, such as those by Usunier et al., arguing that their proposed loss function provides a tighter bound.

The paper has several significant strengths:

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is the novel top-k hinge loss and its corresponding dual formulation, which naturally defines a "top-k simplex." This provides a principled and direct way to optimize for a common and practical evaluation scenario, moving beyond heuristics or less direct ranking-based approaches. The development of an efficient projection algorithm onto this top-k simplex is a valuable technical contribution in its own right.

*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is sound, clearly deriving the convex conjugate of the proposed loss and integrating it into the Prox-SDCA optimization framework. The claims are strongly supported by a thorough empirical evaluation on five diverse and large-scale image classification datasets, including Places 205 and ImageNet. The results consistently demonstrate improved top-k performance over relevant baselines.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the mathematical formulations are presented logically, and the connection to prior work is well-established, making the paper easy for the reader to follow.

A minor weakness is the limited discussion on the trade-offs. The authors note that improving top-k accuracy can sometimes decrease top-1 accuracy, which is an expected outcome. A more detailed analysis of when and why this occurs would be beneficial. Furthermore, while the optimization is well-grounded, a discussion of the generalization bounds for this new loss function could further strengthen the theoretical contribution.

Overall, this is a high-quality submission presenting a novel, elegant, and practical solution to an important problem. The contributions are significant, the experiments are convincing, and the work advances the state of the art in large-scale multiclass classification.
