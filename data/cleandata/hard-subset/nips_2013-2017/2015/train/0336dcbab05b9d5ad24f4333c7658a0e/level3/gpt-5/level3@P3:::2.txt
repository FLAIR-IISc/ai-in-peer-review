Summary and relation to prior work
The paper targets multiclass settings with many semantically similar classes where top-k evaluation is the right metric: an error occurs only if the correct class is not among the top k predictions. Building on the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM, the authors propose a direct surrogate for top-k error. The core idea is a convex top-k hinge loss that upper-bounds the nonconvex “kth score” loss and becomes the standard hinge when k=1. They derive the conjugate of this loss and show it leads to a new “top-k simplex” constraint set in the dual. Optimization is performed via Prox-SDCA with block updates per example, which reduces to repeated (biased) projections onto the top-k simplex. The authors develop an efficient projection algorithm by analyzing two regimes (binding-sum knapsack vs. top-k cone) and provide complexity guarantees comparable to standard simplex projection.

This work is well-positioned against prior literature: (i) it generalizes Crammer–Singer (k=1) while remaining convex; (ii) it relates to Usunier et al.’s ordered-weighted hinge family, showing their loss corresponds to an alternative “top-k” variant that yields a looser bound than the proposed one except at regime boundaries; (iii) it contrasts with Joachims’ structured SVM for multivariate measures (e.g., recall@k), noting the non-decomposable loss limits scalability. The paper also compares to LaRank-style approaches and to binary reductions used by ranking methods (SVMPerf, TopPush).

Strengths
- Motivation and contribution: Clear case for optimizing top-k directly, with a principled convex surrogate that tightens the gap to top-k zero-one relative to ordered-weighted hinge losses. The top-k simplex and its conjugate are neat, interpretable constructions.
- Formulation and theory: Clean derivation of the primal–dual pair via Fenchel duality, including the yi-compatibility condition and a corrected treatment of constraints that clarifies prior typos in SDCA derivations. The analysis of projection regimes (knapsack vs. cone) is technically solid and of independent interest.
- Optimization: Prox-SDCA with per-example block updates, closed-form dual improvements, rank-1 primal updates, and a duality-gap stopping rule. The projection algorithm has near-sorting complexity O(d log d + k d) and handles the bias term.
- Scalability: Demonstrated on large-scale image classification (Places205, ImageNet) with millions of examples and hundreds–thousands of classes; implementation leverages BLAS and could be kernelized.
- Empirical results: Across five datasets, the method consistently improves top-k accuracy over Crammer–Singer and ranking baselines; in several cases it improves top-1 as well. Comparisons include SVMOVA, SVMPerf (recall@k), TopPush, and Wsabie++ with competitive, carefully engineered features (fc7).
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is easy to follow, well-organized, and supported by figures (top-k simplex), algorithm pseudocode, and released code, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Tightness and calibration: While the proposed loss is a tighter convex upper bound than ordered-weighted variants, the paper does not study statistical properties such as top-k calibration or Bayes consistency. A discussion or preliminary analysis would strengthen the theoretical case.
- Empirical breadth: The strongest competitors for top-k in large-scale vision today are deep softmax models trained with cross-entropy; although this work uses deep features, it would be informative to compare to linear softmax (multinomial logistic regression) and report top-k metrics. Even if end-to-end CNNs are out of scope, a linear softmax baseline is feasible.
- Runtime and memory: Results emphasize scalability qualitatively; more detailed wall-clock comparisons and memory profiles versus strong linear baselines (e.g., LibLinear OVA, multinomial logistic regression) would help quantify efficiency, especially for m≈1000.
- Choice of k for training vs. evaluation: The paper shows that training with k′>1 can improve top-k′ and sometimes top-1. It would be useful to systematically study mismatch scenarios (e.g., training k′=10, evaluating top-5/top-1) and provide guidance on selecting k′.
- Class imbalance and cost sensitivity: The method assumes uniform costs within top-k. Discussion of extensions to class- or example-dependent costs or imbalanced settings would broaden applicability.
- Projection algorithm details: The worst-case search over partitions in the cone-projection step is mitigated in practice, but more empirical analysis of its behavior (distribution of cases, number of iterations) would reassure readers.

Quality
Technically sound and complete. The derivations are careful, the optimization is principled, and experiments support claims across multiple datasets. Some theoretical aspects (calibration/consistency) are not addressed, and runtime analyses could be deeper.

Clarity
Very clear and well-structured. The connection to prior work is well-explained. Pseudocode and algorithmic details (dual updates, projection) are sufficiently concrete for reproduction. Public code is a plus.

Originality
The top-k hinge with its top-k simplex conjugate and efficient projection are novel and nontrivial. The integration with Prox-SDCA for exact optimization of both proposed and ordered-weighted variants is also a meaningful advance over prior approximations.

Significance
High practical relevance: top-k metrics are standard in large-class problems (e.g., ImageNet). The method scales and consistently improves top-k accuracy; the projection routine may be reused beyond this application. Likely to be adopted by practitioners working with linear models and large label spaces.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide results with a linear softmax (multinomial logistic regression) baseline on the same features?
- Do you have any theory on top-k calibration of the proposed loss?
- How sensitive is performance to the bias parameter ρ? Is ρ=1 essential?
- Any observations on the number of cone vs. knapsack projections encountered in practice and their runtime distribution?
- Guidance on choosing training k′ when deployment k is fixed?

Pros
- Principled, tighter convex surrogate for top-k; elegant dual/top-k simplex.
- Efficient, scalable optimization with strong empirical gains.
- Clear exposition; released code.

Cons
- No calibration/consistency theory; limited discussion of statistical properties.
- Missing linear softmax comparisons; limited runtime breakdowns.
- Limited guidance on selecting k′ and handling imbalanced/cost-sensitive settings.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper offers a well-motivated, technically solid, and practically impactful contribution to multiclass learning under top-k evaluation.
