

This paper proposes a novel top-k multiclass SVM framework that optimizes for top-k classification performance rather than standard zero-one loss, addressing class ambiguity in large-scale image classification. The authors generalize the multiclass SVM of Crammer and Singer [5] by introducing a convex upper bound of the top-k error, defined as φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑_{j=1}^k (a+c)[j]}, where (a+c)[j] denotes the j-th largest component of the vector (a+c). This formulation naturally extends the standard multiclass SVM (recovered when k=1) and provides a tighter convex upper bound on the discrete top-k loss than previous approaches like the ranking-based loss in [27]. The key technical contribution is an efficient optimization scheme based on projection onto the top-k simplex, which is a convex polytope defined as ∆k(r) = {x | ⟨1,x⟩ ≤ r, 0 ≤ xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩, i∈[m]}, differing from the standard simplex by the upper bound constraint on individual coordinates.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding, establishing the convex conjugate of the proposed loss function and showing its relationship to ranking-based losses [27]. The authors also provide an efficient algorithm for projection onto the top-k simplex, which is crucial for optimization. The optimization framework leverages Prox-SDCA [24], making the approach scalable to large datasets. Experiments on five image classification datasets (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN 397, Places 205, and ImageNet 2012) show consistent improvements in top-k accuracy compared to baselines, including standard multiclass SVM, SVMPerf, and TopPush. Notably, the top-k SVMα (using the proposed loss) achieves +2.6% top-5 accuracy on Caltech 101, +1.2% on MIT Indoor 67, and +2.5% on SUN 397 compared to top-1 SVMα.

Strengths: The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of multiclass classification with a large number of classes. The top-k formulation addresses an important practical problem (class ambiguity in large-scale classification) with a theoretically sound approach. The convex relaxation is well-motivated and tighter than previous alternatives. The optimization algorithm is efficient and well-justified, with the projection onto the top-k simplex being a novel technical contribution with potential applications beyond this specific problem. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering datasets of varying scale and complexity, and the results consistently demonstrate the advantage of the proposed approach. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Weaknesses: While the paper mentions comparisons with [25, 8] (Wsabie and Wsabie++), it states these were not included due to unavailable implementations. The authors should include these comparisons if possible, as they represent relevant baselines for large-scale multiclass classification. Additionally, the paper could provide more discussion on the trade-off between top-1 and top-k performance, particularly why top-1 accuracy sometimes improves with top-k optimization (as observed on Caltech and SUN 397). The authors should also make their code publicly available upon acceptance, as this would greatly facilitate reproducibility and further research.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a solid theoretical foundation. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis (convex conjugate, optimization framework) and experimental results across multiple datasets. The authors are careful to address the relationship to previous work and the limitations of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, with sufficient detail for experts to follow. The experimental setup and results are presented with appropriate precision.

Originality: This is a novel contribution that extends the multiclass SVM framework to the top-k setting. While the concept of top-k classification has been explored before, the authors provide a convex, tight relaxation that is directly optimized, rather than approximating the problem as ranking or using indirect approaches. The top-k simplex and its efficient projection are also novel contributions.

Significance: The problem of large-scale multiclass classification with ambiguous classes is important in computer vision and beyond. The top-k metric is widely used in practice (e.g., in image retrieval and scene recognition), and this work provides a principled approach to optimizing for this metric. The consistent improvements across multiple datasets and the scalability to large-scale problems (Places 205 and ImageNet) demonstrate the practical significance of the work.

Pro: Novel top-k SVM formulation with tight convex relaxation; theoretically sound; efficient optimization; strong experimental results across multiple datasets; well-written; addresses an important practical problem.

Con: Missing comparisons with Wsabie/Wsabie++ baselines; could provide more discussion on top-1/top-k trade-offs; code should be made publicly available.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in multiclass classification. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and demonstrates significant improvements over baselines. The proposed top-k SVM framework provides a principled approach to optimizing for the top-k metric, which is widely used in practice. The efficient optimization algorithm and clear theoretical foundation make this work highly valuable to the community. I recommend acceptance.
