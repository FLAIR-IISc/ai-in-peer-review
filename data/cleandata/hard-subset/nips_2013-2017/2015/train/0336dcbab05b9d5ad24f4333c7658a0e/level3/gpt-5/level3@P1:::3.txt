This paper tackles multiclass classification when evaluation focuses on top-k accuracy. The authors propose a “top-k multiclass SVM” that directly optimizes a convex surrogate of the top-k zero-one loss. Starting from the Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM, they define a top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) sum of the k largest components of (a + c)} and show ψk ≤ φk ≤ φ1, with φk strictly tighter than φ1 unless the top-k scores tie. They derive the convex conjugate of φk and identify the “top-k simplex” Δk as its effective domain, highlighting a key structural difference from the standard simplex (per-coordinate upper bounds depend on the total mass). They develop a fast Prox-SDCA optimization scheme whose inner step reduces to a (biased) projection onto Δk(r). A main algorithmic contribution is an efficient projection method onto the top-k simplex/cone with worst-case O(d log d + kd) time via sorting and a structured search over active sets. Experiments on five image datasets (Caltech-101 silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN397, Places-205, ImageNet-2012 using CNN features) show consistent gains in top-k accuracy over Crammer-Singer, one-vs-all, SVMPerf recall@k, TopPush, and Wsabie++ variants; the method scales to millions of examples and code is released.

Relation to prior work: The formulation connects to ranking-based convex surrogates of Usunier et al. (weighted ordered hinge) and to Joachims’ structured SVM for multivariate measures (recall@k). The authors also present an alternative loss φ̃k that averages the top-k positive parts, placing their top-k hinge as a tighter upper bound except in boundary cases. On optimization, the paper builds on Fenchel duality and Prox-SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang) and contributes a new projection primitive unavailable in standard CQKP algorithms due to the Δk coupling. The approach recovers Crammer-Singer when k=1 and relates to LaRank and binary reductions for ranking baselines.

Quality: The technical development is careful: convexity and conjugates are established; the yi-compatibility condition and dual derivation are clearly stated; the SDCA update solves the joint block subproblem optimally; the new projection algorithm is well motivated with correctness conditions and complexity analysis. The experimental methodology is solid (cross-validation, strong baselines, multiple datasets and k’s), and the gains in top-k accuracy are consistent. Two limitations: (i) theory stops at surrogate design and optimization; calibration/consistency with top-k error or generalization bounds are not discussed; (ii) guidance on choosing between φk and φ̃k and when the Δk constraint is active is mostly empirical.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized, with clear notation and an informative related-work discussion. The top-k simplex is intuitively presented (Figure 2). Suggestions: further motivate the choice of φk vs φ̃k beyond tightness (e.g., with geometric intuition and failure cases), and provide a brief practical recipe for selecting k and regularization in large-scale settings. Minor typo: page 3 “globally optimal leads to” — remove “optimal” (“globally leads to”).

Originality: The top-k hinge, its conjugate characterization via the top-k simplex, and the corresponding projection algorithm are new and nontrivial. While related ranking losses exist, this work supplies a tighter surrogate tailored to multiclass top-k and an efficient solver with a principled duality-gap stopping rule.

Significance: Top-k metrics are ubiquitous in large-scale vision. A scalable, convex, multiclass method that directly optimizes a tight surrogate is practically valuable, and the projection routine may be reusable elsewhere. The observed improvements over well-established baselines on challenging datasets suggest impact for practitioners and a useful building block for future theory on top-k calibration.

Specific comments and suggestions:
- Motivation: Expand on why averaging the top-k margins (φk) is preferable to averaging top-k positive parts (φ̃k) or other ordered-weighted surrogates; discuss implications for class ambiguity and margin distribution.
- Theory: If possible, add remarks on top-k consistency or calibration, even partial results or references, and clarify conditions under which φk equals φ̃k.
- Optimization details: Add a short pseudo-code box or flowchart for the projection routine and practical heuristics for detecting the knapsack vs cone case; report wall-clock comparisons vs standard simplex projection and total training time vs Crammer-Singer SDCA.
- Practicalities: Briefly discuss memory footprint for large m,n, kernelization experiments (even small-scale), and behavior under class imbalance.
- Notation: Summarize symbols (Δk(r), ρ, U/M/L) near first use.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and relevant problem (top-k accuracy) with a principled, convex surrogate.
- Solid theory (conjugates, dual, SDCA block updates) and a novel projection algorithm of independent interest.
- Strong, scalable experiments with consistent top-k gains on multiple datasets, including very large ones.
- Well written; code release enhances reproducibility.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited theoretical guarantees regarding top-k calibration/generalization.
- Empirical guidance on selecting between φk and φ̃k and hyperparameters could be strengthened.
- Some implementation details (projection pseudo-code, runtime breakdown) are deferred or brief.

Overall: A well-executed and timely contribution on an important metric, with both theoretical and practical value. I lean toward acceptance.
