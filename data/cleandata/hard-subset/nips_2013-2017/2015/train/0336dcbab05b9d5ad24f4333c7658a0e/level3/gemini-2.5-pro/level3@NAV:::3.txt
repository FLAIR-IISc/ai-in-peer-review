This paper proposes a "Top-k Multiclass SVM," a principled method to directly optimize for top-k classification performance. This is particularly relevant for modern large-scale classification tasks where class ambiguity is high and top-k accuracy is a standard metric. The core idea is to introduce a novel top-k hinge loss, which serves as a tight, convex upper bound on the non-convex top-k zero-one loss. This work is a direct and elegant generalization of the classic multiclass SVM by Crammer and Singer. The authors also correctly position their formulation with respect to the general family of ranking losses by Usunier et al., demonstrating that their proposed loss is a tighter surrogate. A key technical contribution is an efficient projection algorithm onto the "top-k simplex," a novel convex set arising from the dual of the new loss function, which enables scalable optimization via Prox-SDCA.

The paper has numerous strengths.
**Quality:** The work is technically sound and the material is non-trivial. The theoretical derivations connecting the primal loss, its convex conjugate, and the top-k simplex are solid. Claims are convincingly supported by strong experimental results across five image classification datasets, showing consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over several baselines.
**Originality:** The proposed top-k hinge loss and the associated optimization machinery are novel. While related to prior work on ranking, this specific formulation as a direct multiclass SVM extension is a new and valuable contribution. The projection algorithm is also a novel contribution of independent interest.
**Significance:** The paper addresses a highly relevant problem. By providing a scalable method to directly optimize a surrogate for top-k error, it offers a tool that practitioners and researchers are likely to use and build upon, advancing the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way.
**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and well-organized, making the complex derivations easy to follow.

The paper has very few weaknesses, but I have minor suggestions for improvement. The motivation for the specific form of the loss function—summing the top k scores—could be slightly expanded upon its introduction to further strengthen the narrative. Additionally, there is a minor typo on page 3: in the phrase "globally optimal leads to," the word "optimal" appears to be redundant.

Overall, this is a nice paper describing an interesting and important problem. The combination of a novel theoretical formulation, a significant algorithmic contribution, and strong empirical validation makes for a high-quality submission. I recommend acceptance.
