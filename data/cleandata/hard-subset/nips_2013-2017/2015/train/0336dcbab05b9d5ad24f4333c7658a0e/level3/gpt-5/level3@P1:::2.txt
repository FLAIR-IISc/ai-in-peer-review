This paper addresses multiclass classification when evaluation is based on top-k accuracy, a setting increasingly common in large-scale vision where classes can be semantically similar. The authors propose a top-k multiclass SVM that directly optimizes a convex surrogate tailored to the top-k zero-one loss. The key idea is a top-k hinge loss φk that averages the k largest margin violations, yielding a convex upper bound tighter than the standard multiclass hinge (k = 1), and also (typically) tighter than the ordered-weighted hinge of Usunier et al. [27]. The loss admits a clean Fenchel conjugate whose effective domain is a “top-k simplex” ∆k, a constrained polytope that reduces to the standard simplex when k = 1. Building on this, the paper derives a dual formulation and an efficient Prox-SDCA optimization scheme [24] whose core step reduces to a (biased) projection onto ∆k. The authors develop a specialized projection algorithm by decomposing into a continuous knapsack case and a “top-k cone” case, with complexity O(d log d + kd). Experiments on five image datasets (Caltech101 silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN397, Places205, ImageNet 2012) with fixed CNN features demonstrate consistent gains in top-k accuracy over baselines (including multiclass SVM [5], one-vs-all SVM, SVMPerf [11], TopPush [16], and Wsabie-like methods), and the method scales to millions of examples. The paper relates to listwise/pairwise ranking losses [27], structured SVM for multivariate measures [11], and LaRank [1, 9], and shows how their reduction-based ranking baselines compare.

Quality: The technical development is solid. The convexity of φk, its conjugate, and the emergence of the top-k simplex are carefully established; the j-compatibility device and corrected Prox-SDCA dual constraints (cf. [24]) are useful clarifications. The projection algorithm is well-motivated: the coupling constraint xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1, x⟩ invalidates standard CQKP solvers, and the authors’ case analysis (knapsack vs cone) is principled and efficient. The optimization procedure is complete with a stopping criterion via duality gap, and the rank-1 updates are standard and implementable. Empirically, the gains in top-k are consistent across datasets and k, with sometimes neutral/positive effects on top-1 for larger datasets, aligning with the motivation. Code release further supports reproducibility.

A few technical caveats: (i) While φk is a tighter surrogate than φ̃k [27] in the generic case, the paper does not analyze statistical consistency/calibration for top-k error. Some discussion or bounds (even margin-based) would strengthen the theoretical contribution. (ii) The worst-case enumeration over partitions in the cone projection is O(kd) after sorting; empirical timing plots are helpful (supplement), but a clearer characterization of constants and memory use on very large m would be useful. (iii) Kernelization is mentioned, but experiments are linear; a brief note on practicality with large m (storage of W vs A, impact on projection dimension) would help.

Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and well-structured, with helpful intuition for ψk vs φk and a clean connection to known multiclass SVMs [5]. The “top-k simplex” concept and geometry (Figure 2) aid understanding. Some suggestions:
- Unify the notation/naming of the two variants (α for φk vs β for φ̃k) earlier and maintain this throughout.
- Move high-level pseudocode for the projection (including special cases) from supplement into the main text or provide a concise flowchart.
- Provide a consolidated complexity table for per-iteration costs and end-to-end training time vs baselines.

Originality: The formulation of a tight convex top-k hinge, its conjugate on the top-k simplex, and the tailored projection algorithm appear novel. The relation to and improvement over the ordered-weighted hinge [27] is well articulated. The optimization via Prox-SDCA is an elegant application, and the discovery that the effective domain is a contracted simplex is interesting in its own right.

Significance: Top-k metrics are central in large-scale vision (e.g., ImageNet top-5), and a principled, scalable surrogate is valuable. The method is likely to be adopted by practitioners using linear models with deep features and by researchers studying structured surrogates. The projection routine may be of independent interest for other problems involving sums of the largest components.

Empirical evaluation: The benchmarks and protocols are appropriate; using pre-trained CNN features is a reasonable contemporary setting. However:
- Some ranking baselines did not scale under the multiclass-to-binary reduction, and the one-vs-all fallback may disadvantage them; please clarify computational budgets and report wall-clock and memory to contextualize scalability claims.
- Include additional baselines such as multinomial logistic regression (softmax) trained on the same fixed features to contextualize top-k performance.
- Provide an ablation on the choice of k during training vs evaluation (e.g., train with k′ and evaluate at multiple k) and a sensitivity analysis over λ and ρ (bias term).
- Report the trade-off curves showing changes in top-1 when optimizing for higher k.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear problem motivation with practical importance.
- Novel, principled convex surrogate with a tightness advantage over prior ranking surrogates.
- Strong and complete optimization framework with convergence guarantees and a novel projection routine.
- Demonstrated scalability to million-scale datasets and consistent empirical improvements.
- Clear writing, connections to prior work, and released code.

Cons for acceptance:
- Lack of theoretical guarantees regarding calibration/consistency for top-k error.
- Some reliance on supplement for key algorithmic details and empirical timing; fairness of some baselines limited by scalability.
- Limited analysis of resource usage and of the trade-off between top-1 and top-k across settings.

Overall: A strong contribution that advances both methodology and practice for top-k multiclass learning. With minor clarifications and additional analysis, it will be a solid NIPS paper. I recommend acceptance.
