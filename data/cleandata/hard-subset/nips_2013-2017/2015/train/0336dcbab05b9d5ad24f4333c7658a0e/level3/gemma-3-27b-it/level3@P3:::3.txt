This paper introduces a novel approach to multiclass SVM training by directly optimizing for top-k accuracy. The core idea – learning with respect to a top-k loss – is well-motivated given the challenges of class ambiguity in large-scale classification tasks. The authors propose a convex upper bound on the top-k zero-one loss, termed the top-k hinge loss, and develop an efficient optimization scheme based on projections onto a novel “top-k simplex.” Experimental results across five datasets demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy compared to various baselines. Overall, this is a nice paper describing an interesting and practically relevant problem, offering a solid technical contribution.

The paper is technically sound and well-written. The derivation of the top-k hinge loss and its conjugate is clear and logically presented. The connection to existing work, particularly Crammer and Singer’s multiclass SVM and Usunier et al.’s ranking-based losses, is effectively established. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of related literature. The optimization framework, based on Prox-SDCA, is appropriately chosen and detailed. The experimental setup appears rigorous, with comparisons to several relevant baselines. The scalability of the method is a significant strength, demonstrated by the successful application to datasets like Places 205 and ImageNet. 

One potential weakness is the relatively limited motivation for *why* the proposed top-k hinge loss, specifically its particular form (Equation 3), is chosen as a convex upper bound. While the authors explain *that* it’s an upper bound and its relation to the true top-k loss, further justification regarding its properties and how it balances tightness and tractability would be beneficial. For instance, discussing alternative upper bounds and why they were not chosen could strengthen the argument. 

A minor issue is a typo on page 3: "globally optimal leads to" — this should be simply "globally leads to" or "leads to a globally optimal solution".

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The direct optimization of top-k accuracy is a fresh approach to multiclass SVM.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations and optimization framework are well-defined and justified.
*   **Experimental Validation:** Consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across diverse datasets.
*   **Scalability:** The method is demonstrated to work effectively on large-scale datasets.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-organized and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Motivation of Loss Function Choice:** The justification for selecting the specific form of the top-k hinge loss could be strengthened.
*   **Minor Typo:** A small typographical error on page 3.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a relevant problem (multiclass classification with ambiguous classes).
*   The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The paper's contribution to the field – a tractable convex upper bound for top-k loss and an efficient optimization scheme – is significant.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of deeper justification for the specific form of the top-k hinge loss is a minor weakness. However, it does not fundamentally detract from the overall quality of the work.
*   While innovative, the improvement over existing methods is incremental and might not represent a paradigm shift.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The strengths of the work – its novelty, technical soundness, and experimental validation – outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of machine learning, providing a practical and effective method for improving top-k accuracy in multiclass classification. The authors have clearly demonstrated a solid understanding of the relevant literature and have presented their work in a clear and well-organized manner. Addressing the suggestion regarding further motivating the loss function choice could further strengthen the paper, but is not essential for acceptance.
