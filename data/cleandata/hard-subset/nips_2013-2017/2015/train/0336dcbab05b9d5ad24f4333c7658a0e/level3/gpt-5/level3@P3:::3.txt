Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles multiclass classification when class ambiguity is high and top-k error is the relevant metric: a prediction incurs zero loss if the true label appears among the k highest-scoring classes. The authors propose a direct surrogate for the top-k zero-one loss by introducing a convex “top-k hinge” loss that averages the top-k class margins, yielding a tighter upper bound on top-k error than the standard multiclass SVM hinge (k=1) and than the ordered, weighted family Lβ of Usunier et al. The core idea is to learn with respect to top-k loss rather than top-1 accuracy.

Key technical contributions include:
- The top-k hinge φk and its convex conjugate, which naturally introduces the “top-k simplex” ∆k as the effective domain—an interesting contraction of the standard simplex with adaptive per-coordinate upper bounds.
- A Prox-SDCA formulation for `2-regularized multiclass learning with φk, reducing the per-example dual update to a (biased) projection onto ∆k.
- An efficient algorithm for the (biased) projection onto the top-k simplex (or cone), with worst-case O(d log d + kd) complexity and practical speed competitive with state-of-the-art simplex projection (CQKP) methods when applicable.

The work is positioned relative to: Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM (recovered at k=1); ranking-based surrogates such as Lβ and Wsabie; multivariate performance optimization via SVMPerf/structured SVM; and top-push optimization. Unlike reductions to ranking over m−1 negatives per instance, the proposed surrogate remains instance-decomposable, scalable, and tightly tailored to top-k error.

Strengths
- Material is non-trivial and the paper is well-written. The convex analysis (conjugates, domains), the SDCA dual derivation, and the projection algorithm are technically solid and clearly presented; proofs supplement the main text appropriately.
- The top-k hinge is a principled, tighter upper bound than prior Lβ-style averages of clamped violations; the top-k simplex perspective is elegant and appears novel.
- The optimization scheme is well-motivated, with practical considerations (BLAS operations, rank-1 updates, duality-gap stopping) and a projection routine that seems of independent interest.
- Experimental results are good. Across five image classification datasets (including large-scale), optimizing the top-k hinge reliably improves top-k accuracy over strong baselines (Crammer–Singer, SVMPerf Recall@k, TopPush, Wsabie-like methods). The approach scales to millions of examples and thousands of classes, and the released code increases reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Further motivate the choice of the loss function. While averaging the top-k largest margin violations yields a convex, tighter bound than φ̃k (the Lβ instantiation), a deeper discussion of why this particular averaging is preferable to, e.g., kth-order-statistic hinge, smoothed top-k surrogates, or other convex envelopes would strengthen the case. Geometric intuition (e.g., how the ∆k constraint redistributes probability mass vs the standard simplex), a toy experiment illustrating margin distributions, and guidance on k-dependent behavior would help.
- Theoretical guarantees. The paper would benefit from discussing top-k calibration/Bayes consistency of φk for top-k error (even partial results or conditions), or clarifying why such guarantees are hard.
- Empirical coverage. Many large-scale results are in the supplement; bringing a subset into the main paper would bolster significance. Reporting wall-clock times for training and projection vs baselines (and showing whether projection dominates) would make the scalability claims more concrete. A brief sensitivity analysis for k and for the bias parameter ρ (fixed to 1) would also be useful.
- Baselines. Since the experiments use fixed deep features, a comparison to a cross-entropy softmax classifier trained on the same features would contextualize top-k gains. If out of scope to train end-to-end nets, state this explicitly.
- Clarity. The top-k cone/simplex algorithms are dense; adding a small worked numerical example and a diagram of feasible sets would improve readability. Also, briefly restate in the main text the conditions under which the projection reduces to CQKP.

Quality
- Technically sound with careful derivations, a clear dual/primal connection, and an efficient solver. Claims are supported by experiments across multiple datasets, with consistent top-k improvements. Minor gaps: no formal calibration analysis; limited runtime comparisons in the main text.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Proofs are appropriately deferred. Suggestions above could further improve accessibility. Typo: page 3, “globally optimal leads to” — remove “optimal.”

Originality
- The top-k hinge with its conjugate/top-k simplex and the tailored projection algorithm are original and interesting. The integration into Prox-SDCA and the comparison to Lβ provide a clear differentiation from prior ranking-based approaches and from Crammer–Singer.

Significance
- Optimizing top-k accuracy matters in high-class-count vision tasks. The method is scalable and shows consistent empirical gains; the projection routine may be useful beyond this specific model. Likely to be adopted or built upon by practitioners.

Specific questions and minor comments
- Please provide more intuition for why φk is a better surrogate than φ̃k beyond tightness, and summarize empirical differences directly in the main text.
- Clarify complexity constants for the projection and quantify its fraction of total runtime.
- Briefly discuss kernelization beyond the identity W=XAᵀ; a small kernel experiment or removing the claim would avoid overpromising.
- Notational nits: ensure consistency of indices and sets U, M, L; unify references and years.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important metric (top-k) with a principled, convex, and tighter surrogate.
- Non-trivial technical contributions (top-k simplex, projection algorithm) with clean optimization via Prox-SDCA.
- Strong, consistent empirical improvements and demonstrated scalability.
- Clear writing and released code.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees regarding top-k calibration/consistency.
- Some empirical details (runtimes, large-scale results) relegated to the supplement; limited baselines with learned softmax.
- The motivation for choosing φk over alternative surrogates could be stronger.

Overall recommendation
A nice paper describing an interesting and practically important problem, with solid technical depth and good experimental results. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening the motivation for the specific loss and addressing the minor clarity and empirical reporting suggestions.
