This paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM formulation, generalizing the standard multiclass SVM to optimize directly for top-k accuracy. The core idea is to use a convex upper bound – the top-k hinge loss – on the top-k zero-one loss, specifically the sum of the k largest score components. An efficient optimization algorithm based on proximal stochastic dual coordinate ascent (Prox-SDCA) is presented, along with a detailed analysis of the resulting convex conjugates and projections. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation and technical details. Experiments on five datasets demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy compared to various baselines.

The work builds upon the established foundation of multiclass SVMs (Crammer & Singer, 2006 [5]) and leverages recent advances in optimization techniques like Prox-SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2013 [24]).  It connects to the broader field of ranking-based losses (Usunier et al., 2013 [27]) by demonstrating a relationship between the proposed top-k hinge loss and a general framework for ranking. The authors also acknowledge the connection to related work in recall@k optimization (Joachims, 2005 [11]) and structured SVMs.  Compared to prior NIPS work on ranking and multiclass learning (e.g., Bordes et al., 2005 [1], Gupta & Bengio, 2006 [9]), this work provides a specific and efficient convex relaxation tailored for top-k classification, offering a direct optimization approach rather than approximations or reductions to binary problems.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a technically sound formulation with a rigorous derivation of the top-k hinge loss and its convex conjugate. The efficient projection algorithm for the top-k simplex is a valuable contribution in itself.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, technical details, and experimental setup.  The notation is consistent, and the explanations are concise yet comprehensive.
* **Originality:**  While building on existing foundations, the top-k hinge loss and its optimization offer a novel approach to top-k classification. The analysis of the top-k simplex and the efficient projection algorithm contribute to the originality.
* **Significance:** Optimizing directly for top-k accuracy is practically relevant in many applications, particularly image classification with a large number of classes where ambiguity is common.  The consistent improvements in top-k accuracy demonstrated in the experiments suggest that this work could be influential. The scalability to large datasets is also a significant strength.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Comparison to Specific Baselines:** The authors acknowledge the lack of comparisons to methods from [25, 8] due to unavailable implementations.  This limits the completeness of the evaluation. While understandable, it would strengthen the paper if even an attempt to obtain implementations, or a more detailed justification for their absence, was provided.
* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors establish the convexity of the proposed loss, a deeper theoretical analysis of the approximation quality of the top-k hinge loss compared to the actual top-k zero-one loss would be valuable.  Specifically, quantifying the gap between the upper bound and the true loss could provide further insights.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to top-k multiclass classification.
* The proposed top-k hinge loss and optimization algorithm are efficient and scalable.
* The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across multiple datasets.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the technical details.
* The contribution is relevant and potentially impactful in areas such as image classification and information retrieval.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of comparison to methods from [25, 8] is a minor weakness, though explained by implementation difficulties.
* The theoretical analysis could be expanded to provide a more detailed understanding of the approximation quality of the proposed loss.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multiclass classification. The proposed top-k multiclass SVM is a neat formulation that works well in practice. The clarity of the writing and the thoroughness of the experiments are particularly commendable.  I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors making the code publicly available, as requested by the reviewer. Addressing the missing comparisons from [25,8] if feasible in the future would be a valuable addition.
