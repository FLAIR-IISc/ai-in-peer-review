This paper addresses multiclass recognition with many confusable classes by optimizing top-k accuracy directly. The main contribution is a “top-k multiclass SVM” that replaces the standard multiclass hinge by a top-k hinge loss: φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) sum of the k largest components of a + c}, where c encodes the margin offset relative to the ground-truth class. This is a tight convex upper bound on the discrete top-k error ψk(a); it interpolates between the Crammer–Singer hinge (k=1) and a looser bound as k grows, and is strictly tighter than the ordered-weighted hinge of Usunier et al. for the same k when 0 < (a+c)[k] < (a+c)[1]. The authors characterize the conjugate of the loss and show its effective domain is a novel “top-k simplex” ∆k, a contraction of the standard simplex. Building on this, they derive a clean Fenchel-dual formulation for `2-regularized multiclass learning and propose an efficient Prox-SDCA solver whose core step reduces to a (biased) Euclidean projection onto ∆k(r). They design a fast algorithm for this projection via a decomposition into either a continuous quadratic knapsack subproblem or a projection onto a “top-k cone,” with complexity O(d log d + kd). Experiments on five vision datasets (Caltech-101 silhouettes, MIT Indoor-67, SUN397, Places205, and ILSVRC-2012) demonstrate consistent top-k accuracy gains over strong baselines, including Crammer–Singer, ranking-based methods (SVMPerf for recall@k, TopPush), and WSABIE-type methods.

Relation to prior work: The paper carefully connects to (i) multiclass SVMs (Crammer & Singer) and their dual via Prox-SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang); (ii) ranking-based convex surrogates (Usunier et al.) and LaRank; (iii) structured SVM optimization of recall@k (Joachims), which is theoretically elegant but less scalable; and (iv) large-scale embedding/annotation approaches (WSABIE). The proposed φk is shown to be a tighter convex surrogate of top-k error than the ordered weighted hinge for the critical regime, and the new top-k simplex gives a principled conjugate/dual characterization that is absent in prior ranking losses.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated formulation that directly targets top-k accuracy with a convex, margin-based surrogate; reduces to Crammer–Singer at k=1 and has a natural interpretation for k>1.
- Solid variational analysis: j-compatibility, explicit conjugate, dual with equality constraints 〈1, ai〉=0, and the identification of ∆k as the conjugate’s domain.
- Technically interesting and practically useful projection algorithm onto the top-k simplex/top-k cone; beyond this paper, this may be of independent interest for other top-k objectives.
- Strong, scalable optimization via Prox-SDCA with a principled duality-gap stopping rule; kernelization is also straightforward.
- Consistent empirical gains in top-k metrics on challenging datasets using competitive features; the method scales to million-example settings.

Weaknesses and questions
- Missing comparisons: the paper notes that comparisons to [25, 8] were omitted due to unavailable implementations. Given their potential relevance, it would be helpful to implement simplified or reproducible baselines (e.g., re-implementing the key components, using authors’ reported settings, or contacting authors for evaluation scripts) or to provide a more detailed justification that these methods are orthogonal/noncomparable.
- Baselines and ablations: while both φk and the ordered-weighted variant φ̃k are discussed, the main text would benefit from a more systematic ablation comparing them across k and datasets, quantifying the tightness-vs-performance tradeoff and runtime differences in practice.
- Runtime/memory reporting: the projection algorithm is central to scalability. Please include per-epoch and wall-clock comparisons against standard simplex projection and competing learners (e.g., SGD on φ̃k, SVMPerf) on at least one large dataset, and report memory footprints (especially for large m).
- Statistical significance and calibration: top-k improvements are reported, but significance tests or confidence intervals would help. Also discuss how k chosen at training affects performance across other k at test time (e.g., train with k=10, evaluate k∈{1,5,10}).
- Effect on top-1 and large-k regimes: the paper observes occasional top-1 degradation. Please analyze when and why this happens, and include results for large k (e.g., k≈m/2) to illustrate the behavior as the loss averages over many classes.
- Scope of models: results are primarily for linear models with fixed CNN features. A short discussion on integrating φk into end-to-end deep training (e.g., replacing softmax cross-entropy with top-k hinge) and expected challenges would broaden impact.

Quality
- The theory is sound, with clear primal-dual derivations and well-justified algorithmic choices. Proof sketches are convincing; full proofs in the supplement are appropriate. The projection method is carefully designed and appears correct; corner cases (e.g., Lemma 4’s sufficiency when ρ>0) are acknowledged.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The geometric intuition for ∆k and the role of the upper bound xi ≤ (1/k)〈1, x〉 are helpful. Consider adding pseudo-code for the projection routine and a small worked example to further improve readability.

Originality
- The top-k hinge with its conjugate characterized by the top-k simplex, together with an efficient projection algorithm, are original and nontrivial. The tightness result versus ordered-weighted hinge enhances the novelty.

Significance
- Optimizing top-k is highly relevant in large-scale multiclass recognition. The method is practical, scalable, and shows consistent gains; the projection technique may influence related optimization problems.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add or discuss comparisons to [25, 8], or provide stronger rationale for omission.
- Expand ablations (φk vs φ̃k, effect of k, sensitivity to λ).
- Report detailed runtime/memory and duality-gap convergence plots.
- Release code upon acceptance to facilitate reproduction and follow-up work.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important metric directly with a principled, convex surrogate.
- Technically solid, with a novel optimization component of independent interest.
- Demonstrated empirical gains at scale.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing comparisons to some relevant methods.
- Limited ablations and runtime analysis in the main text.
- Primarily linear models; limited discussion of end-to-end settings.

Overall: a neat and well-executed formulation of top-k multiclass SVM with a convincing optimization scheme and meaningful empirical improvements. Pending stronger experimental comparisons and public code, this is a strong candidate for acceptance.
