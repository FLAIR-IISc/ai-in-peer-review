This paper introduces a novel approach to multiclass Support Vector Machines (SVMs) by directly optimizing for top-k performance, motivated by the common issue of class ambiguity in high-class-count image classification problems. The authors propose a top-k multiclass SVM formulation based on a tight convex upper bound of the top-k error, along with an efficient optimization scheme centered around projection onto a novel “top-k simplex.”  The work builds upon the existing multiclass SVM framework of Crammer and Singer [5] and related work on ranking losses [27], offering a direct method for optimizing the top-k error which, to the best of the authors’ knowledge, hasn’t been addressed directly before. The presented results show consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across five datasets.

The paper is technically sound and well-written. The derivation of the top-k hinge loss and its associated convex conjugate is clearly presented, and the introduction of the top-k simplex is a significant contribution.  The authors convincingly argue for the utility of optimizing the top-k error in scenarios where class discrimination is difficult, and the presented experiments demonstrate the practical benefits of their approach. The optimization framework, leveraging Prox-SDCA [24], is appropriate and the discussion of efficient projection onto the top-k simplex is a key element of the work. The inclusion of a C++ library release further strengthens the contribution and facilitates reproducibility.

A strength of this work lies in its comprehensive treatment of the mathematical foundations. The authors carefully define the problem, propose a convex relaxation, and derive efficient optimization strategies.  The connection to existing work on ranking losses and the insightful discussion of the relationship between different top-k loss formulations (3) and (5) demonstrate a strong understanding of the field. The extensive experimental validation on five datasets, including large-scale datasets like Places 205 and ImageNet 2012 (results in the supplement), adds to the credibility of the findings.  The method shows consistent gains in top-k accuracy, and the authors acknowledge potential trade-offs with top-1 accuracy, presenting a balanced view.

However, a potential weakness lies in the initial motivation for choosing the specific convex upper bound for the top-k error. While the authors acknowledge that the function ψk is non-convex, the rationale behind selecting φk (3) over the alternative φ̃k (5) could be further strengthened. A more detailed discussion on the theoretical properties of each upper bound, and perhaps some ablation studies comparing their performance, would enhance the paper. While the authors briefly mention the relationship to Usunier et al. [27], a slightly more in-depth comparison, detailing the specific advantages of the proposed approach in different scenarios, would be beneficial.  

Additionally, there is a minor typo on page 3: "globally optimal leads to" should be “globally leads to” or “optimal leads to.” Although minor, such details can detract from the overall polish of the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The direct optimization of top-k error in multiclass SVMs is a novel contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations are rigorous and the optimization framework is well-justified.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy.
*   **Practicality:** The release of a C++ library makes the method readily accessible to the research community.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Motivation:** The justification for the choice of the specific convex upper bound could be strengthened.
*   **Minor Typo:** The typo on page 3 should be corrected.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper presenting an interesting and potentially impactful contribution to the field of multiclass SVMs. The authors address a relevant problem, propose a technically sound solution, and validate their approach with comprehensive experiments.  Despite the minor suggestions for improvement, I believe this paper is of high quality and deserves acceptance to NIPS 2015. The core idea of learning directly with respect to the top-k loss is compelling, and the work is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on image classification and related tasks.
