This paper proposes a novel method, the top-k multiclass SVM, for directly optimizing the top-k classification error, a metric widely used in large-scale recognition tasks. The core idea is to learn a classifier that is penalized only if the true class is not within its top k predictions. Standard top-k error is a non-convex objective. The authors' main contribution is to propose a tight, convex upper bound for this error, which they term the "top-k hinge loss". This loss function is a generalization of the standard multiclass SVM loss by Crammer and Singer (NIPS 2000), which is recovered as the special case k=1.

The theoretical analysis reveals that the convex conjugate of this new loss function is defined on a novel convex polytope, which the authors aptly name the "top-k simplex". This is an interesting geometric insight and a natural extension of the standard simplex that arises in the dual of the multiclass SVM. To optimize the resulting objective, the authors employ the Proximal Stochastic Dual Coordinate Ascent (Prox-SDCA) framework. This requires an efficient method to project onto the top-k simplex, which is a non-trivial problem as existing algorithms for simplex projection are not applicable. The authors derive a novel and efficient projection algorithm, which is a significant technical contribution in its own right. The proposed method is compared to several baselines on five image classification datasets, including large-scale ones like Places 205 and ImageNet, showing consistent improvements in top-k accuracy.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of directly optimizing for top-k performance is highly relevant, especially for modern classification problems with thousands of classes where class ambiguity is inherent and top-k accuracy is the standard evaluation metric. Previous approaches often optimize a surrogate loss (like one-vs-all) or use more complex structured prediction frameworks that may not scale well. This paper presents a direct, elegant, and scalable formulation. The theoretical development, including the introduction of the top-k hinge loss and the characterization of its dual domain as the "top-k simplex," is novel and insightful.

2.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The authors provide a solid theoretical justification for their proposed loss function, proving it is a convex upper bound on the target top-k error. The derivation of the dual problem and the development of an efficient projection algorithm for the top-k simplex are non-trivial and well-executed. Claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on multiple standard benchmarks, demonstrating both the scalability and effectiveness of the proposed approach.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly, building from the standard multiclass SVM to the proposed top-k formulation. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the theoretical results are presented in a logical and understandable manner. The connection to related work, particularly the general ranking losses of Usunier et al. (ICML 2009), is clearly explained, highlighting why the proposed loss function is a tighter bound.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  The primary suggestion for improvement is to further motivate the specific choice of the top-k hinge loss function, `φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) * sum_{j=1 to k} (a+c)[j]}`. While the paper correctly shows that it is a convex upper bound on the non-convex top-k loss `ψk(a) = max{0, (a+c)[k]}`, a deeper intuitive discussion of this choice would be beneficial. For example, why is averaging the top-k incorrect scores the "right" relaxation? This loss penalizes a setting where multiple incorrect classes receive high scores more heavily than a setting where only the k-th score is high. Discussing the implications of this modeling choice and whether other convex relaxations were considered would strengthen the paper's rationale.

2.  There is a minor typo on page 3, second paragraph of section 2.2: "Therefore, finding a globally optimal leads to" should likely be "Therefore, finding a global optimum is computationally intractable" or similar. Please correct this.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a practical and important problem in large-scale classification.
*   Presents a novel and elegant convex formulation for directly optimizing top-k error.
*   Contains significant technical contributions, including the derivation of the "top-k simplex" and an efficient projection algorithm onto it.
*   Demonstrates strong, consistent empirical improvements over competitive baselines on several datasets.
*   The paper is very well-written and clear.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The motivation for the specific form of the proposed loss function could be elaborated upon more thoroughly. However, this is a minor point and does not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale classification. The combination of novel theory, a non-trivial algorithmic contribution, and strong empirical results makes it an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
