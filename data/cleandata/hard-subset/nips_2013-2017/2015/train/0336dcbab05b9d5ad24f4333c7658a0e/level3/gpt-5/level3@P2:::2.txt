Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses multiclass classification when evaluation is based on top-k accuracy, a common setting in large-scale image recognition with many semantically similar classes. The authors propose a top-k multiclass SVM that directly optimizes a convex surrogate tailored to top-k performance. Starting from the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM [5], they define a top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑j=1..k (a+c)[j]} that upper-bounds the nonconvex top-k error. They derive the Fenchel conjugate of this loss and show its effective domain is a “top-k simplex” ∆k with a coupling upper bound xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1, x⟩, which generalizes the standard simplex (k=1). They also discuss a related loss φ̃k connected to the ranking framework of Usunier et al. [27], arguing their φk is a tighter bound to the discrete top-k loss.

On the optimization side, they integrate the loss into Prox-SDCA [24], yielding an efficient algorithm with a principled duality-gap stopping criterion. A core technical contribution is a fast algorithm for (biased) Euclidean projection onto ∆k(r), which differs nontrivially from classical continuous quadratic knapsack (CQKP) projections due to the coupling upper bound and an optional bias term. The projection either reduces to a CQKP when the sum constraint is tight or to a new “top-k cone” projection otherwise; the authors provide closed-form characterizations and an O(d log d + kd) procedure. Experiments on five image datasets (Caltech-101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor-67, SUN397, Places205, ImageNet2012) using deep features show consistent top-k accuracy improvements over baselines (one-vs-all SVM, Crammer–Singer, SVMPerf for Recall@k [11], TopPush [16], Wsabie-like [9]). The method scales to million-example datasets and the code is released.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and problem relevance: top-k metrics are standard in large-scale recognition, yet most methods optimize top-1 surrogates.
- Principled loss: φk is a tight convex upper bound to the top-k zero-one loss (tighter than φ̃k in a nontrivial regime). The conjugate and its domain (top-k simplex) are elegant and connect cleanly back to Crammer–Singer at k=1.
- Optimization and scalability: sound Prox-SDCA formulation with per-example block updates, duality-gap stopping, and a novel projection algorithm of independent interest.
- Empirical results: consistent gains in top-k across datasets and values of k, with competitive scalability to very large datasets. Comparisons include ranking-based surrogates (SVMPerf Recall@k, TopPush) and Wsabie-like baselines.
- Clarity: the paper is well written; derivations and algorithmic choices are explained; connections to prior work are discussed.

Weaknesses
- Theory beyond convexity: no analysis of Bayes consistency or calibration of φk for top-k error; no generalization bounds tailored to the top-k structure (beyond standard convex Lipschitz arguments).
- Memory/implementation details: Prox-SDCA for multiclass typically stores per-example dual variables ai ∈ Rm. For m up to 1000 and n in the millions, this is prohibitive unless ai are sparse or not stored. The paper maintains W and ai but does not fully explain memory footprint, sparsity of ai, or any tricks to avoid storing A.
- Baselines: since features come from pretrained CNNs, a strong multinomial logistic regression (softmax) baseline (with L2 regularization) on the same features would be informative; end-to-end fine-tuned baselines (where feasible) would strengthen the empirical case.
- Computational characterization: while the projection algorithm is well motivated, practical constants, frequency of the “general” case, and sensitivity to k are not reported in the main text (relegated to supplement). Wall-clock comparisons vs. strong linear baselines would help.
- Trade-offs: top-1 sometimes degrades when optimizing top-k; more systematic analysis of the trade-off as k varies (including training with ktrain ≠ keval) would be useful.

Quality
- Technically sound: the convex relaxation, conjugate, and dual are derived carefully; the projection algorithm is well justified. The optimization framework (Prox-SDCA) is appropriate and has convergence guarantees.
- Empirical support: multi-dataset evaluation shows improvements in top-k and scalability; however, additional runtime/memory details and stronger baselines would further bolster claims.

Clarity
- Generally very clear and easy to follow, with appropriate notation and motivation. Minor typos are acknowledged and a correction to [24] is carefully explained. It would help to summarize the projection algorithm’s practical behavior (average iterations, time per call) in the main paper.

Originality
- Introducing the top-k hinge φk with its conjugate domain (top-k simplex) and the associated projection algorithm is novel and nontrivial. The relation to ranking-based losses is well contextualized; the proposed bound can be strictly tighter.

Significance
- High practical significance: top-k metrics are ubiquitous in large-scale multiclass tasks. The method is likely to be adopted, and the projection technique may be reused elsewhere. The improvements in top-k are consistent; on larger datasets, even top-1 sometimes benefits.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Memory/scalability: How are the dual variables stored for large n,m? Are ai sparse in practice post-projection? Can the algorithm be implemented with only W and a small working set, avoiding storing A?
- Runtime: Please report average wall-clock training times and projection call statistics across datasets and k. How does runtime scale with k?
- Choice of k: How is k selected for training relative to the evaluation k (e.g., training with k=10 to improve top-5)? Any guidance on tuning ktrain?
- Theory: Is φk calibrated for top-k error? Any negative results? Can you provide margin bounds or data-dependent generalization guarantees tailored to φk?
- Baselines: Please add a multinomial logistic regression (softmax) linear classifier on the same features and, where feasible, a fine-tuned softmax baseline (even on a subset) to contextualize the gains.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem (top-k optimization) with a principled, convex approach.
- Novel “top-k simplex” and efficient projection algorithm of independent interest.
- Strong, scalable optimization via Prox-SDCA with a duality-gap stopping criterion.
- Consistent empirical improvements on multiple datasets; code release improves impact.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees regarding top-k calibration/consistency.
- Incomplete clarity on memory usage and scalability details for very large n,m.
- Missing some strong baselines (softmax linear/fine-tuned) to contextualize gains.
- Top-1 degradation in some settings warrants more analysis of the trade-off.

Overall recommendation
A strong paper with a clean, original formulation and practical impact. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying scalability details and strengthening empirical comparisons.
