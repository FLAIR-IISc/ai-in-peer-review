

This paper proposes an extension of the multiclass SVM to optimize for top-k classification performance, which is particularly relevant for image classification with many classes where class ambiguity is common. The authors define a top-k loss where the prediction is considered correct if the true class is among the top-k predicted classes, and propose a tight convex upper bound of this loss function. This extends the Crammer and Singer multiclass SVM [5] by introducing a top-k hinge loss that can be optimized efficiently via Prox-SDCA with a novel projection onto the top-k simplex. The work makes a clear technical contribution by deriving the convex conjugate of the top-k hinge loss and providing an efficient algorithm for the required projection.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with careful attention to the mathematical foundations. The authors effectively relate their work to previous research, particularly noting the connection to Usunier et al.'s [27] general family of convex losses for ranking and multiclass classification, and showing how their top-k hinge loss provides a tighter convex upper bound than the alternative formulation (5) considered in [27]. The theoretical analysis is complete, with clear propositions and proofs for the conjugate functions and optimization framework.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates strong empirical performance across five datasets of varying scale and complexity (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN 397, Places 205, and ImageNet 2012). The proposed top-k SVM consistently outperforms standard multiclass SVM (top-1) and other baselines in top-k accuracy, with improvements of 1.2-2.6% in top-5 accuracy. Notably, the method scales well to large datasets with millions of examples, which is a significant practical contribution. The authors also provide a clear comparison to ranking-based approaches (SVMPerf, TopPush), showing that their method is both theoretically sound and practically efficient.

The main strength of this work is its elegant extension of multiclass SVM to the top-k setting with a theoretically grounded approach. The efficient optimization scheme using projection onto the top-k simplex is a valuable technical contribution in its own right. The paper demonstrates significant improvements in top-k accuracy across diverse datasets, which is particularly compelling for large-scale image classification problems where class ambiguity is common.

There are a few areas for improvement. The motivation for choosing the specific convex upper bound (3) over other possible formulations could be strengthened. While the authors note that their formulation is a tighter upper bound than (5), they could provide more intuition about why this particular form is particularly well-suited for the top-k setting. A minor typo appears on page 3: "globally optimal leads to" should read "globally optimal solution leads to" or similar. The authors could also briefly discuss the practical implications of their method's performance on top-1 accuracy, as they note that top-k optimization sometimes leads to improved top-1 performance on larger datasets.

The paper addresses all four key criteria effectively:
- Quality: The technical analysis is sound, with well-supported claims and careful consideration of both strengths and weaknesses.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- Originality: The top-k SVM formulation is a novel extension of the standard multiclass SVM, with clear distinctions from previous work.
- Significance: The approach directly addresses a practical problem in image classification with many classes, and the consistent improvements across datasets demonstrate clear utility.

The work is clearly within NIPS scope as it addresses a fundamental machine learning problem with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The top-k loss formulation and efficient optimization scheme are likely to be valuable to researchers and practitioners working on large-scale classification problems.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel extension of multiclass SVM to top-k performance with a tight convex upper bound
- Theoretical contributions including derivation of convex conjugate and top-k simplex
- Efficient optimization scheme with practical scalability to large datasets
- Consistent experimental improvements across five diverse datasets
- Well-written and technically sound paper

Arguments against acceptance:
- Motivation for the specific loss function choice could be strengthened
- Minor typo in the manuscript
- Limited discussion of trade-offs between top-k and top-1 performance

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of multiclass classification. The top-k SVM formulation is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, with the efficient optimization scheme addressing a key practical challenge. The paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the literature on classification performance metrics. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the motivation and typo.
