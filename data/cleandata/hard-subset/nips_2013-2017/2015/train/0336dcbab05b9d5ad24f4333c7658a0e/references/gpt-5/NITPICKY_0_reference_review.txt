Summary
The paper proposes a convex surrogate for top-k multiclass classification. Starting from the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM loss, the authors define a top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) sum of the k largest components of (a + c)}, show ψk ≤ φk ≤ φ (with ψk the nonconvex top-k zero-one surrogate), and derive a primal–dual characterization via Fenchel conjugates. A key construct is the “top-k simplex” Δk(r) = {x | ⟨1,x⟩ ≤ r, 0 ≤ xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩}, which becomes the effective domain of the conjugate. They integrate the loss into an ℓ2-regularized framework and optimize via Prox-SDCA; the dual update reduces to (biased) projection onto Δk(r). They propose an algorithm for projection onto the “top-k cone/simplex” with complexity O(d log d + kd) based on case analysis and an exhaustive search over partitions. Experiments on several image datasets with CNN features show modest gains in top-k accuracy compared to Crammer–Singer and ranking-based baselines. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM and Prox-SDCA [Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang]. It relates to ranking-based convex losses (Usunier et al.) and structured SVM for multivariate measures (Joachims). The paper contrasts its instance-decomposable loss with non-decomposable structured surrogates and discusses one-vs-all/binary reductions. The “sum of k-largest” convexity is standard.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean convex upper bound for top-k error that interpolates between zero-one@k and multiclass hinge.
- Neat dual characterization: φk(a) = max⟨a+c, λ⟩ over Δk and conjugate as indicator of Δk minus a linear term.
- Integrates naturally with Prox-SDCA, including a dual block update with a principled stopping criterion (duality gap).
- Practical algorithmic contribution: projection onto Δk(r) (and cone variant), with complexity comparable to simplex projection.
- Empirical evidence of consistent improvements on top-k metrics across several datasets; scalability claims to large datasets; code release.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Many key proofs are deferred; in the main paper several core steps are asserted without even high-level proof sketches. In particular: Proposition 2 (conjugate and Δk domain), Lemma 3/4/5 (projection characterization) underpin both theory and algorithm.
- Notational inconsistencies and overloading hinder verification:
  - d denotes feature dimension earlier, but later x ∈ R^d is used for the projection dimension, which in context is the number of classes m.
  - Reuse of λ for both regularization parameter and KKT multiplier in Lemma 5 is confusing.
  - In Proposition 4, the mapping between ai, a\yi, x, and the construction of b and q is hard to parse; dimensions and indices are unclear. Please spell out shapes and which coordinates are omitted.
- Algorithm 1 omits the stopping threshold (“until relative duality gap is below …”) and lacks details of update(k, λ, ||xi||^2, yi, si, ai); the guarantee that the inner update solves the dual subproblem to optimality hinges on the projection algorithm, which is only sketched.
- The exhaustive search over partitions U, M, L for the projection has worst-case O(kd) iterations; feasibility checking requires sums over sets—use of prefix sums is not described. Without careful implementation, this may be superlinear per update. A complexity proof (amortized/worst-case) and empirical runtime breakdown would be valuable.
- The claim “( m choose k ) + 1 vertices” for Δk(1) is stated in Fig. 2 but no proof or reference is provided; it is nontrivial due to the coupling xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩.
- Statistical properties are absent: no discussion of classification calibration/Fisher consistency for top-k, Lipschitz constants, or generalization bounds for φk versus φ̃k. This limits theoretical impact relative to ranking-loss literature.
- Kernelization is mentioned but no experiments or memory/runtime discussion for W = XA^T in kernel space are provided.

Clarity
- Numerous formatting/typographical issues impede readability: section numbering (.2), missing words/garbled sentences, inconsistent notation for [j] as order-statistic index, and ambiguous uses of 1 (all-ones vector vs. index). Examples:
  - “let c , 1−eyi” (should be e_{yi}); “Note that thresholding with 0 in φ(a) is actually redundant as (a+ c)[1] ≥ (a+ c)yi = 0” (define [1] clearly).
  - Algorithm 1: missing stopping parameter; comments inconsistent.
  - Lemma 5: λ overloaded; define p, pk consistently.
  - Several references are malformed (e.g., “and L, O. Russakovsky, …”; many missing years/pages).
- The exposition of the dual (Theorem 1) introduces “yi-compatible” and Hj without intuition; a brief explanation of why j-compatibility is needed for enforcing the y_i coordinate being zero would help.
- The paper frequently states that details are in the supplement; critical pieces (e.g., large-scale results, projection runtime comparisons) should be in the main text.

Originality
- The top-k hinge φk based on the sum of k largest components is a natural convex surrogate; the dual formulation via Δk and the associated projection algorithm appear novel. The comparison to φ̃k (weighted ordered hinge) is appropriate and shows a strictly tighter bound in some regimes.
- However, the reduction to projection onto a structured polytope is in line with known simplex projection/CQKP literature; the contribution is an adaptation to a coupled upper bound. A more thorough positioning versus existing knapsack/projection algorithms (beyond “not applicable”) with formal reductions would strengthen novelty.

Significance
- Empirical improvements on top-k accuracy are modest (1–3% absolute) and some datasets show decreased top-1. Statistical significance, variance over splits, and sensitivity to k and λ are not reported.
- Fairness of comparisons: for methods that do not scale under the binary reduction, the paper switches to one-vs-all variants; this changes the problem and may disadvantage baselines. Also, comparing top-10-trained models evaluated at top-5 should be complemented by models trained explicitly for k=5.
- Key scalability and runtime comparisons are deferred to the supplement; without main-text evidence, it is difficult to assess practical impact.

Pros
- Clear motivation for top-k objectives in multiclass settings.
- Convex, tighter surrogate than ordered-hinge baseline; dual domain Δk is elegant.
- Integration with Prox-SDCA and code release.
- Broad empirical coverage of image datasets; shows consistent top-k gains.

Cons
- Many notational/formatting errors; unclear derivations; reliance on supplement for proofs and experiments.
- Projection algorithm lacks complete complexity guarantees and implementation details.
- Limited statistical analysis; calibration/generalization properties not addressed.
- Experimental reporting omits variance and comprehensive fairness checks; some comparisons may be mismatched.

Questions for the authors
- Provide a complete complexity analysis for the projection (including prefix sums/data structures) and per-iteration runtime vs. standard simplex projection.
- Clarify Proposition 4 with explicit dimensions and a step-by-step derivation.
- Are φk losses top-k calibrated? Any bounds relating φk-risk to top-k error?
- Report sensitivity to k (train vs. test), λ, and stopping tolerance; include standard deviations.
- Fix notation overload (d, λ) and unify [j] indexing; correct and complete references.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The core idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the submission, as written, has too many clarity/technical presentation issues and defers too much to the supplement. With substantial revision (cleaned notation, in-paper proofs/algorithms, stronger/clearer experiments), this could be a strong contribution.

Confidence: High.
