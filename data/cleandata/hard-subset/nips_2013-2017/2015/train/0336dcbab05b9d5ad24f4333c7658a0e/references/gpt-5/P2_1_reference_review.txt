Summary
The paper proposes a convex, multiclass top-k surrogate loss and an efficient optimization framework for learning linear classifiers that directly target top-k accuracy. The main ideas are:
- A new convex top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max(0, (1/k) sum of the k largest components of a+c), which upper-bounds the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss ψk and is tighter than the “sum of top-k hinges” loss φ̃k from ranking-based surrogates [27] except in edge cases.
- A conjugate analysis showing that the Fenchel conjugate of φk is the indicator of a novel “top-k simplex” ∆k, defined by sum and per-coordinate constraints xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1, x⟩. This recovers the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM when k=1 and provides a principled dual domain for k>1.
- A Prox-SDCA solver specialized to this loss. The key dual update reduces to a (biased) projection onto ∆k(r), which the authors analyze via new lemmas that handle two regimes: when the sum constraint is tight (reduces to a CQKP instance) and when it is slack (projection onto a top-k cone). They give an algorithm with O(d log d + k d) complexity per update and argue scalability.
- Experiments on five image datasets (including large-scale Places 205 and ILSVRC 2012) with CNN features show consistent improvements in top-k accuracy versus multiclass SVM (k=1) and competitive ranking baselines (SVMPerf [11], TopPush [16], Wsabie-like [9]). Code is released.

Relation to prior work: The approach bridges multiclass SVMs [5], ranking-based convex surrogates with ordered weights [27], structured SVMs for recall@k [11], and optimization with Prox-SDCA [24]. The sum-of-top-k function is known to be convex [3], but its use as a multiclass loss with a clean conjugate and the introduction of the top-k simplex ∆k appear to be new. Unlike [11], the loss is decomposable per instance, enabling scalable linear methods.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is well-motivated; φk is a valid convex upper bound with clear inequalities ψk ≤ φk ≤ φ̃k ≤ φ (and conditions for equality). The conjugacy result (Proposition 2) and the yi-compatibility condition used to derive a clean Fenchel dual (Theorem 1) are plausible and consistent with the k=1 case. The dual update reduces to a projection onto ∆k(r); the analysis separates tight-sum (knapsack-like) and slack-sum (cone) cases and gives constructive conditions (Lemmas 3–5) to recover thresholds and active sets. The optimization via Prox-SDCA, with joint updates over m dual variables for a single example, is appropriate and has convergence guarantees under the framework of [24].
- Empirics: The experiments support the main claims: optimizing φk generally improves top-k metrics (e.g., top-5) and sometimes even top-1 on larger datasets. Comparisons include multiclass SVM, ranking surrogates (via reduction), and Wsabie-like methods. Scalability to millions of examples is claimed and partially evidenced (some results in the supplement).
- Caveats: Many proofs are deferred to the supplement; key proof sketches (e.g., Proposition 2 and Lemma 5) would strengthen the paper. The projection algorithm includes an exhaustive search over feasible partitions in the general case; although bounded and typically fast with sorting, worst-case complexity and practical behavior could be characterized more thoroughly. The statement “for the biased projection, we leave x=0 as the fallback case” is ambiguous—please clarify that this is only used when the sufficient test certifies the zero solution, not as a heuristic that could violate optimality. Finally, there is no consistency or calibration analysis for top-k (i.e., whether φk is top-k Bayes consistent), which would deepen the theoretical contribution.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized: motivation, loss construction, duality, optimization, projections, and experiments flow logically. Definitions (∆k(r), k-th order statistics) and notation are mostly clear, and the connection to k=1 (Crammer–Singer) is explicit.
- Minor issues: There are some typographical/formatting glitches (section numbering, symbols like 1y 6=yi), and occasional overloaded notation (y as class index and vector). The discussion of the error in [24] is helpful but could be moved to the supplement, with a concise main-text fix. Including a small, self-contained proof sketch for Proposition 2 would aid readability.

Originality
- The top-k simplex and the conjugate of the top-k hinge in the multiclass setting are novel and insightful. The identification of dual updates with projections on ∆k(r) and the tailored projection algorithm for the top-k cone/simplex constitute a substantive technical contribution beyond straightforward application of existing CQKP solvers. The comparison to φ̃k [27] and the argument that φk is a tighter surrogate are also new.

Significance
- Practical impact: Top-k performance is a central metric in large-class problems. A decomposable, convex, and scalable surrogate is valuable, and the proposed solver is practical (code released).
- Theoretical impact: The top-k simplex and conjugacy may be of independent interest in convex analysis and optimization. The projection algorithm might be reused in other contexts where sum-of-top-k constraints arise.
- Limitations: Lack of theoretical guarantees on top-k consistency; empirical comparisons to softmax/logistic multiclass with top-k evaluation (a strong baseline in deep feature settings) would provide a more complete picture. Also, end-to-end deep training is out of scope, but discussion of how φk could be used with SGD would broaden applicability.

Pros
- Clear, principled convex surrogate for top-k with a tightness argument.
- Novel conjugacy and the top-k simplex; well-integrated dual optimization via Prox-SDCA.
- Practical projection algorithms; code release; demonstrated scalability and consistent empirical gains on diverse datasets.

Cons
- Key proofs deferred; some algorithmic details (biased projection corner cases, worst-case runtime) need clarification.
- No analysis of statistical consistency for top-k.
- Empirical baselines could include multinomial logistic regression/softmax; some large-scale results relegated to the supplement.

Suggestions and questions
- Clarify the “fallback” in biased projection; ensure correctness is never sacrificed.
- Provide a brief proof sketch for Proposition 2 in the main text; add complexity bounds and empirical profiling for the projection step.
- Discuss (even briefly) conditions under which φk is calibrated for top-k, or cite related work on surrogate calibration.
- Include multinomial logistic regression baseline (linear softmax) and report wall-clock/time-to-ε for large datasets in the main paper.

Recommendation: Accept (strong). The paper makes a solid, well-motivated contribution with both theoretical and practical value. While some details can be tightened, the work is technically sound, original, clearly presented, and likely to be used by practitioners targeting top-k performance.
