Summary
The paper proposes a convex surrogate for top-k multiclass classification by introducing a “top-k hinge” loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) ∑j=1..k (a+c)[j]}. This yields a tighter upper bound on the top-k 0-1 loss than a simple average of k hinge losses (following Usunier et al.). The authors derive the Fenchel conjugate and show the effective domain is a “top-k simplex” ∆k, a contraction of the standard simplex with per-coordinate upper bounds coupled to the total mass. They then formulate an ℓ2-regularized dual via Fenchel duality and optimize it with Prox-SDCA. The key computation reduces to a (biased) projection onto ∆k(r), for which they present an algorithm with O(d log d + kd) time (d ≈ number of classes). Experiments on several image datasets report consistent gains in top-k accuracy versus Crammer–Singer and some ranking-based baselines; code is released.

Strengths
- Clear convex formulation connecting top-k performance to a Ky Fan–type surrogate; the conjugate and the top-k simplex are technically interesting.
- Integration into Prox-SDCA is principled; the dual update reduces to a structured projection problem with an explicit algorithm.
- Practical relevance: top-k metrics are important in large-class regimes; the method appears to scale (claims of million-scale datasets) and code is provided.
- Empirical results indicate top-k accuracy improvements over Crammer–Singer and some ranking baselines on multiple datasets.

Weaknesses
- Missing strong baselines: multinomial logistic regression (softmax) with cross-entropy is a standard and often superior multiclass baseline, especially for top-k; it is not reported. For large-scale image datasets, end-to-end or at least linear softmax on the same features is essential for a fair assessment.
- Experimental evidence is incomplete in the main paper. Large-scale results are deferred to the supplement; statistical significance, variability, training time, and memory are not reported. The observed gains are modest and sometimes come with top-1 degradation; there is limited analysis of trade-offs.
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond convexity and conjugate derivations. No discussion of statistical consistency with respect to top-k risk, calibration properties, or generalization bounds. The claim of a “tighter” convex upper bound would benefit from quantification and conditions under which this translates into improved risk.
- The projection algorithm relies on sorting and (in the general case) exhaustive search over partitions; worst-case complexity and practical constants are not deeply evaluated against alternative projections (e.g., projections related to permutahedra, ordered weighted ℓ1, SLOPE, k-support norms). Related proximal/projection literature is not adequately discussed.
- Some clarity issues: several proofs are deferred; notation is dense; references are incomplete; there are minor typos. The practical role of the bias parameter ρ, and the implications of Lemma 4 being only a sufficient condition (fallback to zero) need clarification.

Quality
- The optimization framework is sound, and the dual derivation appears consistent (the j-compatibility device is reasonable). The conjugate characterization as an indicator over ∆k is plausible and interesting. However, the empirical evaluation lacks essential baselines (softmax) and comprehensive reporting (training time, duality gaps, memory), and some comparisons (e.g., using one-vs-all substitutes when ranking methods do not scale) raise fairness concerns.
- The claim φk ≤ φ̃k and improved tightness is shown qualitatively, but the practical advantage should be demonstrated more convincingly (head-to-head comparison α vs β across datasets, k, and top-k metrics).

Clarity
- Overall readable, but the exposition is heavy and not self-contained without the supplement. The algorithmic presentation of the projection is terse relative to its importance; a more explicit pseudo-code and empirical timing comparisons versus strong projection baselines would help. Bibliography needs fixing; some references lack years or details.

Originality
- Using the average of top-k margins as a convex surrogate for top-k is known conceptually (Ky Fan–type objectives), and connections to Usunier et al. exist. The primary novelty is the conjugate leading to the top-k simplex and the specialized projection algorithm within SDCA. The paper should better situate itself versus related projections/prox for ordered statistics norms and permutahedron-based constraints.

Significance
- If validated with stronger baselines and clearer empirical advantages, the method could be useful for practitioners who optimize top-k metrics with linear models. As is, the demonstrated gains are incremental and not yet convincingly superior to established alternatives (e.g., softmax). The theoretical contribution is modest but neat.

Questions/requests for authors
1) Include multinomial logistic regression (softmax) baselines (linear, same features) across all datasets and report top-k metrics.
2) Provide full large-scale results in the main paper: training time, memory, convergence (duality gap vs epochs), and ablations for k and ρ. Do α and β consistently differ?
3) Analyze sensitivity: how does training with k’ affect evaluation at different k? Are improvements consistent when k’=k?
4) Quantify the “tighter” bound in practice: plot φk vs φ̃k vs true top-k loss on held-out data.
5) Discuss statistical properties: is φk calibrated for top-k error? Any bounds?
6) Compare projection timing to alternatives from related proximal literature (e.g., SLOPE, permutahedron projections).

Pros
- Convex surrogate and dual characterization are elegant.
- Principled integration with SDCA; code release.
- Empirical gains on several datasets.

Cons
- Missing key baselines; incomplete experimental reporting.
- Limited theoretical guarantees about target risk.
- Projection algorithm novelty and efficiency not fully contextualized.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper has promise, but requires stronger empirical validation against established baselines (notably softmax), fuller reporting, and better positioning against related projection/prox work to justify acceptance.

Confidence
High (familiar with multiclass surrogates, SDCA, and top-k metrics).
