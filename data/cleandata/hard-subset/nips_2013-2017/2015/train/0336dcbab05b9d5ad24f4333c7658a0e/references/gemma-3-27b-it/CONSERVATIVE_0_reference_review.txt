This paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM loss and a corresponding optimization framework. The core idea is to relax the penalty for errors if the correct class is within the top-k predictions, aiming for improved performance in scenarios where ranking is crucial. While the motivation is reasonable, and the derivation of the convex upper bound on the loss is mathematically sound, the paper lacks substantial novelty and a compelling demonstration of its superiority over established methods.

The authors correctly identify the non-convexity of a direct extension of the multiclass SVM loss to the top-k setting and propose a convex relaxation (the top-k hinge loss). The theoretical contributions, including the derivation of the Fenchel conjugate and the definition of the top-k simplex, are incremental. The top-k simplex, while interesting, appears to be a relatively minor extension of the standard simplex and its practical impact isn’t clearly articulated. The connection to existing ranking-based losses, specifically the work of Usunier et al. [27], is acknowledged, but doesn't significantly enhance the paper’s contribution.

The primary weakness lies in the limited empirical validation. While the authors evaluate the proposed method on several datasets, the improvements over strong baselines (LibLinear, SVMPerf, TopPush) are often modest.  The statement that "optimizing the top-k hinge loss […] yields consistently better top-k performance" feels overstated given the experimental results. The inclusion of results for larger datasets (Places 205 and ImageNet) in the supplement is positive, but a more thorough comparison and ablation study within the main body of the paper would be beneficial.  Crucially, the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning. The range used (10−5 to 103) is broad, and it’s unclear how the optimal value was selected. 

The optimization framework, leveraging Prox-SDCA, is well-established and its application here isn't a significant innovation. The detailed discussion of the projection onto the top-k simplex, including the algorithms for handling different cases, is mathematically rigorous but feels overly detailed for a NIPS paper.  The practical impact of the optimized projection routine isn't convincingly demonstrated.  The fact that the authors explicitly mention a typo in a previous work and correct it demonstrates carefulness, which is appreciated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Mathematically sound derivation of the top-k hinge loss and its conjugate.
*   Scalability demonstrated on moderately sized datasets.
*   The paper is well-written and the derivation is clear.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – the proposed method is a relatively straightforward extension of existing techniques.
*   Modest empirical improvements compared to strong baselines.
*   Lack of detailed hyperparameter tuning information.
*   Overly detailed discussion of the projection algorithm without a corresponding demonstration of its impact on performance.
*   The claimed “consistent improvements” don’t appear robust across all experiments.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically correct but incremental contribution. The proposed top-k multiclass SVM loss is a reasonable extension of existing methods, but the empirical validation doesn’t convincingly demonstrate its superiority.  The paper lacks the novelty and significance required for acceptance to NIPS. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should consider significantly expanding the experimental evaluation, providing a more thorough hyperparameter tuning analysis, and clearly articulating the practical benefits of the proposed method.  If more substantial improvements can be demonstrated, revisiting submission to a future conference is advised.
