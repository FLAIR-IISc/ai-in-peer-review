Summary
The paper proposes a convex surrogate for top-k multiclass classification, called the top-k hinge loss, and develops an efficient optimization algorithm based on Prox-SDCA. The core idea is to relax the standard multiclass SVM loss so that errors are penalized only when the true class is not within the top-k predicted scores. Directly optimizing the top-k zero-one loss is nonconvex; the authors introduce a convex upper bound φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) sum of the k largest components of a + c}, show that it tightens the standard top-1 hinge bound whenever not all top-k scores tie, and relate it to Usunier et al.’s ranking-based ordered-weighted hinge losses. A key technical contribution is the characterization of the Fenchel conjugate of this loss; the effective domain is a new polytope, the top-k simplex ∆k, which contracts the standard simplex by imposing per-coordinate caps proportional to the total mass. Building on this, the paper derives the Fenchel dual for general L2-regularized multiclass problems, shows yi-compatibility needed for the dual constraints, and reduces the coordinate ascent updates to (biased) projections onto ∆k(r). Since standard continuous quadratic knapsack methods do not apply due to the coupling in the per-coordinate upper bound, the authors design a specialized projection algorithm with O(d log d + kd) complexity. Experiments on several image classification benchmarks (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN 397, and large-scale Places205 and ImageNet in the supplement) show consistent gains in top-k accuracy over multiclass SVM and ranking baselines; code is released.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM and Prox-SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang). It connects to ranking losses (Usunier et al.) and structured SVMs for multivariate performance measures (Joachims), clarifying that their proposed φk is a tighter convex upper bound than the common ordered-weighted hinge with βj = 1/k for j ≤ k. It also discusses binary reduction approaches for ranking-based methods (SVMPerf, TopPush) and explains scalability issues. The top-k simplex and its conjugacy role appear novel; the projection algorithm for this constrained set is new compared to standard simplex/CQKP methods.

Quality
- Theory: The formulation of φk, its sandwich bounds ψk ≤ φk ≤ φ1, and the conjugate characterization via ∆k are sound and well-motivated. The j-compatibility and dual derivation are carefully tied to [24]. The projection problems (top-k cone and top-k simplex) are analyzed with threshold conditions and feasibility checks; while some proofs are relegated to the supplement, the main statements are plausible and internally consistent.
- Algorithms: The Prox-SDCA specialization is appropriate; the dual block update reduces to a projection that the authors solve efficiently. The projection routine involves sorting and a bounded search over partitions (worst-case O(kd)), which is reasonable in practice for moderate m, and the authors discuss when it reduces to CQKP.
- Empirics: The experimental design evaluates both versions of the top-k hinge (φk and the ordered-weighted variant), compares against strong baselines (Crammer-Singer, one-vs-all SVM, SVMPerf, TopPush, Wsabie++), and uses widely adopted features. The reported improvements in top-k accuracy are consistent and practically meaningful. However, the main text defers the largest-scale results to the supplement, and runtime/memory comparisons for the projection and overall training are only briefly summarized (claimed in supplement). It would strengthen the empirical case to include wall-clock plots, scaling with m and k, and an ablation on the cost of projection vs other steps.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized: motivations, loss definition, conjugate/duality, and optimization follow a logical flow. The introduction of the top-k simplex is intuitive (figure helps), and the relation to ranking losses is helpful. Some sections are dense (e.g., projection lemmas and case analysis), and several important proofs and large-scale results are deferred to the supplement; a short proof sketch for the conjugate characterization (Proposition 2) in the main text would aid readability. Minor notational hiccups in the provided text (likely from typesetting) should be cleaned. Pseudocode and algorithmic choices are explained sufficiently for reproduction, especially with released code.

Originality
The specific top-k hinge φk and its dual characterization via the top-k simplex appear novel. The identification of ∆k as a contraction of the standard simplex and the need for specialized projection (distinct from CQKP) constitute a concrete technical contribution. While top-k and ranking-aware losses have been studied, showing that the proposed φk is a strictly tighter bound than the ordered-weighted hinge in the ambiguous regime adds value. The Prox-SDCA instantiation is not novel per se, but well-adapted to the new loss.

Significance
Top-k metrics are highly relevant in large-class problems. A convex, scalable surrogate with a principled optimization algorithm is valuable. The empirical gains over standard multiclass SVM and ranking-based baselines suggest that the method will be useful in practice. The projection algorithm may be of independent interest for other structured losses whose conjugates live in ∆k-like sets. The paper stops short of statistical calibration/consistency analysis for top-k error; such results would increase theoretical impact but are not necessary for a solid NIPS contribution.

Pros
- Clear, principled loss tailored to top-k with a tighter upper bound than prior ranking surrogates.
- Novel dual characterization (top-k simplex) and practical projection algorithm.
- Solid integration with Prox-SDCA and clear stopping criterion via duality gap.
- Consistent top-k accuracy improvements on multiple benchmarks; code release.

Cons
- Many proofs and all large-scale results relegated to the supplement; limited main-text runtime analysis.
- Projection algorithm includes an exhaustive search over partitions; worst-case behavior and constants are not deeply examined.
- Comparisons rely on reduction-based ranking methods that may not scale; one-vs-all fallbacks may bias the comparison.
- No discussion of statistical consistency for top-k or sensitivity to k and λ beyond cross-validation.

Suggestions
- Include wall-clock and memory comparisons in the main text, especially scaling with m and k and per-iteration costs.
- Provide a proof sketch for Proposition 2 and more intuition for Lemma 5.
- Compare to multiclass logistic/softmax trained with cross-entropy, which is a common strong baseline for top-k on these datasets.
- Discuss theoretical calibration to top-k error or provide counterexamples to set expectations.

Overall recommendation
Lean toward accept. The paper meets the quality, originality, and significance criteria and is reasonably clear. The technical contributions (top-k simplex and projection) and consistent empirical improvements justify publication, provided the authors strengthen runtime analyses and clarify proofs in the camera-ready.

Confidence: High.
