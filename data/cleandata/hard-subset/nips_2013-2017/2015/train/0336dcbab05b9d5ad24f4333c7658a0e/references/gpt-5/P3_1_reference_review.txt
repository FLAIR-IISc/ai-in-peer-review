Summary
The paper proposes a convex surrogate for top-k multiclass classification, extending the Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM. The key idea is a top-k hinge loss φ_k(a) = max{0, (1/k) sum_{j=1}^k (a+c)[j]}, where (a+c)[j] denotes the j-th largest entry and c = 1 − e_{y_i}. This loss upper bounds the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss and is strictly tighter than the natural averaged ordered-hinge surrogate φ̃_k(a) = (1/k) sum_{j=1}^k max{0, (a+c)[j]} except at boundary cases. The authors derive a clean Fenchel conjugate: φ_k^*(b) = −⟨c,b⟩ if b lies in a new “top-k simplex” Δ_k = {x: ⟨1,x⟩ ≤ 1, 0 ≤ x_i ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩}, +∞ otherwise. This yields a dual suitable for Prox-SDCA, with updates reducing to a novel (biased) projection onto Δ_k(r) that is not a standard continuous quadratic knapsack due to the coupling u = (1/k)⟨1,x⟩. The paper develops an O(d log d + k d) projection algorithm via thresholding/partitioning and shows scalability. Empirically, the method improves top-k accuracy across several image classification datasets using fixed CNN features, with code released.

Relation to prior work
- Extends Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM via a Ky Fan–type top-k operator. The sum of top-k components is known convex; the paper leverages that to design φ_k.
- Connects to Usunier et al. (ordered weighted hinge losses) and shows φ_k is a tighter surrogate than the straightforward averaged hinge φ̃_k.
- Compares to ranking-based optimization of multivariate measures (Joachims; TopPush), and to structured formulations for recall@k via reduction, noting scalability issues.
- Uses Prox-SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz and Zhang) and fixes a subtlety in the j-compatibility/constraint handling in the multiclass case.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: φ_k is a natural, convex, and tighter surrogate for top-k error than common ordered/averaged hinge constructions. The link to the top-k zero-one with margin is explicit.
- Solid convex-analytic development: the conjugate and domain characterization via the top-k simplex are elegant and seem correct. The representation φ_k(a) = max_{λ∈Δ_k} ⟨a+c, λ⟩ is useful and shows φ_k is a hinge-like support function over a compact polytope containing 0.
- Optimization contribution: deriving Prox-SDCA updates that require a nonstandard projection and then providing an efficient specialized projector for Δ_k(r), including a biased version, is nontrivial. The case analysis (U, M, L sets), thresholds, and complexity guarantees are convincing.
- Practicality and scalability: rank-1 updates, BLAS-friendly implementation, duality gap stopping, kernelization path, and code release increase utility.
- Empirical evidence: consistent gains in top-k metrics across diverse datasets, sometimes also improving top-1. The comparisons include both multiclass SVM and ranking-based baselines; the method scales to million-example datasets.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Theoretical properties beyond convexity are limited. In particular:
  - No calibration/consistency results for top-k error are provided. It would strengthen the paper to show that minimizing the population φ_k risk yields Bayes-optimal top-k decision (or provide counterexamples/conditions). Even a partial analysis (e.g., under separability or margin assumptions) would help.
  - Margin tightness: while φ_k ≤ φ̃_k is shown and intuitively φ_k is closer to the discrete loss, no quantitative bound is given on the surrogate gap vs. ψ_k or on generalization benefits relative to φ̃_k.
- Projection algorithm clarity: although the lemmas are correct-looking, the main text relies on a somewhat dense case analysis with references to the supplement. A self-contained pseudocode for the Δ_k(r) projection and a small illustrative example would improve readability. Also, worst-case iterations scale as (k−1)(d−k+1); a discussion of typical k and observed iteration counts would be useful.
- Empirical comparisons:
  - Several ranking baselines do not scale under the multiclass-to-binary reduction, and the use of one-vs-all fallbacks may understate their performance on some datasets. This should be called out more clearly in the main text, with a fairness caveat.
  - A strong multiclass logistic regression (softmax) baseline is missing, despite its prominence and good top-k performance on CNN features; even without end-to-end fine-tuning, a linear softmax baseline would contextualize the improvements.
  - Results for the largest datasets are relegated to the supplement; a concise summary table/figure in the main paper would strengthen the claims of scalability and accuracy.
- Notational heaviness and minor clarity issues:
  - The “max with 0 is redundant” remark for φ in the k=1 case needs a brief justification for general k (here it is not redundant).
  - Proposition 4 is central but quite terse; provide intuition for the bias term ρ⟨1,x⟩^2 and when constraint tightness triggers the knapsack subroutine.
  - Some references are incomplete/misformatted (e.g., Russakovsky et al.; missing years/pages).
- Practical guidance: Given two variants φ_k and φ̃_k, and the choice of k, more guidance on hyperparameter selection and sensitivity (e.g., training with k’ and testing at different k) would help practitioners.

Quality
Technical development appears sound and complete, with appropriate dual derivations and a carefully engineered projection method. Empirical claims are supported, though additional baselines and summary for large-scale results would improve confidence.

Clarity
Generally well-organized, but the projection section is dense. Adding algorithmic pseudocode and a schematic for Δ_k would help. Tighten notation and fix references.

Originality
The top-k hinge construction, its conjugate via the top-k simplex, and the efficient projector are novel and thoughtfully connected to prior ranking losses and SDCA frameworks.

Significance
Top-k metrics are widely used in large-label problems. Providing a convex, tighter surrogate with an efficient, scalable solver is valuable. The ideas (Δ_k, projector) are likely to be reused beyond this specific SVM.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a solid theoretical and practical contribution to optimizing top-k accuracy in multiclass problems, with a principled loss, an elegant dual, and an efficient algorithm, and demonstrates consistent empirical benefits. Addressing the noted clarity and baseline issues would strengthen the final version.
