Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a convex, top-k-aware extension of the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM. The key idea is to replace the standard multiclass hinge loss with a top-k hinge loss that upper-bounds the discrete top-k error while being tighter than a straightforward “average of positive parts” surrogate. Specifically, the proposed loss is φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) Σ_{j=1}^k (a + c)[j]}, where (·)[j] denotes the j-th largest component, and c encodes the unit-margin shift relative to the ground-truth class. The paper derives the convex conjugate of this loss and shows that its effective domain is a new polytope, termed the top-k simplex Δk, which contracts the standard simplex by imposing componentwise upper bounds xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1, x⟩. This yields a primal-conjugate pair that recovers Crammer–Singer when k=1.

Building on Fenchel duality and Proximal SDCA, the training reduces to repeated updates of a dual block ai under a linear constraint ⟨1, ai⟩=0. The core computational subproblem becomes a (biased) Euclidean projection onto Δk(r). The paper analyzes this projection, relates special cases to the continuous quadratic knapsack problem, and proposes an algorithm with O(d log d + kd) complexity based on sorting and a structured search over partitions of coordinates into sets hitting lower/upper bounds or interior. The method is compared conceptually to ranking-based surrogates (e.g., Usunier et al. with ordered weights, Joachims’ structured SVM for multivariate measures) and to a variant φ̃k that averages the top-k positive parts. The authors show φk ≤ φ̃k ≤ φ1, with strict improvement except in boundary cases.

Experiments on multiple image classification datasets (Caltech101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN397, Places205, ImageNet 2012, using CNN features where applicable) demonstrate consistent gains in top-k accuracy over Crammer–Singer and competitive ranking baselines. Code for the projection and SDCA solvers is released.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction of the top-k hinge, conjugate derivation, and dual problem are coherent. The requirement of j-compatibility and the correction to [24] are carefully handled to ensure the dual constraints. The reduction of the dual update to a biased projection onto Δk(r) is well-motivated. The projection analysis (cone vs. knapsack regimes, feasibility conditions, and threshold characterization) appears correct and complete, though proofs are deferred to the supplement.
- Algorithmic details: The update is a joint m-variable block ascent in SDCA with a principled duality-gap stopping criterion. The proposed projection algorithm is nontrivial but results in near-simplex-projection costs dominated by sorting plus O(kd). The special-casing to reuse knapsack solvers when the sum constraint is tight is appropriate.
- Empirical evaluation: Datasets are standard; features and baselines are reasonable (Crammer–Singer, one-vs-all, SVMPerf for recall@k, TopPush, WSABIE/WARP variants where scaling permits). Gains in top-k metrics are consistent, sometimes with minor trade-offs in top-1. The largest datasets are stated to be in the supplement; scalability claims are supported by method design and partial results. Some implementation details (runtime, convergence curves) would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well-organized. The notation follows prior art, and key definitions (Δk, j-compatibility) are explicit. A few issues detract from clarity: minor formatting/artifact issues in the provided text (e.g., section numbering “.2”, stray symbols in figures), incomplete references, and reliance on the supplement for all proofs. Adding a small running example to illustrate Δk and the projection thresholds would help. Explicit pseudocode for the projection routine would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The top-k hinge φk that averages the top-k largest margins before thresholding is a novel convex surrogate distinct from the ordered-weighted scheme φ̃k. Showing that φk is a tighter upper bound and deriving its conjugate with the top-k simplex domain appears original.
- The characterization and efficient computation of (biased) projections onto Δk(r) represent a nontrivial technical contribution beyond standard simplex/CQKP projections.
- The integration within Prox-SDCA to obtain a scalable top-k multiclass SVM is a solid methodological contribution.

Significance
Top-k metrics are central in large-class recognition (e.g., ImageNet, Places). A convex, scalable, and tighter surrogate that consistently improves top-k accuracy is practically relevant. The projection algorithm may find use in other optimization problems involving top-k constraints. The empirical improvements are moderate but consistent; the method is likely to be adopted for linear multiclass settings where top-k matters.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Theory: No consistency or calibration analysis with respect to the top-k error is provided. Even partial results (e.g., classification-calibration of φk for top-k) would strengthen the theoretical grounding.
- Projection algorithm: The worst-case search over partitions could be costly when k is large relative to m; empirical complexity plots vs. m and k would be useful. A comparison to faster approximate projections, or to smoothing φk, could be informative.
- Empirics: Provide training time, convergence curves, and duality gaps; include ablations on k (train with k vs. evaluate at different k) to clarify how to set k for a target metric; report sensitivity to λ; and include kernel experiments to validate the “easy to kernelize” claim.
- Baselines: Where ranking methods do not scale, one-vs-all versions are used; this may disadvantage them. Clarify memory/runtime constraints leading to this choice. Consider additional multiclass surrogates that optimize top-k-like metrics if available.
- Presentation: Fix reference list formatting and missing years; move essential parts of the projection algorithm from the supplement into the main text.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled convex surrogate tailored to top-k with a tighter bound than existing ordered-weight surrogates.
- Solid duality-based optimization with an efficient block update and a novel projection method.
- Consistent improvements on standard datasets; scalability demonstrated; code released.
- Technical contribution (top-k simplex and projection) of potential independent interest.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical analysis regarding consistency/calibration for top-k.
- Some empirical details deferred to the supplement; largest-scale results not fully presented in the main text.
- The projection algorithm, while elegant, may be complex to implement; practical performance across extreme k not fully characterized.

Overall, the paper provides a technically sound and useful contribution that advances optimization and modeling for top-k multiclass classification, with reasonable clarity and promising empirical results.
