Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a convex “top-k hinge” loss for multiclass classification that directly targets top-k accuracy. Starting from the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM loss [5], the authors define ψk(a) = max{0, (a+c)[k]} as a natural but nonconvex surrogate for top-k zero-one loss, and introduce a convex upper bound φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑j=1..k (a+c)[j]}. They show ψk ≤ φk ≤ φ1 and argue φk is strictly tighter than averaging the top-k standard hinge terms. They derive a primal–conjugate pair: the conjugate is the indicator of a new “top-k simplex” ∆k with an additional coupling constraint xi ≤ (1/k)〈1,x〉, yielding the variational form φk(a) = maxλ∈∆k 〈a+c, λ〉. This generalizes the standard simplex used by Crammer–Singer when k=1.

Within an ℓ2-regularized framework, the paper develops a Fenchel-dual formulation and optimizes with Prox-SDCA [24], updating per-example dual blocks under the constraint 〈1, ai〉=0. The per-block update reduces to a (biased) Euclidean projection onto ∆k(r). The authors analyze this projection, relate the tight-constraint case to continuous quadratic knapsack (CQKP) with xi ≤ r/k, and for the slack-constraint case derive an algorithm based on sorting, a partition of coordinates into active sets, and solving a small linear system, with overall complexity O(d log d + k d). They provide sufficient conditions for zero projection and a test (via a dual variable λ) to decide whether the knapsack or cone case applies.

The paper situates its loss relative to ranking-based surrogates Lβ of Usunier et al. [27]. With βj=1/k for j≤k, one obtains an alternative top-k surrogate φ̃k that averages the top-k hinge violations; the authors show φk ≤ φ̃k and equality conditions, arguing φk is a strictly tighter upper bound than φ̃k in a common regime. They also discuss structured SVM for recall@k [11] and binary reductions for ranking methods, noting scalability limitations. Experiments on multiple image datasets (from Caltech Silhouettes to SUN, Places, and ILSVRC) using CNN features report consistent gains in top-k accuracy over Crammer–Singer and improvements over ranking baselines; code is released.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for optimizing top-k accuracy, important in large-label settings (e.g., ILSVRC top-5).
- A principled convex surrogate φk with a clean variational representation and conjugate; the “top-k simplex” is a neat geometric object that recovers the standard simplex at k=1.
- Solid optimization framework leveraging Prox-SDCA with a block update and a well-defined stopping criterion (duality gap).
- Nontrivial technical contribution: efficient (biased) projection onto ∆k(r), carefully distinguishing knapsack-tight vs cone cases, with provable structure and reasonable complexity.
- Relation to prior ranking losses is explicit; φk is shown to be a tighter upper bound than φ̃k.
- Empirical results across several datasets indicate consistent top-k gains; scalability to large datasets is emphasized; code availability improves reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The paper relies on proofs in the supplement for key claims (conjugate derivation, correctness and completeness of the projection algorithm). While plausible, the main text could better summarize core proof ideas, especially for Proposition 2 and Lemmas 3–5.
- The projection algorithm may require an exhaustive search over feasible partitions in the general case; while bounded by O(k d) after sorting, worst-case behavior and practical constants are not fully characterized. Empirical runtime vs CQKP baselines is said to be in the supplement.
- Experimental section in the main text is short; large-scale results are deferred. Comparisons to softmax cross-entropy (multinomial logistic regression) in the same linear setting would strengthen baselines commonly used for top-k metrics.
- Theoretical properties such as calibration/consistency of φk for top-k error are not discussed. A brief analysis or citation would enhance the theoretical contribution.
- Minor clarity/formatting issues: section numbering glitches, incomplete citation years/metadata, unspecified stopping threshold in Algorithm 1, and occasional notational overload (e.g., ρ set to 1 but treated as general).

Quality
- Technical soundness appears strong: convexity of φk follows from known results on sums of order statistics; the conjugate characterization aligns with a support-function view; the dual derivation with j-compatibility resolves known issues in [24]. The reduction of the block update to a biased projection is standard in SDCA contexts. The projection method is derived with care. Empirical evaluation shows consistent improvements; scalability claims are credible given the algorithmic design and BLAS reliance.

Clarity
- Overall well-written but dense. The high-level narrative is coherent. Specific suggestions:
  - Provide an intuitive explanation or short proof sketch for why ∑ top-k equals a support function over ∆k (Proposition 2).
  - Clarify Algorithm 1’s stopping criterion and specify the duality gap tolerance.
  - Summarize the core steps and complexity of the projection algorithm in the main text; move some lemma statements to a compact algorithm box.
  - Fix minor typos and complete reference metadata.

Originality
- The loss φk and its conjugate domain (top-k simplex) appear novel. The projection algorithm for the coupled-cap upper bound xi ≤ (1/k)〈1,x〉 and biased objective is, to my knowledge, new. The relation to and improvement over the weighted ranking losses [27] is well-positioned.

Significance
- Targeting top-k accuracy with a convex, scalable method is practically significant in multiclass problems with many labels. The projection routine may be of independent interest. The approach is likely to be adopted in applications where linear models on deep features are used, and could inform future work on tighter surrogates for other ranking-based measures.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Can the authors provide theoretical calibration results (or counterexamples) for φk with respect to top-k error?
- What are typical iteration counts and wall-clock times for the projection step versus standard simplex projection on large d and k?
- Are there numerical stability considerations in solving the linear system in Lemma 3; do you regularize or guard against degenerate partitions?
- How sensitive are results to λ and k; any guidance for choosing k during training when the evaluation k differs?

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated convex surrogate tailored to top-k.
- Sound optimization via SDCA with a nontrivial and useful projection method.
- Consistent empirical gains and demonstrated scalability.
- Clear relationship to existing ranking losses with demonstrable improvement.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical analysis beyond convexity and conjugacy (no calibration/generalization guarantees).
- Experimental section in main text is brief; some key results deferred to supplement; baseline coverage could be broader (e.g., softmax).
- Minor clarity/formatting issues.
