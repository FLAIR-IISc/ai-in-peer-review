This paper introduces a top-k multiclass SVM, relaxing the penalty for errors outside the top-k predictions. The core idea is a convex relaxation of a non-convex loss function that directly corresponds to the top-k zero-one loss. The authors derive a primal-conjugate pair for the proposed loss and present an efficient optimization framework based on Prox-SDCA, including a novel projection algorithm onto a modified simplex. The experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach on several image classification datasets.

The paper builds on the existing work on multiclass SVMs (Crammer & Singer, 2006) and extends it to address the top-k prediction scenario. It also connects to recent advances in ranking-based losses (Usunier et al., 2016) and provides a nuanced comparison to related approaches like LaRank and TopPush. The derivation of the conjugate function and the projection algorithm represent notable technical contributions.  The use of Prox-SDCA is a sensible choice, leveraging existing efficient optimization techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed top-k hinge loss and the corresponding convex relaxation are a novel contribution to the field.  Addressing top-k accuracy directly within an SVM framework is a valuable direction.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations, including the primal-conjugate pair and the derivation of the projection algorithm, appear sound and well-justified. The connection to existing work on convex optimization and the continuous quadratic knapsack problem is clearly established.
* **Optimization Framework:** The use of Prox-SDCA provides an efficient and scalable optimization strategy, and the detailed explanation of the dual variable update is helpful.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experiments on several image datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method and show improvements over existing approaches. The scalability to large datasets like Places 205 and ImageNet is a positive aspect.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Approximation:** While the paper establishes that the proposed loss is an upper bound on the true top-k loss, a more detailed theoretical analysis of the approximation quality would be beneficial.  Under what conditions is the gap between the proposed loss and the true loss likely to be small?
* **Projection Algorithm Complexity:** While the authors claim the projection algorithm is comparable to existing simplex projection methods, a more thorough empirical comparison of the runtime of the proposed projection algorithm against state-of-the-art alternatives (e.g., using optimized libraries) would strengthen the claims. The discussion of the search space (up to (k-1)(d-k+1) iterations) for the general case could be clarified – is this worst-case, and how often does it occur in practice?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but doesn't provide detailed insights into the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of the regularization parameter λ. A more comprehensive ablation study, analyzing the impact of different hyperparameters on the results, would be valuable.
* **Comparison to Baselines:**  The comparison to existing methods is good, but could be further strengthened. For instance, evaluating against more recent and specialized top-k prediction methods, beyond SVM-based approaches, would provide a more complete benchmark.
* **Minor Clarifications:** Proposition 3 states the top-k hinge loss is *yi*-compatible, yet the discussion surrounding Definition 2 is somewhat abstract. Explicitly showing *how* the top-k hinge loss satisfies the compatibility condition for a concrete example would solidify this point.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to top-k multiclass classification.
* The proposed convex relaxation and optimization framework are well-justified and efficient.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on several benchmark datasets.
* The paper has the potential to influence future research in multiclass learning and ranking.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The theoretical analysis of the approximation quality of the proposed loss is limited.
* The empirical evaluation of the projection algorithm’s runtime could be more thorough.
* The paper lacks a detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of multiclass learning. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the technical soundness of the derivations, and the positive experimental results outweigh these concerns.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should address the points raised in the "Weaknesses" section, particularly providing a more in-depth discussion of the approximation quality of the proposed loss and a more rigorous comparison of the runtime of the projection algorithm.  Adding a sensitivity analysis for the regularization parameter would further strengthen the paper.
