Summary
The paper proposes a convex, top-k-aware surrogate for multiclass classification, along with an efficient optimization framework. The main idea is to replace the standard multiclass SVM hinge with a top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) ∑j=1..k (a+c)[j]}, which upper-bounds the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss and becomes the Crammer-Singer loss when k=1. A key technical contribution is the characterization of the convex conjugate, which naturally introduces the top-k simplex Δk, a contraction of the standard simplex with a coupling upper bound xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩. Building on this, the authors derive the Fenchel dual of the `2-regularized problem and show that Prox-SDCA can be adapted, with the per-example dual update reducing to a (biased) projection onto Δk(r). They develop algorithms for efficient projections onto Δk(r) by reducing to a continuous quadratic knapsack when tight and otherwise to a projection onto a “top-k cone,” providing conditions and thresholds. They relate their loss to ranking-based losses (e.g., Usunier et al.) and show that their φk is a tighter convex upper bound than the averaged hinge φ̃k. Experiments on five image datasets (including large scale) demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over Crammer-Singer and ranking baselines, with released code.

Quality
– Theory: The convexity, conjugate derivation, and dual formulation look sound. The introduction of Δk as the effective domain and the j-compatibility requirement are well-motivated, and the connection to Prox-SDCA is clean. The projection algorithms are nontrivial; the decomposition into knapsack vs top-k cone and the feasibility conditions are carefully stated, though many proofs and some algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement.
– Algorithms: The biased projection is novel for this domain. The worst-case O(d log d + k d) projection via sorting plus a bounded search over partitions is reasonable, but the worst-case enumeration could be costly; empirical timing comparisons vs strong simplex projection baselines are important (authors claim scalability; details in the supplement).
– Empirics: The experimental suite is solid in scope and includes large datasets. Gains in top-k are consistent but typically modest (≈1–3% in top-5). Some top-1 accuracy drops exist, which is expected but should be discussed in terms of trade-offs. It would be useful to see comparisons against multinomial logistic regression (softmax) on the same features, as that is a common baseline for top-k metrics.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with careful notation and helpful connections to prior work. The introduction of Δk is intuitive (figure helps), though more geometric intuition and examples of extreme points would help. The role of the bias term ρ in the projection (why ρ=1 arises, how sensitive results are to ρ) could be clarified in the main text. Some references in the list appear incomplete or have missing years—please fix. Minor editorial suggestions: briefly summarize the key proof ideas for Prop. 2 and Lemmas 3–5 in the main paper; add a high-level pseudocode for the Δk projection to complement the text.

Originality
This paper introduces a principled convex top-k hinge for multiclass, its dual characterization via a novel top-k simplex, and an efficient projection-based Prox-SDCA solver. While the idea of summing top-k components is known to be convex and ranking-based top-k surrogates exist (Usunier et al., Joachims), the specific multiclass formulation, conjugate/geometry (Δk), and practical projection algorithms appear new and nontrivial. The work is more than an incremental plug-in; the dual and projection machinery open a path for scalable optimization of top-k-aware multiclass SVMs.

Significance
Top-k metrics are central in modern large-class problems (e.g., ImageNet top-5). Providing a tight convex surrogate, a clean dual, and a scalable solver is valuable for both theory and practice. The projection onto Δk may be of independent interest. Although the measured accuracy improvements are moderate, the approach is general, principled, and likely to be adopted and extended.

Pros
– Conceptually clean top-k hinge that recovers Crammer-Singer at k=1 and tightens the upper bound relative to φ̃k.
– New geometric object (top-k simplex) with clear dual interpretation.
– Practical, scalable optimization via Prox-SDCA with per-example updates reduced to projections.
– Technical contribution on projecting onto Δk (including biased case), with connections to CQKP.
– Broad empirical evaluation on multiple datasets; consistent improvements in top-k; code release.

Cons
– Some proofs and algorithmic details relegated to the supplement; main text could give more intuition.
– Worst-case complexity of the partition search in the projection step may be high; more runtime analysis and ablations would help.
– Empirical gains are modest; impact vs strong softmax baselines is unclear.
– Some top-1 accuracy decreases; trade-offs could be better analyzed.
– Reference list has inconsistencies and missing bibliographic details.

Questions and suggestions for authors
1) Please compare against multinomial logistic regression trained with the same features and report top-k metrics.
2) Provide wall-clock training times and projection timing breakdowns vs standard simplex projection to substantiate scalability claims (not only in supplement).
3) Clarify the role and necessity of the bias term ρ in practice; is ρ=1 fixed or tunable?
4) Give more intuition about Δk: extreme points, geometry, and how it contracts Δ.
5) Discuss sensitivity to k and λ; does optimizing top-(k’) transfer to improving top-k for k’>k systematically?

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept to accept). The paper introduces a neat and useful top-k multiclass surrogate with a solid dual and a carefully engineered optimization scheme. The top-k simplex and projection algorithms are conceptually fresh and technically interesting. Despite modest empirical gains and some details in the supplement, the contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful for NeurIPS.
