Summary
The paper proposes a convex surrogate for top-k multiclass classification, extending Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM to directly target top-k accuracy. The key idea is to replace the standard hinge φ(a)=max{0,(a+c)[1]} with the “top-k hinge” φk(a)=max{0,(1/k)∑_{j=1}^k (a+c)[j]}, i.e., the average of the k largest (shifted) margins, which upper-bounds the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss ψk(a). The authors characterize the convex conjugate of φk and introduce the “top-k simplex” Δk={x | ⟨1,x⟩≤1, 0≤xi≤(1/k)⟨1,x⟩}, showing φk(a)=max_{λ∈Δk}⟨a+c,λ⟩ and φk∗(b)=−⟨c,b⟩ if b∈Δk, +∞ otherwise. Building on Fenchel duality, they derive a Prox-SDCA optimization scheme, where each dual update reduces to a regularized projection onto Δk(r). They provide an algorithm for projecting onto the “top-k cone” (the simplex without the sum constraint) and decide between cone and continuous-quadratic-knapsack cases by checking KKT conditions. Experiments on several image classification datasets using CNN features indicate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy versus standard multiclass SVM and ranking methods.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The top-k hinge as a tighter convex upper bound than prior ordered-weighted hinge (Usunier et al.) is well-motivated. The observation φk≤φ̃k≤φ1 with cases of equality is useful.
- Duality and geometry: The conjugate characterization via the top-k simplex (a nonstandard polytope with xi≤(1/k)⟨1,x⟩ coupling) is elegant and naturally generalizes the standard simplex used in Crammer-Singer.
- Optimization: Casting the per-example SDCA update as a (biased) projection onto Δk(r) and decomposing into knapsack vs cone cases is technically interesting. The O(d log d + kd) worst-case complexity is reasonable and the cone projection conditions (Lemmas 3–5) are novel and potentially reusable.
- Practicality: Prox-SDCA, primal-dual gap stopping, BLAS-friendly implementation, kernelization via W=XA> and W>xi=AKi are all solid engineering choices. The library release is appreciated.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and completeness
- Several steps are deferred to the supplement (proofs of Prop. 2, Lemmas 3–5, and exact Prox-SDCA optimization of φ̃k). Given the centrality of these results, at least proof sketches and more intuition should be in the main text. In particular, the conjugate derivation with the “max with 0” term is subtle; the reduction to the support function over Δk hinges on including λ=0, but the full argument should be shown.
- j-compatibility (Def. 2) is nonstandard; more intuition is needed. It is asserted that the top-k hinge satisfies this (Prop. 3), but the proof is not present. Since Theorem 1 relies critically on this property, this omission hurts reproducibility.
- Proposition 4 notation is difficult to verify. b is defined using q\yi and 1, but dimensions and the role of qyi in b’s definition are not entirely clear; please double-check consistency of q, b, and the removal/insertion of coordinates.
- The per-iteration complexity of the cone projection depends on sorting and an exhaustive search over partitions U,M,L with worst-case (k−1)(d−k+1) iterations. Practical bounds and empirical timings versus standard simplex projection are relegated to the supplement; having (at least) a timing table in the main text would substantiate the scalability claim.
- The statement “We choose [13] since it does not require sorting and scales linearly in practice” should be complemented with accuracy/duality gap convergence plots to show the overall training time benefit.

Novelty and related work
- The connection between φk and ordered-weighted hinge losses (Usunier et al. 2009) is acknowledged, but the exact novelty over prior ranking surrogates (including structured SVM formulations for recall@k and other top-k surrogates) should be articulated more precisely. Related notions such as the k-support norm and Lovász-type/ordered weighted losses could be discussed for context.
- The reduction-based comparisons (SVMPerf, TopPush) are not apples-to-apples when they don’t scale and you fall back to one-vs-all versions. This undermines the strength of the empirical comparison; better-scaled structured surrogates or recent large-scale ranking methods should be included where possible.

Empirical evaluation
- The main text references Table 1 and large-scale results “in the supplement,” but the actual numbers are absent here. Claims of consistent improvements should be supported by explicit top-k accuracies, standard deviations over splits, and clear training/runtime/memory metrics for all methods.
- It is unusual to highlight “top-10 SVMα improves top-5 accuracy by +2.5%” without also showing the trade-off on top-1 and top-10 metrics, and without consistent tuning protocols across baselines. Please include ablations: varying k at training vs evaluation k, sensitivity to λ, effect of bias term ρ (even if set to 1), and impact of cone vs knapsack branch frequency.

Clarity and presentation (many minor issues)
- Notation is often ambiguous: 1 denotes both scalar and all-ones vector; 1k appears to mean (1/k) but is not defined; [j] denotes j-th order statistic and also index; c , 1−eyi and aj , … use commas inconsistently; define all operators and symbols once and stick to them.
- Section numbering is broken: “.2”, “.2.1”, “.3”, etc. Algorithm 1 has an incomplete stopping criterion (“below ”, missing ε).
- Several sentences are hard to parse or incorrect (e.g., “Note that thresholding with 0 in φ(a) is actually redundant…”, which is true for k=1 but later needs a caveat for k≥2; add a dedicated remark).
- Figure 2 appears garbled (“/3 1/2 … Top-1 Top-2 Top-3”) and lacks a proper caption and axis/legend explanations.
- References are severely inconsistent and in many cases incomplete: missing years, venues, page ranges, broken author lists (e.g., “[22] and L, O. Russakovsky, … Fei-Fei.”), missing metadata for [24], etc. Please fix to standard NIPS style.
- Typos and grammar: missing articles, inconsistent punctuation around definitions, “respectively the biased projection” should be “i.e., the biased projection,” “nonconvex” vs “non-convex,” etc.

Pros
- Solid, principled convex surrogate specifically tailored to top-k error.
- Clean dual characterization via the top-k simplex; interesting geometry.
- Practical SDCA-based solver with a novel projection routine.
- Promising empirical results claimed on large-scale vision datasets.

Cons
- Key proofs and complexity arguments are offloaded to the supplement; some derivations in the main text are hard to verify.
- Empirical evidence in the main text is insufficient; comparisons are sometimes unfair due to scalability issues of baselines.
- Numerous notation/formatting/reference issues hinder readability and raise concerns about carefulness.
- Some implementation details (stopping criterion, ρ’s role, exact update formulas) are under-specified.

Questions for authors
1) Please provide a clear, self-contained proof sketch of Prop. 2 (conjugate) and Prop. 3 (j-compatibility) in the main text.
2) Clarify the precise definition of 1k, and consistently distinguish scalar 1 vs vector 1. Unify [j] vs (·)[j] notation.
3) In Prop. 4, can you restate b and q more transparently and verify dimensions? A small derivation of the equivalence to a biased projection would help.
4) What fraction of updates fall into the knapsack vs cone branch in practice for m=1000? Provide timing histograms.
5) How does training with ktrain affect evaluation at keval across datasets? Show a matrix of results.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper presents a neat and potentially impactful idea with solid optimization, but the current presentation has too many technical and editorial issues, and the empirical evidence in the main body is insufficient. With strengthened proofs, clearer notation, fixed references/formatting, and fuller experiments in the main text, I would be inclined to support acceptance. Confidence: high.
