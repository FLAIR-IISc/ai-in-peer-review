Summary
This paper proposes a multiclass SVM surrogate tailored to top-k accuracy. The core idea is a convex top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) sum of the k largest components of (a+c)}, which upper bounds the top-k zero-one loss and is strictly tighter than both the standard multiclass hinge (k=1) and the rank-based loss φ̃k(a) = (1/k) sum_j max{0, (a+c)[j]} used in Usunier et al. The authors derive its Fenchel conjugate and identify a new feasible set—the top-k simplex ∆k(r)—which contracts the standard simplex by imposing xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1, x⟩. This geometric insight yields a clean dual for ℓ2-regularized multiclass problems and leads to a Prox-SDCA solver whose per-example dual update reduces to a (biased) projection onto ∆k(r). A main technical contribution is an efficient O(d log d + kd) algorithm for projections onto the top-k simplex/cone, handling coupling upper bounds and a bias term. Experiments on several image datasets (Caltech101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN397, and in the supplement Places205 and ImageNet2012 with CNN features) show consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over multiclass SVM and rank-based baselines, with code released.

Relation to prior work
- Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM is recovered at k=1. The proposed φk generalizes this with a sum-of-top-k operator known to be convex.
- Ranking-based convex families Lβ (Usunier et al.) include φ̃k, but φk is a strictly tighter upper bound except in boundary cases, and the paper shows how to optimize it exactly via SDCA.
- Structured SVM for Recall@k (Joachims) is theoretically elegant but not instance-decomposable and less scalable; here, the authors use a multiclass-to-binary reduction to compare against ranking baselines.
- Optimization builds on Prox-SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang) but fixes a subtlety in the derivation regarding y-compatibility and equality constraints in the dual.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The top-k hinge loss φk is a clean, convex surrogate that directly targets top-k metrics and is tighter than existing rank-based losses. The top-k simplex is a neat geometric object that naturally arises as the conjugate’s domain.
- Technical contribution: Efficient, principled algorithm for (biased) projections onto ∆k(r), addressing coupling upper bounds not handled by standard CQKP solvers.
- Solid optimization framework: Block updates in Prox-SDCA, rank-1 primal maintenance, kernelizability, and a computable duality gap.
- Scalability: Evidence (supplement) of training on millions of examples; implementation uses BLAS and appears practical.
- Empirical utility: Consistent top-k gains across datasets and k. Demonstrates the intended behavior (sometimes sacrificing top-1 slightly while improving top-k), and in some cases improving top-1 too.
- Reproducibility: Public code; many derivations (with proofs in supplement).

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical baselines: Missing a strong multinomial logistic (softmax) baseline trained on the same features, which is often competitive for top-k. Also, modern deep fine-tuning is out of scope here, but a linear softmax would be a fair comparator to linear SVMs.
- Magnitude of gains: Reported improvements are modest (1–3% absolute in top-5), though consistent. A more thorough ablation of φk vs φ̃k in the main paper (not just the supplement) would help attribute where gains come from.
- Projection algorithm clarity: The “biased projection” discussion states “we leave x = 0 as the fallback case as Lemma 4 gives only a sufficient condition.” This is ambiguous. If the general case solver fails, returning zero would break correctness. Please clarify: is x=0 only an early-exit when the sufficient condition is met, or a true fallback? Provide pseudo-code and guarantees of termination/correctness.
- Complexity and constants: Worst-case O(d log d + kd) is reasonable, but practical constants for large m (e.g., 1000) matter. Some profiling versus a standard simplex projection would be useful beyond the supplement, especially for varying k and sparsity.
- Theory: No statistical consistency/top-k calibration analysis. While not required, a brief discussion of when φk is calibrated for top-k error would strengthen the contribution.
- Presentation: Several references are incomplete or malformed; some technical details needed to fully reproduce the projection algorithm are relegated to the supplement. The paper is dense and could benefit from a short running example and an explicit pseudo-code block for the projection.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces an elegant, clearly motivated convex surrogate tailored to top-k accuracy, with a nontrivial geometric/conjugate insight (top-k simplex).
- Provides an efficient optimization algorithm with practical scalability and public code.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements on relevant large-scale vision benchmarks.

Cons for acceptance
- Baseline coverage (lack of softmax) and modest gains limit the empirical punch.
- Some ambiguity and missing details around the projection routine’s correctness in the biased case.
- No calibration/consistency results.

Requested clarifications/suggestions
- Add a multinomial logistic baseline on the same features for at least the smaller datasets.
- Clarify the biased projection algorithm: provide pseudo-code, make the role of the sufficient zero-projection test explicit, and guarantee correctness/termination without ad-hoc fallbacks.
- Report training time per epoch and total time vs LibLinear and a state-of-the-art simplex projection routine across k.
- Include a short ablation of φk vs φ̃k in the main text with error bars.
- Fix references (years, authors, broken entries) and minor typos.

Overall assessment
This paper presents a genuinely novel and well-motivated approach to optimizing top-k accuracy in multiclass settings, supported by solid convex analysis, a new projection algorithm of independent interest, and scalable implementation. Despite some empirical and presentation gaps, the contribution is fresh and likely to be influential for applications where top-k matters. I favor acceptance.

Confidence
High (I am familiar with multiclass SVMs, convex conjugates, and SDCA).
