The paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM, motivated by relaxing the penalty for errors outside the top-k predictions. The core idea is to replace the standard multiclass SVM loss with a hinge loss based on the sum of the k largest scores. The authors derive a primal-conjugate pair for this new loss and present an optimization framework based on Prox-SDCA, including efficient projection onto the top-k simplex. 

While the motivation is reasonable and the extension to top-k SVM conceptually sound, the paper suffers from numerous, often minor, but cumulatively significant flaws. The presentation frequently lacks rigor and suffers from inconsistencies.

**Strengths:**

*   The idea of relaxing the penalty for errors beyond the top-k predictions is intuitively appealing and potentially useful in applications where only the top predictions matter.
*   The derivation of the primal-conjugate pair is a positive contribution, providing a theoretical foundation for optimization.
*   The optimization framework leveraging Prox-SDCA is a standard and well-suited approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational inconsistencies:** The use of ‘a’ is ambiguous. It’s defined as componentwise, but then used in vector operations (e.g., a+c) without clear componentwise indication, leading to confusion. This ambiguity appears throughout.
*   **Redundant Justification:** The statement regarding the redundancy of thresholding with 0 in Proposition 1 feels unnecessary and detracts from the core argument.
*   **Non-convexity Discussion:** The discussion of ψk’s non-convexity is correct, but the paper doesn’t adequately explain *why* this necessitates the convex relaxation φk. A more detailed explanation of the intractability beyond a simple statement is needed.
*   **Top-k Simplex Definition:** The definition of ∆k(r) is presented without sufficient motivation. While Figure 2 helps, the rationale behind the upper bound xi ≤ 1k 〈1, x〉 is not clearly articulated.  Why is this a natural extension of the standard simplex, beyond simply reducing to it when k=1?
*   **Lemma 1 Citation:** The citation to Lemma 1 ([17]) feels disconnected. It's used in the proof (in the supplement), but the connection is not explained within the main text.
*   **Proposition 4 Equivalence:** The equivalence stated in Proposition 4 is a strong claim, and the connection to the optimization problem isn’t fully explained. The statement regarding ‘x’ seems out of place.
*   **Projection Algorithm Details:** The description of the projection algorithms (Section 4) is overly high-level. While references are given, the paper doesn't provide enough detail to assess the practical feasibility or efficiency of the approach. The claim of “linear” time complexity for projection appears unsubstantiated without more thorough analysis.
*   **Experimental Details:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. The choice of CNN features (e.g., “Places 205 CNN”) is mentioned but not justified. Hyperparameter ranges and selection methods are mentioned but not fully described.
*   **Empirical Results:** The improvements reported in Table 1 are modest. While positive, they don't demonstrate a substantial breakthrough. The claim that top-k SVM *may* increase top-1 accuracy on larger datasets is weak and requires further investigation.
*   **Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor typographical errors and grammatical inconsistencies throughout the text distract from the presentation (e.g., “effective domain1”).

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The idea has merit and addresses a potentially useful problem.
*   The theoretical derivation of the primal-conjugate pair is a solid contribution.

**Con:**

*   The presentation is often unclear and lacks rigor.
*   The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the work.
*   The experimental results are modest and do not strongly justify the proposed approach.
*   The reliance on the supplement for crucial details (proofs, implementation specifics) makes it difficult to fully assess the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a reasonable idea but is hampered by numerous flaws in presentation, clarity, and detail. While the theoretical contributions are positive, the lack of rigor and the modest experimental results do not warrant acceptance to NIPS.  The paper feels underdeveloped and requires significant revision to address the identified weaknesses. I recommend **rejection**. The paper, even with its novelty, falls short of the expected quality for NIPS due to a lack of careful attention to detail and a less-than-compelling empirical demonstration.
