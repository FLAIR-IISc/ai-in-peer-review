## Review of Top-k Multiclass SVM

This paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM loss function and its corresponding optimization framework. The core idea is to relax the penalty for errors in the top-k predictions, motivated by scenarios where precise classification is less critical than identifying a set of likely candidates. The authors derive a convex upper bound on a natural but non-convex extension of the standard multiclass SVM loss, and provide a Fenchel dual formulation suitable for efficient optimization via Prox-SDCA. The paper also presents empirical results on several image classification datasets demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

While the motivation is sound and the technical contributions (particularly the convex relaxation and conjugate derivation) are valuable, the paper suffers from a surprising number of minor flaws and a lack of thoroughness in presentation and experimentation, leading me to recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** The top-k classification problem is practically relevant in many applications.
*   **Technical Contributions:** Deriving a convex upper bound for the top-k loss and its conjugate is a significant achievement. This enables efficient optimization.
*   **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate scalability to large datasets using Prox-SDCA.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The rationale behind relaxing the loss for top-k errors is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistency:** The definition of ∆ in section 2.1 is unclear. It's presented as a set of vectors, but then used with components a<sub>j</sub>. Further, the use of '∆' is overloaded (∆ and ∆<sub>k</sub>).
*   **Redundant Thresholding:** The authors acknowledge the redundancy of the thresholding with 0 in (2), yet include it for “enhancing similarity.” This adds unnecessary complexity and potential confusion.
*   **Insufficient Justification for Convex Relaxation:** While the authors state the necessity of a convex relaxation due to the non-convexity of ψ<sub>k</sub>, a more detailed discussion of *why* φ<sub>k</sub> is a reasonable approximation would be beneficial.  The justification "φ<sub>k</sub> < φ<sub>1</sub> unless all k largest scores are the same" is weak and doesn’t address the potential gap between ψ<sub>k</sub> and φ<sub>k</sub>.
*   **Figure 2 Clarity:** Figure 2 is not sufficiently informative.  It requires more explicit labeling and explanation of how the simplex differs from the standard one. The visual impact is limited.
*   **Lemma 1 Citation:** Lemma 1 is cited as [17], yet Lemma 2, which relies on it, is not adequately explained within the context of the top-k loss.  A more thorough discussion is needed.
*   **Theorem 1 Caveats:** The conditions for Theorem 1 (φ<sub>i</sub> being yi-compatible) seem strong but aren’t adequately explained. The authors state top-k hinge loss satisfies it, but without further elaboration on *why* this compatibility is important.
*   **Algorithmic Detail:** The "update" step in Algorithm 1 (Line 9) is referenced to "§ 3.2.1" but the details within are insufficient for complete reproduction. The connection to Proposition 4 isn’t made explicit within the algorithm description.
*   **Experimental Rigor:**  The experimental setup lacks detailed hyperparameter tuning explanations. The range 10<sup>-5</sup> to 10<sup>3</sup> is broad and a more systematic approach is needed. The statement about extending the range "when the optimal value is at the boundary" is vague and unconvincing.
*   **Comparison to Baseline:** The comparison to SVMOVA feels weak, as it's not a direct comparison to a top-k method. A comparison to a naive top-k post-processing step on SVMOVA would be more informative.
*   **Redundancy in Section 2.3:** The discussion regarding the binary reduction feels disconnected and lengthy. The purpose of outlining this reduction isn't entirely clear, and the comparison to methods like SVMPerf is superficial.
* **Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor typos exist throughout the paper (e.g., “notational convenience” should be “for notational convenience,”  “dom f” is defined without defining f).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a relevant problem and proposes a novel approach.
*   The technical derivations (convex relaxation, conjugate derivation) are mathematically sound.
*   The empirical results show consistent improvements in top-k accuracy.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from numerous minor flaws in notation, clarity, and presentation.
*   The justification for the convex relaxation and experimental setup are insufficiently rigorous.
*   The comparison to existing methods is not comprehensive enough.
*   The overall presentation lacks polish and attention to detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the idea is interesting and the technical contributions are valid, the paper's shortcomings in presentation, rigor, and completeness outweigh its strengths.  The sheer number of minor issues suggests a lack of thorough review and polishing. A major revision addressing these points would be necessary for reconsideration.  Therefore, I recommend **rejection**.
