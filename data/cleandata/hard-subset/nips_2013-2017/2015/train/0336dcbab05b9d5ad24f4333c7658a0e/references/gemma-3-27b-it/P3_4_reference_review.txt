## Review of "Top-k Multiclass SVM"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound extension to the multiclass SVM framework, specifically addressing the top-k classification problem. The authors provide a convex relaxation of a natural but non-convex loss function and derive its Fenchel conjugate, enabling efficient optimization via Prox-SDCA. The paper is clearly written and demonstrates a good understanding of relevant literature. While the improvements presented are incremental rather than groundbreaking, the work is solid and addresses a relevant problem. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary:**

The paper introduces a “top-k” multiclass SVM loss function designed to be less penalizing for errors when the correct class is within the top-k predictions. The authors identify a non-convex formulation that directly corresponds to the top-k zero-one loss, then propose a convex upper bound (the top-k hinge loss) and derive its Fenchel conjugate. This allows for efficient optimization using the Prox-SDCA framework. The paper provides experimental validation on several image classification datasets, demonstrating consistent improvements in top-k accuracy compared to the standard multiclass SVM.

**Strengths:**

* **Well-Motivated Problem:** Relaxing the penalty for errors outside the top-k predictions is a practical consideration in many applications, particularly ranking and information retrieval.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the convex relaxation and its conjugate is rigorous and clearly explained. The connection to existing work on multiclass SVMs and ranking losses (Usunier et al.) is well-established.
* **Efficient Optimization:** Leveraging the Prox-SDCA framework ensures scalability to large datasets, a critical factor for many modern machine learning problems. The detailed discussion of projection onto the top-k simplex is a significant contribution, addressing a non-trivial technical challenge.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The evaluation across multiple datasets of varying sizes demonstrates the generalizability of the proposed approach.  The comparison to existing methods (SVMPerf, TopPush) provides valuable context.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the technical details. The release of the C++ library is a commendable step for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Improvement:** While the consistent gains in top-k accuracy are positive, the magnitude of the improvements appears relatively modest. The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the scenarios where these gains are most substantial.
* **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** The core idea of relaxing the loss function for top-k errors is not entirely new; similar approaches exist in ranking literature. The primary novelty lies in the specific formulation within the multiclass SVM framework and the associated theoretical analysis.
* **Complexity of Projection:** The projection onto the top-k simplex, while well-addressed, adds significant complexity to the optimization process. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of this projection compared to standard simplex projection.
* **Lack of Ablation Study:**  Ablation studies examining the impact of different components (e.g., the bias term in the projection) would provide further insight into the effectiveness of the proposed approach.



**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 2.2:** The discussion of why `ψk` is non-convex could be more intuitive. Perhaps a simple example illustrating the non-convexity would be helpful.
* **Section 3.2.1:** The connection to the continuous quadratic knapsack problem is well explained, but the choice of algorithm [13] seems somewhat arbitrary. Were other algorithms considered, and if so, why was this one selected?
* **Section 5:** The statement about "systematic increase in top-k accuracy" could be strengthened by providing quantitative results (e.g., average improvement across datasets).
* **Experiments:** It would be interesting to see results with different values of 'k'. Is there an optimal value of k that maximizes performance?
* **Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, a more thorough discussion of connections to learning-to-rank algorithms that directly optimize for top-k metrics (e.g., ListNet, LambdaMART) would be beneficial.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and practical problem in multiclass classification.
* The theoretical foundation is solid, and the proposed convex relaxation is well-motivated.
* The implementation is efficient and scalable, enabling experimentation on large datasets.
* The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy.
* The code release promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The improvements are incremental rather than transformative.
* The core idea builds on existing work in ranking literature.
* The complexity of the projection step may limit practical applicability in some scenarios.



**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors address the following:

1.  **Strengthen the discussion of the improvements:** Provide more in-depth analysis of when the proposed approach offers the most significant benefits.
2.  **Justify the choice of the projection algorithm:** Explain why algorithm [13] was selected and compare it to other potential options.
3. **Add ablation studies:** Analyze the impact of different components of the proposed method.
4. **Expand the related work section:** Discuss connections to relevant learning-to-rank algorithms.
