Summary
The paper proposes a convex surrogate for top-k multiclass classification: a “top-k hinge” loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑j=1..k (a+c)[j]}, where a stacks class score differences and c = 1 − eyi. The authors derive its convex conjugate and introduce the “top-k simplex” Δk = {x | ⟨1,x⟩ ≤ 1, 0 ≤ xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩}, showing φk(a) = max{⟨a+c, λ⟩ | λ∈Δk}. They then develop an SDCA-based optimization framework with a per-example dual block update that reduces to a (biased) Euclidean projection onto Δk(r). They present an algorithm for efficient projections onto ∆k(r), reducing to a continuous quadratic knapsack problem (CQKP) when ⟨1,x⟩ = r, and otherwise solving a “top-k cone” projection with O(d log d + kd) complexity. Experiments on several image datasets suggest consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over multiclass SVM baselines, and comparisons to ranking surrogates.

Strengths
- Well-motivated objective: directly targets top-k error with a convex surrogate that is tighter than the ordered hinge variant φ̃k in general.
- Convex analysis: clear support-function representation and a neat dual characterization via the top-k simplex; the primal-conjugate pair generalizes Crammer–Singer (k=1).
- Optimization: principled SDCA formulation with a block update and a stopping criterion via duality gap; kernelization is straightforward.
- Algorithmic contribution: projection algorithm onto Δk(r), including the biased case, appears practically useful.
- Empirical scope: multiple datasets and comparisons to several baselines.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Major issues affecting acceptance:
1) Anonymity breach: The paper links to a public GitHub repo with an author-identifying handle (“https://github.com/mlapin/libsdca”). This violates double-blind policy and must be removed/replaced with an anonymous link.

2) Clarity and presentation problems:
- Section numbering and formatting contain numerous errors (e.g., “.2 Top-k Support Vector Machine”, “.2.1”, stray glyphs near Figure 2 like “/3 1/2 /2 1/3 /2 /3”, Algorithm 1 ends with “until relative duality gap is below” with no threshold). Many equations lack numbers.
- Notation inconsistencies: sometimes ej, sometimes eyi; 1 used both as scalar and all-ones vector; bold vs non-bold vectors not clearly indicated; a\j defined but later a\yii appears without prior definition; c , 1−eyi (spacing/commas).
- References are incomplete and garbled (missing years/pages; “and L, O. Russakovsky, ... Fei-Fei.”). Please clean and complete all citations.

3) Theoretical exposition gaps:
- Proposition 2 (conjugate and Δk) is central; only a high-level statement is in the main text with proofs deferred. A brief proof sketch or more intuition in the main paper would strengthen credibility.
- The definition of j-compatibility, Lemma 2 with Hj = I − 1e>j, and the role of the constraint ⟨1,y⟩=0 need clearer motivation and derivation. Hj maps x to x − xj 1, which zeroes the j-th coordinate but shifts all others by −xj; please justify this construction carefully and show the link to the constrained conjugate.
- Figure 2 claims “(m choose k) + 1 vertices” for Δk(1) without proof. This is nontrivial; in fact, the extreme point structure of Δk is not obvious and may be mischaracterized. Provide a formal characterization or remove the claim.

4) Algorithmic novelty vs. prior work:
- The projection onto sums of top-k components connects to Ky Fan norms/ordered statistics and related convex sets; connections to k-support norm, ordered weighted norms, or Lovasz hinge are not discussed. This weakens the originality framing.
- Complexity is stated as O(d log d + kd) due to sorting; consider partial selection (O(d)) and compare empirically. Clarify constants and practical scaling.

5) Experimental reporting:
- Key tables/figures are missing from the main text (e.g., “Table 1” not included). Many large-scale results are deferred to the supplement; the main paper does not provide enough to assess claims.
- Fairness details are insufficient: hyperparameter search budgets per method, stopping criteria, number of epochs/passes, wall-clock time, and memory footprint are not reported. The reduction to md-dimensional binary problems for ranking baselines is infeasible at scale; falling back to one-vs-all variants can be unfair and must be clearly labeled and analyzed.
- Statistical variation (std/CI) is not reported.
- The choice of training k versus evaluation k is unclear (e.g., reporting top-5 accuracy after training with k=10). Please show sensitivity of performance to training k and the cross-metric behavior.

Minor issues:
- Equation (1) lacks an explicit [·]+; you later argue it is redundant since the yi term equals 0, but please state this explicitly with a short justification.
- “We choose [13]” is vague; specify the algorithm and briefly justify the choice.
- Proposition 4’s mapping from ai to x, definition of q, and the role of ρ should be made explicit; currently difficult to follow.
- Lemma 4 gives only a sufficient condition for zero projection when ρ>0; discuss potential impact of using x=0 as a fallback on convergence in practice.
- Please provide at least a brief discussion of calibration/consistency of φk with top-k error.

Quality: The technical direction is promising; however, the presentation has enough gaps and several claims (extreme points, reductions) require either proof or correction. The empirical section, as written, does not allow proper assessment.

Clarity: Currently below NeurIPS standards due to formatting/notation issues and missing essentials in the main text.

Originality: Moderate. The top-k hinge and support-function representation are natural; the Δk conjugate and projection algorithm could be a contribution, but the relation to existing literature is underdeveloped.

Significance: Potentially useful in practice; however, the current submission does not convincingly demonstrate robustness, fairness, and scalability advantages due to insufficient experimental detail.

Pros
- Targets an important metric (top-k) with a principled convex surrogate.
- Clean dual formulation; practical SDCA implementation and stopping criterion.
- Projection algorithm onto a nonstandard convex set; potentially broadly useful.
- Empirical improvements indicated on multiple datasets.

Cons
- Anonymity violation.
- Numerous presentation/formatting errors and incomplete references.
- Insufficient proofs/justifications for key claims in the main text.
- Experimental reporting is incomplete; fairness and runtime not established.

Recommendation: Reject (but encourage resubmission after major revisions).

Confidence: 4/5.
