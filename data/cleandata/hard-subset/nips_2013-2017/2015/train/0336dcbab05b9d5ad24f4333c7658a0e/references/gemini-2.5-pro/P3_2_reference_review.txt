This paper proposes a novel convex loss function, termed the top-k hinge loss, for training multiclass Support Vector Machines (SVMs) to directly optimize top-k classification accuracy. The work is a direct extension of the well-known Crammer and Singer multiclass SVM. The core contributions are threefold: 1) the formulation of a new convex surrogate loss that is a tighter upper bound on the (non-convex) top-k 0-1 loss than a related loss derived from the framework of Usunier et al.; 2) the derivation of the Fenchel conjugate of this loss, which gives rise to a novel convex set the authors call the "top-k simplex"; and 3) an efficient algorithm for projection onto this top-k simplex, which enables the use of a state-of-the-art proximal stochastic dual coordinate ascent (Prox-SDCA) solver. The authors provide strong empirical validation on several image classification benchmarks, demonstrating consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over standard multiclass SVMs and other related methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant formulation:** The paper provides a clear and compelling motivation for moving beyond standard top-1 classification accuracy. The proposed top-k hinge loss, `φk(a) = max{0, 1/k * Σ(a+c)[j]}`, is an intuitive and elegant convex relaxation of the ideal (but non-convex) top-k loss. The authors do an excellent job of placing their work in context, particularly by comparing their formulation to a variant, `φ̃k`, derived from the general ranking loss framework of Usunier et al. [27]. The proof that their proposed loss `φk` is a strictly tighter bound on the top-k 0-1 loss is a significant theoretical strength.

2.  **Rigorous technical contributions:** The paper is technically sound and the derivations are thorough. The characterization of the Fenchel dual of the proposed loss, leading to the "top-k simplex" (∆k), is a novel and interesting result. This provides a clean geometric interpretation of the dual problem. The main algorithmic contribution—an efficient projection algorithm onto this top-k simplex—is non-trivial and essential for making the overall approach practical. The authors correctly identify that existing projection algorithms (e.g., for the standard simplex or CQKP) are not applicable due to the coupled upper bound constraint in ∆k. The proposed algorithm, which intelligently breaks the problem into a knapsack problem or a cone projection, is well-designed and its complexity is comparable to standard simplex projection.

3.  **Scalable and effective optimization:** The choice of the Prox-SDCA framework is appropriate and state-of-the-art for this class of problems. By developing the custom projection operator, the authors successfully integrate their novel loss into a powerful, scalable optimization engine with strong convergence guarantees. The paper also demonstrates careful attention to detail, for instance by noting and correcting a minor issue in the original Prox-SDCA formulation [24] related to the dual constraints.

4.  **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The experiments are extensive and convincing. The method is tested on five standard image classification datasets, including large-scale ones like Places and ImageNet, demonstrating its scalability. The choice of baselines is appropriate, including the standard multiclass SVM, one-vs-all, and competitive ranking-based methods like SVMPerf and TopPush. The results consistently show that optimizing for the top-k loss improves top-k accuracy, often significantly, across all datasets and values of k. The observation that the method can sometimes even improve top-1 accuracy is a strong and interesting finding. The public release of the code is a commendable step that will benefit the community.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited discussion on empirical differences between `φk` and `φ̃k`:** While the paper theoretically establishes that `φk` is a tighter bound than `φ̃k`, the experimental results for top-k SVMα (`φk`) and top-k SVMβ (`φ̃k`) are often very similar. It would strengthen the paper to include a more detailed analysis of these results. Under what conditions does the tighter bound of `φk` lead to a discernible practical advantage? A more in-depth discussion would help clarify the practical implications of this key theoretical contribution.

2.  **Clarity of the projection algorithm:** The description of the projection algorithm in Section 4 is quite dense. While the core ideas are present, a more intuitive explanation or a pseudo-code block for the final combined algorithm (in the main paper, not just the supplement) would improve clarity and readability for non-experts in constrained optimization. Specifically, the interplay between the knapsack solver and the cone projection based on the check in Lemma 5 could be more clearly articulated.

3.  **Broader impact and future work:** The conclusion is somewhat brief. The authors suggest generalizing the approach to the family of ranking losses from [27]. It would be beneficial to also briefly discuss the potential of applying the top-k hinge loss concept outside of SVMs, for example, as a loss function for training deep neural networks. While this is outside the scope of the current work's experiments, a short discussion would broaden the perceived significance of the contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically effective method for top-k multiclass classification. The contributions in loss function design, dual formulation, and optimization are significant and well-executed. The paper is clearly written, and its claims are strongly supported by rigorous theory and extensive experiments. It advances the state of the art in training linear classifiers for top-k performance. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to opportunities for deeper analysis and improved presentation. The paper is an excellent fit for NIPS and I recommend acceptance.
