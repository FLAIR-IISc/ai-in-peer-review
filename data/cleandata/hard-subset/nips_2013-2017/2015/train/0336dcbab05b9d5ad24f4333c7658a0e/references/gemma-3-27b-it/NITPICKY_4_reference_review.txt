The paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM loss function and its corresponding Fenchel dual, aiming to relax the penalty for errors outside the top-k predictions. The core idea is to replace the max-over-all-classes loss with a hinge loss based on the sum of the top-k scores. The authors derive the convex conjugate of this loss and present an optimization framework using Prox-SDCA. While the motivation is reasonable, and the extension to the top-k setting is conceptually sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor issues – some potentially more impactful than others – that collectively detract from its overall quality.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** The top-k accuracy metric is increasingly important in many applications, and addressing it directly within the SVM framework is a valid research direction.
*   **Mathematical Rigor:** The derivation of the conjugate function and the associated theoretical results are presented with a degree of mathematical precision.
*   **Scalability:** The authors claim, and demonstrate in the supplement, that the method scales to large datasets.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately positions its contribution relative to Crammer & Singer’s multiclass SVM and Usunier et al.’s ranking framework.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies/Errors:** Throughout the paper, there are inconsistencies in notation. For example, the definition of ∆k initially uses 'r' as a bound on the sum, then later, it's implicitly 1. There is a crucial difference between the initial definition of ∆k and its application later on (specifically in Proposition 2). The indexing within brackets (e.g., a[k]) is also used inconsistently; while somewhat understandable, it’s non-standard and could cause confusion.
*   **Lack of Detailed Explanation/Justification:** The motivation behind choosing the specific form of the top-k hinge loss (3) is not sufficiently elaborated. The authors mention that ψk is non-convex and propose φk as a convex upper bound. However, a more thorough discussion of *why* this particular upper bound was selected would be beneficial. Why not other convex relaxations?
*   **Overly Dense Equations & Formatting:** The equations are often crammed together without sufficient whitespace, making them difficult to parse.  The use of inline mathematical expressions in certain places, where displayed equations would be clearer, further compounds this issue. Equation (1) is particularly difficult to read.
*   **Redundancy/Unnecessary Detail:** The statement that thresholding in (2) is “redundant” feels like an afterthought and could be removed. Some parts of the discussion (e.g., the reduction to binary classification for SVMPerf) feel tangential and detract from the core contribution.
*   **Missing Details in the Optimization Section:** While the paper mentions Prox-SDCA, the specifics of the dual variable update (Line 9 in Algorithm 1) are relegated to the supplement. This is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. The derivation and justification of Proposition 4 need to be more clearly presented within the main paper.
*   **Experimental Setup:** While experiments are performed on several datasets, the hyperparameter tuning process is described too briefly. The statement about extending the range when the optimum lies on the boundary is vague. The reliance on pre-trained CNN features is standard but should be acknowledged as a potential limitation.
*    **Lemma 2 Typo:** The statement in Section 3.1 referring to a typo in [24] regarding equation (20) feels like shifting blame rather than addressing a potentially crucial point.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and potentially important problem.
*   The mathematical derivations, while dense, appear to be correct.
*   The experimental results demonstrate some improvement over baseline methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The numerous minor flaws in notation, clarity, and presentation significantly detract from the paper's quality.
*   The motivation and justification for the specific loss function formulation are not fully convincing.
*   Critical details of the optimization algorithm are relegated to the supplement.
*   The overall presentation lacks polish and rigor expected of a NIPS paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially valuable contribution, I recommend **rejection**. While the core idea is sound, the execution is marred by a multitude of small errors and omissions. The lack of clarity, inconsistent notation, and missing details make it difficult to fully assess the validity and significance of the work. Addressing these issues would substantially improve the paper, but in its current form, it falls short of the standards expected for NIPS publication. A major revision addressing all the points raised would be necessary for reconsideration. The paper feels rushed, and a more careful and polished presentation is required.
