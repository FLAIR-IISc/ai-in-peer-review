Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a convex surrogate for optimizing top-k accuracy in multiclass classification and an efficient optimization framework. Starting from Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM, the authors define a “top-k hinge loss” φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑j=1..k (a+c)[j]} where (a+c)[j] denotes the j-th largest coordinate; this is a convex upper bound on the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss and tightens the standard top-1 hinge in a way that relaxes penalties when the true class appears among the top-k scores. They derive the convex conjugate and show that the effective domain is a “top-k simplex” ∆k, a contraction of the standard simplex with adaptive upper bounds on coordinates, recovering the Crammer–Singer case at k=1. They connect this loss to Usunier et al.’s ranking family and contrast against the average-of-hinges variant φ̃k, arguing φk is a tighter upper bound.

On the optimization side, the paper derives the Fenchel dual for general `2-regularized multiclass problems under a j-compatibility condition and specializes it to the proposed loss. The dual block updates reduce to a (biased) Euclidean projection onto the top-k simplex ∆k(r), which is different from the standard simplex projection. The authors develop an algorithm for this projection by combining: (i) a reduction to a continuous quadratic knapsack problem when the sum constraint is tight; (ii) a new analysis for projections onto the “top-k cone” when it is not tight, involving a search over feasible partitions into active/saturated/zero sets with complexity O(d log d + k d). They embed this into a Prox-SDCA solver with a duality-gap stopping criterion and provide a rank-1 update scheme.

Empirically, they evaluate on five vision datasets (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN 397, Places 205, ImageNet 2012) using CNN features for the larger ones, comparing top-k SVMα (φk) and top-k SVMβ (φ̃k) against multiclass SVM, one-vs-all, SVMPerf (recall@k), TopPush, and Wsabie++. They report consistent improvements in top-k accuracy (e.g., +2.6% top-5 on Caltech with top-10 SVMα), sometimes also improving top-1. They release code.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: top-k metrics are widely used in large-label problems; top-1 surrogates can be suboptimal.
- Novel convex surrogate: φk is a principled tightening between the zero-one top-k loss and the standard multiclass hinge; clean conjugate with a natural geometric object (top-k simplex).
- Optimization contribution: reduction of the dual update to a biased projection onto ∆k(r) and an algorithmic solution with complexity comparable to standard simplex projection; integration in Prox-SDCA with a duality gap.
- Solid theoretical development: conjugates, compatibility condition, dual derivation, and conditions distinguishing knapsack vs cone cases are carefully laid out (with proofs deferred).
- Practical relevance and scale: experiments on large-scale datasets and release of efficient C++ code are valuable to practitioners.
- Comparisons include both ranking-based reductions and multiclass baselines; ablation comparing φk vs φ̃k.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dependence on supplemental material: many key proofs and algorithmic details (e.g., the exhaustive search over partitions, complexity constants, and correctness guarantees for the biased case ρ>0) are in the supplement. A concise but self-contained description would help.
- Projection algorithm worst-case behavior: the O(k d) loop over partitions may be heavy for large m; empirical timings vs high-quality CQKP/simplex projectors are only said to be “comparable” (details pushed to supplement). Please include per-iteration wall-clock and scaling plots in the main text.
- Experimental reporting could be stronger: 
  - No statistical significance or variance across folds is reported.
  - Limited discussion of sensitivity to k and λ, and of the trade-off between top-1 and top-k under varying k.
  - Wall-clock training time and convergence curves vs multiclass SVM and SGD methods would strengthen the case for scalability.
  - The largest-dataset results are in the supplement; a brief summary table in the main paper would aid the reader.
- Baseline fairness: for ranking methods that do not scale with the multiclass-to-binary reduction, one-vs-all variants are used; this might bias against them. Please clarify memory/time constraints and ensure comparable feature representations and regularization/tuning.
- Relation to other surrogates: the paper situates φk relative to Usunier et al. and SVMPerf, but it would help to discuss how φk compares to other convex top-k surrogates (e.g., smooth top-k hinge, probabilistic n-choose-k models) in tightness and optimization complexity.
- Notational issues and clarity: the paper is dense; some notation (e.g., use of c=1−e_yi, definition of Hj, and reuse of symbols like t,t′,u) could be streamlined. Several references lack years; there are typos and inconsistent section numbering.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the convexity of φk and its conjugate characterization via ∆k are plausible and well-motivated; the dual derivation builds on established Prox-SDCA machinery. The projection algorithm is reasonable and its case analysis is convincing. The empirical results support the main claim of improved top-k accuracy with modest trade-offs on top-1. However, stronger empirical rigor (significance, time/accuracy trade-offs, ablations) would improve the quality.

Clarity
Generally clear but dense. The main ideas are understandable, but presentation can be improved by: adding a running example to illustrate ∆k; giving pseudocode for the projection; moving some essential details from the supplement; fixing typos and reference formatting.

Originality
The top-k hinge φk and the top-k simplex conjugate are, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. The projection algorithm onto ∆k(r) and the j-compatibility-based dual derivation are also novel combinations of known tools. The work clearly differentiates from prior ranking-based and multiclass surrogates.

Significance
High practical significance for large-class problems where top-k metrics matter. The method is scalable, provides a principled convex objective, and shows consistent empirical gains. The projection routine may be of independent interest.

Pros
- New convex top-k surrogate with clean dual geometry (∆k).
- Efficient Prox-SDCA optimization with duality-gap stopping.
- Scalable implementation and code release.
- Consistent top-k accuracy improvements on multiple datasets.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on supplement; projection algorithm details and empirical timing should be more prominent.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity, trade-offs, and training time.
- Some baseline comparisons may be handicapped; fairness needs clearer justification.
- Clarity/notation and reference formatting issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add pseudocode and a concise proof sketch for the projection, plus per-iteration timing vs simplex projection on synthetic and real distributions.
- Report training time, number of passes, and duality gap curves for key datasets.
- Include sensitivity plots over k and λ, and a discussion of when top-1 accuracy degrades or improves.
- Summarize large-scale results in the main text and add variance or confidence intervals where feasible.
- Clarify the role and benefit of the bias term ρ>0; is ρ=1 essential in practice?
- Fix typos, missing years, and section numbering.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a principled and useful contribution with both theoretical and practical value, and the empirical evidence is promising. Strengthening the experimental reporting and clarifying the projection algorithm would make it an excellent fit.

Reviewer confidence
High on optimization and multiclass losses; medium on completeness of empirical assessment due to reliance on the supplement.
