Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a convex, margin-based surrogate for top-k multiclass accuracy and an associated large-scale optimization method. Starting from the Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM, the authors define a “top-k hinge loss” φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑j=1..k (a + c)[j]}, where (·)[j] denotes the j-th largest component and c = 1 − e_yi. This loss is a convex upper bound to the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss and is tighter than the ordered hinge losses of Usunier et al. (ICML’09) for the specific top-k weighting. A key contribution is the characterization of the convex conjugate φk* as the support function of a new “top-k simplex” ∆k, a contraction of the standard simplex with the additional constraint xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1, x⟩. This yields a clean Fenchel dual and enables optimization via Prox-SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang).

The dual updates reduce to (biased) Euclidean projections onto ∆k(r). Because the upper bound depends on the sum of coordinates (coupling), this is not a standard continuous quadratic knapsack problem. The paper develops an algorithm for biased projection onto the “top-k cone” (the simplex without the sum constraint) and shows when the problem reduces to a classic knapsack instance. The method runs in O(d log d + kd) time per projection. Experiments on five vision datasets (Caltech Silhouettes, MIT Indoor, SUN397, Places205, ImageNet’12) report consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over Crammer-Singer and ranking-based baselines (SVMPerf, TopPush, Wsabie++), using precomputed CNN features.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a simple, interpretable top-k surrogate that directly averages the top-k violators, with clear inequalities relating it to top-1 hinge and Usunier’s ordered hinge.
- Dual characterization: the introduction of the top-k simplex and the conjugate derivation are elegant and technically useful. The result neatly generalizes the standard simplex domain used by Crammer-Singer (recovered at k=1).
- Optimization: a principled Prox-SDCA instantiation with a joint m-dimensional update and a duality-gap stopping rule; kernelization is straightforward.
- Projection algorithm: addresses a nontrivial projection with coupled upper bounds; the analysis (lemmas on cases, feasibility conditions, and the λ certificate deciding knapsack vs cone) is novel and of independent interest.
- Empirical relevance: top-k metrics are standard in multi-class vision; results suggest consistent gains in top-k without severe computational overhead; code is released.

Weaknesses
- Experimental depth: improvements, while consistent, are modest on some datasets (e.g., ~1–3% top-5). Statistical significance (variance across splits or seeds) is not reported. The trade-off with top-1 is not systematically analyzed.
- Comparisons: φk vs φ̃k (Usunier-weighted ordered hinge) are compared, but a more thorough empirical and theoretical discussion of when the tighter bound φk matters would strengthen the case. A comparison to cross-entropy (softmax) classifiers’ top-k performance would contextualize gains against the de facto baseline in vision.
- Scalability details: the projection worst-case O(d log d + kd) can be large when k is a significant fraction of m. Practical runtimes and memory vs. Crammer-Singer on the largest datasets should be more fully documented in the main paper (currently deferred to the supplement).
- Clarity/typos: several typographical issues (misnumbered sections “.2”, broken references, incomplete years) and some notational density (reuse of c, a, constraints) hinder readability. Algorithm 1 omits the explicit stopping threshold symbol and the role of ρ, which is central to the “biased” projection.

Quality
Overall technically solid. The convexity, conjugate, and Fenchel dual derivations are consistent with prior literature. The projection algorithm is justified via case analyses and feasibility conditions; while some proofs are in the supplement, the main ideas are credible. The optimization setup with Prox-SDCA is standard and appropriate. Experiments cover multiple datasets with competitive baselines; however, stronger evidence on runtime, robustness, and statistical significance would improve confidence.

Clarity
The core narrative is coherent, but presentation can be tightened:
- Define all symbols upon first use (e.g., c, a, K, Ki), and avoid reintroducing notations midstream.
- Clearly state ρ’s role and its value in practice (Proposition 4 uses ρ=1).
- Provide concise pseudocode for the projection routine (even if detailed proofs are in the supplement).
- Fix section numbering and bibliographic entries, and ensure all cited works have complete references.
- Summarize in the main text the key runtime benchmarks from the supplement.

Originality
- The loss φk as a convex top-k average hinge is a natural but, to my knowledge, novel surrogate in the multiclass SVM framework; the relation to Usunier’s ordered weighted hinge is well-situated, and the “tighter bound” observation is valuable.
- The top-k simplex and its use as the effective domain of the conjugate appear original and conceptually neat.
- The projection algorithm addressing coupled caps xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1, x⟩ and the biased case is, to my knowledge, new and practically useful.

Significance
Top-k metrics are important in large-class recognition. A convex, scalable surrogate with a principled optimizer is likely to be adopted in practice, especially given code release. The projection technique could be reused in other structured loss settings that involve sums of top-k components and related polytopes. While deep nets dominate vision, this method is applicable to linear/kernel models and as a potentially useful layer-wise surrogate.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you provide calibration/consistency results for φk with respect to top-k risk (even partial)? Are there known negative results?
- Sensitivity to the training k: does training at k’ generalize to evaluation at other ks? Any schedule over k?
- How often does the projection fall into the knapsack vs cone case on real data, and how does this impact runtime?
- Could the projection be accelerated to O(d log d) independent of k via parametric search over thresholds?
- Please report mean±std over multiple splits and include ablations on λ, k, and ρ.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled objective aligned with top-k metric
- Elegant dual and new geometric object (top-k simplex)
- Novel and useful projection algorithm
- Solid optimizer with duality-gap stopping
- Consistent empirical gains; code available

Cons against acceptance
- Modest empirical improvements and limited statistical analysis
- Missing comparisons to strong softmax baselines for top-k
- Some clarity and presentation issues; key runtime details in supplement
- Worst-case projection complexity grows with k

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on clarifying presentation, adding brief runtime/statistical evidence in the main text, and tightening comparisons to φ̃k and cross-entropy baselines.
