Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a convex, scalable surrogate for top-k multiclass accuracy and an associated optimization framework. The key elements are:
- Loss: A top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑j=1..k (a + c)[j]}, i.e., the hinge on the average of the k largest class-margin violations. This tightly upper-bounds the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss ψk and is always no worse than the Crammer-Singer hinge (k=1).
- Dual/conjugate: The convex conjugate of φk is the indicator of a new “top-k simplex” ∆k = {x : ⟨1,x⟩ ≤ 1, 0 ≤ xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩}, which contracts the standard simplex. The paper derives a primal–dual pair and shows yi-compatibility to apply Fenchel duality in a multiclass setting.
- Optimization: A Prox-SDCA solver that reduces each dual update to a (biased) Euclidean projection onto ∆k(r). The authors analyze when the projection reduces to a continuous quadratic knapsack problem vs. a novel “top-k cone” projection, and give an O(d log d + k d) algorithm with practical speed.
- Relation to ranking losses: They position φk against Usunier et al.’s ordered-weighted hinge Lβ [27], showing φk ≤ φ̃k for the β with uniform weights on top-k positions and providing an exact Prox-SDCA optimization route for φ̃k as well.
- Empirics: On several vision benchmarks (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN397, and in the supplement Places205, ImageNet), top-k SVM variants improve top-k accuracy over Crammer-Singer SVM and several ranking baselines (SVMPerf, TopPush, Wsabie/W++), with code released.

Relation to prior work
- Crammer-Singer [5] provides the multiclass hinge that this work generalizes. The proposed φk is a Ky Fan–type extension (sum of k largest coordinates) adapted to multiclass margins.
- Ranking-based surrogates (Usunier et al. [27], Wsabie [28], and structural SVM for recall@k [11]) target related metrics but either lack instance-wise decomposability or require reductions. This paper’s φk is instance-decomposable, tighter than φ̃k in the ambiguous margin region ((a+c)[1] > 0 > (a+c)[k]), and amenable to efficient SDCA.
- The “top-k simplex” characterization and the associated projection algorithm appear novel; existing simplex/CQKP projection methods cannot handle the variable upper bound xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩ and the bias term.

Strengths
- Clear, principled surrogate with a compelling geometric dual: the top-k simplex. The conjugate neatly unifies k=1 (Crammer-Singer) and general k.
- Solid optimization design: derivation of the dual via yi-compatibility, efficient per-example block updates in Prox-SDCA, and careful treatment of the projection subproblem (including special cases that reduce to CQKP).
- Practical scalability with strong convergence guarantees, a clear stopping criterion (duality gap), and kernelizability.
- Empirical improvements on standard top-k metrics across datasets, with code provided. The comparison to φ̃k (Usunier-style) is informative and supports the tightness claim.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Theory beyond convexity: No statistical analysis (calibration/consistency for top-k risk) is provided. Since the goal is to optimize a non-decomposable discrete metric, even partial results (e.g., surrogate calibration under mild noise conditions) would strengthen the contribution.
- Projection algorithm details: The general-case search over partitions U, M, L is described as exhaustive with worst-case O(k d) passes after sorting. A more explicit complexity analysis, tightness of the bound, and empirical ablation comparing against advanced CQKP solvers on large d would increase confidence. Are there monotonicity properties enabling faster threshold search (e.g., two-parameter root finding) instead of exhaustive enumeration?
- Experimental scope in the main paper: Most large-scale results and runtime comparisons are relegated to the supplement. In the main text, report at least one large-scale table with wall-clock times, epochs to target gap, and memory usage vs. baselines.
- Baselines: The absence of multinomial logistic regression / softmax (with top-k evaluation) is notable, given its dominance in top-k performance with CNN features. A linear softmax baseline trained with SGD or LBFGS would contextualize the gains. Also include one-vs-all logistic.
- Training k vs. evaluation k: The main-text highlight compares top-5 evaluation for a model trained with k=10 vs. k=1. While this shows flexibility, please also report matched settings (train k=5, eval top-5) to disentangle whether increasing training k beyond the evaluation k is advantageous.
- Fairness of ranking reductions: The binary reduction scales mn negatives and may disadvantage some ranking baselines. Where reduction is infeasible and one-vs-all substitutes are used, discuss the potential bias, and if possible, include methods implemented natively for multiclass.
- Clarity: Some notation is dense. A small illustrative example showing φk and ψk on a 4-class score vector would help intuition. The role and value of the bias parameter ρ in the projection should be summarized in the main text when it first appears (ρ=1 in the algorithm; ρ=0 corresponds to Euclidean projection).
- References: Several entries lack years/page ranges; please fix. Also consider adding related convex top-k/ordered statistics literature (Ky Fan norms, SLOPE-like constraints) to contextualize the sum-of-k-largest convexity.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The dual derivations and conjugates are coherent; the top-k simplex is well-motivated; the optimization strategy is appropriate. Missing are statistical guarantees and a more thorough complexity/runtime evaluation in the main text.

Clarity
Generally well-written, but dense. Improving exposition around the projection algorithm (pseudocode, schematic of U/M/L cases) and adding a running example would help. Fix reference metadata and a few typographical issues.

Originality
The loss, its conjugate/top-k simplex, and the tailored projection algorithm appear novel and nontrivial. The Prox-SDCA application is a thoughtful adaptation rather than a trivial reuse.

Significance
Top-k metrics are central in multiclass recognition; providing a convex, scalable surrogate and open-source implementation is valuable. With stronger baselines and more visible large-scale results, impact would be high.

Questions for the authors
- Do you have any surrogate calibration or consistency results for φk with respect to top-k error?
- How sensitive are results to ktrain relative to keval? Is ktrain > keval consistently better?
- Can you provide ablations comparing the projection algorithm variants (CQKP vs. cone) and timing as d and k vary?
- How does top-k SVM compare to linear softmax on the same features?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled convex surrogate targeting a widely used evaluation metric.
- Clean dual characterization and new projection algorithm with practical performance.
- Scalable implementation with consistent empirical gains and released code.

Cons for acceptance
- No statistical guarantees; limited theoretical analysis beyond convexity/duality.
- Important baselines (softmax) and comprehensive runtime tables are missing from the main text.
- Some exposition and reference polishing needed.

Overall
A strong and timely contribution with solid methodology and practical relevance. With minor additions to experiments and clarity, this would be a valuable NeurIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance.
