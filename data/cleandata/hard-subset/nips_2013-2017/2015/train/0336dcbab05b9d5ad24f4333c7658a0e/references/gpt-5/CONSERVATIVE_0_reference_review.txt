Summary
The paper proposes a convex surrogate for top-k multiclass classification, building on the Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM. The central idea is a “top-k hinge” loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) sum of the k largest components of a+c}, which is a tighter convex upper bound to the top-k zero-one loss than the more standard average of the k largest hinge terms φ̃k. Via Fenchel duality the authors derive a conjugate characterized by a “top-k simplex” ∆k, a contraction of the standard simplex with per-coordinate upper bounds proportional to the sum of coordinates. They adapt Prox-SDCA to optimize the resulting dual, where each dual block update reduces to a (biased) projection onto ∆k(r). A main technical component is an algorithm for efficient projection onto ∆k(r), which differs from classical simplex/CQKP due to the coupling upper bound. Experiments on several image classification datasets (Caltech Silhouettes, MIT Indoor, SUN397, Places205, ImageNet) using CNN features show consistently improved top-k accuracy over Crammer-Singer and several ranking baselines, with code promised.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of multiclass hinge to top-k with a principled convex surrogate that is provably tighter than a known alternative (φ̃k).
- Clear dual analysis yielding a geometrically interpretable constraint set (top-k simplex), which seems of independent interest.
- Integration into Prox-SDCA is natural; block updates are optimal within a batch, and the approach admits a gap-based stopping criterion and kernelization.
- Projection algorithm for ∆k(r) addresses a non-standard constraint (upper bound coupled to the sum), and the decomposition into knapsack vs cone cases is insightful.
- Broad empirical scope with large-scale datasets and comparisons to several baselines; consistent top-k gains.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond convexity: no discussion of statistical consistency/calibration for top-k error, Lipschitz/smoothness constants, or generalization bounds. Given the aim to optimize top-k metrics, some form of top-k calibration result would strengthen the contribution.
- The projection algorithm’s complexity is not tightly characterized. The proposed exhaustive search over partitions in the cone case can take up to O((k−1)(d−k+1)) iterations after sorting; the claim of practical O(d log d + kd) needs empirical support and, ideally, worst-case bounds or pruning guarantees.
- Experimental evidence, while broad, shows modest gains (often ~1–3% top-5) and sometimes worsens top-1. The practical significance of these trade-offs is not fully analyzed. There is no statistical testing or error bars.
- Baselines: the omission of multinomial logistic regression (softmax) is notable, given its prevalence and strong top-k performance with linear CNN features. Some ranking baselines are run in one-vs-all form due to scalability, which weakens the comparison.
- Clarity and presentation issues: several sections rely on the supplement for proofs and key implementation details (exact update for φ̃k, time/memory costs on the largest datasets). Citations are inconsistent/incomplete; there are typos and section numbering glitches. The algorithmic description of the biased projection (ρ > 0) is intricate; a clearer, self-contained pseudocode and complexity discussion would help.
- Reproducibility: details such as stopping criteria (value of the duality gap threshold), cross-validation protocol (splits, grid sizes per dataset), and runtime/memory comparisons vs baselines are sparse in the main text.

Quality
Technically sound at the optimization level, with correct use of Fenchel duality and a reasonable projection algorithm. However, the lack of theoretical properties connecting φk to the discrete top-k risk, and the absence of tighter complexity analysis for the projection step, are weaknesses. Experimental design covers many datasets but lacks some key baselines, statistical rigor, and ablations (e.g., φk vs φ̃k vs Crammer-Singer under matched training time; sensitivity to k; generalization to other k at test time).

Clarity
Readable but dense. Important parts are deferred to the supplement, and the projection section would benefit from clearer exposition and a concise algorithm box. Several references have missing years and a few typos disrupt flow. Figures/tables summarizing runtime and convergence would improve clarity.

Originality
Moderate. The notion of averaging the top-k scores is known to be convex, and φ̃k has appeared in prior ranking work. The specific φk surrogate and its conjugate/top-k simplex appear novel and non-trivial, as does the tailored projection algorithm. The Prox-SDCA adaptation is incremental.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners optimizing top-k metrics, especially with linear models and precomputed features. However, the empirical gains are modest and the missing comparisons to strong baselines limit the demonstrated impact.

Questions and requests for the authors
- Can you provide theoretical results on top-k calibration/consistency of φk (even in simplified settings), or at least discuss when minimizing φk is expected to reduce top-k error?
- Tighten the complexity analysis of the projection, including worst-case bounds and empirical timing vs standard simplex projection across d and k.
- Add multinomial logistic regression as a baseline; report top-k performance and training time.
- Report wall-clock times and memory for all methods on each dataset, including per-iteration cost and convergence rates (duality gap curves).
- Clarify the stopping criterion (value of ) and whether all methods were tuned with comparable effort. Include variance/error bars and statistical tests.
- Provide a direct comparison between φk and φ̃k in both accuracy and training time across k and datasets.
- Fix citations and provide complete bibliographic details.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, convex surrogate specific to top-k with a neat dual geometry.
- Practical optimizer with dual gap and a tailored projection that seems to scale.
- Broad empirical evaluation with consistent top-k gains; code promised.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing theoretical link to top-k risk; no calibration or generalization guarantees.
- Projection algorithm’s worst-case behavior and practical overhead insufficiently analyzed.
- Baseline coverage incomplete (no softmax), modest gains, and limited statistical rigor.
- Heavy reliance on supplement for crucial proofs/details; clarity issues.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper has solid ideas and could be impactful, but I would like to see stronger theoretical justification for φk vis-à-vis top-k error, tighter analysis and clearer presentation of the projection step, and more comprehensive, statistically grounded experiments including softmax and runtime comparisons.
