Summary
The paper proposes a principled multiclass SVM surrogate tailored to top-k error. The key idea is a convex “top-k hinge” loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) ∑j=1..k (a+c)[j]}, which upper-bounds the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss and is tighter than the sum of top-k one-vs-rest hinges used in prior ranking-based surrogates. The authors derive the Fenchel conjugate of φk and introduce the “top-k simplex” Δk, a contraction of the standard simplex that naturally arises as the effective domain of the conjugate. They then formulate an `2-regularized multiclass optimization framework, derive the dual via a j-compatibility device, and propose a Prox-SDCA solver where each coordinate ascent step reduces to a (biased) Euclidean projection onto Δk(r). The main technical contribution on the optimization side is an efficient projection algorithm onto Δk(r), which reduces to a continuous quadratic knapsack when the sum constraint is tight and otherwise to a projection onto a “top-k cone,” handled via thresholding conditions and a bounded search over partitions. Experiments on several image classification benchmarks (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN397, Places205, ImageNet) show consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over Crammer-Singer and ranking baselines; code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Extends Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM (hinge on max-margin violation) to a top-k-aware convex surrogate.
- Closely related to Usunier et al.’s ordered weighted ranking losses Lβ: the paper shows φk is a strictly tighter upper bound than the natural choice with βj = 1/k for j≤k (denoted φ̃k), except in edge cases.
- Compares against SVM^perf (Joachims) and TopPush via a multiclass-to-binary reduction; also discusses recall@k upper bounds not decomposable into instance-wise losses.
- Uses Prox-SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang) but departs in the proximal map and dual constraints; fixes a known typo in [24] and formalizes “j-compatibility”.

Strengths
- Conceptual: The Δk polytope and its role as the effective domain of the conjugate are elegant. The representation φk(a) = max⟨a+c, λ⟩ s.t. λ ∈ Δk clarifies geometry and connects directly to optimization.
- Technical: Derivation of the conjugate, dual, and the reduction of SDCA updates to a biased projection onto Δk(r) are sound and well-motivated. The projection algorithm is nontrivial because the upper bounds couple variables; the proposed case analysis (knapsack vs cone) and thresholds-based characterization are insightful.
- Practical: Prox-SDCA with rank-1 updates and a principled duality-gap stopping rule is a good choice for nonsmooth losses. The per-iteration operations map to BLAS; kernelization is possible in principle.
- Empirical: Demonstrates consistent gains in top-k metrics, sometimes also improving top-1. Scalability to million-example datasets and code release increase impact.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Computational complexity details: The per-example SDCA update requires sorting scores (O(m log m)) plus up to O(kd) search in the cone case. With m up to 1000 this may be fine, but for larger m the cost could dominate; tighter bounds than “(k−1)(d−k+1) iterations in worst case” would be useful, as well as empirical breakdowns (time in sorting vs projection) and sensitivity to k and m. Are there amortization tricks (e.g., partial sorting/select) that preserve optimality?
- Empirical coverage and fairness: Some baselines (SVM^perf, TopPush) do not scale under the reduction and are replaced by one-vs-all variants, which may bias the comparison. There is no comparison to multinomial logistic/softmax (one-vs-rest logistic at minimum), which is a strong baseline for top-k on CNN features. Reported results lack standard deviations/confidence intervals and runtime/convergence plots. More detail on hyperparameter tuning parity across methods is needed.
- Choice of k for training vs evaluation: The paper reports improvements in top-5 when training with top-10. This is interesting—please provide a systematic study of how training k influences performance at different evaluation k’s. Is there a principled choice (e.g., train with k slightly above evaluation k)?
- Theoretical properties: While φk is convex and tighter than φ̃k, calibration for top-k consistency is not discussed. Are there conditions under which minimizing φk yields Bayes-optimal top-k decisions? Later literature touches on this; a brief discussion would strengthen the paper.
- Clarity and presentation: Some notation is heavy and deferred proofs are essential to follow the projection algorithm. The “biased projection” parameter ρ is introduced but its role and choice (ρ=1 vs 0) could be clarified earlier. A pseudocode box for the projection routine would aid reproducibility. Several references are incomplete (years missing; malformed ImageNet citation) and there are minor typos.

Quality
Technically solid overall. The convex analysis and SDCA dualization are correct and appropriately referenced. The projection algorithm appears correct given the lemmas, though a clearer worst-case complexity and empirical profiling are needed. Experimental evidence supports the top-k claims but could be strengthened with broader baselines and statistical reporting.

Clarity
Generally well-written but dense in places. Improving exposition around Δk, providing intuition for the constraints xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩, and including a concise algorithmic summary for the projection would help. Fix citation formatting.

Originality
High. The φk loss and Δk simplex are novel, as is the exact, efficient proximal operator enabling an SDCA solver. The comparison to ranking surrogates and the tightness result relative to φ̃k are valuable.

Significance
High. Top-k metrics are ubiquitous in modern recognition. A convex, tighter surrogate with a scalable solver and open-source implementation is likely to be used and extended.

Recommendations and questions for the authors
- Provide per-iteration and end-to-end runtime comparisons, with breakdowns for sorting/projection, and scaling curves in m and k. Can partial sorting/select plus lazy updates reduce cost?
- Add baselines: multinomial logistic regression (softmax), one-vs-rest logistic, and, if possible, a deep linear readout trained with cross-entropy, to contextualize top-k gains.
- Include a study on train-k vs test-k and sensitivity to λ. Does training with larger k systematically help smaller-k evaluation?
- Discuss top-k calibration: any consistency guarantees for φk? If not, outline conditions or references.
- Clarify the role and selection of ρ in biased projection; if always ρ=1 in your SDCA, say so prominently.
- Improve reference formatting and fix minor typos; add pseudocode for the projection.
