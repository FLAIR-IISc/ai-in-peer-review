The paper proposes a top-k multiclass Support Vector Machine (SVM) that relaxes the penalty for errors in the top-k predictions. The authors introduce a convex upper bound, termed the top-k hinge loss, for a direct but non-convex extension of the multiclass SVM loss. They derive the Fenchel conjugate of this loss and propose an efficient optimization framework based on Prox-SDCA, including an algorithm for projection onto the top-k simplex. The method is evaluated on five image classification datasets, demonstrating improved top-k accuracy compared to standard multiclass SVMs and other ranking-based methods.

This work builds upon the multiclass SVM formulation by Crammer and Singer [5] and the Prox-SDCA optimization framework by Shalev-Shwartz and Zhang [24]. It also connects to recent work on ranking losses by Usunier et al. [27].  The contribution lies in the adaptation of SVMs to directly optimize for top-k accuracy, a common metric in information retrieval and machine learning, and providing a computationally efficient solution.  Prior work often reduces multiclass classification to a series of binary problems or employs approximations for ranking-based losses. This paper offers a direct, convex optimization approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the top-k hinge loss and its conjugate appears mathematically sound, and the proposed optimization framework leveraging Prox-SDCA is well-justified. The analysis of the top-k simplex is interesting and contributes a novel geometric object.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the key concepts are explained clearly. The connection to previous work is well established.
* **Originality:** The formulation of a top-k hinge loss and its associated optimization procedure represents a novel approach to multiclass classification.  While ranking losses have been explored, adapting the SVM framework directly to optimize for top-k accuracy is a significant contribution.
* **Significance:**  Optimizing for top-k accuracy is a relevant and important problem, particularly in applications where the order of predictions matters. The demonstrated improvements in top-k accuracy on multiple datasets suggest the practical utility of the proposed method. The scalability to large datasets (Places205, ImageNet) is also noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Justification for the Top-k Hinge Loss:** While the authors motivate the top-k hinge loss as a convex upper bound, the justification for *why* this particular upper bound is effective is somewhat limited. A more detailed analysis of the approximation error or the impact of the slack introduced by the upper bound would strengthen the paper.
* **Projection Algorithm Complexity:** The discussion of the projection algorithm onto the top-k simplex, while thorough, describes a potentially complex algorithm, especially for large `d`. While the authors claim linear scaling, a more detailed complexity analysis and comparison to existing projection methods would be beneficial. The assertion that it's comparable to [13] needs more support.
* **Empirical Evaluation Scope:** The experimental results primarily focus on image classification.  Demonstrating the effectiveness of the method on other types of data (e.g., text classification, recommendation systems) would broaden the applicability and impact of the work.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons to relevant methods are included, some of the baselines (e.g., SVMPerf, TopPush) are not always state-of-the-art.  Comparing to more recent and powerful ranking/multiclass classification methods would provide a more rigorous evaluation.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to optimizing for top-k accuracy.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Demonstrated improvements in top-k accuracy on multiple datasets.
* Scalability to large datasets.
* Potential for significant impact in applications where top-k predictions are crucial.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited theoretical justification for the chosen top-k hinge loss.
* Potentially complex projection algorithm.
* Scope of empirical evaluation is somewhat limited.
* Comparisons to stronger baselines could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and technically sound approach to multiclass classification. The proposed top-k SVM offers a compelling alternative to existing methods, particularly in scenarios where top-k accuracy is paramount. While some aspects could be strengthened (e.g., theoretical justification, projection algorithm complexity analysis, broader empirical evaluation), the overall contribution is significant and warrants acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above.
