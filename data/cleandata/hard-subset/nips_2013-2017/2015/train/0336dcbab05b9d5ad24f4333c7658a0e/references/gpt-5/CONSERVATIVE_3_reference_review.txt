Summary
The paper proposes a “top-k multiclass SVM” that replaces the standard multiclass hinge with a top-k hinge surrogate. The key idea is to relax penalties when the true class lies within the top-k predicted scores. The authors define a convex upper bound ψk ≤ φk ≤ φ (with φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑j=1..k (a+c)[j]}) and derive its Fenchel conjugate, introducing the “top-k simplex” Δk as the effective domain. This leads to a Prox-SDCA optimization scheme where each dual update reduces to a regularized projection onto Δk(r). The paper provides derivations (mainly in the supplement), an algorithm for (biased) projection onto Δk(r), and empirical results on several image classification datasets, showing consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over the Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM and comparisons to ranking-based baselines (SVMPerf, TopPush, Wsabie-like).

Relation to prior work: The formulation connects to Usunier et al.’s Lβ family of ordered weighted hinge/ranking losses [27], showing that choosing βj = 1/k for j ≤ k yields a related surrogate φ̃k; φk is argued to be a tighter upper bound than φ̃k. The optimization framework builds on Prox-SDCA [24]. The paper also discusses structured SVM for multivariate performance measures [11] and multiclass-to-binary reductions for ranking approaches.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of a top-k hinge surrogate that interpolates between standard multiclass SVM (k=1) and more lenient top-k metrics; the conjugate and the Δk polytope are elegant and connect nicely to Fenchel duality.
- Prox-SDCA instantiation is natural and leverages strong existing theory; the dual update as a projection onto Δk(r) is principled.
- The projection problem is non-trivial due to the coupling xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩; the paper provides an algorithm and complexity considerations (O(d log d + kd)), with discussion of the biased case (ρ>0).
- Empirically shows consistent top-k gains on several datasets using widely adopted CNN features; code release is a plus.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/rigor:
- Key technical components (proofs of Propositions/Lemmas, correctness and completeness of the projection algorithm, numerical stability) are deferred to the supplement. The main text outlines an exhaustive search over partitions for the cone case; without the full proofs and a more careful complexity/degeneracy analysis in the main paper, it is hard to assess robustness. For m up to 1000, O(d log d + kd) is plausible, but worst-case behavior and constants matter in large-scale settings.
- No statistical consistency or calibration result is provided for φk with respect to top-k error. Since φk is proposed as a surrogate, some discussion of classification calibration or at least margin-based generalization bounds would strengthen the theoretical claims.
- The experimental evidence focuses on linear models with fixed deep features. Stronger baselines (e.g., softmax cross-entropy trained linear classifiers on the same features; modern multiclass logistic with tuned label smoothing; structured SVM for top-k where feasible) are missing. The comparison to ranking methods reverts to one-vs-all when reductions don’t scale, which could bias results.
- Reported gains on top-k are modest (often 1–3%) and sometimes accompanied by decreases in top-1. There is no report of variance, confidence intervals, or multiple runs; it is unclear whether gains exceed tuning noise.
- Claims of scalability to Places205/ImageNet are relegated to the supplement; timing/memory profiles for the projection step vs. standard simplex projection are not shown in the main text.

Clarity:
- The paper is dense and occasionally difficult to follow: numerous cross-references to the supplement, some typos and formatting issues, and notational overload (e.g., switching between k-indexed order statistics and set partitions U,M,L) impede readability. The “yi-compatibility” notion is specialized; more intuition would help.
- The relation to Lβ is acknowledged, but the conceptual distinction between φk and φ̃k (beyond “tighter”) could be better motivated with synthetic examples and decision boundary illustrations.

Originality:
- The sum-of-top-k convex functions are known [17], and ordered weighted hinge losses are well-studied [27]. The main novelty here is the explicit top-k multiclass hinge surrogate φk, its conjugate onto Δk, and the tailored projection algorithm. This is an incremental but nontrivial contribution.
- The Prox-SDCA adaptation itself is standard; the value-add is the Δk projection.

Significance:
- Top-k metrics are important in vision. However, given the relatively small improvements over a strong multiclass SVM baseline and the absence of comparisons to widely used multinomial logistic losses on the same features, it is hard to assess practical impact. Without statistical guarantees, the methodological advance alone may be too incremental for NeurIPS.

Suggestions for improvement
- Move at least one central proof (e.g., Proposition 2’s conjugate and the correctness of the projection algorithm with complexity bounds) into the main paper. Provide tighter runtime and memory benchmarks for the projection vs. standard simplex projection on realistic m.
- Add experiments comparing against multinomial logistic regression (softmax) with the same features and careful tuning, as well as Crammer-Singer trained with state-of-the-art solvers. Report standard deviations over multiple splits.
- Analyze sensitivity to k at training vs. evaluation (train with k_train and test various k_test), and ablation of the bias term ρ.
- Provide some theoretical discussion on consistency/calibration for top-k error, or at least Lipschitzness/strong convexity properties to support convergence and generalization claims.
- Clarify the relationship and differences to Lβ beyond the inequality φk ≤ φ̃k, including when the gap matters in practice.

Pros
- Well-motivated problem (top-k metrics), clean convex surrogate and duality.
- Principled optimization via Prox-SDCA with a novel projection subroutine.
- Code release; empirical gains across several datasets.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on supplement for technical correctness.
- Missing strong baselines and statistical rigor in experiments; small gains.
- Limited theoretical guarantees about the surrogate’s statistical properties.
- Clarity/organization can be improved.

Recommendation: Weak reject
While the idea is appealing and the formulation is neat, the paper falls short on empirical rigor and on providing enough technical detail in the main text to fully assess the contribution. Strengthening comparisons, moving key proofs/algorithms into the paper, and adding theoretical analysis would likely elevate it.

Confidence: High
