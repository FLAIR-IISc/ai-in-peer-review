Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a convex, scalable formulation for optimizing top-k performance in multiclass classification. Starting from the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM loss φ(a) = max{0, (a + c)[1]}, the authors motivate the top-k zero-one objective and show that the direct surrogate ψk(a) = max{0, (a + c)[k]} is nonconvex for k ≥ 2. They introduce a new convex upper bound, the top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) ∑j=1..k (a + c)[j]}, i.e., the hinge on the average of the k largest “margin violations.” They analyze its conjugate and identify the “top-k simplex” ∆k, a contraction of the standard simplex: ∆k(r) = {x : ⟨1, x⟩ ≤ r, 0 ≤ xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩}. They show φk*(b) = −⟨c,b⟩ if b ∈ ∆k, +∞ otherwise, leading to a compact dual.

They relate φk to the ordered-weighted hinge family of Usunier et al. (ICML’09), showing their φk is a strictly tighter upper bound than the simple average of top-k hinge violations φ̃k used in ranking-based approaches (LaRank, WSABIE, etc.) except at the extremes. Optimization is carried out via Prox-SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz and Zhang), with each dual block update reducing to a (biased) projection onto ∆k(r). This projection is not a standard continuous quadratic knapsack problem (CQKP) because the upper bound couples coordinates, so the authors derive new projection algorithms: (i) a knapsack case when the sum constraint is tight; (ii) a top-k cone projection when it is not, characterized by thresholds (t,u) and index sets (U,M,L) with closed-form consistency conditions. They prove correctness (lemmas and a case analysis) and provide an O(d log d + kd) method.

Empirically, they evaluate on five image datasets (Caltech101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor-67, SUN397, Places205, ImageNet’12) using CNN features and linear classifiers, comparing to Crammer–Singer (top-1), φ̃k (ranking-based), SVMPerf Recall@k, TopPush, and one-vs-all baselines. They report consistent improvements in top-k accuracy, sometimes also improving top-1. Code is released.

Quality
- Theoretical development appears sound: convexity of the sum of k largest components is well known; the conjugate derivation leading to ∆k is elegant and consistent; the “j-compatibility” condition and its use in the Fenchel dual have been handled carefully (and the paper identifies and corrects a subtlety in [24]). The reduction of the SDCA update to a projection with a bias term is well motivated.
- The projection algorithm is a central technical contribution; the split into knapsack vs top-k cone and the sufficient/necessary conditions are clearly stated. While complete proofs are in the supplement, the main ideas are conveyed with enough detail to assess correctness.
- Experiments cover diverse datasets and show consistent gains on the intended metric. Hyperparameter search is described and reasonable baselines are included, though some large-scale comparisons are deferred to the supplement.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but generally clear and well organized. The introduction of ∆k and its geometry is helpful. The SDCA section clearly states assumptions (yi-compatibility), derives the dual, and explains the update.
- Some notational glitches remain (e.g., stray punctuation, missing tolerance symbol in Algorithm 1, occasional formatting like “.2” section headers). Including a concise pseudo-code for the projection routine in the main text would aid reproducibility. A brief intuition for the top-k cone thresholds and the role of the bias term ρ would help non-experts.

Originality
- The top-k hinge φk is a simple yet nontrivial surrogate that is provably a tighter convex upper bound to top-k error than ordered-weighted hinge averages commonly used in ranking-based approaches.
- The identification of the top-k simplex as the conjugate effective domain and the associated biased projection algorithm appear novel. This geometric view cleanly generalizes the standard simplex used by Crammer–Singer (k=1).
- The integration into Prox-SDCA with exact block updates is principled and, to my knowledge, new for this specific loss.

Significance
- Optimizing top-k is practically important in high-class-count settings (vision, NLP). A convex, scalable method with exact updates is valuable.
- The projection routine could be reused beyond this paper where ∆k-like constraints arise.
- The empirical improvements are consistent, though modest in some cases; still, the method is simple, efficient, and likely to be adopted.

Strengths
- Well-motivated loss with clear theoretical properties and a tighter bound than prior ranking surrogates.
- Elegant convex-analytic treatment yielding a compact dual and a natural geometric object (∆k).
- Practical, scalable optimization via Prox-SDCA with exact multi-coordinate updates.
- New projection algorithm for a nonstandard coupled-constraint simplex.
- Solid experimental coverage on relevant datasets; code release.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Comparison set: While ranking baselines are included, comparisons to standard cross-entropy softmax (multinomial logistic regression) trained on the same features (and reporting top-k) would strengthen the empirical case, as softmax is the dominant multiclass loss in vision.
- Evaluation design: Some large-scale results are in the supplement; including at least one large-scale table in the main text would help assess scalability claims. Reporting wall-clock time and SDCA epochs to convergence, and a runtime breakdown of the projection vs other steps, would quantify efficiency.
- Choice of k: The paper sometimes trains with k different from the evaluation metric (e.g., top-10 loss improving top-5 accuracy). A short analysis or guidance on how training k influences performance across multiple evaluation k’s would be useful.
- Theory: Beyond convexity and conjugates, any generalization or calibration guarantees for φk w.r.t. top-k error would add depth. Even negative results or discussion of consistency would be informative.
- Clarity: Provide pseudo-code for the projection, and clarify the role of ρ (why ρ=1 arises and its effect). Fix minor typos and define all symbols locally (e.g., c, e_yi) at first use.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report top-k results for multinomial logistic regression on the same features? How does φk compare in accuracy and training time?
- How sensitive is performance to λ and to the choice of k during training? Do you observe that training with k slightly larger than the evaluation k is consistently beneficial?
- Does the projection step ever become a bottleneck for large m? Any opportunities for further acceleration (e.g., partial sorting, warm starts across iterations)?
- Do your methods extend straightforwardly to kernels in practice, and what is the empirical behavior then?

Overall recommendation
This is a strong paper: the contribution is conceptually clean, technically solid, and practically relevant. I recommend acceptance.

Confidence
High: I am familiar with multiclass SVMs, convex analysis, and SDCA, and I carefully checked the main derivations and experimental setup.
