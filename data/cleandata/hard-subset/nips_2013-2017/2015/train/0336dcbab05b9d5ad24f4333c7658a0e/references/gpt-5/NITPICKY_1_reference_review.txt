Summary
The paper proposes a convex surrogate for top-k multiclass classification. Starting from the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM loss φ(a) = max{0, (a+c)[1]}, the authors define a nonconvex top-k surrogate ψk(a) = max{0, (a+c)[k]} and then introduce a convex upper bound, the top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑_{j=1}^k (a+c)[j]}, i.e., the average of the top-k scores. They derive its convex conjugate and identify a “top-k simplex” ∆k = {x | ⟨1,x⟩ ≤ 1, 0 ≤ xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩}, showing φk*(b) = −⟨c,b⟩ if b ∈ ∆k, +∞ otherwise. They then develop an optimization method via Prox-SDCA with a dual update that reduces to a (biased) Euclidean projection onto ∆k(r), r = 1/(λn). They give algorithms for this projection by decomposing into cases: when the sum constraint is tight (reducing to a CQKP) and otherwise reducing to a “top-k cone” projection solved via sorting and a bounded search over partitions. They compare to ranking-based losses and an alternative top-k hinge loss φ̃k (averaging k individual hinge terms), show φk ≤ φ̃k ≤ φ (and note equality conditions), and present experimental results on several image datasets, reporting consistent improvements in top-k accuracy.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM (JMLR 2001) and Prox-SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang).
- Connects to the ranking loss family of Usunier et al., and to Joachims’ structured SVM approach for multivariate metrics (Recall@k).
- Addresses computational aspects through projection algorithms; compares to CQKP literature (Kiwiel; Patriksson & Strömberg).

Strengths
- Clear conceptual extension: a tight, convex top-k upper bound that is strictly better than averaging k individual hinges except in boundary cases.
- Neat conjugacy characterization yielding the “top-k simplex,” which is a genuine new object and provides a clean dual view.
- Reduction of dual updates to a projection problem with a careful algorithmic treatment; worst-case complexity O(m log m + km) is reasonable for moderate numbers of classes.
- Practical algorithm within Prox-SDCA, well-motivated stopping via duality gap, and claims of scalability; code release is a plus.
- Empirical results on multiple datasets indicating consistent gains in top-k metrics; discussion that optimizing top-k can trade off top-1 vs. top-k aligns with intuition.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Technical correctness and completeness
- Many proofs are deferred to the supplement. For a theory-heavy contribution (new conjugate, projection algorithm), the main text should at least outline key proof ideas (e.g., how the upper-bound constraint xi ≤ ⟨1,x⟩/k emerges from Fenchel duality). Proposition 2 is central; a sketch would help assess correctness.
- The “j-compatibility” definition is somewhat ad hoc; while Lemma 2 is plausible, the paper should justify why standard losses (including φk) satisfy it beyond a brief claim (Proposition 3). A short derivation would help.
- Proposition 4 requires more detail: the chain from the Prox-SDCA update to the biased projection minx ||b − x||^2 + ρ⟨1,x⟩^2 over ∆k(r) is terse. Please explicitly show how constraints 〈1,ai〉 = 0 and the yi-coordinate elimination produce the stated form.
- The projection onto the top-k cone: Lemmas 3–5 are key. The exhaustive search over partitions U, M, L is justified by bounds, but a more explicit argument on correctness and tightness of the feasibility conditions (7) would increase confidence. Also, the worst-case iteration count (k−1)(d−k+1) with d overloaded (see below) needs clarification with m (number of classes).
- Statistical aspects are missing: is φk classification-calibrated for top-k error? Does optimizing φk minimize top-k risk in the limit? Even a brief discussion or negative result would situate the surrogate’s theoretical desirability.

Clarity and organization
- Notation overload: d denotes feature dimension earlier, but Section 4 uses d to denote the dimensionality for projections (which is m, the number of classes, in this context). This is confusing; please use a distinct symbol (e.g., m) consistently in Section 4 and in complexity statements.
- Several section headings begin with “.2” or “.3” without the leading number; there are apparent typesetting/copy errors (e.g., “/3 1/2 /2 1/3 /2 /3” around Figure 2; missing tolerance symbol in Algorithm 1 “until relative duality gap is below ”; footnote “1” for effective domain is dangling).
- The “Note that thresholding with 0 in φ(a) is actually redundant...” argument for k=1 should be stated carefully (max_j (a+c)[j] ≥ (a+c)_{yi} = 0 implies max≥0). For general k, the thresholding is not redundant; please avoid suggesting otherwise.
- Symbols like w[1],...,w[k] are nonstandard: do these denote the class weight vectors corresponding to the top-k scores? Please formalize the sorting/indexing operator consistently.

Experimental evaluation
- Missing essential details: train/validation/test splits for each dataset, number of trials, standard deviations, and precise hyperparameter selection protocol (grid, metric optimized during CV). The description “extend range when optimal is at boundary” is vague.
- Baselines: comparison to multinomial logistic regression/softmax (arguably the dominant multiclass baseline with top-k metrics) is missing. Given the focus on top-k, this is a significant omission. Also, no results for deep fine-tuning vs. frozen features are discussed.
- Scalability claims would benefit from wall-clock times, memory usage, and per-epoch complexity as a function of n, m, and d. For the largest datasets, main-text results are missing (pushed to supplement); please at least summarize top-line numbers in the paper.
- It is unclear how ranking baselines were handled when multiclass-to-binary reduction was infeasible; the fallback to one-vs-all may be unfair, and should be clearly marked in tables and discussed.
- The choice of k in training vs. evaluation: You mention “top-10 SVMα compared to top-1 SVMα gives +2.6% top-5.” Is training with k larger than the evaluation k always best? Some ablation across training k vs. evaluation k would be informative.

Originality and significance
- The top-k hinge loss φk and its conjugate/top-k simplex are novel and interesting; the projection algorithm addresses a nontrivial constraint coupling. The connection to ranking losses is appropriate and positions the work relative to Usunier et al.
- Practical significance depends on broad adoption; to that end, stronger comparisons (e.g., to softmax, to calibrated surrogates) and clearer scalability evidence would help. As is, the improvements reported are promising but not fully substantiated in the main text.

Formatting and reference issues (non-exhaustive)
- Numerous references lack years, venue details, and have malformed author lists (e.g., [22] “and L, O. Russakovsky, … Fei-Fei.”). Several arXiv-only cites might need proper versions. Ensure all citations are complete and consistent.
- Figure 2’s caption claims (m choose k)+1 vertices; please verify and, if true, provide a brief argument or reference.
- Footnotes are inconsistently placed (e.g., “effective domain1”); ensure all footnotes are rendered properly.
- Equation numbering is inconsistent; some cross-references (e.g., “(3)”, “(5)”) are fine, others seem to be missing.
- Minor typos: “We have repeated the derivation … as there is a typo … leading to the conclusion that ayi,i must be 0” should specify the exact correction precisely.

Pros
- Conceptually clean and novel convex surrogate for top-k with a tightness argument relative to φ̃k and φ.
- New dual view via top-k simplex; principled Prox-SDCA formulation.
- Careful attention to the projection subproblem; algorithmic contribution beyond vanilla simplex.
- Broad empirical scope and code release.

Cons
- Significant clarity/notation and formatting issues hinder readability.
- Key proofs and algorithmic justifications are deferred; limited proof sketches in main text.
- Experimental section lacks critical details, stronger baselines, and in-paper large-scale results; fairness of some comparisons is unclear.
- No discussion of statistical consistency/calibration for top-k.

Recommendation: Reject (as-is)
The paper has strong ideas and potentially impactful tools, but the accumulation of clarity/formatting issues, missing experimental details/baselines, and limited in-text theoretical exposition makes it below the NeurIPS bar in its current form. I encourage the authors to revise thoroughly: fix presentation, provide proof sketches for core results, add solid baselines (softmax), include comprehensive experimental details and runtime/memory metrics, and discuss statistical properties of φk. With these addressed, the work could be a strong contribution. Confidence: high.
