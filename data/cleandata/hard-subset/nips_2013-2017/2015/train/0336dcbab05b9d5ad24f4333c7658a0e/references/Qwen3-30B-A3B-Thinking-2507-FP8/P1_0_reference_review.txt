

# Peer Review: Top-k Multiclass SVM

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel extension of the standard multiclass SVM (Crammer and Singer) to optimize for top-k accuracy rather than just top-1 accuracy. The authors introduce a top-k hinge loss function φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑_{j=1}^k (a+c)[j]}, which serves as a convex upper bound on the non-convex top-k zero-one loss. They derive the convex conjugate of this loss, leading to the definition of the top-k simplex ∆k as the effective domain of the conjugate. The authors also develop an efficient algorithm for projecting onto the top-k simplex, which enables the use of Prox-SDCA for optimization. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments on five image classification datasets that their approach consistently improves top-k accuracy compared to standard multiclass SVM, and sometimes even improves top-1 accuracy.

This work builds directly on the Crammer and Singer multiclass SVM [5] and connects to the broader family of ranking-based losses [27], while addressing a practical need for optimization of top-k metrics in multiclass classification.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors provide a complete derivation of the convex conjugate for their proposed loss function, which is crucial for optimization. The proof of Proposition 2 establishing the primal-conjugate pair for the top-k hinge loss is rigorous and correctly references the necessary lemmas and results from the supplement.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering five image classification datasets of varying scales (from Caltech 101 with 4100 examples to ImageNet 2012 with 1.28 million examples). The results consistently show improvements in top-k accuracy across all datasets and k values, with clear comparisons to the standard multiclass SVM (which corresponds to top-1) and other baseline methods like SVMPerf and TopPush.

The paper appropriately acknowledges the limitations of their approach, noting that optimizing for top-k may sometimes decrease top-1 accuracy (e.g., on MIT Indoor 67), though it can also increase top-1 accuracy on larger datasets. The authors also discuss the relationship between their proposed top-k hinge loss (3) and the alternative version (5) from Usunier et al., showing that (3) is a better upper bound.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written, logically organized, and clearly explains the key concepts. The notation is consistent throughout, and the mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient detail. The introduction of the top-k simplex and its relationship to the standard simplex is well-motivated and illustrated with Figure 2. The description of the efficient projection algorithm is detailed, with clear statements of the main lemmas and propositions.

The experimental setup is described clearly, including the datasets, features used, and comparison methods. The results are presented in a straightforward manner in Table 1, with specific numerical improvements (e.g., +2.6% top-5 accuracy on Caltech 101 with top-10 SVMα).

One minor suggestion: the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of why optimizing for top-k accuracy sometimes improves top-1 accuracy, as this is an interesting observation that isn't fully explained.

## Originality

The core contribution of the paper—the top-k hinge loss function and its associated convex conjugate—is novel. While the concept of top-k classification has been explored before, the authors provide a proper convex relaxation that can be efficiently optimized, which is a significant technical contribution. The derivation of the top-k simplex and the efficient projection algorithm are also original.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in two key ways:
1) It directly extends the Crammer and Singer multiclass SVM framework rather than approaching top-k classification as a ranking problem.
2) It provides a tighter convex upper bound on the top-k zero-one loss compared to existing approaches (specifically, their loss (3) is strictly better than loss (5) from Usunier et al. in most cases).

The authors appropriately reference related work, including Crammer and Singer [5], Usunier et al. [27], and the ranking-based methods SVMPerf [11] and TopPush [16].

## Significance

The significance of this work is strong, as top-k accuracy is an important metric in many real-world applications, particularly in image classification with large numbers of classes (e.g., ImageNet with 1000 classes). The paper demonstrates that optimizing for top-k accuracy leads to consistent improvements across multiple datasets, with the potential to improve top-1 accuracy on larger datasets as well.

The method is scalable to large datasets (up to millions of examples), which is crucial for practical applications. The authors have released a C++ implementation with a MATLAB interface, which will facilitate adoption by the community.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the state of the art by providing a principled approach to optimizing for top-k metrics, rather than relying on heuristic or indirect methods.

## Strengths

- Novel and well-motivated extension of multiclass SVM to optimize for top-k accuracy
- Rigorous theoretical derivation of the convex conjugate and top-k simplex
- Efficient projection algorithm with clear complexity analysis
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets of varying sizes
- Consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across all experiments
- Scalable implementation for large-scale problems
- Clear connection to existing work in multiclass classification and ranking

## Weaknesses

- The paper could provide a more detailed comparison between the two versions of the top-k hinge loss (3) and (5), especially regarding which one performs better in practice.
- The explanation of why top-1 accuracy sometimes improves when optimizing for top-k is limited; a more thorough theoretical analysis would strengthen the paper.
- While the paper mentions that their approach is more scalable than the method in [11], it would be helpful to provide more concrete timing comparisons.
- The experimental section could include more analysis of the effect of different k values on the performance.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of multiclass classification by providing a principled approach to optimizing for top-k accuracy. The theoretical development is sound, the experimental results are convincing, and the implementation is efficient and scalable. The paper addresses an important practical problem with a novel technical solution that advances the state of the art.

**Recommendation: Accept**

## Final Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of multiclass classification. The authors successfully extend the standard multiclass SVM to optimize for top-k accuracy, providing both a theoretical foundation and an efficient optimization algorithm. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k performance across multiple datasets, with the potential to improve top-1 accuracy in some cases. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the literature. I believe this paper would be of interest to the NeurIPS community and would provide a useful addition to the conference proceedings.
