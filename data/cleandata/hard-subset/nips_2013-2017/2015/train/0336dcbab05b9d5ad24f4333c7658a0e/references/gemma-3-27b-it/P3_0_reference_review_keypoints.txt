- Overall: Strong accept — novel, technically sound extension of multiclass SVM to top-k with empirical gains.  
- Problem: Addresses multiclass classification where top-k predictions are sufficient and less costly than single-label errors.  
- Main contribution: Proposes a convex “top-k hinge loss” φk as an upper bound to a non-convex top-k loss and derives its Fenchel dual.  
- Optimization: Develops dual formulation and uses Prox-SDCA for efficient large-scale optimization.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on Crammer & Singer multiclass SVM and connects to ranking-based losses (e.g., Usunier et al.).  
- Novelty: The convex relaxation, duality theory, and the “top-k simplex” (∆k) are novel contributions.  
- Theoretical soundness: Rigorous derivations (Propositions 1–2, Theorem 1) and convex conjugate analysis.  
- Practicality/scalability: Use of Prox-SDCA and experiments on Places205 and ImageNet demonstrate scalability.  
- Empirical results: Consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over standard multiclass SVM and comparisons with ranking methods.  
- Writing and presentation: Clear, well-written, consistent notation, with proofs in the supplement.  
- Weakness — generalization: Limited theoretical analysis of generalization properties for the top-k SVM.  
- Weakness — hyperparameters: Insufficient analysis of sensitivity to regularization parameter λ and hyperparameter tuning details.  
- Weakness — ranking connection: Needs deeper exploration of when top-k hinge loss outperforms other ranking losses.  
- Weakness — projection complexity: Projection algorithms presented but complexity analysis could be more detailed.  
- Detailed comment: Suggest visualizing ψk(a) vs φk(a) to illustrate the relaxation gap.  
- Detailed comment: Provide a more intuitive explanation for the regularized projection derivation (Proposition 4).  
- Detailed comment: Clarify why standard knapsack algorithms can’t be directly used due to xi upper bounds.  
- Detailed comment: Report training computation times on large datasets to better characterize scalability.  
- Pro-accept arguments: Novel/rigorous theory, efficient implementation, empirical improvements, clear presentation.  
- Con-accept arguments: Limited generalization theory, incomplete hyperparameter sensitivity study, need for deeper ranking-loss analysis.
