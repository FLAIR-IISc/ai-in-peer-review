The paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM loss function and its corresponding Fenchel conjugate, aiming to relax the penalty for errors outside the top-k predictions. The core idea is to introduce a convex upper bound to a non-convex function arising from direct extension of the standard multiclass SVM loss. The authors present theoretical results regarding the primal-conjugate pair and demonstrate scalability and improved top-k accuracy on image classification datasets.

While the motivation for relaxing the penalty for errors outside the top-k predictions is reasonable and potentially valuable, and the theoretical derivations are generally sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor issues that, cumulatively, detract from its overall quality. The presentation is often imprecise, lacks sufficient detail, and occasionally contains inconsistencies. 

Specifically:

* **Notation and Definitions:** The notation is inconsistent. For example, ∆ is initially defined as {x ∈ Rm | 〈1, x〉 ≤ 1, 0 ≤ xi, i = 1, . . . ,m}, then later ∆k(r) is defined with a different constraint. This inconsistency is confusing. Definition 1 of ∆k is unclear without referring back to the initial definition of ∆.
* **Proposition 1:** The note stating the thresholding in φ(a) is redundant is misleading. While true for the *specific* loss function, stating it as a general property enhances similarity to the top-k version is a weak justification and doesn't add to the technical understanding.
* **Justification for Convex Relaxation:** The argument for choosing the proposed convex upper bound φk(a) lacks a strong justification. The authors state it's a "convex upper bound," but a more compelling explanation for *why* this particular upper bound was chosen over others, and a discussion of its tightness, would be valuable.
* **Section 2.3 (Relation to Ranking Losses):** The connection to Usunier et al. [27] is presented without sufficient context. The reader unfamiliar with [27] is left wondering why this connection is important. The statement "φk(a) ≤ φ̃k(a) holds with equality if (a+ c)[1] ≤ 0 or (a+ c)[k] ≥ 0" needs clarification; it's not immediately obvious why this is significant.
* **Theorem 1:** The statement about fixing a typo in [24] is presented as a significant contribution, but the explanation is inadequate. A clearer explanation of the original typo and how it’s corrected is necessary. The condition "〈1, ai〉 = 0 ∀i" in the dual objective seems overly restrictive and requires more explanation.
* **Section 3.2.1 (Dual Variable Update):** The explanation of the connection to the continuous quadratic knapsack problem (CQKP) is convoluted. The authors mention a runtime complexity of O(d log(d) + kd), but fail to mention the complexity of the chosen CQKP algorithm [13], hindering the assessment of the overall computational cost.
* **Experimental Details:** The experimental section lacks crucial details. For instance, the choice of CNN features is briefly mentioned, but the specific architecture and pre-training dataset are not always provided. The hyperparameter search range is stated, but not the search strategy (e.g., grid search, random search). The statement that results for Places 205 and ImageNet are in the supplement is insufficient – a summary of the key results should be included in the main paper. 
* **Tables and Figures:** Figure 2 is somewhat helpful, but its visual quality is poor. Table 1 lacks proper captioning and context; it is unclear what metrics are reported, and which method represents the baseline.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* **Numerous Minor Flaws:** The accumulation of minor inconsistencies, imprecise explanations, and missing details significantly detracts from the paper’s quality.
* **Lack of Depth:** While the core idea is reasonable, the paper lacks a deep investigation into the properties of the proposed loss function and its relationship to existing methods.
* **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The lack of detail in the experimental section makes it difficult to assess the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed top-k hinge loss and its Fenchel conjugate are novel contributions.
* **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate the scalability of the proposed method to large datasets.
* **Empirical Improvement:** The experimental results show improvements in top-k accuracy, although the magnitude of these improvements is often modest.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite presenting a novel idea and demonstrating some empirical improvements, the numerous minor flaws and lack of depth make this paper unsuitable for NIPS. The presentation needs significant refinement, and a more thorough investigation of the theoretical and practical implications of the proposed method is required. I recommend **rejection**. The paper has potential, but requires substantial revision to meet the standards of NIPS.
