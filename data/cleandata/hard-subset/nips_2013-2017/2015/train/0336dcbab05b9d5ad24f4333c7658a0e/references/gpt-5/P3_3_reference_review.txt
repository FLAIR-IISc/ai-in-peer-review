Summary
The paper proposes a convex top-k surrogate for multiclass classification and an efficient large-scale optimization scheme. Starting from the Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM, the authors define the top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) ∑_{j=1}^k (a+c)[j]}, i.e., the average of the k largest margins relative to the true class. This is a tight convex upper bound of the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss ψk and generally tighter than the ordered-hinge average φ̃k due to Usunier et al. They derive the convex conjugate of φk and identify its effective domain as a novel “top-k simplex” Δk, a contraction of the standard simplex with coupling constraints 0 ≤ xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩. Using Fenchel duality, they formulate a Prox-SDCA solver where each dual block update reduces to a (biased) Euclidean projection onto Δk(r). They develop an algorithm for this projection by decomposing into two regimes: (i) when the sum constraint is tight, reducing to a continuous quadratic knapsack with fixed upper bound r/k; (ii) otherwise, projection onto a “top-k cone” solved via thresholding over sets U, M, L with O(d log d + k d) complexity. Experiments on several image classification datasets with CNN features show consistent gains in top-k accuracy over Crammer-Singer and ranking-based baselines, with code released.

Strengths
- Clear and principled construction of a convex surrogate that directly targets top-k error, strictly tighter than prior ordered-hinge–type surrogates in a nontrivial regime.
- Elegant dual derivation: the top-k simplex Δk provides a clean conjugate characterization and unifies the analysis. This contributes a reusable tool beyond this specific model.
- Solid optimization framework: Prox-SDCA with exact block updates, a computable duality gap, and a carefully derived projection algorithm. The runtime per update is comparable to simplex projection.
- Empirical evidence across multiple datasets, including large-scale settings, demonstrates improved top-k accuracy and scalability.
- Relation to ranking-based losses and structured SVM for recall@k is discussed, with a fair argument about scalability and decomposability.

Weaknesses
- The theoretical section stops short of statistical consistency or calibration results for top-k error. A discussion of when φk is top-k calibrated would strengthen the contribution.
- Experimental reporting in the main paper is thin: large-scale numbers are deferred to the supplement, and ablation on k (train vs. test) and sensitivity to λ are not systematically analyzed.
- Baselines focus on SVM variants and ranking surrogates; multinomial logistic regression (softmax) or calibrated probabilistic models, strong in top-k metrics, are missing as baselines (even with fixed features).
- The projection algorithm’s correctness and edge cases rely on the supplement; clearer intuition and a compact proof sketch in the main text would help. Complexity O(d log d + k d) can still be a concern for very large m; profiling per-epoch cost vs. LibLinear would be informative.
- Some notational/typo issues (e.g., Proposition 4 definitions, incomplete references) and limited discussion on numerical stability, warm-starting across k, and kernelization practicality.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The convex analysis and dual derivation are coherent, and the Δk domain is well-motivated. The projection algorithm is nontrivial and appears correct, though key proofs are deferred. The Prox-SDCA adaptation is standard but executed carefully, with an optimal joint block update and a principled stopping criterion. Claims about tightness of φk vs. φ̃k are supported analytically and empirically.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The motivation, loss definitions, duality setup, and algorithmic steps are clear. However, the projection section is dense; a higher-level roadmap, an illustrative 1D/2D example with U/M/L evolution, and a brief proof sketch would improve readability. Some references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted. A concise description of hyperparameter search and compute budget would aid reproducibility.

Originality
The specific top-k hinge loss as the average of top-k margins and its conjugate characterization via the top-k simplex appear novel in this form. The connection to Δk and the specialized projection algorithm seem original and practically important. The relation to Usunier’s ordered hinge family is properly acknowledged, and the paper clarifies when φk is strictly tighter. The use of Prox-SDCA is not novel per se, but the integration with the new loss and projection is.

Significance
Top-k metrics are crucial in modern multiclass vision tasks. A convex, tight, and scalable surrogate with a practical solver is impactful. The Δk geometry and projection method may be useful in other structured surrogates (e.g., Ky Fan-type objectives, ordered weighted losses). The empirical gains are moderate but consistent, suggesting practical utility.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide calibration/consistency results for φk with respect to top-k error (even partial, e.g., under margin/noise conditions)? If not, a discussion of known barriers/conditions would help.
- How should k for training be chosen when the evaluation is at a different k′? You mention top-10 training helps top-5. A grid of train k vs. test k would be insightful.
- Please add softmax (multinomial logistic regression) baseline on fixed features; it is a strong and relevant comparison for top-k accuracy.
- Include per-epoch timing and projections/sec as m grows; how does the O(d log d + k d) projection compare to standard simplex projection empirically?
- Clarify Proposition 4 notation (b, q, scaling by ⟨xi,xi⟩) and ensure dimensions are correct. A compact derivation sketch would help.
- The top-k sum is a Ky Fan functional; citing that literature and connecting Δk to known polytopes (e.g., base polytopes of uniform matroids) could strengthen the convex analysis context.

Minor comments
- Several references are missing years and details; please fix. Check typos around Section numbering (e.g., “.2”, “.3”) and punctuation.
- State the Lipschitz constant of φk and whether your SDCA tuning exploits it.
- Briefly discuss numerical stability when top-k scores are tied.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper provides a well-motivated, technically solid convex surrogate for top-k error with a scalable solver and consistent empirical gains. Strengthening theoretical calibration and expanding baselines would further increase impact, but the current contribution is valuable and of interest to the NeurIPS community. Confidence: high.
