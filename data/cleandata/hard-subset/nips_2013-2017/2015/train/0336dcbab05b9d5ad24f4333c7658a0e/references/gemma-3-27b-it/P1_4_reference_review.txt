## Peer Review for "Top-k Multiclass SVM"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically sound and well-motivated extension of the multiclass SVM to a top-k setting. The authors identify a practical relaxation of the standard multiclass SVM loss, derive its Fenchel conjugate, and propose an efficient optimization scheme based on Prox-SDCA. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across several datasets. While the core idea is relatively simple, the technical details – particularly the derivation of the conjugate and the efficient projection onto the top-k simplex – are carefully handled and contribute to the value of the work. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of multiclass classification where the goal is to predict a set of *k* most likely classes, rather than a single best class.  The authors observe that the standard multiclass SVM penalizes errors across all classes equally, which is overly strict when only the top-k predictions matter. They propose a “top-k hinge loss” which relaxes the penalty for errors outside the top-k predictions.  This is achieved by upper-bounding a natural but non-convex extension of the multiclass SVM loss with a convex surrogate. The resulting formulation allows for efficient optimization using Fenchel duality and Prox-SDCA.

The work builds directly on the multiclass SVM formulation of Crammer and Singer [5] and the optimization framework of Shalev-Shwartz and Zhang [24]. It also connects to recent work on ranking losses by Usunier et al. [27], showing how their general framework can be specialized to the top-k setting. The paper smartly leverages existing techniques and carefully analyzes their applicability within the new context.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the Fenchel conjugate for the proposed top-k hinge loss is rigorous and well-explained. The authors correctly identify and address the non-convexity issues in a direct extension of the standard loss.
* **Technical Contribution:** The introduction of the top-k simplex (∆k) and the development of an efficient projection algorithm onto it are significant technical contributions.  The authors convincingly demonstrate that existing simplex projection algorithms are not directly applicable and provide a viable solution.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed—top-k classification—is highly relevant in many applications (e.g., image retrieval, recommendation systems).
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on multiple datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The consistent improvements in top-k accuracy, especially on larger datasets, are encouraging.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the technical details.
* **Implementation Available:** Providing a C++ library with a Matlab interface significantly increases the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:** While the technical details are strong, the core idea of relaxing the loss function for top-k prediction is intuitive and somewhat straightforward.  The paper doesn't necessarily *discover* a new fundamental principle, but rather provides a rigorous and efficient implementation of a sensible extension.
* **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons are made to existing methods, the choice of baselines (e.g., SVMPerf, TopPush) is not always ideal.  A more direct comparison to state-of-the-art ranking or multi-label classification methods could strengthen the results.
* **Discussion of Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions cross-validation for hyperparameters, but lacks a detailed discussion of how sensitive the results are to the choice of *k* and the regularization parameter λ. A more thorough analysis of these parameters would be valuable.
* **Minor Typographical Errors:** A few minor typographical errors and stylistic inconsistencies were observed throughout the manuscript.

**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 2.2:** The motivation for choosing the convex upper bound φk(a) is clear, but a more intuitive explanation of *why* this specific upper bound works well would be helpful.
* **Section 3.2.1:** The notation in Proposition 4 is a bit dense.  Clarifying the relationship between *b* and the training data *xi* would improve readability.
* **Section 4.1:** The discussion of the continuous quadratic knapsack problem is well-explained, but it would be useful to provide a reference to a readily available implementation of the algorithm chosen [13].
* **Section 5:**  The results in the supplement for Places 205 and ImageNet 2012 are essential.  However, presenting these results directly in the main body of the paper would enhance the impact.
* **Experimentation:** Could the authors analyze the trade-off between top-1 and top-k accuracy more extensively?  For example, is there a value of *k* beyond which top-1 accuracy consistently decreases?



**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and technical contributions.
* Consistent empirical improvements in top-k accuracy.
* Well-written and clearly organized.
* Implementation available for reproducibility.
* Addresses a relevant and practical problem.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited novelty of the core idea.
* Could benefit from a more thorough comparison to stronger baselines and a more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity.

**6. Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Provide a more intuitive explanation for the choice of the convex upper bound φk(a).
2.  Expand the discussion of parameter sensitivity, especially regarding the choice of *k*.
3.  Consider including the results from the supplement in the main body of the paper.
4.  Proofread carefully for any remaining typographical errors.
