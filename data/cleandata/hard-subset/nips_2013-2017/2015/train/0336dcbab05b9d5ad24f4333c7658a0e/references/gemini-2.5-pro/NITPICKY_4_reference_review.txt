This paper proposes a "Top-k Multiclass SVM," a novel extension of the well-known multiclass SVM by Crammer and Singer [5]. The central idea is to replace the standard hinge loss with a "top-k hinge loss," which penalizes a prediction only if the ground truth class is not ranked among the top k scores. The authors introduce a specific convex upper bound on the (non-convex) top-k 0-1 loss, defined as the average of the top k scores. A key theoretical contribution is the derivation of the Fenchel conjugate of this loss, which reveals a novel geometric structure the authors term the "top-k simplex." This formulation allows the problem to be solved efficiently within the proximal stochastic dual coordinate ascent (Prox-SDCA) framework. A significant part of the work is dedicated to developing a novel, efficient algorithm for the Euclidean projection onto this top-k simplex, which is a non-trivial subproblem. The method is evaluated on several image classification benchmarks, including large-scale datasets, where it shows consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over the standard multiclass SVM and other related methods.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the highly relevant problem of optimizing directly for top-k performance, which is a more practical evaluation metric than top-1 accuracy for many real-world, large-scale classification tasks.
*   The proposed formulation is theoretically elegant. The derivation of the top-k hinge loss as a convex surrogate and its dual relationship with the novel "top-k simplex" provides a clean and natural generalization of the standard multiclass SVM.
*   The analysis comparing the proposed loss `φk` with a related loss `φ̃k` derived from the work of Usunier et al. [27] is insightful, demonstrating that `φk` is a strictly tighter upper bound on the top-k 0-1 loss under certain conditions.
*   The development of an efficient projection algorithm onto the top-k simplex is a solid and non-trivial technical contribution that could be of independent interest to the optimization community.
*   The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating the scalability and effectiveness of the proposed method on five datasets, including large ones like Places 205 and ImageNet. The consistent improvements in top-k accuracy validate the paper's core hypothesis.
*   The public release of the implementation is a valuable contribution to the community.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, despite its strong core contributions, suffers from a notable number of issues in clarity, technical rigor, and presentation that detract from its overall quality.

*   **Clarity of Notation:** The notation `x[k]` is used inconsistently and without clear definition initially. In Proposition 1, `(a+c)[1]` appears to denote the maximum value over the components, consistent with the standard multiclass SVM loss. However, in Section 2.2, `(a+c)[k]` is used to mean the k-th largest component of the vector. This ambiguity in a core piece of notation is confusing for the reader trying to follow the mathematical development from the standard SVM to the top-k variant.
*   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper contains several statements that lack sufficient justification. For instance, the caption of Figure 2 claims the top-k simplex has `(m choose k) + 1` vertices without providing a proof or citation. In Section 2.3, the authors state that "under certain constraints on the classifier, the recall@k is equivalent to the top-k error" but fail to specify these crucial constraints.
*   **Potential Flaw in the Projection Algorithm:** In Section 4.2, the algorithm for the biased projection onto the top-k cone is described. For the general case with `ρ > 0`, the paper states that Lemma 4 only provides a sufficient condition for the projection to be zero, and "we leave x = 0 as the fallback case". This suggests the proposed algorithm is not guaranteed to be exact. The potential impact of an inexact projection on the convergence guarantees of the Prox-SDCA framework is a critical point that is not discussed. This omission raises questions about the technical soundness of the optimization procedure.
*   **Informal and Vague Language:** The manuscript uses informal phrasing such as "The trick is to augment the training set..." (Sec 2.3) and makes vague statements like "This is a well examined problem" (Sec 4.1) without sufficient context or specific citations. The justification for choosing a particular algorithm in Section 4.1 ("since it is easy to implement") is weak for a top-tier conference paper.
*   **Presentation and Formatting:** The paper exhibits a lack of polish. The reference list is incomplete; for example, references [1], [2], and [5] are missing publication years, and [22] is an incomplete author list. The labels in Figure 2 are aliased, and the axes are unlabeled. The comment in Section 3.1 about fixing a typo in [24] is interesting, but its connection to Lemma 2 is not clearly explained, and the remark that the fix was already in the original authors' code diminishes the novelty of the observation.

**Recommendation:**
Reject.

**Justification:**
While the paper presents a novel and well-motivated extension to multiclass SVMs with strong empirical results, the execution is unfortunately flawed. The combination of notational ambiguity, unsubstantiated technical claims, and, most critically, a potential inexactness in the core projection algorithm without a discussion of its theoretical implications, prevents me from recommending acceptance. The numerous minor presentational issues further reinforce the impression of a manuscript that was not prepared with the care and rigor expected for NIPS. Although the core ideas are significant, the paper in its current form does not meet the high standards of quality and clarity required for publication. A thorough revision that clarifies the notation, substantiates all claims, and rigorously addresses the correctness of the projection algorithm and its impact on convergence is necessary. With such improvements, this work would represent a strong contribution.
