Summary
The paper proposes a convex “top-k hinge” loss for multiclass classification that upper-bounds the top-k zero-one loss, bridging the gap between multiclass SVMs and ranking-based objectives when evaluation is in terms of top-k accuracy. The key ideas are:
- A novel convex surrogate φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑_{j=1}^k (a+c)[j]}, which is tighter than both the Crammer–Singer hinge (k=1) and the ordered-weighted hinge of Usunier et al. with β = 1/k on the top-k positions.
- A convex-analytic characterization via a new “top-k simplex” ∆k: the conjugate φk* is the indicator of ∆k, yielding a clean primal-dual structure. The geometry differs from the standard simplex by an upper bound xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1,x⟩, leading to (m choose k) + 1 vertices.
- An optimization framework based on Fenchel duality and Prox-SDCA; the per-example dual update reduces to a (biased) Euclidean projection onto ∆k(r).
- New algorithms for projections onto the top-k simplex and the related top-k cone, including conditions under which the projection reduces to a CQKP instance and a constructive search over partitions (U,M,L) otherwise, with O(d log d + kd) complexity after sorting.
- Empirical evaluation on five image datasets (including SUN397, Places205, ImageNet), showing consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over standard multiclass SVM and strong ranking baselines; code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Extends Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM (JMLR’01) by replacing the max-margin on the single worst impostor with a margin on the average of the top-k impostors, motivated by top-k evaluation.
- Connects to Usunier et al.’s ordered weighted hinge losses and LaRank; shows φk is a strictly tighter upper bound than the straightforward ordered-weighted variant φ̃k when (a+c)[1] > 0 and (a+c)[k] < 0.
- Relates to Joachims’ structured SVM for multivariate measures (recall@k), noting scalability issues and non-decomposability.
- Builds on convex analysis of sums of k largest components (Ky Fan norms) and CQKP; contributes a distinct projection problem due to the coupling upper bound xi ≤ ⟨1,x⟩/k and the bias term.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: the top-k hinge is a clean, principled convex surrogate tailored to top-k metrics; the “top-k simplex” is an appealing geometric object with a simple conjugate.
- Solid convex-analytic development: j-compatibility, full primal-dual derivation, and clear mapping of SDCA updates to a structured projection problem.
- Algorithmic contribution: practical and efficient projection procedures for a nonstandard constraint set; careful treatment of the knapsack-vs-cone cases and bias term.
- Scalability: the SDCA-based solver with rank-1 updates and BLAS-friendly operations scales to millions of examples and hundreds–thousands of classes.
- Empirical relevance: consistent top-k gains across varied datasets; sometimes improves top-1 as well on larger datasets; compares two variants (α and β) and multiple baselines; code release increases impact.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty relative to known convex constructs: while the loss is well-motivated, the convexity of the sum of top-k and its representation as a support function are classical (Ky Fan). The specific “top-k simplex” and its conjugate characterization look natural; please clarify whether the set and conjugate are genuinely new or a restatement of known support sets for partial sums. A discussion of relations to k-support norms/atomic norm views would strengthen the novelty claims.
- Projection algorithm: the exhaustive search over (U,M,L) has worst-case (k−1)(d−k+1) iterations after sorting. Although d here is the number of classes m (≤ 1000 in your experiments), more insight into average-case behavior and tight runtime comparisons against strong CQKP solvers would help. The biased case falls back to x=0 when the sufficient condition fails; could that harm convergence or optimality in corner cases?
- Experimental scope: models are linear on fixed CNN features; end-to-end neural baselines optimized with cross-entropy (which already yields strong top-k) are absent. Although your framing is SVM-centric, it would be valuable to include modern softmax baselines or discuss how φk could be used in deep settings.
- Reporting: large-scale results are deferred to the supplement; a brief summary in the main text would aid readers. Statistical significance of improvements is not discussed.
- Clarity and polish: section numbering has glitches; some references are missing years or are incomplete; a few definitions (e.g., errk) could be made explicit in the main text. Proofs are in the supplement; a compact intuition for Proposition 2 and Lemma 5 in the main body would improve readability.

Questions for authors
- Can you position ∆k and φk more explicitly against known constructions (Ky Fan, k-support norms, isotonic regression connections)? Are there known dual polytopes equivalent to ∆k?
- What are Lipschitz or smoothness constants for φk, and do they yield improved SDCA convergence rates compared to φ?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of training k versus evaluation k? Do models trained with larger k generalize to smaller k in evaluation?
- Could your projection be accelerated via partial sorting or selection to avoid full sorting when m is very large?

Minor comments
- Clean up references (missing years, truncated author lists).
- Explicitly define the top-k zero-one loss; unify notation for a, c, and indices (minor inconsistencies).
- Add brief runtime plots versus standard simplex projection and per-epoch wall-clock comparisons across methods.

Recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper introduces a clearly motivated and practically useful convex surrogate for top-k accuracy, with an elegant dual geometry and efficient algorithms that scale. Despite some connections to known convex constructs, the package—loss, geometry, projections, and scalable optimization—is cohesive and likely to influence practice where top-k metrics matter.
