This paper introduces a novel relaxation of the multiclass SVM loss function to directly optimize for top-k accuracy. The core idea – replacing the hinge loss with a convex upper bound that encourages larger margins between the correct class and the remaining classes – is compelling and addresses a practical limitation of standard multiclass SVMs. The derivation of the Fenchel conjugate and the introduction of the top-k simplex are interesting theoretical contributions, and the connection to existing ranking losses is well-established. The presented optimization framework, leveraging Prox-SDCA, appears sound, and the reported experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across various datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The top-k hinge loss is a genuinely novel contribution. While relaxing hinge losses is not new, applying it specifically to the top-k setting, along with the accompanying theoretical analysis (conjugate derivation, simplex definition) provides a unique perspective.
*   **Significance:** Optimizing directly for top-k accuracy is relevant to many real-world applications (recommendation systems, information retrieval). The demonstrated improvements, while not always dramatic, are consistent and suggest a practical benefit.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper includes a solid theoretical foundation, deriving the Fenchel conjugate and providing a detailed analysis of the proposed loss function.
*   **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The authors clearly relate their work to existing multiclass SVMs and ranking-based losses, positioning their contribution within the broader literature.
*   **Scalability:** The authors demonstrate the scalability of their method to large datasets like Places205 and ImageNet.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Ablation Study:** While results are presented for two versions of the top-k loss, a more comprehensive ablation study examining the sensitivity to the parameter *k* and comparing against stronger baselines beyond SVMPerf and TopPush would be valuable.
*   **Empirical Gap:** While statistically significant improvements are shown, the magnitude of these improvements is not always substantial. Further analysis to understand *when* the top-k SVM performs best could strengthen the paper.
*   **Projection Implementation Details:** While the paper describes the projection onto the top-k simplex, more details on the efficiency of the implemented algorithm and comparisons to alternative projection methods would be helpful.
*   **Minor Typo/Clarification:** The statement that the initial thresholding in the standard multiclass SVM loss is redundant could benefit from a more detailed explanation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to multiclass classification.
*   The core idea of directly optimizing for top-k accuracy is significant and has practical relevance.
*   The experimental results, while not revolutionary, demonstrate consistent improvements.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The magnitude of the improvements isn't overwhelming, and a more in-depth analysis of the empirical results is needed.
*   The ablation study could be more comprehensive.
*   The practical impact needs to be better articulated - does this translate to significant gains in downstream applications?

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach and the theoretical rigor outweigh the minor weaknesses. While the empirical gains are not massive, the consistent improvements and the direct optimization for a relevant metric (top-k accuracy) are compelling. I am willing to overlook the incomplete ablation study and limited baseline comparisons given the novelty of the approach. I recommend **acceptance**, with a request for the authors to address the concerns regarding the magnitude of the improvements and the completeness of the ablation study in the camera-ready version. The paper pushes the boundaries of existing multiclass SVMs in a worthwhile direction.
