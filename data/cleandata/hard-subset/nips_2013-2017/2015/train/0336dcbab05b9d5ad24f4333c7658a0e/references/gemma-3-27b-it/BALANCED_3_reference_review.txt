This paper introduces a top-k multiclass SVM loss function and its associated optimization framework. The key idea is to relax the penalty for errors outside the top-k predictions, offering a convex relaxation that aims to improve top-k accuracy, especially in scenarios with a large number of classes. The authors derive the Fenchel conjugate of the proposed loss and propose an efficient optimization procedure based on Prox-SDCA, including algorithms for projecting onto the top-k simplex. The experimental evaluation demonstrates consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across several image classification datasets.

The paper builds upon the well-established multiclass SVM framework of Crammer and Singer [5] and extends it to the top-k setting. It correctly identifies the non-convexity of a naive extension and proposes a convex upper bound (the top-k hinge loss). The connection to ranking-based losses, specifically the work of Usunier et al. [27], is also clearly articulated.  Prior work on efficient optimization techniques, such as Prox-SDCA [24], is appropriately leveraged. The work also references and builds upon previous techniques for projection onto the simplex [13] and adapts them for the top-k simplex, which is a novel contribution. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed top-k hinge loss provides a novel convex relaxation for the top-k multiclass SVM problem. The introduction of the top-k simplex and its associated conjugate is also a valuable contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the Fenchel conjugate and the associated theoretical analysis are well-presented and appear correct. The proofs are deferred to the supplement.
* **Optimization Framework:** Utilizing Prox-SDCA and providing efficient projection algorithms are significant practical contributions, enabling the scaling of the method to large datasets.
* **Experimental Validation:** The consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across multiple datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The inclusion of results on large datasets like Places 205 and ImageNet is a strong point.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivations, technical details, and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper compares against other methods, a more detailed ablation study would be helpful. Specifically, comparing the two versions of the top-k hinge loss (3) and (5) in more depth, beyond the general observation that they perform similarly, would be valuable.  What conditions favor one over the other?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the performance to the regularization parameter λ is not thoroughly discussed.  While cross-validation is used, details regarding the range explored and the impact of the choice of λ on the results could be expanded.
* **Projection Algorithm Complexity:** The discussion of the projection algorithm, while detailed, can be challenging to follow.  A more intuitive explanation of the algorithm and its complexity would be beneficial, especially concerning the search strategy in the general case. The claim of linear scaling needs more justification (in practice, the sorting step dominates).
* **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons to several methods are provided, the results against some of the strongest recent baselines for large-scale image classification (e.g., those employing more recent deep learning architectures) are missing. This limits the assessment of the practical significance of the improvements.
* **Justification for Top-k Focus:** The paper argues for the benefits of top-k optimization, particularly with a large number of classes. However, a more detailed discussion of scenarios where optimizing for top-k is genuinely more important than optimizing for top-1 accuracy would strengthen the motivation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to multiclass SVM, addressing a practical problem in scenarios with many classes.
* The optimization framework is efficient and enables scaling to large datasets.
* The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of a detailed ablation study and sensitivity analysis limits the understanding of the method's behavior.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art baselines is not comprehensive.
* The complexity of the projection algorithm discussion and the justification of its linear scaling could be improved.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of multiclass SVM and has the potential to be valuable to researchers and practitioners working on problems where top-k accuracy is a primary concern.  However, the weaknesses mentioned above prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance in its current form. 

**Recommendation:**  **Weak Reject**. 

I recommend rejection but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by conducting a more comprehensive ablation study, providing a more detailed sensitivity analysis, and expanding the comparison to stronger baselines. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. A clearer discussion of the projection algorithm’s complexity would also be beneficial.
