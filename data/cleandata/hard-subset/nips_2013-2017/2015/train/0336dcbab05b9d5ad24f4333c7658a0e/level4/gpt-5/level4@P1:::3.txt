Summary
This paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM that directly optimizes top-k performance by introducing a convex upper bound on the top-k error. The authors define a top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑j=1..k (a + c)[j]}, derive its convex conjugate, and show that it reduces to the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM when k = 1. A central construct is the top-k simplex ∆k, which serves as the effective domain of the conjugate and differs from the standard simplex by imposing an adaptive upper bound xi ≤ (1/k)〈1, x〉. The paper also relates φk to the ranking-based family of losses Lβ (Usunier et al., 2009) and contrasts φk with an alternative averaging loss φ̃k that sums only positive parts of the top-k scores.

Optimization is carried out with Prox-SDCA, updating a block of dual variables per example and maintaining primal variables incrementally. A key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for (biased) projection onto the top-k simplex ∆k(r) and the associated top-k cone, with complexity comparable to classical simplex projection methods. Experiments on five image datasets (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN 397, Places 205, ImageNet 2012) demonstrate consistent gains in top-k accuracy over strong baselines, including multiclass SVM, SVMPerf (recall@k), TopPush, and Wsabie variants. The method scales to millions of examples, and the authors release code.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of a convex top-k hinge loss that tightly upper-bounds the discrete top-k error and recovers Crammer–Singer as a special case.
- Elegant dual derivation using a yi-compatibility condition and a clean connection to the new top-k simplex ∆k.
- A practical, efficient projection algorithm onto ∆k(r) (and top-k cone) that enables exact Prox-SDCA updates; well-motivated complexity and comparison to CQKP-based methods.
- Extensive experiments showing consistent top-k improvements across datasets and k, with scalability to very large datasets and publicly released implementation.
- Thoughtful positioning relative to ranking-based approaches (Usunier et al.’s Lβ, SVMPerf, TopPush) and a clear discussion of φk versus φ̃k.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Motivation for the specific top-k hinge φk could be strengthened. The paper explains φk as a convex upper bound and shows inequalities ψk ≤ φk ≤ φ̃k ≤ φ, but more intuition on why averaging the top-k scores (instead of, e.g., summing positive parts as in φ̃k) is the preferable surrogate would help. In particular, expanding on scenarios where φk is empirically tighter and how this tightness translates to improved generalization would improve the narrative. Similarly, a brief discussion of when optimizing top-k may slightly reduce top-1 (as noted on MIT Indoor 67) would guide practitioners.
- Minor: Typo on page 3 — “globally optimal leads to …” should be “globally leads to …” (remove “optimal”).

Quality
- The technical development appears sound. The convexity of the proposed loss, conjugate derivations, and the dual/primal relationship are carefully laid out, with proofs in the supplement. The projection algorithm is well specified, with clear handling of special cases and complexity claims. The optimization framework (Prox-SDCA) is appropriate and provides a principled stopping criterion via the duality gap.

Clarity
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The definitions of φk and ∆k, the relationship to φ̃k and Lβ, and the optimization details are easy to follow. The only clarity suggestion is to bolster the motivation and intuition around the choice of φk versus alternatives, as noted above.

Originality
- The introduction of the top-k hinge loss tied to the top-k simplex, together with an efficient projection algorithm enabling exact SDCA updates, is novel. The connection to existing ranking losses is well articulated, and the contraction from the standard simplex to ∆k is an appealing geometric perspective.

Significance
- Top-k metrics are crucial in large-scale multiclass problems, especially in vision. Demonstrating consistent top-k gains, scalability to millions of examples, and providing code make this a practically significant contribution likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners.

Pros
- Convex surrogate tailored to top-k; tightness relative to φ̃k discussed.
- Solid dual formulation and an efficient, implementable optimizer.
- Scalable, with thorough empirical validation on diverse datasets.
- Clear relationship to prior work in multiclass SVMs and ranking losses.
- Public code release.

Cons
- The choice and intuition for φk versus φ̃k and other possible surrogates could be motivated more deeply.
- Minor typo as noted above.

Minor comments
- Page 3: “globally optimal leads to” → “globally leads to” (remove “optimal”).
