This paper proposes a Top-k Multiclass SVM that directly optimizes top-k accuracy via a new convex loss, along with an efficient optimization scheme based on Prox-SDCA and a fast projection onto a “top-k simplex.” The authors start from the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM [5] and generalize its hinge loss to the top-k setting by introducing a tight convex upper bound on the top-k zero-one loss. They derive the Fenchel conjugate of the proposed loss, define the top-k simplex (a contraction of the standard simplex with k-dependent upper bounds on coordinates), and develop an algorithm for (biased) projections onto this set. The resulting method scales to large datasets and achieves consistent gains in top-k accuracy across five image classification benchmarks. The work is positioned relative to ranking-based losses (e.g., Usunier et al. [27]) and structured SVM for multivariate measures (e.g., Joachims [11]), highlighting that the proposed loss is instance-decomposable and can be optimized exactly within Prox-SDCA.

Strengths
- Clear and principled extension of the multiclass SVM to the top-k regime with a convex surrogate that is tighter than existing ordered-hinge variants.
- Solid dual derivations via Fenchel duality, including the yi-compatibility condition and the characterization of the top-k simplex as the effective domain of the conjugate.
- A technically interesting and useful projection algorithm onto the top-k simplex/top-k cone that addresses nontrivial coupling constraints absent in standard simplex projection.
- Strong empirical results on multiple datasets (including large-scale), with consistent improvements in top-k accuracy and released code, suggesting practical impact.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Motivation for the specific top-k hinge choice could be strengthened. For example, provide more intuition for why averaging the top k scores (vs. summing positive parts as in the ordered hinge) yields a tighter and more effective surrogate, and when φk is preferable to the variant φ̃k. A small synthetic study illustrating the gap between ψk, φk, and φ̃k would help.
- Analysis of k-mismatch would be valuable: if the model is trained with a particular k (e.g., k=10) but evaluated at smaller k (e.g., top-5), what behavior should one expect theoretically and empirically? The reported gains at top-1/top-5 when training for larger k are intriguing—please analyze sensitivity to k and provide guidance for selection.
- Clarify computational costs. The projection algorithm’s complexity is discussed qualitatively; adding runtime plots versus standard simplex projection (for varying d, k) and end-to-end training times versus strong baselines (e.g., LibLinear CS-SVM, one-vs-all) would better substantiate scalability claims.
- Expand the discussion of the biased projection (ρ > 0): give more intuition for the bias, its impact on solutions, and whether alternative regularizers change behavior or complexity.
- Some references appear incomplete or malformed; please fix years, venues, and author lists (e.g., the ImageNet reference and others with missing fields).
- Minor clarity issues: define the stopping tolerance where “until relative duality gap is below …” is left blank; make explicit how the duality gap is computed in practice.

Quality
The technical development is sound and careful. The convexity arguments, conjugate derivations, and dual formulation align with standard convex analysis. The connection to the top-k simplex is elegant, and the projection algorithm addresses constraints not covered by CQKP. Experiments are solid and use reasonable baselines (Crammer–Singer, ranking-based methods via multiclass-to-binary reduction, Wsabie variants). It would strengthen the empirical section to report variance over multiple splits where applicable and to include ablations on the projection routine’s runtime.

Clarity
Overall the paper is clearly written and well organized. Proof sketches and intuitions are provided, with full proofs in the supplement. The definitions of the top-k simplex/cone and the sets U, M, L are helpful. To improve clarity:
- Provide additional intuition for the loss design and the role of the averaging over the top k scores.
- Clarify the algorithmic stopping criterion and hyperparameter choices (including the regularization λ and the selection of k).
- Fix the typo on page 3: “globally optimal leads to” should be “global optimization leads to” or “finding a globally optimal solution is …”; remove “optimal” as you note.

Originality
The top-k hinge loss as a tight convex upper bound and the associated top-k simplex with its projection algorithm are novel and nontrivial. The work goes beyond prior ordered-hinge and ranking-based formulations [27] by offering a tighter surrogate and an exact, scalable optimizer within Prox-SDCA. The projection method appears of independent interest.

Significance
Top-k metrics are standard in large-scale vision tasks, and a principled, scalable method to optimize them directly is valuable. The empirical improvements and released implementation should make this broadly useful. The projection algorithm may also benefit other problems involving sums of the largest components or capped allocations.

Pros
- Principled, tighter top-k surrogate with clear multiclass SVM connection.
- New top-k simplex and efficient projection algorithm.
- Exact, scalable optimization via Prox-SDCA.
- Consistent empirical gains on diverse datasets; code release.

Cons
- Limited motivation/intuition for the chosen surrogate vs. alternatives.
- Incomplete analysis of training k vs. evaluation k.
- Missing details on runtime/complexity and some minor presentation issues.

Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper addressing an important evaluation criterion with both theoretical and practical contributions. I recommend acceptance after addressing the above clarity and motivation points.
