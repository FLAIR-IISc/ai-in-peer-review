Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM that directly optimizes top-k accuracy via a new convex surrogate, the top-k hinge loss. The loss uses the average of the k largest margins to form a tight convex upper bound on the top-k zero-one loss, generalizing the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM (recovered when k=1). The authors derive the convex conjugate of the loss and introduce the “top-k simplex,” a contraction of the standard simplex that emerges as the effective domain of the conjugate. Building on Fenchel duality, they present an optimization framework based on Proximal Stochastic Dual Coordinate Ascent (Prox-SDCA), which requires repeated projections onto the top-k simplex (or cone). A key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for these (biased) projections, including a careful treatment of cases where the simplex constraint is tight or slack, and connections to the continuous quadratic knapsack problem.

The paper situates the work relative to ranking-based surrogates (e.g., Usunier et al.’s ordered weighted hinge losses) and structured SVM for multivariate measures (e.g., Joachims’ approach to recall@k). The authors show their loss is a tighter bound than the ordered top-k hinge variant and discuss reductions from multiclass to binary for ranking methods such as SVMPerf and TopPush. Extensive experiments on five image classification datasets (Caltech-101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN397, Places205, and ImageNet 2012) demonstrate systematic gains in top-k accuracy over multiclass SVM and ranking baselines, while maintaining scalability to millions of examples.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: top-k metrics are more appropriate when class ambiguity is high.
- Sound convex formulation: a principled surrogate that is tighter than prior ordered hinge losses and reduces to Crammer–Singer for k=1.
- Solid dual derivation: the notion of j-compatibility and the corrected dual form are presented cleanly, with an interpretable top-k simplex domain.
- Novel and practically relevant projection algorithm onto the top-k simplex/cone, with runtime guarantees comparable to standard simplex projection.
- Scalable optimization via Prox-SDCA with a verifiable duality-gap stopping criterion; easily kernelizable.
- Comprehensive empirical study across diverse scales, showing consistent improvements in top-k accuracy.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Comparisons to related probabilistic top-k models (e.g., [25]) and other recent scalable baselines (noted as [8] in the submission) are missing due to implementation availability. Even if code is unavailable, a discussion or approximate re-implementation on small datasets would strengthen the empirical picture.
- While the surrogate is motivated as “tighter,” there is no theoretical analysis of calibration/consistency for top-k risk. A brief discussion of when optimizing φk is Fisher-consistent for top-k error would add value.
- The choice of training k versus evaluation k is important. Guidance or validation curves showing how training with k’ affects top-k accuracy across different evaluation k would help practitioners.
- Some runtime comparisons (projection vs. standard simplex; Prox-SDCA vs. SGD/LaRank/structured SVM) are deferred to the supplement. Including at least one runtime/scale plot in the main paper would improve clarity on scalability.
- The reduction to binary for ranking baselines can be unfavorable on very large datasets. The paper acknowledges this; it would help to report both reduced and one-vs-all variants (with clear notes on resource limits) and ensure hyperparameter tuning parity across methods.
- Minor clarity: a short intuitive explanation of the top-k simplex geometry and its difference from the standard simplex would help readers who skip the supplement. Edge cases (k=1 and k=m) deserve a brief discussion.

Quality:
The technical development is careful and principled. The dual and projection machinery are well described, and the algorithmic choices (batch dual updates, BLAS-friendly operations, duality-gap stopping) are sound. Empirical results support the claims, though adding a bit more in-paper runtime data and a broader set of baselines (or an extended discussion) would further strengthen the case.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The connection to prior work is appropriate and the differences are made explicit (e.g., tighter bound than ordered hinge). Some proofs and projection details are in the supplement; providing a compact high-level algorithm box for the projection in the main text would aid readability.

Originality:
Introducing the top-k hinge loss with its associated top-k simplex and efficient projection is original and nontrivial. The surrogate’s relation to ranking losses is insightful, and the optimization pipeline is a thoughtful adaptation of Prox-SDCA to this new setting.

Significance:
Top-k accuracy is widely used in large-scale vision and recognition. A convex, scalable surrogate with a practical solver is valuable to both researchers and practitioners. The consistent empirical gains, combined with released code, indicate strong potential for impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- New, principled surrogate for top-k with clear advantages over existing ranking losses
- Novel and efficient projection method; practical optimization and stopping
- Strong, scalable empirical results across multiple datasets
- Clear exposition and connection to prior art

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to some related methods ([25], [8]); limited discussion of theoretical consistency
- Some runtime and ablation details deferred to the supplement
- Guidance on selecting training k relative to evaluation k is limited

Requests to authors:
- If anonymity rules preclude sharing now, please ensure the promised code is publicly released upon acceptance, with scripts to reproduce all figures/tables and clear instructions. Including the projection routines and both top-k loss variants (α and β) will be valuable.
- If feasible, add comparisons or a substantive discussion for [25] and [8], even on smaller datasets or with approximate re-implementations.
- Provide guidance on choosing k at training time (e.g., validation studies showing sensitivity).
- Consider a brief discussion of top-k calibration/consistency, or pointers to relevant theory.
- Move a concise runtime comparison (projection and end-to-end training) from the supplement into the main text.

Overall assessment:
A neat and technically solid formulation of top-k multiclass SVM that is both novel and practically effective. With minor additions to empirical comparisons and clarity, this would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
