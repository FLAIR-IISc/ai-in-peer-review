In many multiclass classification problems with a large number of classes, semantic similarity between classes is often observed. This paper proposes a method, termed top-k Multiclass SVM, to explicitly address scenarios where misclassifications are only penalized if the ground truth label falls outside the top *k* predicted classes – a setting relevant to challenges like ImageNet. The authors extend the standard multiclass SVM loss function to incorporate this top-*k* notion and formulate an efficient optimization procedure for the resulting problem. Experiments on multiple datasets, including large-scale benchmarks, demonstrate the scalability of the proposed algorithm and consistent improvements over competitive baselines.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly presented. The problem addressed – handling class ambiguity in multiclass learning – is an important one encountered in numerous practical applications. The authors formulate a top-*k* multiclass SVM algorithm designed for cases where classes exhibit significant similarity. Notably, the algorithm scales effectively to large datasets and achieves demonstrable improvements compared to existing methods. 

The core contribution lies in formulating a convex upper bound on the top-*k* error, enabling efficient optimization via a novel projection onto the top-*k* simplex. This projection, and the derivation of its convex conjugate, represent a non-trivial technical achievement. The authors carefully relate their work to prior research on multiclass SVMs [5, 24] and ranking-based losses [27], placing their contribution within a well-defined research context. The optimization framework, leveraging Prox-SDCA [24], builds on established techniques, while the detailed analysis of the top-*k* simplex and its projection algorithm are original.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a mathematically rigorous approach, with clear derivations of the loss function, its convex conjugate, and the optimization procedure.
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm demonstrates scalability to large datasets (Places 205, ImageNet), a crucial characteristic for real-world applications.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Comprehensive experiments on five datasets consistently show improvements in top-*k* accuracy compared to strong baselines.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed top-*k* hinge loss and the efficient projection algorithm represent novel contributions to the field.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, making the technical details accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Improvement:** While consistent, the improvements in accuracy are sometimes modest, particularly on smaller datasets. The significance of these improvements should be carefully considered.
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparison could be strengthened by including more recent and advanced methods specifically designed for large-scale, high-dimensional classification.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of different hyperparameter settings on performance is not thoroughly explored. A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the convex upper bound is a key innovation, a more in-depth theoretical analysis of the tightness of this bound and its implications for generalization would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in multiclass learning.
*   The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   The algorithm is scalable and demonstrates consistent improvements in performance.
*   The contributions are novel and build upon existing work in a meaningful way.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The improvements in accuracy are sometimes modest.
*   The comparison to existing methods could be more comprehensive.
*   A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity is needed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of multiclass learning. The proposed top-*k* Multiclass SVM offers a practical and scalable solution to the problem of class ambiguity, and the technical innovations – particularly the convex upper bound and the efficient projection algorithm – are significant. While the improvements in accuracy are not always dramatic, the consistency of the results and the theoretical soundness of the approach warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to the conference.
