The paper proposes a top-k hinge loss for top-k classification. It generalizes the multiclass SVM and uses the sum of the k largest components as a convex approximation to the desired top-k loss. An efficient optimization algorithm is proposed, and relationships with previous work are discussed. The paper is clearly written and presents a compelling approach to improving top-k accuracy in multiclass classification. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to several baselines.

The authors acknowledge that comparisons with [25, 8] were not included due to the unavailability of implementations. However, I am particularly interested in seeing a comparison with these methods if possible, as they represent relevant prior work in ranking-based losses and structured SVMs. If implementations become available prior to rebuttal, including these results would strengthen the paper. 

Furthermore, making the code publicly available upon acceptance would be highly valuable for the research community and facilitate reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed top-k hinge loss offers a novel convex relaxation for optimizing top-k performance, extending the well-known multiclass SVM framework.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the primal-conjugate pair and the efficient projection onto the top-k simplex demonstrate a strong technical foundation. The connection to ranking-based losses is also well-established.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on five datasets consistently show improvements in top-k accuracy compared to various baselines, indicating the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Missing Comparisons:** The lack of comparison with [25, 8] is a minor drawback, as these papers represent relevant work.
*   **Scalability Discussion:** While the paper demonstrates scalability, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and memory requirements, especially for extremely large datasets, would be beneficial.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multiclass classification. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. Addressing the minor concerns regarding comparisons with [25, 8] and potentially providing more detail on scalability would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
