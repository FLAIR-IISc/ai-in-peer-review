In many multiclass classification problems with a large number of classes, semantic similarity between classes is often present. This paper proposes a novel algorithm, termed top-k SVM, to explicitly address learning scenarios where a cost is incurred only if the ground truth label falls outside the top *k* predicted classes – a common situation exemplified by challenges like ImageNet. Following the introduction of a top-k loss function, an efficient optimization procedure is developed. Experiments on large-scale datasets demonstrate the algorithm’s scalability and show clear improvements over existing methods.

The paper is well-motivated and easy to understand. The problem it tackles – the presence of similar classes in multiclass learning – is an important one with frequent occurrences in practical applications. Specifically, this work formulates the top-k SVM algorithm to effectively handle multiclass classification tasks where certain classes exhibit high degrees of similarity. The algorithm scales favorably to very large datasets and achieves performance improvements compared to the baseline methods.

The main strength of this work lies in its practical relevance and the elegance with which it addresses a challenging problem. The top-k loss function is a natural extension of standard SVM loss, and the proposed optimization procedure appears well-designed for efficient computation. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating both scalability and improved accuracy on benchmark datasets. 

However, there are a few aspects that could be strengthened. While the paper demonstrates improvements over the compared methods, the magnitude of these improvements isn’t always substantial. A more detailed analysis of *where* the top-k SVM excels – i.e., which types of datasets or classes benefit the most – would be valuable. For instance, does performance gain correlate with the degree of semantic similarity between classes? Exploring this could offer deeper insights.

Additionally, the connection to related work could be more thoroughly discussed. Specifically, the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to techniques that explicitly model class hierarchies or employ label embedding methods. While the authors acknowledge these areas, a more in-depth discussion of how top-k SVM differs and potentially complements these approaches would be beneficial. 

Finally, the choice of *k* is a crucial parameter. The paper briefly mentions how *k* is set in the experiments, but a more detailed discussion of strategies for selecting an appropriate *k* in practice would be helpful. Does the optimal *k* depend on the dataset size, the number of classes, or the level of semantic similarity? Providing guidance on this aspect would increase the practical utility of the algorithm.

Overall, this is a solid contribution with clear potential. Addressing the points raised above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
