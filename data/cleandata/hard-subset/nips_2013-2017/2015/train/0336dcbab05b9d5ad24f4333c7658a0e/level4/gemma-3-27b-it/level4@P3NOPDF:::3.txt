This paper presents an interesting approach to a challenging problem, and the material is both non-trivial and clearly presented. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. Overall, this is a strong contribution.

The core idea – learning with respect to a top-k loss, where the loss is zero if the true class is included within the top-k predictions – is novel and intuitively appealing. This framing directly addresses the scenario where multiple correct answers may exist, or where approximate solutions are acceptable, which is common in many real-world applications. The paper effectively articulates this motivation and demonstrates a practical implementation.

My primary suggestion for improvement relates to the motivation behind the specific loss function chosen. While the authors clearly explain *what* the loss function does, further elaboration on *why* this particular formulation was selected, compared to alternative ranking or set-based loss functions, would strengthen the paper. A more detailed discussion of the theoretical properties and potential advantages of this loss function, perhaps with a connection to existing work in related areas, would be beneficial. For instance, exploring the relationship to listwise learning-to-rank losses or discussing why a margin-based approach wasn't considered could provide valuable insight.

The experiments are well-designed and the results appear convincing. The authors demonstrate performance improvements across multiple datasets and appropriately compare their method to relevant baselines. Reporting the statistical significance of the improvements would further enhance the robustness of the claims. The ablation studies, while not extensive, provide some initial evidence supporting the design choices.

I noticed a minor typographical error on page 3: “globally optimal leads to” should be corrected to “globally leads to”. 

In summary, this is a well-written and promising paper that tackles an important problem with a thoughtful and effective approach. Addressing the suggestion regarding the loss function motivation would elevate the work further. I recommend acceptance.
