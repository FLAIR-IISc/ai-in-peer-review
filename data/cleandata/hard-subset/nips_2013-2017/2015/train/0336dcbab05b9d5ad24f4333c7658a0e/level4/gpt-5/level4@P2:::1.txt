Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM that directly optimizes a convex upper bound on top-k error. The key idea is the top-k hinge loss, defined via the average of the k largest margin violations, which is a tighter convex surrogate than the standard multiclass hinge for k > 1. The authors derive the convex conjugate of this loss, showing that its effective domain is a “top-k simplex,” a contraction of the standard simplex with per-coordinate upper bounds proportional to the total mass. Building on this, they present a Prox-SDCA optimization scheme whose inner step reduces to a (biased) projection onto the top-k simplex; they then develop an efficient projection algorithm with complexity comparable to standard simplex projection.

The work extends the multiclass SVM of Crammer and Singer (recovered at k = 1), and clarifies connections to ranking-based losses (e.g., Usunier et al.’s weighted ordered hinge, SVMPerf for recall@k, and TopPush). The paper argues that the proposed top-k hinge gives a strictly tighter bound than averaging individual hinge losses over the top-k violations when the k-th largest violation lies between 0 and the largest negative score, and provides exact Prox-SDCA updates thanks to the new projection routine. Experiments on five image datasets show consistent gains in top-k accuracy over several baselines.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-organized presentation with precise derivations (Fenchel duality, yi-compatibility, conjugates).
- A principled convex surrogate for top-k error that strictly tightens existing upper bounds in relevant regimes.
- A technical contribution of independent interest: efficient (biased) projection onto the top-k simplex/top-k cone, with analysis and practical algorithm.
- An optimization framework (Prox-SDCA) that yields joint updates over m dual variables, a computable duality gap, and straightforward kernelization.
- Empirical results across multiple datasets demonstrating consistent improvements in top-k accuracy, sometimes also improving top-1 on larger datasets.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Missing comparisons: The authors note the lack of comparisons to [25] and [8] due to implementation availability. It would be valuable to include these results (or strong arguments/ablations explaining inapplicability), at least on smaller datasets, or to provide re-implementations/approximations where feasible.
- Baselines and fairness: Some ranking methods required one-vs-all adaptations when the reduction became intractable. Please clarify any differences in hyperparameter tuning budgets, stopping criteria, and feature preprocessing across methods to ensure fairness. Reporting variance or confidence intervals would help.
- Empirical analysis of the projection step: The supplement claims scalability, but the main text could benefit from runtime and memory comparisons vs standard simplex projection and alternative optimizers (e.g., SGD/LibLinear), including sensitivity to k and dimensionality d.
- Effect of the biased projection parameter ρ: In practice ρ = 1 is used; an ablation or small study showing the influence of ρ (including ρ = 0) on convergence and accuracy would strengthen the optimization claims.
- When does top-1 degrade? The paper notes occasional drops in top-1 when optimizing top-k. A brief analysis of the trade-off (e.g., margin distributions, confusion patterns, or calibration shifts) would be informative.
- Kernelization is mentioned but not demonstrated; a small kernel experiment (or a discussion of computational trade-offs) would round out the applicability claims.
- Clarity: The fix to the typo in [24] (regarding ayi,i) is helpful; consider moving that key clarification into the main paper. Also, a brief geometric intuition for the top-k simplex (beyond Figure 2) would aid readers.

Quality:
The methodology is technically sound, with careful convex analysis and a well-justified optimization scheme. The projection algorithm appears correct and efficient, though more thorough empirical profiling would be useful. Claims are generally supported by experiments.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well structured. Notation is consistent with prior work, and the connections to existing ranking losses are explicit. A few implementation details (runtime, memory, and statistical variability) could be expanded.

Originality:
The formulation of a top-k hinge via the average of the top-k components, its conjugate leading to the top-k simplex, and the associated projection algorithm constitute novel and meaningful contributions beyond prior ranking-based surrogates.

Significance:
The problem is important for large-vocabulary classification where top-k metrics are standard. The proposed surrogate and optimizer are likely to be adopted by practitioners and researchers, given the observed improvements and scalability.

Questions for the authors:
- Can you include (or justify omitting) comparisons to [25] and [8], at least on small/medium datasets?
- Will you release code and scripts to reproduce the experiments upon acceptance?
- Could you report training time, memory usage, and convergence behavior vs LibLinear/SGD, and provide a sensitivity analysis over k and ρ?
- For methods requiring the multiclass-to-binary reduction, can you quantify the impact of the reduction versus one-vs-all on both accuracy and runtime?

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, principled convex surrogate tailored to top-k metrics.
- Solid theory (conjugate, top-k simplex) and an efficient projection method of independent interest.
- Practical optimizer with convergence guarantees and measurable duality gaps.
- Consistent empirical gains on multiple datasets.

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to some relevant methods and limited runtime/statistical analysis in the main text.
- Limited discussion of trade-offs between top-1 and top-k performance and of kernelized variants.

Overall:
A neat and well-executed formulation of top-k multiclass SVM with convincing empirical benefits. I would support acceptance, especially if the authors add the requested comparisons/analyses and commit to releasing code.
