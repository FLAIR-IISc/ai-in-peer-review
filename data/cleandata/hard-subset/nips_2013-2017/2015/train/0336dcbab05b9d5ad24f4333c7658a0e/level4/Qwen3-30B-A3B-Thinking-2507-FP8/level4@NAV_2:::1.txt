

The paper introduces a top-k multiclass SVM framework that generalizes the Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM [5] by proposing a convex upper bound for the top-k error using the sum of the k largest components. This approach extends the standard simplex to the top-k simplex (Proposition 2) and provides an efficient optimization scheme via Prox-SDCA with a novel projection algorithm onto the top-k simplex (Lemma 3-5), addressing a key computational challenge. The work builds on NIPS contributions such as Usunier et al.'s ranking-based losses [27] and Crammer-Singer's multiclass SVM [5], while offering a tighter convex bound than prior ranking losses (Equation 5).  

**Strengths:**  
- *Quality*: Theoretical foundations are rigorous (Propositions 1-4, Lemmas 3-5), with clear convex conjugate derivations and scalability guarantees. Experiments consistently show top-k accuracy improvements across five diverse datasets (Caltech 101, MIT Indoor 67, SUN 397, Places 205, ImageNet), including large-scale settings (e.g., +2.5% top-5 accuracy on SUN 397).  
- *Clarity*: Well-structured with precise notation, helpful illustrations (Figure 2), and concise algorithmic descriptions (Algorithm 1). The connection to prior work (e.g., Usunier [27], Crammer-Singer [5]) is thoroughly established.  
- *Originality*: The top-k simplex and its efficient projection algorithm are novel technical contributions. The method extends ranking losses [27] while providing a tighter convex bound than existing approaches (Equation 5 vs. φ̃ₖ).  
- *Significance*: Directly addresses class ambiguity in large-scale image classification (e.g., ImageNet with 1,000 classes), where top-k metrics are more meaningful than top-1. Results demonstrate practical utility for real-world applications.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- The comparison to Wsabie [8] (referenced as "Wsabie++" in Section 5.1) is incomplete; the authors note implementation constraints but do not clarify why the comparison was omitted despite using Wsabie++ as a baseline. Including this comparison would strengthen the significance argument.  
- The paper does not explore sensitivity to k (e.g., how performance varies with k=2 vs. k=10 beyond Table 1), which is relevant for practitioners selecting k.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro*: Strong theoretical novelty (top-k simplex), robust empirical validation on large datasets, clear connection to prior NIPS work, and a practical optimization algorithm.  
*Con*: Minor omission in comparison to Wsabie [8] and limited analysis of k selection.  

The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a meaningful contribution to multiclass classification. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the Wsabie comparison and k-sensitivity analysis. The authors should clarify the Wsabie comparison in the revised manuscript.
