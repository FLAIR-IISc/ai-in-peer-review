Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces a top-k multiclass SVM that directly optimizes a convex surrogate of the top-k error, a metric widely used in large-scale image recognition where class ambiguity is common. The authors propose a “top-k hinge” loss that averages the k largest margin violations and proves it is a tight convex upper bound on the top-k zero-one loss, strictly tighter than the standard multiclass hinge and the ranking-based surrogate of Usunier et al. for many cases. They derive its convex conjugate and show that the effective domain is a novel “top-k simplex,” a contraction of the standard simplex with data-dependent upper bounds. Building on Fenchel duality, they adapt Prox-SDCA to the multiclass setting with this loss and develop an efficient algorithm for (biased) projection onto the top-k simplex—an optimization subproblem that differs from the continuous quadratic knapsack due to coupled upper bounds. Experiments on five image datasets (Caltech-101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN 397, Places 205, ImageNet 2012) show consistent top-k accuracy gains over Crammer-Singer, ranking-based baselines (SVMPerf, TopPush), and Wsabie variants; code is released.

The work is well positioned relative to: (i) multiclass SVMs (Crammer & Singer), (ii) ranking-based surrogates and LaRank (Usunier et al.; Weston et al.), (iii) structured large-margin optimization for multivariate measures (SVMPerf/recall@k), and (iv) efficient dual coordinate methods (Prox-SDCA). The paper clarifies how its surrogate differs from and can outperform the Usunier family (sum of top-k positive parts), and why structured recall@k surrogates are harder to scale.

Quality:
- Theory: The top-k hinge is convex, with clear inequalities relating it to the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss and to prior surrogates. The conjugate derivation leading to the top-k simplex is neat, and the j-compatibility condition makes the dual derivation clean. The projection algorithm is carefully formulated, distinguishing the knapsack and cone cases and giving complexity guarantees comparable to sorting-based simplex projections. The use of Prox-SDCA provides convergence guarantees inherited from prior work. Proofs are relegated to the supplement, which is standard; main statements are credible.
- Algorithms: The dual update reduces to a (biased) projection onto the top-k simplex, implemented efficiently with rank-1 primal updates. The discussion of when the constraint is tight (knapsack) vs inactive (cone) is helpful.
- Experiments: Datasets, baselines, and cross-validation are appropriate. The method scales to Places and ImageNet features, and top-k gains are consistent; some top-1 trade-offs are expected given the objective. While most comparisons are strong, additional runtime/memory comparisons and ablations (e.g., effect of the bias term ρ, sensitivity of k at train time to k at test time) would strengthen the empirical section. Reporting standard deviations on smaller datasets would also help.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The geometric intuition of the top-k simplex is helpful. Two clarity suggestions:
- Further motivate the choice of φk (average of the top-k scores) versus φ̃k (average of the top-k positive parts): provide more intuition and perhaps a small synthetic example illustrating when φk is strictly tighter and how that translates into empirical gains.
- Briefly explain the role and practical impact of the bias term ρ in the projection (frequency with which the “biased” case occurs and its effect on convergence).

Typo: page 3, “globally optimal leads to” should be “global optimization leads to” or remove “optimal.” Also check the stray “effective domain1” footnote marker.

Originality:
The top-k hinge loss and its conjugate yielding the top-k simplex appear novel, as does the associated projection algorithm with coupled upper bounds. The SDCA adaptation to this multiclass loss is a thoughtful application with nontrivial dual updates. The connections to, and improvements over, the Usunier ranking family and structured recall@k are well articulated.

Significance:
Optimizing top-k directly is important for large-class recognition. The method is scalable, improves top-k accuracy consistently, and offers a reusable optimization primitive (projection onto the top-k simplex) that may interest others. Given the ubiquity of top-k metrics, this contribution is likely to be used and extended.

Pros:
- Clear, principled surrogate with theoretical grounding and tighter bound than prior ranking surrogates in many cases.
- Elegant dual formulation and practical, efficient SDCA updates.
- Novel projection algorithm with careful analysis and competitive complexity.
- Strong empirical results on diverse, large-scale datasets; code release.

Cons:
- Limited discussion on selecting k during training when evaluation k differs; some evidence (e.g., training with k=10 helps top-5) is provided but more systematic guidance would help practitioners.
- Empirical analysis could be expanded: runtime/memory vs baselines, frequency/impact of cone vs knapsack cases, and ablations on ρ.
- No comparison to multinomial logistic regression (softmax) trained end-to-end on the same features, which is a common baseline for top-k; this would contextualize gains further.

Questions for the authors:
- How should users choose k at training time if they care about multiple k at evaluation? Is training with a larger k generally beneficial across smaller k?
- How sensitive are results to λ and ρ? Did the biased projection (ρ > 0) materially change convergence or accuracy compared to ρ = 0?
- What is the empirical distribution of projections falling into the knapsack vs cone regimes? Any observed worst-case behavior?
- Can you report training time and peak memory compared to Crammer-Singer and Wsabie on at least one large dataset?
- Have you tried a softmax baseline on the same fixed features for top-k accuracy?

Recommendation:
This is a solid, well-executed paper with clear theoretical and practical contributions and strong empirical support. I lean toward acceptance. The paper would be strengthened by a bit more motivation for φk vs φ̃k, brief guidance on training k selection, and a runtime/memory comparison on a large dataset.
