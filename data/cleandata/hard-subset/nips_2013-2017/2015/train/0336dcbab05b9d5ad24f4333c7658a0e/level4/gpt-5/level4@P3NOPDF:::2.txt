Summary
The paper proposes a top-k SVM for multiclass classification settings where only errors outside the top k predicted classes incur a cost (e.g., ImageNet-style evaluation). The authors extend the standard multiclass loss to a top-k loss and develop an efficient optimization procedure. Experiments on large-scale datasets demonstrate strong scalability and consistent improvements over competitive baselines. The problem is important and common in practice, especially when classes exhibit semantic similarity.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: optimizing directly for top-k accuracy aligns the training objective with widely used evaluation metrics.
- Methodological contribution: a principled extension of the multiclass loss to a top-k setting, with an emphasis on computational efficiency.
- Scalability: the proposed optimization appears to handle very large datasets, which is crucial for modern multiclass problems.
- Empirical results: improvements over compared methods are clear and practically meaningful, supporting the value of the approach.
- Presentation: the paper is easy to follow, with a coherent narrative from problem setup to algorithm and experiments.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Theory and properties of the loss:
  - Please clarify whether the proposed top-k loss is convex and under what assumptions. If nonconvex, what guarantees can be provided (e.g., convergence to stationary points, empirical stability)?
  - Discuss statistical properties: is the loss calibrated for top-k error, or can you provide bounds relating minimizing your surrogate to minimizing top-k misclassification?
  - Provide generalization guarantees or margin-based bounds tailored to the top-k setting, if available.
- Optimization details:
  - Elaborate on how you compute or approximate the top-k operator efficiently (e.g., partial sorting, heap-based selection), and the per-iteration complexity in terms of classes and features.
  - Clarify the subgradient/smoothing strategy used to handle nondifferentiability, and report convergence criteria and rate empirically.
  - Specify memory requirements and whether the method is amenable to minibatching, parallelization, or GPU acceleration.
- Choice of k and robustness:
  - Discuss how k should be selected in practice. Is the model trained for a single k or can it be adapted at test time to different k values?
  - Include a sensitivity analysis across multiple k’s (e.g., k = 1, 5, 10) and report whether a model trained for one k generalizes well to others.
  - Analyze performance under class imbalance and long-tail distributions common in large-scale datasets.
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Ensure comparisons include strong multiclass baselines, such as multinomial logistic regression (softmax), Crammer–Singer and Weston–Watkins SVM variants, and ranking-based losses optimized for top-k (e.g., WARP/WSABIE-like approaches).
  - Clearly describe feature representations and ensure identical features across methods. Provide comprehensive hyperparameter tuning protocols for all baselines.
  - If deep features or end-to-end neural baselines are relevant to your datasets, include them or justify their exclusion.
- Empirical analysis:
  - Add ablations isolating the contribution of the top-k loss versus standard hinge loss, as well as the effect of regularization and any approximations in the optimization.
  - Provide error analyses (e.g., confusion matrices or nearest-class visualizations) to demonstrate that gains arise particularly among semantically similar classes.
  - Report wall-clock time, throughput, and scaling with classes and examples to substantiate the efficiency claims.
- Clarity and reproducibility:
  - Define the top-k margin and tie-breaking rules precisely, and state how ties among scores within the top k are handled.
  - Include pseudocode for the optimization loop and details about stopping criteria.
  - Consider releasing code or a reference implementation to facilitate adoption and reproducibility.

Minor comments
- Tighten notation early (e.g., explicitly define score vectors, margin definitions, and indexing for the top-k operator).
- If using regularization, state its form (L2 vs. others) and its interaction with the top-k loss.
- Consider discussing how the approach relates to hierarchical or structured label spaces, where semantically similar classes are taxonomically related.

Conclusion
This is a well-motivated and clearly written paper addressing a practically important objective: directly optimizing top-k performance in multiclass classification. The proposed top-k SVM is methodologically sound, scales to large datasets, and delivers empirical gains over strong baselines. Strengthening the theoretical discussion, clarifying optimization details, broadening and tightening the experimental comparisons, and providing additional analyses would further solidify the contribution. Overall, I find the work promising and leaning toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the points above.
