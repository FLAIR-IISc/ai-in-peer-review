Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses multiclass learning when many classes are semantically similar and evaluation emphasizes top-k accuracy (e.g., ImageNet). The authors propose a direct optimization method for top-k performance by introducing a new convex “top-k hinge” loss, which upper-bounds the nonconvex top-k zero-one error more tightly than standard multiclass SVM. They show that this loss leads to a novel dual characterization whose feasible set is a “top-k simplex,” a contraction of the standard simplex that caps each coordinate by a fraction of the total mass. Building on this structure, they derive an efficient optimization algorithm using Proximal Stochastic Dual Coordinate Ascent (Prox-SDCA). A key technical component is a fast routine for (biased) projection onto the top-k simplex/top-k cone, which the authors analyze and implement.

The work extends the multiclass SVM of Crammer and Singer by replacing the standard hinge with a top-k variant, and contrasts with ranking-based losses such as those of Usunier et al., showing their new loss is a tighter convex bound in relevant regimes. It also relates to multivariate-performance optimization via structured SVM (e.g., SVMPerf for recall@k), but aims for decomposable, scalable updates. Experiments on five image classification datasets demonstrate improved top-k accuracy over baselines including Crammer-Singer, SVMPerf, TopPush, and Wsabie-like methods, with good scalability on large datasets.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: optimizing the metric that matters (top-k) in large multiclass problems with class ambiguity.
- Technical novelty: the top-k hinge loss and its conjugate characterization via the top-k simplex; efficient (biased) projection algorithms tailored to this geometry.
- Solid optimization framework: adaptation to Prox-SDCA with batch-wise dual updates and a principled stopping criterion via the duality gap.
- Empirical results: consistent gains in top-k accuracy across multiple datasets; evidence that training with k > 1 can improve top-1 in some cases; open-source implementation.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Fairness of baselines: several ranking methods are evaluated via a multiclass-to-binary reduction or one-vs-all substitutions when scaling is difficult, which may disadvantage them. Consider including multinomial logistic regression (softmax) trained on the same features, a widely used and strong baseline for top-k metrics, and clarify how Wsabie++ was tuned versus SDCA-based models.
- Complexity and practicality: while the projection algorithm has near-sorting complexity in the worst case, the paper should report detailed timing breakdowns (per-iteration projection time, fraction of iterations hitting the knapsack vs. cone cases) and memory usage, especially for large m and n. This would strengthen the scalability claims.
- Sensitivity to k: the experiments suggest that training with k different from the evaluation k can help (e.g., train with k = 10, evaluate top-5). It would be useful to systematize this with a brief sensitivity study and guidance for practitioners on choosing k at training time.
- Kernelization: the dual formulation suggests easy kernelization (W>xi = AKi), but storing A can be prohibitive at scale. Clarify practical limits or provide a note on low-rank/approximate strategies if kernelization is of interest.
- Clarity: the “biased projection equals zero” condition is sufficient (not necessary) for ρ > 0; a short discussion of how often the fallback triggers in practice would help. Minor notational suggestions: recap the roles of sets U, M, L in the main text for readability; provide intuition for the top-k simplex geometry (beyond Figure 2), e.g., how the per-coordinate cap trades off margin against the (m − k) least similar classes.

Quality:
- Theoretical development appears careful: the convex upper bound, conjugate derivation, and dual problem are well motivated. The reduction of the dual update to a (biased) projection problem is elegant and practically useful.
- Convergence guarantees are inherited from Prox-SDCA, and the presence of a computable duality gap is a strong practical point. A brief summary of convergence rates under the proposed loss would further aid readers.

Clarity:
- Overall well organized and readable, especially given the technical content. The algorithmic steps are explicit, and the relation to prior multiclass and ranking losses is clearly positioned. A few typos and notational inconsistencies could be polished, but they do not hinder understanding.

Originality:
- The top-k hinge loss and the associated top-k simplex/domain in the dual are, to my knowledge, new. The projection algorithm tailored to this domain is also novel and of independent interest.

Significance:
- Top-k accuracy is a standard metric in large-scale vision and other multiclass applications. A scalable, convex, and implementable method that directly targets top-k is valuable. The improvements reported, while sometimes modest, are consistent, and the code release increases impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and common evaluation metric with a principled, convex approach.
- Novel theory (loss, conjugate, feasible set) plus a practical algorithm with convergence guarantees.
- Strong, scalable implementation and empirical gains on multiple datasets.
- Clear positioning relative to Crammer-Singer and ranking-based alternatives; tighter bound than prior top-k surrogates.

Cons against acceptance:
- Baseline suite could be expanded (notably softmax logistic regression) and tuning fairness documented more thoroughly.
- Limited analysis of computational breakdown and sensitivity to k; more guidance to practitioners would be useful.
- Some technical details (e.g., biased projection edge cases) could be clarified with additional empirical diagnostics.

Overall assessment:
A well-motivated and technically solid contribution that directly optimizes top-k accuracy via a new convex surrogate and accompanying optimization tools. The paper is within scope for NIPS, advances the state of the art in multiclass learning, and is likely to be useful to practitioners. I recommend acceptance, contingent on strengthening the empirical comparisons and clarifying a few implementation details as noted above.
