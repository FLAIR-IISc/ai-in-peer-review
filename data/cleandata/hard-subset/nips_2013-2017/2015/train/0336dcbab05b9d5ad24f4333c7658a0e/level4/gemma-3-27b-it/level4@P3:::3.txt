This paper introduces a novel approach to multiclass SVMs by directly optimizing for top-k performance, motivated by the common scenario of class ambiguity in large-scale classification problems. The core idea is to generalize the standard multiclass SVM loss function to a top-k hinge loss, providing a convex upper bound on the top-k error. The authors also present an efficient optimization scheme based on projections onto a novel “top-k simplex” and demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across five datasets.

The work builds upon the established framework of Crammer and Singer’s multiclass SVM [5] and connects to recent work in ranking-based losses, specifically citing Usunier et al. [27]. The proposed top-k hinge loss is positioned effectively within this landscape, offering a different trade-off compared to existing formulations. The introduction of the top-k simplex is a noteworthy theoretical contribution, extending the standard simplex concept in a meaningful way.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The direct optimization of top-k performance using a convex upper bound is a compelling approach, particularly relevant for scenarios with numerous classes.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the top-k hinge loss and its conjugate, along with the definition of the top-k simplex, are well-presented and theoretically justified.
*   **Optimization Efficiency:** The proposed optimization scheme based on Prox-SDCA and efficient projections appears well-designed and practical, enabling scalability to large datasets.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results across five datasets consistently demonstrate improvements in top-k accuracy compared to various baselines, including the standard multiclass SVM and ranking-based methods.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the technical details accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Motivation for Loss Function:** While the motivation for using top-k loss in ambiguous classification scenarios is stated, a more detailed discussion of *why* this relaxation is effective compared to other approaches (e.g., adjusting confidence thresholds) would be beneficial. Further intuition beyond simply "relaxing the penalty" could strengthen the contribution.
*   **Connection to Ranking Losses:** The connection to ranking losses [27] is presented, but a more in-depth discussion of the differences and potential advantages of the proposed approach in specific ranking scenarios would be valuable. The authors briefly mention φ̃k(a) and its relation to their approach, however, a more thorough exploration of the conditions when our top-k loss is superior could be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments mention cross-validation for hyperparameters but provide limited detail on the sensitivity of the results to these settings. A brief analysis of hyperparameter robustness could bolster confidence in the findings.
*   **Minor Typo:** A minor typo exists on page 3 ("globally optimal leads to" should be "globally optimal").

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a relevant problem in multiclass classification.
*   The proposed method demonstrates consistent empirical improvements across a diverse set of datasets.
*   The optimization scheme is efficient and scalable.
*   The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The motivation for the specific form of the top-k loss could be further strengthened.
*   A more detailed exploration of the relationship to existing ranking losses would enhance the contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a well-motivated and technically sound solution. The experimental results are convincing, and the optimization framework appears practical. While the motivation for the specific loss function could be further elaborated, and the connection to ranking losses more thoroughly explored, the overall contribution is significant and warrants acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS 2015.
