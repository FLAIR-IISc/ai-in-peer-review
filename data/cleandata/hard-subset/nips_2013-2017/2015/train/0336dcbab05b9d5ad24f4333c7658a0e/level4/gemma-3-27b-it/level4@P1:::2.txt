In many multiclass classification problems with a large number of classes, semantic similarity between classes is often observed. This paper proposes an algorithm, termed top-k SVM, to explicitly address the learning scenario where a cost is incurred only when the ground truth label is outside the top *k* predicted classes (e.g., in the ImageNet challenge). After extending the multiclass SVM loss function to a top-*k* loss, an efficient optimization procedure is formulated. Experiments on large-scale datasets demonstrate the scalability of the algorithm and show clear improvements over compared methods.

The paper is well-motivated and easy to follow. The problem it addresses – the presence of similar classes in multiclass learning – is an important one that arises in many practical applications. This work formulates the top-k SVM algorithm to handle the case in multiclass classification where some classes may be very similar to each other. The algorithm scales to very large datasets and demonstrates improvements over comparable methods.

The authors begin by summarizing the multiclass SVM of Crammer and Singer [5] and then present their generalization to the top-*k* setting. They derive a tight convex upper bound of the top-*k* error and propose a fast optimization scheme based on an efficient projection onto the top-*k* simplex, which is itself a contribution of interest. The core idea is to relax the penalty for errors within the top *k* predictions, allowing for a more robust classifier when classes are difficult to discriminate. The proposed top-*k* hinge loss (Equation 3) is a key component, offering a convex relaxation of the top-*k* zero-one loss. A significant technical contribution lies in the definition and properties of the top-*k* simplex (∆<sub>k</sub>) and the derivation of its convex conjugate (Proposition 2). The paper also connects the proposed approach to ranking-based losses [27], demonstrating its relationship to existing work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper introduces a novel approach to multiclass SVM training that directly optimizes for top-*k* performance, addressing a practical limitation of standard zero-one loss.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation is solid, with a well-defined convex upper bound and a clear derivation of the dual problem and optimization procedure. The introduction of the top-*k* simplex and its conjugate is a valuable contribution.
*   **Scalability:** The proposed optimization scheme, utilizing Prox-SDCA, appears to be scalable to large datasets as demonstrated by the experiments on Places 205 and ImageNet.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently show improvements in top-*k* accuracy across five datasets compared to various baselines, validating the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** While generally clear, the mathematical notation and derivations can be dense and require careful reading.  Readers unfamiliar with convex optimization or structured SVMs might find some sections challenging.
*   **Comparison to Ranking Losses:** The connection to ranking-based losses [27] could be explored in greater depth.  A more detailed comparison, including a discussion of the trade-offs between the two approaches, would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The influence of the regularization parameter (λ) on performance is mentioned, but a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would be useful.
*   **Projection Implementation Details:** While the paper highlights the efficiency of the projection algorithm, providing more details on its implementation, such as the specific library used and computational costs, would be helpful for reproducibility.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a practical problem in multiclass classification.
*   The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements in top-*k* accuracy on a range of datasets.
*   The optimization procedure is scalable and efficient, making the approach applicable to large-scale problems.
*   The theoretical contributions, such as the definition of the top-*k* simplex, are valuable to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The mathematical complexity of the paper may limit its accessibility to some readers.
*   The connection to related work in ranking-based losses could be more thoroughly explored.
*   A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and the projection algorithm's implementation would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of multiclass SVMs. The proposed top-k SVM algorithm offers a practical and effective solution for handling class ambiguity in large-scale classification problems. While the paper could benefit from further elaboration on certain aspects, the novelty, technical soundness, scalability, and empirical results strongly support its acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper.
