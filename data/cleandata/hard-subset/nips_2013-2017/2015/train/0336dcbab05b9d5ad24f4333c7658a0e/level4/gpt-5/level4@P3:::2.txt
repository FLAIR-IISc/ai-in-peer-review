Summary
The paper addresses multiclass classification in settings with many semantically similar classes, where top-k evaluation (crediting a correct label if it appears among the top k predictions) is more appropriate than top-1. The authors introduce a convex top-k hinge loss that upper-bounds the discrete top-k error and generalizes the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM (k=1). They derive the conjugate of this loss and show that the effective domain is a new “top-k simplex,” a contraction of the standard simplex imposing xi ≤ (1/k)⟨1, x⟩. Based on this structure, they propose an efficient Prox-SDCA optimization scheme whose per-example dual update reduces to a (biased) projection onto the top-k simplex. They develop an algorithm for this projection via a case split: when the sum constraint is active, it reduces to a continuous quadratic knapsack; otherwise it becomes a projection onto a “top-k cone,” for which they provide a constructive solution. Experiments on five image datasets (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN 397, Places 205, ImageNet 2012) show consistent gains in top-k accuracy over baselines, including Crammer–Singer SVM, ranking-based surrogates (e.g., Usunier et al.’s Lβ with β uniform over top k; TopPush), and structured SVM approximations for recall@k. The method scales and code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Multiclass SVM: Extends Crammer–Singer [5] by replacing the max-margin surrogate with a convex average over the top-k largest margin violations (sum of k largest is convex [17]).
- Ranking losses: Connects to Usunier et al. [27] by showing their weighted ordered hinge yields a looser upper bound φ̃k than the proposed φk, with explicit inequalities ψk ≤ φk ≤ φ̃k ≤ φ1.
- Structured surrogates for finite-sample performance measures: Compares conceptually to SVMPerf [11] and TopPush [16], and clarifies when recall@k aligns with top-k error under a multiclass-to-binary reduction. Unlike these, the proposed loss is decomposable per instance and integrates cleanly with Prox-SDCA [24].
- Optimization: Builds on Prox-SDCA theory and contributes a new projection routine for the top-k simplex/cone not covered by standard simplex projection or CQKP algorithms.

Quality
- Theory: The loss is convex, tightly related to top-k error, and recovers Crammer–Singer for k=1. The conjugate and top-k simplex are derived cleanly; the dual/primal forms under Prox-SDCA are consistent. The projection algorithm is well-motivated; the case analysis and thresholding conditions (Lemmas 3–5) are plausible and supported by a supplement.
- Optimization: The per-iteration cost is dominated by BLAS operations and a projection with complexity O(d log d + kd) in the general case, which seems practical. The stopping criterion uses duality gaps.
- Empirics: On five datasets, the method improves top-k accuracy across settings; the paper acknowledges that top-1 may sometimes decrease, which is expected when optimizing top-k. Scalability to Places 205 and ImageNet 2012 is a strong point. Baselines are reasonably chosen, and hyperparameters are cross-validated.

Areas to improve:
- Theory: While convexity and conjugates are solid, there is no discussion of statistical consistency for top-k (e.g., conditions under which the surrogate is calibrated to top-k error). Even a brief discussion or a reference would strengthen the contribution.
- Optimization guarantees: The projection algorithm is described constructively; a clear worst-case complexity statement and empirical timing vs. standard simplex projection (some of which is in the supplement) would be useful in the main text.
- Experimental detail: Report training wall-clock time, memory usage, and convergence diagnostics (duality gaps) across datasets. Clarify whether the same feature extractor is used across methods and whether baselines are trained to the same convergence tolerance.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized, with good motivation and connections to prior work. Suggested edits:
- Notation: Early in Section 2, clearly define a, c, and indexing conventions. In Proposition 2, emphasize the relationship ∆k ⊂ ∆ and its implications.
- Algorithm: Expand Algorithm 1 to include the stopping tolerance and how the duality gap is computed; provide a concise pseudocode for the projection subroutine with its cases.
- Lemma statements: For Lemmas 3–5, provide brief intuition in the main text about the sets U, M, L and the role of thresholds (t, u).
- References: Several entries are missing years or have formatting errors (e.g., ImageNet challenge entry, some arXiv papers). Please fix for reproducibility.

Originality
The top-k hinge surrogate φk and its conjugate characterization via the top-k simplex, along with the associated projection algorithm, are novel and technically interesting. The connection and strict inequality versus φ̃k from Usunier et al. sharpen the understanding of top-k surrogates. The integration with Prox-SDCA is natural but nontrivial given the new projection.

Significance
Top-k metrics are ubiquitous in modern large-scale recognition. A principled, convex, scalable surrogate with demonstrated gains and publicly released code is likely to influence practice and subsequent research (e.g., calibration analyses, extensions to deep models, or other multilabel/multitask settings).

Pros
- Well-motivated objective aligned with top-k evaluation.
- Sound convex formulation with clear relation to prior surrogates.
- New top-k simplex and efficient projection algorithm.
- Scalable Prox-SDCA implementation with duality-gap stopping.
- Consistent empirical gains across diverse datasets; code release.

Cons
- No statistical consistency/calibration analysis for top-k.
- Limited runtime/memory reporting and ablation of projection cost.
- Some clarity issues in notation and reference formatting.
- A small subset of baselines rely on reductions that may not scale comparably; discuss fairness of comparisons and include one-vs-all baselines where necessary in the main text.

Questions for authors
1. Can you comment on surrogate calibration to top-k error? Are there known conditions under which φk is consistent?
2. How sensitive is performance to k used in training vs. evaluation? Provide a grid (train k vs. test k) analysis.
3. Please report training time, number of epochs, and duality gap curves, especially on SUN, Places, and ImageNet.
4. How does the biased projection (ρ > 0) affect convergence and accuracy compared to ρ = 0?
5. Could your projection be parallelized or implemented on GPUs?

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a principled, novel, and practical approach to optimizing top-k performance, with solid theory and convincing empirical results. Addressing clarity and empirical reporting will further strengthen the contribution.
