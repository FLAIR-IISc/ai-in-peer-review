

This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound contribution to the problem of top-k classification. The core idea—learning with respect to the top-k loss, where the loss is zero if the true class appears among the top-k predictions—is both novel and practically relevant, addressing a significant gap in handling multi-class prediction scenarios where exact top-1 accuracy is insufficient. The writing is clear and accessible, with a logical flow from problem formulation through methodological development to empirical validation.

The experimental results are comprehensive and compelling, demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines across multiple datasets and tasks. The ablation studies effectively isolate the impact of key design choices, providing strong evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach. The authors convincingly show that their method achieves state-of-the-art performance while maintaining computational feasibility, making a clear case for its practical utility.

The primary suggestion for improvement concerns the motivation behind the chosen loss function. While the top-k loss is introduced, the paper would benefit from a more detailed justification of *why* this specific formulation is preferable to alternatives (e.g., variants of the top-k loss or surrogate losses). Specifically, the authors should clarify: (1) how the chosen loss aligns with the specific objectives of their application domain, (2) its theoretical advantages over standard cross-entropy or top-1 losses in the context of their problem, and (3) whether any theoretical guarantees (e.g., generalization bounds) support its use. This deeper motivation would strengthen the methodological foundation and help readers better appreciate the novelty and necessity of the proposed loss. A brief discussion contrasting the top-k loss with related approaches in the literature would also be valuable.

Additionally, a minor typographical error was noted on page 3: "globally optimal leads to" should be corrected to "globally optimal leads to" (removing the redundant "optimal"). This appears to be a simple proofreading oversight.

Overall, this is a strong submission with significant technical merit. The problem addressed is important, the solution is well-executed, and the results are robust. The minor suggestions regarding loss function motivation and the typo are straightforward to address. The paper makes a clear, valuable contribution to the field of supervised learning for multi-class classification and deserves consideration for acceptance at NIPS. The clarity of presentation and thorough experimental validation further enhance its quality.
