Summary
This paper tackles the important setting of learning under a top-k evaluation criterion: the model produces k predictions, and the loss is zero if the true class is among them. The problem is non-trivial, the presentation is clear, and the experimental results are strong. Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation for focusing on top-k accuracy, which is relevant in many practical applications.
- The technical content appears substantive and generally well explained.
- Empirical results are convincing and suggest the proposed approach is effective.

Main suggestion: motivate and justify the loss function more deeply
The paper would benefit from a clearer and more thorough rationale for the specific loss function you optimize. In particular:
- Intuition: Provide an intuitive explanation of how the loss encourages correct classes to appear within the top k and how it differs from standard top-1 surrogates (e.g., cross-entropy or hinge).
- Theoretical properties: Discuss calibration/consistency with respect to the discrete top-k risk. Under what conditions is minimizing the proposed surrogate aligned with minimizing top-k error? If full proofs are out of scope, include at least a high-level argument or pointers.
- Relation to alternatives: Compare conceptually and empirically to other top-k-aware surrogates (e.g., top-k hinge variants, listwise/ranking-based losses, smoothed order-statistics losses). Clarify why your choice is preferable in terms of convexity/smoothness, optimization stability, and computational cost.
- Sensitivity to k: Explain how the loss behaves as k varies, especially when k is small versus large, and whether a model trained for one k generalizes to nearby k at test time.
- Gradient behavior: Offer insight into how the gradients shift focus from the top-1 margin to the top-k margin (e.g., pressure on the k-th and (k+1)-th scores). A small illustrative example would help.
- Practical considerations: Briefly discuss handling of ties around the k-th score, class imbalance, and the trade-off between top-1 and top-k performance.

Empirical suggestions (optional but helpful)
- Include an ablation varying k (e.g., k ∈ {1, 3, 5, 10}) to show the loss adapts as intended.
- Add comparisons to strong baselines trained with standard cross-entropy but evaluated on top-k, to quantify the added value of a top-k-aware surrogate.
- Report training dynamics (convergence behavior, stability) and computational overhead relative to standard losses.
- If possible, provide results where k differs between training and testing to assess robustness.

Clarity and presentation
- Consider adding a concise running example that demonstrates exactly how the loss is computed for a single instance and how errors at ranks around k are penalized.
- A short “design choices” paragraph summarizing why this loss was chosen (versus noted alternatives) would greatly strengthen the narrative.

Minor issues
- Page 3: “globally optimal leads to” — remove “optimal.”
- Proofread for similar small phrasing issues, though overall the writing quality is high.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written and interesting paper addressing a practically significant problem with solid empirical support. With a stronger motivation and justification for the specific loss function—both conceptually and relative to alternatives—the paper would be even more compelling. I view this as a strong submission and encourage acceptance.
