

This paper introduces a novel approach to multiclass classification by optimizing for top-k accuracy rather than the standard zero-one loss. The authors extend the well-known multiclass SVM of Crammer and Singer to the top-k setting, proposing a convex upper bound on the top-k error that enables efficient optimization. The core technical contribution is a new top-k hinge loss function, φk(a) = max{0, (1/k)∑ⱼ=1ᵏ(a+c)[ⱼ]}, which provides a tighter convex relaxation of the top-k zero-one loss than previous approaches like φ̃k(a) = (1/k)∑ⱼ=1ᵏmax{0, (a+c)[ⱼ]}. The authors derive the convex conjugate of this loss and introduce an efficient projection algorithm onto the top-k simplex, which is a key technical innovation. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across five image classification datasets, including large-scale datasets like ImageNet and Places 205.

The paper excels in theoretical quality, providing a rigorous derivation of the top-k hinge loss and its convex conjugate, which naturally extends the multiclass SVM framework. The authors carefully establish the relationship between their loss and existing ranking-based losses (Usunier et al., 2012), showing that their formulation provides a strictly better convex upper bound than previous approaches. The optimization framework using Prox-SDCA is well-motivated, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the dual problem and efficient projection algorithm onto the top-k simplex. The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across multiple datasets, with improvements of up to +2.6% in top-5 accuracy compared to standard multiclass SVM. The implementation is scalable to large datasets with millions of examples, which is a significant practical contribution.

The paper's clarity is excellent. The technical content is presented in a logical flow, with clear definitions and well-structured proofs. The authors effectively illustrate the top-k simplex in Figure 2, which aids in understanding the geometric interpretation of their approach. The writing is precise and accessible to readers familiar with SVMs and convex optimization.

Regarding originality, this work makes a clear contribution to the field of multiclass classification by addressing the practical limitation of standard zero-one loss in high-class-count scenarios. While the general idea of top-k classification has been explored (e.g., by Joachims, 2005; Li et al., 2013), this paper provides a novel convex optimization framework that directly optimizes for top-k performance rather than using approximations or reductions. The top-k hinge loss and efficient projection algorithm represent significant technical innovations that distinguish this work from previous approaches. The authors appropriately reference related work, including the ranking-based framework of Usunier et al. (2012), and clearly articulate how their approach differs.

The significance of this work is substantial. Many real-world classification tasks involve large numbers of classes (e.g., ImageNet with 1,000 classes), where top-k accuracy is a more meaningful metric than top-1. The paper demonstrates that optimizing directly for top-k performance yields consistent improvements across diverse datasets, which has clear practical implications for applications like image recognition. The scalability to large datasets like ImageNet (with 1.2 million examples) makes this approach immediately applicable to real-world problems. The authors also provide a C++ library with MATLAB interface, facilitating adoption by the research community.

The only minor weakness is that the paper could strengthen its motivation for choosing φk over φ̃k. While the authors note that φk is a strictly better upper bound, a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of this difference—particularly regarding margin maximization and generalization—would be valuable. The authors do compare both versions experimentally in Section 5, but a more thorough theoretical justification for preferring φk would strengthen the paper.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field. The proposed top-k multiclass SVM provides a theoretically grounded and practically efficient method for optimizing top-k accuracy, which is particularly relevant for high-class-count classification problems. The experimental results consistently demonstrate improvements over baselines, and the implementation is scalable to large datasets. The work is well-positioned to influence future research in multiclass classification and has clear potential for practical application in computer vision tasks.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to NIPS. It addresses an important practical problem with a novel technical approach, provides rigorous theoretical analysis, and demonstrates significant empirical improvements. The quality of the work is excellent, the clarity is high, the originality is clear, and the significance is substantial. I recommend acceptance.
