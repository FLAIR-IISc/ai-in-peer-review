This paper addresses the common challenge in multiclass classification – particularly with a large number of classes – where semantic similarity between classes is prevalent. The authors propose a “top-k SVM” algorithm explicitly designed for scenarios where misclassification cost is only incurred if the ground truth label falls *outside* the top *k* predicted classes, a setting frequently encountered in challenges like ImageNet. The paper extends the standard SVM loss function to a top-k loss and, crucially, develops an efficient optimization procedure to handle this extended formulation. Experimental results on large-scale datasets demonstrate both the scalability of the proposed algorithm and a clear performance improvement over existing methods.

The motivation for this work is strong and well-articulated. The problem of handling semantic similarity in multiclass learning is indeed significant and arises frequently in practical applications. The paper clearly identifies this issue and proposes a principled solution. The concept of top-k accuracy and its associated loss function are logically presented and easy to understand.

The core contribution of this work lies in the formulation of the top-k SVM and the efficient optimization strategy. The authors successfully adapt the standard SVM framework to account for the relaxed error criterion inherent in top-k evaluation. The presented optimization procedure appears to be a key enabler for scaling the algorithm to large datasets, a critical requirement for the intended application domain. 

The experimental evaluation is a significant strength of this paper. The authors demonstrate scalability on large-scale datasets, which is essential for establishing the practical utility of the algorithm. The reported performance improvements over competitive baselines are encouraging and support the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  It would be beneficial to include a more detailed analysis of *where* the improvements are most pronounced – are certain types of classes, or particular datasets, more amenable to the top-k SVM? A discussion of the computational cost of the proposed method relative to the baselines would also be valuable. 

While the paper is generally well-written, some minor areas could benefit from further clarification. For example, the specific details of the optimization procedure, beyond its stated efficiency, could be expanded upon.  A more detailed description of how the algorithm handles ties in the top-k predictions would also be helpful.  

Overall, this is a well-motivated and technically sound paper. The proposed top-k SVM algorithm offers a valuable contribution to the field of multiclass classification, particularly for large-scale problems where semantic similarity between classes is a key consideration. The strong experimental results and the emphasis on scalability make this a promising approach.
