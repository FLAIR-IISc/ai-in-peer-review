This paper presents a novel and interesting approach to learning with a top-k classification loss. The core idea is to train a model to produce a set of *k* predictions, where the loss is zero if the true class is included within this set. This directly addresses a practical and important problem setting, particularly relevant in large-scale classification tasks where providing a small, accurate set of options is more valuable than insisting on a single top-ranked guess.

The material is technically non-trivial, and the paper is very well-written and clearly structured, making the proposed methodology easy to follow. The problem itself is well-motivated, and the authors make a compelling case for its relevance. Furthermore, the empirical evaluation is thorough, and the experimental results are strong, providing convincing evidence for the effectiveness of directly optimizing the proposed top-k objective.

My main suggestion for improvement is to further motivate and justify the specific choice of the loss function. While the proposed formulation is intuitive and clearly performs well in practice, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed discussion of its theoretical properties and a comparison with potential alternatives. For instance, is the proposed loss a convex surrogate for the true (0-1) top-k loss? A discussion of its relationship to other established loss functions (e.g., hinge loss, logistic loss) and an explanation of why this particular formulation was chosen over other plausible surrogates would provide valuable context and make the authors' design choices more compelling. Clarifying these points would solidify the paper's contribution.

On a minor note, I spotted a typo on page 3. In the phrase "globally optimal leads to," the word "optimal" appears to be redundant and should be removed.

Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper that addresses an interesting problem with a sound approach. The contributions are clear, and the results are convincing. With a slightly expanded discussion on the motivation for the loss function, this would be an excellent contribution to the conference.
