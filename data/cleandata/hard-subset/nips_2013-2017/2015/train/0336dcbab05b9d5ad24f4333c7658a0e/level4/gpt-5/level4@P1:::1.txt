Summary
This paper introduces a top-k multiclass SVM that directly optimizes a convex surrogate of the top-k error. The key idea is a top-k hinge loss that upper-bounds the nonconvex top-k zero-one loss by averaging the k largest margins, exploiting the convexity of the sum of the k largest components. The authors derive the convex conjugate of this loss and show that it leads to a “top-k simplex” constraint set, which extends the standard simplex used in the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM (recovered when k = 1). Building on this formulation, they develop an efficient optimization method using Prox-SDCA, where the core computational step reduces to a (biased) projection onto the top-k simplex. They provide an algorithm for this projection, analyze its complexity, and demonstrate that the overall approach scales to large datasets. Empirically, on five image classification benchmarks, the method yields consistent gains in top-k accuracy compared to strong baselines, including standard multiclass SVMs and ranking-based approaches.

Relation to prior work
The work extends Crammer and Singer’s multiclass SVM by replacing the top-1 hinge with a top-k hinge based on the sum of the k largest scores. It is closely related to the ranking losses of Usunier et al., where the authors compare two variants: their proposed tighter top-k hinge and a weighted ordered loss that averages k truncated hinges. They also connect to structured SVM approaches for recall@k (e.g., SVMPerf) via multiclass-to-binary reductions, and to prior work on continuous quadratic knapsack problems for simplex projections, highlighting why existing projection algorithms do not directly apply to the top-k simplex.

Strengths
- A clean, well-motivated convex surrogate for top-k error that directly targets the evaluation metric of interest in large-class settings.
- Solid theoretical development: conjugate derivations, the definition and properties of the top-k simplex, and compatibility with Fenchel duality for Prox-SDCA.
- An efficient and practically useful projection algorithm onto the top-k simplex, which is of independent interest.
- Clear presentation and thorough experimental evaluation showing consistent improvements in top-k metrics across multiple datasets, including large-scale scenarios.

Weaknesses and questions
1) The paper does not include comparisons with [25] and [8], reportedly due to lack of available implementations. I am genuinely interested in these comparisons; even partial or approximate reproductions (or a careful discussion of expected behavior) would strengthen the empirical section.

2) Code availability: Could the authors commit to releasing the code upon acceptance? This would facilitate reproducibility and adoption.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The paper appears technically sound. The derivations are carefully presented, and the empirical results support the claims about top-k performance. The optimization method is well grounded with a clear stopping criterion via the duality gap.
- Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. The introduction of the top-k simplex and its geometric intuition are helpful, and the relationship to existing ranking losses is well articulated.
- Originality: The top-k hinge loss and its conjugate via the top-k simplex constitute a novel and interesting extension of multiclass SVMs. The projection algorithm is another original and useful contribution.
- Significance: Top-k metrics are central in high-cardinality classification. Providing a scalable, principled method that directly optimizes a tight surrogate for top-k error is likely to be impactful for both research and practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, elegant formulation of top-k multiclass SVM with a tight convex upper bound.
- A practical optimization framework with an efficient projection routine and strong convergence guarantees.
- Consistent empirical gains on multiple datasets, including large-scale benchmarks.
- Clear writing and well-motivated connections to prior work.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing comparisons with [25] and [8]; including these (or discussing them more deeply) would further validate the approach.
- Clarification on code release would improve reproducibility.

Overall assessment
A neat, well-executed formulation of top-k multiclass SVM that works well in practice. The theoretical and algorithmic contributions are solid, and the empirical results are compelling. I recommend acceptance, and I encourage the authors to add the requested comparisons and to release the code.
