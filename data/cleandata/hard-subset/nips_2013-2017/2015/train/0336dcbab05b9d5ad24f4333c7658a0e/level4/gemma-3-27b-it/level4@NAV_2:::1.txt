This paper introduces a novel top-k multiclass SVM formulation designed to directly optimize for top-k accuracy, a relevant metric for image classification tasks with a large number of classes. The core idea is to leverage a convex upper bound – based on the sum of the k largest components – to approximate the top-k zero-one loss, enabling efficient optimization. The authors establish connections to existing work, including Crammer and Singer [5] and Usunier et al. [27], and propose an optimization scheme with an efficient projection onto the top-k simplex. Experiments on five datasets demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy compared to various baselines.

The paper is technically sound and presents a logical progression from motivation to method and experimental validation. The proposed convex relaxation of the top-k loss is a key contribution, and the development of an efficient projection onto the top-k simplex is a valuable technical result in its own right. The connection to ranking-based losses and the discussion of the multiclass to binary reduction further demonstrate the authors' awareness of the broader field. The experimental results are well-presented and support the claims of improved top-k performance. The clarity of the paper is commendable; it is well-organized and the technical concepts are explained thoroughly. The inclusion of the theoretical derivations and algorithmic details enables reproducibility for expert readers. 

The main strength of the work lies in its direct optimization of the top-k metric, offering a principled approach to address class ambiguity in large-scale classification problems. The proposed optimization framework, leveraging Prox-SDCA and the efficient projection, appears well-suited for practical application. The comparisons to existing methods (LibLinear, SVMPerf, TopPush, Wsabie++) provide a solid benchmark for the proposed method.

However, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm compared to existing approaches, especially regarding the projection step. While the authors mention the efficiency of the projection, a more detailed analysis would be helpful. The authors acknowledge the lack of comparison to methods like Swersky et al. [25] and Zhou et al. [8] due to implementation availability; while understandable, it would be beneficial if the authors could indicate whether these comparisons will be added if the paper is accepted and code is released. Furthermore, while the paper demonstrates performance gains on the tested datasets, exploring the sensitivity of the method to the choice of the regularization parameter and the value of k would strengthen the analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled formulation of top-k multiclass SVM.
*   Efficient optimization algorithm with a novel projection method.
*   Consistent experimental improvements over strong baselines.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Strong theoretical grounding and connections to related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of comparison to certain relevant methods ([25, 8]) due to implementation constraints.
*   Limited analysis of computational complexity.
*   Sensitivity analysis of key parameters (regularization, k) could be expanded.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The proposed method addresses a practical problem with a sound theoretical and algorithmic foundation, and the experimental results are convincing. While the minor weaknesses mentioned above could be addressed, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
