Summary
This paper introduces a top-k hinge loss tailored for top-k multiclass classification. The loss is defined via the sum of the k largest class-score differences, providing a convex surrogate to the desired top-k error and reducing to the multiclass SVM when k=1. The authors present an efficient optimization algorithm and situate the method within relevant prior work. The paper is clearly written, and empirical results indicate consistent improvements over strong baselines.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated formulation: The proposed loss directly targets top-k performance and elegantly generalizes the multiclass SVM by varying k.
- Convexity and practicality: Using the sum of the k largest components yields a convex, tractable objective, which is attractive both theoretically and in practice.
- Optimization: The paper provides an efficient training algorithm, making the approach scalable and usable.
- Positioning within prior work: The connections to existing multiclass SVM surrogates and related top-k objectives are well discussed.
- Empirical effectiveness: Experiments demonstrate meaningful gains over baseline methods across datasets, supporting the main claims.

Weaknesses / Concerns
- Missing baselines: The paper does not report comparisons with [25, 8] due to unavailability of implementations. While this is understandable, these methods appear to be among the most directly comparable, and their absence weakens the empirical validation.
- Reproducibility: It is unclear whether code and scripts will be released. Given that the contribution is both methodological and algorithmic, public code would meaningfully strengthen the paper’s impact and reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. Comparisons with [25, 8]: If obtaining the original implementations is infeasible, could you provide:
   - A reimplementation of the key components sufficient to benchmark on at least one dataset, or
   - A careful proxy comparison (e.g., using reported numbers on shared datasets, or a controlled ablation that mimics their loss where possible), and
   - A discussion of where and why your method is expected to outperform or underperform these approaches?
2. Code release: Can you commit to releasing code and experiment scripts upon acceptance? Please specify the planned platform (e.g., Python/MATLAB, dependencies), and whether you will include routines for computing the top-k hinge, gradients/subgradients, and the optimization algorithm.

Minor suggestions (if space permits in the camera-ready)
- Clearly state the computational complexity per iteration and how it scales with number of classes and k, and include basic timing results to complement accuracy metrics.
- Provide guidance on choosing k and any observed sensitivity to hyperparameters, especially regularization.
- Clarify how ties in scores are handled in the top-k evaluation and whether this affects the surrogate’s alignment with the metric.

Overall assessment
A neat and well-executed formulation of a top-k multiclass SVM that works well in practice. The contribution is clear, the method is principled and convex, and the optimization appears efficient. Addressing the missing comparisons with [25, 8]—or at least providing a stronger discussion/approximation—together with a commitment to release code would substantially strengthen the paper. I lean positive, contingent on clarifying these points.
