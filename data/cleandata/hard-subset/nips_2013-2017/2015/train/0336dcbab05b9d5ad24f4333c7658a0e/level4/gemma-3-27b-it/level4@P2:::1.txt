This paper introduces a novel approach to multiclass classification by directly optimizing for top-k accuracy, proposing a “top-k hinge loss” as a convex relaxation of the top-k zero-one loss. The method generalizes the well-known multiclass SVM formulation of Crammer and Singer [5] and leverages the convexity of the sum of the k largest components to enable efficient optimization. A key contribution is the derivation of a tractable optimization scheme, alongside an analysis of its relation to existing ranking-based losses, like those presented in Usunier et al. [27]. The authors demonstrate empirically, across five datasets, that their approach consistently improves top-k accuracy compared to several baselines.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in multiclass SVMs, notably Crammer and Singer [5], and connects to recent advancements in ranking-based losses [27]. It addresses the common issue of class ambiguity in large-scale classification problems by shifting the optimization target from strict correctness (zero-one loss) to a more lenient criterion that values having the correct class within the top-k predictions. The choice to use the sum of the k largest components as a convex upper bound is a clever and practical solution, and the associated development of an efficient projection onto the top-k simplex is a noteworthy technical contribution. The presentation is generally clear and well-organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The direct optimization of top-k accuracy with a convex relaxation is a valuable contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the convex upper bound and the derivation of the dual problem, appears rigorous.
*   **Efficiency:** The proposed optimization scheme, based on Prox-SDCA and efficient projection, addresses a key challenge in scaling these methods to large datasets.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The consistent improvements observed across five diverse datasets provide strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the ideas are presented in a logical and understandable manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Comparison Limitations:** The authors acknowledge a lack of comparison to methods like Swersky et al. [25] and Zhou et al. [8] due to implementation availability. While understandable, this limits the scope of the empirical evaluation.  The impact of this omission on the reported gains should be acknowledged more explicitly.
*   **Code Availability:** The authors do not currently plan to release their implementation. Making the code publicly available would greatly benefit the research community and facilitate further investigation and reproducibility.
*   **Discussion of Failure Cases:**  The paper focuses primarily on successful results. Exploring instances where the top-k approach *doesn’t* outperform the standard SVM, and analyzing the reasons for this, could provide valuable insights.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a relevant problem (top-k classification).
*   The theoretical contributions (convex relaxation, efficient optimization) are significant.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate consistent improvements.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of comparison to certain related methods ([25, 8]) limits the comprehensiveness of the evaluation.
*   The absence of publicly available code hinders reproducibility and further research.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of multiclass classification. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated.  While the limitations regarding comparisons and code availability are present, the strengths of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with the encouragement that the authors consider releasing their code post-acceptance and include a discussion of scenarios where the proposed method might not be superior.
