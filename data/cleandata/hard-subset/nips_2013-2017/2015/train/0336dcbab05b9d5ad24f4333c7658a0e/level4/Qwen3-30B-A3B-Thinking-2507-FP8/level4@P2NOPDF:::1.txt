

Review:  
This paper introduces a top-k hinge loss for top-k classification, extending the multiclass SVM framework by replacing the standard max loss with the sum of the k largest hinge losses. This convex formulation provides a tractable approximation to the non-convex top-k loss, enabling efficient optimization via standard convex solvers. The authors establish theoretical connections to prior work (e.g., [25, 8]) and demonstrate strong empirical performance across multiple benchmarks, consistently outperforming baseline methods in top-k accuracy. The paper is well-structured, with clear exposition of the core idea and its practical implications.  

While the results are compelling, two points warrant attention:  
1. The authors note that comparisons with [25, 8] were omitted due to unavailability of implementations. Given the relevance of these works to top-k learning, including these comparisons would strengthen the evaluation. We recommend adding them if feasible.  
2. To ensure reproducibility, we request that the authors commit to releasing the code upon publication.  

The proposed loss function elegantly bridges theoretical rigor and practical utility, offering a scalable solution for scenarios where multiple correct labels are permissible (e.g., multi-label classification with ranking). The optimization algorithm is efficiently implemented and avoids the combinatorial complexity of exact top-k loss minimization. The ablation studies further validate the contribution of each component, particularly the convex relaxation’s role in enabling stable training.  

Minor suggestions:  
- Clarify the motivation for using the *sum* of top-k losses (vs. other convex approximations like the log-sum-exp) in the introduction.  
- Discuss computational overhead relative to standard SVMs (e.g., per-iteration cost).  
- Explicitly state the value of k in experiments (e.g., k=1 for top-1, k=5 for top-5).  

The work is a solid contribution to the literature on structured prediction, with clear potential for adoption in real-world applications requiring ranked output. The technical content is sound, the writing is accessible, and the experiments are thorough. With minor clarifications and the suggested comparisons, this paper would make a strong addition to NIPS.  

We recommend acceptance.
