Summary
The paper proposes a convex, margin-based formulation for optimizing top-k accuracy in multiclass classification, extending the Crammer-Singer multiclass SVM. The key contributions are: (1) a top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) Σ_{j=1..k} (a+c)[j]}, which is a tight convex upper bound to the discrete top-k error and tighter than the ordered hinge studied by Usunier et al.; (2) a clean Fenchel-dual derivation yielding a conjugate with feasible set given by a novel “top-k simplex” ∆k, leading to a compact dual; (3) an efficient Prox-SDCA optimization scheme where per-example dual updates reduce to (biased) Euclidean projections onto ∆k(r); (4) an algorithm for projecting onto the top-k simplex/top-k cone with O(d log d + k d) complexity; and (5) empirical evidence on five image datasets (including SUN397, Places205, and ImageNet) showing consistent improvements in top-k accuracy over Crammer-Singer SVM and ranking-based baselines (SVMPerf, TopPush, Wsabie++), with open-source code.

Relation to prior work
The work generalizes Crammer-Singer (JMLR’01/02) and connects to ranking-based convex surrogates (Usunier et al., ICML’09) and Joachims’ multivariate performance optimization (ICML’05). The proposed φk is shown to be strictly tighter than the average-of-top-k positive-part surrogate used in ranking. Unlike structured SVM formulations for recall@k, the proposed loss decomposes instance-wise, enabling scalability via SDCA. The reduction-to-binary baselines are appropriate and common. The paper appears to fill a gap between ranking surrogates and multiclass SVMs by providing a principled top-k multiclass loss with efficient optimization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction of φk as a convex upper bound, the derivation of its conjugate with domain ∆k, and the SDCA dual are carefully presented. The yi-compatibility condition and the correction to Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang’s derivation are thoughtful. The projection algorithm is novel because the upper bounds depend on the sum, coupling coordinates; the analysis via partitions (U, M, L) and thresholds (t, u) is plausible, with proofs deferred to the supplement. The bias term ρ is handled systematically.
- Empirical support: The experiments demonstrate consistent gains in top-k accuracy across several datasets. However, many large-scale results and timing comparisons are relegated to the supplement, and statistical significance or confidence intervals are not reported. Improvements are modest (often 1–3% absolute) but consistent.
- Reproducibility: The algorithmic description, dual/primal forms, and code release increase reproducibility. Some implementation details (e.g., stopping tolerance, precise SDCA schedule, projection corner cases) could be clarified in the main paper.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, though notation is dense. The introduction of ∆k is intuitive (nice 2D illustration). The dual derivation is compact and readable. The projection section is the most challenging; adding an algorithm box or pseudocode for the cone/simplex projection (not only the high-level strategy) would help. A brief intuition for the bias term ρ (why ρ = 1 arises and its effect) would improve readability.

Originality
Defining the top-k hinge φk that averages the k largest margins and showing its conjugate with the top-k simplex is, to my knowledge, novel. The projection algorithm for the top-k simplex with variable upper bounds tied to the sum is also new and of independent interest. The combination with Prox-SDCA is natural but well executed.

Significance
Optimizing top-k accuracy is practically important in large-class recognition, where top-5/10 metrics are standard. A scalable, decomposable surrogate with an efficient solver is valuable. The improvements over strong linear baselines are consistent, and the projection method could be reused elsewhere. The impact would be higher with broader comparisons and stronger evidence on very large m (e.g., 10k+ classes).

Strengths
- Clear, principled surrogate for top-k error with provable tightness relative to existing surrogates.
- Elegant dual with a new geometric object (top-k simplex) that clarifies the structure.
- Practical, scalable optimization via Prox-SDCA and an efficient projection algorithm.
- Consistent empirical gains in top-k metrics; code released.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Missing comparisons to multinomial logistic regression/softmax (cross-entropy), which is a standard multiclass baseline and often strong on top-k metrics with CNN features.
- Limited discussion on the choice of training k versus evaluation k (e.g., train with k=10 but evaluate top-5); more systematic ablations would help.
- Lack of statistical significance analysis; improvements are modest on some datasets.
- Many timing and large-scale results are only in the supplement; main paper should include at least one detailed wall-clock/time-per-epoch plot and memory footprint.
- Scalability in the number of classes m: per-update cost scales with sorting O(m log m). Some discussion or experiments varying m (beyond 1k) would strengthen claims.
- No theory on calibration/consistency with top-k error. Even a brief discussion (e.g., conditions under which φk is top-k calibrated) would be valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a multinomial logistic regression baseline (one-vs-rest logistic or true softmax) trained on the same features; report top-k metrics.
- Provide a controlled ablation: train with k ∈ {1, 3, 5, 10} and evaluate top-k’ for k’ ∈ {1,3,5,10} to illustrate trade-offs and robustness to mismatch.
- Report variance over splits or confidence intervals; indicate whether improvements are statistically significant.
- Include main-text runtime/memory comparisons for the projection (vs simplex projection) and end-to-end training on at least one large dataset.
- Discuss possible warm-starts or partial sorting to reduce per-iteration overhead when m is large.
- Expand on the intuition and role of the bias term ρ and why ρ=1 arises in the dual update.
- Clarify whether the method kernelizes efficiently in practice and provide at least a small-scale kernel result.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, novel surrogate and optimization method for an important metric.
- Clear theoretical development; practical algorithm with code.
- Consistent empirical gains across datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing key baseline (softmax/logistic); modest improvements in places without statistical analysis.
- Some critical scalability and runtime evidence only in the supplement.
- Limited theoretical discussion on calibration/consistency.

Minor comments and typos
- Check reference details (years/pages missing in several citations).
- Define all variables upon first use (e.g., pk in Lemma 5; ensure consistent notation for vectors vs indices).
- Consider adding pseudocode for the projection algorithm in the main text for completeness.

Overall, this is a well-motivated, technically sound contribution with practical relevance. Addressing the baseline and empirical clarity concerns would strengthen the paper further.
