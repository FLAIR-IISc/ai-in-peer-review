Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a direct large-margin method for optimizing top-k accuracy in multiclass classification. Starting from the Crammer–Singer multiclass SVM loss [5], the work introduces a convex upper bound to the top-k zero-one loss, termed the top-k hinge loss φk(a) = max{0, (1/k) Σ_{j=1}^k (a+c)[j]}, where (a+c)[j] denotes the j-th largest component among class score differences. The formulation recovers the standard multiclass SVM at k=1. A central technical contribution is the characterization of the convex conjugate of φk, whose effective domain is a “top-k simplex” ∆k, a contraction of the standard simplex with an additional coupling upper bound xi ≤ (1/k)〈1,x〉. This leads to a dual problem amenable to Proximal Stochastic Dual Coordinate Ascent (Prox-SDCA) [24], with the per-instance update reducible to (biased) Euclidean projection onto ∆k(r). The paper derives an efficient projection algorithm via a case analysis (knapsack regime vs “top-k cone”), providing complexity O(d log d + k d).

The paper situates the loss within the ranking-based family of ordered weighted surrogates Lβ(a) by Usunier et al. [27]. With βj = 1/k for j ≤ k and 0 otherwise, a related surrogate φ̃k(a) = (1/k) Σ_{j=1}^k max{0, (a+c)[j]} is obtained; the proposed φk is shown to be a strictly tighter upper bound than φ̃k in a nontrivial regime ((a+c)[1] > 0 but (a+c)[k] < 0). Connections to structured optimization of multivariate measures (e.g., recall@k via SVMPerf [11]) and top-push surrogates [16] are discussed, and a multiclass-to-binary reduction is employed for experimental comparisons.

Experiments on five image classification datasets (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN 397, Places 205, and ImageNet 2012) using CNN features show consistent gains in top-k accuracy over one-vs-all SVM, Crammer–Singer SVM [5], SVMPerf [11], TopPush [16], and a Wsabie-style method [9]. The solvers and projection code are released.

Strengths:
- Well-motivated objective: directly targets top-k accuracy, a prevalent metric in large-scale, ambiguous multiclass recognition.
- Sound convex surrogate: φk is convex, tighter than a natural ordered-hinge surrogate φ̃k [27], and strictly tighter than standard multiclass SVM for k>1; the conjugate derivation is clean and connects naturally to ∆k.
- Optimization framework: principled dual derivation under Fenchel duality with yi-compatibility; Prox-SDCA is a strong choice with convergence guarantees and a computable duality gap.
- Novel projection primitive: the top-k simplex is a nontrivial feasible set not handled by standard CQKP approaches [18,19]; the proposed projection algorithm, with a clear case distinction and complexity analysis, is of independent interest.
- Empirical evidence: broad evaluation with competitive baselines, including ranking-based methods [11,16], shows consistent improvements in top-k metrics; scalability to Places205 and ImageNet is emphasized.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Theoretical guarantees beyond convexity: while φk is a tighter upper bound, there is no analysis of calibration/consistency to the top-k risk or margin-based generalization bounds specialized to φk. A brief discussion (or a pointer to known results that would imply Fisher consistency for top-k under separability assumptions) would strengthen the theoretical contribution. This is an optional improvement.
- Projection algorithm clarity and worst-case complexity: the exhaustive search over partitions in the “cone” case is bounded by (k−1)(d−k+1) with sorting cost O(d log d). More guidance on expected behavior in practice (e.g., empirical distribution of cases; frequency of falling back to cone vs knapsack; effect of k and d) and a small ablation comparing projection wall-clock with standard simplex projections [13] would improve clarity. This seems feasible given the existing supplement.
- Empirical reporting details: top-k gains are reported, but wall-clock time and memory comparisons to baselines (including SVM-OVA, Crammer–Singer, SVMPerf/TopPush) are not clearly summarized in the main text. Adding a concise runtime table for at least the medium-scale datasets (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT67, SUN397) would help assess practicality. This is an important but tractable revision.
- Hyperparameter selection: the cross-validation protocol optimizes over a wide range, but it is not explicit which metric was used for model selection when optimizing top-k losses (e.g., top-k on a validation set vs top-1). Clarifying this and ensuring that baselines are tuned for the same evaluation metric would address potential fairness concerns.
- Scope of baselines: deep softmax models trained end-to-end often excel at top-k accuracy on large-scale datasets. While the paper’s focus is linear classifiers on fixed CNN features, including a comparison to multinomial logistic regression on the same features (top-k performance) would be informative and easy to add.

Quality:
The derivations are careful and technically sound, with appropriate use of convex analysis (conjugates, compatibility condition, dual derivation). The novel feasible set ∆k and the projection procedures are well-motivated, and the optimization method is standard but solid. Claims about tightness relative to φ̃k [27] are justified. Experimental methodology is broadly appropriate; scalability claims are credible given the SDCA framework and per-iteration complexity.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The exposition of ∆k, the role of k, and the relationship to prior ranking losses is effective. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement; brief proof sketches for key lemmas (e.g., Lemma 3’s partition structure) would aid readability. Algorithmic pseudocode is helpful; adding a concise complexity summary table for projection variants would further improve clarity.

Originality:
The top-k hinge loss and the associated top-k simplex/cone projection are novel and nontrivial extensions of multiclass SVM and simplex projection, respectively. The connection to ordered hinge losses [27] is acknowledged, and the proposed surrogate is strictly tighter in relevant regimes. The optimization via Prox-SDCA is a judicious adaptation rather than a novelty per se.

Significance:
Top-k metrics are central in modern large-scale recognition. Providing a direct, convex, and scalable surrogate with demonstrated gains over strong baselines is valuable. The projection algorithm may be reused in related structured problems. The significance would be further enhanced by adding logistic regression baselines and runtime comparisons, but current evidence already suggests practical impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation of a top-k large-margin surrogate with tighter bounds than prior ordered-hinge variants.
- Novel and useful projection onto the top-k simplex with practical complexity.
- Strong empirical improvements in top-k accuracy across multiple datasets, including large-scale settings; code release.
- Solid optimization framework with convergence guarantees and duality gap stopping.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited theoretical guarantees on consistency/calibration to top-k risk.
- Incomplete runtime/memory comparisons and selection-metric details in the main text.
- Missing multinomial logistic regression baseline on the same features.

Requested revisions (prioritized):
- Essential: Clarify validation metric used for hyperparameter tuning; add a runtime comparison table on at least two datasets; include multinomial logistic regression baseline on fixed features if feasible.
- Nice-to-have: Brief discussion of top-k calibration/consistency; add empirical breakdown of projection cases and runtimes.

Overall assessment:
A solid and timely contribution with clear technical novelty and practical relevance. With minor additions on empirical reporting and a clarification of the model selection protocol, the paper would make a strong addition to the program.
