This paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM, motivated by the observation that in problems with many classes, allowing for *k* guesses is often more appropriate than requiring a single correct prediction. The authors formulate a convex upper bound on the top-k error and develop an efficient optimization scheme based on projections onto a novel “top-k simplex.” Experimental results on five datasets demonstrate improvements in top-k accuracy compared to baseline methods.

The paper builds upon the well-established multiclass SVM framework of Crammer and Singer [5] and extends it to handle top-k errors. The motivation is clearly articulated and relevant to scenarios with high class ambiguity, common in large-scale image classification.  The work connects to existing research on ranking losses [27] and multiclass to binary reduction techniques [11, 16]. The introduction of the top-k simplex is a novel contribution, and its connection to the standard simplex is well-explained.

**Strengths:**

* **Sound Technical Foundation:** The derivation of the top-k hinge loss and its convex upper bound appears mathematically sound.  The connection to Fenchel duality provides a strong theoretical basis for the optimization procedure.
* **Novelty:** The top-k simplex and its conjugate are novel and interesting contributions.
* **Efficient Optimization:** The proposed optimization scheme leveraging projections onto the top-k simplex appears efficient, and the authors highlight its compatibility with existing BLAS libraries.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on five datasets demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy.  The inclusion of results on large-scale datasets like Places 205 and ImageNet is a positive aspect.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of φ̃k(a):** While the authors mention the tighter upper bound φ̃k(a) from Usunier et al. [27], the discussion feels somewhat cursory.  A more thorough comparison, perhaps including experiments directly comparing φk(a) and φ̃k(a), would strengthen the paper.  Why did they choose to implement and evaluate φk(a) rather than φ̃k(a)?
* **Projection Algorithm Details:** The description of the projection algorithm in Section 4, while referencing existing work [13], could benefit from more detail, particularly regarding the exhaustive search strategy mentioned in Lemma 3. It’s unclear how computationally expensive this search is in practice, and its performance characteristics are not discussed.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions cross-validation for hyperparameters, but doesn't provide much insight into the sensitivity of performance to these parameters.  Are the results robust across different hyperparameter settings?
* **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons to SVMPerf and TopPush are included, these methods rely on binary reduction. A more direct comparison to other multiclass ranking-based methods, optimized directly for the multiclass setting, would be valuable.
* **Practical Significance:**  While the improvements in top-k accuracy are statistically significant, the magnitude of these improvements (e.g., 1-2% in many cases) may not be practically significant depending on the application.  A discussion of the practical implications of these improvements would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to multiclass classification that addresses a relevant problem in scenarios with many classes.
* The proposed method demonstrates consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across multiple datasets.
* The optimization scheme appears efficient and scalable.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The improvements in accuracy, while statistically significant, may be limited in practical significance.
* The discussion of related work, particularly regarding φ̃k(a), and the details of the projection algorithm could be more thorough.
* The comparison to other ranking-based methods is somewhat indirect, relying on binary reduction techniques.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field. The technical work is sound, the approach is novel, and the experimental results are encouraging. While there are a few areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Expand the discussion of φ̃k(a) and consider including a direct comparison in experiments.
2.  Provide more details about the projection algorithm, including its computational complexity and practical performance.
3.  Discuss the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings.
4. Consider adding a comparison to other directly optimized multiclass ranking based methods.
5. Provide a discussion on the practical significance of the observed improvements.
