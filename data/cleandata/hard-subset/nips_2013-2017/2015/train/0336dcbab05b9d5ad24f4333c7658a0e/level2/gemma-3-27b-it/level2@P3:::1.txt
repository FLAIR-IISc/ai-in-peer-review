This paper introduces a top-k multiclass SVM, motivated by the ambiguity often present in large-scale image classification tasks. The core idea is to directly optimize for top-k accuracy using a convex upper bound of the top-k error, along with an efficient projection scheme onto the top-k simplex. The work builds upon the existing multiclass SVM formulation of Crammer and Singer and proposes a novel convex relaxation for the top-k loss, accompanied by a tailored optimization procedure.

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretical soundness, particularly the derivation of the primal-conjugate pair for the top-k hinge loss and the definition of the top-k simplex. The proposed convex relaxation appears well-motivated and provides a tractable optimization problem. The algorithm, leveraging Prox-SDCA, benefits from existing convergence guarantees and efficient implementation opportunities. The extensive experimental evaluation on five datasets demonstrates consistent improvements in top-k accuracy compared to various baselines. The inclusion of results on large datasets like Places205 and ImageNet (in the supplement) is commendable. The connection to ranking-based losses, specifically highlighting the relationship to Usunier et al.’s framework, provides context and clarifies the contribution.

However, several areas could benefit from improvement. While the paper establishes theoretical connections, the intuition behind why optimizing for a *convex upper bound* of the top-k error actually leads to consistent performance gains could be more thoroughly explained. The results section, while comprehensive, lacks a deeper analysis of *when* the top-k SVM performs best. Are certain datasets or class distributions more conducive to improvements using this approach? A discussion of the computational cost associated with the projection onto the top-k simplex compared to the standard simplex projection would be valuable. The ablation study could be expanded to better understand the impact of the different components of the proposed method, like the specific form of the convex upper bound (comparing (3) and (5)). Although the algorithm is presented, some implementation details—beyond mentioning the use of BLAS—could improve reproducibility. It’s also worth mentioning that the presented implementation is available. Finally, the paper's claims of scalability on large datasets should be more robustly supported by providing runtime figures or a detailed analysis of computational complexity as the dataset size increases.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to top-k multiclass classification.
*   The proposed convex relaxation is well-motivated and leads to a tractable optimization problem.
*   The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across multiple datasets.
*   The work builds upon established foundations in SVM theory and provides a valuable extension.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The intuition behind the effectiveness of the convex upper bound could be more thoroughly explained.
*   A deeper analysis of the algorithm’s performance characteristics and computational complexity would be beneficial.
*   A more detailed discussion of the applicability of the method to different datasets and scenarios is needed.
*   Lack of details on implementation could hinder reproducibility.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multiclass classification. The proposed method is theoretically sound, efficiently implemented, and demonstrably effective in practice. While some aspects could be further elaborated, the overall quality of the work warrants acceptance. The potential for impact in applications where top-k performance is critical is significant. Addressing the minor weaknesses identified above would further strengthen the paper.
