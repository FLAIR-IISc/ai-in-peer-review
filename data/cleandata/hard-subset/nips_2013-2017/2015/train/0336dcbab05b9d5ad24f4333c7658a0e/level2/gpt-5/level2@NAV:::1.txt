This paper proposes Top-k Multiclass SVM, which directly optimizes top-k accuracy by replacing the multiclass hinge with a new top-k hinge loss that upper-bounds the nonconvex top-k error. The authors derive a tight convex formulation, its Fenchel conjugate, and introduce the “top-k simplex” as the effective domain, yielding a Prox-SDCA training algorithm with a principled duality-gap stopping rule. A key technical contribution is an efficient algorithm for (biased) projection onto the top-k simplex, which is of independent interest. Empirically, on five image datasets (Caltech 101 Silhouettes, MIT Indoor 67, SUN 397, Places 205, ImageNet 2012), the method improves top-k accuracy over Crammer–Singer SVM and ranking-based baselines (e.g., SVMPerf, TopPush), with consistent gains. The work is positioned relative to multiclass SVM [5], ranking losses of Usunier et al. [27] (contrasting φk vs φ̃k), and multivariate performance SVMs [11], and builds on Prox-SDCA [24].

Quality: The theoretical development is careful: the top-k hinge is a convex, strictly tighter bound than φ̃k in the intermediate regime; conjugates and dual are derived with clarity; the top-k simplex and projection algorithm are well formalized with complexity arguments. The optimization is principled and scalable, and code is released. However, statistical significance of empirical gains is not reported, and ablations isolating the contribution of the projection routine vs. the loss variant (α vs. β) are limited in the main text.

Clarity: The paper is dense but generally well organized. Some key proofs and large-scale results are deferred to the supplement; adding more intuition (e.g., geometry of the top-k simplex) and an algorithmic sketch for the projection in the main paper would aid readability.

Originality: Deriving the top-k hinge with its conjugate and introducing the top-k simplex/projection appear novel. The connection and comparison to ranking-based weighted losses are insightful, and the tighter bound is a meaningful advance.

Significance and usefulness: Top-k metrics are central in large-class recognition; a scalable convex method with measurable improvements is valuable. The approach is practical for linear models on strong features. A limitation is the lack of comparison to end-to-end deep softmax models optimized for top-k, and discussion of calibration/top-1 trade-offs.

Suggestions: (1) Report confidence intervals or paired tests. (2) Provide runtime/memory vs. simple simplex projection, and ablate φk vs. φ̃k. (3) Analyze training with ktrain ≠ keval. (4) Discuss extension to deep or kernelized settings and generalization guarantees for top-k risk.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, tight convex top-k hinge and top-k simplex with efficient projection
- Sound dual optimization with stopping criterion; released code
- Consistent top-k gains and scalability to million-scale datasets
- Clear relation to and improvement over prior ranking-based surrogates

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited statistical analysis and ablation in the main text
- Sparse comparison to modern deep baselines optimized for top-k
- Notation-heavy; key proofs/results relegated to supplement
- Some top-1 degradation without deeper analysis of trade-offs
