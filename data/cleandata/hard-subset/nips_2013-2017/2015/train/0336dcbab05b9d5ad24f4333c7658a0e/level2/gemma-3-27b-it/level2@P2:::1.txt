This paper proposes a top-k multiclass SVM, motivated by the ambiguity often present in high-class image classification problems. The core idea is to directly optimize for top-k accuracy using a convex upper bound on the top-k error. A fast optimization scheme based on projections onto the top-k simplex is also presented. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, and the experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across several datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The direct optimization for top-k accuracy in a multiclass SVM setting is a novel contribution. While ranking-based losses exist, this work provides a specific formulation tailored for multiclass classification with a focus on the top-k performance. The introduction of the top-k simplex and its properties is also a notable contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The authors provide a rigorous mathematical formulation of the top-k hinge loss and its convex conjugate. The connection to existing work on ranking losses (Usunier et al., 2017) is clearly established. The derivation of the optimization framework based on Fenchel duality is well-explained.
* **Optimization Efficiency:** The proposed optimization scheme, leveraging Prox-SDCA and efficient projections onto the top-k simplex, appears computationally viable, especially given the scalability demonstrated in the experiments. The implementation details and the handling of the projection onto the top-k simplex are particularly well-described.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on five datasets (including large-scale datasets like Places205 and ImageNet) consistently demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method over several baselines, including the standard multiclass SVM and existing ranking-based approaches.
* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is clear and consistent. Figures and tables are appropriately used to illustrate the concepts and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** While the experimental results are promising, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of the practical implications of using top-k SVM versus standard multiclass SVM.  What scenarios would benefit the most from this approach? What are the trade-offs in terms of interpretability or ease of deployment?
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons to existing methods are present, some potentially stronger baselines are missing. For instance, recent advancements in large-scale multiclass classification with deep learning (e.g., using softmax with label smoothing or contrastive loss) could provide a more challenging comparison.  The scaling results on ImageNet seem to be deferred to the supplement - a more thorough discussion in the main paper would be useful.
* **Complexity of Projection Algorithm:** The description of the projection algorithm onto the top-k simplex, while detailed, is somewhat complex and might be difficult for some readers to fully grasp. A more intuitive explanation or a simplified algorithm sketch could be helpful. The practical runtime of the projection step for very large dimensions *d* isn’t clearly discussed.
* **Connection to Existing Ranking Metrics:** The discussion of the relation to ranking-based losses, while present, could be expanded. Specifically, exploring the connections to Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) or other commonly used ranking metrics could enrich the analysis.
* **Impact of k:** The optimal value of *k* is dataset-dependent and requires cross-validation. A more in-depth analysis of how the performance varies with different values of *k* and a discussion on strategies for selecting the appropriate *k* could be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to multiclass SVM optimization, directly targeting top-k accuracy.
* The optimization scheme appears efficient and scalable, as demonstrated by the experimental results.
* The empirical validation is convincing, showing consistent improvements over existing methods.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The practical implications of the proposed method are not fully explored.
* The comparison to some potentially stronger baselines is limited.
* The complexity of the projection algorithm might be a barrier to adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multiclass classification. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the empirical validation all support acceptance. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above regarding practical implications, comparison to stronger baselines, and simplification of the projection algorithm explanation.
