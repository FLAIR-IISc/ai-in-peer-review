This paper presents a top-k multiclass SVM, a novel method for directly optimizing a classifier for top-k performance. The core idea is to generalize the multiclass SVM of Crammer and Singer by proposing a new convex loss function, the "top-k hinge loss," which serves as a tight upper bound on the non-convex top-k zero-one loss. This formulation leads to the definition of a novel convex polytope, the "top-k simplex," which is a key theoretical contribution. The authors situate their work effectively with respect to the general ranking losses of Usunier et al., arguing their proposed loss is a tighter bound. For optimization, the paper develops a scalable solver based on Proximal Stochastic Dual Coordinate Ascent (Prox-SDCA), which required the development of an efficient algorithm to project onto the top-k simplex—a significant technical contribution in its own right.

The paper’s strengths are numerous.
**Quality and Originality:** The work is technically sound, with clear theoretical derivations for the novel loss function and the geometry of the top-k simplex. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a well-founded generalization of a classic method to a new, important objective.
**Significance:** The problem of optimizing for top-k error is highly relevant in modern machine learning, especially in domains like large-scale image recognition where class ambiguity is prevalent. The proposed method provides a principled and direct way to tackle this problem, moving beyond the standard practice of optimizing for top-1 and hoping for good top-k results. The experimental validation is strong, showing consistent improvements in top-k accuracy across five datasets against several competitive baselines. The development of a scalable solver, which the authors make public, ensures the work is likely to be used by other researchers and practitioners.
**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making the complex material accessible.

The paper has very few weaknesses. The main limitation is that the full experimental results for the largest datasets (Places 205 and ImageNet) are in the supplement. While this is a common space-saving measure, including a summary of these key results in the main text would have further substantiated the method’s impressive scalability. Furthermore, while the paper honestly reports instances of decreased top-1 accuracy, a deeper analysis of this trade-off would be a welcome addition.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, significant, and well-executed contribution to the field.
