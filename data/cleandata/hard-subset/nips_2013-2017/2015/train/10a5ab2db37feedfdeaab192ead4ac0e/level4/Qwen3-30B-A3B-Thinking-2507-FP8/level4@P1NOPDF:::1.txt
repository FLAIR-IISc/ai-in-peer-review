

The authors should explicitly acknowledge that the algorithm in Section 3.2 is directly adapted from the approaches in references [14] and [15], forming a hybrid of these methods through identical approximation techniques. Current phrasing fails to convey this foundational relationship.  

The discussion's opening paragraph incorrectly implies that the "constrain-then-train" methodology (learning a weight distribution and sampling to form a fixed ensemble at test time) is novel. This approach was explicitly proposed and validated in reference [15].  

The novelty claim (i) in the introduction is misleading. Reference [15] introduced "Expectation Backpropagation," an algorithm for training binary-weighted networks with sign-activated neurons (where zero inputs are theoretically unreachable). This method inherently supports constrained connectivity and uses expectation propagation for weight updates—directly aligning with the current paper's technical approach, not a novel concept.  

The paper's claim about prior state-of-the-art performance is inaccurate. For binary-weight and binary-neuron networks, the strongest reported results on MNIST (without data augmentation) were achieved by the method in reference [15] (arXiv:1503.03562v3). The current paper's reported accuracy on MNIST thus does not surpass the existing SOTA.  

While the experimental results—particularly regarding accuracy and power efficiency—are strong and well-documented, the methodological and algorithmic claims require substantial revision. The core contributions are not sufficiently distinct from prior work, particularly [15], which already addressed binary networks with constrained connectivity, expectation-based training, and comparable performance on MNIST. The authors must revise the introduction and related work to accurately position their work within this established context.
