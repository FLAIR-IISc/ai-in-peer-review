1) The authors should explicitly acknowledge that the algorithm in Section 3.2 closely resembles the cited methods—[14] J. Zhao, J. Shawe-Taylor, and M. van Daalen, "Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks," Neural Networks, vol. 9, no. 6, pp. 991–998, 1996; and [15] Soudry, Daniel, Itay Hubara, and Ron Meir, "Expectation backpropagation: Parameter-free training of multilayer neural networks with continuous or discrete weights," Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems, 2014—in its final form (a hybrid of the two), and that it was derived using identical approximations.

2) The first paragraph of the Discussion creates the impression that the “constrain-then-train” methodology (learning a weight distribution and then sampling from it to produce a fixed network ensemble at test time) is novel, even though it was already proposed and tested in [15].

3) Novelty claim (i) in the last paragraph of the Introduction is also misleading. The “expectation backpropagation” algorithm in [15] is a backpropagation update rule (based on expectation propagation), implemented for binary neurons (i.e., a sign activation function, for which zero input is never reached exactly) with binary weights, and it can readily support constrained connectivity.

4) The cited prior state of the art is incorrect. To the best of my knowledge, the previous best results for binary weights (and neurons) were achieved by the algorithm in [15], which was tested on MNIST without data augmentation, as in this paper (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf).

Edited after the authors’ feedback: Thank you for agreeing to address these concerns. This paper presents strong and novel experimental results in terms of accuracy and power consumption. However, both the overall methodology and the algorithm itself are not as novel as claimed.
