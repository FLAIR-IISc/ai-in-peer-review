This paper addresses the critical challenge of deploying deep learning models on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware, specifically focusing on reconciling the incompatibility between backpropagation, which relies on continuous values, and spiking neural networks with discrete synapses. The authors propose a novel approach of treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, enabling the use of standard backpropagation for training. The resulting network is then mapped to neuromorphic hardware through sampling and ensemble averaging. They demonstrate this approach on the TrueNorth chip with the MNIST dataset, achieving impressive results in terms of both accuracy (99.42% with an ensemble of 64) and energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image with an ensemble of 1).

The core idea builds upon existing work in both deep learning and neuromorphic computing. Backpropagation has seen tremendous success in achieving high performance on various tasks [1, 2, 3], while neuromorphic chips like TrueNorth [7] offer significant energy efficiency. The authors effectively bridge these two areas by formulating a training methodology compatible with the constraints of neuromorphic hardware. The proposed probabilistic approach shares similarities with previous work, notably [14] and [15], which also explore learning in networks with stochastic binary neurons and synapses. However, this work distinguishes itself by successfully combining spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, constrained connectivity, and achieving state-of-the-art results on a real neuromorphic platform.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results on the TrueNorth chip, demonstrating a significant improvement in energy efficiency for digit recognition compared to existing approaches [16].
*   **Practical Approach:** The “constrain-then-train” strategy offers a practical pathway for deploying deep learning models on neuromorphic hardware.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed methodology and experimental setup.
*   **Novel Combination:** The work effectively integrates multiple elements—spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, constrained connectivity, and backpropagation training—into a functioning system.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overstated Novelty:** The paper’s claims regarding the novelty of the approach, particularly the "constrain-then-train" methodology, are overstated. The concept of learning a weight distribution and then sampling to create fixed network ensembles has been previously explored in [15].
*   **Algorithm Similarity:** The training algorithm presented in Section 3.2 bears a strong resemblance to existing methods, particularly those described in [14] and [15], employing similar approximations. The paper would benefit from explicitly acknowledging these connections.
*   **Inaccurate State-of-the-Art Comparison:** The comparison to prior art regarding MNIST accuracy is potentially inaccurate. The authors claim to outperform [12] and [15], but the previous best results for binary weights and neurons were achieved by the algorithm in [15] (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf), and should be properly contextualized.
*   **Limited Discussion of Trade-offs:** While the results are impressive, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between accuracy, energy consumption, and network size would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents significant empirical results demonstrating a practical solution for deploying deep learning models on neuromorphic hardware.
*   The combination of techniques is a valuable contribution to the field, and the results show real-world applicability on the TrueNorth chip.
*   Addressing the minor issues related to novelty and prior art comparisons would strengthen the paper considerably.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The overstated claims of novelty could mislead readers.
*   The lack of clear acknowledgment of related work in [14] and [15] weakens the contribution.
*   The inaccurate comparison with prior state-of-the-art results raises concerns about the thoroughness of the evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The experimental results are compelling, and the proposed approach addresses an important problem. However, the overstated claims of novelty and the inaccuracies in the comparison with prior work need to be addressed. If the authors revise the paper to more accurately reflect the relationship to existing work and clarify the state-of-the-art, it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
