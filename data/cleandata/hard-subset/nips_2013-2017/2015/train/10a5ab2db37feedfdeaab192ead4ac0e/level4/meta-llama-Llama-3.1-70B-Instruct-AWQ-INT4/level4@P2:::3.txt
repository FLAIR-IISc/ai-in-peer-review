The authors present an interesting paper about a backpropagation training method that utilizes spike probabilities and considers the hardware constraints of a particular platform, specifically one with spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This topic is of current interest in mapping deep networks to multi-neuron hardware platforms.

Quality: The proposed training method is useful, especially when considering new multi-neuron hardware platforms with constraints. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges in reconciling the incompatibility between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware, and their approach provides a viable solution.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to read. However, some minor rewording is suggested for the claims made at the end of Section 1. As written, the text may be misinterpreted to suggest that the paper proposes a training methodology that employs spiking neurons and synapses with reduced precision for the first time. Instead, the novelty of the paper lies in its approach to reconciling the incompatibility between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware.

Some minor clarifications are also needed in Section 2. Specifically, the authors should provide a clearer explanation of what is meant by "0.15 bits per synapse." Additionally, the network topology could be more clearly described in a single section. The authors should also discuss the implications of the probabilistic synaptic connection update on training time.

The results section raises several questions. For instance, the training time for the 30 core network is not provided, and it would be helpful to know how much longer the training time is compared to the 5 core network. Additionally, the results from a single ensemble of the 30 core network are surprisingly low, especially when compared to the 5 core network. The authors should provide an explanation for this discrepancy. Furthermore, the results of the different ensembles of the 5 core network are similar, which may indicate that the ensemble size does not significantly impact the performance of the network.

Originality: The authors should provide a discussion or comparison with other spiking backprop-type rules, such as SpikeProp. This would help to contextualize their contribution and demonstrate the novelty of their approach.

Significance: The development of new training methods that consider constraints of hardware platforms is of great interest. The constraints considered during training in this paper are based on the TrueNorth architecture, but these constraints may not all apply to other platforms. The authors should discuss the potential applicability of their approach to other hardware platforms.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting training method that takes into account the hardware constraints of a particular platform. While some minor clarifications and discussions are needed, the paper is well-written and contributes to the growing body of research on mapping deep networks to multi-neuron hardware platforms.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to reconciling the incompatibility between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware.
* The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges in mapping deep networks to multi-neuron hardware platforms.
* The paper is well-written and easy to read.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper may benefit from a clearer explanation of the network topology and the implications of the probabilistic synaptic connection update on training time.
* The results section raises several questions that are not fully addressed.
* The authors should provide a discussion or comparison with other spiking backprop-type rules to contextualize their contribution.
