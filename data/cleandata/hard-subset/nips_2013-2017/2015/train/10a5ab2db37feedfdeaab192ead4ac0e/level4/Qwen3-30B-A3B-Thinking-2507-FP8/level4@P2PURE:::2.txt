

The authors propose a method to reconcile the need for high-precision continuous variables in backpropagation with the use of spike-based neural network hardware featuring binary synapses. They introduce a strategy treating analog variables as probabilities, which are then sampled to map an offline-trained neural network onto a spiking hardware device for deployment. The authors adapt backpropagation to align with this approach and validate it on the MNIST benchmark, mapping the trained network onto two distinct architectures optimized for size and accuracy, with performance results reported from the chip. The manuscript is of high quality and exceptionally clear, presenting a novel contribution with potential applicability to other hardware platforms. Additionally, while reviewing hardware platforms, the authors should reference Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the arXiv preprint arXiv:1506.05427. However, the significance of the work is not adequately emphasized; it remains unclear how this method specifically enables practical deployment on the TrueNorth chip or identifies concrete application domains. Furthermore, the advantages of this method over alternatives (e.g., those cited in [9]–[12]) are not sufficiently demonstrated. The paper describes a method for applying backpropagation to spiking neural networks with binary synapses, validated by successful mapping to the TrueNorth device. While well-structured, clear, and compelling in its results, it lacks essential implementation details, including input spike conversion methods, spike frequencies, shared weight values, and neuron model specifications.
