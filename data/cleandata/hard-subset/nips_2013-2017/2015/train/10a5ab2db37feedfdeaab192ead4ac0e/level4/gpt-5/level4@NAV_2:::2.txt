The authors introduce a method to bridge the reliance on continuous-valued variables in backpropagation with the constraints of spiking neuromorphic hardware featuring binary synapses. They reinterpret continuous activations and weights as probabilities, which are then sampled to transfer the offline-trained model onto a spiking deployment platform. They describe how to adapt backpropagation to this probabilistic framework and validate the approach on MNIST. The trained model is instantiated on two hardware configurations, one optimized for compactness and one for accuracy, with performance measured directly on chip. The manuscript is of very high quality and clarity. The contribution is original and likely transferable to other methods and hardware targets. When surveying available hardware, the authors should also cite Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015), and the work at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. The paper does not sufficiently articulate its significance: it remains unclear how this technique concretely enables practical TrueNorth deployments and in which specific applications; moreover, the comparative benefits over alternatives, such as those in [9]–[12], are not clearly established.

The paper presents a procedure for training spiking neural networks with binary synapses via backpropagation and demonstrates its viability by deploying the learned network on the TrueNorth chip. The presentation is well organized, clear, and concise. The results are compelling, but some experimental and implementation details are missing (e.g., the exact input-to-spike encoding scheme, firing rates used, shared/lookup weight values, neuron model configurations, etc.).
