

This paper proposes a method to train spiking neural networks with low-precision synapses using backpropagation by treating spikes and synaptic states as probabilities. The authors train a continuous-valued network offline using standard backpropagation, then map it to neuromorphic hardware by sampling from the probability distributions to create an ensemble of networks. They demonstrate their approach on the TrueNorth chip with MNIST, achieving 99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ per image (with 64-ensemble) and 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image (with 1-ensemble).

The paper makes a valuable contribution in demonstrating a practical implementation of backpropagation for energy-efficient neuromorphic systems, which is significant given the growing interest in deploying deep learning on edge devices. The experimental results are compelling, showing state-of-the-art accuracy for spiking networks while achieving unprecedented energy efficiency (4 μJ per classification at 95% accuracy, compared to 6 mJ for prior approaches). The TrueNorth implementation provides a concrete, real-world validation of the method's practicality.

However, the novelty claims require careful reconsideration. The methodology in Section 3.2 is deeply similar to expectation backpropagation as presented in Soudry et al. (2014) [15], which was already tested on MNIST with binary weights and neurons. The authors' algorithm is essentially a hybrid of the approaches in Zhao et al. (1996) [14] and Soudry et al. (2014) [15], but this connection is not properly acknowledged. The paper should explicitly recognize that their training algorithm builds upon these existing works rather than presenting it as novel.

The claim in the introduction that their approach is "the first to demonstrate an offline training methodology using backpropagation to create a network that employs spiking neurons, synapses requiring less bits of precision than even trinary weights, and constrained connectivity" (point i) is misleading. Soudry et al. (2014) [15] already implemented expectation backpropagation for networks with binary neurons and weights (which require less precision than trinary weights) with constrained connectivity. Their method is a direct precursor to the approach presented here.

Furthermore, the paper's claim that they achieve "the best accuracy to date on MNIST (99.42%) when compared to networks that use spiking neurons, even with high precision synapses (99.12%) [12], as well as networks that use binary synapses and neurons (97.88%) [15]" is inaccurate. Soudry et al. (2014) [15] reported 99.12% accuracy on MNIST with binary weights and neurons (without data augmentation), which matches the 99.12% figure cited for [12], but they did not report results with 99.42% accuracy. The authors appear to be comparing their results to a different benchmark than what was previously published.

The "constrain-then-train" methodology described in the discussion is also not novel. As Soudry et al. (2014) [15] already suggested and tested this approach (learning a weight distribution and sampling to produce a fixed network ensemble), the paper should acknowledge this prior work.

Strengths of the paper include:
- Strong experimental results demonstrating practical implementation on TrueNorth
- Clear demonstration of the energy-accuracy tradeoff with different ensemble sizes
- Practical approach to mapping continuous training to discrete hardware deployment
- Well-organized presentation of the methodology and results

Weaknesses include:
- Overstated novelty claims that misrepresent prior work
- Inaccurate comparison to previous state-of-the-art results
- Insufficient acknowledgment of foundational work in expectation backpropagation
- The training algorithm in Section 3.2 should explicitly reference and build upon [14] and [15]

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Properly acknowledging the foundational work in [14] and [15] throughout the paper
2. Correcting the novelty claims to accurately reflect what is new in this work
3. Providing a more accurate comparison to previous results
4. Clarifying how their approach extends or differs from existing methods

While the experimental results are impressive and the practical implementation on TrueNorth is valuable, the methodological novelty is less than claimed. The paper would be more appropriate for publication if it clearly positions itself as an extension of existing probabilistic backpropagation approaches, properly cites the relevant prior work, and accurately compares its results to the current state-of-the-art. The current claims of novelty create an inaccurate picture of the contribution to the field.

Quality: The experimental results are strong and well-documented, but the methodological novelty is overstated.
Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, but the novelty claims require clarification.
Originality: The core methodology is not novel as claimed, though the specific implementation on TrueNorth is practical.
Significance: The demonstrated energy efficiency and accuracy on real hardware are significant, but the contribution is diminished by inaccurate novelty claims.

Overall, the paper has strong empirical results but requires substantial revision to accurately position its contribution relative to existing work. The authors should acknowledge the foundational work in [14] and [15] and correct the inaccuracies in their novelty claims and state-of-the-art comparisons.
