- Proposes a backpropagation training method for spiking neural networks using spike probabilities and TrueNorth-like hardware constraints.  
- Addresses the important problem of mapping deep learning models onto neuromorphic hardware.  
- Quality: method is valuable for emerging multi-neuron hardware with inherent operational constraints.  
- Paper is generally well-written but several clarifications are needed to improve readability.  
- Rephrase claims in Section 1 to avoid implying this is the first method for spiking neurons with reduced-precision synapses.  
- Frame the TrueNorth demonstration as a strong validation of the method rather than the primary novel contribution.  
- Clarify the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" in Section 2.  
- Provide a consolidated, clear description of the network topology in one location.  
- Report the training time overhead introduced by the probabilistic synaptic connection update.  
- Explain why a single 30-core network ensemble performs worse than the 5-core network and why 5-core ensembles show consistent performance.  
- Specify the nature of inputs during testing (are spike trains used and what are the spike rates?).  
- Note that Reference [12] also includes training constraints (e.g., bias=0), so it is not a pure "train-then-constrain" method; fix the "approach approach" typo.  
- Originality: include comparison/discussion with other backprop-based spiking rules (e.g., SpikeProp).  
- Significance: hardware-constrained training is timely, but TrueNorth-specific constraints may limit generality; reliance on a 64-member ensemble implies substantial hardware trade-offs that should be discussed.  
- Overall: an interesting contribution that effectively integrates specific neuromorphic hardware constraints into training.
