This paper presents an interesting approach to training neural networks with binary weights and neurons. While the experimental results are strong and novel, particularly in terms of accuracy and power consumption, there are concerns regarding the novelty and presentation of the methodology and algorithm.

Firstly, the authors should acknowledge the deep similarity between their algorithm in Section 3.2 and the cited references [14] and [15]. The final form of their algorithm appears to be a hybrid of both, derived using identical approximations. A clear mention of this connection would strengthen the paper by providing a more accurate representation of the algorithm's origins.

Furthermore, the first paragraph of the discussion may give readers the impression that the "constrain-then-train" methodology is novel. However, this approach was already suggested and tested in reference [15]. The authors should clarify this point to avoid any potential misconceptions.

Additionally, the novelty claim (i) in the last paragraph of the introduction is misleading. Reference [15]'s "expectation backpropagation" algorithm is a backpropagation update rule that utilizes expectation propagation, specifically designed for binary neurons with binary weights. This algorithm can easily accommodate constrained connectivity, making the novelty claim less accurate.

Another point of concern is the citation of previous state-of-the-art results. To the best of my knowledge, the previous best results for binary weights and neurons were achieved by the algorithm in reference [15], which was tested on the MNIST dataset without data augmentation, similar to this paper.

In light of the authors' feedback, I would like to reiterate that this paper has strong and novel experimental results. However, it is essential to rephrase the methodology and algorithm's novelty to accurately reflect their origins and connections to existing work. By doing so, the paper can provide a clearer and more accurate representation of its contributions to the field.
