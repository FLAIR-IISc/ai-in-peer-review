Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a principled way to reconcile continuous-valued backpropagation with spiking neuromorphic hardware that uses low-precision, often binary, synapses. The key idea is to treat neuron outputs and synaptic connections as probabilities in [0,1] during training. The authors train an offline, continuous-valued proxy network with backpropagation, then map it to hardware by sampling spikes and synapse on/off states from the learned probabilities. They also use ensembles by sampling multiple instantiations and averaging outputs to boost accuracy. The approach is demonstrated on MNIST and deployed on the TrueNorth chip using two architectures optimized respectively for compactness and accuracy, with performance measured directly on chip.

This work complements and extends prior efforts: backpropagation applied to spiking neurons with high-precision weights [9–12], networks with discrete/trinary synapses and continuous activations [13], and probabilistic backprop variants for binary neurons/synapses with full connectivity [14,15]. Here, the authors combine spiking neurons, synapses with even fewer bits than trinary, constrained connectivity, and an explicit training-to-deployment mapping on real neuromorphic hardware. The manuscript’s review of neuromorphic platforms is useful, though it should also cite Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015), and arXiv:1506.05427 for completeness.

Quality:
The manuscript is technically sound and clearly written. The probabilistic forward model (Gaussian approximation of summed Bernoulli inputs) and its use to derive gradients is coherent, and the constrained initialization for balanced, low-redundancy synaptic strengths is sensible for TrueNorth. The results are convincing, with direct on-chip measurements and a clear accuracy–energy trade‑off across two topologies and different ensemble sizes. However, some important experimental details are missing and should be provided to ensure reproducibility and fair comparison:
- How inputs are converted into spikes, including exact encoding scheme and spike rates.
- Neuron model details as instantiated on TrueNorth (e.g., leak, threshold, reset, dynamics beyond the simple thresholding description).
- The set of shared synaptic strength values actually used and how axon types are distributed.
- Any constraints on firing rates or refractory periods during deployment.
- Clarification on whether the Gaussian approximation accuracy was empirically validated in this regime.

Clarity:
The paper is well organized and easy to follow. The description of the training/deployment mapping and the ensemble strategy is clear. Adding the missing implementation details above, and a brief algorithmic summary (e.g., a pseudo-code block in the supplement) would further improve clarity for readers aiming to reproduce the method or adapt it to other hardware.

Originality:
The approach is original in its explicit probabilistic reinterpretation of spikes and synaptic on/off states for backprop, combined with constrained connectivity tailored to TrueNorth. The “constrain-then-train” viewpoint is a useful conceptual contribution that contrasts with the more common “train-then-constrain” pipelines used in spiking conversions.

Significance:
While the technical contribution is strong, the paper underplays its practical significance. It remains unclear how the method translates into real applications on TrueNorth and in what scenarios it offers clear advantages over existing alternatives (e.g., [9]–[12]). The authors should articulate:
- Concrete application domains where their encode–train–sample pipeline and energy/latency profile would be compelling.
- A comparative discussion of advantages and drawbacks relative to alternative spiking conversions or high-precision spiking training (accuracy, energy, latency, memory footprint, ease of deployment).
- How the approach scales beyond MNIST (e.g., natural images, audio), and any anticipated bottlenecks.

Requests for clarification and suggestions:
- Provide a detailed description of the spike encoding, time budget per inference, and measured firing statistics.
- Specify neuron model parameters and synapse strength values used on TrueNorth.
- Include ablations: effect of ensemble size on accuracy and energy; sensitivity to dropout, data augmentation, and learning-rate schedule.
- Discuss generalization to convolutional topologies and to other neuromorphic platforms; add citations to Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015), and arXiv:1506.05427 when surveying hardware.
- Expand the discussion on when constrain-then-train outperforms train-then-constrain, and under what hardware constraints.

Pros:
- Clear, well-structured manuscript with a principled learning-to-deployment pipeline.
- Original probabilistic formulation enabling backprop with spiking neurons and ultra-low-precision synapses.
- Real hardware deployment with measured energy, showing meaningful accuracy–efficiency trade‑offs.
- Likely useful to other platforms and methods.

Cons:
- Practical significance and application scenarios are not well articulated.
- Limited comparison to alternative methods; unclear advantage over prior spiking training/conversion approaches [9]–[12].
- Missing implementation details hinder reproducibility and assessment.
- Evaluation limited to MNIST.

Overall assessment:
A strong and timely contribution that bridges deep learning and neuromorphic computing with an elegant probabilistic backprop framework and on-chip validation. The paper is clear and technically sound, but its impact would be strengthened by fuller experimental details, a more explicit comparison to alternatives, and a clearer articulation of practical significance and target applications.
