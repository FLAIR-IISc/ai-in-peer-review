The authors propose a strategy to reconcile the precision requirements of backpropagation with spike-based neural network devices that implement binary synapses. They reinterpret analog variables as probabilities that are sampled to map the network trained off-line to the spiking hardware used at deployment. They adapt backpropagation to be compatible with this probabilistic strategy and validate the method on MNIST. The trained network is mapped onto two different architectures—one optimized for size and one for accuracy—and on-chip performance measurements are reported. The manuscript is of high quality and clarity. The approach is original and appears broadly useful for other methods and hardware platforms. In surveying hardware platforms, the authors should also cite Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015), and http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427.

The significance of the work is not sufficiently highlighted. It is unclear how the proposed method will enable deployment of the TrueNorth chip in practical applications and which specific applications would benefit. It is also not clear whether, and to what extent, this method offers advantages over alternative options, including those cited in [9]–[12].

The paper presents a method for applying backpropagation to spiking neural networks with binary synapses and validates it by successfully mapping the trained network onto the TrueNorth device. The paper is well structured, clear, and to the point. The results are convincing, but some important details are missing:
- How inputs are converted into spikes
- The firing rates/frequencies used
- The shared weight values
- The neuron models employed
