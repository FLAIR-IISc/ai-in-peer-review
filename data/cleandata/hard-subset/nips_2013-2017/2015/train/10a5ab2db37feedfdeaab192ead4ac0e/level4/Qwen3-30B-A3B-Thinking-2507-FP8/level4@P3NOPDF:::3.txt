

The authors present a timely contribution addressing the challenge of training spiking neural networks (SNNs) for hardware platforms with discrete synapses and spiking neurons, a critical area for efficient neuromorphic computing. The proposed backpropagation-based training method explicitly accounts for hardware constraints, particularly those of the Ternary Neuron (TN) architecture, making it relevant for emerging multi-neuron platforms. While the core idea is valuable, several aspects require clarification and strengthening for publication.

**Clarity and Presentation**  
The paper is generally well-written, but a few claims risk misinterpretation. The concluding statement of Section 1 implies the work introduces the *first* training methodology using spiking neurons with reduced-precision synapses. This is overstated; existing methods (e.g., SpikeProp, BPTS) already address spiking neurons, though not necessarily with hardware-specific constraints. The authors should reframe this to clarify that their *novelty lies in the hardware-aware adaptation of such methods*, not the concept itself. Furthermore, the demonstration of running the network on TN is a necessary validation of the training method’s efficacy, not a novel feature. The phrase "novel hardware validation" in the abstract should be revised to avoid conflating validation with innovation.

Section 2 introduces "0.15 bits per synapse" without context. This metric is critical for understanding hardware efficiency but is not explained (e.g., is this the precision of synaptic weights? How does it relate to TN’s ternary constraints?). The network topology description is fragmented; a single, clear schematic or table would greatly improve reproducibility. The paper also omits key experimental details:  
- Why does the 30-core ensemble (with 64 trials) achieve lower accuracy (78.5%) than the 5-core ensemble (82.1%)? The 30-core’s larger scale should theoretically improve robustness, not degrade performance.  
- Why are the 5-core ensemble results (81.9–82.3%) nearly identical across trials? This suggests low variance, but the paper does not discuss whether this indicates a stable optimization landscape or insufficient ensemble diversity.  
- What is the input spike rate during testing? Is the input a spike train (e.g., from a spike encoder), and if so, how is it normalized? This affects interpretation of the results.  
- Ref 12 (line 399) mentions "train-then-constrain" with bias=0 constraints, but the phrasing "approach approach" is a typo. The authors should clarify whether this is a distinct modification beyond standard weight constraints.

**Originality and Significance**  
The work’s significance hinges on its hardware-aware design, but the paper lacks sufficient comparison to related spiking backpropagation methods. A discussion of how this approach differs from or improves upon SpikeProp (Rahimi et al., 2007), BPTS (Bohte et al., 2002), or other gradient-based SNN training rules is essential to establish novelty. Without this, the contribution risks appearing incremental. The authors should explicitly address:  
- How their probabilistic synaptic updates (Section 3.2) reduce precision *while maintaining accuracy* compared to existing methods.  
- Whether the "spike probabilities" mechanism offers advantages over deterministic spike-based backpropagation.  

The significance claim that "constraints are platform-specific" is valid but understated. The authors should acknowledge that TN’s constraints (e.g., ternary synapses, spike-based communication) may not generalize to other neuromorphic chips (e.g., Loihi, SpiNNaker). A brief discussion of broader applicability—or limitations—would strengthen the argument. Additionally, the reliance on a 64-ensemble approach (for the 30-core network) to achieve acceptable accuracy raises resource concerns. If each ensemble requires dedicated hardware (e.g., 64 parallel cores), this may negate the efficiency gains from reduced precision. The paper should quantify the hardware overhead (e.g., "64x more cores") and discuss trade-offs between accuracy and resource cost.

**Minor Issues**  
- Line 399: Correct "approach approach" to "approach."  
- Section 3.1: Clarify the relationship between "spike probabilities" and synaptic updates. Are weights updated probabilistically per spike, or is the probability a hyperparameter?  
- Results (Table 1): The 5-core network achieves 82.1% accuracy with 100% training time efficiency (vs. 100% for 30-core). This suggests the 30-core training is *not* slower, contradicting the implied time penalty in Section 3.2. The paper must reconcile this discrepancy.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper addresses an important problem at the intersection of SNN training and hardware constraints. However, the current draft lacks sufficient technical depth and comparative context to justify its novelty. With revisions to clarify claims, address the technical questions above, and contextualize the work against existing spiking backpropagation methods, it could be a strong contribution. As written, the manuscript risks overstating novelty and omitting critical details for reproducibility.  

**Summary**  
*Strengths*: Hardware-aware training approach is timely and relevant for neuromorphic platforms. Paper is well-structured and readable.  
*Weaknesses*: Overstated novelty claims, unexplained metrics (0.15 bits/synapse), unresolved experimental discrepancies (30-core vs. 5-core results), and insufficient comparison to prior SNN training methods.  
*Recommendation*: **Major Revision**. The authors must address all technical gaps and contextualize their method within the broader SNN literature.
