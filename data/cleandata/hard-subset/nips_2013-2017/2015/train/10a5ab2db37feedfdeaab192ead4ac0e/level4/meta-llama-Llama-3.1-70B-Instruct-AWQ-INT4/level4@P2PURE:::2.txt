The authors propose a method to reconcile the need for high precision in continuous variables in backpropagation algorithms with the desire to utilize spike-based neural network devices featuring binary synapses. They introduce the concept of treating analog variables as probabilities, which are sampled to map the neural network used offline for training to the spiking network chip employed for application deployment. This approach is then integrated into the backpropagation algorithm, and its effectiveness is demonstrated on the MNIST benchmark. The trained network is successfully mapped onto two distinct architectures, optimized for size and accuracy, with performance figures measured directly from the chip. The manuscript is of exceptionally high quality, with clarity being one of its notable strengths. The work is quite original and has the potential to be beneficial for other approaches and hardware platforms.

However, in reviewing the various hardware platforms available, the authors should also consider mentioning the work by Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the paper available at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. A notable omission in the paper is the significance of the proposed method and its potential impact on practical applications, particularly in terms of deploying the TrueNorth chip. It is unclear how this method offers advantages over alternative options, such as those cited in references [9] to [12].

The authors present a well-structured and clear method for applying backpropagation to spiking neural networks with binary synapses, successfully validating it by mapping the trained network onto the TrueNorth device. The results are convincing; however, some details are lacking, including the conversion of inputs into spikes, the frequencies used, shared weight values, and neuron models.
