This paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks that can be efficiently deployed on neuromorphic hardware. The authors propose a "constrain-then-train" methodology, where the network is trained with constraints that match the deployment hardware, allowing for a direct mapping between the trained network and the hardware implementation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on the MNIST dataset, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy and power efficiency on the TrueNorth chip.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental results. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the benefits and limitations of their approach, as well as potential avenues for future work.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its experimental results, which demonstrate a significant improvement in accuracy and power efficiency compared to previous state-of-the-art results. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy and power consumption, which is useful for understanding the practical implications of their approach.

However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty and originality of the methodology and algorithm presented in the paper. Specifically:

1. The algorithm presented in section 3.2 is deeply similar to the cited references [14] and [15], and was derived using identical approximations. The authors should at least mention this similarity and provide a clear explanation of how their algorithm differs from these previous works.

2. The "constrain-then-train" methodology, which is presented as a novel contribution, was already suggested and tested in ref [15]. The authors should acknowledge this and provide a clear explanation of how their approach builds upon or differs from this previous work.

3. The novelty claim (i) in the last paragraph of the introduction is also misleading. Ref [15] "expectation backpropagation" algorithm is a backpropagation update rule (using expectation propagation), implemented for binary neurons (i.e., with a sign activation function, in which zero input is never reached exactly) with binary weights, and can easily have constrained connectivity.

4. The cited previous state-of-the-art are wrong. To the best of my knowledge, the previous best results for binary weights (and neurons) were achieved by the algorithm in ref [15], which was tested on the MNIST dataset without data augmentation, as was done in this paper.

Despite these concerns, the paper presents strong and novel experimental results, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the benefits and limitations of their approach. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents strong and novel experimental results, demonstrating a significant improvement in accuracy and power efficiency on the TrueNorth chip.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy and power consumption, which is useful for understanding the practical implications of their approach.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental results.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The novelty and originality of the methodology and algorithm presented in the paper are questionable, and the authors should provide a clear explanation of how their approach builds upon or differs from previous work.
* The paper does not provide a thorough discussion of the limitations and potential drawbacks of the proposed approach.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the authors revise the paper to address the concerns mentioned above. Specifically, they should:

* Provide a clear explanation of how their algorithm differs from previous works, such as [14] and [15].
* Acknowledge the previous work on the "constrain-then-train" methodology and provide a clear explanation of how their approach builds upon or differs from this previous work.
* Correct the novelty claim (i) in the last paragraph of the introduction.
* Update the cited previous state-of-the-art to reflect the correct results.

With these revisions, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
