- Problem: standard backprop uses continuous neurons/weights that are incompatible with spiking neurons and discrete, low-precision synapses on neuromorphic hardware.  
- Proposed method: "constrain-then-train" — constrain architecture to hardware limits, train using continuous probabilistic relaxations of spikes/synapses, then sample deterministic networks and ensemble for deployment.  
- Demonstration: trained on MNIST and deployed on IBM TrueNorth, achieving up to 99.42% accuracy and a low-energy point of 0.268 µJ/image, showing an accuracy–energy trade-off.  
- Strength — significance: addresses an important, timely problem of running high-performance DNNs on ultra-low-power embedded devices.  
- Strength — experiments: strong real-hardware validation on TrueNorth with concrete, valuable accuracy vs. energy results.  
- Strength — clarity: paper is well written and explains the methodology and mapping procedure clearly.  
- Weakness — algorithmic novelty: training algorithm closely mirrors prior work (Zhao et al. and Soudry et al.’s Expectation Backpropagation), so novelty claims are overstated.  
- Weakness — methodological novelty: the "constrain-then-train" idea (learning distributions then sampling networks/ensembles) is central to EBP and not presented as genuinely new.  
- Weakness — misleading claims: statements like “first demonstration” for backprop with spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity are not fully supported given related literature.  
- Weakness — comparisons: paper lacks direct, careful comparison to prior EBP results on MNIST and should more fully contextualize performance against related binary/neuron work.  
- Recommendation: valuable engineering/hardware contribution worth acceptance if revised — authors should properly cite and position relative to [14]/[15], tone down novelty claims, and add fair comparisons; the paper’s main value is the successful hardware demonstration rather than a new training algorithm.
