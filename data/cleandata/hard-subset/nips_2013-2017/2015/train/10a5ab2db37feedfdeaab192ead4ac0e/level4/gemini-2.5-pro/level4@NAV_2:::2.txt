This paper presents a novel method to train deep neural networks for deployment on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware that uses spiking neurons and low-precision, discrete synapses. The central contribution is a "constrain-then-train" framework that reconciles the continuous-valued nature of backpropagation with the discrete, spiking nature of neuromorphic systems. The authors treat synaptic connections and neuron activations as probabilities during offline training, which allows for the direct application of a modified backpropagation algorithm. The trained probabilistic network is then mapped to the hardware by sampling from these probabilities, creating one or more network instances for an ensemble. This approach is validated on the MNIST dataset using IBM's TrueNorth chip, demonstrating a compelling trade-off between classification accuracy and energy efficiency.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Quality:** The paper introduces an original and elegant solution to the significant problem of training spiking neural networks for neuromorphic hardware. The core idea of framing the training in a probabilistic domain that maps directly to the hardware constraints is novel. The manuscript is of very high quality, being well-structured, clearly written, and technically sound.
*   **Significance and Strong Results:** The work successfully bridges the gap between high-performing deep learning algorithms and highly efficient neuromorphic hardware. The experimental results are convincing, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy (99.42%) for spiking networks on MNIST while demonstrating remarkable energy efficiency on the TrueNorth platform. The demonstration on real hardware is a major strength.
*   **Generalizability:** Although demonstrated on a specific chip, the proposed training methodology appears general enough to be adapted for other neuromorphic platforms with similar constraints (spiking neurons, low-precision synapses), which significantly broadens its potential impact.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Discussion of Significance:** The paper could be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of its broader impact. It is not entirely clear how this method will enable the deployment of the TrueNorth chip in specific real-world applications beyond digit recognition.
*   **Comparison to Alternatives:** The paper would benefit from a more direct comparison against alternative approaches for training spiking networks, such as those cited in references [9-12]. A clearer articulation of the specific advantages (e.g., in terms of performance, training stability, or hardware mapping efficiency) of this probabilistic method over train-then-constrain techniques would better highlight its contribution.
*   **Reproducibility:** While the results are impressive, some experimental details could be clarified to improve reproducibility. For example, a more consolidated description of the input encoding process (how pixel values are converted to spike probabilities), the exact neuron parameters configured on the chip, and the set of shared synaptic strength values (`s_ij`) would be helpful for readers wishing to build upon this work.
*   **Missing Citations:** The review of neuromorphic hardware platforms could be more comprehensive. The authors may want to consider including and discussing other relevant platforms, for instance, the work by Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and Min et al. (arXiv:1506.05427).
