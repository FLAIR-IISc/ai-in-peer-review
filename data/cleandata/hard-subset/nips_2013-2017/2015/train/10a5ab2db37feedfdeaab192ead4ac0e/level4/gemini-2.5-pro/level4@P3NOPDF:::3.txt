This paper presents a novel backpropagation-based training method for spiking neural networks that directly incorporates the hardware constraints of a specific neuromorphic platform, namely discrete synapses and spiking neurons. The topic is of significant and current interest as the field seeks effective ways to map deep neural networks onto specialized, energy-efficient hardware. The proposed method is validated by deploying the trained network on the TrueNorth neuromorphic system.

**Quality and Significance**

The paper addresses a critical challenge in neuromorphic engineering: creating training algorithms that are aware of the underlying hardware's limitations. The development of such constraint-aware training methods is highly significant for the practical application of neuromorphic systems. The proposed method, which utilizes spike probabilities within a backpropagation framework, is a valuable contribution. The demonstration of the trained network on actual TrueNorth hardware serves as a strong validation of the algorithm's effectiveness and its ability to produce functional, deployable models.

However, the significance is somewhat tied to the specific constraints of the TrueNorth architecture. While valuable, a discussion of the method's adaptability to other neuromorphic platforms with different constraints would broaden its perceived impact. Furthermore, achieving high accuracy relies on ensembling 64 networks, which has considerable implications for hardware resource allocation. A more thorough discussion of this accuracy-versus-resource trade-off is warranted.

**Originality**

The core idea of integrating hardware constraints into the training loop for spiking networks is compelling. While other methods exist for training SNNs, including backpropagation-like rules, the specific formulation presented here appears novel. To better situate the work, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a detailed discussion and comparison with other established backpropagation-style rules for spiking networks, such as SpikeProp and its successors. This would help clarify the unique contributions and advantages of the proposed approach.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, several areas require clarification to improve the manuscript's completeness and impact.

**Major Points for Clarification:**

1.  **Novelty Claims:** The claims at the end of Section 1 could be rephrased. As currently written, they might inadvertently suggest that this is the first paper to propose a training methodology for spiking neurons with reduced-precision synapses. A more nuanced statement that acknowledges prior work while highlighting this paper's specific contributions would be more accurate.

2.  **Hardware Demonstration:** The successful deployment on the TrueNorth chip is an excellent validation of the training method. It would be clearer to frame this as a crucial proof-of-concept and validation rather than as a core novel feature of the training algorithm itself.

3.  **Technical and Experimental Details:** Several key details are missing or unclear, which hinders a full understanding and potential reproducibility of the work.
    *   What is the precise meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" in Section 2? Please elaborate on how this metric is derived.
    *   The network topology is mentioned throughout the paper, but a consolidated, clear description in one place is missing. This would greatly aid readability.
    *   The probabilistic synaptic updates are central to the method. What is the impact on training time compared to a standard backpropagation algorithm? An estimate of the computational overhead would be very informative.
    *   What is the input representation during testing? Are the inputs encoded as spike trains, and if so, what are the characteristic spike rates?
    *   The experimental results for a single 30-core network ensemble show surprisingly low performance, especially when compared to the 5-core network. Could the authors provide insight into why this is the case?
    *   Conversely, the results for the different 5-core network ensembles are very consistent. What is the reason for this low variance across ensembles?

4.  **Comparison to Related Work:** The characterization of reference [12] on line 399 as solely a "train-then-constrain" approach might be incomplete. That work appears to incorporate some constraints (e.g., bias=0) during training. Please clarify this comparison to ensure an accurate representation of prior art.

**Minor Points:**

*   There is a typo on line 399 ("approach approach").

**Summary**

This paper presents a well-motivated and interesting training algorithm for spiking neural networks that successfully accounts for hardware constraints. The validation on a physical neuromorphic platform is a significant strength. The manuscript would be substantially improved by addressing the points of clarity raised above, particularly regarding the experimental details, comparison to related work, and the trade-offs involved in using large ensembles. Addressing these issues will strengthen the paper's contribution and provide the community with a clearer understanding of the method's capabilities and limitations.
