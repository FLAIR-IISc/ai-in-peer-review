The authors present a compelling work on a backpropagation training method leveraging spike probabilities, specifically designed with the hardware limitations of a spiking neural network platform featuring discrete synapses. This is a timely and relevant topic given the growing interest in deploying deep networks on dedicated neuromorphic hardware.

**Quality:** The proposed training methodology is valuable, particularly in the context of emerging multi-neuron hardware platforms with inherent constraints.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and accessible. However, the claims presented at the end of Section 1 could benefit from rephrasing. As currently stated, they might be misinterpreted to suggest a novel training approach employing spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses – a claim that requires careful nuance. Furthermore, demonstrating the network’s functionality on a specific hardware platform (TN, in this case) should be positioned as a validation of the training method, rather than a novel contribution in itself. 

In Section 2, further clarification is needed regarding the meaning of “0.15 bits per synapse.” The network topology would be more easily understood with a consolidated description. Additionally, the paper should address the increase in training time resulting from the probabilistic synaptic update scheme. The notably lower performance observed from a single ensemble of the 30-core network, compared to the 5-core network, warrants explanation. Similarly, the consistency of results across different ensembles of the 5-core network should be discussed. Finally, providing the input spike rates during testing, and clarifying whether the input is presented as a spike train, would be beneficial. Reference 12 (line 399) describes training with constraints (e.g., bias=0), indicating a more complex approach than a simple “train-then-constrain” strategy (note a typographical repetition: “approach approach” on this line).

**Originality:** It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion comparing the proposed method to other established spiking backpropagation algorithms, such as SpikeProp.

**Significance:** The development of training methods that explicitly account for hardware constraints is a significant contribution. However, the constraints considered in this work are specific to the TN architecture and may not generalize to all neuromorphic platforms. The reliance on a 64-ensemble configuration to achieve the reported accuracy implies a substantial demand for hardware resources. Overall, this is an interesting paper detailing a backpropagation-based training method that addresses the hardware constraints inherent in spiking neural networks with discrete synapses.
