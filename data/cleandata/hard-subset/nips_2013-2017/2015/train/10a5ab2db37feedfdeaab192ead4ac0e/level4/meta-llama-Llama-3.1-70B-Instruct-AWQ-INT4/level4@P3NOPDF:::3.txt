The authors present an interesting paper that explores a backpropagation training method utilizing spike probabilities, taking into account the hardware constraints of a specific platform featuring spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This topic is of current interest in mapping deep networks to multi-neuron hardware platforms.

Quality: The proposed training method is useful, particularly when considering new multi-neuron hardware platforms with constraints. Its ability to adapt to these constraints makes it a valuable contribution to the field.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to follow. However, some claims made at the end of Section 1 could be rephrased for clarity. As it stands, the text may suggest that the paper proposes a novel training methodology employing spiking neurons and synapses with reduced precision for the first time. This is not entirely accurate, and rewording these claims would improve the paper's clarity.

Additionally, the demonstration of running the network on a specific hardware platform, in this case, the TrueNorth (TN) chip, should not be presented as a novel feature. Instead, it should be viewed as a validation of the training method.

Several questions and clarifications are needed in Section 2. Specifically, the authors should explain what is meant by "0.15 bits per synapse." A clear and concise description of the network topology is also missing and should be provided in one place. Furthermore, the authors should discuss how the probabilistic synaptic connection update affects training time. The results from a single ensemble of the 30-core network are surprisingly low, especially when compared to the 5-core network. The authors should provide insight into this discrepancy. Additionally, the consistent results from different ensembles of the 5-core network require explanation. The spike rates for the inputs during testing should also be provided, and the authors should clarify whether the input is a spike train.

A minor correction is needed on line 399, where a typo occurs ("approach approach"). Moreover, the authors should note that Reference 12 includes constraints (e.g., bias=0) during training, which is not a simple train-then-constrain approach.

Originality: The authors should provide a discussion or comparison with other spiking backprop-type rules, such as SpikeProp, to further establish the originality of their work.

Significance: The development of new training methods that consider the constraints of hardware platforms is of great interest. However, the constraints considered in this paper are based on the TN architecture, and it is unclear whether these constraints apply to other platforms. The authors should discuss the generalizability of their approach. If the results are based on a 64-ensemble, it implies that more hardware resources are required to achieve the desired accuracy. This trade-off should be acknowledged and discussed.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting training method that takes into account the hardware constraints of a specific platform. While some clarifications and discussions are needed, the work is a valuable contribution to the field of spiking neural networks and their application to multi-neuron hardware platforms.
