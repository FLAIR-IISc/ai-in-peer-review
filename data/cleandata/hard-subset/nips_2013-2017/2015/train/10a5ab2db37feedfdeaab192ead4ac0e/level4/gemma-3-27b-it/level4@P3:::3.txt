This paper addresses the critical challenge of bridging the gap between the algorithmic success of backpropagation and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing. The authors propose a novel training methodology that treats spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, allowing standard backpropagation to be used while maintaining compatibility with spiking neural networks and low-precision hardware like the TrueNorth chip. This is a timely and relevant topic given the growing interest in deploying deep learning models on resource-constrained embedded systems.

**Quality:** The technical approach appears sound and well-motivated. The authors effectively demonstrate a method for training networks that can be directly mapped to neuromorphic hardware, overcoming a key incompatibility. The experimental results on MNIST, particularly the achieved accuracy and energy efficiency on TrueNorth, are compelling. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the impact of the probabilistic synaptic update on training convergence speed and stability. The justification for specific parameter choices (e.g., the learning rate schedule, dropout rate) could also be strengthened. While the approach seems promising, a more thorough comparison with alternative training methods designed for spiking neural networks would solidify the contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, although some sections could be improved. The claims made at the end of Section 1 regarding novelty are somewhat overstated. While the combination of elements presented is novel, framing it as the “first” methodology to achieve certain things could be misleading. The phrase "demonstrate for the first time" should be carefully reviewed throughout the paper. Section 2 lacks sufficient detail regarding the "0.15 bits per synapse" – a clearer explanation is needed. The network topology description is fragmented; consolidating this information into a single section with a clear diagram would improve readability. The discussion of results is somewhat unclear, specifically regarding the significantly lower performance of the single-member ensemble of the 30-core network compared to the 5-core network. More explanation is required as to why the ensembles of the 5-core network yield similar results. Details about the input spike representation used during testing (spike rates, whether it’s a spike train) are also missing and would be beneficial. Finally, a minor typo exists on line 399 ("approach approach").

**Originality:** The approach of using probabilistic representations to reconcile backpropagation with spiking networks is a valuable contribution. However, the authors should explicitly discuss and compare their work with existing spiking backpropagation rules such as SpikeProp [references needed], highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of their method. A more comprehensive literature review in this area would strengthen the originality argument. While the demonstration on TrueNorth is valuable validation, it is not inherently novel in itself; the core innovation lies in the training method.

**Significance:** The development of training methodologies that account for hardware constraints is a significant step towards practical neuromorphic computing. However, the current work is heavily tied to the specific architecture of TrueNorth. While this is a useful demonstration, the authors should discuss the generalizability of their approach to other neuromorphic platforms with different constraints. The need for a 64-member ensemble to achieve high accuracy raises concerns about scalability and resource requirements. It would be helpful to explore methods to improve the performance of smaller ensembles or even single networks. Despite these limitations, the paper presents a compelling approach to bridging the gap between algorithm and hardware, with the potential to enable energy-efficient deep learning on neuromorphic systems.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical problem in neuromorphic computing: training spiking networks with backpropagation.
*   Demonstrates a viable training methodology that can be mapped directly to hardware.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art energy efficiency on the TrueNorth chip.
*   Clear and well-motivated technical approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Claims of novelty are slightly overstated.
*   Insufficient comparison with existing spiking backpropagation methods.
*   Limited discussion of the generalizability to other neuromorphic architectures.
*   Scalability concerns related to the ensemble size required for high accuracy.
*   Lack of detail regarding input spike representation and training convergence analysis.



**Overall Assessment:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field and has the potential to be impactful. However, addressing the concerns outlined above – particularly regarding novelty, comparison to existing work, and generalizability – would significantly strengthen the manuscript. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should focus on clarifying the technical details, strengthening the originality argument, and providing a more comprehensive discussion of the limitations and potential future directions of their work.
