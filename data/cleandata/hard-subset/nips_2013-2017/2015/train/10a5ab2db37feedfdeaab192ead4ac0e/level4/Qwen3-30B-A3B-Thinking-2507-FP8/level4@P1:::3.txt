

The authors present a compelling approach to bridging the gap between backpropagation training and neuromorphic hardware constraints, specifically targeting platforms with spiking neurons and low-precision synapses. This work addresses an increasingly important challenge as the field moves toward deploying neural networks on energy-constrained embedded systems. The paper's core contribution is a training methodology that treats synaptic connections and neuron outputs as probabilities, enabling standard backpropagation to be applied within hardware-compatible constraints. The authors demonstrate their approach on the TrueNorth neuromorphic platform using MNIST, achieving notable accuracy and energy efficiency metrics.

Quality: The technical approach is sound and well-justified. The probabilistic interpretation of synaptic states provides a natural mapping from continuous training to discrete hardware deployment, avoiding the need for post-training discretization that often degrades performance. The authors carefully address both the training methodology and hardware mapping, with appropriate experimental validation on TrueNorth. The claims about accuracy (99.42% with ensemble of 64) and energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image with ensemble of 1) are well-supported by their results. The paper demonstrates a complete, rigorous approach rather than merely proposing a concept.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, though some sections could benefit from additional clarification. The key innovation—treating synaptic states as probabilities for backpropagation—could be more explicitly distinguished from previous work in Section 1. The phrase "for the first time" regarding the combination of spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity is slightly misleading; while the specific combination is novel, similar elements have appeared in prior work. The claim about running on TrueNorth being a novel feature is inaccurate—this is validation of the training method rather than a novel contribution. The description of 0.15 bits per synapse (Section 2) requires more explanation; this appears to refer to the additional storage cost for the lookup table scheme, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate this. The network topology section would benefit from a more concise, unified description rather than scattering details throughout.

Originality: The paper makes a meaningful contribution by integrating three key elements—spiking neurons, extremely low-precision synapses (less than 2 bits), and constrained connectivity—into a single training framework. While prior work has addressed subsets of these elements (e.g., [12] uses spiking neurons with high-precision weights, [15] uses binary synapses with continuous neurons), this is the first to combine all three within a single training methodology. The authors should more explicitly compare their approach to SpikeProp [22] (not cited) and other spiking backpropagation methods, as this would strengthen the novelty claim. The "constrain-then-train" methodology provides a clearer performance correspondence than "train-then-constrain" approaches, which is a significant conceptual contribution.

Significance: This work addresses a critical bottleneck in deploying deep learning on neuromorphic hardware. As energy efficiency becomes increasingly important for edge computing applications, the ability to train networks directly for hardware constraints represents a valuable advance. The demonstrated energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image) significantly outperforms previous approaches (e.g., 4 μJ per classification in [16]), making this work highly relevant to the field. The approach's compatibility with TrueNorth and potential adaptability to other neuromorphic platforms (Section 6) broadens its applicability. However, the authors should acknowledge that the 64-ensemble approach requires significantly more hardware resources than the single-ensemble approach, which may limit its practical deployment in resource-constrained settings.

Strengths: The paper provides a technically sound methodology with clear hardware mapping; the experimental results on TrueNorth are compelling; the "constrain-then-train" approach is conceptually novel and well-motivated; the energy efficiency results represent a significant improvement over prior work.

Weaknesses: The paper overstates novelty by claiming the first demonstration of training with spiking neurons and low-precision synapses (subsets of this combination have appeared previously); the description of the 0.15 bits per synapse is unclear; the comparison to SpikeProp is missing; the explanation for why the 30-core network with single ensemble performs worse than the 5-core network with single ensemble is insufficient; the paper doesn't discuss the training time overhead compared to standard backpropagation.

Pro-acceptance arguments: This paper provides a valuable, practical solution to a significant problem in neuromorphic computing; the methodology is technically sound and well-validated; the results demonstrate meaningful improvements in energy efficiency; the approach is generalizable to other neuromorphic platforms.

Con-acceptance arguments: The novelty claim is overstated; the paper contains some unclear technical descriptions; the paper doesn't sufficiently address the training time overhead; the ensemble approach requires significant hardware resources for high accuracy.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes an important contribution to the intersection of deep learning and neuromorphic computing. While some claims about novelty need refinement and certain technical details require clarification, the core methodology is compelling and well-executed. The work advances the state of the art in energy-efficient neural network deployment on neuromorphic hardware. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues and refine the novelty claims.
