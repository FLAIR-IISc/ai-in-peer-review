The authors present an interesting paper about a backpropagation training method using spike probabilities and taking into account the hardware constraints of a particular platform which has spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This is a topic of current interest in mapping deep networks to multi-neuron hardware platforms.

The paper's main contribution is a learning rule and a network topology that reconciles the apparent incompatibility between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware. The authors demonstrate a novel approach to train a network offline with hardware-supported connectivity, as well as continuous-valued input, neuron output, and synaptic weights, but values constrained to the range [0, 1]. These values represent probabilities, either of a spike occurring or of a particular synapse being "on", providing a means of mapping the training network to the deployment network, while providing a continuous and differentiable space for backpropagation.

Quality: The proposed training method is useful, especially in consideration of new multi-neuron hardware platforms with constraints. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the method's performance, including accuracy and energy efficiency. However, some claims made at the end of Section 1 could be reworded to avoid suggesting that the paper proposes a training methodology that employs spiking neurons and synapses with reduced precision for the first time.

Clarity: The paper is easy to read, but some sections could be improved for clarity. For instance, the explanation of the 0.15 bits per synapse in Section 2 could be expanded. Additionally, the network topology is not clearly described in one place, making it difficult to understand the architecture of the proposed network. The authors could also provide more information on the training time, including how much longer it takes due to the probabilistic synaptic connection update.

The results section raises some questions, such as why the result from a single ensemble of the 30 core network is so low, especially when compared to the 5 core network. The authors could also provide more insight into why the results of the different ensembles of the 5 core network are about the same. Furthermore, the spike rates for the inputs during testing could be provided, as well as information on whether the input is a spike train.

Originality: The authors could provide a discussion or comparison with other spiking backprop-type rules, such as SpikeProp, to better situate their work in the context of existing research.

Significance: The development of new training methods that consider constraints of hardware platforms is of great interest. However, the constraints considered during training in this paper are based on the TN architecture, which might not all apply to other platforms. The authors could discuss the potential applicability of their method to other platforms and the limitations of their approach.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks that takes into account the constraints of neuromorphic hardware.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the method's performance, including accuracy and energy efficiency.
* The paper is well-written and easy to read.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the network topology and the 0.15 bits per synapse.
* The results section raises some questions that could be addressed with additional information.
* The authors could provide more insight into the potential applicability of their method to other platforms and the limitations of their approach.
