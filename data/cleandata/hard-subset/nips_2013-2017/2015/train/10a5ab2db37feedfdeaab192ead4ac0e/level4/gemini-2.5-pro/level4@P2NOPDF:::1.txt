This paper presents strong and compelling experimental results, particularly in terms of classification accuracy and estimated power consumption for binarized networks. The primary concern with the manuscript, however, lies in its claims of novelty, as both the general methodology and the specific learning algorithm appear to be significantly less novel than presented.

The main points requiring revision are as follows:

1.  **Similarity to Prior Algorithms:** The learning algorithm detailed in Section 3.2 bears a strong resemblance to prior work. Specifically, its final form appears to be a hybrid of the algorithms presented in Zhao et al. [14] and Soudry et al. [15], derived using nearly identical mathematical approximations. The paper should be revised to explicitly acknowledge this significant overlap.

2.  **Novelty of the "Constrain-then-Train" Methodology:** The first paragraph of the discussion section presents the "constrain-then-train" methodology—learning a weight distribution and then sampling from it to produce a fixed network for inference—as a new contribution. However, this approach was previously proposed and experimentally validated in Soudry et al. [15]. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this prior work when discussing their methodology.

3.  **Misleading Novelty Claims:** The claims of novelty in the introduction are not entirely accurate. Specifically, claim (i) is misleading. The "Expectation Backpropagation" algorithm from [15] already provides a backpropagation-based update rule for networks with both binary weights and binary neurons (i.e., using a sign activation function, where zero input is a measure-zero event). Furthermore, this existing framework can be readily adapted for constrained connectivity, which challenges the authors' assertion of novelty in this area.

4.  **Inaccurate State-of-the-Art Comparison:** The paper's positioning relative to the state-of-the-art (SOTA) needs revision. The cited SOTA results for binary networks appear to be outdated. To my knowledge, the previous benchmark for networks with binary weights and neurons on MNIST, tested without data augmentation (consistent with the experimental setup here), was set by the algorithm in [15] (see arXiv:1503.03562v3). A proper comparison against this work is necessary to accurately contextualize the paper's contributions.

In summary, while the experimental achievements of this paper are significant, the manuscript in its current form overstates its novelty. The algorithm and methodology draw heavily from existing literature, particularly [14] and [15], and the comparison to the state-of-the-art is incomplete. I would recommend that the authors thoroughly revise the paper to accurately position their work within the context of existing research, clearly delineating their specific contributions from those already established in the cited references. Addressing these concerns would substantially strengthen the paper.
