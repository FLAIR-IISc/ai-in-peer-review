Summary
The paper proposes a backpropagation-based training method that uses spike probabilities and explicitly accounts for hardware constraints of neuromorphic platforms with spiking neurons and discrete synapses (e.g., TN). This is a timely topic given the growing interest in mapping deep networks to multi-core spiking hardware.

Strengths
- Addresses practical constraints of neuromorphic hardware during training rather than retrofitting post hoc.
- Method appears useful for platforms with limited synaptic precision and spiking dynamics.
- The paper is generally easy to follow, and the hardware validation supports the feasibility of the approach.

Clarity and Presentation
- The claims at the end of Section 1 should be reworded to avoid implying that this is the first training methodology to use spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses. As written, the novelty may be overstated if read quickly.
- Demonstrating the network on TN is a valuable validation, but should not be framed as a standalone novelty.
- The network topology is not described in one consolidated place. Please provide a clear, self-contained description of all architectural details (layer sizes, connectivity patterns, synaptic discretization scheme, neuron model, and any constraints applied).

Technical Questions and Requests for Clarification
- Section 2: What does “0.15 bits per synapse” mean operationally? Please define how you compute or interpret fractional bits per synapse, and how this maps to the actual discretization used on hardware.
- Training time: How much longer is training due to the probabilistic synaptic update mechanism? Please quantify wall-clock overhead and sample efficiency compared to a deterministic baseline.
- Performance across core counts: Why is the result from a single 30-core ensemble so low, especially relative to the 5-core network? What factors (e.g., communication constraints, spike-rate scaling, quantization) drive this difference?
- Ensemble behavior: Why are different ensembles of the 5-core network performing about the same? Is there reduced diversity across ensemble members, and if so, why? Please report variance across runs.
- Input representation: What are the input spike rates during testing, and is the input provided as spike trains or as rate-coded Poisson spikes derived from static inputs? Clarify the encoding and any normalization applied.
- Reference 12 (line 399) is not purely “train-then-constrain”; it includes constraints (e.g., bias = 0) during training. Please correct this characterization and the typo on that line (“approach approach”).

Originality
- Please discuss how your approach relates to prior spiking backpropagation-type methods, such as SpikeProp and its variants. In particular:
  - What are the conceptual and practical differences between using spike probabilities in your gradient estimation and the surrogate/approximate gradients used in prior work?
  - How does your method compare in terms of stability, convergence, and compatibility with discrete synapses and hardware constraints?
  - If possible, include a brief empirical or qualitative comparison to contextualize novelty.

Significance
- Developing training methods that incorporate hardware constraints is important for making neuromorphic computing practical. This work contributes to that direction.
- However, many constraints used in training appear specific to TN. Please discuss which constraints are generic (e.g., limited precision, synaptic sparsity, no bias) versus TN-specific (e.g., core-to-core routing limits), and how portable the method is to other platforms.
- If the best results rely on an ensemble of 64 networks, this implies substantial hardware resource requirements to achieve target accuracy. Please quantify the trade-off between ensemble size and accuracy, including inference latency, energy, and area implications on TN (or estimated for other platforms).

Suggestions for Improvement
- Consolidate the architectural description and training constraints in a dedicated subsection, with a table or bullet list specifying each constraint and its origin (hardware-imposed vs. design choice).
- Provide ablations isolating the impact of key constraints (e.g., synaptic precision, no bias, spike-rate limits) on accuracy and training time.
- Report detailed training curves and variance across seeds to assess stability of the probabilistic updates.
- Clarify the calibration between the probabilistic training objective (spike probabilities) and measured spike statistics on hardware at test time.

Minor Issues
- Correct the description of Reference 12 as noted above.
- Fix the typo “approach approach” on line 399.
- Define all acronyms (e.g., TN) on first use.

Overall Assessment
The paper addresses a relevant and practical problem: training spiking networks under realistic hardware constraints. The approach is promising and the experimental validation is useful. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should (i) temper claims of novelty, (ii) clarify technical details and training overhead, (iii) analyze performance differences across core counts and ensembles, and (iv) position the method relative to prior spiking backpropagation approaches such as SpikeProp. With these revisions, the work would offer a clearer and more broadly applicable contribution to neuromorphic learning under constraints.
