

This paper addresses the important challenge of training neural networks for neuromorphic hardware platforms that employ spiking neurons and discrete synapses. The authors propose a "constrain-then-train" approach where they treat synaptic connections and neuron outputs as continuous probabilities during backpropagation training, which can then be mapped directly to the discrete hardware constraints of neuromorphic chips like TrueNorth. This is a timely contribution as it bridges the gap between the algorithmic success of backpropagation-based deep learning and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic hardware.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach that successfully demonstrates how to train networks for neuromorphic hardware. The probabilistic interpretation of synaptic connections and neuron outputs provides a theoretically grounded method for bridging the continuous backpropagation framework with discrete hardware constraints. The experimental results are well-supported with comprehensive energy-accuracy tradeoff analysis on the MNIST dataset. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the theoretical guarantees of the Gaussian approximation used in the training process (Equation 3), particularly regarding its validity under the specific network configurations used. The authors should clarify why the approximation is appropriate for their network architecture and how it might impact generalizability.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, with clear descriptions of the training methodology and hardware mapping. However, some technical details require clarification. The claim in Section 1 that "we demonstrate for the first time an offline training methodology using backpropagation to create a network that employs spiking neurons, synapses requiring less bits of precision than even trinary weights, and constrained connectivity" is misleading. While the combination is novel, the use of spiking neurons with backpropagation has been explored previously (e.g., [12]), and the paper should clarify how their approach differs from prior work. The reference to "0.15 bits per synapse" in Section 2 requires elaboration on what this metric represents. The paper would also benefit from a more detailed explanation of the network topology (Section 4) in one cohesive section rather than scattered descriptions.

**Originality:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution by combining three key elements (spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity) in a single training framework. The "constrain-then-train" approach is more principled than "train-then-constrain" methods, as it ensures that training directly optimizes for the hardware constraints. However, the paper would strengthen its originality claim by more thoroughly comparing with related approaches, particularly SpikeProp [14] and other spiking backpropagation methods. The authors should clarify how their probabilistic approach differs from and improves upon these existing methods.

**Significance:** The work addresses a critical bottleneck in deploying deep learning to energy-constrained embedded systems. The demonstrated results (99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ per image with ensemble of 64, and 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image with single ensemble) represent significant improvements in energy efficiency compared to previous neuromorphic approaches. The approach is particularly valuable as it shows how to leverage existing deep learning success for hardware-aware deployment. However, the paper should more clearly discuss the practical tradeoffs of using ensembles (e.g., requiring 64 times the hardware resources for the high-accuracy configuration) and whether the approach scales to more complex tasks beyond MNIST.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Addresses a timely and important problem in energy-efficient AI deployment
- Provides a principled "constrain-then-train" approach that bridges backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware
- Demonstrates significant energy-accuracy tradeoffs with real hardware results on TrueNorth
- Well-structured methodology with clear experimental validation

*Con:*
- Some claims about novelty are overstated without sufficient comparison to related work
- Key technical details (Gaussian approximation validity, 0.15 bits per synapse) require clarification
- Network topology description is fragmented and could be more cohesive
- Limited discussion of practical hardware tradeoffs for ensemble approaches

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Clarify the novelty claim by more precisely comparing with prior work on spiking backpropagation (e.g., [12], [14], [15])
2. Expand the discussion of the Gaussian approximation's validity and limitations
3. Provide a consolidated description of the network topology in a single section
4. Clarify the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" and its significance
5. Discuss the hardware resource implications of using ensembles more thoroughly
6. Add a brief comparison with SpikeProp and other spiking backpropagation methods

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing field of hardware-aware deep learning, and the authors have successfully demonstrated a practical approach to training networks for neuromorphic hardware. With some clarifications to the methodology and more precise discussion of novelty and limitations, this work would make a strong contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
