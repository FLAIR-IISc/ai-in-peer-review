This paper addresses the important challenge of bridging the gap between the success of backpropagation in deep learning and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing. The authors propose a training method that utilizes probabilistic representations of spikes and discrete synapses, allowing standard backpropagation to be applied to networks designed for deployment on neuromorphic hardware. This work is timely and relevant given the increasing interest in mapping deep networks onto specialized, energy-efficient platforms.

**Quality:** The proposed training methodology is a valuable contribution, particularly in the context of emerging neuromorphic hardware. The approach of constraining the network to align with hardware limitations *during* training, rather than as a post-processing step, is well-justified and demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of the system design trade-offs. The experimental results on the MNIST dataset, and specifically the demonstration of real-time performance on the TrueNorth chip, provide strong support for the effectiveness of the method.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, the claims of novelty in Section 1 could be more precisely stated. Suggesting it’s the “first” methodology to employ spiking neurons and low-precision synapses is potentially misleading, as prior work exists (see Originality). It would be more accurate to emphasize the novelty of simultaneously addressing all constraints – spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, *and* constrained connectivity – within a backpropagation framework. The explanation of the 0.15 bits per synapse in Section 2 requires clarification. The description of the network topology could be consolidated for better readability; a clear diagram illustrating the connections between layers would be highly beneficial. The impact of the probabilistic synaptic connection update on training time should be discussed. Furthermore, the discrepancy in performance between the different ensemble sizes for the 30-core network warrants further investigation and explanation. Details regarding input spike rates during testing are needed (is the input a spike train?). There is a minor typographical error on line 399 (“approach approach”).

**Originality:** While the paper builds upon existing work in probabilistic backpropagation and neuromorphic computing, the combination of constraints and the direct mapping to hardware represent a novel contribution. However, the authors should explicitly address and compare their approach to related spiking backpropagation methods such as SpikeProp. A discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of their method relative to these alternatives would strengthen the originality argument. The work presented is significantly different from papers in previous NIPS proceedings in terms of combining all the mentioned constraints.

**Significance:** The development of training methods that explicitly consider hardware constraints is highly significant. While the specific constraints addressed here are rooted in the TrueNorth architecture, the underlying principle of constraint-aware training is broadly applicable to other neuromorphic platforms. However, the reliance on a 64-member ensemble to achieve high accuracy raises a practical concern: the increased hardware resources required to attain the desired performance. A discussion of the trade-offs between accuracy, energy efficiency, and ensemble size would be valuable. Overall, this paper presents a promising step toward realizing the potential of energy-efficient, brain-inspired computing systems.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical problem in neuromorphic computing: bridging the gap between backpropagation and spiking neural networks.
*   Demonstrates a novel training method that effectively incorporates hardware constraints.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on MNIST with excellent energy efficiency on the TrueNorth chip.
*   Provides a clear and well-written presentation of the method and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Claims of novelty could be more precise and accurately positioned within the existing literature.
*   Several aspects of the experimental results require further explanation and analysis (e.g., ensemble size performance, input spike rates).
*   The practicality of large ensembles for resource-constrained applications should be discussed.
*   A comparison to other spiking backpropagation algorithms (e.g., SpikeProp) is missing.
