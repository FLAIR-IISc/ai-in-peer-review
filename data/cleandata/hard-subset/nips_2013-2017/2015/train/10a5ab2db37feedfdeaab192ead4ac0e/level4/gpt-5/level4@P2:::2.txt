Summary
This paper presents a hardware-aware training method that reconciles backpropagation with neuromorphic deployment constraints. The key idea is to interpret spikes and binary synapses as Bernoulli random variables and to train, offline, a network whose activations and connection states are continuous probabilities in [0,1]. After training, the model is mapped to spiking hardware by sampling these probabilities to instantiate one or more networks; outputs are combined by ensemble averaging. The network topology mirrors TrueNorth constraints (limited fan-in, binary crossbar, axon types) via a sliding-window, sparsely connected, multi-core layout. The authors also introduce an initialization scheme that balances excitatory/inhibitory synaptic strengths and reduces redundancy across neurons by minimizing pairwise input covariance under hardware weight constraints. The approach is validated on MNIST by deploying two architectures on TrueNorth: a compact 5-core network and a larger 30-core network. Reported performance ranges from 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image (single model) to 99.42% at 108 μJ per image (64-member ensemble), all at 1 ms/image (1000 images/s).

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to work that trains spiking networks with high-precision weights [9–12], and to methods using low-precision or trinary synapses but continuous neurons [13], and to binary/binary probabilistic backpropagation with full connectivity [14–15]. Its contribution is to combine spiking neurons, sub-2-bit synapses, and constrained connectivity within a single backpropagation-based training framework, and to demonstrate real-time deployment and energy measurements on TrueNorth with competitive MNIST accuracy. The results compare favorably with prior SNN accuracies, including those using higher-precision weights.

Strengths
- Clear, well-written presentation with a coherent end-to-end story from learning rule to hardware mapping.
- Hardware-aware “constrain-then-train” formulation that directly optimizes for deployment constraints rather than post hoc discretization.
- Practical deployment on TrueNorth with measured energy and throughput, not just simulation.
- Thoughtful network initialization to reduce redundancy; topology that respects core input constraints while expanding receptive fields.
- Strong accuracy/energy trade-offs and a clean ensemble mapping strategy with a single training run.

Weaknesses and requested clarifications
- Practical significance and application scope are under-discussed. Please clarify which real-world tasks (beyond MNIST) are most suitable, and how the ensemble size affects deployability under memory/latency constraints.
- Comparative advantage over alternatives (e.g., train-then-constrain SNN conversions [9–12]) is not rigorously quantified. A controlled comparison under matched connectivity/precision and latency would strengthen the case.
- Experimental details are sparse:
  - Input encoding: how continuous inputs are converted to spikes (rate or single-shot Bernoulli), firing frequencies, and time windowing.
  - Neuron model configuration: exact leak, threshold, reset, floor, and axon-type LUT values; mapping of biases to hardware parameters.
  - Synaptic strength sharing: specifics of the 4 axon types and the distribution of ±1 assignments across cores.
  - Ensemble implementation: independence between members, memory/core usage, and aggregation latency.
- Evaluation is limited to MNIST; no ablations (e.g., effect of initialization, sliding-window parameters, or constrained connectivity) or robustness tests (noise, input variability).
- Related hardware platforms: the survey would be more complete if it also cited Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015), and arXiv:1506.05427.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and well-motivated. The probabilistic forward model and gradient approximations are reasonable given the hardware regime (balanced weights, large fan-in). The experimental validation is convincing for MNIST, but fuller methodological details and controlled comparisons would improve reproducibility and support broader claims.

Clarity
The paper is clear and well organized. Adding the requested implementation details (input-to-spike conversion, neuron/axon configuration, and weight-sharing specifics) would make the work easier to reproduce and to compare against prior SNN conversion pipelines.

Originality
The constrain-then-train probabilistic backpropagation tailored to binary synapses, spiking neurons, and limited connectivity—together with an explicit TrueNorth deployment—is a novel and valuable combination relative to prior art.

Significance
The demonstrated energy efficiency and real-time throughput are noteworthy, and the method could generalize to other neuromorphic substrates. However, significance would be stronger with results on more complex datasets or sensor modalities, and with clearer guidance on when ensembles are practical for embedded applications.

Pros
- Elegant bridge between backprop and neuromorphic constraints.
- Real hardware measurements with excellent energy/throughput.
- Single training pass supports flexible ensemble construction.
- Clear exposition and thoughtful initialization/topology design.

Cons
- Limited task scope (MNIST only) and missing ablations.
- Incomplete methodological details hinder reproducibility.
- Comparative advantage over alternative SNN training/conversion methods not fully established.
- Practical application scenarios and ensemble trade-offs not discussed in depth.

Recommendations for improvement
- Provide a detailed description of input spike encoding, neuron model parameters, axon-type LUT configuration, and the exact shared weight values.
- Include controlled comparisons with conversion-based SNNs under matched constraints, and ablations isolating the contributions of initialization, topology, and ensemble size.
- Expand related work to include additional neuromorphic platforms (e.g., Qiao et al., 2015; arXiv:1506.05427).
- Discuss deployment scenarios (e.g., vision, audio) and the operational cost/benefit of ensembles in embedded settings.

Overall assessment
A strong, clear contribution that advances hardware-aware learning for neuromorphic deployment. With added implementation details and broader evaluation or comparisons, this work would have high impact.
