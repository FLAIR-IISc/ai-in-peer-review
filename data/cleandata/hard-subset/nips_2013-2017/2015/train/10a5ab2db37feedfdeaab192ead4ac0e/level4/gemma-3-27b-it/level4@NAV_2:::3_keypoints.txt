- Proposes a backpropagation training method using spike probabilities tailored to neuromorphic hardware with spiking neurons and discrete synapses.  
- Method is technically sound and demonstrates a functional system mapped to TrueNorth hardware.  
- Need a detailed analysis of computational cost and scalability of probabilistic synaptic updates during training.  
- Compare training time and complexity directly to standard backpropagation to strengthen the contribution.  
- Reword Section 1 claims to avoid implying novelty of training spiking, low-precision networks when that may not be new.  
- Frame the TrueNorth implementation as validation of the training method rather than a primary novelty.  
- Clarify the meaning and calculation of “0.15 bits per synapse.”  
- Consolidate network topology descriptions into a single subsection for readability.  
- Explain the large accuracy discrepancy between the 30-core single-member ensemble and the 5-core network.  
- Address the curious consistency (or lack thereof) across different ensembles of the 5-core network.  
- Provide input spike-rate details during testing and state whether inputs are spike trains.  
- Fix typographical errors (e.g., “approach approach” on line 399).  
- Better situate the work relative to prior spiking backpropagation rules (e.g., SpikeProp) and highlight distinctions.  
- Discuss generalizability of the TrueNorth-driven constraints to other neuromorphic platforms.  
- Address resource concerns from reliance on a 64-member ensemble and explore methods to improve single-ensemble performance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: viable neuromorphic-compatible training method, strong MNIST accuracy under TrueNorth constraints, useful bridge between backpropagation and neuromorphic computing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: novelty not clearly distinguished from prior work, insufficient discussion of computational cost and generalizability, and unexplained inconsistencies between network sizes and ensembles.
