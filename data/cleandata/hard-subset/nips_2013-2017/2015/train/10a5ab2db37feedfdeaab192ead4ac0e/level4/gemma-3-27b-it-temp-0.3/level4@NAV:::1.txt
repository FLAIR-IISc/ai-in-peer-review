1) Section 3.2 should acknowledge the strong relationship between the presented algorithm and the approaches detailed in references [14] J. Zhao, J. Shawe-Taylor, and M. van Daalen, "Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks," Neural Networks, vol. 9, no. 6, pp. 991 - 998, 1996 and [15] Soudry, Daniel, Itay Hubara, and Ron Meir. "Expectation backpropagation: Parameter-free training of multilayer neural networks with continuous or discrete weights." Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems. 2014. The current algorithm represents a hybrid of these existing methods and relies on identical approximations, a connection that should be explicitly stated.

2) The discussion’s opening paragraph currently implies the “constrain-then-train” methodology – learning a weight distribution followed by sampling for a fixed network ensemble at test time – is a novel contribution. However, this approach was previously proposed and evaluated in reference [15].

3) The novelty claim (i) presented in the introduction’s final paragraph is potentially misleading. The “expectation backpropagation” algorithm described in ref. [15] constitutes a backpropagation update rule (utilizing expectation propagation) specifically designed for binary neurons (employing a sign activation function that avoids exact zero inputs) and binary weights, and is readily adaptable to constrained connectivity scenarios.

4) The reported comparison to prior state-of-the-art results appears inaccurate. Based on my understanding, the algorithm presented in ref. [15] previously achieved the best performance with binary weights and neurons, demonstrated on the MNIST dataset (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf) without the use of data augmentation, unlike the current work.
