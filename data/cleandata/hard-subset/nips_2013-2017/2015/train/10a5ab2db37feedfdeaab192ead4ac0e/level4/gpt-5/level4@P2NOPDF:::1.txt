1) Please explicitly acknowledge that the algorithm in Section 3.2 is, in its final form, closely related to the methods in [14] (Zhao, Shawe-Taylor, and van Daalen, 1996) and [15] (Soudry, Hubara, and Meir, 2014). The presented procedure appears to be a hybrid of these two approaches and is derived using essentially the same approximations.

2) The first paragraph of the Discussion gives the impression that the “constrain-then-train” approach—learning a distribution over weights and then sampling to form a fixed ensemble at test time—is novel. This strategy was already proposed and empirically evaluated in [15].

3) Novelty claim (i) in the last paragraph of the Introduction is overstated. The “expectation backpropagation” algorithm in [15] is a backpropagation-style update derived via expectation propagation, implemented for binary neurons (sign activations, for which zero input is almost surely not attained) with binary weights, and it readily supports constrained connectivity.

4) The prior state-of-the-art cited in the paper is incorrect. To the best of my knowledge, the strongest previous results for binary weights (and neurons) were achieved by [15] on MNIST without data augmentation, which matches the evaluation protocol used here (see http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf).

Edited after authors’ feedback: Thank you for agreeing to address these issues. The paper presents compelling and, in several respects, novel experimental results, particularly regarding accuracy and power consumption. However, both the overarching methodology and the specific algorithm are less novel than currently claimed. The manuscript should be revised to (i) clearly position the Section 3.2 algorithm as a hybrid closely related to [14] and [15], derived under the same approximations; (ii) clarify that the “constrain-then-train” paradigm has prior art in [15]; (iii) temper novelty claim (i) in light of the capabilities of expectation backpropagation for binary neurons and weights with constrained connectivity; and (iv) correct the description of prior state-of-the-art results to properly credit [15] under a comparable evaluation setting. These changes will improve the paper’s clarity, fairness, and scholarly positioning while preserving its strong empirical contributions.
