This paper addresses the crucial challenge of bridging the gap between the high performance of deep learning with backpropagation and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing. The authors propose a method to train networks with continuous-valued weights and activations using backpropagation, while constraining these values to represent probabilities of spiking or synaptic activation. This allows for a natural mapping to neuromorphic hardware like the TrueNorth chip, and they demonstrate strong results on the MNIST dataset, achieving both high accuracy (99.42% with an ensemble of 64) and excellent energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image with an ensemble of 1).

The core idea is well-motivated, stemming from the inherent incompatibility between the continuous nature of backpropagation and the discrete, spiking nature of neuromorphic hardware. The paper correctly identifies previous attempts to reconcile these approaches (e.g., [9-12, 13-15]) and positions its contribution as the first to successfully combine backpropagation training with spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity. The experimental results on the TrueNorth chip are particularly compelling, showcasing a significant improvement in power efficiency compared to existing approaches [16]. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Experimental Results:** The reported accuracy and energy efficiency numbers are impressive and demonstrate the practical potential of the proposed method.
*   **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and the need for a solution that bridges the gap between backpropagation and neuromorphic computing.
*   **Practical Implementation:** The focus on a specific hardware platform (TrueNorth) lends credibility to the work and highlights its real-world applicability.
*   **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-structured and easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core algorithm presented in section 3.2 shares significant similarities with previously published work, particularly [14, 15]. The authors should acknowledge this more explicitly and discuss the specific differences and contributions in greater detail. The current description implies a greater degree of originality than is warranted.
*   **Misleading Claims of Novelty:** The claim in the introduction (i) that this work is the first to train a network with the specified characteristics is inaccurate, as demonstrated by ref [15]. Similarly, the discussion section's portrayal of the “constrain-then-train” methodology as novel overlooks its prior introduction in [15].
*   **Inaccurate Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper’s claim of outperforming previous state-of-the-art results requires clarification.  The cited accuracy for ref [12] (99.12%) and ref [15] (97.88%) are not directly comparable to the presented results given the differing training and evaluation methodologies (e.g., data augmentation). A more rigorous comparison to ref [15] is needed, especially considering that ref [15] also achieved strong results on MNIST with binary weights and neurons. The statement regarding ref [15] should be revised.
*   **Limited Discussion of Approximation:** The approximation made in Equation 4 for computational efficiency is justified but could benefit from a more thorough discussion of its potential impact on accuracy.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing by demonstrating a practical method for training spiking neural networks using backpropagation. The experimental results are highly encouraging. However, the paper suffers from overstated claims of novelty and a lack of sufficient acknowledgment of related work. Addressing these weaknesses would significantly strengthen the paper. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong experimental results demonstrating impressive accuracy and energy efficiency.
*   Practical relevance and potential impact on the development of energy-efficient embedded systems.
*   Clear presentation of the method and its implementation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Overstated claims of novelty and insufficient acknowledgment of related work (particularly [14, 15]).
*   Potential for misleading comparisons to existing state-of-the-art results.
*   Limited discussion of the impact of approximations made in the algorithm.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the identified weaknesses by:

1.  Explicitly acknowledging the relationship between their approach and the work in [14, 15], clearly articulating the specific differences and contributions.
2.  Revising the claims of novelty in the introduction and discussion sections to accurately reflect the state-of-the-art.
3.  Providing a more rigorous and fair comparison to the results reported in [15], clarifying the differences in methodology and evaluation.
4.  Providing a more in-depth discussion of the approximation made in Equation 4 and its potential impact on accuracy.
