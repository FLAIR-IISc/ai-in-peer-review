The authors present an engaging paper proposing a backpropagation-based training scheme that models spikes and discrete synapses as probabilities while honoring the constraints of a specific neuromorphic platform with spiking neurons and low-precision synapses. This direction is timely and aligns with ongoing efforts to port deep networks to multi-neuron neuromorphic hardware.

Quality: The proposed training approach is practically useful, particularly given emerging neuromorphic platforms that impose constraints on connectivity, precision, and neuron dynamics.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and readable. However, the claims at the end of Section 1 could be phrased more carefully; as written, they can be interpreted (if read quickly) as asserting a first demonstration of training with spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses. I would not frame the TrueNorth (TN) deployment itself as a novelty claim; rather, it serves as a valuable validation of the method. In Section 2, please clarify what “0.15 bits per synapse” concretely means in practice. The network topology is described across multiple sections; consolidating the complete topology description in one place would improve readability. How much additional training time results from the probabilistic synaptic connection updates? Why is the single-ensemble accuracy of the 30-core network relatively low, especially compared to the 5-core network? Why do different ensemble sizes for the 5-core network yield similar performance? What spike rates are used for inputs during testing—i.e., is the input encoded as spike trains, and if so, with what rates or time windows? Also, Reference 12 incorporates constraints during training (e.g., bias = 0), so it is not a purely train-then-constrain approach; please correct the phrasing there (and note the duplicated word “approach approach”).

Originality: Please discuss or compare with other spiking backprop-like methods such as SpikeProp, clarifying similarities and differences in assumptions, objectives, and applicability to constrained hardware.

Significance: Methods that integrate hardware constraints into training are of substantial interest. That said, many of the constraints used here are specific to the TN architecture and may not directly transfer to other platforms without modification. Additionally, achieving the top-line accuracy with a 64-member ensemble implies a larger hardware footprint to meet target performance. Overall, this is an interesting contribution that leverages backpropagation while explicitly accounting for the constraints of spiking neuromorphic hardware with discrete synapses.
