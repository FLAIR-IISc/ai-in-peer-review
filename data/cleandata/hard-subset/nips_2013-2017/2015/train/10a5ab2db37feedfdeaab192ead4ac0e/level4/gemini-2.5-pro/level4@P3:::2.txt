This paper presents a novel and practical method for training spiking neural networks with binary synapses and constrained connectivity, making them suitable for deployment on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware. The core contribution is a technique that reconciles the continuous-valued nature of the backpropagation algorithm with the discrete, spiking nature of hardware like the TrueNorth chip. The authors' central idea is to treat continuous values in the training network—such as neuron activations and synaptic weights—as probabilities. A modified backpropagation algorithm is then used to train these probabilities offline. For deployment, the trained probabilistic network is sampled one or more times to generate concrete instances of a spiking network that can be directly mapped to the hardware.

This work effectively bridges the gap between two powerful but seemingly incompatible paradigms: the high-performance deep learning models trained with backpropagation and the ultra-low-power computation offered by neuromorphic platforms. The authors correctly position their work relative to previous efforts that have tackled subsets of this problem, such as training spiking neurons with high-precision weights [9-12] or training binary networks with full connectivity [14, 15]. The novelty of this paper lies in its unified approach that simultaneously addresses the constraints of spiking neurons, low-precision binary synapses, and limited connectivity, all within a single training framework.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Elegance:** The proposed probabilistic framework is a highly original and elegant solution to a significant and challenging problem in the field. By framing the learning problem in terms of probabilities, the authors create a differentiable space amenable to gradient-based optimization while maintaining a direct and principled mapping to the discrete target hardware. This "constrain-then-train" philosophy is a powerful conceptual contribution.

2.  **Significant and Impressive Results:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of its method with strong empirical results on the MNIST benchmark. Achieving 99.42% accuracy (with a 64-member ensemble) is state-of-the-art for spiking networks on this task and validates the high performance of the approach. Simultaneously, the demonstration of a single network achieving 92.7% accuracy while consuming only 0.268 μJ per image on the TrueNorth chip is a compelling testament to the method's ability to produce extremely energy-efficient solutions. These results clearly advance the state of the art in deploying high-accuracy networks on neuromorphic hardware.

3.  **High Quality and Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically structured. The methodology is explained in a step-by-step manner, from the hardware constraints to the probabilistic training network and the final mapping procedure. The theoretical underpinnings are sound, and the experimental validation is thorough and convincing.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Limited Discussion of Broader Significance:** While the results are impressive, the paper could be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of its broader significance and applicability beyond the MNIST dataset. It is not entirely clear how this approach would scale to more complex, real-world problems (e.g., ImageNet-scale classification) or what specific applications would most benefit from the demonstrated energy/accuracy trade-off. A more detailed discussion on these points would help contextualize the importance of the contribution.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison with Alternative Methods:** The paper cites alternative approaches, such as those in references [9-12], but would benefit from a more direct comparison. It is not immediately clear what the precise advantages of this probabilistic "constrain-then-train" method are compared to "train-then-constrain" approaches, where a full-precision network is trained first and then discretized and converted to a spiking model. A qualitative discussion of the trade-offs would help readers better understand the unique benefits of the proposed method.

3.  **Missing Details for Reproducibility:** While the paper is generally clear, it lacks certain key implementation details that would be necessary for another researcher to reproduce the work. Providing these details would substantially improve the quality and impact of the manuscript.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

The work is strong, and the following suggestions are intended to help improve the final manuscript:

*   **Input Encoding:** Could you please elaborate on how the input data (MNIST pixels) is converted into the input spike probabilities (x̃i)? The paper states that data is converted "using P(xi = 1) ≡ x̃i", but it is not specified how the continuous-valued x̃i is derived from the integer pixel values. Is it a simple linear mapping of pixel intensity to the [0, 1] range?
*   **Neuron Model Details:** The paper mentions that deployment network dynamics are implemented on TrueNorth by setting specific neuron parameters (leak, threshold, etc.). Could you provide more explicit detail on the neuron model and its parameterization? This would make the work more accessible to those not deeply familiar with the TrueNorth architecture.
*   **Synapse Strength Mapping:** The network initialization involves setting synaptic strength values (sij) to either +1 or -1. However, on TrueNorth, these strengths are implemented via a small set of axon types and per-neuron lookup tables. Could you clarify how the binary {-1, 1} strengths are mapped onto this hardware substrate?
*   **Related Hardware Platforms:** In your review of neuromorphic hardware, the discussion could be made more comprehensive by including other relevant platforms. For example, you may wish to consider citing the work of Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) on a mixed-signal neuromorphic system and the recent work on a ReRAM-based neurosynaptic core (http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427).

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, well-executed, and significant contribution to the fields of machine learning and neuromorphic engineering. The proposed method provides a crucial link between deep learning algorithms and energy-efficient hardware. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed by clarifying certain details and expanding the discussion. I strongly recommend acceptance.
