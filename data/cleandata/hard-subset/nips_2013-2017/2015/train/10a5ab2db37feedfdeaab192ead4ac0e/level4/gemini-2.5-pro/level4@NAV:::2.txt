This paper presents a novel method for bridging the gap between the high-precision, continuous weights required by backpropagation-based training and the binary synaptic states characteristic of many spike-based neuromorphic hardware platforms. The central idea is to interpret the continuous-valued weights learned during off-line training as probabilities, which are then stochastically sampled to determine the binary synaptic connections on the target spiking hardware for deployment. The manuscript details the necessary adaptations to the backpropagation algorithm to accommodate this probabilistic framework and demonstrates the method's effectiveness on the MNIST dataset. Furthermore, the authors successfully map the resulting network onto two distinct hardware configurations, one optimized for network size and the other for accuracy, and report performance metrics directly measured from the neuromorphic chip.

The manuscript is of exceptional quality and is written with remarkable clarity. The proposed work is highly original, and its principles could readily be extended to other methodologies and hardware systems. As a minor point, the review of existing hardware platforms could be strengthened by including references to Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the work detailed in arXiv:1506.05427.

A primary weakness of the paper is its failure to adequately articulate the broader significance of the research. Specifically, the authors do not clarify how this method facilitates the deployment of the TrueNorth chip in real-world scenarios or identify the specific application domains that would most benefit from this approach. The manuscript also lacks a comparative analysis that would establish the advantages of this technique over alternative methods, such as those mentioned in references [9-12].

In summary, the authors introduce a viable technique for training spiking neural networks with binary synapses using backpropagation, providing a compelling validation by deploying a trained network on the TrueNorth hardware. The paper is well-organized, concise, and presents persuasive results. However, the work would be strengthened by the inclusion of crucial implementation details. For instance, the manuscript should specify the method used for converting inputs to spike trains, the spike frequencies employed, the values of shared weights, and the specific neuron models implemented on the hardware.
