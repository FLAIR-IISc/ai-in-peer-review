The authors propose a method to bridge the gap between the high-precision continuous variables required for backpropagation and the binary synaptic constraints of spike-based neural network devices. Their approach frames analog variables as probabilities, sampled to map a conventionally trained neural network to a spiking network implemented on hardware. They demonstrate how to adapt the backpropagation algorithm to accommodate this mapping strategy and validate it on the MNIST benchmark. The trained network is then deployed onto two distinct architectures, optimized for both size and accuracy, with corresponding performance metrics reported from the chip. The manuscript is of very high quality and exceptionally clear. This work is original and potentially valuable for extending to other approaches and hardware platforms. When discussing relevant hardware, the authors should also consider the work of Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the paper available at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. 

However, the significance of this work is not sufficiently emphasized. It remains unclear how this method facilitates the practical deployment of the TrueNorth chip, and in what specific application domains it would be most effective. Furthermore, the advantages of this particular method over existing alternatives, such as those referenced in [9]–[12], are not clearly delineated.

The authors present a method for applying backpropagation to spiking neural networks with binary synapses and successfully demonstrate its viability by mapping a trained network onto the TrueNorth device. The paper is well-structured, concise, and clearly written. The results are convincing, but lack sufficient detail – for instance, concerning the input encoding scheme (how inputs are converted into spikes and the frequencies used), the shared weight values, and the specific neuron models employed.
