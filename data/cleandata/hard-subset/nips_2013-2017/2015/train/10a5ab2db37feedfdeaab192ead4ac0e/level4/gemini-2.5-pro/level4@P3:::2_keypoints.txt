- Novel method for training spiking neural networks with binary synapses and constrained connectivity for neuromorphic hardware deployment.  
- Core technique: treat activations and weights as probabilities, train with a modified backprop offline, then sample trained probabilities to produce deployable spiking networks.  
- Effectively bridges high-performance backprop-trained models and ultra-low-power neuromorphic platforms.  
- Unified approach simultaneously addresses spiking neurons, low-precision binary synapses, and limited connectivity within one training framework.  
- Originality and elegance: probabilistic “constrain-then-train” formulation creates a differentiable space mapping principledly to discrete hardware.  
- Strong empirical results: 99.42% MNIST (64-member ensemble) and 92.7% single network while consuming 0.268 μJ/image on TrueNorth.  
- High manuscript quality: clear, well-structured presentation, sound theoretical backing, and thorough experimental validation.  
- Limited discussion of broader significance and applicability beyond MNIST, and unclear scalability to larger real-world tasks (e.g., ImageNet).  
- Insufficient direct comparison with alternative approaches (e.g., train-then-constrain) and lack of qualitative trade-off discussion.  
- Missing implementation details that hinder reproducibility.  
- Input encoding ambiguity: unclear how integer MNIST pixel values are mapped to spike probabilities x̃i.  
- Neuron model details on TrueNorth (leak, threshold, parameterization) need more explicit description.  
- Clarify how binary synapse strengths ({-1, +1}) are mapped onto TrueNorth’s axon types and per-neuron lookup tables.  
- Suggest expanding discussion of other neuromorphic platforms and related work (e.g., Qiao et al., ReRAM-based cores).  
- Overall verdict: excellent, significant contribution with minor fixable issues; strong recommendation for acceptance.
