- Tackles the important problem of bridging backpropagation success with neuromorphic energy efficiency.  
- Proposes training with probabilistic spike representations and discrete synapses so standard backprop can target neuromorphic hardware.  
- Strength: constrains networks during training to match hardware limits rather than as post-processing.  
- Experimental support: MNIST results and real-time TrueNorth demonstration validate effectiveness.  
- Writing is generally clear and well-presented.  
- Claiming to be the “first” is misleading; novelty should be framed as simultaneously handling spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity.  
- Explanation of the “0.15 bits per synapse” requires clarification.  
- Network topology description should be consolidated and accompanied by a clear connectivity diagram.  
- Need to discuss how probabilistic synaptic updates affect training time and specify input spike rates during testing (is input a spike train?).  
- Minor typo noted (“approach approach” on line 399).  
- Originality: combining constraints and direct hardware mapping is novel, but must compare explicitly to prior spiking backprop methods (e.g., SpikeProp).  
- Significance: constraint-aware training is broadly relevant beyond TrueNorth, but reliance on large ensembles (e.g., 64 members) raises practical hardware-resource concerns.  
- Recommend discussing trade-offs between accuracy, energy efficiency, and ensemble size.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses a critical problem, presents a novel constraint-aware method, achieves strong MNIST/TrueNorth results, and is clearly presented.  
- Cons for acceptance: novelty claims need precision, several experimental aspects lack explanation (ensemble performance, input spike rates), large ensemble practicality is questionable, and comparisons to other spiking backprop algorithms are missing.
