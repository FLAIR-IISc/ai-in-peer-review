This paper presents a methodology for training spiking neural networks for deployment on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware using a modified backpropagation algorithm. The central contribution is a technique to bridge the incompatibility between backpropagation's requirement for continuous values and the discrete, spiking nature of hardware like the IBM TrueNorth chip. The authors' approach is to treat spikes and binary synapses as continuous probabilities during training, allowing for gradient-based optimization, and then sampling from these learned probabilities to generate one or more network instances for hardware deployment. This work is situated within the highly relevant area of mapping high-performance deep networks onto power-constrained computing substrates.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of co-designing training algorithms and network architectures for constrained neuromorphic hardware.
*   The proposed "constrain-then-train" methodology is well-motivated. By incorporating hardware constraints directly into the training process, it ensures that improvements in training error correspond directly to improvements on the target hardware. This can be more effective than post-hoc quantization or conversion methods.
*   The experimental results on the TrueNorth chip serve as a strong validation of the approach. The reported performance, achieving 99.42% accuracy on MNIST with a 64-member ensemble and 92.7% accuracy at only 0.268 µJ per image with a single network, effectively demonstrates a compelling trade-off between high performance and extreme energy efficiency.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is generally well-written, but several areas could be improved for clarity, context, and completeness.

1.  **Framing of Contributions:** The novelty claims at the end of Section 1 could be rephrased for greater precision. The current wording could be misread as suggesting this is the first work to train spiking networks or networks with low-precision synapses via backpropagation. It would be clearer to state that the novelty lies in the specific, unified framework that successfully combines spiking neurons, extremely low-precision binary synapses, and hardware-constrained connectivity. Additionally, while the demonstration on TrueNorth is a powerful result, it serves primarily as a validation of the training method rather than being a core algorithmic contribution itself.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Details:**
    *   In Section 2, could the authors please clarify the "0.15 bits per synapse" metric for the lookup table? An explanation of how this value is derived would be helpful.
    *   The description of the network topology is spread across Sections 4 and 5. The paper's clarity would benefit from a consolidated and more detailed description in a single location.
    *   Could you please comment on the computational cost of this training method? Information on how the training time compares to a standard backpropagation approach would be valuable context.
    *   For reproducibility, please provide more details on the input encoding. What are the spike rates for the inputs during testing, and is the input encoded as a rate or a single spike per pixel within the 1ms time step?

3.  **Analysis of Results:** The results presented in Figure 3C raise a couple of questions:
    *   Could the authors provide an intuition for why the single-member 30-core network performs significantly worse than the single-member 5-core network?
    *   Similarly, why does the performance of the 5-core network appear to plateau almost immediately, with different ensemble sizes yielding very similar accuracy?

4.  **Comparison to Related Work:**
    *   The paper would be strengthened by a discussion that contrasts the proposed probabilistic method with other backpropagation-inspired training rules for spiking networks, such as SpikeProp and its variants.
    *   The characterization of prior work as purely "train-then-constrain" could be more nuanced. For instance, Ref [12] also incorporates hardware-specific constraints (e.g., bias=0) during training. (Note: There is a minor typo on line 399: "approach approach").

5.  **Generality and Limitations:**
    *   The training constraints are closely matched to the TrueNorth architecture. A discussion on the generality of the method and the steps required to adapt it to other neuromorphic platforms with different constraints would significantly broaden the paper's impact.
    *   Achieving the highest accuracy requires a 64-member ensemble, which translates to a 64-fold increase in hardware resource utilization for inference. This is a critical trade-off between accuracy and cost that should be discussed more prominently as a limitation of the current application.
