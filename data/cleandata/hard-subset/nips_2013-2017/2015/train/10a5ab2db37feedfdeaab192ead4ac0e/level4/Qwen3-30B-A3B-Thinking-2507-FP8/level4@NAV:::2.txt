

The authors present a novel approach to bridge the precision requirements of continuous-variable backpropagation with the constraints of spike-based neural network hardware featuring binary synapses. They propose treating analog variables as probabilities, which are then sampled to transition neural networks trained offline to spiking network hardware for deployment. The work details an adapted backpropagation algorithm compatible with this strategy, validated on the MNIST benchmark. The authors map the trained network onto two distinct hardware architectures optimized for size and accuracy, reporting measured performance metrics from the physical chip. The manuscript is of high quality, exceptionally clear, and demonstrates significant originality with potential applicability across other hardware platforms. However, the significance of the work is underemphasized: it remains unclear how this method specifically enables practical TrueNorth chip deployment or identifies concrete application domains. Additionally, the advantages over alternatives (e.g., referenced in [9]–[12]) are not sufficiently justified. While the paper is well-structured and the results are convincing, critical implementation details are missing, including input-to-spike conversion methods, spike frequencies, shared weight values, and neuron model specifications. The authors should also cite Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the arXiv:1506.05427 paper when discussing hardware platforms.
