- Paper proposes training networks for neuromorphic hardware by treating synapses and neuron outputs as probabilities so standard backprop can train models that are sampled into low-precision spiking ensembles.  
- Demonstrated on TrueNorth: 99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ/image (ensemble of 64) and 92.7% at 0.268 μJ/image (single sample).  
- Authors cite related binary/neuromorphic work but fail to properly acknowledge the most relevant prior art.  
- Key missing references: Soudry et al. (2014) "Expectation Backpropagation" and Zhao et al. (1996), which use conceptually identical probabilistic training for discrete weights.  
- Strength — Significance: strong empirical results showing excellent energy-efficiency and high MNIST accuracy compared to low-power approaches.  
- Strength — Quality: sound experimental methodology with ablation studies and careful TrueNorth energy measurements.  
- Strength — Clarity: well-organized paper with clear descriptions of training, topology, and hardware deployment.  
- Weakness — Originality: novelty claims are misleading because the core probabilistic/ensemble approach mirrors prior work (Soudry 2014, Zhao 1996).  
- Weakness — Algorithmic overlap: the Gaussian approximation for neuron firing probability and ensemble sampling are essentially identical to Soudry et al.'s approach.  
- Weakness — State-of-the-art comparison: claimed superiority is misleading because comparisons ignore that prior work (Soudry et al.) achieved competitive results without data augmentation.  
- Recommendation: revise to explicitly acknowledge Soudry et al. (2014) and Zhao et al. (1996), correct overstated novelty claims, and clarify that the contribution is a practical application of existing probabilistic backpropagation to neuromorphic hardware.  
- Pro/Con acceptance summary: Pro — impressive experimental implementation and energy-efficiency; Con — misleading novelty claims and insufficient citation/comparison to prior work.
