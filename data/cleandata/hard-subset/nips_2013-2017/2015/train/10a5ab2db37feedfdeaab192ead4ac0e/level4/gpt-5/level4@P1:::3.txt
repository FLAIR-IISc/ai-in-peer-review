Summary
This paper introduces a backpropagation-based training method that treats spikes and discrete synapses as probabilities in [0, 1], enabling training with standard gradient descent while respecting neuromorphic hardware constraints (spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, limited fan-in). The trained network is mapped to hardware by sampling synaptic on/off states and spike events, and ensemble averaging across multiple sampled networks can improve accuracy. The method is demonstrated on IBM’s TrueNorth (TN) chip with a sparsely connected, feedforward topology designed to fit TN’s core-to-core connectivity. A Gaussian approximation is used to compute neuron firing probabilities during the forward pass; gradients are derived under assumptions of balanced positive/negative synaptic strengths and sufficiently large fan-in. A weight-initialization scheme minimizes pairwise redundancy via balanced ±1 synaptic strengths arranged to reduce inner products between columns. On MNIST, the authors report 99.42% accuracy (64-member ensemble; 108 μJ/image) and 92.7% accuracy (single member; 0.268 μJ/image), both at 1 kHz throughput. This work connects to prior lines of research that either trained spiking neurons with high-precision weights [9–12] or trained binary/trinary synapses with continuous neurons [13–15], and to “train-then-constrain” conversions of ANNs to SNNs [11–12].

Quality
- The approach is well-motivated and technically sound at a high level: constraining the training domain to match hardware and learning synapse probabilities and biases is a practical way to bridge backprop with discrete spiking substrates.
- The derivations rely on a Gaussian approximation of weighted Bernoulli inputs and on dropping a higher-order term in the gradient. While plausible under the stated assumptions (balanced weights; large fan-in), the paper would benefit from clarifying the conditions under which these approximations hold and any sensitivity analyses (e.g., with smaller fan-in or unbalanced inputs).
- Experimental results are compelling, especially the energy/accuracy trade-offs and real-time operation on TN. Running on specific hardware is an important validation of deployability, though I view it as validation rather than a distinct conceptual contribution.

Clarity
- The paper is generally easy to read. However, please temper the end-of-Section 1 novelty claims. As written, they can be misread to imply the first training method with spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses. The core novelty is the specific constrain-then-train probabilistic backprop under TN-like constraints (spikes, very low precision, constrained connectivity), plus the ensemble mapping.
- I would not present running on TN itself as a novel contribution; it is a strong validation of the training approach.
- Section 2: Please clarify the statement “0.15 bits per synapse.” Is this an amortized cost per synapse from the axon-type lookup-table scheme? Explain the calculation and assumptions.
- The network topology is described across multiple sections/figures. A single consolidated subsection summarizing the full topology (window sizes, strides, feature selection, pyramid connectivity, neuron counts per core) would improve readability.
- Typo: “approach approach” in Section 6.
- Ref. 12 includes constraints during training (e.g., bias=0), so the comparison should note it is not purely “train-then-constrain.”

Originality
- The idea of learning synapse probabilities and biases with a probabilistic forward model that matches spiking, binary-weight hardware is interesting and, to my knowledge, novel under the combined constraints used here (very low synaptic precision and constrained fan-in/fan-out).
- Please discuss and compare to spiking backprop-type rules such as SpikeProp and related methods, clarifying similarities/differences (continuous-time vs discrete-time, surrogate gradients, treatment of discontinuities, precision of weights, and connectivity constraints).

Significance
- Hardware-aware training is timely and important as neuromorphic platforms mature. The reported energy efficiency and accuracy on MNIST are strong, and the method is likely to be of interest to researchers attempting to deploy learned models on spiking hardware.
- A limitation is the platform-specific nature of some constraints (axon-type LUTs, fan-in, neuron model). Please expand on generalization to other neuromorphic systems (e.g., SpiNNaker, Neurogrid) and which elements of the method are portable versus TN-specific.
- The improvement from ensembles comes at the cost of additional hardware resources. A discussion of resource, energy, and latency trade-offs versus ensemble size would help practitioners.

Questions for the authors
- What exactly is meant by “0.15 bits per synapse” in Section 2? Please detail the amortization and assumptions.
- Training time: How much longer (wall-clock or iteration time) is training due to the probabilistic synaptic connection updates and Gaussian computations compared to a standard ANN of similar size?
- Network topology: Please provide a single, self-contained description of the full topology and parameters (R, F, S per layer; feature selection policy; neuron counts; per-core mapping).
- Why is the single-member accuracy for the 30-core network relatively low, especially compared to the 5-core network? What factors (e.g., overfitting, variance from sampling, class-vote averaging) explain this behavior?
- Why do different ensemble sizes for the 5-core network yield similar performance? Is this due to limited model capacity or low variance across samples?
- What are the input spike rates during testing? Is each input a spike train or a single-timestep Bernoulli sample per input line? Please specify encoding, time window, and any rate normalization.
- Please discuss how your method relates to SpikeProp and other surrogate-gradient approaches for SNNs.

Pros
- Clear, practical method to align backprop with spiking, low-precision, constrained-connectivity hardware.
- Strong energy/accuracy results; real-time deployment on TN.
- Network initialization strategy to reduce redundancy; training integrates standard deep-learning heuristics (dropout, momentum, augmentation).
- Ensemble sampling maps naturally from the probabilistic training domain.

Cons
- Some novelty claims need to be tempered; deployment on a specific chip is validation rather than a core contribution.
- Clarity gaps: 0.15 bits explanation, consolidated topology description, and precise test-time encoding.
- Limited discussion of training overhead and of generalizability beyond TN.
- Missing comparison to SpikeProp-style methods.

Overall, this is an interesting and timely paper. With clarifications, a more careful positioning of novelty relative to prior spiking backprop methods, and additional details on complexity and encoding, it would make a solid contribution.
