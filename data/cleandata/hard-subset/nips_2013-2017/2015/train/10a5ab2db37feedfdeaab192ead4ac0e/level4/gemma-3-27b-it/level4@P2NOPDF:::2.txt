This paper presents a compelling approach to bridging the gap between the continuous-valued precision required for training neural networks via backpropagation and the binary synaptic constraints of spiking neural network hardware, specifically targeting deployment on neuromorphic chips. The authors propose a novel method of interpreting analog variables as probabilities, enabling a mapping from a conventionally trained network to a spiking network implementation. They demonstrate a modified backpropagation algorithm compatible with this probabilistic mapping and provide validation through experiments on the MNIST dataset. The work is then successfully implemented on the TrueNorth platform, showcasing performance results for two distinct network architectures optimized for size and accuracy.

The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clear, demonstrating a high level of technical proficiency. The core idea is original and holds potential for broader applicability across various hardware platforms and approaches to neuromorphic computing. When discussing existing hardware platforms, the authors should consider including the work by Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the paper available at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. 

However, the paper would benefit from a stronger articulation of its significance. While the technical details are sound, the practical implications of this method for deploying the TrueNorth chip, and the specific application areas where it offers advantages, are not sufficiently emphasized. A clearer discussion of how this approach compares to, and potentially surpasses, alternative methods – such as those referenced in [9]-[12] – would significantly strengthen the paper. It is currently unclear whether the proposed method offers a substantial advantage over existing alternatives.

The experimental results are convincing, successfully demonstrating the feasibility of the approach on the TrueNorth hardware. However, the presentation of these results lacks sufficient detail. Providing more information regarding key implementation choices would increase the reproducibility and impact of the work. Specifically, details concerning the input encoding scheme (how inputs are converted into spikes), the spiking frequencies employed, the handling of shared weight values, and the specific neuron models used would be valuable additions. 

Overall, this is a strong contribution with a promising approach. Addressing the concerns regarding the broader significance and providing more granular experimental details would elevate the paper further.
