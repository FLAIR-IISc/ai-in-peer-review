

The authors present a novel approach to bridge the gap between high-precision backpropagation requirements and the constraints of spike-based neural network hardware with binary synapses. They propose treating continuous variables as probabilities, which are then sampled during deployment to map an offline-trained network onto a spiking neural network chip. The core contribution is an adapted backpropagation algorithm compatible with this probabilistic sampling strategy, validated on the MNIST benchmark. The authors successfully map the trained network onto the TrueNorth chip, optimizing for both size and accuracy, and report empirical results from hardware deployment. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clearly structured, and demonstrates significant technical merit. The work is highly original and holds potential applicability beyond the specific hardware context, suggesting broader relevance for neuromorphic computing research.  

However, the paper would benefit from several key revisions. First, the significance of the work is inadequately contextualized. The authors do not sufficiently articulate how this method enables practical deployment of the TrueNorth chip in real-world applications or specify target use cases (e.g., edge computing, low-power sensors). Without this, the contribution’s real-world impact remains ambiguous. Second, the paper fails to compare the proposed method against alternative approaches, particularly those cited in references [9]–[12]. It is unclear whether the probabilistic sampling strategy offers tangible advantages—such as improved energy efficiency, reduced hardware overhead, or higher accuracy—over existing techniques for binary synaptic networks. A comparative analysis addressing these trade-offs would strengthen the technical contribution.  

Additionally, critical implementation details are omitted, limiting reproducibility and technical assessment. The review lacks specifics on:  
- Input spike encoding mechanisms (e.g., rate coding, temporal coding) and their parameters,  
- Spike frequency ranges used during inference,  
- How shared weight values are managed across spiking neurons,  
- The exact neuron model (e.g., leaky integrate-and-fire) and its parameters.  
These omissions prevent a thorough evaluation of the method’s feasibility and performance characteristics.  

The authors should also acknowledge relevant prior work. Specifically, they should reference Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the arXiv preprint (arXiv:1506.05427), which discuss complementary hardware platforms and methodologies for spike-based neural networks. Omitting these undermines the paper’s positioning within the broader neuromorphic computing landscape.  

While the results are promising and the methodology sound, the current presentation does not fully justify the work’s novelty or practical value. The authors should explicitly address:  
1. *Application-specific relevance*: Which real-world tasks (e.g., image classification, sensor fusion) would benefit most from this approach, and why?  
2. *Comparative advantages*: How does the method outperform or complement existing binary synaptic techniques (e.g., in terms of accuracy, latency, or energy)?  
3. *Implementation transparency*: Provide sufficient detail to enable replication of the spike encoding, weight sharing, and hardware mapping steps.  

The paper’s core idea—probabilistic sampling for analog-to-spiking conversion—is conceptually elegant and well-executed in the MNIST validation. With the suggested revisions, it would make a strong contribution to the field. As currently written, however, the lack of clear significance, comparative analysis, and technical detail slightly diminishes its impact. The authors are encouraged to address these points to elevate the work from a solid technical demonstration to a compelling case for practical neuromorphic deployment. The manuscript’s high quality and originality make these revisions feasible and worthwhile.
