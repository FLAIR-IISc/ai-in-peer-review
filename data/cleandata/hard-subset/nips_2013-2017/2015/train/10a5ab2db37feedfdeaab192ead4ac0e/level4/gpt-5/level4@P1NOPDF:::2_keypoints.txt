- Proposes mapping continuous backprop variables to binary-synapse spiking hardware by interpreting analog values as probabilities and sampling them.  
- Adapts backpropagation for this probabilistic mapping and validates the approach on MNIST.  
- Demonstrates deployment on IBM TrueNorth with two configurations (size-optimized and accuracy-optimized) and reports hardware-measured performance.  
- Manuscript is high quality, clearly written, original, and appears transferable to other methods and hardware.  
- Fails to clearly articulate the practical significance and which real application domains would benefit from this method.  
- Does not establish comparative advantages over alternative strategies (refs [9]–[12]); needs conceptual and, if possible, empirical comparisons.  
- Missing critical implementation detail: how inputs are converted to spikes (encoding scheme and temporal windowing).  
- Missing detail on spike rates/frequency ranges used during inference and any hardware-imposed constraints.  
- Missing detail on on-chip neuron models and any deviations from the models assumed during training.  
- Missing detail on shared-weight values and schemes and how weight sharing interacts with probabilistic mapping.  
- Should cite Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015) and arXiv:1506.05427 when discussing related neuromorphic hardware.  
- Overall: end-to-end mapping to a commercial-scale neuromorphic device is a strong contribution; addressing the above issues would substantially strengthen the paper.
