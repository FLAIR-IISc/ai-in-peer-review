This paper presents a compelling approach to bridging the gap between the continuous-valued precision required for training neural networks via backpropagation and the binary synaptic constraints of spiking neural network hardware, specifically targeting deployment on the TrueNorth chip. The authors introduce a probabilistic mapping strategy, framing analog variables as probabilities from which samples are drawn to translate a trained, conventional neural network onto the spiking hardware. They demonstrate a modified backpropagation algorithm compatible with this mapping and validate their method on the MNIST dataset, showcasing performance on two distinct network architectures optimized for size and accuracy. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clear, representing a significant and original contribution to the field. This work has the potential to be broadly applicable beyond the specific hardware platform investigated.

The core strength of this work lies in its elegant solution to a critical challenge in neuromorphic engineering: efficient deployment of trained networks onto hardware with limited synaptic precision. The probabilistic mapping offers a practical route for translating established training methodologies to spiking systems. The experimental validation on TrueNorth is particularly valuable, demonstrating the feasibility of the approach with real-world hardware. 

However, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of its significance and potential impact. While the technical details are sound, the authors do not fully articulate *why* this method is crucial for unlocking practical applications of the TrueNorth chip and similar technologies. Expanding on the specific applications where this approach offers a substantial advantage would greatly strengthen the paper. 

Furthermore, while the related work section is reasonably comprehensive, it could be enriched by including recent contributions such as Qiao et al., *Frontiers in Neuroscience*, 2015, and the work presented in http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. A clearer comparison of this method's advantages and disadvantages relative to alternative techniques, including those cited in [9]–[12], would further solidify its contribution. The extent to which this method outperforms existing solutions needs to be explicitly addressed.

Finally, while the results are convincing, the paper lacks some crucial implementation details necessary for reproducibility and deeper understanding. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on: 

* **Input Encoding:** How are continuous-valued inputs converted into spike trains? What encoding scheme (e.g., rate coding, temporal coding) is employed?
* **Spiking Parameters:** What spike frequencies are used, and how are they determined?
* **Weight Sharing:** Detailed explanation of how shared weight values are handled in the spiking network.
* **Neuron Model:** A clear description of the neuron model used in the TrueNorth implementation (e.g., integrate-and-fire, leaky integrate-and-fire) and its specific parameters. 

Providing these details would enhance the paper's clarity and allow other researchers to build upon this valuable work. 

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-executed contribution. Addressing the concerns regarding the discussion of significance, comparative analysis with existing methods, and inclusion of implementation details would elevate the paper further. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
