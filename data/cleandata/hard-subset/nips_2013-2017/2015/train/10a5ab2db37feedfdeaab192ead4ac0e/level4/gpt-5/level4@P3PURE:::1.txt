1) At a minimum, the authors should acknowledge that the algorithm in Section 3.2 is very similar to the cited references [14] J. Zhao, J. Shawe-Taylor, and M. van Daalen, "Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks," Neural Networks, vol. 9, no. 6, pp. 991–998, 1996, and [15] Soudry, Daniel, Itay Hubara, and Ron Meir. "Expectation backpropagation: Parameter-free training of multilayer neural networks with continuous or discrete weights." Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems, 2014. In its final form, the algorithm is essentially a hybrid of these two approaches, derived using identical approximations.

2) The first paragraph of the Discussion gives the impression that the "constrain-then-train" methodology (learning a weight distribution and then sampling from it to produce a fixed network ensemble at test time) is novel. However, this approach was already suggested and empirically tested in [15].

3) Novelty claim (i) in the last paragraph of the Introduction is also misleading. The "expectation backpropagation" algorithm in [15] is a backpropagation update rule (using expectation propagation), implemented for binary neurons (i.e., with a sign activation function, in which zero input is never reached exactly) with binary weights, and it can easily accommodate constrained connectivity.

4) The cited previous state-of-the-art results are incorrect. To the best of my knowledge, the strongest prior results for binary weights (and neurons) were achieved by [15], which was tested on MNIST (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf) without data augmentation, as in this paper.

Edited after authors' feedback: Thank you for agreeing to address these concerns. This paper presents strong and novel experimental results in terms of accuracy and power consumption. However, both the general methodology and the algorithm itself are not as novel as claimed in the paper.
