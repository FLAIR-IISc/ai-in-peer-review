The authors propose a method to reconcile the high-precision continuous variables required by backpropagation with the binary synapses used in spiking neuromorphic hardware. They treat the analog variables as probabilities and sample them to transfer an offline-trained network to the spiking chip used at deployment. They adapt the backpropagation algorithm to support this probabilistic mapping and validate the approach on the MNIST benchmark. The trained network is mapped onto two alternative architectures—one optimized for size and one for accuracy—and on-chip performance measurements are reported. The manuscript is of very high quality and clarity. The contribution is original and potentially useful for other approaches and hardware platforms. When surveying existing hardware, the authors should also cite Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015, and the paper at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427.

The significance of the work is not sufficiently emphasized. It remains unclear how this method will facilitate deploying the TrueNorth chip in practical settings and for which specific applications. It is also not clear whether, and to what extent, this approach offers advantages over alternative methods, such as those cited in [9]–[12].

Overall, the paper presents a clear, well-structured method for applying backpropagation to spiking neural networks with binary synapses and demonstrates it by mapping a trained model onto the TrueNorth device. The results are convincing, but important experimental details are missing, including how inputs are converted into spikes, the firing rates or frequencies used, the shared weight values, and the neuron models.
