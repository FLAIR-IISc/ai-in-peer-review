- Introduces backprop-based training that models spikes and discrete synapses as probabilities in [0,1], enabling gradient descent under neuromorphic constraints.  
- Maps trained probabilistic networks to hardware by sampling synaptic on/off states and spike events; ensemble averaging improves accuracy.  
- Demonstrated on IBM TrueNorth with sparsely connected feedforward topology tailored to core-to-core connectivity.  
- Uses a Gaussian approximation to compute neuron firing probabilities and derives gradients assuming balanced weights and large fan-in.  
- Proposes a weight-initialization scheme that minimizes pairwise redundancy via balanced ±1 synaptic patterns.  
- Reports MNIST: 99.42% (64-member ensemble; 108 μJ/image) and 92.7% (single member; 0.268 μJ/image) at 1 kHz.  
- Method connects to prior work training spiking neurons with high-precision weights, binary/trinary synapse training, and train-then-constrain ANN→SNN conversions.  
- Approach is well-motivated and technically sound at a high level, practically bridging backprop and discrete spiking hardware.  
- Derivations rely on Gaussian and higher-order-term approximations; paper should clarify conditions and sensitivity (e.g., small fan-in, unbalanced inputs).  
- Experiments compelling for energy/accuracy and real-time TN operation, serving as important validation rather than a standalone conceptual advance.  
- End-of-Section 1 novelty claims should be tempered to avoid implying first-ever method for spiking neurons with reduced-precision synapses.  
- Running on TrueNorth is strong validation but not itself a novel contribution.  
- Clarify “0.15 bits per synapse” (amortization and assumptions) and consolidate network topology description into one subsection.  
- Typo detected: “approach approach” in Section 6.  
- Ref. 12 includes training constraints (e.g., bias=0); comparison should acknowledge it’s not purely “train-then-constrain.”  
- Learning synapse probabilities under combined TN-like constraints is an interesting and (to authors’ knowledge) novel contribution.  
- Request discussion/comparison with SpikeProp and surrogate-gradient spiking backprop methods (differences in time domain, surrogate gradients, weight precision, connectivity).  
- Hardware-aware training is timely and significant; energy efficiency and MNIST accuracy are strong and relevant to deployment.  
- Some method elements are platform-specific; expand on generalization to other neuromorphic systems and which parts are portable.  
- Ensemble gains require extra hardware; discuss resource, energy, and latency trade-offs versus ensemble size.  
- Questions for authors: clarify “0.15 bits per synapse” calculation and assumptions.  
- Questions: report training time overhead vs standard ANN (wall-clock or iterations) due to probabilistic updates and Gaussian computations.  
- Questions: provide a single, self-contained description of full topology and per-core mapping (R, F, S per layer; feature selection; neuron counts).  
- Questions: explain why single-member accuracy is lower for 30-core vs 5-core network and factors causing this behavior.  
- Questions: why do different ensemble sizes for 5-core network yield similar performance—limited capacity or low sample variance?  
- Questions: specify input spike encoding during testing (spike rates, single-timestep Bernoulli vs spike trains, time window, rate normalization).  
- Questions: discuss relation to SpikeProp and other surrogate-gradient SNN approaches.  
- Pros: clear practical method aligning backprop with spiking, low-precision, constrained hardware; strong energy/accuracy results and TN deployment; good initialization strategy and integration of training heuristics; ensemble mapping is natural from probabilistic training.  
- Cons: novelty claims should be moderated; clarity gaps on 0.15 bits, topology, and test encoding; limited discussion of training overhead and generalizability beyond TN; missing comparisons to SpikeProp-style methods.  
- Overall: interesting and timely paper that, with clarifications, better positioning vs prior spiking backprop work, and more details on complexity/encoding, would make a solid contribution.
