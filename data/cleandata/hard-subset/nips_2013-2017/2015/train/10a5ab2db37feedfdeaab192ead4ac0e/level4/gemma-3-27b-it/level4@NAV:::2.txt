This paper addresses a key challenge in deploying trained neural networks onto spike-based hardware: bridging the gap between the continuous-valued precision required for backpropagation training and the binary synaptic constraints of devices like the TrueNorth chip. The authors propose a novel probabilistic mapping approach, treating analog backpropagation variables as probabilities from which spikes are sampled, effectively translating a trained network to the spiking domain. They demonstrate the adaptation of backpropagation for this strategy and validate it using the MNIST dataset, successfully mapping the resulting network to two different TrueNorth architectures, and providing on-chip performance results. 

The manuscript is of excellent quality and clearly presented. This work is original and holds potential for broader applicability across different approaches and hardware platforms. When discussing existing hardware, the authors should consider including the work of Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the paper available at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. 

However, the paper would benefit from a stronger articulation of the work's significance. The potential impact of this method on the practical deployment of chips like TrueNorth, and in specific application domains, remains unclear. Further discussion is needed to delineate the advantages of this approach compared to existing alternatives, including those referenced in [9-12]. While the results are convincing, greater detail regarding implementation specifics would be valuable – for example, details regarding input encoding (spike conversion, frequencies), weight sharing, and the neuron models employed.
