This paper presents strong and novel experimental results, particularly in terms of the accuracy and power consumption achieved on neuromorphic hardware. However, the claims regarding the novelty of the general methodology and the specific algorithm appear to be overstated. While the hardware demonstration is impressive, the contribution should be more carefully situated within the context of existing literature.

Here are the primary concerns regarding the paper's claims:

1.  **Similarity to Prior Algorithmic Work:** The algorithm presented in Section 3.2 is highly similar to methods in the cited references [14] (Zhao et al., 1996) and [15] (Soudry et al., 2014). In its final form, the proposed algorithm appears to be a hybrid of these two previous works and seems to be derived using identical approximations. This strong similarity should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed to clarify the paper's incremental contribution.

2.  **Novelty of the "Constrain-then-Train" Methodology:** The discussion section presents the "constrain-then-train" methodology as a novel contribution. However, this approach—learning a weight distribution and then sampling from it to create a network ensemble for testing—was previously proposed and demonstrated in reference [15]. The paper's contribution should be framed more clearly in the context of this prior work.

3.  **Misleading Novelty Claim on Training Method:** Novelty claim (i) in the introduction is misleading. Reference [15] already introduced the "Expectation Backpropagation" algorithm, a backpropagation-based update rule for networks with binary neurons (e.g., using a sign activation function) and binary weights. This existing algorithm can also be straightforwardly applied to networks with constrained connectivity, which calls into question the novelty claimed in this paper.

4.  **Inaccurate Comparison to the State-of-the-Art:** The paper's claims regarding state-of-the-art performance for networks with binary synapses appear to be inaccurate. To my knowledge, the previous best result for binary-weight networks on MNIST was achieved by the algorithm in reference [15] (see arXiv:1503.03562v3). Notably, that result was achieved *without* the data augmentation techniques (rotation, shifting, etc.) used in this work. The authors should correct their comparison to the state-of-the-art and acknowledge that the use of data augmentation may account for a significant portion of the performance improvement.
