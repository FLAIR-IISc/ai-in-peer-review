- Introduces a novel "constrain-then-train" method that trains continuous probabilities to map onto discrete, spiking, low-precision neuromorphic hardware.  
- Treats neuron outputs and synaptic weights as probabilities during backpropagation, then samples them to create hardware network instances.  
- Enables ensembles of sampled networks on-chip to trade off accuracy versus energy efficiency.  
- Demonstrates strong MNIST results on TrueNorth: 99.42% accuracy (high-performance) and 0.268 μJ/image (high-efficiency).  
- Bridges deep learning (backprop) and neuromorphic computing by jointly handling spiking neurons, binary synapses, and hardware connectivity constraints.  
- Original and technically strong: principled probabilistic training and thoughtful synaptic initialization to reduce neuron redundancy.  
- Extremely clear and well-written presentation, with a helpful overview figure and strong experimental exposition.  
- Significant impact potential for low-power, embedded deep learning and broad applicability to neuromorphic platforms.  
- Evaluation limited to MNIST; paper lacks discussion on scaling to harder datasets (CIFAR/ImageNet) and real-world tasks.  
- Missing direct empirical comparison to "train-then-constrain" or conventional quantization/binarization approaches to establish advantages.  
- Some experimental details are absent, hindering reproducibility (input spike conversion, final synaptic strength values, TrueNorth neuron parameters).  
- Literature survey of related hardware is incomplete; should cite additional large-scale neuromorphic systems (e.g., Qiao et al., Neurostream).  
- Reviewer recommendation: accept—strengths outweigh weaknesses—request authors add implementation details and expand scalability discussion.  
- Main caution against acceptance: limited evaluation scope and lack of direct baselines make practical advantages on complex tasks uncertain.
