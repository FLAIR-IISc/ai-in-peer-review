The authors present an interesting paper that introduces a backpropagation training method utilizing spike probabilities, while considering the hardware constraints of a specific platform featuring spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This topic is particularly relevant in the context of mapping deep networks to multi-neuron hardware platforms.

Quality: The proposed training method holds value, especially when considering new multi-neuron hardware platforms with constraints.

Clarity: The paper is generally easy to read. However, the claims made at the end of Section 1 could be rephrased to avoid suggesting that the paper proposes a novel training methodology employing spiking neurons and synapses with reduced precision for the first time. Instead, it would be more accurate to focus on the method's consideration of hardware constraints. Additionally, demonstrating the network's functionality on a specific hardware platform, in this case, the TrueNorth (TN) chip, should be viewed as a validation of the training method rather than a novel feature.

Several clarifications are needed in Section 2. Specifically, the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" is unclear. A concise description of the network topology is also lacking. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to know the extent to which the training time is increased due to the probabilistic synaptic connection update. The results from a single ensemble of the 30-core network are surprisingly low, especially when compared to the 5-core network. In contrast, the results from different ensembles of the 5-core network show little variation. The spike rates for the inputs during testing should also be provided, along with information on whether the input is a spike train. Lastly, Reference 12 on line 399 mentions constraints (e.g., bias=0) during training, indicating that it is not a straightforward train-then-constrain approach (note: there is a typo on this line, "approach approach").

Originality: A discussion or comparison with other spiking backprop-type rules, such as SpikeProp, would be a valuable addition to the paper.

Significance: The development of new training methods that account for hardware platform constraints is of great interest. However, the constraints considered in this paper are specific to the TN architecture and may not be universally applicable. If the results are based on a 64-ensemble configuration, it implies that more hardware resources are required to achieve the desired network accuracy. Overall, the paper presents an interesting training method using backpropagation that takes into account the hardware constraints of a particular platform with spiking neurons and discrete synapses.
