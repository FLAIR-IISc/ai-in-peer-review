This paper presents interesting experimental results, demonstrating strong performance in terms of accuracy and power consumption. However, the claims of novelty regarding both the general methodology and the specific algorithm require revision.

Specifically, Section 3.2 describes an algorithm with substantial similarities to existing work. The authors should acknowledge the close relationship to the approaches presented in [14] (Zhao et al., 1996) and [15] (Soudry, Hubara, & Meir, 2014), noting that their implementation represents a hybrid of these two algorithms derived using comparable approximations.

Furthermore, the discussion section initially presents the “constrain-then-train” methodology – learning a weight distribution followed by sampling to generate a fixed network ensemble for testing – as a novel contribution. This framing is inaccurate, as a similar approach was previously proposed and evaluated in [15]. 

The claim of novelty (i) in the introduction’s final paragraph is also misleading. The “expectation backpropagation” algorithm [15] is a backpropagation update rule based on expectation propagation, specifically designed for binary networks employing binary weights and a sign activation function (where an input of zero is rarely, if ever, exactly achieved). This algorithm is readily adaptable to constrained connectivity scenarios, undermining the assertion of novelty.

Finally, the paper’s assessment of prior state-of-the-art results appears inaccurate. To the best of my knowledge, the highest reported performance for binary weights and neurons was achieved using the algorithm detailed in [15]. Importantly, this performance was demonstrated on the MNIST dataset (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf) *without* data augmentation, a technique employed in the current work. A fairer comparison would involve replicating the [15] results without data augmentation to provide a more accurate benchmark.
