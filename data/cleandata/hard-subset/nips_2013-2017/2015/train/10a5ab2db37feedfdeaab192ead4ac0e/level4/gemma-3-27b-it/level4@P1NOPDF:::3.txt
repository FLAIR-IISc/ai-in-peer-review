The authors present an interesting paper on a backpropagation training method using spike probabilities, specifically designed to account for the hardware constraints of spiking neuron and discrete synapse platforms. This is a timely topic, given the increasing interest in mapping deep networks onto specialized multi-neuron hardware.

**Quality:** The proposed training method is particularly valuable given the emergence of new multi-neuron hardware platforms with inherent constraints. 

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, the claims made at the end of Section 1 could be rephrased. As currently written, they might be misinterpreted to suggest that this paper introduces, for the first time, a training methodology employing spiking neurons and synapses with reduced precision. I would also categorize the demonstration of running this network on the TrueNorth (TN) platform as a validation of the training method, rather than a novel feature in itself.

In Section 2, the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" requires clarification. The network topology is not described comprehensively in a single location, making it difficult to quickly grasp the network’s structure. Furthermore, the paper doesn’t explain how much the probabilistic synaptic connection update increases training time. Why is the performance from a single ensemble of the 30-core network so low, particularly when compared to the 5-core network? Conversely, why are the results consistent across different ensembles of the 5-core network? Finally, what are the spike rates of the inputs during testing? Are the inputs presented as spike trains? Reference 12 (line 399) mentions constraints (e.g., bias=0) applied *during* training, so it's not simply a "train-then-constrain" approach (there’s a typographical error on this line: "approach approach").

**Originality:** 

Could the authors provide a discussion comparing their work to other spiking backpropagation-type rules, such as SpikeProp? This would help to contextualize the novelty of their approach.

**Significance:** The development of new training methods that consider hardware constraints is highly significant. However, the constraints considered in this paper are specific to the TN architecture, and may not be universally applicable to other platforms. Additionally, the reliance on a 64-ensemble suggests that substantial hardware resources are required to achieve the desired network accuracy. 

In summary, this is an interesting paper describing a backpropagation training method that considers the hardware constraints of a spiking neural network platform with discrete synapses.
