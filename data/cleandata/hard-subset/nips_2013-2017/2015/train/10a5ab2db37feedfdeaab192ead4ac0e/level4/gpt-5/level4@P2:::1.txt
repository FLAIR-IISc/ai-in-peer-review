Summary
This paper proposes a training method that reconciles backpropagation with spiking neuromorphic hardware featuring binary synapses and constrained connectivity (TrueNorth). The key idea is to represent spikes and binary synapses as continuous probabilities in [0,1], enabling standard backpropagation in a differentiable surrogate space. After training, the network is mapped to hardware by sampling synapses and spikes from these probabilities; multiple sampled networks can be averaged as an ensemble. The authors also introduce a topology compatible with TrueNorth’s core-to-core constraints (sliding windows with limited fan-in) and a weight-initialization scheme that balances excitatory/inhibitory strengths to reduce redundancy. On MNIST, they report 99.42% with a 64-member ensemble (108 μJ per image) and 92.7% with a single member (0.268 μJ per image), both running in real time on TrueNorth.

Relation to prior work
The probabilistic backpropagation approach in Section 3.2 is closely related to prior work on stochastic/binary networks, notably Zhao et al. [14] (stochastic bit-stream neural networks) and Expectation Backpropagation (EBP) [15], which trains networks with binary or low-precision parameters by optimizing over distributions and sampling at test time. The specific approximations (e.g., Gaussian approximations to weighted Bernoulli sums and gradient propagation through expectations) and the train-on-probabilities/sample-at-test strategy are highly similar to these references. Prior work has also considered spiking networks trained via backprop or conversion with high-precision weights [9–12] and discrete-weight training with continuous activations [13].

Strengths
- Strong experimental results: Excellent accuracy–energy trade-offs on real hardware, including a compelling 0.268 μJ/classification regime and a high-accuracy ensemble reaching 99.42% on MNIST.
- Real-time deployment: The method runs on TrueNorth with a carefully designed topology honoring constrained connectivity and low-precision synapses.
- Engineering contributions: The core-level initialization (balanced s-values and minimized column inner products) is well-motivated and likely useful for neuromorphic systems with severe precision limits.
- Clear description of the hardware mapping, ensemble formation, and training heuristics (dropout, data augmentation, learning-rate schedule).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Novelty claims: The paper implies that the “constrain-then-train” methodology and the probabilistic backpropagation rule are novel. However, EBP [15] explicitly trains distributions over discrete parameters and samples at test time (and reported MNIST results), and stochastic bit-stream networks [14] use closely related approximations. Please revise the introduction and discussion to accurately credit this prior art and position your contributions as: (i) adapting a probabilistic backprop framework to TrueNorth’s specific constraints (binary synapses with shared strength blocks, spiking output, and limited fan-in), (ii) the topology/initialization tailored to TrueNorth, and (iii) the hardware demonstration and energy measurements.
- Claim (i) in the introduction: As written, it suggests a first demonstration of backprop for spiking neurons with sub-trinary synapse precision and constrained connectivity. EBP [15] is a backprop-like update derived via expectation propagation that handles binary neurons/weights and can be used with constrained connectivity. Please clarify the differentiators (e.g., TrueNorth-compatible strength sharing, specific gradient approximations, and the end-to-end hardware mapping).
- Prior SOTA comparison: The stated “previous best” for binary weights/neurons appears to overlook EBP’s MNIST results [15]. Ensure comparisons use consistent settings (e.g., no data augmentation when comparing to [15]) and correct the SOTA attributions.
- Algorithmic clarity: Section 3.2 would benefit from explicit acknowledgment that the Gaussian/independence approximations and gradient-through-expectations are similar in spirit to [14,15]. Clearly state where your derivation deviates (e.g., dropping σ^−3 terms in Eq. 4, block-quantized strength handling, bias constraints).
- Fairness of comparisons: Since ensembles and augmentation substantially boost performance, include ablations: (a) single-member vs ensemble; (b) with vs without augmentation; (c) energy–accuracy scaling as ensemble size grows. Where possible, contrast with EBP under matched conditions.
- Reporting details: Clarify energy accounting (active vs leakage, per-core normalization) and provide accuracy at equal time budgets (e.g., single 1 ms tick) to aid reproducibility.

Evaluation
- Quality: The experimental section is strong, with rigorous hardware measurements and competitive accuracy. The theoretical approximations are standard and plausible for large fan-in; still, a brief validation of the Gaussian approximation’s accuracy (e.g., empirical vs predicted firing probabilities) would strengthen the paper.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well organized. It should better delineate prior methodological overlap, carefully qualify novelty claims, and expand the algorithmic connections to [14,15].
- Originality: Methodological originality is incremental relative to [14,15]. The main novelty lies in adapting probabilistic backprop to TrueNorth’s constraints, the weight initialization/topology tailored to its architecture, and the scale/efficiency of the hardware demo.
- Significance: High. Demonstrating state-of-the-art energy efficiency and strong accuracy on actual neuromorphic hardware is valuable to both the ML and neuromorphic communities.

Pros
- Excellent energy/accuracy trade-offs on real hardware
- Practical topology and initialization for constrained neuromorphic systems
- Clear path to scalable, low-power deployment

Cons
- Overstated novelty; close overlap with [14,15]
- Incomplete/possibly inaccurate SOTA comparisons
- Limited ablations on ensemble/augmentation contributions

After the authors’ feedback, I appreciate their willingness to revise claims and citations. With corrections to novelty statements, more precise SOTA comparisons (especially to [15]), and clearer attribution of the probabilistic training framework, this paper would make a strong contribution due to its compelling hardware results and practical deployment insights.
