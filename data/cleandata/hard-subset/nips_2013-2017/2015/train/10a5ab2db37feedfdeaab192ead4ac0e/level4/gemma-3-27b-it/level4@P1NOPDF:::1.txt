1) The authors should at least acknowledge the strong similarity between the algorithm described in section 3.2 and the approaches presented in references [14] (J. Zhao, J. Shawe-Taylor, and M. van Daalen, "Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks," *Neural Networks*, vol. 9, no. 6, pp. 991 - 998, 1996) and [15] (Soudry, Daniel, Itay Hubara, and Ron Meir. "Expectation backpropagation: Parameter-free training of multilayer neural networks with continuous or discrete weights." *Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems*. 2014). The current method represents a hybrid of these two algorithms and relies on identical approximations.

2) The first paragraph of the discussion section gives the impression that the “constrain-then-train” methodology – learning a weight distribution and then sampling from it to create a fixed network ensemble at test time – is a novel contribution. However, this approach was previously suggested and tested in reference [15].

3) The novelty claim (i) in the last paragraph of the introduction is also misleading. The “expectation backpropagation” algorithm [15] is a backpropagation update rule (using expectation propagation) implemented for binary neurons (with a sign activation function where an input of exactly zero is rarely, if ever, reached) and binary weights. It can readily accommodate constrained connectivity.

4) The cited previous state-of-the-art results appear to be inaccurate. To the best of my knowledge, the highest previously reported results for binary weights *and* neurons were achieved using the algorithm described in reference [15], which was evaluated on the MNIST dataset (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf) *without* data augmentation, a technique employed in the current paper.
