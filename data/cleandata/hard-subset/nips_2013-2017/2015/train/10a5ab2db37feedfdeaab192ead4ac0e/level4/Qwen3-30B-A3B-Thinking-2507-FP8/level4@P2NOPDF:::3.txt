

The authors present an interesting paper on a backpropagation training method that leverages spike probabilities while accounting for hardware constraints specific to a platform featuring spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This topic is highly relevant for mapping deep networks onto emerging multi-neuron hardware platforms.  

**Quality:** The proposed training method is valuable for developing deep networks tailored to constrained hardware architectures. However, the paper should clarify that the demonstration on the TN platform serves as validation of the training approach, not as a novel contribution in itself.  

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but Section 1’s concluding claims risk misinterpretation as asserting the first use of spiking neurons with reduced-precision synapses in training. This should be reworded to avoid ambiguity. Section 2 requires significant clarification:  
- The phrase “0.15 bits per synapse” lacks explanation; its technical meaning must be defined.  
- The network topology is described piecemeal, not consolidated in one location.  
- The increased training time due to probabilistic synaptic updates is not quantified.  
- The 30-core ensemble achieves notably lower accuracy than the 5-core ensemble (despite larger scale), yet this discrepancy is unexplained.  
- The near-identical results across 5-core ensembles require justification.  
- Input spike rates during testing and whether inputs are spike trains are unspecified.  
- Ref 12 (line 399) describes a constrained training approach (e.g., bias=0), not merely “train-then-constrain,” and contains a typo (“approach approach”).  

**Originality:** The paper would benefit from a comparison with established spiking backpropagation methods (e.g., SpikeProp) to contextualize its novelty. Without such a discussion, the contribution’s uniqueness remains unclear.  

**Significance:** While hardware-aware training methods are timely, the constraints considered are TN-specific and may not generalize to other platforms. Additionally, the reported results rely on a 64-ensemble setup, implying substantial hardware overhead to achieve target accuracy—a trade-off that should be explicitly addressed. The focus on TN constraints limits broader applicability, and the paper would strengthen its significance by discussing how these constraints might adapt to alternative architectures.  

Overall, the work addresses a timely problem, but key technical details and contextualization require refinement to ensure rigor and clarity. The core idea holds promise, but the current presentation necessitates revisions to resolve ambiguities and strengthen the contribution.
