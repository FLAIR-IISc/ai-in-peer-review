1) Section 3.2 should acknowledge the strong similarity between the presented algorithm and the approaches described in [14] J. Zhao, J. Shawe-Taylor, and M. van Daalen, "Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks," Neural Networks, vol. 9, no. 6, pp. 991 - 998, 1996 and [15] Soudry, Daniel, Itay Hubara, and Ron Meir. "Expectation backpropagation: Parameter-free training of multilayer neural networks with continuous or discrete weights." Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems. 2014. The current algorithm represents a hybrid of these two, derived using identical approximations, and this connection should be explicitly stated.
2) The discussion's opening paragraph currently implies that the “constrain-then-train” methodology—learning a weight distribution followed by sampling to create a fixed network ensemble for testing—is a novel contribution, despite being previously proposed and evaluated in [15].
3) The novelty claim (i) in the introduction’s final paragraph is also inaccurate. The "expectation backpropagation" algorithm [15] is a backpropagation update rule (employing expectation propagation) implemented for binary neurons (using a sign activation function that avoids exact zero inputs) with binary weights, and is readily adaptable to constrained connectivity.
4) The reported comparison to prior state-of-the-art results appears flawed. To my knowledge, the highest previously reported performance for binary weights and neurons was achieved by the algorithm in [15], which demonstrated results on MNIST (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf) without the data augmentation used in this paper.
