This paper proposes a method to reconcile the high-precision, continuous variables required by backpropagation with the binary synapses of spike-based neuromorphic hardware. The core idea is to treat the continuous-valued weights learned during off-line training as probabilities, which are then stochastically sampled to configure the binary synapses of the spiking network for deployment. The authors present a corresponding adaptation of the backpropagation algorithm and validate the approach on the MNIST benchmark. They demonstrate a successful mapping of the trained network onto two different hardware architectures, optimized for either size or accuracy, and report performance figures measured directly from the chip.

The manuscript is of very high quality, and the work is presented with excellent clarity. The proposed technique is original and could prove valuable for other training methods and hardware platforms.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed:

**Significance and Context:** The broader significance of the work is not sufficiently highlighted. It is unclear how this method facilitates the deployment of the TrueNorth chip in practical applications or in which specific domains it would be most useful. Furthermore, the paper does not clearly articulate the advantages of this specific method over alternative approaches, such as those cited in references [9-12].

**Missing Details:** While the results are convincing, the experimental section lacks crucial details needed for reproducibility. The authors should specify how inputs are converted into spikes, the spike frequencies used, the values of the shared weights, and the precise neuron models implemented on the chip.

**Missing Citations:** The review of available hardware platforms should be expanded to include relevant work, such as Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the paper available at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427.
