This paper presents a method for training spiking neural networks that can be deployed on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware, specifically the IBM TrueNorth chip. The core contribution is a training framework that reconciles the continuous nature of backpropagation with the discrete, spiking dynamics and low-precision synapses of neuromorphic systems. The authors achieve this by treating synaptic connections and neuron activations as probabilities during training, allowing for gradient-based optimization via a probabilistic version of the backpropagation algorithm. The trained probabilistic network is then mapped to the hardware by sampling from the learned distributions to create one or more deterministic network instances for an ensemble. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on the MNIST dataset, showcasing a compelling trade-off between accuracy and energy consumption. An ensemble of 64 networks achieves a state-of-the-art accuracy of 99.42% for spiking networks, while a single network achieves 92.7% accuracy with extremely low energy usage.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive and highly significant experimental results. The paper provides a compelling demonstration of a high-performance network trained with a backpropagation-based algorithm running in real-time on a physical neuromorphic platform. The reported energy efficiency (e.g., 0.268 µJ per classification) is remarkable and represents a significant step forward in making deep learning practical for low-power embedded applications. The "constrain-then-train" approach, which directly optimizes a network model compatible with the target hardware's constraints (spiking neurons, binary synapses, limited connectivity), is well-motivated and effectively bridges the gap between deep learning software and neuromorphic hardware. This work is likely to be impactful for both the neuromorphic engineering and machine learning communities.

**Weaknesses:**

While the experimental results are strong, the paper's claims regarding the novelty of the methodology and algorithm could be more carefully situated within the context of existing literature.

1.  **Relation to Prior Work:** The training algorithm presented in Section 3.2, which approximates the activation probability with a Gaussian CDF and derives a backpropagation rule, is conceptually very similar to previous work on training networks with discrete or stochastic components. In particular, it shares fundamental ideas with "Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks" (Zhao et al., 1996) and "Expectation Backpropagation" (Soudry et al., 2014). The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of how its proposed algorithm relates to, builds upon, or differs from these earlier probabilistic backpropagation methods.

2.  **Novelty of the "Constrain-then-Train" Paradigm:** The discussion frames the "constrain-then-train" approach as a key distinction from other methods. However, the idea of learning a probability distribution for weights and then sampling from it at test time was previously explored in Soudry et al. (2014), which also fits this paradigm. Acknowledging this precedent would help clarify that the main novelty here is the successful adaptation and application of this paradigm to a specific, highly constrained spiking hardware platform.

3.  **Clarity of Novelty Claims and Comparisons:** The novelty claim (i) in the introduction appears overstated. The Expectation Backpropagation algorithm, for instance, is a backpropagation method designed for networks with binary neurons and binary weights, and can be readily applied to architectures with constrained connectivity. Furthermore, the cited state-of-the-art results for networks with binary weights appear to be out of date. For example, more recent work (e.g., Cheng et al., arXiv:1503.03562, which builds on Soudry et al.) reports higher performance on MNIST for binary networks than the baseline cited in this paper. For a fair comparison, the authors should ensure they are comparing against the most relevant and current benchmarks.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Significance:** The paper reports outstanding experimental results, demonstrating a practical and effective pathway to deploying high-accuracy deep networks on extremely low-power neuromorphic hardware. This is a significant and timely contribution.
*   **Quality:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, providing a clear analysis of the accuracy-vs-energy trade-off on a real hardware system. The work is a complete piece of engineering and applied research.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the proposed method is clearly explained.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Originality:** The novelty of the core training algorithm and the overarching "constrain-then-train" methodology is overstated. The work bears a strong resemblance to previously published probabilistic backpropagation techniques, and this connection is not adequately discussed.
*   **Clarity of Contribution:** The paper could more accurately position its contribution not as a fundamentally new algorithm, but as a successful and important adaptation and large-scale application of existing principles to a challenging and relevant hardware target, which in itself is a valuable contribution.

In summary, this paper presents strong and important experimental results that will be of great interest to the community. However, its claims of algorithmic novelty should be tempered, and the work must be more carefully contextualized with respect to highly relevant prior research. I would recommend acceptance if the authors can address these issues of attribution and positioning in a revised version.
