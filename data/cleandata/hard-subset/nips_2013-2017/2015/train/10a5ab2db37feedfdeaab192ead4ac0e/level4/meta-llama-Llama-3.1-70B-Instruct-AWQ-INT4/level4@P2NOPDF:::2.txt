The authors propose a novel approach to reconcile the need for high precision in continuous variables used in backpropagation algorithms with the desire to utilize spike-based neural network devices featuring binary synapses. They introduce the concept of treating analog variables as probabilities, which are then sampled to map the neural network used offline for training onto the spiking network chip employed for application deployment. This strategy is adapted to the backpropagation algorithm, and the proposed method is validated using the MNIST benchmark.

The authors successfully map the trained network onto two distinct architectures, optimizing for both size and accuracy, and present performance figures measured directly from the chip. The manuscript's quality and clarity are exceptionally high, and the work demonstrates considerable originality, with potential applications in other approaches and hardware platforms.

However, in reviewing existing hardware platforms, the authors should consider mentioning Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the paper available at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. A notable omission is the lack of emphasis on the significance of the work and its potential impact on practical applications, particularly in relation to deploying the TrueNorth chip. Furthermore, it is unclear whether and to what extent this method offers advantages over alternative options, such as those cited in references [9] to [12].

The authors present a well-structured and clear method for applying backpropagation to spiking neural networks with binary synapses, successfully validating it by mapping the trained network onto the TrueNorth device. While the results are convincing, some details are lacking, including the conversion of inputs into spikes, the frequencies used, shared weight values, and neuron models. Providing these details would further enhance the paper's clarity and overall impact.
