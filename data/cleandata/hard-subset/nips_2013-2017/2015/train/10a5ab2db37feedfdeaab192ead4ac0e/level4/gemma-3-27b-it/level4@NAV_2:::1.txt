This paper addresses the critical challenge of bridging the gap between the high performance of backpropagation-trained deep neural networks and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing. The core idea – treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities to enable backpropagation – is a compelling approach. The authors demonstrate this by training a sparsely connected network for MNIST, achieving impressive results on the TrueNorth chip, showcasing both high accuracy (99.42% with an ensemble of 64) and remarkable energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image with an ensemble of 1).

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear motivation and introduction to the problem space. The description of the deployment hardware (TrueNorth) is concise and sufficient. The detailed explanation of the network training methodology, including the probabilistic representation and gradient calculations, is commendable. The network topology section clearly articulates the approach to constrained connectivity. The results are presented effectively, providing both accuracy and energy consumption figures, and comparisons to existing work. 

A key strength lies in the experimental validation on actual neuromorphic hardware. The performance figures are significant, representing a substantial advance in power efficiency for digit recognition. The “constrain-then-train” approach is a sensible strategy for reconciling the algorithmic and hardware constraints. The paper also demonstrates a good understanding of related work in both backpropagation and neuromorphic computing.

However, the paper's claims of novelty require refinement. The algorithm presented in section 3.2 bears a strong resemblance to previously published work, specifically [Zhao et al., 1996] and [Soudry et al., 2014], which utilize similar approximations for learning in networks with stochastic binary units. This connection should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed. The claim in the discussion that the “constrain-then-train” methodology is novel is also misleading, as it was already proposed and evaluated in [Soudry et al., 2014]. Additionally, the novelty claim (i) in the introduction regarding the demonstration of backpropagation for spiking neurons with constrained connectivity and low-precision synapses is overstated; [Soudry et al., 2014] presents an algorithm (expectation backpropagation) capable of similar feats, though with different constraints. Finally, the comparison to the state-of-the-art in the introduction appears inaccurate. The best published results for binary weights and neurons were, to the best of my knowledge, achieved by [Soudry et al., 2014] on MNIST, and these should be considered in the comparative analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong experimental results demonstrating state-of-the-art energy efficiency on a relevant hardware platform (TrueNorth).
*   Clear and well-motivated approach to bridging the gap between backpropagation and neuromorphic computing.
*   Detailed and technically sound description of the proposed method.
*   Significant contribution to the field of low-power neural network implementations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Overstated claims of novelty regarding the algorithm and methodology.
*   Insufficient acknowledgment of closely related prior work ([Zhao et al., 1996] and [Soudry et al., 2014]).
*   Inaccurate comparison to prior state-of-the-art results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a valuable contribution with strong experimental results. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more nuanced discussion of related work and a more accurate assessment of the novelty of its approach. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing these concerns in a revised version. Specifically, acknowledging the similarities to [Zhao et al., 1996] and [Soudry et al., 2014] and providing a more accurate comparison to the current state-of-the-art would be essential.
