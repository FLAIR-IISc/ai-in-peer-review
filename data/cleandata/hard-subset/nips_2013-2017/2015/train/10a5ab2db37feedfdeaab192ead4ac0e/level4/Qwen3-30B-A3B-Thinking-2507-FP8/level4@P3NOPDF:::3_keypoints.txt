- Contribution is timely and valuable: hardware-aware backprop for SNNs targeting TN architecture.  
- Overstated novelty: authors should not claim "first" training method for spiking neurons with reduced-precision synapses; novelty is hardware-aware adaptation.  
- "Novel hardware validation" wording is misleading—running on TN is validation, not a novel feature.  
- "0.15 bits per synapse" is unexplained and needs context (what it measures and relation to TN ternary constraints).  
- Network topology description is fragmented; include a single schematic or table for reproducibility.  
- Explain why 30-core ensemble (64 trials) yields lower accuracy (78.5%) than 5-core ensemble (82.1%), contrary to expectations.  
- Address why 5-core ensemble results are nearly identical across trials—low variance could indicate stable optimization or lack of ensemble diversity.  
- Report input spike rate and encoding/normalization used during testing.  
- Fix typos and clarify phrasing (e.g., "approach approach"; clarify "train-then-constrain" with bias=0).  
- Provide clearer explanation of "spike probabilities" and whether synaptic updates are probabilistic per spike or a hyperparameter.  
- Lack of comparison to prior spiking backprop methods (SpikeProp, BPTS); must discuss differences and novelty.  
- Show how probabilistic synaptic updates maintain accuracy with reduced precision and whether spike probabilities outperform deterministic methods.  
- Discuss generalizability limits: TN-specific constraints may not apply to other neuromorphic chips (Loihi, SpiNNaker).  
- Quantify hardware overhead of ensemble approach (e.g., 64x cores) and trade-offs between accuracy and resource cost.  
- Reconcile contradictory training-time/efficiency claims between 5-core and 30-core results (Table 1 vs. Section 3.2).  
- Recommendation: Major Revision—address technical gaps, clarify claims, add comparative context and experimental details to justify novelty.
