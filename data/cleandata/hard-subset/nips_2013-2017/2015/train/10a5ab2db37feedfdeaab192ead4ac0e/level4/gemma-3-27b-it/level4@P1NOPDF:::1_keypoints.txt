- Algorithm in Sec. 3.2 is strongly similar to methods in refs [14] and [15]; current method is essentially a hybrid using identical approximations.  
- The “constrain-then-train” approach (learn a weight distribution then sample a fixed test-time ensemble) is presented as novel but was previously proposed and tested in [15].  
- Novelty claim (i) in the introduction is misleading: expectation backpropagation [15] already provides a backprop update (via expectation propagation) for binary neurons and weights.  
- Expectation backpropagation can handle constrained connectivity and binary activations (sign), contrary to the paper’s novelty implications.  
- The paper’s citation of prior state-of-the-art is inaccurate: ref [15] reports the highest prior results for binary weights and neurons on MNIST without data augmentation.  
- Edited conclusion: experimental results (accuracy and power) are strong and novel, but the overarching methodology and algorithmic contributions are overstated and not as novel as claimed.
