

The authors propose a novel approach to bridge backpropagation-based training with neuromorphic hardware by treating synaptic connections and neuron outputs as continuous probabilities, enabling direct deployment on spiking neuromorphic chips like TrueNorth. This work extends prior efforts (e.g., [9]–[12], [14]–[15]) by integrating probabilistic backpropagation with constrained connectivity and ultra-low-precision synapses (1 bit per synapse), achieving state-of-the-art MNIST accuracy (99.42%) at unprecedented energy efficiency (108 μJ/image for ensemble of 64). The paper demonstrates a complete pipeline: training with continuous-valued probabilistic representations, mapping to discrete hardware via ensemble sampling, and validating on TrueNorth with detailed energy measurements.  

Strengths: The work is technically sound (Quality: high), with rigorous theoretical grounding (Gaussian approximation in Eq. 3–4), comprehensive experiments (energy-accuracy tradeoffs across network sizes), and clear implementation details. Clarity is excellent, with well-structured sections and intuitive figures. Originality is strong (differs significantly from NIPS 2015 work on binary synapses [15] and spiking backpropagation [12] by resolving the hardware-algorithm incompatibility end-to-end). Significance is partially supported by the energy efficiency gains (0.268 μJ/image vs. 4 μJ in [16]), but could be strengthened by contextualizing practical deployment scenarios (e.g., mobile vision systems).  

Weaknesses: The significance section lacks concrete application examples and comparative analysis against alternatives (e.g., how does this method outperform [9]–[12] in real-world settings?). Key implementation details are missing: spike conversion methodology (Section 3.3), temporal encoding frequencies, shared weight schemes, and neuron dynamics specifics (e.g., leak rates). The manuscript should cite Qiao et al. (Front. Neurosci. 2015) and arXiv:1506.05427 to acknowledge related hardware approaches.  

Pro: Complete solution for backpropagation-compatible neuromorphic deployment; state-of-the-art energy-accuracy tradeoff; clear probabilistic framework; thorough TrueNorth validation.  
Con: Insufficient discussion of real-world applicability; missing technical details on spike encoding and hardware mapping; limited comparison to alternative methods.  

Overall, this is a high-quality, original contribution with strong technical execution. With minor revisions to clarify significance and implementation details, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
