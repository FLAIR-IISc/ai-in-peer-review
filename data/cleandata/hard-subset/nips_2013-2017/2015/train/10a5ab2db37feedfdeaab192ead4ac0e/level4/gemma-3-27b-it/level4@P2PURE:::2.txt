The authors address the challenge of deploying backpropagation-trained continuous-variable neural networks on binary-synapse spiking neural network hardware. Their approach elegantly bridges this gap by interpreting analog variables as probabilities used for sampling, effectively mapping a traditionally trained network to a spiking network for implementation on dedicated chips. They demonstrate the adaptation of backpropagation to accommodate this probabilistic mapping and validate their method on the MNIST dataset. The trained network is successfully mapped onto two distinct architectures, optimized for size and accuracy, with performance results reported directly from hardware measurements. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clear. This work is original and potentially impactful, offering insights applicable to other hardware platforms. When discussing related hardware, the authors should also consider the work of Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the paper available at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. 

However, the paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of the work’s significance. The practical implications of this method for deploying the TrueNorth chip – specifically, in which applications it proves most beneficial – remain unclear. Furthermore, it is unclear how this specific approach compares to and advantages over alternative strategies for training spiking networks, such as those referenced in [9]–[12]. 

The authors present a method for applying backpropagation training to spiking neural networks with binary synapses and successfully demonstrate its feasibility by mapping the resulting network onto the TrueNorth device. The paper is well-structured, concise, and easy to follow. While the results are convincing, further detail regarding implementation specifics would be helpful – for example, details on input encoding (spike conversion and frequencies), shared weight values, and the employed neuron models.
