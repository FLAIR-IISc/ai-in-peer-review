

1) The authors should explicitly acknowledge that the algorithm in Section 3.2 is conceptually identical to the approaches in references [14] and [15], as it is derived using identical approximations and represents a hybrid of both methods. This similarity must be stated in the final version.

2) The first paragraph of the Discussion misrepresents the "constrain-then-train" methodology (learning a weight distribution and sampling from it to form a fixed network ensemble at test time) as novel, despite its prior presentation and empirical validation in reference [15].

3) The novelty claim (i) in the introduction's final paragraph is misleading. Reference [15] ("Expectation Backpropagation") presents a backpropagation update rule based on expectation propagation, designed for binary neurons (with a sign activation function, where zero input is never attained exactly) and binary weights, and inherently supports constrained connectivity.

4) The cited previous state-of-the-art results are incorrect. To the best of my knowledge, the highest reported accuracy for binary weights and neurons on MNIST (without data augmentation) was achieved by the algorithm in reference [15] (see arXiv:1503.03562v3), which this paper uses as a baseline without acknowledging this prior SOTA.

Thank you for addressing these concerns. While the paper presents strong experimental results regarding accuracy and power consumption, both the core methodology and the algorithm itself lack the novelty claimed in the manuscript.
