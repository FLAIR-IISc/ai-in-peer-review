Summary
The paper proposes a backpropagation-based training methodology for neuromorphic hardware with spiking neurons, low-precision (binary on/off) synapses, and constrained connectivity. The central idea is to interpret spikes and discrete synapses as probabilities in [0, 1], enabling standard gradient-based optimization on a differentiable surrogate model. The trained “probabilistic” network is then sampled to produce one or more spiking networks; multiple sampled networks are combined via ensemble averaging at inference. A hardware-aware initialization balances synaptic strengths to reduce redundancy and simplify optimization. The approach is demonstrated on IBM’s TrueNorth with MNIST, achieving 99.42% with a 64-member ensemble and 92.7% with a single network at very low energy per inference.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned between (i) conversion methods that train high-precision ANNs and post-hoc map to SNNs with thresholds/normalization [e.g., 11,12], and (ii) methods that directly train constrained networks (binary/trinary weights/neurons) with probabilistic surrogates [14,15]. Unlike SpikeProp-style temporal gradient methods, this work uses a probabilistic static neuron model with a Gaussian approximation to enable gradient flow through spiking and binary synapse constraints. The authors also emphasize constrained connectivity and per-core weight sharing specific to TrueNorth. Compared to prior spiking results, the reported accuracy/energy trade-off is strong, though gains at the high-accuracy end rely on large ensembles.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The probabilistic surrogate and Gaussian approximation are reasonable given the large fan-in (256) and balanced weights. The gradient approximation dropping the σ^3 term is plausible, but the paper would benefit from a quantitative check (e.g., ablations showing negligible difference when keeping/dropping that term).
- Empirics: Results on MNIST are solid and include an energy-accuracy trade-off across network sizes and ensemble counts. However, several methodological details and analyses are missing (see questions below) that would help assess robustness and generality.
- Hardware validity: The TrueNorth deployment is a meaningful validation. Energy measurements are reported fairly (including leakage amortized to active cores).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well written, but some claims and descriptions should be tightened:
- Novelty claims in Section 1 currently read as if using backprop to train spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses is first-of-its-kind. Given related work with high-precision spiking networks [9–12] and binary/trinary networks [14,15], please rephrase to emphasize the specific combination of spiking neurons, sub-trinary effective precision, constrained connectivity, and hardware-consistent training and initialization.
- Running on TrueNorth is best framed as an important validation rather than a novelty claim.
- Please consolidate the network topology description (currently spread across Sections 3 and 4) into a single, self-contained specification (R, F, S, number of cores/neurons per layer, fan-in per neuron, axon type usage, output pooling).
- Minor: fix the duplicated phrase “approach approach.”

Specific questions and requested clarifications
- “0.15 bits per synapse”: Please clarify the amortization calculation for the axon-type lookup tables (number of neurons sharing the table per core and how 0.15 b/syn arises).
- Training cost: How much additional training wall-clock time results from the probabilistic forward pass and gradient approximations relative to a comparable dense ANN? Please report epochs-to-convergence and compute resources.
- Gaussian approximation: Provide a sanity check that the CLT-based approximation holds (e.g., histogram of summed inputs with fitted Gaussian; failure modes at low fan-in).
- Gradient approximation: Show an ablation with/without the σ^3 term (Eq. 4) to justify dropping it.
- Initialization: Quantify the impact of the weight/sign-balancing initialization versus naive random initialization on convergence and final accuracy.
- Ensemble behavior:
  - Why is the single-network accuracy for the 30-core model substantially lower than the 5-core model’s ensembles? Is this due to higher variance in the sampled binary connectivity? Please include mean±std over multiple independent samplings for each ensemble size.
  - The 5-core ensembles yield similar accuracies across ensemble sizes; provide analysis (bias/variance decomposition, capacity limits).
- Input encoding: During testing you state a single 1 ms time step. Is the input a single Bernoulli spike per pixel per targeted input line (i.e., rate = x̃i over 1 ms) or a longer spike train? Please report effective spike rates at input and average firing rates per layer.
- Generality: To what extent do the constraints (axon types, blockwise weight sharing, 256 fan-in) limit portability to other neuromorphic platforms? What minimal assumptions are required?
- Prior art: Please discuss relation to SpikeProp-like methods and expectation backprop for binary networks (e.g., Soudry et al., arXiv:1503.03562), highlighting differences in neuron models, gradient estimators, and connectivity constraints.
- Train-then-constrain vs. constrain-then-train: Ref. 12 also enforces constraints during training (e.g., threshold/bias settings); please nuance the comparison.

Originality
The combination of a probabilistic backprop surrogate with explicit modeling of spiking neurons, extremely low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity, all co-designed with TrueNorth’s architectural constraints, is a notable contribution. The ensemble sampling and hardware-aware initialization add practical value. The novelty claim should be scoped carefully relative to prior binary/spiking training methods.

Significance
The reported accuracy/energy results are compelling and relevant to low-power embedded inference, and demonstrate that end-to-end training under hardware constraints can outperform post-hoc conversion in some regimes. The reliance on large ensembles at the highest accuracy raises practical deployment questions (area/core count), but the single-network result is very energy efficient. Extending beyond MNIST and demonstrating portability to other neuromorphic substrates would strengthen impact.

Pros
- Clear, hardware-aware training method bridging backprop and spiking, low-precision hardware.
- Strong energy-accuracy trade-offs with real-time TrueNorth deployment.
- Practical initialization and mapping procedure; simple, scalable training loop.

Cons
- Novelty claims need careful scoping relative to prior binary/spiking training.
- Limited empirical breadth (MNIST only); heavy reliance on ensembles for SOTA accuracy.
- Missing analyses: training cost, effect of approximations/initialization, ensemble variance, detailed topology in one place, and input encoding specifics.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper addresses a timely problem with a practical and effective method validated on real hardware. With clarified claims and additional analyses answering the questions above, it would make a solid NIPS contribution.
