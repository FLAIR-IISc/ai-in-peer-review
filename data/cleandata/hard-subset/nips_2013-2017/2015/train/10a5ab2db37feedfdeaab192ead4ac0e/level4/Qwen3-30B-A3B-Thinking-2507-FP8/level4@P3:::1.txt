

**Review**

This paper proposes a method for training neural networks that can be deployed on neuromorphic hardware with spiking neurons and low-precision synapses. The authors address the incompatibility between backpropagation (which requires continuous weights and outputs) and neuromorphic systems (which use discrete synapses and spiking neurons) by treating synaptic connections and neuron outputs as probabilities. This probabilistic approach allows standard backpropagation to train networks that can then be mapped to hardware by sampling the probabilities to create ensemble networks. The authors demonstrate their approach on the TrueNorth chip, achieving 99.42% accuracy on MNIST at 108 μJ per image (ensemble of 64) and 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image (ensemble of 1).

The work builds on previous efforts in neuromorphic computing and binary neural networks. The authors correctly cite several relevant papers, including [9-13] that explore subsets of the elements they combine. However, the paper fails to properly acknowledge the most relevant prior work: Soudry et al. (2014) introduced "Expectation Backpropagation" for binary neural networks with binary weights, which is conceptually identical to the authors' probabilistic approach. Similarly, Zhao et al. (1996) developed "Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks" that shares the same core idea of using probability distributions for discrete weights. The authors' method is essentially a hybrid of these approaches, but they do not clearly acknowledge this in their related work or novelty claims.

**Strengths:**
1. **Significance:** The paper demonstrates strong empirical results with excellent energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image) and high accuracy (92.7%) on MNIST, which is a significant contribution to the field of energy-efficient neural network deployment. The results show clear advantages over existing low-power approaches.
2. **Quality:** The experimental methodology is sound, with appropriate ablation studies and careful measurement of energy consumption on the TrueNorth chip. The training procedure, while not novel, is technically sound and well-executed.
3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the training methodology, network topology, and hardware deployment. The figures effectively illustrate the network architecture and results.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Originality:** The paper's novelty claims are misleading. The "constrain-then-train" methodology described in the Discussion section was already proposed in Soudry et al. (2014), which uses expectation propagation to train binary neural networks with ensemble sampling. The authors' algorithm (Section 3.2) is directly derived from the same probabilistic approximations as Soudry et al., but the paper fails to acknowledge this connection. Specifically, the Gaussian approximation for neuron firing probability (Equation 3) is identical to the approach in Soudry et al. (2014), and the ensemble sampling technique is explicitly described in that paper.
2. **Novelty Claim (i):** The claim that the paper is "the first time" to demonstrate backpropagation for networks with spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity is incorrect. Soudry et al. (2014) demonstrated binary weights with constrained connectivity, and Zhao et al. (1996) used similar probabilistic approaches for binary networks.
3. **State-of-the-Art Comparison:** The paper incorrectly claims that their results outperform previous work on spiking networks. The cited comparison with 99.12% accuracy (from [12]) is misleading because Soudry et al. (2014) already achieved 97.88% on MNIST with binary weights and neurons (without data augmentation), which the authors' paper also uses. The authors' 99.42% accuracy is achieved with data augmentation (as noted in their methodology), while Soudry et al. did not use data augmentation, making the comparison invalid.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a strong contribution in demonstrating the practical implementation of energy-efficient neural networks on neuromorphic hardware with impressive results. However, the novelty claims are overstated due to insufficient acknowledgment of prior work. The core algorithm is not novel, and the paper's comparison to prior work is misleading.

The authors should revise their paper to:
1. Clearly acknowledge Soudry et al. (2014) and Zhao et al. (1996) as the primary references for their probabilistic training approach
2. Correct the novelty claims, particularly regarding the "constrain-then-train" methodology and the state-of-the-art comparison
3. Clarify that their work represents an application of existing probabilistic backpropagation techniques to neuromorphic hardware rather than a novel algorithm

With these revisions, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating a practical implementation of probabilistic neural networks on neuromorphic hardware. As written, the paper's claims of novelty are not well-supported, which undermines the significance of their experimental results.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
- **Pro:** Strong experimental results with excellent energy efficiency, well-executed deployment on TrueNorth, clear presentation of methodology.
- **Con:** Misleading novelty claims, insufficient acknowledgment of prior work, invalid state-of-the-art comparison.
