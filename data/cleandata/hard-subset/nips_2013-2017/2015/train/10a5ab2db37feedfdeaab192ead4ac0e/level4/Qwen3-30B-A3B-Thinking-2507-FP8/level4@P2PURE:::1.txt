

1) The authors should explicitly state in Section 3.2 that the algorithm is fundamentally a hybrid of the approaches in references [14] and [15], derived using identical approximations. The text should clarify that this final form is deeply similar to both cited works: [14] J. Zhao, J. Shawe-Taylor, and M. van Daalen, "Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks," *Neural Networks*, vol. 9, no. 6, pp. 991–998, 1996, and [15] Soudry, Daniel, Itay Hubara, and Ron Meir. "Expectation backpropagation: Parameter-free training of multilayer neural networks with continuous or discrete weights." *Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems*, 2014.

2) The first paragraph of the Discussion incorrectly suggests the "constrain-then-train" methodology (learning a weight distribution and sampling it to form a fixed network ensemble at test time) is novel. This approach was explicitly proposed and validated in reference [15].

3) The novelty claim (i) in the final paragraph of the Introduction is misleading. Reference [15] describes an "expectation backpropagation" algorithm that is a backpropagation update rule (utilizing expectation propagation), implemented for binary neurons (with a sign activation function where zero inputs are never achieved exactly) and binary weights. This method inherently supports constrained connectivity, contradicting the paper's assertion of novelty for this aspect.

4) The cited previous state-of-the-art results are incorrect. To the best of my knowledge, the highest reported accuracy for binary weights and neurons was achieved by the algorithm in reference [15] on MNIST (without data augmentation, as tested in this paper and documented in http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf).
