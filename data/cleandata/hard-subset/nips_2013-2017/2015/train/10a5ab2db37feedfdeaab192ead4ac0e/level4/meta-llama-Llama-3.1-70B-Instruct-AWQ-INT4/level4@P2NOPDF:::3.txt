The authors present an interesting paper that proposes a backpropagation training method utilizing spike probabilities, while taking into account the hardware constraints of a specific platform featuring spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This topic is particularly relevant in the context of mapping deep networks to multi-neuron hardware platforms.

Quality: The proposed training method is useful, especially when considering new multi-neuron hardware platforms with constraints. Its ability to adapt to hardware limitations makes it a valuable contribution to the field.

Clarity: The paper is generally easy to read, but some sections require rewording for clarity. Specifically, the claims made at the end of Section 1 could be rephrased to avoid suggesting that the paper proposes a novel training methodology that employs spiking neurons and synapses with reduced precision for the first time. Instead, the focus should be on the method's adaptability to hardware constraints.

Additionally, the demonstration of running the network on a specific hardware platform, TN, should be viewed as a validation of the training method rather than a novel feature. 

Some points of clarification are needed in Section 2. The meaning of 0.15 bits per synapse is not explicitly stated. A clear and concise description of the network topology is also lacking. Furthermore, the authors should provide information on the increase in training time due to the probabilistic synaptic connection update. The results from a single ensemble of the 30 core network are surprisingly low, especially when compared to the 5 core network. The authors should also explain why the results of the different ensembles of the 5 core network are similar. The spike rates for the inputs during testing should be provided, and it should be clarified whether the input is a spike train.

A minor correction is needed on line 399, where Ref 12 is mentioned. The reference includes constraints (e.g., bias=0) during training, making it not solely a train-then-constrain approach.

Originality: The authors could strengthen their paper by providing a discussion or comparison with other spiking backprop-type rules, such as SpikeProp.

Significance: The development of new training methods that consider hardware platform constraints is of great interest. However, the constraints considered in this paper are specific to the TN architecture and may not be universally applicable. If the results are based on a 64 ensemble, it implies that more hardware resources are required to achieve the desired network accuracy. Nevertheless, the paper presents an interesting training method using backpropagation that takes into account the hardware constraints of a particular platform featuring spiking neurons and discrete synapses.
