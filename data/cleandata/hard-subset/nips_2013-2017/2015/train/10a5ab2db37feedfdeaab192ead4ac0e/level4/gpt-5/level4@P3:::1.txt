Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a training methodology that bridges backpropagation and spiking neuromorphic hardware with low-precision synapses. The key idea is to represent spikes and binary synapses as continuous probabilities in [0,1] during training, enabling the use of standard backpropagation. The forward pass approximates the summed input to a neuron as Gaussian, yielding a differentiable expression for the spike probability; gradients are computed with respect to synapse connection probabilities and biases, with an efficiency-motivated approximation that drops a variance-dependent term. Synaptic strengths are not learned; instead, a TrueNorth-compatible initialization assigns balanced ±1 weights arranged to reduce pairwise redundancy. After training, the network is mapped to hardware by sampling synaptic on/off states and input spikes; multiple independent samples can be ensembled by averaging outputs.

The network topology respects TrueNorth’s constrained core-to-core connectivity using sliding windows with progressively increasing receptive fields. On MNIST, a 30-core model with a 64-member ensemble achieves 99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ/classification; a 5-core single-member model achieves 92.7% at 0.268 μJ/classification, both at 1 kHz throughput.

The probabilistic backpropagation framework and the “constrain-then-train” philosophy are closely related to prior work on training networks with binary variables, notably Zhao et al. (1996) on stochastic bit-stream neural networks and Expectation Backpropagation (EBP; Soudry et al., NIPS 2014; Cheng et al., 2015). Like EBP, this paper trains in a continuous/probabilistic domain and samples discrete networks at test time; the Gaussian/independence approximations and ensemble sampling strategy are very similar in spirit. Compared to conversion-based spiking approaches (e.g., Cao et al., 2015; Diehl et al., 2015), the present work trains directly under deployment constraints and targets low-precision crossbars with limited fan-in on TrueNorth.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results: excellent energy–accuracy trade-offs and a real-time hardware demonstration on a large-scale neuromorphic chip. Reporting active plus amortized leakage power is useful for system-level comparisons.
- Clear mapping from a probabilistic training network to hardware; the method respects discrete synapses, spiking neurons, and constrained connectivity.
- Practical network topology and initialization tuned to the hardware (balanced ±1 strengths, windowed connectivity, progressive mixing).
- Thorough training recipe and system details (batching, dropout, data augmentation, ensemble size, throughput), aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions for improvement
- Overstated novelty: Section 3.2’s probabilistic backpropagation and the “constrain-then-train + sample/ensemble” strategy are closely related to Zhao et al. (1996) and especially EBP (Soudry et al., 2014; Cheng et al., 2015). Please explicitly acknowledge these connections, clarify what is new (e.g., the particular hardware-constrained topology and weight initialization for TrueNorth), and temper claims such as “for the first time.”
- Novelty claim (i) in the Introduction is misleading as written. EBP provides a backpropagation-based learning rule for binary neurons and binary/trinary weights and can, in principle, accommodate constrained connectivity. The contribution here is better characterized as adapting probabilistic backpropagation to the specific constraints of TrueNorth (spikes, 1-bit synapses plus axon-type lookup, limited fan-in) and demonstrating state-of-the-art energy-efficiency on hardware.
- Prior SOTA comparison: The paper should ensure a fair and precise comparison to EBP’s MNIST results with binary neurons/weights. Since this work uses data augmentation and large ensembles, please also report accuracy for single-sample networks without augmentation, and for matched settings (e.g., no augmentation, no ensembling), to enable apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Theoretical approximations: The Gaussian and independence assumptions and the gradient simplification (dropping the σ−3 term) are reasonable heuristics but should be empirically justified. An ablation showing the impact of the approximation on training stability/accuracy would strengthen the technical claims.
- Ablations and sensitivity: It would help to quantify the contribution of (a) the balanced ±1 initialization and column-orthogonalization heuristic, (b) receptive field size and stride, (c) ensemble size, and (d) data augmentation. Report variance across different random samplings and ensembles.
- Generality: Results are only on MNIST. While hardware constraints are platform-specific, a small-scale demonstration on a second dataset (e.g., Fashion-MNIST, simple audio features) or a more direct discussion of how the method extends to convolutional layers would improve the case for broader impact.
- Clarity/consistency: Reconcile energy numbers across sections (e.g., 0.268 μJ vs the 4 μJ figure cited elsewhere) and ensure consistent accuracy reporting across ensemble sizes. Minor typos include “backpropgation.”

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The method is technically sound within its approximations, and the engineering is solid. More rigorous validation of the approximations and additional ablations would improve confidence.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Please clarify novelty relative to EBP/Zhao and ensure fair baselines. Adding a brief algorithm box or pseudocode could help readers reproduce the training loop.
- Originality: The core probabilistic backpropagation and sampling/ensemble idea is not new; the distinctive aspects are the tailored initialization/topology and the end-to-end hardware realization on TrueNorth. Novelty should be framed accordingly.
- Significance: High. Demonstrating state-of-the-art energy-efficiency and real-time performance on neuromorphic hardware is impactful and relevant to the NIPS community. The practical pathway from training to hardware is valuable.

Pros
- Compelling hardware demo with excellent energy–accuracy trade-offs.
- Clear, practical mapping from training to deployment under tight hardware constraints.
- Detailed system description likely to be useful to practitioners.

Cons
- Novelty overstated; close parallels to EBP and earlier probabilistic treatments of binary networks are not fully acknowledged.
- Fairness of SOTA comparisons needs tightening (augmentation, ensembling).
- Limited empirical scope and few ablations on key design choices/approximations.

Recommendation
I lean toward acceptance based on the strength of the hardware results and practical relevance, provided the paper (1) clearly situates itself with respect to EBP and Zhao et al., (2) tempers novelty claims, and (3) strengthens empirical comparisons (reporting matched baselines and additional ablations). These changes would make the contribution both accurate in its claims and compelling in its demonstrated impact.
