This paper presents a novel training methodology for deploying neural networks on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware, specifically the IBM TrueNorth chip. The core contribution is a "constrain-then-train" approach that reconciles the continuous nature of backpropagation with the discrete, spiking dynamics of the target hardware. The authors treat synaptic connections and neuron activations as probabilities, allowing a network with hardware-compatible topology and constraints to be trained offline using a modified backpropagation algorithm. The trained probabilistic network is then mapped to one or more deterministic spiking networks for deployment by sampling from these probabilities. The method is validated on the MNIST dataset, demonstrating an impressive trade-off between high accuracy (99.42% with a 64-network ensemble) and high efficiency (92.7% at just 0.268 μJ per image with a single network).

**Strengths**

*   The paper addresses the significant and timely problem of bridging high-performance deep learning algorithms with energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware. This is a crucial step for deploying advanced neural networks in real-world, power-constrained embedded systems.
*   The proposed "constrain-then-train" methodology is well-motivated. By incorporating hardware constraints directly into the training process, the authors ensure that improvements in training error translate directly to improvements in deployment performance, which is a key advantage over common "train-then-constrain" approaches.
*   The experimental results on the TrueNorth chip are compelling, clearly demonstrating the method's ability to achieve either state-of-the-art accuracy or remarkable energy efficiency, depending on the desired operating point.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

The paper is well-written and the approach is promising, but several areas could be improved with further clarification and discussion.

1.  **Clarity of Contributions:** The claims of novelty at the end of Section 1 could be refined. While the combination of techniques is novel, the phrasing could be misinterpreted as claiming to be the first to train spiking networks or networks with low-precision synapses. It would be beneficial to more precisely state that the contribution is the *simultaneous* handling of spiking neurons, extremely low-precision binary synapses, and hardware-specific connectivity constraints within a backpropagation framework. Furthermore, while demonstrating the method on TrueNorth is a valuable validation, it is more of an experimental result than a standalone novel contribution.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** Several key details of the method and setup could be clarified:
    *   In Section 2, the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" for the lookup table is unclear and would benefit from a more detailed explanation.
    *   The description of the network topology is spread across the text. A consolidated description, perhaps with a clearer diagram or table in one place, would greatly improve the paper's readability and reproducibility.
    *   The input encoding scheme for the testing phase is not fully described. What were the input spike rates, and was the input presented as a spike train over time or a single pattern?
    *   The computational cost of training is an important practical consideration. Could the authors comment on how the training time of this probabilistic method compares to standard backpropagation?

3.  **Analysis of Results:** Some of the results in Figure 3C are counterintuitive and warrant further discussion:
    *   Why does the single-member ensemble of the larger 30-core network perform significantly worse than the single-member 5-core network? This is unexpected, as one would typically anticipate a larger network to have a higher capacity for learning.
    *   Conversely, why do the results for the 5-core network show almost no improvement as the ensemble size increases from 1 to 64? This suggests that ensembling provides little benefit for this smaller architecture, which is a point worth exploring.

4.  **Comparison with Related Work:**
    *   The paper positions its work against "train-then-constrain" approaches. However, the characterization of some prior work, such as Ref [12], may not be entirely accurate, as that work also incorporates hardware-aware constraints (e.g., zero bias) during the training phase. A more nuanced comparison would strengthen this section. (Note: there is a typo on line 399: "approach approach").
    *   The discussion of related work could be broadened. A comparison with other backpropagation-inspired algorithms for spiking neural networks, such as SpikeProp and its variants, would help to better position the contribution within the wider literature.

5.  **Generality and Practicality:**
    *   The proposed method is closely tied to the specific constraints of the TrueNorth architecture (e.g., axon types, crossbar connectivity). It would be helpful for the authors to discuss the method's generality and what modifications would be needed to apply it to other neuromorphic platforms with different constraints.
    *   The highest accuracy of 99.42% is achieved using a 64-member ensemble. While impressive, this requires a significant dedication of hardware resources (1920 cores). A brief discussion of the practical trade-offs between accuracy and resource utilization would be valuable.
