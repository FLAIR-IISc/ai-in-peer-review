The authors propose a method to reconcile the high precision required for continuous variables in backpropagation algorithms with the constraints of spike-based neural network devices featuring binary synapses. Their key idea is to interpret analog variables as probabilities, sampling from which allows mapping a neural network trained offline to a spiking network chip for application deployment. They demonstrate how to adapt the backpropagation algorithm to accommodate this strategy and validate their method on the MNIST benchmark. They map the trained network onto two different architectures, optimizing for both size and accuracy, and present performance figures measured directly from the chip. 

The manuscript is of very high quality and remarkably clear. The work is quite original and has the potential to be useful in other approaches and with different hardware platforms. While reviewing existing hardware platforms, the authors should also consider referencing Qiao et al., *Frontiers in Neuroscience* 2015, and the paper available at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. 

However, the significance of this work isn’t sufficiently highlighted. It’s unclear how this method will facilitate the practical deployment of the TrueNorth chip, or in what specific applications it will be most beneficial. Furthermore, it's not evident whether, and to what extent, this specific method offers advantages over alternative options, such as those cited in [9] to [12].

The authors present a method for applying backpropagation to spiking neural networks with binary synapses and validate it by successfully mapping a trained network onto the TrueNorth device. The paper is well-structured, concise, and clearly written. The results are convincing, but lack some details. For instance, it would be helpful to know how inputs are converted into spikes (e.g., what frequencies are used), how shared weight values are handled, and what neuron models were employed.
