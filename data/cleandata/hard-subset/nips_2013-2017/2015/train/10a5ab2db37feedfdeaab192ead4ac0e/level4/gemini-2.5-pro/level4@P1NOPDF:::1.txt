This paper presents strong experimental results in terms of both accuracy and power consumption. However, these contributions are diminished by overstated claims of novelty concerning the underlying methodology and the algorithm itself. While the authors have agreed to address these issues, the following points must be resolved to properly contextualize the paper's contributions.

1.  The algorithm detailed in Section 3.2 bears a strong resemblance to the methods proposed in the cited references [14] and [15]. In its final form, the update rule appears to be a hybrid of these two algorithms, derived using identical mathematical approximations. This significant overlap with prior work must be explicitly acknowledged and discussed.

2.  The first paragraph of the discussion incorrectly presents the "constrain-then-train" methodology as a novel contribution. This approach, which involves learning a weight distribution and then sampling from it to produce a fixed network ensemble at test time, was previously proposed and evaluated in reference [15].

3.  Novelty claim (i) in the introduction is misleading. The "Expectation Backpropagation" algorithm from reference [15] already provides a backpropagation update rule for networks with both binary weights and binary neurons (i.e., using a sign activation function). This existing method can be readily applied to networks with constrained connectivity, which contradicts the authors' claim.

4.  The paper's comparison against the state-of-the-art is incomplete. To the best of my knowledge, the strongest prior results for networks with binary weights and neurons on MNIST without data augmentation—the same experimental setup used here—were achieved by the algorithm in reference [15]. The authors should include this key result in their comparison to provide an accurate assessment of their method's performance.
