The authors should acknowledge that the algorithm presented in section 3.2 bears a strong resemblance to the methods described in references [14] and [15]. Specifically, the final form of the algorithm appears to be a hybrid of these two approaches, derived using identical approximations. A clear mention of this connection would be beneficial to provide proper context and credit to the original sources.

The discussion's first paragraph may be misinterpreted as introducing a novel "constrain-then-train" methodology, which involves learning a weight distribution and then sampling from it to produce a fixed network ensemble at test time. However, this approach was previously suggested and tested in reference [15]. To avoid confusion, the authors should clarify the relationship between their work and existing research in this area.

The novelty claim (i) presented in the introduction's last paragraph is also misleading. Reference [15]'s "expectation backpropagation" algorithm is a backpropagation update rule that utilizes expectation propagation, specifically designed for binary neurons with binary weights. This algorithm can easily accommodate constrained connectivity, making the novelty claim in the paper less accurate.

Furthermore, the authors' citation of previous state-of-the-art results appears to be incorrect. To the best of my knowledge, the algorithm presented in reference [15] achieved the best results for binary weights and neurons, as demonstrated on the MNIST dataset without data augmentation (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf), similar to the setup in this paper.

I appreciate the authors' agreement to address these concerns. While the paper presents strong and novel experimental results in terms of accuracy and power consumption, both the general methodology and the algorithm itself are not as novel as claimed.
