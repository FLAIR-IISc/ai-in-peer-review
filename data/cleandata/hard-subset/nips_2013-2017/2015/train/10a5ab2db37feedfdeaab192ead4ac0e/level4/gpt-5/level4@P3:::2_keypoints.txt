- Proposes a probabilistic surrogate that maps continuous inputs, outputs, and binary synapses to probabilities, enabling backprop in a differentiable space and sampling to deploy on neuromorphic hardware.  
- Uses ensemble averaging of multiple sampled binary networks to trade energy for accuracy.  
- Demonstrates the approach on IBM TrueNorth with on-chip measurements and reports MNIST accuracy/energy trade-offs for 5-core and 30-core topologies.  
- Novelty: jointly handles spiking neurons, binary/sub-trinary synapses, and constrained fan-in with a “constrain-then-train” formulation and explicit hardware mapping.  
- Technical method: approximates weighted sums of Bernoulli variables with a Gaussian and derives gradients for synaptic probabilities and biases.  
- Concerns about the Gaussian approximation and omission of a higher-order gradient term—ablation studies quantifying its impact are missing.  
- Experimental results strong on MNIST (99.42% with 64-members; 92.7% single sampled net) and include energy numbers down to 0.268 µJ/class at 1 kHz.  
- Scope limitation: evaluation only on MNIST and no convolutional architectures; generalization to harder vision tasks is untested.  
- Several implementation details are under-specified: input spike encoding, timing/firing-rate assumptions, hardware neuron parameters, s-value quantization and sharing, and energy measurement protocol.  
- Strength: principled training framework that directly maps to spiking hardware with binary synapses and honors connectivity constraints.  
- Strength: strong, transparent on-chip energy/throughput reporting and useful weight-initialization strategy to decorrelate columns.  
- Strength: flexible energy/accuracy trade-off via ensemble size and practical TrueNorth mapping.  
- Weakness: missing ablations on ensemble size, sampling variance, longer integration times, initialization effect, and gradient approximation validity.  
- Weakness: fairness of comparisons to prior train-to-spike methods and low-power platforms needs consistent baselines, data augmentation control, and confidence intervals.  
- Practicality question: need discussion of target applications where single-pass (1 ms) inference and reported single-network accuracy suffice.  
- Suggested experiments: small convolutional test or second dataset, detailed ablations, and more complete energy measurement methodology.  
- Related-work gap: add citations such as Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015) and arXiv:1506.05427 when surveying neuromorphic implementations.  
- Minor fixes: correct typos, clarify bias implementation via leak and fractional handling, and specify mapping from class scores to output neuron averages.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, practical method tightly aligned with hardware constraints and impressive real-hardware energy/accuracy results.  
- Rejection arguments: limited evaluation (MNIST only), unvalidated theoretical approximations, and missing implementation/comparison details that affect reproducibility and generality.
