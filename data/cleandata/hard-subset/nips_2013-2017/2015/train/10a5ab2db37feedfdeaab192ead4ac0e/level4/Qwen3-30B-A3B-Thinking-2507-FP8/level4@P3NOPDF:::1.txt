

The paper presents promising experimental results on binary neural networks, achieving strong accuracy and power efficiency gains. However, the claimed novelty is significantly overstated, and the methodology requires substantial clarification regarding prior work. Below are critical concerns that must be addressed before publication.

First, Section 3.2 describes an algorithm that is fundamentally derived from the same approximations as [14] and [15], forming a hybrid of both approaches. The authors must explicitly acknowledge this lineage in the final manuscript. Specifically:  
- Reference [14] (Zhao et al., 1996) introduced stochastic bit-stream networks with identical probabilistic approximations.  
- Reference [15] (Soudry et al., 2014) formalized "expectation backpropagation" for binary weights/neurons, using the *exact same approximations* for gradient estimation and handling constrained connectivity.  
The algorithm presented here is not novel but a direct synthesis of these prior techniques. The current description obscures this critical connection, misrepresenting the work’s foundation.  

Second, the discussion’s opening paragraph incorrectly implies the "constrain-then-train" methodology (learning weight distributions first, then sampling fixed ensembles at test time) is novel. Reference [15] explicitly proposed and validated this exact approach for binary networks on MNIST (without data augmentation), as demonstrated in their arXiv preprint (1503.03562v3). The paper’s phrasing ("we propose a novel constraint-then-train framework") is misleading given this prior work.  

Third, the novelty claim (i) in the introduction is factually incorrect. The authors state that "expectation backpropagation" (ref. [15]) "cannot handle constrained connectivity," but [15]’s method *explicitly accommodates* constrained connectivity (e.g., for sparse or structured weights). Moreover, [15] implemented expectation backpropagation for binary neurons with sign activation (where inputs never exactly reach zero), which aligns precisely with the paper’s approach. The claim that [15] is "not applicable" to constrained connectivity mischaracterizes the prior work and exaggerates the current contribution.  

Fourth, the comparison to "previous state-of-the-art" is inaccurate. The paper cites [15] as achieving "only 99.0% on MNIST," yet [15]’s results (1503.03562v3, Table 1) report **99.3%** on MNIST *without data augmentation*—the exact same protocol used here. This is the established SOTA for binary weights/neurons under identical conditions. The paper’s assertion that prior work "did not achieve this accuracy" is therefore false.  

The authors have acknowledged these issues in their response, and I commend them for addressing the novelty concerns. However, the manuscript must be revised to:  
1. **Explicitly credit [14] and [15]** in Section 3.2, detailing how the algorithm synthesizes their approaches via identical approximations.  
2. **Correct the "constrain-then-train" description** to cite [15] as the originator of the methodology.  
3. **Retract the false claim about [15]’s inability to handle constrained connectivity** and clarify that [15]’s framework *does* support it.  
4. **Update the SOTA comparison** to reflect that [15]’s 99.3% MNIST result is the current benchmark (not 99.0%).  

While the experimental results—particularly the 4.1× power reduction and competitive accuracy—are strong and valuable, the paper’s academic integrity hinges on accurately situating its contributions within the existing literature. The current text misrepresents prior work, which undermines the paper’s credibility. The methodology is not novel, and the experimental narrative is built on incorrect premises about prior SOTA.  

To summarize:  
- **Novelty**: The core algorithm and methodology are not new but a hybrid of [14] and [15].  
- **Accuracy claim**: The SOTA comparison misstates [15]’s results (99.3% vs. 99.0%).  
- **Technical claim**: [15] *does* support constrained connectivity, contrary to the paper’s assertion.  

The authors’ experimental contributions are compelling, but the paper must be rewritten to reflect the true relationship to [14] and [15]. Without these revisions, the paper risks misleading readers about its technical contribution and historical context. I recommend rejection *unless* the authors fully revise the manuscript to correct these issues. The experimental results alone do not justify the current novelty claims.  

**Recommendation**: Major Revision (with mandatory text revisions to address all points above). The paper has strong empirical value but requires significant clarification to align with prior work.
