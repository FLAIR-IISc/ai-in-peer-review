- Summary: Proposes probabilistic training of networks for neuromorphic hardware by learning continuous synapse/activation probabilities and sampling binary ensembles for deployment, demonstrated on TrueNorth with MNIST.

- Strength — Experimental results: Achieves SOTA 99.42% (64-ensemble) and 92.7% (single net) on MNIST with very low energy per image on TrueNorth.

- Strength — Clarity: Paper is well written, clearly explains the constrain-then-train approach, topology, and mapping to hardware.

- Strength — Practical impact: Provides a compelling end-to-end pathway from backprop-style training to energy-efficient embedded neuromorphic deployment.

- Weakness — Overstated novelty: The Gaussian-approximation training and gradient propagation closely resemble prior work (e.g., Zhao et al. 1996 and Expectation Backpropagation by Soudry et al.), and novelty is overstated.

- Weakness — Misleading methodology framing: The "constrain-then-train" sampling-at-test-time approach is central to EBP, so framing it as a key new contribution is misleading.

- Weakness — Inaccurate novelty claims: The claim of being first to use offline backprop-like training for spiking/binary networks is incorrect given EBP; the contribution is more in application/scale and hardware demonstration.

- Weakness — Incomplete comparisons: Fails to cite/compare directly to relevant binary-weight/activation results (e.g., EBP arXiv:1503.03562v3), leading to an incomplete performance context.

- Pro: Demonstrates SOTA accuracy for spiking networks and an extremely energy-efficient real-hardware implementation, offering a practical framework for deployment.

- Con: Paper overclaims algorithmic novelty, omits key prior-art comparisons, and contains "for the first time" assertions that are not accurate.

- Recommendation: Acceptable if authors substantially revise to (1) explicitly relate their algorithm to Expectation Backpropagation, (2) tone down novelty claims about constrain-then-train, and (3) add comparisons to binary-weight MNIST results; otherwise the paper is misleading about its contributions.
