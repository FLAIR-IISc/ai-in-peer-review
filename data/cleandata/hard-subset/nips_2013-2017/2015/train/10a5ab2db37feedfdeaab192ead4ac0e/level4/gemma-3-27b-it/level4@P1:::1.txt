This paper addresses the crucial problem of deploying deep learning models on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware. The authors tackle the incompatibility between backpropagation, which relies on continuous values, and neuromorphic systems using spiking neurons and discrete synapses. Their approach cleverly treats spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, enabling the use of standard backpropagation for training. The trained network is then mapped to neuromorphic hardware by sampling these probabilities, and an ensemble of resulting networks is used for inference. The authors demonstrate this methodology on the TrueNorth chip with the MNIST dataset, achieving impressive results in terms of both accuracy and energy efficiency – 99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ per image with a 64-member ensemble, and 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image with a single-member ensemble.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the need to bridge the gap between powerful deep learning algorithms and energy-constrained neuromorphic hardware. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches and propose a novel solution that addresses these challenges. The experimental results are particularly compelling, showcasing significant improvements in power efficiency compared to previous work. The use of the TrueNorth chip adds practical relevance to the research.

However, the paper’s claims of novelty could be more nuanced. The authors should acknowledge the close relationship between their probabilistic backpropagation approach and existing work, specifically citing [14] Zhao et al., “Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks,” and [15] Soudry et al., “Expectation backpropagation.” The algorithm presented in Section 3.2 shares significant similarities with these references, utilizing comparable approximations, and is essentially a hybrid of both.

Furthermore, the discussion section gives the impression that the "constrain-then-train" methodology – learning a weight distribution and then sampling to generate a fixed network ensemble – is novel, but this was already proposed and evaluated in [15]. Similarly, the novelty claim (i) in the introduction regarding the offline training methodology is somewhat overstated.  Soudry et al. [15] demonstrated expectation backpropagation, a backpropagation update rule, for binary neurons and binary weights, which inherently supports constrained connectivity.

The reported state-of-the-art comparisons also require careful attention. The authors claim to achieve the best accuracy to date on MNIST compared to networks using spiking neurons or binary synapses. However, the previous best results for binary weights and neurons were, to the best of my knowledge, achieved by the algorithm presented in [15], tested on MNIST without data augmentation. The authors should verify these comparisons and ensure accurate attribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Bridging the gap between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware is critical for real-world applications.
*   **Novel approach:** The probabilistic treatment of spikes and synapses is a clever and effective solution.
*   **Strong experimental results:** The demonstrated accuracy and energy efficiency on the TrueNorth chip are highly impressive.
*   **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overstated novelty:** The paper’s claims regarding the originality of the methodology and algorithm need to be more nuanced and acknowledge related work.
*   **Inaccurate state-of-the-art comparisons:** The reported comparisons with previous work require verification.
*   **Lack of detailed comparison:** A more thorough comparison with related algorithms, particularly expectation backpropagation [15], would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and well-supported solution to a significant problem.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate substantial improvements in energy efficiency.
*   The work is likely to have a significant impact on the field of neuromorphic computing.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper’s claims of novelty are overstated and should be revised.
*   The state-of-the-art comparisons require verification and more careful consideration.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. While the claims of novelty should be tempered, the technical contributions and impressive experimental results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, specifically regarding the acknowledgment of related work and the verification of state-of-the-art comparisons. Addressing these concerns will enhance the paper's credibility and impact.
