This paper presents a backpropagation-based training method for spiking neural networks that is specifically designed to accommodate the hardware constraints of neuromorphic platforms, such as spiking neurons and discrete-valued synapses. The topic is timely and relevant, as mapping deep networks onto specialized, low-power neuromorphic hardware is an area of significant research interest. The authors validate their approach by implementing the trained network on the TrueNorth neurosynaptic system.

**Quality and Significance**

The primary strength of this work is the development of a training methodology that directly incorporates hardware constraints. Such co-design of algorithms and hardware is crucial for the practical application of neuromorphic systems. The demonstration of the trained network on actual hardware serves as a valuable proof of concept and validation of the method's effectiveness in a real-world constrained environment.

However, the significance could be broadened by addressing the method's generality. The constraints considered are specific to the TrueNorth architecture. A discussion on how these techniques might apply or be adapted to other neuromorphic platforms with different constraints would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the reliance on a large, 64-member ensemble to achieve high accuracy raises practical concerns about resource utilization. This trade-off between accuracy and hardware cost is a critical point that warrants a more detailed discussion.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, several points require clarification to improve readability and precision:

1.  **Framing of Contributions:** The claims at the end of Section 1 could be rephrased. As currently written, they might be misconstrued to suggest this is the first training methodology for spiking neurons with reduced-precision synapses. It would be beneficial to more precisely position the work in the context of existing literature. Additionally, the implementation on the TrueNorth platform should be framed as a validation of the training method's efficacy rather than a novel contribution in itself.

2.  **Technical and Experimental Details:** Several key details are either missing or unclear:
    *   **Network Topology:** A clear and consolidated description of the network topology is needed.
    *   **Synaptic Representation:** In Section 2, the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" is ambiguous and requires explanation.
    *   **Training Overhead:** What is the impact of the probabilistic synaptic updates on training time compared to a standard backpropagation algorithm?
    *   **Input Representation:** Please clarify the nature of the input during the testing phase. Were spike trains used, and if so, what were their characteristic spike rates?

3.  **Analysis of Results:** The results presented raise some questions that should be addressed in the paper:
    *   Why does a single 30-core network ensemble exhibit significantly lower performance compared to a 5-core network? This result is counterintuitive and requires an explanation.
    *   Conversely, why is the performance variance so low across the different 5-core network ensembles?
    *   There is a typo on line 399 ("approach approach"). More importantly, the characterization of Reference [12] as a purely "train-then-constrain" method is inaccurate; that work also incorporates constraints during the training process.

**Originality**

The paper's approach of integrating specific hardware constraints into a backpropagation framework is valuable. To better establish its originality, the authors should provide a more thorough comparison with other backpropagation-inspired learning rules for spiking networks, such as SpikeProp and its variants. A discussion of the key differences and relative advantages of the proposed method would help contextualize its contribution to the field.
