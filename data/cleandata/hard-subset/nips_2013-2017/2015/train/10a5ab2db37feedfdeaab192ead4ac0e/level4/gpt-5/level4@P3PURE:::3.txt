Summary
The paper presents a backpropagation-based training method that operates on spike probabilities and explicitly accounts for hardware constraints of a specific neuromorphic platform with spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This is a timely topic given the interest in mapping deep networks to neuromorphic hardware. Overall, the method appears useful, especially in light of emerging multi-neuron hardware platforms with stringent constraints.

Quality
- The proposed training approach is practically relevant and well motivated by hardware considerations.
- Demonstrating the trained network on the target hardware (TN) is valuable as a validation of the method. However, I would not consider the mere act of running on TN as a novelty in itself; rather, it serves to substantiate the training framework.

Clarity
- The paper is generally easy to read.
- The claims at the end of Section 1 should be reworded. As written, they can be misread as asserting a first-of-its-kind training methodology using spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses. Please clarify the novelty without implying undue primacy.
- The network topology is not clearly and succinctly described in a single place. A consolidated description (layers, neuron counts, connectivity, synapse precision, and any architectural constraints enforced during training) would improve readability.

Specific questions and requests for clarification
- Section 2: What exactly does “0.15 bits per synapse” mean in practice? How is this quantity defined and measured, and how does it relate to the discrete synapse implementation on TN?
- Training time: How much additional training time is introduced by the probabilistic synaptic connection update relative to a comparable non-probabilistic baseline? Please quantify the overhead and discuss any convergence implications.
- Ensemble performance:
  - Why is the result from a single ensemble of the 30-core network so low, especially compared to the 5-core network?
  - Why are the results across different ensembles of the 5-core network approximately the same? Some analysis of variance across ensembles and a hypothesis for the observed behavior would be helpful.
- Inputs during testing: What are the spike rates for the inputs during testing? Is the input provided as spike trains, and if so, how are rates and durations chosen? If inputs are rate-coded or otherwise encoded, please specify the encoding, spike statistics, and any impact on latency/accuracy trade-offs.
- Reference 12 (line 399): This work includes constraints during training (e.g., bias = 0); it is therefore not solely a “train-then-constrain” approach. Please correct this characterization. Also, there is a typo on that line (“approach approach”).

Originality
- The paper would benefit from a discussion or comparison with prior spiking backpropagation-type rules, such as SpikeProp. Please position your method relative to these approaches (objective, learning signals, differentiability assumptions, handling of discrete synapses, and hardware-awareness).

Significance
- Developing training methods that account for hardware constraints is important and of broad interest.
- The constraints addressed here are tailored to the TN architecture. Please discuss which aspects are platform-specific versus generalizable to other neuromorphic systems (e.g., different synaptic quantization schemes, neuron models, connectivity limits).
- If the reported accuracies rely on a 64-ensemble, this implies a proportional increase in required hardware resources to achieve the target accuracy. Please discuss the accuracy–resource trade-off, including the practical implications for deployment (latency, power, area), and compare to single-instance performance.

Minor issues
- Reword the novelty claim at the end of Section 1 to avoid overstatement.
- Correct the typo “approach approach” (line 399).
