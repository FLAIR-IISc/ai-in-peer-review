This paper presents a novel method for training spiking neural networks with binary synapses using backpropagation. The core contribution is a technique to reconcile the high-precision, continuous-valued weights required by backpropagation with the binary constraints of neuromorphic hardware. The authors propose treating the continuous-valued weights as probabilities, which are then sampled to determine the binary synaptic states on the hardware. This probabilistic approach allows for a modified backpropagation algorithm to be used for off-line training before the resulting network is mapped to a spiking neural network chip for deployment.

The method is validated on the MNIST benchmark, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness by mapping the trained network onto the TrueNorth chip. They present performance results for two different network architectures, one optimized for size and the other for accuracy, with performance metrics measured directly from the hardware.

Overall, the manuscript is of high quality: it is well-structured, clearly written, and presents a highly original contribution. The proposed technique could prove valuable for training other types of constrained networks and may be applicable to a variety of hardware platforms beyond TrueNorth.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses that should be addressed:

1.  **Significance and Impact:** A primary weakness is the lack of discussion regarding the work's broader significance. The authors do not clearly articulate how this method will facilitate the deployment of chips like TrueNorth in real-world scenarios or identify which specific applications stand to benefit the most.

2.  **Comparison to Alternatives:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more direct comparison with alternative approaches. It is currently unclear what specific advantages this method offers over the techniques cited in references [9-12].

3.  **Missing Experimental Details:** While the results presented are convincing, the experimental section lacks crucial implementation details. To ensure reproducibility and allow for a thorough evaluation, the authors should provide more information on key parameters, such as the input-to-spike conversion method, the spike frequencies used, the values of the shared weights, and the specific neuron models employed.

4.  **Incomplete Literature Review:** The review of related hardware platforms could be made more comprehensive by including references to other relevant work, such as Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the work presented in arXiv:1506.05427.

In summary, this paper introduces an innovative and promising method for training SNNs on neuromorphic hardware. The results are compelling, but the manuscript would benefit from a clearer articulation of its significance, a more thorough comparison with existing methods, and the inclusion of key experimental details.
