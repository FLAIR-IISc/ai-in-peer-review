This paper presents a methodology for training neural networks with binary weights and neurons, along with a novel algorithm for achieving this goal. However, there are several concerns regarding the novelty and accuracy of the claims made in the paper.

Firstly, the authors should acknowledge that the algorithm presented in Section 3.2 bears a strong resemblance to the algorithms described in references [14] and [15]. In its final form, the proposed algorithm appears to be a hybrid of these two, derived using identical approximations. A clear mention of this connection would be beneficial to provide a more accurate representation of the work.

Furthermore, the discussion section gives the impression that the "constrain-then-train" methodology, which involves learning a weight distribution and then sampling from it to produce a fixed network ensemble at test time, is a novel contribution. However, this approach was already suggested and tested in reference [15]. The authors should clarify the relationship between their work and this existing research.

Additionally, the novelty claim (i) in the last paragraph of the introduction is misleading. Reference [15] describes an "expectation backpropagation" algorithm, which is a backpropagation update rule using expectation propagation, specifically designed for binary neurons with binary weights. This algorithm can easily accommodate constrained connectivity, making the novelty claim in the paper less accurate.

Another point of contention is the citation of previous state-of-the-art results. To the best of my knowledge, the previous best results for binary weights and neurons were achieved by the algorithm in reference [15], which was tested on the MNIST dataset without data augmentation, similar to the setup in this paper. The authors should correct the citations to reflect the accurate state of the art.

In light of the authors' feedback, I appreciate their willingness to address these concerns. While the paper's experimental results are strong and novel, particularly in terms of accuracy and power consumption, both the general methodology and the algorithm itself are not as novel as claimed.
