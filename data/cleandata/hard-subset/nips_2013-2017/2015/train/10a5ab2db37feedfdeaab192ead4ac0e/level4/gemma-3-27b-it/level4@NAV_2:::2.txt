The authors address the critical challenge of bridging the gap between the algorithmic strengths of backpropagation and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic hardware. They propose a novel approach to train networks using backpropagation, treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, allowing for a natural mapping to neuromorphic platforms via sampling and ensemble averaging. This method is demonstrated on the MNIST dataset using the TrueNorth chip, achieving competitive accuracy with significantly improved energy efficiency compared to existing approaches.

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The core idea of probabilistically representing spikes and synapses to enable backpropagation is well-motivated and clearly explained. The experimental validation on TrueNorth is particularly valuable, demonstrating the practical feasibility of the approach. The authors carefully evaluate the trade-offs between performance and energy consumption, providing results for both a high-performance and a high-efficiency network. The inclusion of detailed network parameters and the explanation of the weight initialization strategy are commendable. 

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The introduction effectively frames the problem and motivates the proposed solution. The methodology is described in a logical and accessible manner, with helpful figures and equations. The writing is concise and avoids unnecessary jargon. The level of detail provided is sufficient for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results.

The work is highly original. While previous efforts have explored subsets of the presented ideas – such as backpropagation with spiking neurons (e.g., [9, 10, 11, 12]) or probabilistic approaches with binary synapses (e.g., [14, 15]) – this paper uniquely combines these elements with constrained connectivity and achieves state-of-the-art performance on TrueNorth. The network topology, designed to align with the core-to-core connectivity of neuromorphic hardware, is also a novel contribution. Related work is adequately referenced, though the authors might consider including Qiao et al., *Frontiers in Neuroscience* 2015, and the paper at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427 for completeness.

The significance of this work is substantial. The demonstrated ability to train spiking networks with backpropagation and deploy them efficiently on neuromorphic hardware opens up exciting possibilities for real-world applications. While the paper focuses on MNIST, the underlying principles are applicable to more complex datasets and tasks. The potential impact of this work lies in its ability to leverage the strengths of both algorithmic and hardware approaches to build low-power, intelligent systems. However, the paper could be strengthened by further discussing the broader applicability of this method and how it compares to alternative deployment strategies (e.g., [9]-[12]).  More details regarding the spike encoding scheme (frequencies used, etc.), neuron models, and shared weight values would further enhance the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to bridging backpropagation and neuromorphic computing.
*   Strong experimental validation on TrueNorth with state-of-the-art performance.
*   Clear and well-written paper with a thorough explanation of the methodology.
*   Significant potential impact on the field of neuromorphic computing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of broader applicability and comparison to alternative methods.
*   Some details regarding spike encoding and neuron models could be expanded.
*   The impact on real-world applications and advantages over existing methods need to be more thoroughly discussed.
