The authors present a training method for spiking neural networks based on backpropagation, which incorporates hardware constraints from a specific platform featuring spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This work addresses the timely and important problem of effectively mapping deep networks onto neuromorphic hardware.

**Quality and Significance**

The proposed training method is valuable, particularly for emerging neuromorphic hardware where architectural constraints are a primary concern. The development of training techniques that are co-designed with hardware is a significant and interesting direction for the field. However, the significance is somewhat tied to the specific TrueNorth (TN) architecture. The generalizability of this method could be discussed further, as the constraints considered here might not all apply to other neuromorphic platforms. Furthermore, the reliance on a 64-member ensemble to achieve high accuracy implies a significant hardware resource cost, a trade-off that warrants more explicit discussion.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, several points could be clarified to strengthen the manuscript:

*   The claims at the end of Section 1 could be rephrased. As currently written, they might be misinterpreted as claiming to be the first training methodology for spiking neurons with reduced-precision synapses. A more nuanced phrasing would prevent this potential confusion.
*   The demonstration on the TN hardware is a strong validation of the proposed training method. However, it should be framed as such, rather than being presented as a primary novel contribution of the paper.

I have several specific questions and suggestions for the authors:

1.  In Section 2, could you please clarify the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse"?
2.  A consolidated description of the network topology would be beneficial, as the details are not currently presented in a single location.
3.  What is the training time overhead introduced by the probabilistic synaptic updates compared to a standard backpropagation approach?
4.  Could you provide an explanation for the poor performance of a single 30-core network ensemble, especially when compared to the 5-core network?
5.  Similarly, why is there such low variance in the results across the different 5-core network ensembles?
6.  Please specify the nature of the input during testing. Are the inputs spike trains, and if so, what are their corresponding spike rates?
7.  The characterization of reference [12] (line 399) as a purely "train-then-constrain" approach appears to be inaccurate, as that work also incorporates constraints (e.g., bias=0) during its training phase. Please also note the typo ("approach approach") on this line.

**Originality**

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion and comparison with other backpropagation-inspired learning rules for spiking networks, such as SpikeProp and its variants. This would help to better position the current work within the existing literature.
