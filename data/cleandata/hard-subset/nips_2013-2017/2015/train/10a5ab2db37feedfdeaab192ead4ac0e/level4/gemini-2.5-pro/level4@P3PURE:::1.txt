This paper presents strong and novel experimental results, particularly in terms of accuracy and power consumption. However, the claims regarding the novelty of both the general methodology and the algorithm itself are overstated.

1.  The algorithm presented in Section 3.2 bears a strong resemblance to methods in the cited literature. The authors should acknowledge that its final form is effectively a hybrid of the algorithms from [14] and [15] and was derived using identical approximations.
    *   [14] J. Zhao, J. Shawe-Taylor, and M. van Daalen, "Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks," Neural Networks, vol. 9, no. 6, pp. 991 - 998, 1996.
    *   [15] Soudry, Daniel, Itay Hubara, and Ron Meir. "Expectation backpropagation: Parameter-free training of multilayer neural networks with continuous or discrete weights." Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems. 2014.

2.  The first paragraph of the Discussion section gives the impression that the "constrain-then-train" methodology is a novel contribution. This approach, which involves learning a weight distribution and then sampling from it to produce a fixed network ensemble at test time, was previously proposed and tested in reference [15].

3.  Novelty claim (i) in the last paragraph of the introduction is misleading. The "Expectation Backpropagation" algorithm from [15] is also a backpropagation-based update rule for networks with binary weights and neurons (using a sign activation function, where zero input is a zero-probability event) and can be straightforwardly adapted for constrained connectivity.

4.  The paper's claims regarding the previous state-of-the-art are incorrect. To my knowledge, the best prior results for networks with binary weights and neurons on the MNIST dataset without data augmentation (the same experimental setup used here) were achieved by the algorithm in [15] (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf).
