The authors propose a method to reconcile the high precision requirement of continuous variables in backpropagation algorithms with the desire to use spike-based neural network devices with binary synapses. They present the idea of treating analog variables as probabilities, which is the model for mapping the spiking network chip used for application deployment to the neural network used for off-line training. They show how to adapt the backpropagation algorithm to be compatible with this strategy and validate the proposed method on the MNIST benchmark. They map the trained network into two different architectures, optimize for size and accuracy, and present performance figures measured from the chip. The manuscript quality is very good, as is its clarity. This work is quite original and may be useful for other approaches and other hardware platforms. By the way, while reviewing the various HW platforms available, the authors should also mention the paper by Qiao et al, Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015, and the paper at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. The paper does not highlight the importance of the work. It is not clear what specific applications the proposed method will be useful for deploying the TrueNorth chip, and in what practical applications. It is not clear whether and to what extent this specific method has advantages over other alternative options, such as those mentioned in [9] to [12].

The authors present a method for applying backpropagation to spiking neural networks with binary synapses and validate it by successfully mapping the trained network onto the TrueNorth device. The paper is well-structured, clear, and accurate. The results are reassuring, but there is a lack of some details (for example, how the input is converted into spikes, what frequencies are used, shared weight values, neuron models, etc.).
