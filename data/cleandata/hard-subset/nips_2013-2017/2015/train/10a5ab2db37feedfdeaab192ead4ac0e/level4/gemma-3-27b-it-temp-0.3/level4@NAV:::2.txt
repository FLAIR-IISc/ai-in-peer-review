The authors address the challenge of deploying backpropagation-trained continuous variable networks onto binary-synapse spiking neural network hardware. Their approach cleverly frames analog variables as probabilities, enabling a sampling-based mapping from a training network to a spiking network implemented on a dedicated chip. They demonstrate a backpropagation adaptation compatible with this probabilistic mapping and validate its effectiveness on the MNIST dataset. The method is evaluated across two distinct network architectures, optimized for both size and accuracy, with corresponding performance metrics reported from hardware measurements. The manuscript is of excellent quality and exceptionally clear. This work is highly original and offers potential benefits beyond the specific hardware considered. When discussing relevant hardware, the authors should also consider the work of Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the research detailed at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. 

Currently, the paper does not sufficiently emphasize the significance of this contribution. The practical implications of this method for deploying the TrueNorth chip – specifically, *which* applications would benefit – remain unclear. Furthermore, a more thorough comparison to existing alternatives, including those referenced in [9-12], is needed to establish the unique advantages of this specific approach. 

The authors successfully demonstrate backpropagation in a spiking neural network with binary synapses and validate the method through successful deployment on the TrueNorth device. The paper is well-organized, concise, and the results are compelling. However, the presentation would be strengthened by including additional details regarding input encoding (spike conversion, frequencies), weight sharing specifics, and the neuron models employed.
