The authors present an interesting paper on a backpropagation training method that uses spike probabilities and explicitly accounts for the hardware constraints of a specific platform with spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This is a timely topic for mapping deep networks to multi-neuron hardware platforms.

Quality: The proposed training method is useful, particularly given the constraints of emerging multi-neuron hardware platforms. However, I would not count the demonstration on the TN platform itself as a novel contribution; rather, running the network on TN serves as a validation of the training method.

Clarity: The paper is generally easy to read. That said, the claims at the end of Section 1 should be reworded. As written, they may be interpreted as claiming a first demonstration of a training methodology that uses spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses, which I do not believe is the intent. In addition, several points require clarification:
- In Section 2, what does “0.15 bits per synapse” mean?
- The network topology is not clearly described in one place; please provide a concise, complete specification.
- How much longer is training due to the probabilistic synaptic update mechanism?
- Why is the result from a single ensemble of the 30-core network so low, especially compared with the 5-core network?
- Why are the results across different ensembles of the 5-core network nearly identical?
- What are the input spike rates during testing? Is the input presented as a spike train?
- Reference 12 on line 399 includes constraints (e.g., bias = 0) during training; it is therefore not purely a train-then-constrain approach. Also, there is a typo on this line (“approach approach”).

Originality: Please include a discussion or comparison with other spiking backpropagation-type rules, such as SpikeProp.

Significance: Developing training methods that incorporate hardware constraints is of high interest. However, the constraints considered here are specific to the TN architecture and may not all translate to other platforms. Also, if the reported results rely on a 64-member ensemble, this implies that substantially more hardware resources are required to achieve the target accuracy. 

Overall, this is an interesting paper proposing a backpropagation-based training method that accounts for the constraints of a particular spiking-neuron, discrete-synapse platform. With clearer positioning of the novelty, additional quantitative detail, and comparisons to related spiking backprop methods, the contribution would be stronger.
