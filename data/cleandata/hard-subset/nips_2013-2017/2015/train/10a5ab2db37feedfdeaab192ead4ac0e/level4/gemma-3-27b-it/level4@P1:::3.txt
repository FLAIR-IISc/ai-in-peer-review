The authors present an interesting paper on a backpropagation training method utilizing spike probabilities, specifically designed to address the hardware constraints of neuromorphic platforms featuring spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This is a timely topic, given the increasing interest in mapping deep networks onto specialized, multi-neuron hardware.

**Quality:** The proposed training method is valuable, particularly in the context of emerging multi-neuron hardware platforms with inherent constraints. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges in bridging the gap between the continuous-valued operations of backpropagation and the discrete nature of neuromorphic hardware. The experimental results on the TrueNorth chip are compelling, showcasing both high accuracy and energy efficiency.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, the claims made at the end of Section 1 could be rephrased. As currently stated, they might be misinterpreted to suggest that the paper introduces the *first* training methodology employing spiking neurons and synapses with reduced precision. It would be more accurate to emphasize that the novelty lies in the combination of these elements – spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, constrained connectivity, *and* backpropagation training – within a single framework. Furthermore, demonstrating the network’s operation on TrueNorth is primarily a validation of the training method, rather than a novel contribution in itself. 

In Section 2, further clarification is needed regarding the 0.15 bits per synapse. What does this value represent, and how does it contribute to the overall efficiency? The network topology could be described more concisely in a single location. It would be helpful to address the computational overhead associated with the probabilistic synaptic connection updates. Does this significantly increase training time compared to standard backpropagation? The comparatively low result from the single ensemble of the 30-core network, especially when contrasted with the 5-core network, warrants further investigation. Similarly, understanding why the results across different ensembles of the 5-core network remain consistent would be beneficial. Finally, providing information about the spike rates during testing, and confirming whether the input is represented as a spike train, would enhance the reproducibility of the work. There's a minor typographical error on line 399 (“approach approach”).

**Originality:** The authors should consider discussing and comparing their approach with other spiking backpropagation-type rules, such as SpikeProp. A brief overview of these related methods would provide valuable context and highlight the unique contributions of their work.

**Significance:** Developing new training methods that account for hardware constraints is a significant contribution. However, the constraints considered in this paper are largely based on the TrueNorth architecture. It would be helpful to discuss the generalizability of these constraints to other neuromorphic platforms. The reliance on a 64-member ensemble to achieve peak accuracy raises a practical consideration: the increased hardware resources required to attain the desired network performance. Despite this, the paper presents a valuable contribution towards bridging the gap between algorithmic advancements in deep learning and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic hardware. It’s a well-motivated and executed piece of work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of backpropagation with spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity.
*   Strong experimental results on the TrueNorth chip demonstrating high accuracy and significant energy efficiency.
*   Addresses a critical challenge in deploying deep learning models on neuromorphic hardware.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of some claims in Section 1 may be overstated.
*   Some details regarding the hardware implementation (0.15 bits per synapse) and training process (computational overhead) require further clarification.
*   The results from ensembles of different sizes require more explanation.
*   Generalizability to other neuromorphic platforms could be better discussed.
