- Proposes a training methodology to bridge backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware constraints (spiking neurons, low-precision synapses).  
- Treats synaptic connections and neuron outputs as probabilities to enable standard backprop under hardware-compatible constraints.  
- Demonstrated on TrueNorth with MNIST, reporting 99.42% accuracy (ensemble of 64) and 0.268 μJ/image (ensemble of 1).  
- Technical approach is sound and well-justified; probabilistic synapse interpretation avoids post-training discretization loss.  
- Paper provides careful hardware mapping and experimental validation on TrueNorth.  
- Clarity issues: the key innovation isn’t clearly distinguished from prior work and the “for the first time” phrasing is misleading.  
- Claim that running on TrueNorth is novel is inaccurate—it's validation of the method, not a standalone novelty.  
- The meaning and accounting of “0.15 bits per synapse” is unclear and needs clearer explanation.  
- Network topology details are scattered; a concise, unified description is needed.  
- Original contribution: meaningful integration of spiking neurons, extremely low-precision (<2-bit) synapses, and constrained connectivity in one training framework.  
- Missing prior-art comparisons—SpikeProp and other spiking backpropagation methods should be cited and discussed.  
- The “constrain-then-train” methodology is a significant conceptual advance over “train-then-constrain” approaches.  
- Significance: addresses a critical bottleneck for energy-efficient edge neuromorphic deployment and shows substantial energy improvements over prior work.  
- Practical limitation: the high-accuracy ensemble (64) requires substantially more hardware resources, limiting deployability in constrained settings.  
- Additional weaknesses: overstated novelty, insufficient explanation for why some network/core configurations perform worse, and no discussion of training time overhead.  
- Pro-acceptance: practical, validated, technically sound solution with generalizability to other neuromorphic platforms.  
- Con-acceptance: novelty claims need tempering, clarifications required, and hardware/resource/training overheads insufficiently addressed.  
- Overall: strong paper meriting acceptance with minor revisions to clarify novelty and technical descriptions.
