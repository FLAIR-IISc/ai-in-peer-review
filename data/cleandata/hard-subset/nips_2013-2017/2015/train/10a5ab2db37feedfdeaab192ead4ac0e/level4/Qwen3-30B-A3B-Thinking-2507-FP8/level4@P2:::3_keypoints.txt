- Addresses training neural networks for neuromorphic hardware with spiking neurons and discrete synapses.  
- Proposes a "constrain-then-train" probabilistic approach that treats synapses and neuron outputs as continuous probabilities for backpropagation then maps to discrete hardware.  
- Timely contribution bridging backpropagation-based deep learning and energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware.  
- Technically sound method with a theoretically grounded probabilistic interpretation linking continuous training to discrete constraints.  
- Experimental results include comprehensive energy–accuracy tradeoffs on MNIST, demonstrating practical hardware mapping (TrueNorth).  
- Requests more detailed justification of the Gaussian approximation (Eq. 3), its validity for the network configurations used, and its impact on generalizability.  
- Generally well-written and logical, but a claim of being the "first" offline backpropagation-to-spiking methodology is misleading and needs clarification relative to prior work (e.g., [12]).  
- The metric "0.15 bits per synapse" needs clarification on its definition and significance.  
- Network topology description is fragmented across sections and should be consolidated into a single cohesive section.  
- Originality: meaningful by combining spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity in one framework, but needs stronger comparison to existing spiking backpropagation methods (SpikeProp [14], etc.).  
- Significance: shows strong energy-accuracy tradeoffs (e.g., 99.42% at 108 μJ with ensemble of 64; 92.7% at 0.268 μJ with single ensemble), highlighting practical value for energy-constrained systems.  
- Practical concerns: ensemble-based high-accuracy configs require ×64 hardware resources and scaling to tasks beyond MNIST is unclear.  
- Pros: addresses timely problem, principled constrain-then-train approach, real hardware validation on TrueNorth, clear experimental methodology.  
- Cons: novelty claims overstated without comparisons, unclear Gaussian approximation validity, unclear "0.15 bits" metric, fragmented topology description, limited discussion of ensemble hardware tradeoffs.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify novelty with precise comparisons to prior spiking backprop methods, expand discussion of Gaussian approximation limits, consolidate network topology description, explain "0.15 bits per synapse", discuss hardware resource implications of ensembles, and add direct comparison with SpikeProp and related methods.
