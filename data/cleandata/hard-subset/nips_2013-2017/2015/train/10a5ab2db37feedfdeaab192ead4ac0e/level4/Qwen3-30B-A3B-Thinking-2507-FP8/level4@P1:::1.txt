

This paper addresses an important challenge at the intersection of deep learning and neuromorphic computing: bridging the gap between backpropagation-based training and energy-efficient spiking neural network hardware. The authors propose a method where they train networks using continuous-valued probabilities representing spike occurrence and synaptic states, then map these to discrete spiking networks for deployment. The approach demonstrates promising results on MNIST, achieving 99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ per image (ensemble of 64) and 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image (single network).

The paper makes several noteworthy contributions. The training methodology effectively reconciles backpropagation with neuromorphic constraints by treating synaptic connections and spike probabilities as continuous variables during training, then sampling to create discrete hardware-compatible networks. The deployment architecture on TrueNorth achieves impressive energy efficiency while maintaining high accuracy. The network topology with progressive mixing and constrained connectivity is well-suited for neuromorphic hardware limitations.

However, the paper's novelty claims require careful reconsideration. The core training algorithm in Section 3.2 is deeply similar to approaches presented in [14,15], particularly the expectation backpropagation method described in Soudry et al. (2014). Both methods use probabilistic representations of binary weights and neurons, and employ similar approximations to compute gradients. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this relationship and clarify how their specific implementation differs from these prior works.

The "constrain-then-train" methodology is presented as novel in the Discussion, but this approach was already demonstrated in Soudry et al. (2014) where they trained networks with binary weights and neurons using expectation backpropagation. The ensemble sampling approach for deployment is also directly analogous to the method described in that reference, where the authors sampled from a learned weight distribution to create multiple network instances.

The novelty claim (i) in the introduction is particularly problematic. The paper states it's the first to demonstrate "an offline training methodology using backpropagation to create a network that employs spiking neurons, synapses requiring less bits of precision than even trinary weights, and constrained connectivity." However, Soudry et al. (2014) already implemented backpropagation for binary weights and neurons with constrained connectivity, as evidenced by their MNIST results (97.88% accuracy without data augmentation). The authors' claim appears to overlook this important prior work.

The cited previous state-of-the-art results are also inaccurate. The paper claims their 99.42% accuracy "is the best accuracy to date on MNIST (99.42%) when compared to networks that use spiking neurons, even with high precision synapses (99.12%)" [12]. However, Soudry et al. (2014) achieved 97.88% accuracy on MNIST with binary weights and neurons (without data augmentation), which was the best result for binary networks at that time. The authors' own results with high-precision synapses (99.42%) are indeed better than the 99.12% reported in [12], but this doesn't make their approach novel for binary weights as claimed.

The paper's experimental results are strong and well-documented, demonstrating impressive energy efficiency on TrueNorth. The 0.268 μJ per image for the single-network implementation significantly outperforms other low-power approaches (6 mJ per classification at 95% accuracy [16]). However, the novelty claims about the methodology and algorithm require substantial revision to accurately reflect the existing literature.

Strengths:
- Strong experimental results showing significant energy efficiency gains on TrueNorth
- Well-structured deployment approach for neuromorphic hardware
- Clear explanation of the probabilistic training methodology
- Useful comparison to previous approaches in the literature

Weaknesses:
- Overstated novelty claims that ignore key prior work
- Insufficient acknowledgment of similarities to existing probabilistic backpropagation approaches
- Inaccurate comparison to previous state-of-the-art results
- Lack of detailed discussion about how their method differs from Soudry et al. (2014)

This paper makes a valuable contribution to energy-efficient neuromorphic computing with compelling experimental results. However, the authors should revise their novelty claims to properly acknowledge prior work, particularly the expectation backpropagation approach by Soudry et al. (2014), which shares significant conceptual and methodological similarities with their proposed approach. The core algorithm is not as novel as presented, and this needs to be clarified for the reader.

The paper would benefit from a more precise discussion of how their approach differs from prior work, especially regarding the specific implementation details and the advantages of their constrained training methodology. The current presentation overstates the novelty of the algorithm while underestimating the significance of existing work in the field.

Overall, the technical contributions are sound and the results are impressive, but the paper's claims about novelty need to be tempered to align with the existing literature. With appropriate revisions to address these concerns, this would be a strong contribution to the field.
