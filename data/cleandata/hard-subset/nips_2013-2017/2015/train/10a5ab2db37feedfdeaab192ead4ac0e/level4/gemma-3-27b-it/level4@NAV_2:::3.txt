The authors present an interesting paper concerning a backpropagation training method utilizing spike probabilities, explicitly considering the hardware constraints of neuromorphic platforms featuring spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This addresses a topic of current interest – mapping deep networks onto specialized, multi-neuron hardware.

**Quality:** The proposed training method is valuable, particularly given the rise of novel multi-neuron hardware platforms with inherent constraints. The technical approach appears sound and the results demonstrate a functional system. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the computational cost associated with the probabilistic synaptic connection updates during training. Are there scalability limitations? A more detailed analysis of training time compared to standard backpropagation would strengthen the contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, the claims made at the end of Section 1 could be reworded. As currently phrased, they might inadvertently suggest that the paper introduces, for the first time, a training methodology encompassing spiking neurons and synapses with reduced precision. The demonstration of running the trained network on the TrueNorth chip is best framed as a validation of the training method, rather than a novel contribution in itself. 

In Section 2, further clarification is needed regarding the meaning of “0.15 bits per synapse.” The network topology is described across multiple sections; consolidating this information into a single, dedicated subsection would improve readability. The discrepancy between the performance of the 30-core network’s single-member ensemble and the 5-core network warrants explanation. Why is the accuracy so significantly lower? Furthermore, the consistency of results across different ensembles of the 5-core network is curious and should be addressed. Providing information regarding the input spike rates during testing, and clarifying whether the input is a spike train, would be beneficial. There is a typographical error on line 399 ("approach approach").

**Originality:** The work builds on existing research in both backpropagation and neuromorphic computing. A more detailed comparison with related spiking backpropagation rules, such as SpikeProp [1], would help to contextualize the novelty of the proposed method. While the combination of techniques is valuable, explicitly highlighting the distinctions from prior work is crucial.

**Significance:** The development of training methods that account for hardware constraints is highly relevant. However, the current constraints are largely dictated by the TrueNorth architecture. A discussion of the generalizability of these constraints to other neuromorphic platforms would broaden the impact. The reliance on a 64-member ensemble to achieve high accuracy raises questions about resource requirements. Exploring techniques to improve single-ensemble performance would enhance the practical utility of the method. Overall, this is a well-executed and promising contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a viable training method for spiking neural networks compatible with neuromorphic hardware.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on MNIST within the constraints of the TrueNorth architecture.
*   Provides valuable insights into bridging the gap between backpropagation and neuromorphic computing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach could be better highlighted through a more thorough comparison with existing literature.
*   The limitations of the approach, particularly regarding computational cost and generalizability to other platforms, require further discussion.
*   The inconsistency in results between different network sizes and ensembles necessitates clarification.



[1] Rodney, S. A., & Hassoun, P. M. (1993). Spike-prop: Adaptive learning in spiking neural networks. *Biological Cybernetics*, *69*(4), 311–318.
