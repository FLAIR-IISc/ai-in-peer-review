

This paper presents a novel approach to bridge the gap between high-precision backpropagation training and binary-synapse spiking neural network (SNN) hardware deployment. The core idea—treating continuous weights as probabilities and sampling them to map trained networks onto spike-based devices—offers a compelling solution to a critical challenge in neuromorphic computing. The authors convincingly adapt backpropagation for this probabilistic sampling framework, validate the method on MNIST with competitive accuracy, and demonstrate successful deployment on the TrueNorth chip across two hardware-optimized variants (size-focused and accuracy-focused). The manuscript is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and technically sound, with a high-quality presentation of results.

However, the paper’s significance and practical impact require substantial clarification. While the method is technically sound, the work fails to articulate *why* this specific approach is transformative for real-world TrueNorth deployment. The authors do not specify concrete applications where this method provides a decisive advantage over existing alternatives (e.g., quantization, weight binarization, or alternative SNN training paradigms). Without this, the contribution risks being perceived as a niche technical tweak rather than a meaningful enabler for practical neuromorphic systems. The paper should explicitly address:  
- *Which specific applications* (e.g., edge AI, low-power sensors) would benefit most from this mapping strategy, and *why* this method is uniquely suited for them.  
- *How this approach compares* to alternatives cited in [9]–[12] (e.g., [9]’s weight binarization, [11]’s surrogate gradients), including quantitative trade-offs in accuracy, energy efficiency, or hardware overhead.  

The omission of key prior work further weakens the context. The authors should cite Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the arXiv:1506.05427 paper to position their hardware comparison within the broader landscape of neuromorphic platforms. These works directly address TrueNorth-like architectures and would strengthen the paper’s credibility and relevance.

Additionally, critical technical details are missing, undermining reproducibility and the reader’s confidence in the results. The authors should clarify:  
1. **Input spike conversion**: How are MNIST images transformed into spike trains (e.g., rate coding, temporal coding)? What encoding parameters (e.g., spike frequency, time window) are used?  
2. **Neuron and weight models**: Are neurons leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF) or another model? How are shared weights implemented (e.g., weight-sharing patterns, precision)?  
3. **Hardware specifics**: What are the exact chip configurations (e.g., number of cores, memory constraints) for the two variants? How were accuracy metrics computed on-chip (e.g., post-spike decoding)?  

The validation on TrueNorth is promising but lacks depth. While the paper states "performance figures measured from the chip," it omits key metrics like energy per inference, latency, or power consumption—data essential for evaluating hardware utility. Without these, the claimed advantages over conventional implementations remain unquantified.

The paper’s core innovation—probabilistic weight sampling for binary synapses—is highly original and could generalize to other neuromorphic platforms (e.g., Loihi, SpiNNaker). This potential is hinted at but not leveraged. A stronger discussion of broader applicability (e.g., "This framework could enable similar mappings for other binary-synapse hardware") would elevate the work beyond TrueNorth-specific contributions.

In summary, the paper addresses a significant problem with a technically rigorous solution, and the writing is excellent. But it falls short in demonstrating *why* this work matters for practitioners and how it advances beyond existing methods. The significance is buried, critical details are omitted, and related work is incomplete. To meet NeurIPS’ standards, the authors must:  
1. Explicitly define the practical impact with concrete use cases and comparative advantages.  
2. Provide missing technical specifications (input encoding, neuron models, hardware metrics).  
3. Integrate the cited prior work (Qiao et al., arXiv:1506.05427) into the discussion.  

With these revisions, the paper would become a valuable contribution to neuromorphic computing. As it stands, the significance and completeness require major revision. The technical foundation is strong, but the narrative must shift from "this works" to "this enables real-world deployment in ways others cannot."
