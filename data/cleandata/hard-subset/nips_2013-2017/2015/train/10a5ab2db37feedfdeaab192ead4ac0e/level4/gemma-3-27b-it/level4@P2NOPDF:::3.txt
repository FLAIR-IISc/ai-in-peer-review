This paper presents an interesting backpropagation training method for spiking neural networks, specifically designed with the hardware constraints of spiking neuron and discrete synapse platforms in mind. This work addresses a timely and important topic – mapping deep networks onto emerging multi-neuron hardware.

**Quality:** The proposed training method is valuable, particularly given the increasing development of constrained, multi-neuron hardware platforms. The approach offers a practical solution for training networks intended for deployment on such systems.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and accessible. However, the claims made towards the end of Section 1 could be rephrased. As currently stated, they potentially imply a novel *first* demonstration of training spiking networks with reduced-precision synapses, which may be misleading. While the work is a solid contribution, novelty lies in the *combination* of probabilistic training within specific hardware constraints, rather than the individual components themselves. Furthermore, demonstrating the network’s functionality on the TN platform is best framed as a validation of the training method, rather than a primary novel contribution. 

Section 2 raises several questions. The meaning of “0.15 bits per synapse” requires clarification. A consolidated description of the network topology would improve understanding. It's important to quantify the increased training time introduced by the probabilistic synaptic update scheme. The unexpectedly low performance of the 30-core ensemble, compared to the 5-core network, needs to be addressed. Similarly, an explanation for the consistent performance across different ensembles of the 5-core network would be helpful. Providing the input spike rates during testing, and clarifying whether the input is presented as a spike train, is crucial for reproducibility and interpretation. Finally, there is a minor error on line 399 (“approach approach”), and it’s important to note that Reference 12 employs training constraints (e.g., bias=0) and is therefore not solely a “train-then-constrain” methodology.

**Originality:** To strengthen the paper, the authors should discuss and compare their approach to other established spiking backpropagation methods, such as SpikeProp. Highlighting the specific advantages of their method in the context of hardware constraints would be beneficial.

**Significance:** Developing training methods cognizant of hardware limitations is highly significant. However, the constraints considered in this work are tied to the specific TN architecture and may not generalize to all spiking hardware platforms. The requirement for a 64-ensemble to achieve desired accuracy implies considerable hardware resource demands, a point that should be discussed further. In summary, this is a well-executed and potentially impactful paper focusing on a training method for spiking neural networks that addresses the practical challenges posed by specific hardware constraints.
