The paper proposes a method to reconcile the high-precision continuous variables used in backpropagation with spike-based hardware that supports only binary synapses. The key idea is to interpret analog variables as probabilities and sample them to map an offline-trained network onto a spiking neural network for deployment. The authors adapt backpropagation to accommodate this probabilistic mapping and validate the approach on MNIST. They further demonstrate deployment on the TrueNorth chip by mapping the trained network onto two configurations—one optimized for size and one for accuracy—and report performance figures measured directly on hardware. The manuscript is of high quality and clearly written. The approach is original and appears transferable to other methods and hardware platforms.

That said, the paper does not sufficiently articulate its practical significance. It remains unclear how the proposed method will concretely facilitate deployment of TrueNorth in real applications, and in which specific application domains this would matter most. In addition, the paper does not clearly establish whether, and to what extent, this approach offers advantages over alternative strategies, including those cited in [9]–[12]. A more explicit comparison—conceptual and, if possible, empirical—would help position the contribution.

The results are convincing, but important implementation details are missing. In particular, please clarify:
- How inputs are converted into spikes (encoding scheme, temporal windowing).
- The spike rates or frequency ranges used during inference and any constraints imposed by the hardware.
- The neuron models employed on-chip and any deviations from the models assumed during training.
- The values and scheme for shared weights, if applicable, and how sharing interacts with the probabilistic mapping.
These details are critical for assessing reproducibility and for understanding the trade-offs between the two deployment configurations (size versus accuracy).

When reviewing related neuromorphic hardware platforms, the authors should also cite Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015), and the work at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427, which are relevant to the landscape surveyed.

Overall, the paper is well structured, clear, and to the point. Demonstrating an end-to-end mapping from backprop-trained networks to a commercial-scale neuromorphic device is a strong asset. Addressing the questions above and better articulating the practical impact and comparative advantages would substantially strengthen the contribution.
