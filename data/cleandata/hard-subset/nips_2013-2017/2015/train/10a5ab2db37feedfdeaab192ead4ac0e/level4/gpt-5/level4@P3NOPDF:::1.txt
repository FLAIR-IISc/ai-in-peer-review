Updated assessment after author feedback: Thank you for engaging with the concerns. The paper presents strong and, on the experimental side, novel results—particularly in accuracy and reported power consumption. However, the general methodology and the core algorithm appear less novel than claimed, and several statements about prior work and state-of-the-art should be corrected for accuracy.

Summary and strengths
- Compelling empirical performance, with competitive accuracy and clear attention to power/efficiency.
- The experimental section is thorough and, if fully reproducible, would be a valuable contribution.

Major concerns about novelty and relation to prior work
1) Algorithmic overlap with prior art (Section 3.2):
- The algorithm in Section 3.2 appears to be a hybrid of Zhao, Shawe-Taylor, and van Daalen (Neural Networks, 1996) [14] and Soudry, Hubara, and Meir, “Expectation Backpropagation” (NIPS 2014) [15]. The derivation seems to rely on essentially the same approximations as those works.
- Requested action: Explicitly acknowledge this overlap, carefully delineate what is identical, what is a recombination, and what is genuinely new. A side-by-side mapping of your update rules and approximations to those in [14] and [15] would greatly clarify the contribution.

2) “Constrain-then-train” novelty claim:
- The first paragraph of the Discussion gives the impression that learning a weight distribution and then sampling from it to form a fixed ensemble at test time is novel. This “constrain-then-train” approach was already suggested and empirically tested in [15].
- Requested action: Revise the text to credit [15] for this methodology, and clarify any substantive differences in your instantiation (e.g., objective, constraints, or inference scheme).

3) Misleading characterization of expectation backpropagation:
- Novelty claim (i) in the Introduction is misleading. Expectation Backpropagation (EP) in [15] is a backpropagation-style update rule derived via expectation propagation. It was implemented for binary neurons (sign activation, with zero input almost surely not attained) with binary weights, and can readily accommodate constrained connectivity.
- Requested action: Correct the description of EP and adjust the novelty claims accordingly. If there are theoretical or practical barriers that prevent EP from matching your setup, please specify them precisely.

4) Incorrect statements about prior state-of-the-art:
- To the best of my knowledge, the prior best results for binary weights and binary neurons were achieved by [15] on MNIST without data augmentation, which matches your evaluation protocol.
- Requested action: Correct the SOTA claims and include a direct, like-for-like comparison to [15] under identical training and evaluation settings (no augmentation), including error bars where possible.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper
- Clarify what is new:
  • If the key novelty lies in engineering choices (e.g., specific constraints, implementation optimizations, or power measurement methodology), say so explicitly and separate these from algorithmic novelties.  
  • If there is a new derivational step, theorem, or approximation, highlight it precisely and show its empirical impact via ablations.

- Provide a detailed relationship-to-prior-work section:
  • Map each approximation and update in Section 3.2 to corresponding steps in [14] and [15].  
  • Explain how “constrain-then-train” here differs from [15] beyond problem framing or notation.

- Add head-to-head baselines:
  • Implement or cite a faithful reproduction of [15] and compare on all datasets where you claim superiority, matched for data preprocessing, augmentation (or lack thereof), and model capacity.  
  • If you employ constrained connectivity, show EP with analogous constraints.

- Clarify modeling details affecting comparability:
  • State explicitly whether neurons use the sign activation; discuss how zero-input cases are handled (if at all) and how gradients or surrogate gradients are computed.  
  • Describe any stochasticity (e.g., sampling of weights/activations) used during training and testing, and how ensembles are formed at test time.

- Reproducibility and reporting:
  • Provide complete hyperparameter settings, training schedules, and initialization details.  
  • For power measurements, describe the setup, metrics (e.g., average vs. peak), and measurement methodology to enable replication.

Minor comments
- Fix typographical errors (e.g., “backpropgation” → “backpropagation”).  
- Ensure all references are complete and consistently formatted.

Overall evaluation
- The empirical results are strong and could be impactful, especially given the reported gains in power efficiency. However, the current framing overstates novelty and under-cites direct antecedents. If the paper is revised to (i) accurately position the work relative to [14] and [15], (ii) correct the state-of-the-art claims, and (iii) include rigorous head-to-head comparisons under matched conditions, I would be inclined to support acceptance on the strength of the experimental contribution. Without these changes, the paper risks misrepresenting prior art and obscuring what is genuinely new.
