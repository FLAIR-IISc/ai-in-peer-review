This paper presents a method for training neural networks with binary weights, yielding impressive experimental results in terms of both accuracy and potential power consumption. However, the paper's claims regarding the novelty of its methodology and algorithm are significantly overstated, and its comparison to prior work is based on an inaccurate representation of the state-of-the-art.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Algorithmic Novelty:** The core training algorithm presented in Section 3.2 bears a strong resemblance to previously published work, which is not adequately acknowledged. Specifically, the final form of the algorithm appears to be a direct hybrid of the methods proposed by Zhao et al. [14] and Soudry et al. [15], derived using nearly identical approximations. The authors should explicitly discuss the relationship between their algorithm and these prior works, clarifying what, if any, novel technical contributions their derivation provides.

    Furthermore, novelty claim (i) in the introduction is misleading. The claim is that the paper introduces a "backpropagation-compatible" learning rule for networks with binary weights and constrained connectivity. However, the Expectation Backpropagation (EBP) algorithm from [15] already provides a backpropagation update rule, derived from expectation propagation, for training networks with binary weights and binary neurons (i.e., sign activation function). The EBP framework can also readily accommodate constrained connectivity. This prior work substantially preempts the novelty claimed by the authors in this area.

2.  **Methodological Framing:** The "constrain-then-train" methodology, described in the discussion as a key contribution, is also not novel. This approach, which involves learning a weight distribution during training and then sampling from it to produce a fixed network ensemble for inference, was previously proposed and experimentally validated in [15]. The paper gives the impression that this is a new concept, which is inaccurate. The authors should revise their discussion to properly attribute this methodology to prior work and situate their own contribution more accurately within that established context.

3.  **Incorrect State-of-the-Art Comparison:** The paper's experimental evaluation is weakened by its comparison against outdated or inappropriate baselines. The authors claim state-of-the-art results, but they fail to compare against the most relevant and powerful prior work. To my knowledge, the best previously published results for networks with both binary weights and binary neurons on the MNIST dataset (without data augmentation), the same experimental setting used here, were achieved by the algorithm in [15]. A direct comparison with the results reported in that work is necessary to properly contextualize the performance of the proposed method and validate claims of superiority. Without this comparison, the significance of the experimental results is difficult to assess.

**Summary:**

While the empirical performance demonstrated in this paper is strong, the work suffers from a significant lack of novelty in its core algorithm and general methodology. The contributions are framed in a way that overlooks direct and substantial precedents in the literature. To strengthen the paper, the authors must thoroughly revise their claims of novelty, properly situate their work with respect to references [14] and [15], and compare their experimental results against the true state-of-the-art baseline established by [15].
