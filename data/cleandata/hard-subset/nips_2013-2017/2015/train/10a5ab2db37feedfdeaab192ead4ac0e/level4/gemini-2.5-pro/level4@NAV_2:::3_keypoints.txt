- Proposes training spiking neural networks for neuromorphic hardware by treating spikes and binary synapses as continuous probabilities during backpropagation and sampling networks for deployment.  
- Strongly addresses co-design of training algorithms and architectures for constrained neuromorphic hardware.  
- “Constrain-then-train” methodology is well-motivated and aligns training improvements with hardware performance.  
- Experimental validation on IBM TrueNorth is convincing: 99.42% MNIST with 64-network ensemble and 92.7% at 0.268 µJ/image for a single network.  
- Novelty wording is ambiguous; clarify that the contribution is the unified framework combining spiking neurons, binary synapses, and hardware-constrained connectivity, not the first backprop training of spiking/low-precision networks.  
- Clarify and justify the “0.15 bits per synapse” lookup-table metric and show how it is derived.  
- Consolidate the network topology description currently spread across Sections 4 and 5 into a single, detailed location.  
- Report computational cost and training time compared to standard backpropagation for context.  
- Provide input-encoding details for reproducibility: spike rates, rate vs single-spike encoding, and the 1 ms time-step behavior during testing.  
- Explain why a single-member 30-core network underperforms relative to a single-member 5-core network.  
- Explain why the 5-core network’s accuracy plateaus quickly and shows similar accuracy across ensemble sizes.  
- Contrast the probabilistic training method with other backpropagation-inspired spiking training rules (e.g., SpikeProp and variants).  
- Soften the characterization of prior work as purely “train-then-constrain” and note examples (e.g., Ref [12]) that incorporate hardware constraints during training; fix minor typos.  
- Discuss the method’s generality and steps required to adapt it to other neuromorphic platforms with different constraints.  
- Highlight the practical limitation that peak accuracy requires a 64-member ensemble, implying a 64× increase in inference hardware resources and an important accuracy–cost trade-off.
