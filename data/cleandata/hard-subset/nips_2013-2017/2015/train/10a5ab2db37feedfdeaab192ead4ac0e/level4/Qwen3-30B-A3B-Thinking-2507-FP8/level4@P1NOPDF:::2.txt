

The authors propose a novel approach to bridge the high-precision requirements of backpropagation with the binary synapse constraints of spike-based neural network hardware. They introduce a method where continuous backpropagation variables are treated as probabilities, sampled to map an off-line trained network onto a spiking hardware device. The paper details an adapted backpropagation algorithm compatible with this strategy, validates it on MNIST, and demonstrates successful deployment on the TrueNorth chip across two optimized architectures (prioritizing size and accuracy), reporting measured chip performance. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, technically sound, and clearly structured. The core idea is original and holds potential relevance for broader hardware platforms beyond the specific context.

However, the paper would benefit from several clarifications. First, the significance of the work is not sufficiently articulated. It remains unclear how this method specifically enables practical deployment of TrueNorth in real-world applications, nor are concrete use cases provided. Second, the advantages of this approach over existing alternatives (such as those referenced in [9]–[12]) are not adequately demonstrated or quantified. A comparative analysis would strengthen the contribution. Third, critical implementation details are missing: how input data is converted to spikes, the specific spike frequencies employed, the shared weight values used in mapping, and the exact neuron model details. These omissions hinder reproducibility and limit the reader's ability to assess the method's practical feasibility.

The authors should also cite relevant prior work on neuromorphic hardware platforms, specifically Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the arXiv preprint (arXiv:1506.05427), which address similar challenges. While the results are convincing for MNIST, the lack of discussion on the method's generalizability to more complex tasks or datasets weakens the broader impact claim. The paper effectively demonstrates technical feasibility but would be significantly strengthened by explicitly addressing the practical utility, comparative advantages, and implementation specifics mentioned above. Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear potential, but its full impact requires addressing these points.
