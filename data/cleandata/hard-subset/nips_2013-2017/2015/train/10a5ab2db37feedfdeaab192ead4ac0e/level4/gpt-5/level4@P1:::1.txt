1) The algorithm described in Section 3.2 is very similar to those in [14] J. Zhao, J. Shawe-Taylor, and M. van Daalen, “Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks,” Neural Networks, 1996, and [15] Soudry, Hubara, and Meir, “Expectation Backpropagation: Parameter-free training of multilayer neural networks with continuous or discrete weights,” NIPS 2014. In its final form, the presented method appears to be a hybrid of these two approaches and relies on the same approximations. The paper should explicitly acknowledge this connection.

2) The first paragraph of the Discussion suggests that the “constrain-then-train” methodology—learning a distribution over weights and then sampling from it to form a fixed ensemble at test time—is novel. However, this idea was already proposed and empirically evaluated in [15]. Please revise the text to reflect this prior work.

3) Novelty claim (i) in the Introduction is also misleading. The “expectation backpropagation” (EBP) algorithm of [15] is itself a backpropagation-style update rule (derived via expectation propagation), implemented for binary neurons (i.e., a sign activation, where zero input is never reached exactly) with binary weights, and can readily accommodate constrained connectivity. This reduces the novelty of the current claim that backpropagation can be used to train spiking/binary networks with limited precision and connectivity.

4) The cited previous state-of-the-art results appear to be incorrect. To the best of my knowledge, the strongest prior results for binary weights (and neurons) on MNIST were obtained by the algorithm in [15], which was evaluated on MNIST without data augmentation, as was done in this paper (see http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf). Please verify and correct the comparisons.

Edited after authors’ feedback:
Thank you for agreeing to address these concerns. This paper presents strong and novel experimental results in terms of both accuracy and power consumption on neuromorphic hardware. However, the general methodology and the specific training algorithm are not as novel as currently claimed. I encourage the authors to (i) clarify the relationship to [14] and [15], (ii) temper the novelty claims regarding “constrain-then-train,” and (iii) correct the state-of-the-art comparisons. Doing so will better position the paper’s real contribution: a careful instantiation and compelling demonstration of these ideas on TrueNorth with impressive energy–accuracy trade-offs.
