1) In Section 3.2, the authors should acknowledge the strong similarity between their algorithm and the approaches presented in [14] J. Zhao, J. Shawe-Taylor, and M. van Daalen, "Learning in stochastic bit stream neural networks," Neural Networks, vol. 9, no. 6, pp. 991 - 998, 1996 and [15] Soudry, Daniel, Itay Hubara, and Ron Meir. "Expectation backpropagation: Parameter-free training of multilayer neural networks with continuous or discrete weights." Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems. 2014. The presented method represents a hybrid of these algorithms and relies on identical approximations.

2) The opening paragraph of the discussion section gives the impression that the “constrain-then-train” methodology – learning a weight distribution and subsequently sampling from it to generate a fixed network ensemble for testing – is novel. However, this approach was previously suggested and tested in ref. [15].

3) The novelty claim (i) in the final paragraph of the introduction is also misleading. The “expectation backpropagation” algorithm described in ref. [15] is a backpropagation update rule (utilizing expectation propagation) implemented for binary neurons (employing a sign activation function, where zero input is not precisely reached) with binary weights and is readily adaptable to constrained connectivity.

4) The cited previous state-of-the-art results appear inaccurate. To the best of my knowledge, the highest previously reported performance for binary weights and neurons was achieved using the algorithm in ref. [15], which demonstrated results on the MNIST dataset (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf) without data augmentation, unlike the current work.
