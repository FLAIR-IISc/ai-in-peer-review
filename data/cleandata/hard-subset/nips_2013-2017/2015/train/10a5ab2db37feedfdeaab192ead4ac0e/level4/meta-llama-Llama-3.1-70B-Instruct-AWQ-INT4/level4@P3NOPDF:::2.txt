The authors propose a novel approach to reconcile the need for high precision of continuous variables in backpropagation algorithms with the desire to use spike-based neural network devices with binary synapses. They introduce the idea of considering analog variables as probabilities that are sampled to map the neural network used offline for training to the spiking network chip used for deployment of the application. This strategy allows for the adaptation of the backpropagation algorithm to be compatible with spike-based neural networks.

The authors validate their method on the MNIST benchmark, demonstrating its effectiveness in mapping the trained network onto two different architectures, optimized for size and accuracy. They present performance figures measured from the chip, showcasing the potential of their approach. The quality of the manuscript is high, and its clarity is commendable. The work is original and has the potential to be useful for other approaches and hardware platforms.

In reviewing the different hardware platforms available, the authors may also consider mentioning the work of Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the paper available at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. These additional references could provide a more comprehensive overview of the existing landscape.

One area for improvement is highlighting the significance of the work. The authors could elaborate on how their method will be useful in deploying the TrueNorth chip in practical applications and specific use cases. Furthermore, it is not entirely clear how this method compares to alternative options, such as those cited in references [9] to [12]. A more detailed discussion on the advantages and limitations of their approach would strengthen the paper.

The authors present a well-structured and clear method for applying backpropagation to spiking neural networks with binary synapses. The results are convincing, but some details are lacking. For instance, the authors could provide more information on how inputs are converted into spikes, the frequencies used, the shared weight values, and the neuron models employed. Including these details would enhance the reproducibility and understanding of their approach.

Overall, the authors have made a significant contribution to the field, and with some revisions to address the mentioned points, their work has the potential to make a more substantial impact.
