This paper introduces a backpropagation training method for spiking neural networks that utilizes spike probabilities and incorporates the hardware constraints of a specific platform featuring spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This work addresses the highly relevant topic of mapping deep learning models onto neuromorphic hardware.

**Quality:** The proposed training methodology holds significant value, particularly in the context of emerging multi-neuron hardware platforms that have inherent operational constraints.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. However, several points could be clarified:
*   The claims at the end of Section 1 should be rephrased. In their current form, they could be misinterpreted as suggesting this is the first training methodology for spiking neurons with reduced-precision synapses.
*   The demonstration on the TrueNorth (TN) hardware is a strong validation of the method, but it might be more accurately framed as such, rather than as a primary novel contribution.
*   In Section 2, could the authors clarify the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse"?
*   A consolidated, clear description of the network topology is needed in one location.
*   What is the training time overhead introduced by the probabilistic synaptic connection update?
*   An explanation is needed for why the performance of a single 30-core network ensemble is notably lower than that of the 5-core network. Conversely, why is the performance so consistent across different ensembles of the 5-core network?
*   The nature of the inputs during testing should be specified. Are spike trains used, and if so, what are their corresponding spike rates?
*   Regarding prior work, Reference [12] (line 399) also includes constraints (e.g., bias=0) during training, making it more than a pure "train-then-constrain" method. Note also a typo on this line ("approach approach").

**Originality:** To better contextualize the contribution, the paper would benefit from a discussion and comparison with other backpropagation-based learning rules for spiking networks, such as SpikeProp.

**Significance:** The development of training methods that account for hardware constraints is a significant and timely research direction. While the constraints considered here are specific to the TrueNorth architecture, which may limit the method's direct applicability to other platforms, the work is nonetheless valuable. The reliance on a 64-member ensemble to achieve high accuracy implies a substantial trade-off in required hardware resources, which warrants discussion. Overall, this is an interesting paper on a training method that effectively integrates the hardware constraints of a particular neuromorphic platform.
