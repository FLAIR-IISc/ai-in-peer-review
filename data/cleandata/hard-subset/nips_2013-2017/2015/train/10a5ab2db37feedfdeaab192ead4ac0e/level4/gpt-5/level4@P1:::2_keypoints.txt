- Proposes training continuous-valued proxy networks by treating neuron outputs and synapses as probabilities, then sampling spikes/synapse on/off states to map to low-precision spiking hardware; ensembles average multiple samples to boost accuracy.
- Demonstrated on MNIST and deployed on IBM TrueNorth using two architectures optimized for compactness and accuracy, with performance measured directly on chip.
- Extends prior work by combining spiking neurons, ultra-low-precision synapses, constrained connectivity, and an explicit training-to-deployment mapping; suggests adding Qiao et al. (Frontiers 2015) and arXiv:1506.05427 to hardware survey.
- Methodologically sound: probabilistic forward model (Gaussian approximation of summed Bernoulli inputs) and derived gradients are coherent; constrained initialization for TrueNorth is sensible.
- Results are convincing with direct on-chip measurements and a clear accuracy–energy tradeoff across topologies and ensemble sizes.
- Reproducibility concern: missing exact input-to-spike encoding scheme and spike rates.
- Reproducibility concern: missing TrueNorth neuron model details (leak, threshold, reset, dynamics).
- Reproducibility concern: missing the actual set of shared synaptic strength values and axon-type distribution used.
- Reproducibility concern: missing any constraints on firing rates or refractory periods during deployment.
- Validation concern: unclear whether the Gaussian approximation accuracy was empirically validated in this regime.
- Clarity is good overall; adding missing implementation details and a pseudo-code algorithm in the supplement would help reproducibility.
- Originality: novel probabilistic reinterpretation of spikes/synaptic on/off states for backprop and a useful “constrain-then-train” conceptual contribution.
- Practical significance is underplayed: unclear target application scenarios and when this method outperforms alternatives.
- Authors should articulate concrete application domains and comparative advantages (accuracy, energy, latency, memory footprint, deployment ease) versus other spiking/conversion methods.
- Need discussion on scalability beyond MNIST (e.g., natural images, audio) and anticipated bottlenecks.
- Requested clarification: detailed spike encoding, time budget per inference, and measured firing statistics.
- Requested clarification: specify neuron parameters and synapse strength values used on TrueNorth.
- Requested experiments: ablations on ensemble size vs accuracy/energy and sensitivity to dropout, data augmentation, and learning-rate schedule.
- Requested discussion: generalization to convolutional topologies and other neuromorphic platforms; add the recommended hardware citations.
- Requested discussion: when constrain-then-train outperforms train-then-constrain under specific hardware constraints.
- Pros: clear, well-structured manuscript; principled learning-to-deployment pipeline; original probabilistic formulation; real hardware deployment showing meaningful accuracy–efficiency tradeoffs; likely useful to other platforms.
- Cons: practical significance and application scenarios not well articulated; limited comparison to alternatives; missing implementation details hinder reproducibility; evaluation limited to MNIST.
- Overall: strong and timely contribution bridging deep learning and neuromorphic computing with elegant probabilistic backprop and on-chip validation, but impact would increase with fuller experimental details, explicit comparisons, and clearer practical significance.
