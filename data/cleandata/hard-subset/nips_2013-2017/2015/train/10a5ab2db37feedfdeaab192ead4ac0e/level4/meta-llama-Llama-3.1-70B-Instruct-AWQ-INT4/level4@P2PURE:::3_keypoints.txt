- Paper introduces a backpropagation training method using spike probabilities that accounts for hardware constraints of a spiking-neuron, discrete-synapse platform relevant for mapping deep networks to multi-neuron hardware.

- Proposed method is valuable for new multi-neuron hardware platforms with constraints.

- Clarity is generally good, but the claims in Section 1 should avoid implying the approach is the first to train spiking, low-precision networks; emphasize hardware-constraint consideration instead.

- Demonstrating the network on the TrueNorth (TN) chip should be framed as validation of the training method, not as a novel contribution.

- Section 2 needs clarification: the meaning of “0.15 bits per synapse” is unclear.

- Section 2 lacks a concise description of the network topology.

- Report the training-time overhead caused by probabilistic synaptic updates.

- Explain why a single ensemble of the 30-core network yields surprisingly low results compared to the 5-core network, while 5-core ensembles show little variation.

- Provide input spike rates during testing and clarify whether inputs are spike trains.

- Note that Reference 12 implies training constraints (e.g., bias=0), so the approach is not a simple train-then-constrain method; also fix the typo “approach approach.”

- Add discussion or comparison with other spiking backpropagation rules (e.g., SpikeProp).

- Significance: hardware-aware training methods are important, but the constraints are TN-specific and a 64-ensemble configuration may demand substantially more hardware to reach desired accuracy.
