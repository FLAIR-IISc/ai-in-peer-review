Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a practical bridge between backpropagation-based training and energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware with spiking neurons and low-precision (binary) synapses. The key idea is to interpret continuous inputs, neuron outputs, and synaptic connectivity as probabilities in [0,1], enabling standard backpropagation in a differentiable surrogate space. After training, the network is mapped to hardware by sampling synaptic on/off states and input spikes according to these learned probabilities. Multiple sampled networks can be combined via ensemble averaging to improve accuracy. The authors demonstrate the approach on IBM’s TrueNorth chip, report accuracy/energy trade-offs on MNIST with two network topologies (5-core and 30-core), and provide on-chip measurements.

This work connects several threads: prior backpropagation with spiking neurons but high-precision weights [9–12], low-precision synapses with continuous neurons [13], and probabilistic/binary neural networks with full connectivity [14–15]. The novelty lies in jointly handling spiking neurons, sub-trinary synaptic precision (binary crossbar with small shared s-value lookup), and constrained connectivity, while training directly under deployment constraints (“constrain-then-train”) and demonstrating on-chip energy measurements.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The probabilistic surrogate model uses a Gaussian approximation for weighted sums of Bernoulli variables and derives gradients with respect to synaptic connection probabilities and biases. The paper justifies dropping a higher-order term in the derivative under balanced weights and large fan-in. While reasonable, the approach would benefit from ablations quantifying the impact of this approximation (e.g., accuracy vs. including the full gradient, sensitivity to violations of independence/balance).
- Experimental support: Results on MNIST are strong (up to 99.42% with 64-member ensemble; 92.7% with a single sampled network) and include real-time, on-chip energy measurements (down to 0.268 µJ per classification at 1 kHz throughput). However, some important experimental details are missing (see below).
- Completeness: The study focuses on MNIST; additional datasets or analysis of generalization to more complex vision tasks would strengthen the claims about practical deployment.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a careful description of hardware constraints, the probabilistic training formulation, and the network topology. The initialization rationale (decorrelating columns under balanced ± weights) is insightful. That said, several implementation details are under-specified:
- Input spike encoding (exact mapping from pixel intensity to spike probability; any pre-processing/normalization).
- Timing and firing-rate assumptions (single 1 ms step; whether multi-step rate coding was tested).
- Neuron model parameters on hardware (leak, threshold, reset, refractory) and any deviations from the “history-free” thresholding approximation.
- s-value choices per axon type and per neuron (quantized levels, positive/negative balancing, and how lookup tables are shared).
- Energy measurement methodology (instrumentation, how leakage is apportioned across used cores; absolute active/leakage power).

Originality
The “constrain-then-train” probabilistic formulation that directly reflects binary synapses, spiking outputs, and limited fan-in is a meaningful advance over train-then-quantize/spike conversion. The ensemble sampling of trained probabilities to form multiple hardware networks is a practical and elegant way to trade energy for accuracy. Relative to expectation backpropagation and stochastic bitstream networks, the constrained connectivity and explicit TrueNorth mapping are new and relevant.

Significance
The reported energy/accuracy trade-offs on real hardware are impressive and, to my knowledge, set a new bar for MNIST on spiking systems at the time of writing. This is a credible path toward deploying deep learning ideas on ultra-low-power neuromorphic platforms. However, the broader impact would be clearer with:
- Evidence beyond MNIST or with convolutional architectures.
- A more detailed comparison to alternative training-to-spiking pipelines [9–12], including accuracy, on-chip energy, and latency.
- Discussion of application domains where single-pass (1 ms) inference and the achieved single-network accuracy are sufficient, versus when ensembles or longer integration are required.

Strengths
- Clear, principled training framework that maps naturally to spiking hardware with binary synapses.
- Strong on-chip results and transparent reporting of throughput and energy.
- Thoughtful weight initialization to reduce redundancy and respect hardware constraints.
- Flexible accuracy/energy trade-off via ensemble size.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope: Only MNIST; no convolutional layers. A small-scale convolutional experiment or a second dataset would strengthen significance.
- Missing details: Please specify input encoding, neuron model parameters, s-value quantization per axon type, and exact energy measurement protocol.
- Ablations: Show sensitivity to ensemble size; variance across different samplings; effect of longer integration times; contribution of the initialization scheme (balanced ± weights) and of dropping the higher-order gradient term.
- Fair comparisons: Ensure accuracy baselines versus [9–12], [15], and other low-power platforms use consistent data augmentation and evaluation. Report confidence intervals.
- Practicality: Discuss target applications and operating points (e.g., when is 92.7% single-network accuracy adequate? what latency/energy budgets are typical?).
- Related work coverage: Please also cite Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015) and arXiv:1506.05427 when surveying neuromorphic hardware and low-power spiking implementations.

Minor comments
- Fix minor typos (e.g., “apriori,” “well-approximated,” “backpropgation”).
- Clarify whether biases are implemented solely via leak and how fractional parts are handled.
- Provide the exact mapping between class scores and output neuron averages, and whether thresholds are learned or fixed.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and practical training method that aligns tightly with neuromorphic constraints.
- Strong empirical evidence on real hardware with state-of-the-art energy efficiency.
- Clear presentation and potential applicability to other spiking platforms.

Arguments against acceptance
- Evaluation limited to MNIST without convolution; uncertain generality to harder tasks.
- Some theoretical approximations are not empirically validated via ablations.
- Important implementation details and fairness of comparisons need clarification.

Overall assessment
A well-executed and timely contribution that credibly advances learning for neuromorphic computing by aligning training with deployment constraints and demonstrating excellent energy/accuracy trade-offs on silicon. With added details, ablations, and broader evaluation or convolutional results, this work would be a strong fit for NIPS.
