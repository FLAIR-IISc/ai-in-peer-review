The authors propose a method to reconcile the high-precision requirements of continuous variables in backpropagation with the use of spike-based neural network hardware featuring binary synapses. They reinterpret the analog variables as probabilities that are sampled to transfer the network trained offline to the spiking chip used for application deployment. They detail how to adapt backpropagation to align with this scheme and validate the approach on the MNIST benchmark. They map the trained model onto two different architectures, optimizing respectively for size and for accuracy, and report performance figures measured directly on the chip. The manuscript is of very high quality and clarity. The contribution is original and potentially beneficial for other methods and hardware platforms. In reviewing available hardware, the authors should also cite Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015, and the paper at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. The significance of the work is not adequately emphasized. It remains unclear how this method will enable practical deployments of the TrueNorth chip and in which specific applications. It is also not clear whether, and to what extent, this method offers advantages over alternative approaches such as those cited in [9]–[12].

The authors present a technique for applying backpropagation to spiking neural networks with binary synapses and validate it by successfully mapping the trained network onto the TrueNorth device. The paper is well structured, clear, and concise. The results are convincing but omit some important details (e.g., how inputs are converted into spikes, the firing rates used, the shared weight values, the neuron models, etc.).
