## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission

This paper presents an interesting investigation into training deep neural networks with binary weights and activations. The authors demonstrate strong experimental results, achieving competitive accuracy while significantly reducing power consumption. However, while the experimental validation is compelling, the paper overstates the novelty of both the methodology and the core algorithm. I recommend acceptance with major revisions addressing the points outlined below.

My primary concern lies with the presentation of novelty. The "constrain-then-train" methodology – learning a weight distribution and subsequently sampling to generate a fixed network ensemble at test time – is not, as the discussion suggests (paragraph 1), a novel contribution of this work. This approach was previously proposed and evaluated in Soudry et al. [15]. 

Furthermore, the novelty claim (i) in the introduction’s final paragraph is misleading. The Expectation Propagation algorithm introduced in Soudry et al. [15] *is* a backpropagation update rule, leveraging expectation propagation specifically for training networks with binary neurons (utilizing a sign activation function where zero input is rarely, if ever, reached exactly) and binary weights. This algorithm is readily adaptable to constrained connectivity, effectively addressing the points made in the novelty claim.

The algorithm detailed in section 3.2 also bears a strong resemblance to existing work. The authors should explicitly acknowledge the deep similarities to Zhao et al. [14] and Soudry et al. [15]. The current algorithm appears to be a hybrid of these two approaches, derived using identical approximations. A clear discussion of these connections, and how this work builds upon or differs from them, is crucial.

Finally, the paper's comparison to prior state-of-the-art is inaccurate. To the best of my knowledge, the best previously published results for binary weights *and* neurons were achieved using the Expectation Propagation algorithm described in Soudry et al. [15]. Critically, these results were obtained on the MNIST dataset (as reported in http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf) *without* data augmentation. The current paper utilizes data augmentation, making a direct comparison challenging and potentially inflating the perceived improvement. A fairer comparison would involve reporting results on MNIST without data augmentation.

**Specific Comments and Suggestions:**

* **Section 1 (Introduction):** Revise the novelty claim (i) to accurately reflect the existing work on Expectation Propagation [15].
* **Section 2 (Discussion):** Rephrase the first paragraph to acknowledge the prior art regarding the "constrain-then-train" methodology [15].
* **Section 3.2 (Algorithm):** Add a paragraph explicitly discussing the relationship between the proposed algorithm and the methods presented in Zhao et al. [14] and Soudry et al. [15]. Explain how the current work integrates or extends these existing approaches.
* **Section 4 (Experiments):** Include results on MNIST *without* data augmentation to provide a more direct comparison to the existing state-of-the-art [15].
* **Throughout:** Ensure all claims of novelty are carefully substantiated and do not overshadow the contributions of related work.

**Overall:**

Despite the concerns regarding novelty, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of efficient deep learning. The experimental results are strong and demonstrate the potential of binary networks for low-power applications. Addressing the points raised above – particularly clarifying the relationship to prior work – will significantly strengthen the paper and ensure a more accurate and balanced presentation of its contributions.
