The authors propose a way to reconcile the high accuracy requirement of continuous variables in backpropagation algorithms with the desire to use binary-synaptic spike-based neural network devices. They suggest considering analog variables, which are used as samples to map the neural network used for offline training onto the spiking network chip used for application deployment. They demonstrate how the backpropagation algorithm can be adapted to this technique and validate the proposed method in the MNIST benchmark. They map the trained network into two different architectures, optimize for size and accuracy, and present performance statistics measured from the chip. The manuscript quality is very good, as is its clarity. The work is quite original and may be useful for other methods and other hardware platforms. However, when reviewing the various available HW platforms, the authors should also refer to the paper by Kiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015 and http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427. The paper does not highlight the significance of the work. It is not clear how the proposed method can be used to deploy True North chips in practical applications and in which specific applications it can be used. It is also not clear whether this specific method has advantages over the alternatives mentioned in [9] to [12] and to what extent.

The authors present a method for applying backpropagation to binary-synaptic spiking neural networks and verify it by successfully mapping the trained network onto a True North device. The paper is well-structured, clear, and specific. The results are convincing, but some details are missing (such as how the inputs are transformed into spikes, what frequency is used, shared weight values, neuron models, etc.).
