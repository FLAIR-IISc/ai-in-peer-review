The paper proposes a backpropagation-based training method that operates on spike probabilities and explicitly incorporates hardware constraints of a specific platform with spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This is timely given the growing interest in mapping deep networks to multi-neuron hardware.

Quality: The training approach is useful, especially for emerging multi-neuron hardware platforms with tight resource and precision constraints. Accounting for these constraints during training is a clear strength.

Clarity: The paper is generally easy to follow. However, the claims at the end of Section 1 should be rephrased; as written, they can be read as asserting a first demonstration of training with spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses. Also, I would not present running the network on TN as a novel contribution in itself; rather, execution on TN serves as a validation of the training method.

Section 2 raises several questions that need clarification:
- What precisely does “0.15 bits per synapse” mean?
- Please present the network topology clearly in a single location (layers, neuron counts, connectivity, fan-in/fan-out, quantization levels, etc.).
- How much additional training time is incurred by the probabilistic synaptic update mechanism?
- Why is the result from a single ensemble of the 30-core network so low, particularly compared to the 5-core network?
- Why do the different ensembles of the 5-core network produce similar performance?
- What are the input spike rates during testing? Is the input presented as a spike train?
- Reference 12 (line 399) imposes constraints during training (e.g., bias = 0), so it is not purely a train-then-constrain approach. Also, please fix the typo “approach approach” on this line.

Originality: Please include a discussion and empirical or conceptual comparison with other spiking backprop-type rules, such as SpikeProp. Highlight similarities, differences, and any advantages of your spike-probability formulation under hardware constraints.

Significance: Methods that integrate hardware constraints into training are of high interest. However, the constraints you incorporate appear tailored to the TN architecture and may not directly generalize to other platforms; a discussion of portability would strengthen the paper. Additionally, if the reported accuracy relies on a 64-ensemble, this implies significant hardware resources; please discuss the trade-off between accuracy, ensemble size, and resource usage, and whether similar gains can be achieved with fewer cores or ensembles.

Overall, this is an interesting and relevant paper on training with backpropagation while respecting platform-level constraints in spiking systems with discrete synapses. With the clarifications and comparisons above, the contribution would be much clearer and its impact easier to assess.
