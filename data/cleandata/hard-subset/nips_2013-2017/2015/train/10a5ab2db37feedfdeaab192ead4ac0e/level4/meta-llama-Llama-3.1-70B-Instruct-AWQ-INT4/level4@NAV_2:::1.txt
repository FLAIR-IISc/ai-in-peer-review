This paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks for energy-efficient neuromorphic computing, specifically on the TrueNorth chip. The authors propose a method to bridge the gap between backpropagation, which uses continuous-output neurons and synaptic weights, and neuromorphic designs, which employ spiking neurons and discrete synapses. They achieve this by treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, allowing training using standard backpropagation.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the network topology, training methodology, and mapping from the training network to the deployment network. The results are impressive, with a high-performance network achieving 99.42% accuracy on the MNIST dataset at 108 μJ per image, and a high-efficiency network achieving 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image.

However, I have some concerns regarding the novelty and originality of the approach. The algorithm presented in Section 3.2 bears a strong resemblance to the cited references [14] and [15], and the authors should acknowledge this similarity and provide more context on how their approach differs from these previous works.

Additionally, the "constrain-then-train" methodology, as described in the discussion, is not novel and has been previously suggested and tested in ref [15]. The authors should be more accurate in their novelty claims, particularly in the introduction, where they state that their approach is the first to demonstrate an offline training methodology using backpropagation to create a network that employs spiking neurons, synapses requiring less bits of precision than even trinary weights, and constrained connectivity.

Furthermore, the authors claim that their approach achieves the best accuracy to date on MNIST, but this is not entirely accurate. Ref [15] presents an algorithm that achieves comparable results on MNIST without data augmentation.

Despite these concerns, the paper presents strong experimental results, and the authors' approach to energy-efficient neuromorphic computing is well-motivated and timely. With some revisions to address these concerns, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
