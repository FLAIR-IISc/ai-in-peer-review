- Presents a backpropagation training method using spike probabilities that explicitly accounts for TN hardware constraints; timely for multi-neuron hardware mapping.
- Proposed training method is useful for constrained hardware, but running the network on TN is validation rather than a novel contribution.
- Paper is generally readable, but claims in Section 1 should be reworded to avoid implying a first demonstration of spiking/reduced-precision training.
- Clarify what “0.15 bits per synapse” means.
- Provide a concise, complete specification of the network topology in one place.
- Report how much longer training takes due to the probabilistic synaptic update mechanism.
- Explain why a single ensemble of the 30-core network yields much lower results than the 5-core network.
- Explain why results across different ensembles of the 5-core network are nearly identical.
- State the input spike rates during testing and whether inputs are presented as spike trains.
- Note that Reference 12 includes training constraints (e.g., bias=0) so it is not purely a train-then-constrain approach; fix typo “approach approach.”
- Add discussion/comparison with other spiking backpropagation rules (e.g., SpikeProp).
- Training methods that incorporate hardware constraints are significant, but the specific TN constraints may not generalize to other platforms.
- If results rely on a 64-member ensemble, this suggests substantially greater hardware resources are required to reach target accuracy.
- Overall: interesting contribution that would be stronger with clearer novelty positioning, additional quantitative details, and comparisons to related spiking backprop methods.
