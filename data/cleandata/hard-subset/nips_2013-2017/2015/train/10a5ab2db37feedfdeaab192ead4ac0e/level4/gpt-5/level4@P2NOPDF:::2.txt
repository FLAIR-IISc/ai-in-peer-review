This paper addresses the mismatch between the high-precision continuous variables used in backpropagation and the binary synapses typical of spike-based neuromorphic hardware. The authors propose interpreting analog parameters as probabilities that are sampled to construct binary synapses, enabling a trained offline network to be deployed on a spiking chip. They adapt backpropagation to support this probabilistic mapping and validate the approach on MNIST. The trained network is implemented on the TrueNorth device in two configurations that trade off size and accuracy, and the paper reports measured on-chip performance.

Strengths:
- The manuscript is well written, clearly structured, and easy to follow.
- The approach is original and methodologically sound, offering a practical bridge between conventional training and spike-based deployment.
- The experimental validation on TrueNorth and the presentation of measured results significantly strengthen the contribution.
- The technique appears broadly applicable and may benefit other neuromorphic platforms.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- The significance and practical impact are underemphasized. It remains unclear how the proposed method will enable real-world deployments on TrueNorth and for which classes of applications it is most suitable.
- The paper does not clearly articulate advantages over alternative strategies, including those cited in [9]–[12].
- Important implementation details are missing, limiting reproducibility and interpretability. In particular:
  - How inputs are encoded as spikes (e.g., coding scheme and preprocessing).
  - The firing-rate regimes used during inference.
  - The handling and values of shared weights.
  - The neuron models and parameters employed on hardware.

Suggestions:
- Expand the discussion of practical significance by identifying concrete application scenarios where the proposed method provides clear benefits (e.g., latency, energy efficiency, memory footprint, or robustness) relative to competing approaches.
- Provide a more explicit comparison with the alternatives in [9]–[12], including any trade-offs in accuracy, resource usage, and deployment complexity. If possible, include empirical or analytical comparisons.
- Add methodological details needed to reproduce or extend the work:
  - Precise input-to-spike conversion method and its rationale.
  - Typical spike rates and timing assumptions used on-chip.
  - Details of shared weight implementation and constraints.
  - Full specification of neuron models and parameters on TrueNorth.
- When surveying hardware platforms, please also cite Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015), and the work available at http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.05427 to place this contribution in a broader context.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents a compelling method for adapting backpropagation to spiking neural networks with binary synapses and demonstrates successful deployment on TrueNorth with convincing results. The contribution is clear and potentially impactful across neuromorphic systems. With a stronger articulation of significance, clearer differentiation from existing methods, and additional implementation details, this work would be a strong addition to the literature.
