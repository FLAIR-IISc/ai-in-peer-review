The authors should acknowledge that the algorithm presented in Section 3.2 bears a strong resemblance to those in the cited references [14] and [15]. The proposed algorithm, in its final form, appears to be a hybrid of these two methods and relies on identical theoretical approximations.

The first paragraph of the discussion section incorrectly implies that the "constrain-then-train" methodology is novel. This approach, which involves learning a weight distribution and then sampling from it to produce a fixed network ensemble at test time, was previously proposed and tested in reference [15].

Novelty claim (i) in the last paragraph of the introduction is also misleading. The "Expectation Backpropagation" algorithm from reference [15] is a backpropagation update rule derived via expectation propagation. It was implemented for binary neurons (using a sign activation function) with binary weights and can easily be applied to networks with constrained connectivity.

The cited state-of-the-art benchmarks are incorrect. To the best of my knowledge, the previous best results for networks with binary weights and neurons were achieved by the algorithm in reference [15]. That work was evaluated on MNIST without data augmentation (http://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.03562v3.pdf), which is the same setup used here, and should therefore serve as the primary point of comparison.
