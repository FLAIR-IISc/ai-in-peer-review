This paper addresses a key challenge in neuromorphic computing: reconciling the continuous-valued weights required for standard backpropagation with the binary synapses characteristic of many spike-based hardware platforms. The authors introduce a novel method where continuous weights, learned off-line, are interpreted as probabilities. These probabilities are then sampled to configure the binary synaptic connections on a target spiking neural network chip for deployment.

The paper demonstrates how the backpropagation algorithm can be adapted to this probabilistic framework and validates the approach on the MNIST benchmark. A significant contribution is the successful mapping of the trained network onto the TrueNorth hardware platform. The authors present results for two different network architectures, optimizing for either size or accuracy, and report performance figures measured directly from the chip.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity and Quality:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically structured. The core ideas are presented effectively, making the paper easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** The proposed probabilistic mapping from continuous-valued weights to binary synapses is an original and insightful contribution. This methodology is of potential interest to researchers working with other neuromorphic hardware platforms that have similar constraints.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The successful deployment and testing on the TrueNorth chip provide a compelling proof of concept and ground the theoretical work in a practical hardware implementation.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

*   **Significance and Context:** The primary weakness of the paper is its limited discussion of the work's broader significance and a lack of comparative analysis. While the method is technically sound, the manuscript would be substantially strengthened by addressing the following:
    *   **Practical Impact:** The paper does not sufficiently articulate the practical advantages of this approach for deploying applications on the TrueNorth chip. What specific types of applications would benefit most from this training and deployment pipeline? A discussion on this would help frame the importance of the contribution.
    *   **Comparison to Alternatives:** The authors should provide a more in-depth comparison with alternative methods for training Spiking Neural Networks or mapping pre-trained networks, such as those cited in references [9-12]. A discussion of the relative trade-offs in terms of accuracy, computational cost during training, and hardware resource utilization would help position the contribution more clearly within the existing literature.

*   **Missing Experimental Details:** The results are convincing, but the paper lacks several key implementation details that are crucial for reproducibility. The authors should clarify:
    *   The specific method used for converting MNIST pixel values into input spike trains.
    *   The spiking frequencies and temporal dynamics used during inference on the chip.
    *   Details about the neuron model implemented on the hardware.
    *   If and how weight sharing was implemented, and the specific values of the shared weights.

**Minor Suggestions:**

*   In the discussion of related hardware platforms, the authors may consider including references to other relevant work, such as Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the work presented in arXiv:1506.05427.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting an original and valuable method for training networks for deployment on neuromorphic hardware with binary synapses. The main weaknesses are the lack of discussion on the broader impact and a detailed comparison with alternative approaches. Addressing these points and providing additional experimental details would significantly enhance the manuscript.
