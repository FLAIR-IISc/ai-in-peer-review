

The paper proposes a training methodology enabling backpropagation to be applied to neuromorphic hardware with spiking neurons and low-precision synapses. It introduces a probabilistic framework where continuous-valued synaptic connections and neuron outputs represent probabilities, allowing standard backpropagation training while enabling direct mapping to hardware via ensemble sampling. The authors demonstrate this on TrueNorth for MNIST, achieving 99.42% accuracy (64-ensemble) at 108 μJ/image and 92.7% (1-ensemble) at 0.268 μJ/image.  

Strengths: The experimental results are compelling, with the highest accuracy reported for spiking networks (99.42%) and the best energy efficiency (0.268 μJ/image) for real-time MNIST classification on neuromorphic hardware. The probabilistic training framework is technically sound and well-motivated, with clear mapping to hardware constraints. The architecture design (progressive mixing topology) effectively respects TrueNorth's connectivity limitations.  

Weaknesses:  
1. The training algorithm in Section 3.2 is structurally similar to expectation backpropagation (Soudry et al., 2014, [15]) and stochastic bit-stream networks (Zhao et al., 1996, [14]), using identical Gaussian approximations for gradient computation. The paper fails to acknowledge this fundamental similarity, presenting the method as novel when it is a hybrid of these prior approaches.  
2. The "constrain-then-train" methodology is claimed as novel in the Discussion, but Soudry et al. (2014) explicitly use ensemble sampling from a probabilistic weight distribution (Section 3.2) to produce fixed networks at test time, directly contradicting the novelty claim.  
3. The novelty assertion (i) in the Introduction is overstated: Soudry et al. (2014) already demonstrated backpropagation for binary neurons and weights with constrained connectivity (see their Section 3.1), which aligns with the paper's approach. The cited state-of-the-art comparison is incorrect; Soudry et al. achieved 97.88% accuracy on MNIST with binary weights/neurons *without* data augmentation, matching the paper's experimental setup.  

Pro-acceptance: Strong empirical results, clear hardware integration, and practical energy-performance tradeoffs.  
Con-acceptance: Methodological novelty is significantly overstated due to inadequate citation of prior work on probabilistic backpropagation and ensemble sampling.  

The paper makes a valuable contribution to energy-efficient neuromorphic deployment, but the claims of novelty must be revised to accurately reflect existing literature. The experimental rigor and results are sufficient for acceptance *if* the authors address these citation and novelty issues in revision.
