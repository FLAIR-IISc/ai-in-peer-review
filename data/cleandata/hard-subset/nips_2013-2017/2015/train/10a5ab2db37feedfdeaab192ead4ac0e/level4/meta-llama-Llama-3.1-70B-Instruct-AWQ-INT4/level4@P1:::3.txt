The authors present an interesting paper about a backpropagation training method that utilizes spike probabilities and takes into account the hardware constraints of a particular platform, specifically one with spiking neurons and discrete synapses. This is a topic of current interest in mapping deep networks to multi-neuron hardware platforms.

Quality: The proposed training method is useful, especially in consideration of new multi-neuron hardware platforms with constraints. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by achieving high accuracy on the MNIST dataset while minimizing energy consumption. However, it would be beneficial to provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy and energy efficiency.

Clarity: The paper is generally easy to read, but there are a few areas that could be improved for clarity. The claims made at the end of Section 1 could be reworded to avoid suggesting that the paper proposes a training methodology that employs spiking neurons and synapses with reduced precision for the first time. Additionally, the demonstration of running the network on the TrueNorth (TN) platform could be framed as a validation of the training method rather than a novel feature.

In Section 2, the explanation of 0.15 bits per synapse could be clarified. Furthermore, the network topology is not clearly described in one place, making it difficult to understand the architecture of the proposed network. The authors could provide more information on how the probabilistic synaptic connection update affects the training time. It is also unclear why the result from a single ensemble of the 30 core network is so low, especially when compared to the 5 core network. The results of the different ensembles of the 5 core network are also surprisingly similar, and the authors could provide more insight into this phenomenon. Finally, the authors could provide more details on the spike rates for the inputs during testing and clarify whether the input is a spike train.

Originality: The authors could provide a more detailed discussion or comparison with other spiking backprop-type rules, such as SpikeProp. This would help to situate their work within the broader context of spiking neural networks and highlight the unique contributions of their approach.

Significance: The development of new training methods that consider constraints of hardware platforms is of great interest. However, the authors could provide more discussion on the generalizability of their approach to other platforms. While the constraints considered during training in this paper are based on the TN architecture, it is unclear whether these constraints would apply to other platforms. Additionally, the authors could provide more insight into the implications of requiring a 64 ensemble to achieve high accuracy, particularly in terms of hardware resources.

Overall, this is an interesting paper about a training method using backpropagation that takes into account the hardware constraints of a particular platform with spiking neurons and discrete synapses. With some revisions to address the above points, the paper could be even stronger.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel training method that takes into account the hardware constraints of a particular platform.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the MNIST dataset.
* The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from more detailed analysis and discussion of the trade-offs between accuracy and energy efficiency.
* The authors could provide more insight into the implications of requiring a 64 ensemble to achieve high accuracy.
* The paper could be improved with more comparisons to other spiking backprop-type rules and a clearer discussion of the generalizability of the approach to other platforms.
