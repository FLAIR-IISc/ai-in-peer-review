This paper proposes a novel backpropagation training method that takes into account the hardware constraints of a particular platform with spiking neurons and discrete synapses. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by training a network on the MNIST dataset and deploying it on the TrueNorth chip, achieving high accuracy and low power consumption.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of a training method that bridges the gap between deep learning and neuromorphic computing. The authors propose a "constrain-then-train" approach, where they first constrain the network to provide a direct representation of the deployment system and then train within those constraints. This approach allows for a direct mapping between the training network and the deployment network, enabling the use of backpropagation to train networks that can be efficiently deployed on neuromorphic hardware.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its advantages. The authors provide a thorough description of their approach, including the network topology, training methodology, and mapping from the training network to the deployment network. The results are impressive, with the authors achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on the MNIST dataset while consuming significantly less power than other approaches.

One potential improvement to the paper is to provide a clearer comparison with other spiking backprop-type rules, such as SpikeProp. The authors mention SpikeProp in the discussion, but a more detailed comparison would be helpful in understanding the advantages and limitations of their approach.

Another potential area for improvement is to discuss the scalability of the proposed method. While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a small-scale dataset, it is unclear how well the method will scale to larger datasets and more complex networks. Additionally, the authors mention that the constraints considered during training are specific to the TrueNorth architecture, which may limit the applicability of the method to other platforms.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-written paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. The proposed training method has the potential to enable the deployment of deep neural networks on low-power neuromorphic hardware, which could have significant implications for a wide range of applications.
