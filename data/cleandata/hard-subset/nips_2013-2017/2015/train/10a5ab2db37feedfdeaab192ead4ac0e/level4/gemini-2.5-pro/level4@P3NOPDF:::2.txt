This paper addresses the significant challenge of reconciling backpropagation, which relies on high-precision continuous variables, with the deployment of networks on spike-based neuromorphic hardware that often features constrained, binary synapses. The central contribution is a novel method that treats the analog weights derived from offline training as probabilities. These probabilities are then stochastically sampled to map the trained network onto the target spiking network chip. The authors detail the necessary adaptations to the backpropagation algorithm to support this strategy and validate their approach on the MNIST benchmark. Furthermore, they demonstrate the practical utility of their method by mapping the trained network onto the TrueNorth chip in two distinct configurations—one optimized for size and the other for accuracy—and present performance metrics measured directly from the hardware.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Quality:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically structured. The authors effectively communicate a complex idea with precision.
2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The concept of using continuous-valued weights as probabilities for setting binary synaptic states is an original and insightful contribution. This probabilistic bridge between offline training and hardware deployment is a clever solution to a difficult problem in the field of neuromorphic engineering.
3.  **Potential Impact:** The proposed technique appears broadly applicable and could be highly valuable for other researchers working with different neuromorphic hardware platforms that have similar synaptic constraints.
4.  **Experimental Validation:** The validation on the TrueNorth chip is a significant strength. By presenting concrete performance figures from a physical hardware implementation, the authors move beyond simulation and demonstrate the real-world viability of their method.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Context and Significance:** The paper's primary weakness is its failure to adequately articulate the broader significance of the work. While the technical contribution is clear, the manuscript does not explain *why* this method is a crucial step forward for the practical deployment of neuromorphic systems. The authors should elaborate on the specific applications where this approach would be most beneficial and how it enables the TrueNorth chip (or similar hardware) to tackle practical problems that were previously intractable.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Alternatives:** The paper needs a more thorough comparison with existing methods for training networks with discrete or binary weights, such as those cited in references [9] through [12]. It is not clear what the explicit advantages and disadvantages of this probabilistic sampling method are when compared to other techniques. A discussion on comparative performance, training stability, or hardware efficiency would significantly strengthen the paper and better position its contribution within the literature.

3.  **Missing Experimental Details:** While the results are convincing, the experimental section omits several details that are essential for reproducibility and a complete understanding of the work. The authors should clarify the following:
    *   **Input Encoding:** How are the static MNIST images converted into the temporal spike trains used as input to the network?
    *   **Network and Neuron Parameters:** What neuron model was used on the chip? What were the characteristic spike frequencies during operation?
    *   **Weight Parameters:** What were the specific values of the shared weights used in the hardware implementation?

**Minor Suggestions:**

*   In the review of available hardware platforms, the discussion would be more comprehensive if it included a mention of related work, such as Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and the work detailed in arxiv:1506.05427.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and promising method for training SNNs for deployment on hardware with binary synapses. The work is well-presented and supported by strong experimental results. The paper would be significantly improved by expanding on the significance of the work, providing a more detailed comparison with alternative methods, and including the missing experimental details to aid in reproducibility.
