The authors present a compelling study of a backpropagation training method leveraging spike probabilities, specifically designed with the hardware limitations of spiking neuron and discrete synapse platforms in mind. This work addresses a timely challenge in deploying deep networks onto emerging multi-neuron hardware.

**Quality:** The proposed training methodology is particularly valuable given the growing landscape of novel, constrained multi-neuron hardware platforms.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and accessible. However, the claims made towards the end of Section 1 could benefit from rephrasing. The current wording risks implying a novel *introduction* of spiking neuron and reduced-precision synapse training, rather than a novel *application* within specific hardware constraints. Demonstrating operation on the TN platform should be presented as a validation of the training method, rather than a core novelty. 

In Section 2, further clarification is needed regarding “0.15 bits per synapse.” The network topology would be more easily understood with a consolidated description. Additionally, the paper should address the increase in training time resulting from the probabilistic synaptic updates. The unexpectedly low performance observed from a single ensemble of the 30-core network, particularly in comparison to the 5-core network, warrants explanation. Similarly, the consistency of results across different ensembles of the 5-core network deserves discussion. Finally, the spike rates of the inputs during testing, and whether the input data is presented as a spike train, should be specified. Reference 12 (line 399) describes training with constraints (e.g., bias=0) and therefore doesn’t strictly represent a “train-then-constrain” approach (note a typographical repetition: "approach approach" on this line).

**Originality:** A discussion comparing this work to existing spiking backpropagation algorithms, such as SpikeProp, would strengthen the originality assessment.

**Significance:** The development of training methods mindful of hardware constraints is a significant contribution. However, the constraints considered here are specific to the TN architecture, potentially limiting the direct applicability of these findings to other platforms. Furthermore, achieving the reported accuracy necessitates a 64-ensemble configuration, implying a substantial demand for hardware resources. This is an interesting investigation into backpropagation-based training accommodating the hardware realities of spiking neural networks and discrete synapses.
