- Presents a backpropagation-based training method for SNNs that incorporates neuromorphic hardware constraints and demonstrates it on TrueNorth.  
- Strength: co-design of training and hardware constraints is a practical contribution; hardware implementation validates the approach.  
- Limitation: method’s generality is unclear because constraints are specific to TrueNorth—needs discussion on applicability to other neuromorphic platforms.  
- Practical concern: achieving high accuracy requires a 64-member ensemble, raising resource/utilization and cost trade-off issues that need deeper analysis.  
- Framing: claims should be reworded to avoid implying this is the first reduced-precision synapse training method; TrueNorth implementation should be framed as validation, not novelty.  
- Missing detail: provide a clear, consolidated description of the network topology.  
- Ambiguity: explain the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" and how synapses are represented.  
- Experimental cost: quantify the training overhead introduced by probabilistic synaptic updates versus standard backpropagation.  
- Input representation: clarify whether spike trains were used during testing and report their characteristic spike rates.  
- Result anomaly: explain why a single 30-core network ensemble performs significantly worse than a 5-core network.  
- Result consistency: explain why performance variance is so low across different 5-core network ensembles.  
- Corrections: fix the typo ("approach approach") and correct the inaccurate characterization of Reference [12] as purely "train-then-constrain."  
- Originality/context: compare and contrast with other backpropagation-inspired SNN learning rules (e.g., SpikeProp and variants) to clarify novelty and advantages.
