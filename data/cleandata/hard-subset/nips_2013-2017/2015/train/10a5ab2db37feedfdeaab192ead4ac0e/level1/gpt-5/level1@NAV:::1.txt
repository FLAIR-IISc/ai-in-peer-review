This paper proposes a probabilistic relaxation that reconciles backpropagation with spiking neuromorphic hardware featuring binary synapses and constrained connectivity. The key idea is to treat spikes and discrete synapse states as continuous probabilities in [0,1], enabling standard backprop to learn synaptic connection probabilities and biases while respecting hardware topology. After training, deployment samples binary synapses and spike inputs from these probabilities; optionally, multiple samplings form an ensemble whose outputs are averaged. A sliding-window, progressively mixing topology matches TrueNorth’s core-to-core constraints, and a weight-initialization scheme balances ±1 strengths to reduce redundancy. On MNIST, the approach achieves 99.42% with a 64-member ensemble (108 μJ/image) and 92.7% with a single network (0.268 μJ/image) at 1 kHz throughput on TrueNorth. This connects to “train-then-constrain” conversion of ReLU CNNs to SNNs (e.g., Diehl et al., IJCNN’15), binary/binarized networks (e.g., BinaryConnect, NIPS’15), and expectation/probabilistic backprop for discrete networks (Cheng et al., 2015), but differs by jointly handling spiking neurons, 1-bit synapses, and restricted connectivity with a single backprop-compatible training objective and a concrete hardware deployment.

Quality: The derivation uses a Gaussian approximation of weighted Bernoulli inputs and drops a higher-order term in the gradient; while reasonable under stated assumptions (balanced ± weights, many inputs), the paper would benefit from ablations validating these approximations and the independence assumptions. The training protocol and topology are described in useful detail, and the energy measurements on-chip are a strong point, though apples-to-apples baselines on the same hardware would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized; notation could be streamlined (e.g., unify x̃/ñ, state θ consistently, and tidy Eq. 3/4 formatting). A brief algorithm box for mapping and ensemble construction would aid reproducibility.

Originality: The constrain-then-train probabilistic formulation plus topology/initialization tailored to binary, constrained SNN hardware is a novel and coherent combination beyond prior conversion or binarization-only methods.

Significance: Demonstrates state-of-the-art energy/accuracy trade-offs on real hardware, a valuable contribution for embedded AI. The evaluation is limited to MNIST; extending to harder vision datasets would bolster impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled bridge between backprop and spiking neuromorphic hardware
- Real-time hardware results with excellent energy efficiency
- Practical topology and initialization aligned with TrueNorth constraints

Cons against acceptance:
- Evaluation limited to MNIST; no comparisons to strong train-then-constrain baselines on the same platform
- Approximations (Gaussian, gradient truncation) lack empirical justification
- Synaptic strengths are fixed rather than learned; missing ablation on this design choice

Overall, I lean toward acceptance given the novelty and strong hardware evidence, with suggestions to broaden empirical validation.
