Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a hardware-aware training method that reconciles backpropagation with spiking neuromorphic hardware featuring binary synapses and constrained connectivity (TrueNorth). The key idea is to treat spiking activations and discrete synapse states as probabilities in [0,1], enabling a differentiable forward model: the weighted sum of Bernoulli variables plus bias is approximated as Gaussian, and the spiking probability is computed via the Gaussian CDF. Backpropagation optimizes synaptic connection probabilities and neuron biases; synaptic strengths are fixed a priori in balanced ±1 patterns chosen to decorrelate neuron inputs within a core. The topology uses locally connected, sliding windows (weight-unshared) that respect TrueNorth’s core-level fan-in constraints. At deployment, a Bernoulli sampling of the learned probabilities produces one or more discrete networks; ensembling multiple samples improves accuracy via averaging. On MNIST, the method achieves 99.42% with an ensemble of 64 of a 30-core network (1920 cores total) at 108 µJ/classification, and 92.7% with a minimal 5-core network at 0.268 µJ/classification. This approach bridges strands of prior work: conversion of trained ReLU/ANNs to SNNs [Diehl et al. 2015], training binary/trinary synapses with continuous neurons (e.g., rounding/low-precision training), and expectation backpropagation for binary networks [Cheng et al. 2015]. Unlike prior SNN conversions, the method is constrain-then-train with a forward model matched to the deployment constraints, and unlike binary network work with full connectivity, it handles tight hardware connectivity and synapse precision.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The probabilistic forward model (Gaussian approximation to weighted Bernoulli sums) is reasonable under independence and moderate fan-in; the gradients are derived and further simplified by dropping a σ^−3 term under assumptions of balanced weights and many inputs. However, the paper does not quantify approximation error or provide ablations to justify the derivative simplification, nor does it assess the impact of input correlations introduced by local receptive fields. The choice to learn only connection probabilities/biases while fixing synaptic strengths by a decorrelation scheme is clever and hardware-aligned, but it is not clear how much accuracy is sacrificed relative to learning the small set of allowable strengths (axon-type lookup), or to learning axon-type assignments. The ensemble approach is effective but raises fairness questions relative to single-model baselines.
- Empirics: Results are compelling in energy terms and competitive on MNIST accuracy for SNNs. The energy measurement methodology is described (dynamic plus pro-rated leakage), which is appreciated. Missing are: single-ensemble-member accuracy for the 30-core network; a comparison to a matched-parameter conventional ANN (no weight sharing) to isolate the cost of hardware constraints; and ablations on key choices (Gaussian vs. exact enumeration on small fan-in, with/without the σ^−3 term, learned vs. fixed s-values, stride/window choices). Reporting confidence intervals across ensemble samplings and runs would strengthen claims.
- Claims vs. evidence: The paper claims state-of-the-art MNIST for SNNs and significant energy efficiency; those claims are largely supported, though comparison to other low-power platforms should carefully control throughput/latency, augmentation, and ensembling.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The hardware constraints and mapping are clearly explained, as is the topology. Some notational issues remain: Equation (3) is awkwardly typeset; define all terms clearly (θ, µ, σ) and ensure consistent use of x̃ vs. ñ across layers. The description of pk (class probability under averaging multiple outputs) would benefit from a short derivation or pseudocode. The rationale for multiple output neurons per class and its effect should be explained quantitatively.

Originality
Training a hardware-matched probabilistic surrogate that maps directly to binary-spiking hardware with constrained fan-in, and combining this with a principled weight initialization for decorrelation, is novel in combination. The use of ensembling via independent Bernoulli samples of the learned connectivity is a simple but effective twist. Related work on stochastic/binary neurons and expectation backprop should be discussed more deeply; also consider referencing Bengio et al. (2013) on straight-through estimators for stochastic neurons and contemporaneous low-precision training methods (e.g., BinaryConnect) to position the contribution.

Significance
Bridging backprop and neuromorphic deployment with demonstrated real-time, energy-efficient inference on silicon is impactful. The method offers a practical recipe for mapping learned models onto chips like TrueNorth and is likely to be built upon. That said, the experimental scope is limited to MNIST and weight-unshared local connectivity; demonstrating viability on more challenging datasets or with convolutional weight sharing would significantly increase broader impact.

Strengths
- Clear, hardware-aware training objective and mapping; constrain-then-train is compelling.
- Principled Gaussian surrogate enabling backprop through spiking/binary elements.
- Thoughtful synapse-strength initialization to decorrelate core outputs.
- Real chip results with thorough energy reporting; strong energy-accuracy trade-offs.
- Simple sampling-to-ensemble mechanism; single training run supports multiple deployment points.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on Gaussian/independence approximations without ablation or error analysis; derivative simplification lacks empirical justification.
- Ensembling of 64 needed for top accuracy; unclear single-model performance for the 30-core network and fairness of comparisons.
- Only MNIST; no convolutional weight sharing or more challenging benchmarks.
- Synaptic strengths are fixed; no exploration of learning axon-type assignments or few-level strengths.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to topology choices (R, S, F) and to the “multiple outputs per class” design.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Report accuracy for the 30-core network with ensemble sizes 1, 4, 16; include variance over multiple samplings.
- Provide ablations: (a) include/exclude the σ^−3 term in ∂ñ/∂c̃; (b) compare Gaussian vs. exact Bernoulli convolution on small fan-in layers; (c) fixed vs. learned synapse strengths or axon-type assignments.
- Compare to a matched-parameter, locally connected, weight-unshared ANN trained with ReLUs/sigmoids to quantify the cost of hardware constraints.
- Analyze the effect of input correlations due to overlapping windows on the Gaussian approximation and learning stability.
- Clarify whether deterministic thresholding c = 1[c̃>τ] at deployment was tried; how does it compare to stochastic sampling?
- Discuss applicability to convolution (weight sharing) on TrueNorth-like substrates and any constraints that preclude it.
- Expand related work to include straight-through estimators for stochastic/binary neurons and contemporaneous low-precision training.

Overall
A strong, timely contribution that meaningfully advances training for energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware, with a solid real-hardware demonstration. With added analyses to shore up the approximations and a clearer accounting of single-model performance, this would be a compelling accept.
