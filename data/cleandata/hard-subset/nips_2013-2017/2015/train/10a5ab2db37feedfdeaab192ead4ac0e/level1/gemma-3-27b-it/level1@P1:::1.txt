This paper presents a compelling approach to bridging the gap between the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing and the performance of backpropagation-trained deep neural networks. The core idea – treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities to enable standard backpropagation – is clever and addresses a significant challenge in the field. The demonstration on the TrueNorth chip with MNIST, achieving both high accuracy (99.42%) and impressive energy efficiency (0.268 µJ per image with a single ensemble member), is a strong result.

The work is well-motivated, clearly situating the problem within the broader context of deploying neural networks in embedded systems. The authors effectively highlight the incompatibility between backpropagation’s requirements (continuous values, high precision) and neuromorphic hardware’s constraints (spikes, low precision, limited connectivity). The related work section is comprehensive, covering relevant advancements in both spiking neural networks and probabilistic backpropagation. They appropriately position their work as novel in combining these elements – specifically, offline training of spiking networks with low-precision synapses and constrained connectivity – and achieving state-of-the-art results on MNIST and superior power efficiency on TrueNorth.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of representing discrete elements as probabilities for backpropagation is a significant contribution.
*   **Strong Results:** Achieving 99.42% accuracy on MNIST with a 64-member ensemble and 92.7% with a single member, alongside the demonstrated energy efficiency on TrueNorth, is impressive. The comparison to prior work is convincing.
*   **Clear Explanation:** The paper is well-written and explains the technical details – the probabilistic neuron model, the training procedure, the network topology, and the mapping to hardware – in a clear and accessible manner. The inclusion of equations aids understanding.
*   **Thorough Justification:** The design choices, particularly the network topology and initialization strategy, are well-justified and explained. The rationale behind minimizing redundancy in synaptic weights is sound.
*   **Practical Demonstration:** The deployment on TrueNorth adds significant practical value and demonstrates the feasibility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Approximations:** Several approximations are made in the derivation of the backpropagation gradients (e.g., dropping terms in Equation 4). While justified for computational efficiency, the potential impact of these approximations on training performance and accuracy isn’t fully explored.  A more thorough analysis of the error introduced by these approximations would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters (learning rate, dropout rate, mini-batch size, etc.).  Providing some insight into how these parameters were chosen and their influence on performance would strengthen the work.
*   **Limited Dataset:** The evaluation is limited to the MNIST dataset.  While this provides a good starting point, demonstrating the approach on more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) would significantly increase its impact.  The authors acknowledge the need for adapting to convolutional networks, but experimental results would be more persuasive.
*   **Network Topology Specificity:** The progressive mixing approach to network topology, while effective, appears somewhat tailored to the TrueNorth architecture. Exploring alternative connectivity schemes and their impact on performance and energy efficiency would be valuable.
* **Initialization Dependency:** The initialization strategy relies on balancing synapse strengths to zero. While justifiable, its dependence on this precise balance could limit generalization to different datasets or network architectures.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a crucial problem in neuromorphic computing—bridging the gap between powerful learning algorithms and efficient hardware.
*   The proposed solution is novel, well-motivated, and technically sound.
*   The experimental results are strong and demonstrate a significant improvement in energy efficiency compared to existing approaches.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on approximations in the gradient calculations and the lack of detailed sensitivity analysis are concerns.
*   The evaluation is limited to a relatively simple dataset (MNIST).
*   The network topology may be specific to the TrueNorth architecture.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. The proposed approach is innovative, the results are compelling, and the paper is well-written. While some weaknesses exist, they are outweighed by the strengths of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding the approximations used in the gradient calculations and to provide a more thorough discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.  Further results on more complex datasets would also greatly strengthen the paper.
