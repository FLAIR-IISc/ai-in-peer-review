This paper tackles the critical challenge of bridging the gap between the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing and the performance of backpropagation-trained deep networks. The core idea – treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities to enable backpropagation – is elegant and potentially impactful. The authors demonstrate this approach on the TrueNorth chip with competitive MNIST results, showing both high accuracy (99.42%) and excellent energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image).

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear motivation and introduction of the problem. The methodology is explained in detail, covering the deployment hardware (TrueNorth), the network training procedure (probabilistic backpropagation), the mapping process, network topology, and experimental results. The inclusion of network initialization details (minimizing covariance between neuron inputs) is particularly strong, demonstrating attention to practical implementation challenges. The comparison to existing work (both spiking and non-spiking approaches) is comprehensive and convincingly positions the work as state-of-the-art in energy efficiency.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of treating spiking components as probabilities is a clever way to reconcile backpropagation with neuromorphic hardware. While probabilistic approaches exist (cited in the paper), applying it specifically to *both* synapses *and* neuron activations within the constraints of TrueNorth is novel.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivation of the gradient updates appears sound, and the approximations made are clearly justified. The detailed description of the network initialization process is a strong indication of careful consideration of practical constraints.
* **Significance:** The demonstrated energy efficiency is a substantial contribution, especially given the maintained accuracy. The potential to deploy high-performance deep learning models on low-power neuromorphic hardware is significant for embedded systems and edge computing.
* **Clarity:** The paper is largely well-written and organized. The figures are informative and help to illustrate the key concepts. The breakdown of the training and deployment networks is helpful.
* **Completeness:** The paper presents a complete system – from training to hardware deployment – and includes a thorough evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Approximations in Gradient Calculation:** While justified, the approximation in Equation 4 introduces a potential source of error. A more rigorous analysis of the impact of this approximation would be beneficial.  It is stated that the conditions under which the approximation holds are met, but this feels somewhat hand-wavy and more quantitative justification would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Dataset:** The results are currently limited to the MNIST dataset. While MNIST is a good starting point, demonstrating the approach on more complex and realistic datasets would further strengthen the claims.  The authors mention plans for convolution networks, which addresses this point but isn’t *in* the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the results to various hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, dropout rate, network topology).  A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter tuning would increase confidence in the robustness of the approach.
* **Network Topology Justification:** While the sliding window approach is described, the reasoning behind the specific parameters chosen (R, S, F) could be more thoroughly explained. Why these specific values were chosen, and what impact they have on performance, is not explicitly clear.
* **Potential for Optimization:** While the initialization strategy is good, it seems relatively basic.  Exploring more sophisticated initialization techniques tailored to sparse, binary-weighted networks could potentially lead to further improvements.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a significant problem in neuromorphic computing.
* The demonstrated energy efficiency is a major achievement.
* The paper is well-written, complete, and provides a thorough evaluation.
* The results suggest a promising path towards deploying high-performance deep learning models on low-power hardware.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on approximations in the gradient calculation introduces a potential source of error.
* The limited dataset restricts the generalizability of the results.
* The lack of detailed hyperparameter analysis weakens the robustness argument.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and deserves acceptance. The novelty and significance of the work, coupled with the technical soundness and clarity of the presentation, outweigh the relatively minor weaknesses.  Addressing the concerns regarding the gradient approximation and expanding the evaluation to more complex datasets would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I would encourage the authors to: (1) provide a more quantitative justification for the approximation in Equation 4, (2) briefly discuss future work planned for more complex datasets, and (3) include a small ablation study exploring the impact of key hyperparameters.
