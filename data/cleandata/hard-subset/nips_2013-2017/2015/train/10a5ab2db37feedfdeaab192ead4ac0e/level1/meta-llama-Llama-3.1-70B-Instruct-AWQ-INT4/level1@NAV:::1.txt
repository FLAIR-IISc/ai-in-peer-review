This paper, "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing," presents a novel approach to training neural networks that are compatible with neuromorphic hardware. The authors propose a method to bridge the gap between backpropagation, which uses continuous-output neurons and high-precision synapses, and neuromorphic designs, which employ spiking neurons and discrete synapses.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors also carefully evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce their results. However, some sections, such as the derivation of the gradient equations, may be challenging for non-experts to follow.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques. The authors' approach to treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities is innovative and allows for the use of standard backpropagation to train networks that are compatible with neuromorphic hardware.

**Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to impact the field of neuromorphic computing. The authors demonstrate that their approach can achieve high performance and energy efficiency on the MNIST dataset, making it a promising solution for real-world applications.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

1. The paper presents a novel and innovative approach to training neural networks that are compatible with neuromorphic hardware.
2. The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach and support their claims with theoretical analysis and experimental results.
3. The results are significant and have the potential to impact the field of neuromorphic computing.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

1. Some sections of the paper may be challenging for non-experts to follow, which may limit the paper's accessibility to a broader audience.
2. The authors' approach may be specific to the TrueNorth chip and may not be generalizable to other neuromorphic hardware.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Consider adding more background information on neuromorphic computing and the challenges of training neural networks for this type of hardware.
2. Provide more details on the experimental setup and the hyperparameters used for training the networks.
3. Consider including more comparisons with other state-of-the-art approaches to demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method.
