## Review of "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing"

This paper addresses the critical challenge of bridging the gap between the algorithmic success of backpropagation and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic hardware. The authors propose a novel approach of treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, enabling the application of standard backpropagation for training networks directly deployable on neuromorphic chips like TrueNorth. This is a significant contribution, as it tackles the inherent incompatibility between the continuous nature of backpropagation and the discrete, spiking nature of neuromorphic systems. The work builds upon previous efforts in training spiking networks with backpropagation (e.g., [9,10,11,12]) and probabilistic approaches ([14,15]), but uniquely combines these with constrained connectivity and low-precision synapses, specifically tailored for neuromorphic hardware.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on MNIST when deployed on TrueNorth (99.42% with an ensemble of 64), surpassing previous results with spiking neurons ([12]) and binary synapses ([15]). The demonstrated energy efficiency, particularly with the single-member ensemble (0.268 μJ per image), is impressive and highlights the potential of this approach for embedded applications. The paper is well-motivated, clearly explains the methodology, and provides a detailed network initialization scheme to optimize for hardware constraints. The experimental results are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed method.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the explanation of the probabilistic backpropagation is clear, the approximation made in Equation 4, while justified for computational efficiency, could benefit from a more detailed analysis of its impact on gradient accuracy. The network topology, while functional, feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a strong theoretical justification beyond respecting TrueNorth’s connectivity constraints.  Furthermore, the generalization to more complex datasets beyond MNIST isn’t discussed in detail, and it's unclear how well this approach would scale. The discussion section briefly mentions future work, but a more in-depth exploration of these avenues would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to bridging backpropagation and neuromorphic computing.
*   State-of-the-art accuracy and energy efficiency on TrueNorth.
*   Clear explanation and thorough experimental validation.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in the field of neuromorphic computing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Approximation in Equation 4 requires further justification.
*   Network topology lacks strong theoretical grounding.
*   Limited discussion of scalability to more complex datasets.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The authors present a compelling solution to a challenging problem, and the results are impressive. Despite the minor weaknesses, the originality, significance, and quality of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
