Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a probabilistic training methodology that reconciles backpropagation with deployment on spiking neuromorphic hardware with extremely low-precision synapses and constrained connectivity (IBM TrueNorth). The key idea is to represent spikes and binary synaptic connections as continuous probabilities in [0,1], allowing standard backpropagation to optimize synapse connection probabilities and neuron biases, while synaptic strengths are fixed a priori to a balanced set chosen to minimize pairwise redundancy. The forward model uses a Gaussian approximation of weighted sums of Bernoulli inputs to compute neuron firing probabilities via a cumulative Gaussian; gradients are derived analytically, with a computationally motivated approximation. After training, the binary connectivity is sampled from the learned probabilities, and one or more sampled networks are ensembled by averaging outputs. A locally connected “progressive mixing” topology with sliding windows over cores supports limited fan-in per core while increasing receptive field across layers. On MNIST, a 30-core network with an ensemble of 64 achieves 99.42% at 108 µJ per image; a 5-core single-network model achieves 92.7% at 0.268 µJ per image, both in real time on TrueNorth.

The work builds on several strands: (i) conversion-based spiking networks with high-precision weights (e.g., Diehl et al., 2015; Cao et al., 2015), (ii) binary/trinary neural networks trained with probabilistic or expectation backpropagation, typically with full connectivity (e.g., Zhao et al., 1996; Cheng et al., 2015), and (iii) neuromorphic hardware platforms (TrueNorth, SpiNNaker, Neurogrid). Unlike conversion-based “train-then-constrain” pipelines, the method is “constrain-then-train,” optimizing directly in the space dictated by hardware constraints. The topology is akin to locally connected layers used in early CNN work (LeCun et al., 1998), but without weight sharing and with hard limits imposed by core I/O and synapse precision.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual bridge between backpropagation and spiking neuromorphic deployment via a probabilistic surrogate model that is differentiable and maps naturally to binary spikes and synapses.
- Hardware relevance and empirical validation: networks run in real time on TrueNorth with explicit energy measurements, showing strong energy-accuracy trade-offs and high throughput (1 kHz).
- Methodologically coherent: consistent use of constrained values as probabilities; a principled a priori initialization of synaptic strengths to reduce redundancy; and a topology aligned with hardware constraints.
- Competitive accuracy for spiking systems; the reported MNIST accuracy with ensembles exceeds prior spiking results cited by the authors.
- Training pipeline uses standard deep learning heuristics (mini-batching, momentum, dropout, augmentation) and could be adapted to other architectures.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- The forward-probability and gradient derivations rely on independence and Gaussian approximations of weighted Bernoulli sums and further approximate the gradient by dropping a term involving σ^3. While reasonable for large fan-in and balanced weights, the paper lacks an empirical sensitivity/ablation showing the impact of these approximations (e.g., exact vs. approximate gradient, or non-Gaussian corrections).
- Synaptic strengths are not learned but fixed; this simplifies mapping to TrueNorth but may limit representational capacity. An ablation comparing fixed vs. learned strengths (within the allowed blocks) would help quantify the trade-off.
- Heavy reliance on large ensembles to reach top accuracy (64 networks, 1920 cores) raises questions about memory/area efficiency and scalability to larger tasks. A discussion of ensemble overheads, cross-member diversity, and whether bagging provides most of the gains would be useful.
- Evaluation is limited to MNIST; while standard in neuromorphic literature, the significance for broader ML is constrained. A second dataset (e.g., NMNIST or a simple vision task with convolution-like features) would strengthen claims.
- Energy comparisons across platforms (e.g., vs. SpiNNaker results) may be affected by differing operating points, accuracies, and throughput. More detail on the power accounting (I/O, host, leakage allocation, randomness generation) and a normalized comparison would improve rigor.
- Some notational clarity issues: the expression for the firing probability (Equation 3) appears typeset incorrectly; define pk more precisely; explicitly state θ and whether thresholds differ across layers; and detail the axon-type strength lookup and its relation to the s-values used in training.

Quality:
- The approach is technically plausible and internally consistent. The Gaussian/independence assumptions are standard approximations; however, the paper would benefit from empirical checks (e.g., comparing empirical membrane distributions to Gaussian, and reporting µ/σ across layers).
- Experimental support is solid for the target hardware and MNIST, with careful reporting of ensemble sizes and energy. Missing ablations (learning s-values, exact vs. approximate gradients, role of dropout given stochastic c̃) reduce completeness.

Clarity:
- Generally well organized and readable. The training-to-deployment mapping is clearly specified. Improve clarity by fixing Equation 3, expanding the description of pk and the class-averaging scheme, and adding algorithmic detail for the synaptic-strength initialization (how near-orthogonality is achieved and measured).

Originality:
- The “constrain-then-train” probabilistic treatment of both spikes and binary synapses under hard connectivity limits, with direct mapping to a commercial neuromorphic chip, is novel relative to prior spiking-conversion methods and binary-network training with full connectivity. The synapse-strength initialization to reduce redundancy is a neat hardware-aware contribution.

Significance:
- High for neuromorphic computing: demonstrates a practical path to backprop-trained, energy-efficient, real-time inference on hardware. Moderate for mainstream ML due to dataset scope and dependence on ensembles; nonetheless, the methodology could transfer to other architectures (e.g., locally connected or convolutional variants).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide ablations: effect of gradient approximation; fixed vs. learned s-values within allowed blocks; impact of ensemble size and diversity metrics; with/without dropout.
- Clarify energy measurement methodology and provide normalized comparisons where possible.
- Report statistics validating Gaussian assumptions (histograms, QQ plots, µ/σ).
- Discuss extension to convolutional weight sharing and constraints specific to TrueNorth routing.
- Add a second dataset or at least a locally connected/no-weight-sharing baseline on MNIST with high-precision neurons to isolate the effect of binary synapses/spikes.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Well-motivated, hardware-relevant problem; elegant probabilistic bridge enabling backprop on spiking hardware with ultra-low precision.
- Strong empirical demonstration on real hardware with state-of-the-art spiking accuracy and excellent energy efficiency.
- Potentially impactful for deploying learned models in embedded, low-power settings.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited evaluation scope (single dataset) and reliance on large ensembles for top accuracy.
- Unvalidated approximations and missing ablations reduce confidence in robustness.
- Some clarity gaps in equations and initialization details.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and practically meaningful contribution at the intersection of deep learning and neuromorphic computing, with room to strengthen empirical validation and clarity.
