Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a training methodology that reconciles standard backpropagation with the constraints of neuromorphic hardware (TrueNorth): spiking neurons, binary synapses (with a small lookup of shared signed strengths), and limited fan-in/fan-out. The key idea is to represent spikes and on/off synapses as Bernoulli random variables with continuous parameters in [0,1]. During training, a “probabilistic” network with the same topology as the deployment network performs a forward pass that approximates the summed input as Gaussian; the probability of neuron firing is then a probit-like nonlinearity (the Gaussian CCDF), which is differentiable, enabling backpropagation with respect to synapse connection probabilities and biases. Synapse strengths are not learned; instead, they are fixed per core using a balanced ±1 scheme designed to reduce pairwise correlations (minimize inner products) across neurons while satisfying TrueNorth’s axon-type constraints. After training, the binary network is obtained by sampling the learned probabilities; multiple samplings form an ensemble whose outputs are averaged. A sparse “progressive mixing” topology is used to respect TrueNorth’s constrained core-to-core connectivity.

On MNIST, running in real time on TrueNorth, the authors report 99.42% with a 64-member ensemble (30 cores per member; 1920 cores total) at 108 μJ per classification, and 92.7% with a single 5-core network at 0.268 μJ per classification, both at 1 ms/image (1000 images/s). This exceeds prior spiking-network accuracies cited (e.g., 99.12%) and shows substantially better energy/latency than previously reported low-power systems.

The approach relates to: (i) conversion methods from ReLU CNNs to SNNs with high-precision weights (e.g., Diehl et al. 2015; Cao et al. 2015), (ii) training with low-precision/binary weights or neurons (Fiesler 1990; Zhao et al. 1996; “Expectation Backpropagation” by Cheng et al. 2015), and (iii) neuromorphic hardware platforms (TrueNorth, SpiNNaker, Neurogrid). It differs by jointly handling spiking neurons, binary on/off synapses with shared strength tables below trinary precision, and structured sparse connectivity compatible with hardware. It is also related to contemporary binary-weight training with straight-through estimators (e.g., BinaryConnect; Courbariaux et al., NIPS 2015), which the authors may wish to cite and compare.

Quality:
- Soundness: The Gaussian approximation of the weighted sum of independent Bernoulli inputs is reasonable for 256 inputs with balanced positive/negative strengths; using a probit-like activation makes gradients tractable. The approximation that drops the σ^−3 term in ∂ñ/∂c̃ (Eq. 4) needs more justification or an empirical sensitivity analysis (e.g., plot the magnitude ratio across training). The claim “bias gradient equals one” is imprecise; ∂µ/∂b = 1, so ∂ñ/∂b = ∂ñ/∂µ.
- Empirics: The hardware demonstrations and energy measurements are compelling. However, experiments are limited to MNIST. Important ablations are missing: effect of the balanced ±1 initialization vs naive initialization; effect of learning s-values (if possible); validation of the Gaussian/probit approximation against Monte Carlo; impact of constrained connectivity vs denser variants; and comparison to train-then-constrain baselines on the same hardware.
- Measurement: The authors account for active and proportional leakage power; more detail on how leakage is apportioned, and scaling with ensemble size, would improve interpretability. Accuracy/energy tradeoffs vs ensemble size are helpful; adding latency scaling and throughput limits would be useful.

Clarity:
The manuscript is generally well organized and readable. A few issues:
- Equation 3 appears to have typos; the CCDF of a Gaussian should be written clearly (e.g., with Φ or erfc) and with θ specified.
- Equation 4 and the approximation step could be derived more cleanly; explicitly state assumptions (µ≈0, c̃, ñ small vs σ), and show empirical histograms of σ during training.
- The mapping from continuous probabilities to sampled synapses/inputs and the multi-neuron-per-class scheme are clear; a brief pseudocode snippet would aid reproducibility.
- Clearly define the axon-type LUT strengths and the “block” sharing constraint on s-values.

Originality:
The integration of a differentiable probabilistic surrogate for spiking/binary synapses with constrained topology and a decorrelating strength initialization tailored to TrueNorth is novel. Prior work has handled some, but not all, of these constraints simultaneously. The ensemble-from-probabilities trick is simple and effective in this context. The work overlaps conceptually with EBP and binary-weight training, but applied here to spiking deployments with extremely low-precision synapses and hardware-constrained connectivity.

Significance:
Demonstrating high accuracy and unprecedented energy/latency on real neuromorphic silicon is significant. Bridging mainstream backprop with practical deployment constraints can influence both neuromorphic algorithm design and low-power embedded ML. That said, the empirical scope (MNIST only) limits broader impact; showing viability on more challenging datasets (e.g., permutation-invariant MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10 with appropriate input encoding, or event-camera datasets) would strengthen significance.

Specific suggestions:
- Add comparisons to: (a) train-then-convert SNNs under similar connectivity constraints; (b) BinaryConnect/EBP-style training on the same topology, reporting both software accuracy and sampled-hardware accuracy.
- Provide ablations: with/without decorrelation-based s-init; different proportions of positive/negative strengths; learning a small discrete set of s-values; varying fan-in.
- Validate the Gaussian/probit surrogate: compare exact Monte Carlo estimates of firing probability for sampled subsets and quantify approximation error across training.
- Clarify energy measurement methodology and scaling with ensemble size; report per-core active/leakage power and system overheads.
- Fix equation typos; clarify the bias gradient; define all symbols in each equation.
- Discuss whether convolutional weight sharing could be incorporated while meeting TrueNorth constraints, and whether your method extends to convolutional topologies.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled method to train spiking, binary-synapse networks that map directly to real hardware.
- Strong hardware results with excellent energy/latency tradeoffs and competitive MNIST accuracy for SNNs.
- Novel initialization and topology tailored to neuromorphic constraints; simple ensemble mapping.
- Generally well written and reproducible (with minor fixes).

Cons for acceptance:
- Experiments limited to MNIST; no results on more challenging datasets or tasks.
- Missing comparisons to closely related binary-weight training methods and to train-then-constrain baselines under the same topology.
- Approximations in gradients are only heuristically justified; no sensitivity analysis.
- s-values are fixed rather than learned; no ablation to assess suboptimality.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a timely, practical contribution that bridges deep learning and neuromorphic deployment with impressive energy numbers on real hardware. Expanding empirical validation and tightening some theoretical/clarity aspects would make it stronger.
