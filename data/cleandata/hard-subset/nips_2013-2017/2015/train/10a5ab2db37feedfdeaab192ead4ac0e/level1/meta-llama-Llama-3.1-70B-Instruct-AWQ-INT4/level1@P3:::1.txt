This paper proposes a novel approach to bridge the gap between deep learning using backpropagation and energy-efficient neuromorphic computing. The authors present a method to train a neural network using standard backpropagation, while taking into account the constraints of neuromorphic hardware, such as spiking neurons and discrete synapses. The key idea is to treat spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, allowing the network to be trained using backpropagation. The trained network can then be mapped to neuromorphic hardware by sampling the probabilities to create one or more networks, which are merged using ensemble averaging.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. The use of the TrueNorth chip as a deployment platform is a great example of how this approach can be applied in practice. The results are impressive, with the authors achieving 99.42% accuracy on the MNIST dataset with a high-performance network and 92.7% accuracy with a high-efficiency network.

The main strength of this paper is its originality. The authors have identified a significant problem in the field of neuromorphic computing and have proposed a novel solution. The approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of why their method is necessary. The use of backpropagation to train a network that can be mapped to neuromorphic hardware is a significant contribution to the field.

One potential weakness of the paper is the limited scope of the evaluation. The authors only evaluate their approach on the MNIST dataset, which is a relatively simple task. It would be interesting to see how their approach performs on more complex tasks, such as object recognition or natural language processing. Additionally, the authors could provide more details on the computational resources required to train and deploy their network.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a good overview of the existing approaches to neuromorphic computing and deep learning. However, they could provide more details on how their approach differs from existing methods, such as the work on spiking deep neural networks.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. The authors have identified a significant problem and have proposed a novel solution. The approach is well-motivated, and the results are impressive.
