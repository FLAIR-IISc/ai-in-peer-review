This paper presents a compelling approach to bridging the gap between the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing and the performance of deep learning via backpropagation. The central idea – treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities to enable backpropagation training – is well-motivated and demonstrably effective, particularly given the successful deployment on the TrueNorth chip. The MNIST results are impressive, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy and energy efficiency for spiking neural networks.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively sets the stage, highlighting the incompatibility between traditional backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware and outlining the contributions of this work. The detailed explanation of the training network, deployment network, and mapping procedure is commendable. The inclusion of the network initialization strategy based on minimizing covariance between neuron inputs is a particularly insightful contribution, addressing a practical challenge in neuromorphic implementations. The results section is thorough, presenting both high-performance and high-efficiency network configurations with corresponding accuracy and energy consumption figures. The discussion section appropriately contextualizes the work within the broader field and suggests promising avenues for future research.

However, the paper isn't without its weaknesses. While the probabilistic approach is elegant, the approximation used in the backpropagation gradient calculation (dropping the σ<sup>3</sup><sub>j</sub> term) could be further justified. The authors mention the expected conditions for this approximation to hold (µ near zero, ñ<sub>j</sub> and c̃<sub>ij</sub> much less than σ), but a more rigorous analysis or empirical validation of this approximation’s impact on training convergence and accuracy would be valuable. A sensitivity analysis of the learning rate, momentum, and dropout parameters could also strengthen the results. 

The network topology section, while explaining the sliding window approach, lacks a comprehensive justification for the chosen parameters (R, F, S).  Why were these specific values selected? A comparison of different topologies or a more detailed exploration of the parameter space would be beneficial. The explanation of how the “pyramid” shape contributes to performance could be expanded.

Furthermore, while the paper convincingly demonstrates results on MNIST, the generalizability of this approach to more complex datasets remains an open question. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion, but a preliminary investigation, even with a small-scale experiment on a more challenging dataset (e.g., Fashion-MNIST), would strengthen the argument for the broader applicability of their method.  The reliance on a specific hardware platform (TrueNorth) is also a potential limitation, and while the authors acknowledge the possibility of generalization to other neuromorphic architectures, specific considerations for adapting the approach to different platforms should be briefly discussed.

The references are comprehensive and relevant, demonstrating awareness of the state-of-the-art in both deep learning and neuromorphic computing. However, citing work on event-driven spiking convolutional neural networks (e.g., related to the SpikingJelly library) could provide further context.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of treating spiking neurons and synapses as probabilistic variables to enable backpropagation is a significant contribution.
* **Strong Results:**  State-of-the-art performance on MNIST, demonstrating both high accuracy and energy efficiency on TrueNorth.
* **Clarity & Thoroughness:**  The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and provides a detailed methodology.
* **Practical Relevance:**  Addresses a key challenge in deploying deep learning models on energy-constrained embedded systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Approximation Justification:** The gradient approximation requires more rigorous justification.
* **Parameter Selection:**  The rationale behind the network topology parameters is somewhat lacking.
* **Generalizability:**  Limited evidence of performance on datasets more complex than MNIST.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. The authors have successfully demonstrated a practical method for training spiking neural networks using backpropagation, achieving impressive results on a real-world hardware platform. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work far outweigh them.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised regarding the gradient approximation, network topology parameter selection, and potential avenues for demonstrating generalizability.
