Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a probabilistic backpropagation framework that trains networks under the exact constraints of spiking neuromorphic hardware with extremely low-precision synapses and limited fan-in. Discrete synapses and spikes are modeled as continuous probabilities in [0,1], enabling gradient-based optimization of synaptic connection probabilities and biases while keeping synaptic magnitudes fixed to a hardware-friendly set (balanced ±1 blocks). A Gaussian approximation to the preactivation distribution yields a closed-form, differentiable surrogate for spike probability, together with efficient gradients (dropping a small term under a variance-dominance assumption). After training, deployment proceeds by sampling synaptic on/off states and spike inputs; multiple independent samples can be ensembled at test time with simple averaging. A “progressive mixing” topology, akin to locally connected layers with sliding windows and without weight sharing, respects TrueNorth’s constrained core-to-core connectivity. On TrueNorth, MNIST experiments show an energy/accuracy Pareto: a 30-core, 64-member ensemble reaches 99.42% at 108 μJ per image; a 5-core single network reaches 92.7% at 0.268 μJ per image, both at 1 kHz throughput.

Relative to train-then-constrain SNN conversions (e.g., spiking CNNs with high-precision weights and threshold balancing [11,12]), the work performs constrain-then-train so that the training objective matches deployment constraints. Relative to probabilistic/backprop methods for binary/trinary units with full connectivity [14,15], this work tackles spiking neurons with sub-trinary effective synapse precision, block-quantized weights, and strict connectivity constraints, and demonstrates real-time operation on silicon. Reported MNIST accuracy exceeds prior spiking networks with high-precision weights (99.12% in [12]) and binary networks (97.88% in [15]) and achieves substantially better energy/latency than SpiNNaker DBN results (6 mJ at 50 img/s in [16] vs. 108 μJ at 1000 img/s here).

Quality:
- Methodological soundness: Modeling spike events and binary synapses as Bernoulli variables and approximating their weighted sum by a Gaussian is reasonable under independence and sufficient fan-in; the gradient derivation is consistent with this surrogate. The approximation that drops the σ^{-3} term merits empirical validation (e.g., gradient error vs. exact or Monte Carlo estimates), but the balancing of ± weights and 256 inputs per neuron supports the high-variance regime assumption.
- Hardware realism: The mapping to TrueNorth is explicit, including bias-to-leak, binary crossbar, axon-type lookup, and block weight sharing. The initialization strategy that minimizes pairwise inner products between effective weight columns is well-motivated by independence and redundancy reduction arguments.
- Experimental evaluation: Results convincingly demonstrate the energy/accuracy trade-off and real-time performance on a large chip. However, the evaluation is limited to MNIST and relies on sizable ensembles for state-of-the-art accuracy. More granular reporting (e.g., 1/4/16/64-member accuracies for both network sizes; accuracy for a single 30-core member) and ablations (effect of Gaussian approximation, weight balancing, dropout, data augmentation) would strengthen the empirical case. The power metrology includes active plus apportioned leakage; clarifying whether I/O and off-chip host costs are included would aid comparability.
- Comparisons: The claim of best spiking MNIST accuracy appears consistent with [12,15], and the energy/latency comparison versus [16] is favorable, though architectures and tasks differ (DBN vs. feedforward, different hardware). Where possible, matching time windows and reporting latency/energy as a function of time steps would improve fairness.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized and thorough. Some equations suffer from typographic issues (e.g., Equation (3) CCDF form and Equation (4) formatting), and a compact notation table would help. The description of the axon-type LUT and how s-values are assigned per neuron/core is clear but could use a concise schematic. The topology description is clear; explicitly contrasting with standard locally connected and convolutional layers would help readers unfamiliar with TrueNorth constraints.

Originality:
Treating both spikes and discrete synapses as probabilities to enable backprop under neuromorphic constraints, combined with a principled initialization to minimize inter-neuron redundancy and a topology matched to core-level connectivity, represents a novel and practically relevant synthesis. While probabilistic training of binary networks exists [14,15], applying it to spiking neurons with sub-trinary synapse precision, block-quantized strengths, and constrained fan-in, together with on-silicon validation, is a substantive advance.

Significance:
The work provides a concrete bridge between modern supervised training and highly energy-efficient neuromorphic deployment, with strong practical implications for embedded inference. The results are impactful within neuromorphic computing and hardware-aware learning. Broader impact beyond MNIST will depend on demonstrating scalability to larger vision tasks and architectures (e.g., convolutional front-ends) and characterizing the ensemble-resource trade-offs.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
Essential
- Report full accuracy vs. ensemble size for both networks, including single-member accuracy for the 30-core topology; include variance across runs.
- Provide an ablation on the gradient approximation (with/without dropping the σ^{-3} term) or a sanity check via Monte Carlo gradient estimates on a small layer.
- Clarify the power measurement methodology (inclusion of I/O, host, and readout energy) and provide latency per inference, not only throughput.
- Improve equation formatting (particularly Equations (3–4)) and provide a brief notation summary.

Nice-to-have
- Compare against a train-then-constrain baseline implemented on the same hardware (e.g., weight/threshold balancing [12]) to isolate the benefit of constrain-then-train under identical measurement conditions.
- Explore a convolutional or locally shared-weight variant compatible with TrueNorth constraints to gauge scalability to more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10).
- Investigate learning a small set of s-values per axon type (rather than fixing ±1), if hardware permits, to quantify potential gains.
- Discuss robustness to input correlations that violate independence assumptions and whether the initialization continues to minimize redundancy under realistic input statistics.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, hardware-aligned learning framework with principled derivations and an efficient gradient.
- Strong on-chip demonstration with record energy/latency for MNIST and competitive spiking accuracy.
- Novel integration of probabilistic training with extreme synaptic precision and connectivity constraints.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Evaluation limited to MNIST; reliance on large ensembles for top accuracy raises questions about practicality and scalability.
- Some theoretical assumptions (Gaussianity, independence, dropped terms) are not empirically validated.
- Comparisons to prior methods could be made fairer by controlling for hardware and measurement protocols.

Overall, the paper makes a compelling case for a practical and theoretically grounded pathway to backprop-trained, energy-efficient spiking deployment, with room for broader validation and clearer empirical analyses.
