This paper addresses the critical problem of bridging the gap between the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing and the performance of backpropagation-trained deep neural networks. The core idea – treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities to enable backpropagation – is a clever and potentially impactful approach. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and demonstrates promising results on the MNIST dataset using the TrueNorth chip.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The problem addressed is highly relevant to the field, particularly as interest in deploying deep learning models on edge devices increases. The probabilistic approach to mapping continuous-valued networks to discrete neuromorphic hardware is novel and elegantly described. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating both high accuracy (99.42% with an ensemble of 64) and impressive energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image with an ensemble of 1) on TrueNorth. The comparison to previous work, both on spiking networks and low-power approaches, clearly highlights the contribution of this work. The detailed description of the network topology and training methodology, including the initialization procedure and the derivation of gradients, provides sufficient information for reproducibility. The inclusion of a discussion section acknowledging limitations and future directions is commendable.

However, there are some weaknesses that could be addressed. While the results on MNIST are compelling, the generalization to more complex datasets is not explored. Expanding the experiments to include more challenging image classification tasks (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) would significantly strengthen the paper.  The assumption of independence between inputs in the Gaussian approximation used for the neuron activation function (Equation 2) may be a simplification. Investigating the impact of input correlations on the accuracy of this approximation would be valuable. The hyperparameter settings (learning rate decay schedule, dropout rate, mini-batch size, etc.) are mentioned briefly but a more comprehensive ablation study to demonstrate their influence on performance would be helpful. Although the paper describes balancing synaptic strength values to minimize redundancy, it would be beneficial to show empirically how this initialization affects training speed and final accuracy.  The network topology, while suitable for TrueNorth, might not be optimal for all neuromorphic architectures. A brief discussion of how the proposed approach could be adapted to other platforms would broaden the appeal of the work.  Finally, the comparison to other methods is limited to accuracy and energy efficiency. Including metrics such as inference latency and model size would provide a more complete picture of the performance trade-offs.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and impactful approach to bridging the gap between backpropagation and neuromorphic computing.
*   Strong experimental results on MNIST, demonstrating high accuracy and impressive energy efficiency on TrueNorth.
*   Clear and well-written paper with sufficient detail for reproducibility.
*   Addresses a highly relevant and important problem in the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Limited evaluation to a single dataset (MNIST).
*   Simplifying assumptions (e.g., input independence) that may affect accuracy.
*   Lack of comprehensive hyperparameter ablation study.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results. While some limitations exist, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  A strong recommendation is to expand the experimental evaluation to more complex datasets. Addressing the other minor weaknesses would further strengthen the paper.  Given the novelty and potential impact of this work, it is recommended for acceptance.
