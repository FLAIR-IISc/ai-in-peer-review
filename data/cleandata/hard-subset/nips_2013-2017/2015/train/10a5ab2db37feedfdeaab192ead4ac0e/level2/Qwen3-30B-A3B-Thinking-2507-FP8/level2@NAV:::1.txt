

This paper presents a compelling approach to bridge the gap between backpropagation-based deep learning and energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware. The authors propose a "constrain-then-train" methodology where they treat spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, enabling standard backpropagation training for networks that can directly map to neuromorphic hardware with spiking neurons and low-precision synapses. The main claims are: (1) they've demonstrated the first training methodology using backpropagation for networks employing spiking neurons, sub-trinary synapses, and constrained connectivity; (2) they achieve 99.42% accuracy on MNIST with an ensemble of 64 networks (108 μJ/image), surpassing previous spiking network results; and (3) they achieve 92.7% accuracy with a single network at 0.268 μJ/image, representing the best published power efficiency for digit recognition.

The paper's strengths lie in its practical implementation on TrueNorth hardware, clear demonstration of the training-deployment mapping, and comprehensive comparison to previous work. The experimental results are well-supported with statistically significant MNIST benchmarks, showing clear energy-accuracy tradeoffs. The approach is novel in combining all three elements (spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity) in a single training framework, addressing a significant limitation in neuromorphic computing.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The claim of novelty is slightly overstated, as probabilistic approaches to backpropagation with binary neurons/synapses have been previously explored (e.g., Cheng et al., 2015). More critically, the paper only evaluates on MNIST, which is a relatively simple dataset, and provides no evidence of scalability to more complex vision tasks where deep learning typically excels. The theoretical justification for the Gaussian approximation in Equation 3 appears weak, with insufficient analysis of approximation errors. The network initialization method, while clever, lacks empirical validation of its effectiveness.

The paper is clearly written with good organization, though some technical details about the Gaussian approximation could be clarified. The significance is high for energy-constrained edge computing applications, but the limited scope on MNIST somewhat diminishes the impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better contextualize the novelty and address the theoretical limitations.
