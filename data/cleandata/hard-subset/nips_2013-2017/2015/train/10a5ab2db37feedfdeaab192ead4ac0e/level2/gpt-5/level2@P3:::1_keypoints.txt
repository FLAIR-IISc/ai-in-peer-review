- Proposes a probabilistic backprop framework modeling discrete synapses and spikes as probabilities to enable gradient-based training under TrueNorth constraints.
- Models synaptic magnitudes as hardware-friendly block-quantized balanced ±1 weights while training connection probabilities and biases.
- Uses a Gaussian approximation to preactivation to obtain a closed-form differentiable surrogate for spike probability and efficient gradients.
- Drops a σ^{-3} term in gradient computation under a variance-dominance assumption to simplify optimization.
- Deployment samples synaptic on/off states and spike inputs; multiple samples can be ensembled by simple averaging.
- Introduces a “progressive mixing” topology (locally connected sliding windows without weight sharing) matching TrueNorth core-to-core connectivity.
- Demonstrates on-chip MNIST: 30-core 64-member ensemble 99.42% at 108 μJ/img and 5-core single net 92.7% at 0.268 μJ/img at 1 kHz.
- Approach is constrain-then-train, differing from train-then-constrain SNN conversions and prior probabilistic binary/trinary methods with full connectivity.
- Modeling spikes/synapses as Bernoulli and Gaussian approximation is reasonable with independence and sufficient fan-in; gradient derivation is consistent.
- The σ^{-3} term omission requires empirical validation, though balanced weights and large fan-in support the high-variance assumption.
- Mapping to TrueNorth is explicit (bias-to-leak, binary crossbar, axon-type LUT, block sharing) and initialization minimizing pairwise inner products is well-motivated.
- Experiments convincingly show energy/accuracy trade-offs and real-time on-chip performance but are limited to MNIST and rely on large ensembles for top accuracy.
- Needs more granular reporting (accuracies for 1/4/16/64-member ensembles, single 30-core member) and variance across runs.
- Ablations required: effect of Gaussian approximation, weight balancing, dropout, data augmentation, and sanity checks via Monte Carlo gradients.
- Power metrology needs clarification on inclusion of I/O, host, and off-chip readout energy; report latency per inference in addition to throughput.
- Comparisons to prior work are favorable but would be fairer if architectures, time windows, and measurement protocols were matched.
- Paper is generally clear and thorough but has typographic/formatting issues in equations and would benefit from a compact notation table and schematic of axon-type LUT.
- Originality: novel synthesis applying probabilistic training to spiking neurons with sub-trinary synapse precision, block quantization, constrained fan-in, and on-silicon validation.
- Significance: provides a practical bridge between supervised training and energy-efficient neuromorphic deployment; broader impact depends on scalability to larger vision tasks and architectures.
- Essential suggested improvements: full accuracy vs. ensemble size, gradient-ablation or Monte Carlo check, clarify power/latency methodology, fix equation formatting and add notation summary.
- Nice-to-have extensions: compare to train-then-constrain baseline on same hardware, explore convolutional/shared-weight variants, learn limited s-values per axon, and test robustness to input correlations.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled, hardware-aligned learning framework with efficient gradients and strong on-chip energy/latency results and competitive accuracy.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited evaluation (MNIST), reliance on ensembles raises practicality concerns, key theoretical assumptions lack empirical validation, and comparisons could be fairer.
- Overall: compelling, practically relevant, and theoretically grounded work that needs broader validation and clearer empirical analyses.
