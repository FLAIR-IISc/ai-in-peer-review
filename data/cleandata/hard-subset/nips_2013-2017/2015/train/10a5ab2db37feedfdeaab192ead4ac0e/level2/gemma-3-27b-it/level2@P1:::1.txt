This paper addresses the critical challenge of bridging the gap between the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing and the performance of deep learning algorithms trained with backpropagation. The core idea – treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities to enable backpropagation training, then mapping this probabilistic network to a neuromorphic hardware implementation – is a compelling and potentially impactful contribution. The authors demonstrate this approach on the TrueNorth chip with the MNIST dataset, achieving promising results in terms of both accuracy and energy efficiency.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of probabilistic backpropagation with constrained network topology and direct mapping to neuromorphic hardware (specifically TrueNorth) is novel. The approach of "constrain-then-train" is a sensible strategy.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported accuracy (99.42% with ensemble of 64, 92.7% with ensemble of 1) on MNIST is competitive, particularly considering the energy efficiency. Achieving 4 µJ per classification at 95% accuracy is a significant improvement over previously published results.
* **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The introduction clearly articulates the incompatibility between traditional deep learning and neuromorphic hardware, and the need for a solution like the one presented.
* **Detailed Implementation:** The paper provides a good level of detail regarding the network topology, training methodology, and mapping process. The inclusion of equations for the gradient calculations and network initialization is valuable.
* **Hardware Demonstration:** Validating the approach on actual hardware (TrueNorth) is a strong point, demonstrating the feasibility of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Dataset:** The evaluation is solely based on the MNIST dataset. While a good starting point, it doesn’t demonstrate the generalizability of the approach to more complex, real-world problems (e.g., image classification with ImageNet, object detection, or speech recognition).
* **Network Topology Choices:** The rationale behind the specific network topology (sliding window approach, R, F, S parameters) could be further elaborated. How sensitive are the results to these parameter choices?  A more thorough exploration of different topologies might be beneficial.
* **Approximations in Backpropagation:** The simplification of the backpropagation gradient calculation (dropping the second term in Equation 4) could potentially impact accuracy. A justification for this approximation, beyond the stated assumptions, would be helpful.  Did the authors explore the impact of including the full term?
* **Initialization Strategy Complexity:** The detailed explanation of the network initialization (minimizing the absolute value of the inner product between columns of the weight matrix) is quite complex. While aiming for efficiency, this could be a barrier to adoption for others. A more intuitive explanation or simplification might be considered.
* **Ensemble Averaging Overhead:** While ensemble averaging improves accuracy, it significantly increases the computational cost and hardware requirements (number of cores). The trade-off between accuracy and efficiency needs to be discussed more thoroughly.  Is the performance gain worth the additional hardware?

**Originality:**

The paper presents a novel combination of techniques. While individual elements (backpropagation with spiking neurons, probabilistic approaches) have been explored previously, the specific integration of these elements – constrained network topology, probabilistic representation, and direct mapping to neuromorphic hardware – is original. The "constrain-then-train" philosophy is also a distinct contribution. The authors do a good job of referencing related work and highlighting the differences.

**Significance:**

This work has the potential to be significant. Bridging the gap between backpropagation and neuromorphic computing is a crucial step towards building energy-efficient embedded systems for real-world applications.  The demonstrated energy efficiency on TrueNorth is particularly promising.  If the approach can be generalized to more complex datasets and tasks, it could have a substantial impact on the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and potentially impactful approach to bridging deep learning and neuromorphic computing.
* Strong empirical results on MNIST, demonstrating both accuracy and energy efficiency.
* Clear motivation, detailed implementation, and validation on hardware.
* The “constrain-then-train” approach is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited evaluation on a single dataset (MNIST).
* Approximations in backpropagation and the complexity of the initialization strategy warrant further scrutiny.
* The reliance on ensemble averaging adds overhead.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. The authors have clearly demonstrated the feasibility of their approach on the TrueNorth chip. While the limitations regarding dataset complexity and some implementation details exist, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should be encouraged to expand their evaluation to more complex datasets and provide a more detailed analysis of the approximations made in the backpropagation algorithm and the impact of ensemble size. Addressing these points would further strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
