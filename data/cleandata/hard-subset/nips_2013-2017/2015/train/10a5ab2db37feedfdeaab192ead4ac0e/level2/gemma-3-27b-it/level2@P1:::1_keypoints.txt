- Core idea: Treat spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities to enable backpropagation, then map the probabilistic network to neuromorphic hardware (TrueNorth).
- Novelty: Combining probabilistic backpropagation with constrained topology and direct hardware mapping ("constrain-then-train") is novel and sensible.
- Strong empirical results: High MNIST accuracy (99.42% ensemble of 64, 92.7% single) with excellent energy efficiency (4 µJ/classification at 95%).
- Clear motivation: Paper clearly explains the incompatibility between standard deep learning and neuromorphic hardware and the need for this approach.
- Detailed implementation: Provides useful details on topology, training, mapping, gradient equations, and initialization.
- Hardware demonstration: Validated approach on real TrueNorth hardware, demonstrating practical feasibility.
- Limited dataset: Evaluation is restricted to MNIST, limiting evidence of generalizability to complex, real-world tasks.
- Network topology choices: Rationale for sliding-window topology and parameters (R, F, S) is underexplained; sensitivity to these choices is unclear.
- Backpropagation approximations: Dropping the second term in the gradient (Equation 4) may affect accuracy; justification and exploration of the full term are lacking.
- Initialization complexity: The weight-initialization strategy (minimizing inner-product magnitudes) is complex and may hinder adoption; a simpler explanation or method is needed.
- Ensemble averaging overhead: Accuracy gains from ensembles come at significant computational and hardware cost; trade-offs between accuracy and efficiency need deeper analysis.
- Originality: While building on prior elements, the specific integration and "constrain-then-train" philosophy constitute an original contribution.
- Significance: Potentially significant for energy-efficient embedded systems if generalizable to more complex datasets and tasks.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, impactful approach with strong MNIST results, clear motivation, detailed methods, and hardware validation.
- Arguments against acceptance: Single-dataset evaluation, questionable gradient approximations, complex initialization, and reliance on costly ensembles.
- Overall assessment: Strong paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions; authors should expand evaluations and analyze approximation and ensemble impacts.
