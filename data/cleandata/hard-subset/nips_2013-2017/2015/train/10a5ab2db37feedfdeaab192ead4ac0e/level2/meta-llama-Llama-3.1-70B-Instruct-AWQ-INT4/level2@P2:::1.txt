This paper proposes a novel approach to reconcile the incompatibility between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware. The authors introduce a learning rule and network topology that enables training a network using backpropagation, while ensuring a direct representation in the spiking, low synaptic precision deployment system. The key idea is to treat spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, allowing the network to be trained using standard backpropagation.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the network topology, training methodology, and mapping from the training network to the deployment network. The results are impressive, with the authors achieving 99.42% accuracy on the MNIST dataset using an ensemble of 64 networks, and 92.7% accuracy using an ensemble of 1 network, with a significant reduction in energy consumption.

The paper's main contribution is the demonstration of a learning rule and network topology that can bridge the gap between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware. The authors show that their approach can be used to train networks that naturally map to neuromorphic hardware, while achieving high performance and energy efficiency.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. The results are well-presented, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of their findings. The paper is well-referenced, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of related work.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the authors do not provide a detailed comparison with other approaches that have been proposed to address the incompatibility between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware. While the authors mention some related work, a more detailed comparison would be helpful in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
