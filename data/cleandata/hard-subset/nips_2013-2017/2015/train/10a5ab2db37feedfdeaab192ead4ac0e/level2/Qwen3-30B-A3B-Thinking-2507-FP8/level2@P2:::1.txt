

This paper proposes a novel approach to bridge the gap between backpropagation-based deep learning and neuromorphic hardware by treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities. The authors present a "constrain-then-train" methodology where networks are trained offline with continuous-valued inputs, neuron outputs, and synaptic weights (constrained to [0,1]), then mapped to neuromorphic hardware by sampling these probabilities to create one or more networks that are merged using ensemble averaging. The approach is demonstrated on the TrueNorth neuromorphic chip using MNIST, achieving 99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ per image with an ensemble of 64, and 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image with a single network.

The paper's main strengths lie in its clear problem formulation, well-motivated methodology, and impressive experimental results. The "constrain-then-train" approach is well-justified as a more direct alternative to "train-then-constrain" methods, with the authors convincingly arguing that training within hardware constraints provides a direct correspondence between training error and deployment error. The experimental results on TrueNorth are particularly compelling, demonstrating both high accuracy and unprecedented energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image) compared to prior low-power approaches. The paper provides sufficient technical detail to reproduce the key steps of the methodology, including the probabilistic representation of spiking neurons and the network initialization procedure.

However, several significant weaknesses require attention. First, the theoretical justification for the Gaussian approximation in Equation 3 is insufficiently rigorous. The authors assume independence of inputs and sufficient numbers to approximate the neuron firing probability as Gaussian, but this approximation's validity across different network architectures and layers is not thoroughly analyzed or validated. The approximation's error could significantly impact training, yet the paper provides no evidence of how this affects the final network performance.

Second, the paper's claims of novelty and "best accuracy to date" are not fully substantiated. While the authors cite prior work on backpropagation with spiking neurons [9-12] and networks with binary synapses [13-15], they do not provide a comprehensive comparison with all relevant prior work. For instance, the paper claims their approach achieves "best accuracy to date" (99.42%) compared to [12] (99.12%) and [15] (97.88%), but does not compare with other recent spiking network approaches that might have achieved higher accuracy. The paper should include a more thorough comparison with the state of the art.

Third, the paper does not adequately address the limitations of the approach. The authors claim the method can be extended to more complex datasets, but provide no analysis of how the Gaussian approximation might fail in deeper networks or with more complex data distributions. The scalability to larger datasets and more complex architectures is not demonstrated or even discussed in detail.

The paper is well-written and organized, with clear technical explanations. However, the references to prior work could be strengthened to better establish the novelty of the contribution. The paper mentions "probabilistic backpropagation approaches" [14,15] but does not clearly differentiate their approach from these existing methods.

In terms of significance, the work addresses a critical problem in deploying deep learning on energy-constrained hardware, and the demonstrated energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image) is impressive. However, the paper would be stronger if it more clearly articulated how this approach advances the state of the art beyond incremental improvements, particularly regarding the theoretical foundation of the probabilistic representation.

Overall, this is a promising and potentially impactful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of energy-efficient neural network deployment. The main issues are the insufficient theoretical justification for key approximations, incomplete comparison with prior work, and inadequate discussion of limitations. Addressing these would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

The paper should be accepted, but the authors should address the theoretical concerns regarding the Gaussian approximation, provide a more comprehensive comparison with prior work, and more thoroughly discuss the limitations and scalability of their approach. The experimental results are compelling and well-documented, and the "constrain-then-train" methodology represents a meaningful step toward practical deployment of deep learning on neuromorphic hardware.
