- Paper introduces a probabilistic "constrain-then-train" method to map backprop-trained networks to spiking networks with low-precision synapses.  
- Validated on MNIST using IBM TrueNorth, achieving 99.42% accuracy and 0.268 µJ per classification.  

Strengths:
- Novel reconciliation of backpropagation with neuromorphic constraints (spiking neurons + low-precision synapses).  
- Strong experimental results showing high accuracy and unprecedented energy efficiency for SNNs.  
- Practical relevance for embedded, energy-constrained systems and real hardware deployment (TrueNorth).  
- Clear, well-organized presentation with sufficient methodological and reproducibility detail.  
- Discussion of potential extensions to more complex architectures (e.g., convolutional networks).  

Weaknesses:
- Evaluation limited to MNIST, which is not representative of real-world dataset complexity.  
- Approach is tailored to TrueNorth without empirical evidence of portability to other neuromorphic platforms.  
- Insufficient discussion of trade-offs and limitations of "constrain-then-train" versus "train-then-constrain" (training complexity, convergence).  
- Top accuracy relies on a 64-member ensemble, raising practicality concerns for single-network deployment.  

Suggestions:
- Test the method on more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) to demonstrate scalability.  
- Provide experiments or analysis on other neuromorphic hardware to support generalizability claims.  
- Add a detailed discussion of the limitations and trade-offs of the constrain-then-train paradigm.  
- Investigate approaches to improve single-network performance without large ensembles.  

Recommendation:
- Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the noted weaknesses.
