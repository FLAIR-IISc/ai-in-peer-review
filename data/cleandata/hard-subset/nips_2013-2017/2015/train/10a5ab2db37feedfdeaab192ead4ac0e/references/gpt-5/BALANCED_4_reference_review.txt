Summary
This paper proposes a hardware-aware training method that reconciles backpropagation with highly constrained neuromorphic substrates (specifically IBM TrueNorth). The core idea is a constrain-then-train approach: during offline training, inputs, neuron outputs, and synaptic connections are represented as continuous variables in [0,1] that encode probabilities of spikes or of synapses being “on.” A Gaussian approximation of the sum of (weighted) Bernoulli variables yields a differentiable mapping from synapse-connection probabilities and biases to firing probabilities, enabling backpropagation to learn binary connectivity (c̃ij) and neuron biases, while synaptic strengths (sij) are fixed a priori to balanced ±1 patterns to reduce redundancy. The topology uses sliding windows with constrained fan-in per core and progressive mixing across layers, matching TrueNorth’s limited, shared core inputs. Deployment samples the trained probabilistic network to produce one or more binary/spiking instantiations; an ensemble average further improves accuracy.

On MNIST, the authors report energy–accuracy trade-offs on TrueNorth: a 5-core network achieves 92.7% at 0.268 µJ/classification; a 30-core network with a 64-member ensemble achieves 99.42% at 108 µJ/classification (1 kHz throughput). They claim to surpass prior spiking or binary networks and to demonstrate state-of-the-art energy per classification among low-power approaches.

Relation to prior work: The approach is related to spiking network training and conversions to SNNs (e.g., Cao et al. 2015; Diehl et al. 2015), and to probabilistic/binary backprop variants (e.g., Zhao et al. 1996; Soudry et al. 2015). The paper differs by jointly addressing spiking neurons, sub-trinary synaptic precision (effectively binary crossbar with amortized LUT), and constrained connectivity, with direct mapping to a deployed neuromorphic chip.

Strengths
- Clear hardware–algorithm co-design: training explicitly respects TrueNorth constraints (binary synapses, spike communication, limited fan-in), avoiding post hoc discretization misalignment.
- Elegant probabilistic relaxation: the Gaussian approximation yields a simple, differentiable surrogate for spiking/binary behavior, enabling standard backprop machinery.
- Practical deployment: real-time inference on TrueNorth with measured energy and throughput; the ensemble trick leverages the probabilistic training without retraining.
- Weight initialization motivated by decorrelation: balanced ±1 and LUT usage to minimize pairwise correlations provides a principled and hardware-realizable initialization.
- Strong MNIST results and energy numbers; the low-energy operating point is impressive, and the 99.42% accuracy with ensembles is competitive for spiking systems.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical soundness:
- Equation/derivation issues: Equation (3) appears malformed (the complementary Gaussian CDF should yield a 0.5·erfc((θ−μ)/(√2σ)) or equivalent). More critically, the text states “the corresponding gradient with respect to the bias term equals one,” which contradicts the earlier derivation; ∂ñ/∂b = ∂ñ/∂μ·∂μ/∂b with ∂μ/∂b = 1, but ∂ñ/∂μ ≠ 1. These errors need correction and could affect implementation clarity.
- Approximation justification: Dropping the σ−3 term in Eq. (4) relies on assumptions (balanced weights, μ≈0, large σ) but no empirical ablation is shown to confirm negligible impact. Independence assumptions and the CLT/Gaussian approximation for relatively small fan-in per synapse-type also merit sensitivity analysis.
- Limited evaluation: Only MNIST is considered. No results on more challenging datasets (SVHN, CIFAR-10) or on convolutional variants, despite the stated generality.
- Comparisons: Accuracy comparisons use a large ensemble (up to 64 members); fair comparisons should control for parameter/compute budget and ensemble usage across baselines. Also, the use of data augmentation is not matched across quoted baselines.
- Energy accounting: There is a discrepancy between the abstract-level claim (4 µJ/class at 95% acc.) and the Results section (0.268 µJ/class at 92.7% and 108 µJ/class at 99.42%). Please reconcile measurement methods (active vs leakage inclusion, number of cores in use, I/O cost) and ensure apples-to-apples comparisons with prior hardware.

Clarity:
- Several equations are hard to parse or contain typos. The gradient derivation is sketched but not fully detailed; a supplementary derivation would help reproducibility.
- Details of mapping biases to TrueNorth (leak, threshold, floor), quantization effects (bias clipping to integer), and LUT programming for s-values are under-specified. How are fractional biases handled and what is the quantization error?
- The topology description would benefit from a concise, parameterized specification and a justification of chosen R,F,S values and fan-in coverage.

Originality:
- The combination—probabilistic backprop over connectivity probabilities, constrained connectivity, fixed low-precision weights, and direct deployment on TrueNorth—is novel and practically meaningful. That said, the training objective is close in spirit to expectation backpropagation and other probabilistic/binary relaxations; the paper would benefit from a deeper contrast (estimator variance, gradient forms, treatment of σ, and handling of hardware constraints).

Significance:
- If broadly validated, the approach is valuable for energy-efficient deployment of DNN-like models on neuromorphic hardware. The demonstrated energy–accuracy trade-offs are compelling. However, significance is limited by the narrow evaluation (MNIST) and reliance on ensembles to reach top accuracy.

Questions for the authors
1. Please correct Eq. (3) and the bias-gradient statement; provide the exact expressions used in code.
2. How sensitive is training to the dropped term in Eq. (4) and to the independence/Gaussian assumptions? Any ablation?
3. How are per-neuron LUT s-values chosen across axon types? Are values strictly ±1, or are multiple magnitudes used?
4. Can you provide accuracy vs ensemble size for both networks, and compare to a single larger non-ensemble model with similar core budget?
5. Energy accounting: clarify active vs leakage, I/O overhead, and reconcile the 4 µJ figure with the numbers in Fig. 3C. Provide per-classification energy at 95% under the same accounting used in Fig. 3C.
6. How does this method compare to train-then-constrain SNN conversion run on the same TrueNorth topology?

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix equations/typos and add a supplementary derivation for gradients, including the bias case.
- Add ablations: effect of dropping the σ−3 term; role of the ±1 initialization vs random; impact of constrained connectivity; ensemble size vs single-model capacity.
- Evaluate on at least one more dataset and/or a convolutional variant (your method seems readily adaptable).
- Provide a fair baseline on TrueNorth using a train-then-constrain conversion or a high-precision synapse version, matched for cores and without ensembles.
- Clarify energy methodology and report confidence intervals or variance across runs/samplings.
- Discuss training stability and convergence (e.g., learning rate schedules, sensitivity to momentum, batch size).

Pros for acceptance
- Timely and relevant hardware-aware learning method with direct deployment.
- Strong energy efficiency and real-time demonstration on a neuromorphic chip.
- Conceptually simple, practical, and extensible probabilistic relaxation.

Cons for acceptance
- Equation/derivation errors and lack of rigorous validation of approximations.
- Limited empirical scope (MNIST only) and heavy reliance on ensembles for top accuracy.
- Incomplete and potentially inconsistent energy comparisons.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on correcting the technical/clarity issues and strengthening empirical validation. The paper advances an important direction in neuromorphic learning and provides a concrete, deployable methodology, but it would benefit from broader evaluation and tighter analysis.
