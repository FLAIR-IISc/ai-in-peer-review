Summary
This paper proposes a constrain-then-train methodology that reconciles backpropagation with the constraints of neuromorphic hardware (demonstrated on IBM TrueNorth). The key ideas are: (1) represent inputs, neuron outputs, and synaptic connectivity as probabilities in [0,1] so that the same network can be trained with backprop and then mapped to a spiking deployment by sampling; (2) factor synaptic weights into binary connectivity variables cij and a small set of fixed strength values sij (shared through the TrueNorth axon-type table), and learn only connection probabilities and biases; (3) use a Gaussian approximation to the sum of weighted Bernoulli inputs to derive differentiable neuron activation probabilities and gradients; (4) pre-initialize the discrete strength matrix S to maximize pairwise decorrelation (balanced ±1, minimized column inner products) under hardware constraints; and (5) adopt a progressive “sliding-window” topology that respects TrueNorth’s core-level fan-in limits while mixing inputs across layers.

On MNIST, the approach attains 99.42% accuracy using a 30-core network and a 64-member ensemble (1920 cores total) at 108 µJ per classification, and 92.7% with a minimal 5-core network at 0.268 µJ per classification. The system runs in real time at 1 kHz on-chip. The authors argue these are the best reported accuracy and energy-efficiency among spiking systems and low-power deployments at the time.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself relative to: spiking networks trained with backprop but high-precision weights; networks with discrete weights but continuous neurons; binary/binarized networks with full connectivity; and spiking conversions of trained ANNs. The novelty here is the joint handling of spiking neurons, sub-trinary per-synapse memory (binary crossbar plus shared-weight lookup), and constrained connectivity, with a direct probabilistic mapping from training to deployment. The progressive mixing topology is related to convolutional sliding windows but uses independent weights to align with hardware constraints. The paper cites relevant 2013–2015 work (e.g., Diehl & Neil, SpiNNaker DBNs, expectation backprop) and clarifies differences.

Strengths
- Conceptually bold and elegant bridge: the probabilistic training domain maps directly to stochastic spikes and binary connectivity via sampling, avoiding lossy post-hoc discretization.
- Hardware relevance: the design respects TrueNorth’s architectural constraints (1-bit crossbar, 4 axon types, limited fan-in) and runs in real time with measured energy, not just simulation.
- Weight initialization scheme grounded in decorrelation and balance under discrete constraints; simple and hardware-friendly.
- Topology that scales input mixing under limited core connectivity, with a clear sliding-window construction.
- Empirical results are compelling for MNIST: high accuracy at low energy, with a clean accuracy–energy trade-off via ensemble size. The 0.268 µJ/class figure is particularly impressive.
- Practical training recipe with standard deep learning heuristics (minibatching, momentum, dropout, augmentation), supporting reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Derivation/assumptions: The Gaussian/independence approximation and the gradient simplification (dropping the σ^-3 term) are plausible but not validated with ablations or diagnostics. It is unclear how often µ ≈ 0 and µ, ñi, c̃ij ≪ σ actually hold during training and how sensitive training is if they do not.
- Limited scope: Only MNIST is evaluated. This leaves open whether the method scales to more complex vision tasks (e.g., CIFAR-10/100) or non-vision modalities.
- Ensemble dependence: The best accuracy uses a 64-member ensemble; single-model performance for the 30-core network is not reported. The energy–accuracy curve is promising, but the headline 99.42% is partly an ensemble effect.
- Comparisons: No head-to-head on the same hardware versus train-then-constrain spiking conversions, or versus contemporary binary/binarized networks trained with STE-like gradients. A controlled comparison would strengthen claims.
- Clarity/details: Some mapping details (bias/leak quantization effects, the exact nine-bit strength values and their assignment across axon types, thresholds) are lightly specified; reproducibility would benefit from more explicit parameterization. Minor equation formatting issues (Eq. 3) and notational overload (c vs c̃).
- Claim phrasing: “less bits than trinary” could be clarified. Each synapse is 1 bit plus a fractional share of the axon-type lookup table; the effective precision per synapse is architecture-dependent.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a coherent algorithm–hardware story and real measurements. The main technical risk is the strength of the approximations and lack of ablations, but the empirical results suggest the approach works in practice on MNIST.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Figures/tables (referenced) appear to support the narrative. The paper could add: (i) a brief algorithm box for training and mapping; (ii) more explicit TrueNorth parameter settings; and (iii) a small ablation on the gradient approximation or initialization scheme.

Originality
High. The constrain-then-train probabilistic bridge to spiking with binary connectivity and constrained fan-in, plus the decorrelating initialization within TrueNorth’s idiosyncrasies, is novel relative to prior spiking conversions or binary networks.

Significance
For neuromorphic computing, this is an impactful step: it provides a principled, backprop-compatible path to train networks that map 1:1 onto real hardware with excellent energy efficiency. Broader significance would be stronger with results beyond MNIST, but the methodological contribution is promising and likely to spur follow-up.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Report single-model accuracy for the 30-core network, and a more complete accuracy–ensemble-size curve with energy.
- Provide an ablation: full Eq. 4 vs. the approximation; and with/without the S initialization.
- Compare against a train-then-constrain baseline on TrueNorth (e.g., a converted convnet) under similar budgets.
- Clarify how nine-bit strengths and four axon types are chosen/assigned and the exact quantization/rounding of biases.
- Discuss training compute cost and wall-clock vs. conventional training.

Pros
- Novel, principled bridge between backprop and spiking neuromorphics
- Real-time hardware demo with state-of-the-art energy efficiency
- Simple, scalable training and mapping procedure
- Clear, hardware-aware topology and initialization

Cons
- Only MNIST; heavy reliance on ensembles for top accuracy
- Approximations not empirically justified via ablations
- Limited comparative baselines and some clarity gaps

Recommendation: 8 (Strong accept). Despite limited scope, the conceptual advance and hardware-backed validation are significant and timely; minor methodological gaps are acceptable given the novelty and potential impact.
