Summary
This paper proposes a “constrain-then-train” approach that enables backpropagation-based offline training of networks that map directly to neuromorphic hardware with spiking neurons, 1-bit synapses (plus a small per-neuron LUT), and limited fan-in/out (TrueNorth). The training network uses continuous variables in [0,1] that represent probabilities of spikes (activations) and synapse-on states, with a Gaussian approximation of the weighted sum of Bernoulli inputs to compute firing probabilities and their gradients. The learned parameters are synapse connection probabilities and biases; synaptic strengths are fixed a priori via an initialization scheme designed to reduce inter-neuron redundancy by balancing ±1 and minimizing column inner products under TrueNorth constraints. A sliding-window “progressive mixing” topology provides increasingly global receptive fields while respecting per-core connectivity limits. On MNIST, the authors report 99.42% accuracy using a 30-core network sampled into a 64-member ensemble (1920 cores) at 108 µJ/classification, and 92.70% at 0.268 µJ/classification for a 5-core single-member model, both at 1 kHz throughput.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to ANN-to-SNN conversion approaches and spiking training methods (e.g., Diehl et al. 2015, Cao et al. 2015), probabilistic/backprop in low-precision/binary networks (e.g., Zhao et al. 1996; Cheng et al. 2015), and low-resolution synapses (Müller & Indiveri 2015). The claimed novelty is the combination of spiking neurons, sub-trinary effective synapse precision (1-bit on/off + shared LUT) and constrained connectivity, trained end-to-end to map directly to deployment. This is an interesting and useful contribution, though stronger positioning against contemporary binary-weight training and off-the-shelf ANN→SNN methods would help.

Strengths
- Clear target: practical training that matches neuromorphic constraints (spikes, 1-bit synapses, limited connectivity) and runs on silicon with impressive energy efficiency.
- Principled, if approximate, probabilistic forward model enabling gradients through thresholding via Gaussian CCDF.
- Hardware-aware topology and initialization: the sliding-window scheme and ±1 weight design are well-motivated by the architecture.
- Real-time hardware results; accuracy/energy tradeoff via ensembles is demonstrated.

Weaknesses
- Over-reliance on approximations and unvalidated assumptions (independence, Gaussianity, balanced positive/negative weights, σ-dominance) without ablation or sensitivity analysis.
- Limited empirical scope (MNIST only), with ensemble-heavy claims for accuracy and ambiguous energy accounting; no fair comparison to single-model SNN baselines with similar compute/cores.
- Methodological gaps/ambiguities (loss definition for multiclass, bias gradient, dropout implementation, mapping quantization effects).
- Several mathematical/notation errors and imprecise statements; numerous formatting issues.
- Claims like “optimal weight subspace” are too strong given the heuristic construction.

Quality
- The forward probabilistic model and gradient derivation are reasonable in spirit, but important details are shaky:
  - Eq. 3: the Gaussian CCDF is written unclearly and appears to omit the √2 factor; the standard form is 0.5 erfc((θ−μ)/(√2σ)).
  - Eq. 4: the approximation dropping the σ−3 term is asserted without empirical validation; the stated assumptions (μ≈0, ñi, c̃ij ≪ σ) are not guaranteed across layers, especially with structured, overlapping inputs that violate independence.
  - “A similar treatment can be used to show the gradient with respect to the bias term equals one” is incorrect as written. ∂μ/∂b = 1, but ∂ñ/∂b = ∂ñ/∂μ, not 1.
- The loss uses independent per-class log loss with pk defined as “probability the average spike count for the class is greater than 0.5.” MNIST is single-label; why not softmax? The approximation for pk via Gaussian on an average is under-specified (covariances across neurons/classes ignored).
- The mapping drops fractional bias (“leak equal to learned bias, dropping fractional portion”). The effect of this quantization on accuracy is not analyzed.
- The fixed ±1 s-values leave most of the 9-bit dynamic range unused; no evidence that this is near-optimal vs learning s or at least choosing multiple magnitudes per axon type.
- Experimental methodology lacks key details: accuracy of the 30-core single member, energy/accuracy vs ensemble size in tabular form, confidence intervals across runs, random seeds, exact measurement setup (how leakage is apportioned; whether host I/O is included).
- Comparisons: The “best published” accuracy/efficiency claims should be contextualized with single-model SNN results and ANN→SNN conversions under matched core/latency/precision constraints.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but several places are confusing or inconsistent:
  - Section headers: “.1 Deployment network” (likely 3.1) and other numbering glitches.
  - Notation drifts between S, W; mismatch between c̃ and c; missing parentheses in expectations; occasional typos (“backpropgation”, “lowlatency”, “largescale”, “the the”).
  - Pseudocode/algorithmic description of the backward pass is terse; the exact tensors updated (c̃ vs s vs bias), dropout placement (on inputs? activations? c̃?), and schedule are unclear.
  - Topology description would benefit from a concise per-layer dimension table; Fig. 3 is referenced but critical quantitative details are not in text.
- Reproducibility is hindered without precise architecture specs, exact s-value LUTs, axon-type assignments, and deployment sampling protocol.

Originality
- The training-with-probabilities mapped to binarized synapses/spikes under hardware constraints is a useful synthesis. The constrained connectivity plus per-core initialization is tailored to TrueNorth and less explored in prior SNN conversion works. However, the high-level idea (probabilistic relaxation for binary structures) is related to existing literature; positioning vs. EBP and binary-weight training could be sharper.

Significance
- Demonstrating real-time, low-energy inference on silicon is valuable. However, the empirical scope (MNIST only) and dependence on large ensembles reduce broader impact. The approach may generalize, but evidence on harder datasets or convolutional architectures is absent.

Minor comments and formatting
- References contain numerous formatting errors (extraneous commas, inconsistent capitalization, “in press”, missing venues/years, typos). Please fix throughout.
- Remove “DARPA: Approved for Public Release…” from the main text.
- Clarify whether data augmentation is train-only; confirm no test-time augmentation.
- State number of output neurons per class; report ablations on this choice.
- Provide the 30-core single-member accuracy/energy and a clear table of ensemble size vs accuracy/energy.

Pros
- Practical, hardware-aware training procedure with direct deployment mapping.
- Solid energy measurements on real hardware; tunable accuracy/energy via ensembling.
- Thoughtful core-level initialization consistent with architectural constraints.

Cons
- Mathematical inaccuracies and unvalidated approximations.
- Sparse and somewhat unfair empirical evaluation (single dataset, ensemble-dependent SOTA).
- Insufficient clarity and missing experimental details impede reproducibility.
- Overstated claims (e.g., “optimal” subspace) without rigorous support.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is promising and relevant, but the paper needs substantial corrections to the math/derivations, clearer and fairer experiments (including single-model baselines and broader datasets), and improved clarity/formatting to meet NeurIPS standards.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate.
