This paper attempts to bridge the gap between the efficiency of neuromorphic hardware and the training power of backpropagation by proposing a constrained-then-train methodology. The core idea – training a network with continuous values representing probabilities of spiking or synapse activation, mappable to a low-precision, constrained-connectivity neuromorphic system – is conceptually reasonable. The reported MNIST accuracy (99.42%) is impressive, exceeding existing spiking network results and achieving state-of-the-art power efficiency on TrueNorth.

However, despite these positive aspects, I remain skeptical. The paper feels overly focused on demonstrating a functional system and achieving high numbers, with insufficient attention paid to rigorous analysis and broader implications. The "constrain-then-train" approach, while presented as a key advantage, is not thoroughly contrasted with alternative “train-then-constrain” methods, beyond a brief, dismissive statement. The justification for the specific choices made during the constraint phase – particularly the Gaussian approximation and the neuron activation function – is somewhat lacking. While practical for the TrueNorth implementation, the rationale for *why* these specific constraints lead to efficient learning isn't clearly articulated.

My primary concern lies with the limited scope and validation. The MNIST dataset, while a common benchmark, is relatively simple. The performance on more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) is unknown, and it’s unclear whether the proposed approach would scale effectively. The network topology, with its sliding window approach, seems specifically tailored to TrueNorth's architecture.  The generalizability of this topology and training method to *other* neuromorphic hardware platforms is questionable, despite claims to the contrary. Furthermore, the reported power efficiency relies heavily on the specific TrueNorth implementation. A more comprehensive power analysis, considering the energy cost of the probabilistic mapping and ensemble averaging, would be beneficial.

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations. The choice of synaptic strengths being “blocks” as dictated by the TrueNorth hardware is a constraint, and how this impacts the network's capacity or expressive power is not addressed.  The sensitivity of the training process to hyperparameters (learning rate, dropout, batch size) is not discussed – how robust is this method to variations in these settings? The assumption of independence between inputs during the Gaussian approximation is also a potential source of error and should be discussed further, along with its potential impact on accuracy. 

The explanation for the chosen network initialization, while mathematically detailed, feels somewhat contrived. Minimizing the covariance between neuron inputs to achieve independence appears overly complex and its true contribution to the overall performance is unclear. It's not obvious why this specific initialization scheme is superior to simpler alternatives. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on MNIST for spiking neural networks.
*   Demonstrates impressive power efficiency on TrueNorth hardware.
*   Presents a potentially valuable methodology for bridging the gap between backpropagation and neuromorphic computing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited validation on a simple dataset (MNIST).
*   Lack of comparison with alternative approaches (e.g., train-then-constrain).
*   Insufficient justification for key design choices (Gaussian approximation, activation function).
*   Questionable generalizability to other neuromorphic platforms.
*   Limited discussion of limitations and potential failure modes.
*   Overly complex initialization scheme with unclear benefits.



Overall, while the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution, I believe it falls short of NIPS standards. The results, while promising, are not sufficiently robustly demonstrated or thoroughly analyzed.  The lack of broader validation and the limited discussion of limitations raise concerns about the generalizability and long-term impact of this work. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address these concerns and resubmit in the future after demonstrating the scalability and robustness of their approach on more challenging datasets and across different neuromorphic platforms.
