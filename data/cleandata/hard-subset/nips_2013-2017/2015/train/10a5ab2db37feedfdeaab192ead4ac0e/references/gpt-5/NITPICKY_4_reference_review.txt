Summary
The paper proposes a “constrain-then-train” approach to reconcile backpropagation with spiking neuromorphic hardware (TrueNorth). The training network uses continuous variables in [0,1] that are interpreted as probabilities of spikes and binary synapse states; forward propagation uses a Gaussian approximation of the sum of weighted Bernoulli variables to obtain spiking probabilities (Eq. 3), and gradients are computed w.r.t. synapse connection probabilities c̃ij and biases. Synaptic strengths are not learned; instead, the authors initialize a balanced ±1 weight pattern per neuron (with TrueNorth’s 4 axon types) to minimize pairwise redundancy via approximate covariance arguments. The network topology respects TrueNorth’s core constraints via sliding-window sparse connectivity. For deployment, c̃ij and input spike probabilities are sampled to produce binary synapse masks and Poisson-like spiking; optionally, an ensemble of independent samplings is averaged. On MNIST, they report up to 99.42% with a 64-member ensemble (30 cores per member; 1920 cores total, 108 µJ/classification), and extremely low energy at lower accuracy (0.268 µJ/classification at 92.7% with a tiny 5-core network). They claim this bridges backprop with energy-efficient neuromorphic computing, outperforming prior spiking/binary methods.

Relation to prior work
The work is related to training spiking networks via backprop with high-precision weights (Diehl & Neil), binary/trinary synapses with continuous activations, and probabilistic/binary networks (e.g., expectation backprop). The novelty claimed is the combination of spiking neurons, <2-bit synapses, and constrained connectivity with a single backprop-compatible probabilistic surrogate, plus a mapping to TrueNorth. However, several relevant lines of work are missing or under-discussed (e.g., binary/binarized networks and connection-probability learning from 2015-era literature; fairness of comparisons to train-then-constrain approaches).

Strengths
- Clear and timely goal: training directly under hardware constraints to ensure deployability.
- Clever probabilistic surrogate with a direct mapping to spiking/binary deployment.
- Practical initialization aimed at minimizing redundancy under TrueNorth constraints.
- Real hardware deployment and energy measurements; impressive energy/latency regime.
- Simple ensemble construction by re-sampling the trained probability model.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Mathematical clarity/ correctness issues:
  - Eq. 3 is malformed as written (missing constants/parentheses for the Gaussian CCDF; the standard erfc/sqrt(2) factorization is unclear). This is central to the method.
  - The statement “the corresponding gradient with respect to the bias term equals one” is incorrect as phrased (∂µ/∂b = 1, but ∂ñ/∂b = ∂ñ/∂µ · 1 ≠ 1 in general).
  - In Eq. 4, the approximation dropping the σ^3 term is justified heuristically (µ, ñi, c̃ij ≪ σ), but no empirical ablation quantifies its impact on accuracy or training stability.
  - Independence assumptions for the Gaussian approximation are violated in deeper layers (correlated inputs due to shared upstream units). No sensitivity analysis is provided.
- Mapping and evaluation:
  - The main accuracy (99.42%) requires a 64-member ensemble (1920 cores). Single-member accuracy for the 30-core network is not reported; the energy/accuracy Pareto for that model is unclear. The 4 µJ at 95% claim in the Introduction is not supported by Results (closest numbers are 0.268 µJ at 92.7% and 108 µJ at 99.42%).
  - The stochastic sampling of c̃ij at deployment implies variance in accuracy across instantiations; no confidence intervals or variability analysis are shown. How many independent runs per point in Fig. 3C?
  - The averaging across multiple output neurons per class is claimed to “improve performance” but there is no ablation.
- Initialization:
  - The derivation relies on strong assumptions (equal means/variances across inputs, identical off-diagonal covariances γ, decorrelated inputs/synapse states, bias near 0). Practical violations and their effect on the objective (minimizing inner products) are not examined.
  - The mapping from “±1” strengths to TrueNorth’s 4 axon types with 9-bit s-values is under-specified. Are all four types used as ±1, or are different magnitudes used? How is the block-tying enforced in training?
- Experimental scope and fairness:
  - Only MNIST is used. Given the paper’s hardware-centric claim, at least one additional dataset (e.g., Fashion-MNIST or simple audio/event tasks) would strengthen significance.
  - The “best published power efficiency” comparison is weakly contextualized; the baselines differ in hardware, throughput, precision, and ensemble size. A normalized comparison (e.g., energy per MAC-equivalent, or per spike) is needed.
  - No comparison against a train-then-constrain spiking baseline with the same network topology on TrueNorth is provided.
- Reproducibility:
  - Missing training details: epochs, per-layer dropout placement, whether test-time augmentation was used, exact optimizer hyperparameters per stage, random seeds, single-vs-multi-core parallelization for the ensemble.
  - No code or weight maps; limited description of measurement methodology (supply voltage, temperature, router/noC overheads). Leakage “fractioning” is approximate.

Minor issues and presentation
- Numerous typographical and formatting errors: malformed Eq. 3; inconsistent notation (W vs S); missing brackets in the thresholding definition; dangling commas and “in press” across references; duplicated/garbled bibliography entries; inconsistent symbol usage (θ sometimes 0, sometimes 0.5).
- Section numbering: “.1 Deployment network” appears misnumbered; “Network Initialization” introduced before section numbering consistency.
- Figures and tables are referenced (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3, Table 1) but not included; ensure captions, units, and axis labeling support reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide the correct closed-form for Eq. 3 and the exact expression used in code, and report an ablation showing the effect of dropping the σ^3 term in Eq. 4.
- Report single-member accuracy (and 4-/16-member accuracies) for the 30-core network with confidence intervals across multiple instantiations. Clarify the 4 µJ at 95% claim with exact configuration and measured data.
- Detail the mapping of s-values to the 4 axon types and how block-tying is enforced; are strengths truly ±1, or are different magnitudes used?
- Provide ablations: with/without multi-neuron class voting; with/without data augmentation; with learned vs fixed s-values (if feasible).
- Elaborate on energy measurement methodology and fairness of baselines.

Evaluation
- Quality: 5/10. Interesting idea and hardware results, but mathematical derivations have errors/ambiguities; key approximations lack validation; evaluation is narrow and partially inconsistent.
- Clarity: 4/10. Several notation/formatting issues; missing details hinder reproducibility.
- Originality: 7/10. The specific combination (probabilistic backprop to binary/spiking with constrained connectivity and TrueNorth mapping) is novel.
- Significance: 6/10. Potentially impactful for neuromorphic deployment, but limited to MNIST and reliant on large ensembles.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The paper has promising ideas and an appealing hardware demonstration, but the accumulation of technical/notation errors, missing ablations, unclear energy claims, and limited experimental scope make it premature for NeurIPS. Addressing the mathematical clarity, providing rigorous ablations and variability analyses, clarifying energy measurements, and expanding evaluation would substantially strengthen the submission.
