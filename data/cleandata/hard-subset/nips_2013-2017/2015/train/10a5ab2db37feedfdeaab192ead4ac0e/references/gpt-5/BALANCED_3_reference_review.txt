Summary
The paper proposes a “constrain-then-train” backpropagation framework for neuromorphic deployment that reconciles spiking neurons, extremely low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity. The key idea is to train a network whose inputs, neuron outputs, and synaptic connections are continuous variables in [0,1] interpreted as probabilities (of spikes and synapse “on” states). Training optimizes synaptic connection probabilities and neuron biases via a differentiable probabilistic forward model that approximates the weighted sum of Bernoulli inputs by a Gaussian, enabling a tractable gradient. Synaptic strengths are fixed a priori to a balanced ±1 pattern designed to minimize pairwise covariance across neurons on a TrueNorth core, creating a decorrelated subspace for learning. A hardware-compatible topology uses sliding local receptive fields with progressive mixing under TrueNorth’s core-level fan-in constraints. At deployment, the trained probability network is sampled to produce one or more discrete spiking networks; outputs are averaged across neurons and (optionally) across ensemble members. On MNIST, a 30-core, 64-member ensemble achieves 99.42% accuracy at 108 µJ per classification at 1 kHz throughput; a 5-core single network achieves 92.7% at 0.268 µJ/class. The work is positioned against (i) spiking networks trained by backprop but with high-precision weights, (ii) low-precision synapses with continuous activations, and (iii) binary neurons/weights with full connectivity, claiming novelty in jointly addressing spiking, sub-trinary synapses, and constrained connectivity with backprop and achieving state-of-the-art MNIST accuracy among spiking approaches and superior energy efficiency on TrueNorth.

Quality
- Soundness: The probabilistic forward model (Gaussian approximation of weighted Bernoullis) is standard but relies on independence and sufficiently large fan-in. The gradient approximation drops a σ^{-3} term; this is plausible under their balancing assumptions but deserves empirical or theoretical justification (e.g., ablation showing negligible impact). Only synapse on/off probabilities and biases are learned; fixed ±1 strengths simplify optimization but may limit representational power compared to learning strength templates.
- Empirics: The hardware results are valuable. However, evaluation is limited to MNIST; no tests on more challenging datasets or neuromorphic variants (e.g., N-MNIST) are provided. The reported 99.42% uses a large ensemble (64 members, 1920 cores). It would help to report accuracies per ensemble size and per-core budget to clarify the trade-offs. The energy comparisons to other systems are not apples-to-apples; differences in hardware, throughput, and measurement methodology complicate claims of “by far the best.”
- Reproducibility: Training details are reasonably specified (batch size, momentum, dropout, augmentation, LR schedule), but some equations/notations appear incorrect or imprecise (see Clarity). Code or pseudocode for the training loop and the TrueNorth mapping would aid reproducibility.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, with a clear high-level narrative. Several equations/claims need correction or clarification:
  - Eq. (3): The expression for ñj appears malformed; it should correspond to 1 − Φ((θ − µj)/σj), not “1 − 1 + erf …”.
  - “A similar treatment… gradient with respect to the bias term equals one” is incorrect; it should be ∂ñj/∂bj = ∂ñj/∂µj.
  - Some typos: “backpropgation”, “the the average”, spacing issues. Figures/Table references are essential for understanding but not all details are present in text.
- The progressive mixing topology could use a more explicit comparison to locally connected layers vs convolutional layers (no weight sharing).

Originality
- The combination is novel: a backprop-compatible probabilistic training of binary connectivity for spiking neurons under hard fan-in constraints, with an initialization targeting decorrelated neuron inputs on TrueNorth. Related lines include expectation backprop for binary networks and train-then-constrain spiking conversions; this work differs by learning connection probabilities only, fixing balanced strength templates, and directly targeting hardware constraints. The ensemble-from-one-trained-probability-network is a neat deployment twist.
- Missing or under-cited related work: binary/binarized weight training such as BinaryConnect (Courbariaux et al., 2015) and stochastic straight-through estimators are relevant baselines to discuss and contrast.

Significance
- The approach is practically impactful for neuromorphic deployment and shows impressive energy/throughput on TrueNorth. However, the scope is limited by evaluation on MNIST only. Demonstrating generality on harder vision (e.g., CIFAR-10 with locally connected layers), neuromorphic datasets, or audio would substantially increase significance. The training framework could be broadly useful across neuromorphic substrates, but this is not empirically demonstrated.

Pros
- Clear motivation and concrete alignment with hardware constraints; end-to-end mapping to a deployed spiking system.
- Elegant probabilistic relaxation enabling backprop on binary connectivity with spiking neurons.
- Hardware-aware initialization minimizing pairwise covariance is interesting and plausible.
- Real-time, low-energy hardware results with accuracy-energy trade-off studies.

Cons
- Limited empirical scope (MNIST only) and heavy reliance on large ensembles for top accuracy.
- Some mathematical approximations are insufficiently justified; a few equations appear incorrect.
- Energy/efficiency comparisons are not strictly comparable across platforms and setups.
- Fixed strength templates may cap performance; no ablation on learning strength blocks vs fixing.
- Clarity issues and minor errors.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and clarify Eq. (3), the bias gradient, and any typographical errors; add derivation details in the supplement.
- Provide ablations: with/without the σ^{-3} term; learned strength templates (within allowed axon-type constraints) vs fixed; effect of balanced vs unbalanced strengths; different time steps for input encoding vs energy/accuracy.
- Report full trade-off tables: accuracy vs ensemble size vs core count vs energy, including single-network results for the 30-core model. Ensure the 4 µJ at 95% claim is supported in the Results section.
- Compare to train-then-constrain spiking conversions and to binary weight training (e.g., BinaryConnect) on the same topology, where feasible.
- Evaluate on at least one additional dataset (CIFAR-10 with locally connected layers, N-MNIST) to demonstrate generality.
- Discuss deployment fairness: include I/O and host overheads if material; normalize comparisons or clearly caveat claims.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a compelling, hardware-grounded learning framework with novel elements and strong practical relevance to neuromorphic computing. Strengthening clarity, experimental breadth, and justification of approximations would make the contribution more robust and broadly impactful. My confidence is moderate.
