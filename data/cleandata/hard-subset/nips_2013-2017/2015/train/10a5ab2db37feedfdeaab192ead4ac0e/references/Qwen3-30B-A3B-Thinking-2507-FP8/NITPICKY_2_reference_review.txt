

# Review: Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing

This paper proposes a method to bridge backpropagation training with neuromorphic hardware deployment by training networks in a probabilistic domain (with continuous values in [0,1] representing spike probabilities and synapse states) and mapping to spiking hardware with low-precision synapses. The authors claim to achieve 99.42% accuracy on MNIST using spiking neurons with less than 2 bits per synapse, and demonstrate real-time operation on TrueNorth with 4 µJ per classification.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses a meaningful challenge in neuromorphic computing—the incompatibility between backpropagation training and spiking hardware deployment. The "constrain-then-train" approach is conceptually sound and represents a meaningful integration of several existing ideas. The experimental results on TrueNorth are relevant and demonstrate a practical implementation of the approach. The paper is well-organized and the writing is generally clear.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Misrepresentation of prior work:** The paper claims [12] (Diehl et al.) is "networks with spiking neurons but with high-precision weights" but [12] is actually "Fast-classifying, high-accuracy spiking deep networks through weight and threshold balancing," which uses spiking neurons with high-precision weights. This misrepresentation undermines their comparative claims.

2. **Unjustified Gaussian approximation:** In Section 3.2, the authors claim they can approximate the sum of weighted Bernoulli variables as Gaussian with "sufficient numbers" but provide no theoretical justification for why this approximation is valid for neural network parameters. The variance calculation (Equation 2) is incorrect for Bernoulli variables.

3. **Problematic gradient approximation:** Equation 4 contains a gradient derivation where the authors claim to drop a term "because it's much smaller" without mathematical justification. Their assumption that "µ, ñi and c̃ij to be much less than σ" is not generally true for neural networks, especially as training progresses.

4. **Inconsistent energy efficiency claims:** The paper states "4 µJ per classification" but later reports "108 µJ per classification" for the same setup (64-member ensemble). This significant discrepancy undermines their energy efficiency claims.

5. **Misleading precision claim:** The paper claims "synapses requiring less bits of precision than even trinary weights" but trinary weights require log2(3) ≈ 1.58 bits, and their approach uses binary weights (1 bit) plus additional bits for lookup table (0.15 bits), totaling 1.15 bits. They're not using less than trinary precision—they're using binary weights (1 bit) plus extra bits.

6. **Network initialization flaw:** The paper claims to set "half of each neuron's s-values to −1 and the other half to 1" but doesn't address how they handle odd numbers of weights, which could lead to imbalance in weight sums (violating Equation 5).

7. **Formatting and technical errors:** Equation 3 contains a clear formatting error: "1− 1 + erf θ − µj√ σ2j" should be "1 - 0.5*erf((θ-μj)/σj)". The reference list incorrectly cites [12] as "in press" when it was published in 2015.

8. **Confusing terminology:** The paper uses "synaptic strength" inconsistently, sometimes representing it as fixed values (Section 3.2) and sometimes as learnable parameters (Section 3.4), creating confusion about the actual learning process.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper addresses an important problem in neuromorphic computing, the technical issues described above are substantial. The misrepresentation of prior work, unjustified approximations, inconsistent results, and formatting errors significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The claimed results (99.42% accuracy, 4 µJ per classification) cannot be trusted due to the inconsistencies in the paper. These are not minor issues—they directly impact the paper's scientific validity and the significance of its claims.

The paper's contribution is not sufficiently novel given the existing work on probabilistic backpropagation and spiking networks. The "constrain-then-train" approach is conceptually similar to existing work, and the paper fails to clearly articulate how it differs from previous methods.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial technical flaws that compromise the paper's scientific validity. The authors should address these issues before resubmitting.
