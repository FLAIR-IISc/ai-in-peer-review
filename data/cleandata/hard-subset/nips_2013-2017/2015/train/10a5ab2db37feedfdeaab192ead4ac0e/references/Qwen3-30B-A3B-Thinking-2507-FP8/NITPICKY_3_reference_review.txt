

# Review of "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing"

This paper proposes a training methodology for neuromorphic hardware that bridges the gap between backpropagation training and spiking neuromorphic deployment. The authors claim to demonstrate a learning rule and network topology that enables training networks with spiking neurons, low-precision synapses (less than trinary), and constrained connectivity, while achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on MNIST (99.42%) with exceptional energy efficiency (4 µJ per classification).

## Major Technical Concerns

The most significant issue lies in the theoretical foundation of the training procedure. The authors derive a complex gradient expression (Equation 4) for the synaptic connection probability and then claim they can approximate it by dropping a term without proper justification. The paper states: "we can expect the right term of Equation 4 containing the denominator σ^3j to be much smaller than the left term" but provides no theoretical justification or empirical evidence for this claim. The authors also repeatedly assume "µ to be close to zero" without proving this assumption holds in practice, creating a circular argument. The justification "we can expect µ to be close to zero" is based on the assumption that "the synapse strengths to each neuron are balanced between positive and negative values" - which is exactly what they're trying to prove.

The paper contains critical notational inconsistencies: Equation (3) uses θ = 0 for neuron activation probability, while the loss function uses θ = 0.5, creating confusion about the thresholding behavior. The paper also uses inconsistent notation for variables (e.g., c̃ij with tilde vs. cij without, ñj vs. nj), which undermines clarity.

## Methodological and Experimental Issues

The claim of "synapses requiring less bits of precision than even trinary weights" is unsubstantiated. The paper states "each axon is assigned 1 of 4 axon types, which is used as an index into a lookup table of s-values, unique to each neuron, that provides a signed 9-bit integer synaptic strength to the corresponding synapse. This approach requires only 1 bit per synapse for the on/off state and an additional 0.15 bits per synapse for the lookup table scheme." However, they don't specify how many distinct s-values are in the lookup table or how this compares to trinary weights (which would require 2 bits per synapse).

The paper reports "the highest overall performance we observed of 99.42% was achieved with a 30 core trained network using a 64 member ensemble, for a total of 1920 cores." This is problematic because the TrueNorth chip has only 4096 cores, meaning they're using nearly half the chip for a single classification. The paper doesn't explain why this massive core allocation is necessary for a single classification when they claim to achieve "1000 images per second."

The paper states "each image was encoded using a single time step (1 ms)," which contradicts the claim of processing "1000 images per second" (which would require 1 ms per image, but with a single time step, it's unclear how they achieve this throughput).

## Minor but Significant Issues

1. The paper claims "we demonstrate for the first time an offline training methodology" but cites [14][15] for probabilistic backpropagation approaches with binary neurons and binary/trinary synapses, which contradicts their novelty claim.

2. The paper states "we use dropout 0.5" but doesn't explain how dropout is implemented in their probabilistic framework or where it's applied.

3. The paper claims "the training network forward pass is a probabilistic representation of the deployment network forward pass" but provides no formal proof that the training in the probabilistic space corresponds to the deployment network behavior.

4. Figure 2 is referenced but not included in the text, making it impossible to verify the claimed synaptic strength matrix.

5. The paper states "we can assume that the synapse strengths to each neuron are balanced between positive and negative values" but doesn't explain how this balance is maintained during training.

## Strengths

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the neuromorphic computing community by demonstrating a practical approach to training networks for deployment on spiking hardware. The energy efficiency results (4 µJ per classification) are impressive, and the MNIST accuracy of 99.42% is indeed competitive with other spiking network approaches.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a promising approach to neuromorphic computing, the methodological flaws are too severe to accept. The lack of theoretical justification for the key approximation in the gradient calculation (Equation 4), the circular assumptions about the mean activation, and the inconsistent notation undermine the paper's technical credibility. The experimental claims about energy efficiency and accuracy are difficult to verify due to insufficient methodological details.

The paper would benefit from a complete theoretical reworking of the gradient calculation, explicit justification for all assumptions, and more precise notation. Without these fundamental revisions, the paper's claims cannot be trusted.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper contains substantial technical flaws that undermine its core contribution. The lack of theoretical justification for a key approximation in the training algorithm is particularly problematic, as it renders the entire training procedure theoretically unsound. While the experimental results are impressive, they cannot be trusted without a solid theoretical foundation.
