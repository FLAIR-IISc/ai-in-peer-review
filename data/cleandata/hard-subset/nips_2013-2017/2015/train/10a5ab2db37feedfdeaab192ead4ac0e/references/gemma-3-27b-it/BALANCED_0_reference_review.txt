This paper addresses the critical challenge of reconciling the efficiency of neuromorphic hardware with the training power of backpropagation. The core idea – a “constrain-then-train” approach utilizing probabilistic representations within a limited connectivity network – is both novel and promising. The authors demonstrate impressive results on MNIST, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy for spiking networks and showcasing excellent power efficiency on the TrueNorth chip. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and provides a detailed explanation of the methodology and experimental setup.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of probabilistic backpropagation with constrained connectivity and low-precision synapses for neuromorphic hardware is a significant contribution.  Previous work has explored elements of this individually (e.g., [9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15]), but this paper presents the first integrated solution.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving 99.42% accuracy on MNIST, surpassing previous spiking network results (e.g., 99.12% [12] and 97.88% [15]), is a compelling demonstration of the approach’s effectiveness.  The power efficiency results on TrueNorth (4 µJ/classification) are particularly striking, significantly outperforming prior work (6 mJ/classification [16]).
* **Thorough Explanation:** The paper provides a detailed and well-structured explanation of the training network, deployment network, mapping procedure, and network topology. The mathematical derivations are clear and helpful.
* **Practical Focus:**  The use of TrueNorth as a deployment platform and the careful consideration of hardware constraints (e.g., limited connectivity, low-precision synapses) demonstrate a strong focus on real-world applicability.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand, despite the complexity of the topic.  The figures and tables are informative and effectively illustrate the key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the MNIST results are impressive, the evaluation is limited to a single dataset. It would be valuable to see performance on more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) to assess the generalizability of the approach.
* **Approximations in Backpropagation:** The simplification of the backpropagation gradient calculation (dropping the right term in Equation 4) is justified, but the impact of this approximation on the training process and final accuracy is not thoroughly investigated.  A more rigorous analysis of the approximation’s error bounds would be beneficial.
* **Network Topology Design:** The rationale behind the specific parameters chosen for the network topology (e.g., R, S, F) isn’t fully explained.  A more systematic exploration of different topology configurations and their impact on performance would strengthen the paper.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** While the network initialization procedure is well-described, the paper does not discuss its sensitivity to different initialization schemes or parameters.  Investigating the robustness of the training process to initialization variations would be helpful.
* **Ensemble Size Impact:**  The significant performance improvement with larger ensembles (up to 64 members) is noted, but the computational overhead and practical limitations of deploying such large ensembles are not adequately addressed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective solution to a significant challenge in neuromorphic computing.
* The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on MNIST and excellent power efficiency on TrueNorth.
* The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and provides a thorough methodology.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of energy-efficient machine learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The evaluation is limited to a single dataset, raising concerns about generalizability.
* The approximations made in the backpropagation gradient calculation could potentially affect accuracy.
* The lack of a comprehensive analysis of the network topology design and initialization sensitivity weakens the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing.  The novelty of the approach, the compelling empirical results, and the thorough explanation make it a worthy candidate for acceptance.  However, the limited scope of evaluation and the potential issues with the backpropagation approximation are concerns that should be addressed.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Expand the evaluation:**  Evaluate the approach on more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10) to assess its generalizability.
2.  **Analyze the backpropagation approximation:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of the approximation made in Equation 4, including an estimate of its error bounds and its potential impact on performance.
3.  **Investigate topology sensitivity:** Conduct a systematic exploration of different network topology configurations and their impact on performance.
4.  **Discuss ensemble limitations:** Address the computational overhead and practical limitations of deploying large ensembles.
5. **Clarify initialization sensitivity:** Include a brief discussion of the impact of different initialization schemes on the training process.
