- Proposes a "constrain-then-train" method training continuous probabilistic surrogates (x̃, ñ, c̃ ∈ [0,1]) that map directly to TrueNorth spiking/binary hardware.  
- Uses a CLT-based Gaussian approximation for the weighted sum of Bernoulli inputs to get a differentiable P(nj=1) and derives gradients for SGD.  
- Keeps synaptic strengths s fixed (balanced ±1, low-correlation per-core) to satisfy axon-type constraints and reduce redundancy instead of learning them.  
- Deploys by sampling binary synapses and spikes from learned probabilities; optional ensembles of sampled instances improve accuracy.  
- Network topology is a progressive sliding-window/mixing scheme designed to fit TrueNorth’s limited fan-in and shared-axon constraints.  
- Reports MNIST results: 99.42% accuracy with 30 cores × 64-instance ensemble (1920 cores) and 0.268 µJ/classification at 92.7% with a 5-core single instance.  
- Real hardware runs at 1000 images/s and includes leakage energy share; energy methodology described but excludes some system-level overheads.  
- Technical soundness: Gaussian approximation and gradient derivations are reasonable, but dropping the σ−3 term needs empirical validation.  
- Initialization justification (minimize pairwise redundancy via inner-product minimization) is plausible given assumptions.  
- Experimental support is strong on MNIST with real-time hardware measurement, but ensemble usage complicates fair SOTA comparisons.  
- Completeness is limited: missing ablations (ensemble size, fixed s vs learned s, Gaussian term effect, neuron input count) and tests on harder datasets.  
- Honesty: paper is transparent about hardware coupling and trade-offs, but “best to date” claims need controlled baseline comparisons.  
- Clarity: well-written overall, but some equations (e.g., Eq.3) and topology details (layer-by-layer specs, inputs/neuron) need clearer presentation for reproducibility.  
- Originality: novel end-to-end, hardware-aware surrogate training with fixed low-precision synapses and deployment sampling; distinct from prior SNN/quantized work.  
- Significance: bridging deep learning and neuromorphic efficiency with real silicon demos is impactful, though MNIST-only evaluation limits generality.  
- Pros: principled differentiable surrogate, real hardware deployment and energy-accuracy trade-offs, novel topology/initialization tailored to hardware.  
- Cons: limited to MNIST, heavy reliance on ensembles for peak accuracy, unvalidated approximations, no comparison to learning s, and incomplete reproducibility details.  
- Key suggestions: report accuracy/energy vs ensemble size (including single-instance) and compare to equivalent-core single models to separate capacity vs averaging.  
- Key suggestions: validate Gaussian/gradient approximation via Monte Carlo or ablation that retains the σ−3 term.  
- Key suggestions: ablate s-initialization by comparing balanced ±1 to random assignments and to learned s (even coarse).  
- Key suggestions: explicitly state inputs per neuron per layer and clarify output averaging/log-loss θ usage.  
- Key suggestions: discuss applicability and hurdles for convolutional architectures on TrueNorth and consider evaluations on additional datasets (CIFAR-10/SVHN).  
- Minor fixes: tighten Eq.3 typesetting, define θ when reused, report random seeds/variance or confidence intervals, and clarify any test-time augmentation.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept — original and practically validated but needs additional experiments/ablations or a second dataset to be a clear accept.
