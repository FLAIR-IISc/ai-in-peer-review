Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a “constrain-then-train” method to reconcile backpropagation with neuromorphic hardware constraints (spikes, low-precision synapses, limited fan-in/fan-out), using IBM’s TrueNorth as the deployment vehicle. The key idea is to train a probabilistic surrogate of the deployment network in which inputs x̃, neuron outputs ñ, and binary synaptic connections c are represented as continuous probabilities in [0,1], enabling gradient-based learning. Synaptic weights are factored as c_ij (binary “on/off”) times s_ij (signed strengths), where s_ij are drawn from a small, hardware-supported set and fixed per neuron/axon-type “block”; the training learns only c̃_ij (connection probabilities) and neuron biases. The forward pass uses a Gaussian approximation to the sum of weighted Bernoulli variables to compute ñ_j = P(n_j=1), and the backward pass differentiates this expression; a higher-order term is dropped under assumptions of balanced weights and large fan-in. After training, deployment samples binary synapses and spike inputs from the learned probabilities; optionally, an ensemble of independently sampled networks is averaged at inference to improve accuracy. The network topology uses sliding-window “progressive mixing” across cores to respect TrueNorth’s 256-axon/core limit while increasing receptive fields with depth. A principled initialization assigns s-values to minimize pairwise inner products and enforce zero-sum per neuron to decorrelate core outputs and balance dynamics.

The approach is positioned relative to: spiking nets trained via backprop but with high-precision weights (e.g., Cao et al., Diehl et al.), binary/trinary synapses with continuous activations (e.g., Müller & Indiveri), and probabilistic backprop for binary neurons/weights with full connectivity (e.g., Expectation Backprop). The novel combination here is spiking neurons, sub-trinary per-synapse memory (1 bit plus small LUT overhead), and constrained connectivity trained end-to-end with backprop-compatible surrogates, together with a deployed, real-time neuromorphic demonstration.

Quality
– Methodology: The probabilistic surrogate, Gaussian approximation, and gradient derivation are sound at a high level, though they rely on independence and CLT assumptions with large fan-in and balanced weights; the paper drops a σ^-3 term for efficiency, arguing it is small. However, there is no empirical validation of the approximation’s accuracy or an ablation to assess the impact of the dropped term, initialization, or progressive mixing vs alternatives (e.g., convolution/weight sharing).
– Engineering: The TrueNorth mapping is carefully considered (axon types, LUTs, s-value sharing, topology), and the initialization to decorrelate columns is thoughtful.
– Empirics: Results are on MNIST only. Two networks (5-core and 30-core) evaluated with ensembles of size 1–64; best accuracy 99.42% with a 64-member ensemble (1920 cores) at 108 µJ/class; lowest energy 0.268 µJ/class at 92.7% with a single 5-core member. Claims of 4 µJ/class at 95% also appear; reconciling these numbers would help. Comparisons to prior spiking/binary work are favorable but may not be apples-to-apples (different architectures, ensembling, hardware). No comparison to convolutional baselines or to train-then-constrain conversions on the same hardware.
– Reproducibility: Many hyperparameters and topology details are given, but code is not provided; the precise s-value construction and mapping particulars may be hard to reproduce without tools. Energy measurement details (active vs leakage apportionment) are described but could be elaborated.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The derivations are readable, but a few typos (e.g., Equation 3 formatting) and notational density could be improved. The topology description is serviceable, though diagrams (Fig. 3) are essential. A more explicit statement of memory/bit budgets per synapse/core would clarify the “< 2 bits per synapse” claim.

Originality
The work’s main novelty is the specific constrain-then-train probabilistic formulation targeting spiking neuromorphic hardware with 1-bit synapses and limited connectivity, combined with a practical topology and initialization that map to TrueNorth. While related to stochastic/binary networks (e.g., DropConnect/Expectation Backprop) and to spiking network conversions, the end-to-end treatment under stringent hardware constraints and the deployment are original and valuable.

Significance
Demonstrating real-time, ultralow-energy MNIST classification on physical neuromorphic hardware is impactful, and bridging backprop training to spiking deployment is of broad interest. However, restricting evaluation to MNIST and relying on large ensembles to reach SOTA accuracy limits immediate significance for mainstream NeurIPS audiences. Evidence of scalability to harder vision tasks (SVHN, CIFAR-10) and to convolutional structures would substantially raise impact.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
– Clear, principled bridge between backprop and neuromorphic constraints; elegant probabilistic surrogate and mapping.
– Strong hardware demonstration with compelling energy/latency metrics.
– Careful treatment of topology and initialization under core-level constraints.
– Advances state of the art in spiking MNIST accuracy and energy efficiency.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
– Evaluation limited to MNIST; no harder datasets or tasks.
– Heavy reliance on large ensembles; unclear fairness vs prior work and energy comparisons.
– Approximations (Gaussian, dropped terms) not empirically validated; limited ablations.
– Comparisons not normalized for parameter count/compute; no comparison to convolutional baselines or train-then-constrain on the same chip.
– Reproducibility and measurement details could be strengthened.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
1) Validate the Gaussian/independence assumptions: compare surrogate activations/gradients against Monte Carlo estimates on held-out layers; quantify errors.  
2) Provide ablations: effect of (i) ensemble size; (ii) initialization vs random; (iii) the dropped σ^-3 term; (iv) progressive mixing vs convolution/weight sharing.  
3) Clarify energy accounting and reconcile 0.268 µJ vs 4 µJ claims; report energy per ensemble member, per classification, and include leakage sensitivity.  
4) Report parameter counts and synapse utilization; normalize comparisons where possible.  
5) Demonstrate on at least one non-trivial dataset (e.g., SVHN/CIFAR-10) and/or a convolutional variant to substantiate generality.  
6) Discuss learning s_ij (e.g., STE or discrete relaxation) and whether jointly learning s and c improves accuracy/energy.  
7) Release training/mapping code and s-value construction scripts for reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper presents a thoughtful, end-to-end path from backprop to spiking neuromorphic deployment with impressive energy results on hardware and a novel training/mapping scheme. The main shortcomings are the narrow empirical scope and limited validation of approximations; addressing these would elevate the contribution.
