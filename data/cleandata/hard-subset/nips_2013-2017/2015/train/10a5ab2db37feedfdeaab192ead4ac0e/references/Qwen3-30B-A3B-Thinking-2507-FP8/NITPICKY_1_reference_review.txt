

# Review of "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method to train networks with spiking neurons and low-precision synapses using a probabilistic backpropagation approach. The authors claim to bridge the gap between standard backpropagation training and neuromorphic hardware constraints by training networks offline with continuous-valued probabilities (constrained to [0,1]) that map directly to spiking hardware. They demonstrate their approach on the MNIST dataset using the TrueNorth chip, reporting 99.42% accuracy (outperforming previous spiking networks) and 4 µJ per classification energy efficiency.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important practical challenge in neuromorphic computing - bridging the gap between standard deep learning training and hardware constraints. The probabilistic interpretation of network parameters provides a theoretically sound mapping to spiking hardware. The energy efficiency claim (4 µJ per classification) appears to be a significant improvement over prior work, and the experimental results on TrueNorth are well-motivated. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the hardware constraints and their impact on the learning algorithm.

## Weaknesses

**Technical flaws and unsupported claims:**
1. The Gaussian approximation in Equation 3 lacks justification. The paper states "we can approximate the probability distribution of this summation as a Gaussian," but provides no evidence that this approximation is valid for their specific network architecture (256 inputs) or MNIST data. The authors should provide empirical evidence showing the accuracy of this approximation.

2. The gradient approximation in Equation 4 is questionable. The claim that "the right term... can be expected to be much smaller than the left term" lacks mathematical justification. Without a proper analysis of the relative magnitudes of these terms under the described conditions, the approximation is unsupported.

3. The network initialization method (Section 3.4) contains a mathematical error. The paper states "E[xixq] = σ(xi, xq) + E[xi]E[xq]" which is correct for covariance, but then assumes "inputs have equal mean and variance" without justification. This assumption may not hold for MNIST data, potentially undermining the initialization approach.

4. The energy efficiency claim (4 µJ per classification) is misleading. The paper reports this for a 64-member ensemble, but doesn't clarify how much energy the ensemble adds compared to a single network. The comparison to "6 mJ per classification" (for 50 images per second) is not directly comparable to their 1000 images per second throughput, making the energy efficiency claim potentially deceptive.

5. The precision claim ("synapses requiring less bits of precision than even trinary weights") is inconsistent with the TrueNorth description. The paper states "1 bit per synapse for the on/off state and an additional 0.15 bits per synapse for the lookup table scheme," which totals 1.15 bits per synapse. However, trinary weights require 2 bits (for -1, 0, 1), so 1.15 bits is indeed less than 2 bits. But the paper should clarify that they're using a lookup table approach rather than directly implementing trinary weights.

**Methodological and presentation issues:**
1. The paper claims "99.42% accuracy" but doesn't provide error bars or confidence intervals, making it difficult to assess statistical significance.

2. The comparison to prior work (e.g., 99.12% in [12]) lacks context. The paper doesn't clarify whether the comparison networks used the same architecture, hyperparameters, or training procedure, making the accuracy comparison potentially unfair.

3. The ensemble approach (Section 3.3) is not properly analyzed. The paper reports results for ensembles of 1, 4, 16, and 64 members but doesn't discuss how ensemble size affects energy consumption versus accuracy, which is critical for practical deployment.

4. The network topology description (Section 4) is vague. The "progressive mixing" approach lacks concrete details on how inputs are mixed progressively across layers, making it difficult to understand or reproduce.

5. The paper claims their approach "requires only 1.15 bits per synapse" but doesn't explain why they don't use the full 9-bit precision available in TrueNorth, which would reduce the bit count further.

## Conclusion
While the paper addresses an important problem in neuromorphic computing and makes a reasonable contribution to the field, the technical flaws in the core learning algorithm (particularly the unsupported Gaussian approximation and gradient approximation) undermine the validity of the method. The paper also contains several misleading claims and insufficient experimental details that prevent proper evaluation of the results. The energy efficiency claim is particularly problematic due to the lack of proper comparison metrics.

The paper has significant potential value, but the technical shortcomings and lack of proper justification for key claims prevent it from being considered a strong contribution to NIPS. The authors should address these methodological concerns before the work can be considered for publication.

## Recommendation
Reject

The paper presents a potentially interesting approach to neuromorphic computing but contains too many technical flaws and unsupported claims that undermine its scientific contribution. The core learning algorithm relies on unverified approximations, and the experimental results are presented with insufficient detail and potentially misleading comparisons. While the topic is relevant to NIPS, the quality of the scientific contribution does not meet the conference's standards.
