This paper presents an intriguing approach to bridging the gap between the efficiency of neuromorphic hardware and the training power of backpropagation. The core idea – constraining a backpropagation-trained network to operate within the limitations of spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity – is conceptually sound and addresses a critical challenge in deploying deep learning models on specialized hardware. The reported MNIST accuracy (99.42%) is impressive, exceeding prior work with spiking neurons and demonstrating compelling power efficiency on the TrueNorth chip. However, a multitude of seemingly minor, yet cumulatively significant, issues detract from the overall impact and raise concerns about the rigor of the work.

The writing, while generally understandable, suffers from inconsistencies and a lack of precision. For example, the phrase “less bits of precision than even trinary weights” is vague – trinary weights require log2(3) ≈ 1.58 bits, so the claim needs to be quantified. Similarly, the assertion that TrueNorth requires “0.15 bits per synapse” for the lookup table scheme is misleading. It’s not 0.15 *bits* but rather utilizes a 9-bit lookup table indexed by 1 bit, implying 9 bits total, not a fractional bit value. This level of imprecision permeates the paper.

Methodologically, several points require clarification. Equation 2’s derivation of the Gaussian approximation feels hand-wavy and lacks justification for assuming independence of inputs, especially given the constrained connectivity. While acknowledged, the implications of this assumption are not fully discussed. The choice of θ=0 in Equation 3, while convenient for log loss, is not adequately motivated. Furthermore, the justification for discarding the second term in Equation 4 – based on the expectation that µ, ñi, and c̃ij are small – feels overly optimistic and lacks quantitative support. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of this approximation is crucial.

The network topology description is incomplete. While the sliding window approach is explained, the rationale for the specific parameters (R=16, S=12, etc.) isn’t provided. How were these values selected? Was a hyperparameter search performed? The network initialization procedure, while theoretically motivated by maximizing information transfer, feels complex and potentially difficult to reproduce. The statement about “balancing the possible permutations” lacks clarity.

The experimental setup and results presentation could be improved. While the energy numbers are impressive, the details regarding “leakage power” are insufficient. What percentage of the total power consumption is leakage? The claim of “real-time” performance (1000 images/second) needs further qualification. Is this sustained performance, or an average over a limited period? A more detailed breakdown of the inference time for each layer would be beneficial. The figure captions are often terse and lack sufficient context. Figure 2, displaying the weight matrix, is difficult to interpret without a clear color scale or labeling.

Several related works are missing from the citations. For instance, recent advances in binarized neural networks (BNNs) and XNOR-Net, which offer alternative approaches to low-precision inference, are not discussed. The authors claim novelty in training a network with synapses requiring “less than 2 bits,” but BNNs operate with 1 bit synapses and should be addressed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to reconciling backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware constraints.
*   State-of-the-art MNIST accuracy compared to other spiking networks.
*   Demonstration of real-time performance and impressive power efficiency on TrueNorth.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Numerous instances of imprecision and lack of detail in the writing.
*   Methodological justifications are often weak or lacking.
*   Incomplete description of experimental setup and results.
*   Missing citations to relevant related work.
*   The cumulative effect of minor flaws raises concerns about the overall rigor of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the promising ideas and significant results, the paper suffers from a concerning number of minor flaws that, collectively, undermine its credibility. While the authors demonstrate a viable path toward deploying deep learning models on neuromorphic hardware, the lack of rigor and clarity prevent me from recommending acceptance. A major revision addressing the points outlined above is necessary. I lean towards **rejection** due to the pervasiveness of these issues. The core idea deserves further investigation, but this submission, in its current form, does not meet the high standards expected for NIPS publication.
