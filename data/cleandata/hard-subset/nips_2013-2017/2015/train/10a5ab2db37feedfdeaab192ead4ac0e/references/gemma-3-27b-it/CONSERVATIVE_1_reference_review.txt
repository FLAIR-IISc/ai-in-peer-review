This paper presents an interesting attempt to bridge the gap between the efficiency of neuromorphic hardware, specifically the TrueNorth chip, and the training power of backpropagation. The core idea – training a network with continuous values representing probabilities of spiking and synaptic activation, then mapping to a binary/low-precision neuromorphic implementation – is conceptually sound. The reported results on MNIST, achieving 99.42% accuracy and impressive power efficiency on TrueNorth (4 µJ per classification), are noteworthy. However, despite these promising results, I have significant reservations about the overall strength of the contribution and its readiness for NIPS publication.

My primary concern revolves around the lack of comparative depth beyond the single hardware platform (TrueNorth). While the performance *on* TrueNorth is highlighted, the paper doesn’t sufficiently contextualize this within the broader landscape of software-based training and deployment of similarly accurate networks. For example, how does the energy consumption of the TrueNorth implementation compare to running an equivalently accurate, standard deep learning model on a modern GPU or even a mobile processor? The single comparison point [16] feels dated and insufficiently representative of current capabilities. It's crucial to demonstrate a genuine advantage in power efficiency, not just an improvement over a specific, older benchmark. 

The network topology, while motivated by the constraints of TrueNorth, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The progressive mixing approach and parameter choices (R, S, F) lack a strong theoretical justification and appear empirically determined. A more rigorous analysis of the impact of these choices on network performance and efficiency would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the network initialization procedure, though innovative, is complex and heavily reliant on assumptions about balanced weights and independence. The sensitivity of the results to deviations from these assumptions should be explored.

The paper also glosses over potential challenges in mapping the trained continuous-valued network to the discrete hardware. The sampling process of synaptic connection states (P(cij = 1) ≡ c̃ij) introduces stochasticity. While the use of ensembles mitigates this, the impact of ensemble size on performance and the computational cost of running multiple ensembles are not thoroughly discussed.

The mathematical derivation in Section 3.2, while attempting to simplify the gradient calculation, relies on approximations that require more careful justification. The assumption that µ, ñi, and c̃ij are much less than σ, while potentially valid in certain regimes, needs to be supported by empirical evidence. Furthermore, the impact of the dropped term in Equation 4 on the accuracy of the gradient and the overall training process should be investigated.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel approach to reconciling backpropagation with neuromorphic constraints.
*   High accuracy on MNIST (99.42%).
*   Demonstrated real-time execution and impressive power efficiency on TrueNorth.
*   Intriguing network initialization strategy.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited comparative analysis beyond TrueNorth. Lack of comparison to conventional implementations on modern hardware.
*   Ad-hoc network topology and parameter choices.
*   Over-reliance on approximations in the gradient calculation.
*   Insufficient discussion of the impact of stochasticity introduced by the mapping process.
*   The mathematical justifications feel incomplete and require stronger empirical validation.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a timely and important problem: enabling efficient deployment of deep learning models on neuromorphic hardware.
*   The demonstrated performance on TrueNorth is promising.
*   The proposed approach is novel and potentially impactful.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of comprehensive comparative analysis weakens the claim of significant advancement.
*   The theoretical justifications are incomplete and rely on potentially questionable approximations.
*   The paper doesn’t fully address potential challenges in mapping the trained network to hardware.
*   The originality, while present, is incremental, building on previous work in probabilistic backpropagation and neuromorphic computing without a substantial breakthrough.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the work is interesting and demonstrates a potential path towards energy-efficient neuromorphic computing, it falls short of the rigor and comparative analysis expected of a NIPS publication. The claims of significance are not sufficiently supported by evidence, and the potential limitations of the approach are not adequately addressed. I would encourage the authors to strengthen the paper with a more comprehensive evaluation on standard hardware, a more rigorous theoretical analysis, and a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges of implementation.
