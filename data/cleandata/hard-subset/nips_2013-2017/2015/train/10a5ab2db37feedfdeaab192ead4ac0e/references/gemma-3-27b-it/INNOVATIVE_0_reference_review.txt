This paper tackles a crucial problem: bridging the gap between the efficiency of neuromorphic hardware and the training power of backpropagation. The core idea – constraining a backpropagation-trained network to operate within the bounds of spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and limited connectivity – is genuinely novel and exciting. It's a clever “constrain-then-train” approach, a refreshing change from the more common “train-then-constrain” paradigm. The reported MNIST accuracy (99.42%) exceeding prior spiking network results, combined with the demonstrated real-time performance on TrueNorth (4 µJ per classification), is a significant achievement. This isn’t an incremental improvement; it’s a substantial step towards practical, energy-efficient deep learning on neuromorphic platforms.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The technical details appear sound, with a clear explanation of the training methodology and the mapping to TrueNorth. The network topology, with its progressive mixing approach, is well-motivated and appears effective in leveraging the hardware constraints. The initialization strategy, focusing on decorrelation of neuron responses, is a particularly insightful contribution, demonstrating a deep understanding of the hardware limitations. The experimental validation on TrueNorth is compelling, providing concrete evidence of the approach’s viability. The authors clearly understand the existing literature and position their work effectively within the field.

However, some aspects could benefit from further elaboration. While the Gaussian approximation in the backward pass is justified for computational efficiency, the potential impact of this approximation on gradient accuracy should be discussed. A more detailed analysis of the trade-off between accuracy and energy consumption as a function of network size and connectivity would be valuable. The paper mentions potential extensions to convolutional networks and other learning methods, but doesn't delve into specific challenges or preliminary results. Further investigation into the robustness of the network to variations in input data or network parameters would also strengthen the work. While the results on MNIST are excellent, evaluating the approach on more complex datasets would provide a broader assessment of its generalization capability. The ablation study showing the impact of each design choice (e.g. progressive mixing, initialization) is missing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of reconciling backpropagation with neuromorphic constraints is genuinely new and highly promising.
*   **Significance:** The results demonstrate a substantial improvement in accuracy and energy efficiency for spiking neural networks.
*   **Practicality:** The demonstrated operation on TrueNorth indicates the real-world applicability of the approach.
*   **Soundness:** The technical details appear well-considered and justified.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Approximation:** The Gaussian approximation in the backward pass introduces a potential source of error that could be better quantified.
*   **Limited Datasets:** Evaluation is currently limited to MNIST; performance on more complex datasets is unknown.
*   **Ablation Study:** Lack of ablation study makes it difficult to assess the impact of individual components.



Despite these minor weaknesses, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its limitations. This work presents a bold and innovative approach to a critical challenge in neuromorphic computing. The results are impressive, and the potential impact is significant. While further validation and analysis would be beneficial, the novelty and promise of this work justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, even with the understanding that some aspects could be further refined in future work. This is a paper that moves the field forward and is well-deserving of a NIPS presentation.
