Summary
The paper proposes a “constrain-then-train” method to reconcile backpropagation with highly constrained neuromorphic hardware (TrueNorth). During training, inputs x̃, neuron outputs ñ, and synaptic connection states c̃ are continuous in [0,1] and interpreted as probabilities (of spikes and synapse “on” states). The forward pass uses a Gaussian approximation to the sum of weighted Bernoulli variables to compute firing probabilities; backpropagation updates c̃ and biases b, while synaptic strengths s are fixed a priori to a balanced ±1 pattern per neuron chosen to reduce pairwise redundancy (via minimizing inner products). The network topology uses sliding windows over cores (progressive mixing) under TrueNorth’s 256-axon/core constraint. After training, deployment samples binary c and spike inputs per ensemble member and averages outputs. On MNIST, a 30-core, 4-layer network with a 64-member ensemble obtains 99.42% accuracy at 108 µJ/classification; a 5-core network achieves 92.7% at 0.268 µJ/classification. The paper claims state-of-the-art accuracy for spiking networks and superior energy efficiency versus prior low-power approaches.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of a practical bridge between gradient-based training and spiking hardware with extremely low-precision synapses and constrained connectivity.
- The probabilistic training view yields a direct mapping to deployment (no post-hoc discretization), a key engineering advantage.
- Hardware-aware weight initialization to reduce redundancy is principled and consistent with infomax intuition.
- Real-time, on-chip results with explicit energy measurements are valuable and rare.
- Topology respects hardware constraints while providing increasing receptive field coverage.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and completeness
- Equation 3 appears typeset incorrectly. The probability should be ñj = 1 − Φ((θ − µj)/σj) (or 0.5 erfc((θ − µj)/(√2 σj))). As written, “1 − 1 + erf …” is nonsensical. Please fix and ensure consistency in subsequent derivations.
- In Section 3.2, the derivative w.r.t. c̃ij (Eq. 4) drops the σ dependence (“right term”) on heuristic grounds. This ignores ∂σ/∂c̃ contributions which can be significant when c̃ and x̃ are not tiny or when σ is not large. No empirical ablation is provided to justify the approximation.
- “A similar treatment can be used to show that the gradient with respect to the bias term equals one” is incorrect. ∂µj/∂bj = 1, but ∂ñj/∂bj = ∂ñj/∂µj, not 1.
- The independence assumptions (inputs xi independent, Bernoulli sampling of c and x, pairwise independence post nonlinearity) are strong and not validated. After training, dependencies are likely. Please quantify the quality of the Gaussian and independence approximations (e.g., via moment matching or KL) and their impact on accuracy.
- The fixed ±1 s-values and the assumption that ∑i sij = 0 per neuron guarantee E[Ij] ≈ 0 only if the learned c̃ are balanced across signs. Training may bias selection toward one sign; does this destabilize µ? Any regularizer to preserve balance?
- The loss is a per-class log loss on pk = P(average spike count > 0.5) rather than standard softmax cross-entropy. This creates K independent binary tasks. Please justify this choice and compare empirically to softmax.
- Ensemble methodology: The 99.42% result uses 64 sampled members (1920 cores). This conflates model capacity/ensembling with the core training contribution. Provide single-member accuracy and variance over different samples, and compare fairly to non-ensembled baselines.
- Mapping: biases are quantized by “dropping any fractional portion.” What is the effect of this quantization on accuracy? Any calibration step?
- Energy measurement: leakage allocation “fractionalized” by cores-in-use assumes linear scaling of static power, which is not generally accurate. Provide measurement methodology details (clock gating, DVFS, I/O contribution) and error bars. The inconsistency between the 4 µJ figure in the intro and the 108 µJ result later needs clarification.

Experimental scope and baselines
- Only MNIST is reported. No results on more challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10) or event-driven datasets. No convolutional variant is implemented despite the claim of easy adaptation. This limits significance.
- Missing ablations: effect of (i) dropping the σ-term in Eq. 4; (ii) balanced ±1 versus other s choices; (iii) number of outputs per class; (iv) window parameters R,F,S; (v) dropout; (vi) data augmentation.
- Baselines are weak/outdated. Please compare to: train-then-constrain SNN conversions on MNIST; binary/ternary-weight networks (e.g., BinaryConnect, ternary networks); expectation backprop and straight-through gradient estimators; recent SNN surrogate-gradient methods. Several are not cited.
- Reproducibility: Figures/Tables referenced (Fig. 1–3, Table 1) are absent; hyperparameters are only partially specified (e.g., number of output neurons per class; exact s LUT configuration). No code or seeds.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous notation and formatting issues: section “.1 Deployment network” likely 3.1; use of W versus S (“effective weight matrix”) is inconsistent; typos (“backpropgation,” “the the,” “apriori,” “lowlatency,” etc.); Eq. 3 typeset error; missing definition of pk’s averaging details.
- References are inconsistent and incomplete (missing venues/years/pages; “in press”/arXiv without stable citations); capitalization inconsistencies; some citations not aligned with claims.
- The topology description permits input duplication without justification; discuss implications for normalization and redundancy.

Originality
- The constrain-then-train idea for neuromorphic mapping is interesting, but related ideas exist: training with probabilistic neurons/weights, binary/trinary synapses, expectation backprop, and SNN conversion. The novelty is in combining probabilistic backprop on c̃ with fixed, hardware-aware s-values and constrained connectivity. This is incremental but potentially useful; however, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate from prior art with rigorous comparisons.

Significance
- Demonstrating real-time, low-energy inference on TrueNorth is valuable, but the reliance on large ensembles and a single easy dataset limits impact. Without broader datasets and stronger baselines, it is hard to assess whether this approach advances state of the art versus simpler binary networks on conventional hardware.

Pros
- Practical, hardware-aware training method with direct deployability.
- Principled initialization to reduce redundancy.
- On-chip, measured energy/throughput; strong MNIST accuracy (with ensemble).

Cons
- Several technical inaccuracies/derivation issues (Eq. 3, bias gradient).
- Heavy reliance on approximations without validation; limited ablations.
- Narrow experimental scope; weak baselines; ensemble-dependent headline result.
- Clarity/notation/reference problems; missing figures/tables.

Questions for authors
- Provide correct Eq. 3 and full gradients; quantify the error from dropping ∂σ/∂c̃.
- Report single-member performance and variance across samples; ablate ensemble size.
- Compare to softmax loss and to train-then-constrain SNNs and binary/ternary nets.
- Detail energy measurement methodology and reconcile all energy numbers.
- Include results on at least one harder dataset and/or a convolutional variant.

Recommendation: Reject (weak). The idea is promising and the hardware results are compelling, but the accumulation of technical, experimental, and presentation issues prevents acceptance in its current form.
