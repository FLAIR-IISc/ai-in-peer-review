This paper presents a method for training neural networks using backpropagation with constraints designed to facilitate deployment on neuromorphic hardware, specifically the TrueNorth chip. The core idea is to train a network with continuous-valued weights and activations constrained to [0, 1], interpreting these as probabilities of spike occurrence or synapse activation. The network topology employs a progressive mixing approach to limit connectivity. The authors demonstrate promising results on MNIST, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy for spiking networks and impressive power efficiency on TrueNorth.

While the concept of bridging the gap between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware is valuable and the reported results are encouraging, numerous details and inconsistencies throughout the paper raise significant concerns. The review below details these issues, leading to a recommendation for rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty (Conceptual):** The idea of constrain-then-train, aligning the training process directly with deployment constraints, is a reasonable approach.
*   **Performance:** The reported MNIST accuracy (99.42%) is competitive, exceeding previous spiking network results. The power efficiency demonstration on TrueNorth is noteworthy.
*   **Real-World Demonstration:** Validating the approach on actual hardware (TrueNorth) adds practical relevance.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Detail & Reproducibility:** The description of the training procedure is often vague. For example, the "transformations of the training data" are mentioned but not comprehensively listed or justified (rotation up to ±15◦, shift up to ±5 pixels and rescale up to ±15%). The specifics of the mini-batch size, momentum, and learning rate decay schedule are provided, but the motivation and sensitivity to these parameters are not discussed. Crucially, details about the initialization of synaptic strengths beyond the core principle are lacking. How are the initial random assignments generated?
2.  **Approximations & Justification:** The simplification of Equation (4) based on assumptions about µ, ñi and c̃ij being much smaller than σ is presented without rigorous justification. While the authors state these conditions are “expected,” a more thorough analysis, perhaps through simulations or empirical verification, is needed. The impact of this approximation on gradient accuracy is unclear.
3.  **Network Topology Details:** The rationale behind the specific values chosen for R, F, and S in the network topology (Figure 3A) is poorly explained. Why are these parameters optimal, and how sensitive are the results to changes in these values? The explanation of the "pyramid" shape is insufficient.
4.  **Ensemble Methodology:** The description of the ensemble method is rudimentary. Why is training done only *once* before sampling for ensemble members? This suggests a potential for limited diversity within the ensemble. A discussion of ensemble size saturation would be beneficial.
5.  **Inconsistent Notation & Definitions:** There's a subtle but concerning shift in notation. 'c' is used both as a binary indicator for synapse presence and in equations representing synaptic connection probabilities (c̃ij). This ambiguity can lead to confusion. The definition of x̃i as the “probability of an input spike occurring” is not consistently applied throughout the equations.
6.  **Overstated Claims & Comparisons:** Comparing to other works (e.g., [16]) requires more nuance. A direct comparison of hardware and software implementations, considering factors beyond power efficiency (e.g., chip area, design complexity) is missing. Claiming "by far the best published power efficiency" needs a more thorough justification and acknowledgement of limitations.
7.  **Minor Errors & Formatting:** Numerous minor errors detract from the paper's professionalism. For example, inconsistent use of units, and a lack of detailed figure captions. Table 1 is referenced but not fully described in the main text.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Potentially significant contribution to enabling backpropagation-trained networks on neuromorphic hardware.
*   Promising experimental results, particularly on power efficiency.

**Con:**

*   Lack of reproducibility due to insufficient detail in methodology.
*   Unjustified approximations and simplifications.
*   Ambiguous notation and inconsistencies.
*   Overstated claims and weak comparisons to related work.
*   Numerous minor errors indicating a lack of meticulous attention to detail.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the interesting concept and encouraging results, the numerous methodological shortcomings, inconsistencies, and lack of detailed explanation preclude acceptance. The paper feels incomplete and insufficiently validated. The approximations made without proper justification raise concerns about the robustness of the approach. While the authors demonstrate a potential path forward, the current presentation falls short of NIPS quality. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the issues outlined above, provide a more rigorous analysis of their approximations, and ensure that their methodology is fully reproducible before resubmitting. A significant overhaul is required to address these concerns.
