Summary
The paper proposes a “constrain-then-train” method to reconcile backpropagation with neuromorphic deployment constraints (spiking neurons, very low-precision/binary synapses, limited fan-in). During training, synaptic on/off states are represented as probabilities c̃ij∈[0,1], neuron activations are treated as spike probabilities via a Gaussian approximation of thresholded weighted Bernoulli sums, and gradients are derived in closed form (with an additional approximation that drops a σ−3 term). Synaptic strengths sij are fixed a priori (balanced ±1, block-shared per TrueNorth axon type) to minimize redundancy via pairwise decorrelation arguments. A “progressive mixing” topology akin to sliding windows is used to match TrueNorth’s core-to-core connectivity limits. Trained networks are mapped to TrueNorth by sampling binary synapses and spikes; accuracy is improved via ensembles of independently sampled networks. On MNIST, they report up to 99.42% using a 30-core design and a 64-member ensemble (1920 cores), and extreme energy efficiency on small networks (down to 0.268 µJ/classification at 92.7%) on TrueNorth.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the neuromorphic constraints and a training procedure directly reflecting deployment constraints (spikes, binary synapses, limited connectivity).
- Differentiable probabilistic formulation with an analytic forward model for threshold crossings, avoiding straight-through estimators; pragmatic gradient approximation.
- Hardware relevance: concrete mapping to TrueNorth, real-time operation, and energy measurements at 1 kHz throughput.
- Competitive MNIST accuracy for spiking networks; compelling energy-accuracy trade-offs for small models.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope: results are only on MNIST. No evaluation on more demanding datasets (e.g., SVHN, CIFAR-10), no convolutional instantiation despite claiming applicability, and no comparison to modern binarized/quantized CNNs on embedded platforms.
- Heavy reliance on large ensembles to reach top accuracy (64 members, 1920 cores). The single-network performance of the larger model is not reported; the 5-core single-network accuracy is only 92.7%. The practical trade-offs between ensembling vs. single-instance determinization (e.g., thresholding c̃) are not analyzed.
- The probabilistic forward model rests on several assumptions (independence, CLT, balanced ±1 weights, 256 inputs) and a gradient approximation (dropping σ−3 term) without ablation or error analysis. There is no empirical check of the Gaussian/gradient approximations’ fidelity or their effect on training and final accuracy.
- The initialization rationale (pairwise decorrelation via minimizing column inner products) is appealing but is not validated by ablations (e.g., randomized vs. proposed S, learning sij vs. fixing them).
- Baseline comparisons are weak. The “best published” claims are made against heterogeneous setups, including [12] and [16], with different hardware, precision, and speeds. Energy comparisons appear apples-to-oranges; the accounting (active + proportional leakage) and the discrepancy between the 4 µJ claim (95% acc) vs. 108 µJ at 99.42% require clarification.
- Reproducibility: several implementation choices (exact topology parameters, output neuron counts per class, mapping and sampling details, training variance across runs) are insufficiently specified for an expert to reproduce results without the figures/tables. No confidence intervals or variability across training seeds or ensemble samplings.

Quality
- Technically coherent but relies on unvalidated approximations and strong independence assumptions. The empirical validation is too narrow (MNIST only) and omits key ablations (effect of ensemble size, gradient approximation, initialization, fixed vs. learned synaptic strengths).

Clarity
- Generally well-written with a clear narrative. However, critical experimental details are missing or only in figures. The energy measurement methodology and the ensemble vs. single-instance trade-off need more precise reporting.

Originality
- The “constrain-then-train” probabilistic relaxation to binary synapses and spikes with constrained connectivity is a thoughtful combination of ideas. Related work on spiking networks, binary/trinary weights, and expectation backprop is cited. The particular closed-form Gaussian treatment and TrueNorth-specific mapping are novel in combination, though conceptually close to prior probabilistic/backprop relaxations.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for neuromorphic deployment, but current evidence is limited. Demonstrations on MNIST alone, heavy use of ensembles, and lack of rigorous comparisons to strong baselines reduce confidence that this approach will generalize or be adopted.

Questions for the authors
1. What is the single-network accuracy of the 30-core model (no ensembling)? How does deterministic thresholding of c̃ compare to sampling?
2. How sensitive are results to the Gaussian/gradient approximation? Please include ablations (full gradient vs. approximated, different fan-in).
3. Can you report variance across training seeds and across ensemble samplings? Are MNIST improvements over [12] statistically significant?
4. Please clarify energy accounting and reconcile the 4 µJ (95%) claim with the 108 µJ at 99.42%. How does energy scale with ensemble size?
5. What is the effect of learning sij versus fixing them? Any evidence that the decorrelation initialization improves learning vs. random assignments?
6. Can you provide results on at least one additional dataset and/or a convolutional instantiation to support generality?

Pros
- Practical, hardware-aware training that maps cleanly to a major neuromorphic platform.
- Competitive spiking-network accuracy and impressive energy on tiny models.
- Clear methodological framing; plausible path to broader architectures.

Cons
- Evidence limited to MNIST; heavy reliance on ensembles; weak baselines.
- Unvalidated modeling/gradient approximations; missing ablations and statistical rigor.
- Energy/efficiency comparisons across systems may not be fair or clearly reported.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper addresses a timely and important problem and presents a promising, hardware-conscious training approach. However, given NIPS standards and the emphasis on rigorous validation, the experimental scope (MNIST only), reliance on large ensembles, lack of ablations and statistical analyses, and unclear baseline/energy comparisons make the current submission premature. Strengthening with broader datasets, ablations on the approximations and initialization, deterministic single-network results, and clearer, fairer comparisons could lift this to a strong submission. Confidence: medium.
