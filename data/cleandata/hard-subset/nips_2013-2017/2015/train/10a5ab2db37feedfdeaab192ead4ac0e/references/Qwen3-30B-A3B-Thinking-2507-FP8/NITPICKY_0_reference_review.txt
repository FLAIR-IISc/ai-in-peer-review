

# Review of "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing"

This paper proposes a learning rule and network topology that enables backpropagation training for neuromorphic hardware with spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity. The authors claim to bridge the gap between traditional deep learning and neuromorphic computing by training networks with continuous-valued probabilities that map directly to hardware constraints.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper claims three main contributions: (1) an offline training methodology using backpropagation for networks with spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity; (2) superior MNIST accuracy (99.42%) compared to previous spiking network approaches; and (3) superior power efficiency (4 µJ/classification) on TrueNorth compared to other low-power approaches. While previous work has explored subsets of these elements (e.g., backpropagation with spiking neurons [9-12], or networks with low-precision synapses [13-15]), the authors claim novelty in combining all three elements.

## Strengths

The paper presents a clear motivation for energy-efficient neuromorphic computing and provides a potentially useful framework for mapping deep learning models to neuromorphic hardware. The experimental results on TrueNorth demonstrate a concrete implementation with energy efficiency metrics that appear to be competitive with the state of the art.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Inconsistencies

1. **Contradictory claims about precision**: The paper claims "synapses requiring less bits of precision than even trinary weights" but then states "1 bit per synapse for the on/off state and an additional 0.15 bits per synapse for the lookup table scheme." This totals 1.15 bits, which is not "less than" trinary (which would require at least 1.58 bits for ternary representation). The claim is misleading and contradicts the stated technical details.

2. **Inconsistent threshold values**: The paper uses θ = 0 in Equation 3 for neuron activation but then uses θ = 0.5 in the loss function (Equation 7). This inconsistency could lead to significant errors in the gradient calculation, as the threshold value fundamentally changes the activation probability.

3. **Questionable approximation**: The approximation in Equation 4 (dropping the right-hand term) is not justified. The authors claim that the right-hand term is "much smaller" due to μj being close to zero, but if μj is close to zero and θ = 0 (as used in Equation 3), then (θ-μj) is close to zero, making the right-hand term potentially large, not small.

4. **Misleading terminology**: The paper describes "trinary synapses" but actually implements binary synapses with a lookup table for strength values. This misnomer misrepresents the precision level of the approach.

### Methodological Issues

1. **Marginal accuracy improvement**: The claimed 99.42% accuracy on MNIST is only 0.3% better than the 99.12% reported in reference [12], yet the paper claims this is "the best accuracy to date." This is an overstatement for such a marginal improvement, especially when standard CNNs achieve over 99.7% accuracy on MNIST.

2. **Lack of comparison with non-spiking state of the art**: The paper doesn't adequately compare with standard CNNs that achieve much higher accuracy on MNIST, making the claimed "best accuracy" appear less impressive.

3. **Insufficient experimental details**: The paper lacks critical details about the training procedure (number of epochs, learning rate schedule beyond the description), network initialization specifics, and hardware configuration details necessary to reproduce the results.

### Presentation Issues

1. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses both "cij" (binary indicator) and "c̃ij" (probability) without clear justification for the tilde notation. This inconsistency makes the mathematical description difficult to follow.

2. **Unclear mapping procedure**: The procedure for mapping the continuous training network to the binary deployment network lacks sufficient detail. Specifically, the paper doesn't explain how the random sampling of synaptic connections (in the ensemble approach) affects the final network performance.

3. **Formatting inconsistencies**: The references contain numerous formatting errors (e.g., inconsistent punctuation, missing commas, and inconsistent capitalization of journal titles).

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- The problem addressed is relevant to the field of neuromorphic computing.
- The paper provides a concrete implementation on TrueNorth with energy efficiency claims.
- The approach could be useful for researchers working on energy-efficient neural network deployment.

**Against Acceptance:**
- The technical claims are inconsistent and misleading (particularly regarding precision requirements).
- The accuracy improvement is marginal compared to existing methods.
- The paper contains multiple mathematical errors and unjustified approximations.
- The presentation contains numerous inconsistencies and formatting errors.
- The novelty claim is overstated compared to previous work.

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses an important problem in neuromorphic computing, the technical inconsistencies, misleading claims, and methodological flaws significantly undermine its contribution. The paper's claims about precision requirements are directly contradicted by its own technical description, and the marginal accuracy improvement is overstated. The mathematical errors in the gradient derivation (Equation 4) and the inconsistent use of threshold values (θ) are particularly concerning as they directly impact the validity of the learning algorithm.

As a perfectionist reviewer who scrutinizes every technical detail, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper in its current form. The minor flaws identified are not merely "minor" but are fundamental to the paper's technical claims. The paper would require substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. I recommend rejection.
