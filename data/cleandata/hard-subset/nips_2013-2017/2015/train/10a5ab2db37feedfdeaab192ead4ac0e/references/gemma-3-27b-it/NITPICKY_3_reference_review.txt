This paper attempts to bridge the gap between the efficiency of neuromorphic hardware and the training power of backpropagation. The core idea – constraining a backpropagation-trained network to operate within the limitations of spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and limited connectivity – is conceptually sound and addresses a significant challenge in deploying deep learning models on specialized hardware. However, while the results are presented as strong (99.42% MNIST accuracy, impressive power efficiency on TrueNorth), the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, omissions, and a concerning lack of thoroughness in certain areas, leading me to recommend rejection.

The introduction establishes the motivation well, but the writing is occasionally verbose and repetitive. The novelty claims are somewhat overstated; previous work *has* combined elements of these approaches, as the authors acknowledge, but not in this specific configuration. The framing of “first time” claims needs more nuance. 

Section 2, describing the deployment hardware, is concise but lacks critical detail. While referencing TrueNorth is sufficient for context, the paper doesn’t clearly articulate *how* the constrained network maps onto the chip’s architecture beyond high-level statements. Specifically, how are the probabilistic synaptic weights handled in the TrueNorth implementation? The statement about requiring "0.15 bits per synapse" for the lookup table scheme is perplexing; it's unclear how a fractional bit is utilized.

Section 3, detailing network training, is the most problematic. The mathematical derivations, while not intrinsically *wrong*, are presented without sufficient justification for key approximations. The simplification of Equation (4), while computationally convenient, is glossed over with a hand-wavy explanation (“we can expect…”) and lacks rigorous analysis of its impact on accuracy. The assumption of near-zero mean and small values for µ, ñi, and c̃ij needs to be validated, not merely asserted. The explanation of the loss function and gradient calculation feels rushed and assumes a level of familiarity with backpropagation that isn’t warranted in a paper aiming for broad impact.  Crucially, the training hyperparameters (learning rate decay schedule, momentum, dropout) are listed but *without justification*. Why were these specific values chosen? Were they tuned systematically? A sensitivity analysis would greatly strengthen the claims.

The "Mapping training network to deployment network" subsection is weak. The use of ensembles is mentioned, but the rationale for ensemble size selection is absent. Simply stating "we can utilize an ensemble" isn’t a justification for a 64-member ensemble. The procedure for generating random numbers for synapse activation and input spiking feels ad-hoc. 

Section 3.4, describing the network topology, is better explained but still lacks detail. The sliding window approach is clear, but the rationale behind the specific parameters (R=16, S=12, etc.) is not provided. These choices likely impact performance significantly and deserve discussion.

Section 5, presenting results, is where the paper’s flaws are most glaring. The energy numbers are impressive, but the comparison to [16] lacks context. The details of *their* implementation aren’t provided, making a fair comparison difficult. The statement that the network classified 1000 images/second is insufficient; what was the latency? Were there any bottlenecks in the TrueNorth pipeline?  The presentation of results lacks statistical rigor. Are the accuracy improvements statistically significant? Were multiple runs performed to account for variability in the random initialization and ensemble sampling? The graphs are not clearly labeled and require more detailed captions.

The discussion section is overly optimistic and fails to adequately address the limitations of the approach. The claim that this method is “particularly timely” feels like hyperbole. While the potential for deployment on neuromorphic hardware is exciting, the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a path to scaling this approach to more complex datasets.

**Pro Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem.
*   Demonstrates promising results on MNIST.
*   Shows feasibility of deploying a backpropagation-trained network on TrueNorth.

**Con Acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor flaws and omissions throughout the paper.
*   Lack of rigorous justification for key approximations and hyperparameter choices.
*   Insufficient detail regarding the mapping from training network to deployment hardware.
*   Weak statistical analysis and lack of reproducibility information.
*   Overstated novelty claims.
*   Insufficient comparison with existing work.
*   Overly optimistic discussion.



In conclusion, despite the potential significance of the work, the lack of thoroughness, the numerous minor flaws, and the absence of a convincing justification for the reported results lead me to recommend rejection. The paper requires significant revision to address these issues before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS.
