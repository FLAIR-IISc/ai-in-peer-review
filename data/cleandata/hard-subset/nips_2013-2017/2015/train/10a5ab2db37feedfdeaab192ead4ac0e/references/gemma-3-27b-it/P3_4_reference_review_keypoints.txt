- Overall: Compelling, technically sound approach that bridges backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware with strong MNIST accuracy and energy results; strong candidate for acceptance.  
- Main contribution: "Constrain-then-train" method that trains networks with 0–1 probabilistic parameters directly mappable to spiking, low-precision neuromorphic hardware.  
- Architecture contribution: Introduces a sliding-window/progressive-mixing topology to satisfy connectivity constraints while maximizing information flow.  
- Empirical result: Achieves state-of-the-art spiking-network MNIST accuracy (99.42%), surpassing prior spiking results.  
- Power efficiency: Demonstrates impressive energy use on TrueNorth (≈4 µJ/classification), far lower than prior low-power approaches.  
- Novelty: Combines probabilistic backpropagation, constrained topology, and direct hardware mapping in a uniquely comprehensive way.  
- Clarity and presentation: Paper is well-written, figures/tables and derivations are clear and accessible.  
- Thoroughness: Addresses initialization, mapping to hardware, and provides detailed training and experimental setup.  
- Relevance: Strong fit for NIPS at the intersection of ML and neuromorphic computing.  
- Limitation — dataset: Experiments are limited to MNIST, raising questions about generalization to higher-dimensional datasets (e.g., ImageNet).  
- Limitation — gradient approximation: Backpropagation simplification (dropping a term) is justified for efficiency but needs further analysis of its impact and sensitivity.  
- Limitation — topology design: The sliding-window topology appears somewhat ad-hoc and would benefit from systematic exploration of alternatives.  
- Limitation — hardware dependence: Heavy focus on TrueNorth may limit perceived generality; preliminary results on other platforms would strengthen claims.  
- Limitation — scalability/ensembles: Best results use a large ensemble (64), which increases hardware resource needs and warrants discussion of accuracy vs. resource trade-offs.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Tackles an important problem, offers a novel and technically sound solution with strong empirical and energy-efficiency gains, and is well-documented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Evaluation confined to MNIST and unexamined backpropagation approximations require further justification.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: Provide (even preliminary) results on more complex datasets, conduct sensitivity analysis/justification of gradient approximations, and discuss scalability and ensemble trade-offs.
