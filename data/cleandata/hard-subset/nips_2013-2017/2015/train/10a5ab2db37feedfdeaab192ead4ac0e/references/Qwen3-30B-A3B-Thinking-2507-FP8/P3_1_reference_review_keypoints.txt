- Proposes a "constrain-then-train" method to train networks with weights in [0,1] that map directly to neuromorphic spiking hardware.  
- Trains continuous-valued inputs/outputs and interprets weights as probabilities to avoid post-training discretization.  
- Reports 99.42% accuracy on MNIST and 4 µJ per classification on TrueNorth, outperforming previous low-power approaches.  
- Main contributions: probabilistic backpropagation for spiking/low-precision networks, a progressive-mixing topology respecting connectivity constraints, and an infomax-based initialization.  
- Technical soundness: probabilistic representation and initialization strategy are well-motivated and theoretically grounded.  
- Claim support is limited: results on MNIST are solid but lack comparison to recent SNN advances (surrogate gradients, STDP, temporal coding).  
- Paper completeness is lacking: no discussion of limitations or behavior on more complex datasets (CIFAR-10, ImageNet).  
- The Gaussian approximation for neuron output probabilities (Eq. 3) is used without sufficient justification or error analysis.  
- Evaluation is narrow: only MNIST tested, no ablation studies, and no analysis of scalability to larger networks/tasks.  
- Clarity is good overall, but derivations (Eq. 4), the Gaussian approximation, and the training-to-deployment mapping need clearer explanation and examples.  
- Originality: the constrain-then-train paradigm and combination of constraints with backprop are novel, though aspects overlap with stochastic computing and convolutional designs.  
- The infomax-based initialization appears to be a novel and interesting contribution to neuromorphic network design.  
- Significance: provides substantial energy-efficiency gains relevant to edge/embedded computing, but applicability beyond MNIST/TrueNorth is unproven.  
- Strengths: novel methodology, impressive energy metrics, well-motivated initialization, clear tradeoff analysis, and strong writing.  
- Weaknesses: limited dataset evaluation, insufficient SNN comparisons, unjustified Gaussian approximation, no ablations, and limited scalability/limitations discussion.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on addressing comparative, justificatory, and ablation shortcomings.  
- Required revisions: expand related work and comparisons to recent SNN papers, justify the Gaussian approximation (ideally with experiments), add ablation studies, and discuss scalability/limitations.  
- Suggested additional experiments: validate the Gaussian approximation empirically and evaluate at least one additional dataset (e.g., CIFAR-10).  
- Final assessment: strong, technically sound paper with important energy-efficiency results that would be improved by broader evaluation and clearer justification of approximations.
