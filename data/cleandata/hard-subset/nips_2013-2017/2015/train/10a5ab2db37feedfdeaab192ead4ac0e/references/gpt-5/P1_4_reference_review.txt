Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a “constrain-then-train” approach to bridge backpropagation-based learning and energy-efficient neuromorphic deployment. During training, each input x, synapse state c, and neuron output n is represented by a continuous variable in [0,1] interpreted as a probability: x̃i = P(xi = 1), c̃ij = P(cij = 1), ñj = P(nj = 1). The forward pass approximates the summed input to each neuron as a Gaussian (sum of weighted Bernoulli variables), with closed-form mean and variance, and computes the probability of firing via the Gaussian ccdf at a threshold. Gradients are obtained by differentiating this probabilistic forward model; a higher-order term in ∂ñ/∂c̃ is dropped for efficiency under assumptions of zero-mean balanced weights and sufficiently large input fan-in.

The training learns only synapse connection probabilities and biases; synaptic strengths are fixed to a hardware-compatible pattern of ±1 values per neuron, chosen to approximately minimize pairwise inner products across columns (to reduce redundancy and decorrelate inputs), satisfy zero-sum constraints, and fit TrueNorth’s 4 axon types. A topology termed progressive mixing uses sliding-window, sparsely connected layers (not weight sharing), so deeper layers integrate information from progressively larger input regions while respecting TrueNorth constraints (256 axons/core, binary synapses, 1-bit per synapse plus LUT).

After training, deployment samples synapse states and spikes according to the learned probabilities; ensembles are formed by multiple independent samplings without retraining, and class predictions are averages across multiple output neurons per class (and across ensemble members). On MNIST, a 30-core, 4-layer network with a 64-member ensemble achieves 99.42% accuracy at 108 µJ/classification at 1000 images/s, reported as better than prior spiking networks with high-precision weights (99.12%) and binary neuron/synapse networks (97.88%). A 5-core, 2-layer network yields 92.7% at 0.268 µJ/classification; the introduction also claims 4 µJ/classification at 95% accuracy.

This work relates to: (i) spiking networks trained with backprop but high-precision synapses; (ii) networks with low-precision (e.g., trinary/binary) synapses but continuous activations; (iii) expectation/backprop in probabilistic/binary networks with full connectivity. The novelty is to combine spiking neurons, lower-than-trinary synaptic precision, sparse constrained connectivity, and a probabilistic relaxation enabling backprop, with direct deployment on TrueNorth.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled probabilistic relaxation that maps directly onto spiking, binary-synapse hardware; well-motivated by TrueNorth constraints.
- Innovative synapse-strength initialization that approximately decorrelates neuron inputs within hardware constraints; connects to infomax/orthogonal initialization intuitions.
- Strong empirical results on MNIST for spiking hardware, including excellent energy/latency numbers; real-time deployment on a large-scale chip.
- Simple training pipeline that reuses standard tricks (mini-batch, momentum, dropout, augmentation) and yields ensemble members “for free” via sampling.
- The progressive-mixing topology is practical for constrained fan-in and illustrates a path toward scalable deployment.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Comparisons rely heavily on a 64-member ensemble; single-network accuracy for the 30-core model is not reported. Fairness of comparing ensembling vs. prior single models should be discussed; also compare under equal resource or energy budgets (e.g., one larger single model vs. many sampled members).
- The Gaussian approximation and independence assumptions (and the omission of the σ^3 term) are heuristic. No empirical ablation shows the approximation’s impact on accuracy, gradient quality, or training stability.
- Fixed synaptic strengths (±1 blocks) may limit representational capacity; no comparison to learning strengths within allowed LUT constraints is shown.
- Topology choice (non-shared convolution-like windows) sacrifices weight sharing; a comparison to true convolutional filters (with learning of shared kernels under the same connectivity budget) would clarify trade-offs.
- Energy numbers: the text cites 4 µJ/classification at 95% accuracy but the Results section highlights 0.268 µJ at 92.7% and 108 µJ at 99.42%. Please report the exact configuration achieving 95%/4 µJ and include error bars/variance across runs. Also clarify how leakage was apportioned and how energy scales with ensemble size.
- Some notation/equation clarity issues (e.g., Equation 3 appears mis-formatted; pk definition via Gaussian approximation for averaged outputs may need more detail).

Quality:
- Technically sound overall, with a coherent training-to-deployment pipeline. The gradient derivations are reasonable but include approximations that should be validated empirically (e.g., compare full vs. approximated gradient on a subset).
- Experimental section demonstrates the concept convincingly on MNIST with real hardware. However, ablations are missing: effect of ensemble size; effect of fixed s vs. learned s; effect of progressive mixing choices; sensitivity to R, S, F; robustness to the Gaussian/independence assumptions.
- Comparisons: accuracy comparisons are promising but would benefit from controlled resource- and latency-normalized baselines.

Clarity:
- Generally well-organized with helpful descriptions of TrueNorth constraints, mapping, and topology. Include: number of output neurons per class, full hyperparameter details, training time, and variability across different random seeds/samplings.
- Fix typographic issues in equations; explicitly write the ccdf form used. Clarify the loss definition for pk and how averaging across neurons/classes is handled in the probabilistic model.

Originality:
- The constrained probabilistic backprop framework tailored to binary synapses, spiking neurons, and sparse fan-in is novel in this specific combination and deployment. The synapse-strength initialization under hardware constraints is also original in this context.
- Relation to expectation backprop and stochastic bit-stream networks is adequately cited, but a more explicit contrast to those methods’ assumptions (e.g., full connectivity, binary neurons) would help.

Significance:
- Demonstrates a practical path to training deployable, energy-efficient spiking systems with strong accuracy on a canonical dataset and real-time operation. Likely to be of interest to neuromorphic and efficient inference communities.
- Broader significance would be strengthened by results on more challenging datasets and by comparisons to convolutional architectures compatible with the same hardware.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report single-member performance for all network sizes; show ensemble gains vs. number of members, including energy/latency trade-offs and variance across samplings.
- Provide ablations: (i) full vs. approximated gradient; (ii) learned vs. fixed synaptic strengths within the LUT constraints; (iii) progressive mixing vs. convolutional weight sharing.
- Clarify energy measurement methodology and provide per-core power, leakage apportioning, and scaling with throughput.
- Include statistical confidence intervals for accuracy across multiple independent trainings/samplings.
- Provide more details/code/pseudocode for the mapping and initialization procedures to aid reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance:
- Compelling solution to an important problem (training for neuromorphic deployment) with real hardware validation.
- Strong energy-efficiency and competitive accuracy for spiking systems.
- Simple, general training recipe that may transfer to other neuromorphic platforms.

Cons against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on large ensembles for top accuracy; limited controlled comparisons under equal resource budgets.
- Approximations not validated by ablation; limited analysis of when assumptions break.
- Results limited to MNIST; no demonstration on more complex, modern benchmarks.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept.
Reviewer confidence: Medium.
