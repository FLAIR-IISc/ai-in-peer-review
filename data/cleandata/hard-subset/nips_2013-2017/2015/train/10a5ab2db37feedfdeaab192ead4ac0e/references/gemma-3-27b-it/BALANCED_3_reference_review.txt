This paper addresses the crucial problem of reconciling the efficiency of neuromorphic hardware with the training power of backpropagation. The authors propose a novel learning rule and network topology that allows training with continuous values while maintaining a direct mapping to spiking, low-precision synapses and constrained connectivity, suitable for deployment on neuromorphic chips like TrueNorth. The core idea of representing continuous values as probabilities of spikes or synapse activation is insightful and effectively bridges the gap between the training and deployment domains.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of directly applying backpropagation to neuromorphic systems and framing the contribution as a solution to this incompatibility. It’s strongly connected to previous work in neuromorphic computing (references [4-7]) and backpropagation-based learning ([8-15]). The authors convincingly position their work as a step forward, highlighting how it combines elements from prior research in a novel way, namely, probabilistic backpropagation with constrained connectivity and low-precision synapses. 

The strengths of the paper are considerable. First, the performance on MNIST (99.42% accuracy) surpasses existing spiking neural network results, even those using higher-precision synapses [12] or full connectivity [15]. This demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach. Second, the demonstration of real-time operation on TrueNorth, achieving a significantly improved power efficiency for digit recognition (4 µJ per classification) compared to other low-power methods [16], is a compelling result and a key contribution. Third, the detailed description of the network topology, training methodology, and mapping process is thorough and facilitates reproducibility. The careful consideration of synapse initialization to maximize information transfer is a clever optimization. The paper is also well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the results are impressive, the scope is limited to the MNIST dataset. It is not immediately clear how well this approach would generalize to more complex datasets like CIFAR-10 or ImageNet.  The derivation of the gradient approximation in Equation 4 relies on several assumptions (µ ≈ 0, small ñj and c̃ij) that might not hold for all network configurations or datasets. A more rigorous analysis or investigation of the impact of these assumptions on performance would be beneficial.  The explanation of the network topology, particularly the sliding window approach and the rationale behind the chosen parameters (R, F, S), could be more intuitive and detailed. While Figure 3A provides a visual representation, a more in-depth discussion of the design choices and their impact on performance would be helpful.  The section on network initialization, while insightful, is quite dense and could benefit from clearer explanations and potentially more illustrative examples.  Finally, the energy efficiency comparison relies on a single prior work [16]. Including a broader comparison with other low-power approaches would strengthen the claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective approach to reconcile backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware.
*   State-of-the-art accuracy on MNIST for spiking neural networks.
*   Compelling demonstration of real-time operation and superior power efficiency on TrueNorth.
*   Thorough description of the methodology and clear writing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope (MNIST dataset only).
*   Gradient approximation relies on potentially restrictive assumptions.
*   Network topology explanation could be improved.
*   Network initialization section is dense and could be clearer.
*   Energy efficiency comparison could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of neuromorphic computing. The authors successfully demonstrate a practical and effective approach to training spiking neural networks using backpropagation, achieving impressive results on MNIST and demonstrating real-time operation on TrueNorth with superior power efficiency. While the scope is limited and some aspects of the methodology could be further clarified, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding the generalization to more complex datasets, the gradient approximation, the network topology explanation, and the energy efficiency comparison. A discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential future research directions would also be valuable.
