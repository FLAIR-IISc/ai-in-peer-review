- Presents a "constrain-then-train" probabilistic backpropagation method to train networks under neuromorphic hardware constraints and map them directly to spiking, low-precision hardware.  
- Trains continuous-valued probabilities constrained to [0,1] to represent spike probabilities or synapse on/off states for deployment.  
- Introduces a Gaussian-approximation-based derivation for gradients (Equations 3-4) and a progressive mixing network topology.  
- Demonstrates strong MNIST results on TrueNorth: 99.42% accuracy and 4 µJ per classification at 95% accuracy.  
- Technical approach is sound with detailed derivations, justified approximations, and a well-designed experimental setup with relevant baselines.  
- Novelty: first end-to-end solution combining spiking neurons, sub-trinary synapses, constrained connectivity, and backpropagation training.  
- Significance: addresses the gap between standard backpropagation and energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware, with state-of-the-art spiking-network accuracy and power efficiency.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, organized, and provides reproducible training and hardware details with helpful figures.  
- Limitation: evaluation confined to MNIST, so scalability and applicability to more complex datasets remain unproven.  
- Limitation: Gaussian approximation is used without empirical error bounds or analysis of its impact on training performance.  
- Limitation: lacking comparisons to other low-precision/quantization methods (e.g., BinaryConnect, XNOR-Net) that could achieve similar ends.  
- Limitation: generalizability to other neuromorphic hardware besides TrueNorth is claimed but not demonstrated.  
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution that effectively bridges training and neuromorphic deployment, deserving acceptance.  
- Recommendation: accept for NIPS, with suggested improvements in broader evaluations and deeper theoretical/comparative analysis.  
- Pro points: novel paradigm, strong experimental results, technical rigor, clear presentation.  
- Con points: narrow evaluation scope (MNIST), incomplete justification of approximations, limited comparison to other low-precision approaches.
