This paper presents an interesting approach to bridging the gap between the efficiency of neuromorphic hardware and the training power of backpropagation. The core idea of constraining values to [0, 1] and interpreting them as probabilities, coupled with a progressive mixing network topology, is a clever way to make backpropagation compatible with spiking neurons and low-precision synapses. The reported MNIST accuracy of 99.42% and the power efficiency results on TrueNorth are impressive. However, despite these positive aspects, a multitude of minor but significant issues detract from the overall quality of the work, leading me to recommend rejection.

The introduction, while setting the stage, suffers from a lack of precision. The claim of achieving “the best accuracy to date” requires more rigorous comparison to recent literature; simply citing one older result ([12]) is insufficient. The phrasing regarding the novelty (“i) we demonstrate for the first time…”) is overstated, as several papers have explored related ideas, albeit with different combinations of constraints. The lack of a clear, concise statement of the primary contributions within the introduction hinders understanding.

Section 2 provides a reasonable overview of the TrueNorth chip, but the explanation of the 4 axon types and the 0.15 bits per synapse for the lookup table is vague. How is this 0.15 bits actually achieved? What is the distribution of values in the lookup table? This level of detail is crucial for assessing the practicality of the approach.

Section 3, detailing network training, is the most problematic. Equation (1) is standard, but the subsequent description lacks nuance. The justification for factoring the synaptic weight into *c<sub>ij</sub>* and *s<sub>ij</sub>* is weak and doesn’t fully convince. The probabilistic framing is introduced without sufficient motivation; why is approximating the summation as a Gaussian justified beyond "sufficient numbers"? The conditions under which the approximation in Equation (4) holds are stated but not adequately substantiated – simply stating “we can expect µ to be close to zero” is insufficient. A more rigorous analysis or empirical validation is needed. The rationale for dropping the right term in Equation 4 needs better justification. The statement that the gradient is approximated “for computational efficiency” feels like a shortcut taken without proper consideration of potential error. The clipping of bias terms to [-255, 255] is mentioned almost as an afterthought, and its impact on training isn't discussed. The description of the mini-batch training details is adequate, but the precise learning rate schedule would be beneficial.

Section 3.3, concerning the mapping of the training network to the deployment network, is concise but lacks detail. The phrase “sampling the training network” is unclear. Is this akin to ensemble methods? If so, it should be explicitly stated.

Section 3.4, on network topology, introduces a sliding window approach. While the explanation is generally clear, the rationale behind the chosen parameters (R=16, S=12, etc.) is not provided. These choices seem arbitrary and could significantly impact performance.

Section 5, presenting the results, lacks sufficient experimental detail. How many training epochs were used? What was the validation accuracy during training? The table displaying the energy/accuracy tradeoffs is helpful, but additional metrics, such as training time, would be valuable. The statement "Both networks classified 1000 images per second" is somewhat misleading; is this sustained performance, or an average? The comparison to other low-power approaches ([16]) should include a more detailed breakdown of their methodologies and hardware platforms.

Section 6, the discussion, is largely qualitative and reiterates points made earlier. The statement about "constrain-then-train" versus "train-then-constrain" is insightful but lacks specific examples to illustrate the advantages of their approach. The suggestions for future work are reasonable but lack concrete details.

Throughout the paper, the writing style is uneven. There are numerous instances of imprecise language and a tendency towards hand-waving arguments. The formatting also contains minor inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing around equations). References are generally appropriate, but a more thorough literature review would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of techniques for bridging backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware.
*   Impressive MNIST accuracy and power efficiency results.
*   Potential for real-world applications in low-power embedded systems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of rigor in the theoretical analysis and justification of approximations.
*   Insufficient experimental detail and validation.
*   Imprecise language and uneven writing style.
*   Overstated claims of novelty.
*   Numerous minor methodological flaws and inconsistencies.



In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting idea and promising results, the numerous shortcomings in terms of rigor, clarity, and detail prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper feels incomplete and requires significant revisions to address the issues outlined above.
