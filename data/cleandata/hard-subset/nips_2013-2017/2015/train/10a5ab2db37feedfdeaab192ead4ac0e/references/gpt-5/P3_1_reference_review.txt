Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a “constrain-then-train” methodology to reconcile gradient-based learning with highly constrained neuromorphic hardware (IBM TrueNorth). The training network uses continuous variables in [0,1] that are interpreted as probabilities of spikes (inputs/neurons) and synapse-on states (c̃ij). A Gaussian approximation to the sum of weighted Bernoulli inputs yields a differentiable surrogate for the spiking neuron’s firing probability ñj, enabling backpropagation. Only the connection probabilities c̃ij and biases are learned; synaptic strengths sij are fixed a priori in balanced ±1 patterns chosen to minimize pairwise correlations across neurons on a core. After training, the deployment network is obtained by independent Bernoulli sampling of synapses and input spikes, optionally forming an ensemble by multiple samples. The topology uses sliding windows over limited fan-in cores (“progressive mixing”), similar in spirit to local receptive fields but without weight sharing.

This connects to prior lines of work: backpropagation for spiking networks with high-precision synapses; binary/trinary synapses or neurons trained with probabilistic surrogates and straight-through estimators; and conversions of ReLU CNNs to SNNs. The main novelty is to jointly handle spiking neurons, one-bit synapses (with shared 9-bit per-axon-type scaling), and hard connectivity constraints of TrueNorth, while reporting a real-time deployment with low energy.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus on bridging algorithm-hardware mismatch; method is tailored to practical neuromorphic constraints (binary synapses, limited fan-in, shared axon types).
- A principled, differentiable surrogate model based on a Gaussian CDF for firing probability, with explicit gradients and an approximation to reduce compute.
- Hardware-aware initialization that aims to decorrelate pre-activations across neurons on a core, with balanced ±1 strengths and even axon-type distribution.
- Demonstrated real-time deployment on TrueNorth with measured energy/throughput; strong energy efficiency numbers.
- Results on MNIST are competitive for spiking/low-precision settings; careful use of data augmentation and standard training heuristics.

Weaknesses
- Scope is limited to MNIST; no evaluation on more challenging vision tasks or event-based data. Gains at SOTA level rely on large ensembles.
- The gradient approximation drops the dependence of σj on c̃ij; no ablation shows the effect of this approximation or its validity beyond heuristic arguments.
- sij are fixed rather than learned (even within allowed blocks), which could significantly limit expressivity; there is no comparison to partially learning sij.
- The ensemble mapping (sampling c and x) is central to accuracy, but the trade-off between ensemble size, accuracy, latency, and energy is not deeply analyzed; fairness of comparisons to single-model baselines is unclear.
- Some theoretical claims hinge on Gaussianity and independence approximations; empirical validation (e.g., measuring correlations of pre-activations and outputs before/after initialization/training) is missing.
- Energy accounting and measurement methodology (e.g., I/O, control overhead, off-chip energy) are only briefly described; one claim in the abstract (4 µJ @ 95%) appears inconsistent with the later 0.268 µJ @ 92.7% and 108 µJ for the 64-member ensemble.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with a reasonable surrogate model and consistent mapping to hardware. However:
- The derivation of ∂ñj/∂c̃ij omits the σ-term in the final approximation; near decision boundaries, this may matter. An ablation comparing full vs. approximate gradient would strengthen the claim.
- The independence/zero-covariance argument relies on a bivariate Gaussian approximation; while plausible for large fan-in, the finite, structured inputs and learned c̃ij may violate independence assumptions. Validating with measured covariances would be helpful.
- Learning only c̃ij and biases may force the network to solve everything via sparse connectivity “gating,” which could encourage brittle solutions and necessitate ensembles. Exploring learning of per-axon-type s-values or block-wise discrete optimization would be valuable.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, but several equations and implementation details need clarification:
- Eq. 3 is hard to parse; please provide the exact Gaussian CDF expression for P(Ij > θ) with θ specified, and clearly show µj, σj dependence on c̃ij.
- The derivative in Eq. 4 would benefit from a compact expression using the Gaussian pdf φ and explicit terms for ∂µ/∂c and ∂σ/∂c, plus a justification for neglecting the σ-term with empirical checks.
- Clarify the neuron parameterization on TrueNorth (sign conventions for leak, floor, threshold) and how non-integer biases are handled.
- Energy measurement: specify measurement setup, what is included/excluded (I/O, host), and how leakage apportioning is done. Reconcile the early 4 µJ @ 95% claim with later results.

Originality
Novel combination: probabilistic backprop for binary synapses with constrained fan-in and spiking deployment, plus hardware-aware initialization and topology. Related work on binary/binarized networks and spiking conversions is acknowledged, but additional citations to contemporary binarization and STE approaches would help position the work. The ensemble-as-sampling idea is straightforward but effective in this constrained setting.

Significance
Bridging backprop to highly energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware is important. The real-time deployment and energy measurements make this practically relevant. However, demonstrating scalability beyond MNIST and reducing reliance on large ensembles are important next steps to broaden impact.

Questions and requests for authors
- Provide an ablation: full gradient vs. the σ-term-omitted approximation; report convergence and final accuracy differences.
- Quantify how much accuracy is lost by fixing sij vs. learning block-wise s-values; even a small-scale experiment would be informative.
- Report the correlation between neuron pre-activations within a core before/after your initialization and after training; does the initialization achieve the intended decorrelation?
- Analyze accuracy/energy vs. ensemble size more thoroughly and compare to single-model spiking baselines under matched latency/throughput.
- Clarify the discrepancy among energy numbers (0.268 µJ @ 92.7%, 4 µJ @ 95%, 108 µJ @ 99.42%); ensure apples-to-apples conditions.

Minor comments and typos
- Eq. 3 appears malformed; please rewrite cleanly using Φ or erf.
- Specify θ in all contexts (θ=0 for neuron firing, θ=0.5 for class averaging).
- Define pk precisely; the mapping from per-class multiple outputs to pk via Gaussian approximation of the mean needs a clear derivation.
- Provide architecture details sufficient for reproduction (exact core counts per layer, window strides, neuron allocation per core).

Pros
- Practical, hardware-constrained training with real deployment and energy measurements.
- Competitive accuracy for spiking/low-precision hardware on MNIST.
- Thoughtful initialization and topology that respect hardware constraints.
- Clear path to generalization to other neuromorphic substrates.

Cons
- Limited task scope; heavy reliance on ensembles for top accuracy.
- Approximate gradient without empirical validation.
- Fixed synaptic strengths may unduly limit model capacity.
- Energy comparisons and measurement details need clarification.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper provides a valuable, hardware-aware learning method with compelling energy results on a real neuromorphic chip. Strengthening the empirical analysis of the gradient approximation, initialization benefits, and energy reporting—and expanding beyond MNIST in future work—would further solidify its contribution.
