This paper addresses the critical challenge of reconciling the efficiency of neuromorphic hardware with the training efficacy of backpropagation. The authors propose a learning rule and network topology designed to bridge this gap, enabling backpropagation training with constraints suitable for spiking neural networks with low-precision synapses and limited connectivity. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the MNIST dataset, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy for spiking networks and demonstrating real-time performance with impressive power efficiency on the TrueNorth chip.

The paper builds on previous work in several areas. It acknowledges prior efforts to combine backpropagation with spiking neurons (e.g., [9-12]) and low-precision synapses (e.g., [13-15]). However, it distinguishes itself by presenting the *first* methodology for backpropagation training resulting in a network directly deployable on neuromorphic hardware with all three constraints simultaneously: spiking neurons, low synaptic precision (less than ternary), and constrained connectivity. The work also connects to the broader field of probabilistic backpropagation, but extends it to this specific hardware-aware context.  The reference list is comprehensive and includes key papers in neuromorphic computing and deep learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combined constraints and resulting training methodology represent a significant step forward in enabling efficient deep learning on neuromorphic hardware. The approach of “constrain-then-train” is well-motivated and distinguishes this work from “train-then-constrain” strategies.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the training process, including the approximation used for simplifying the gradient calculation. The network topology design, based on a sliding window approach, seems well-suited for the TrueNorth architecture.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The MNIST results are compelling, demonstrating state-of-the-art accuracy for spiking networks and a substantial improvement in power efficiency compared to existing low-power approaches. The real-time demonstration on TrueNorth adds significant weight to these findings.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations of the training network, deployment network, and mapping procedure are clear and concise. The inclusion of figures (although some are referenced before appearing) aids understanding.
* **Significance:**  Bridging the gap between algorithmic advances in deep learning and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic hardware is a highly significant goal. This work provides a practical methodology for achieving this, potentially paving the way for wider adoption of neuromorphic computing in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Approximation Justification:** The approximation made in Equation 4, while computationally efficient, could benefit from a more thorough justification of its accuracy under various network configurations and input data distributions. A sensitivity analysis evaluating the impact of this approximation on the final results would be valuable.
* **Network Topology Parameter Selection:** The choice of network parameters (R, F, S) appears somewhat arbitrary. While the authors provide some rationale, a more systematic exploration of the parameter space and a discussion of their impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Limited Dataset:** The evaluation is limited to the MNIST dataset. While a good starting point, demonstrating the generalizability of the approach to more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Hardware Dependency:** The paper is heavily tied to the TrueNorth architecture. While this provides a concrete demonstration, discussing the extent to which the proposed methodology could be adapted to other neuromorphic platforms would broaden its impact.
* **Figure Ordering:** Figures 2 and 3 are referenced before they are introduced, which disrupts the flow of the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a significant problem in neuromorphic computing.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field by enabling efficient deep learning on specialized hardware.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The approximation used in the gradient calculation requires further justification.
* The evaluation is limited to a single dataset and a specific hardware platform.
* Some aspects of the methodology (e.g., network topology parameter selection) could benefit from a more rigorous analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. While some minor weaknesses exist, the novelty, technical soundness, and strong empirical results outweigh these concerns. The work is highly relevant to NIPS and has the potential to inspire further research in this important area.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section, particularly the justification of the approximation in Equation 4 and a discussion of the generalizability to other datasets and hardware platforms.
