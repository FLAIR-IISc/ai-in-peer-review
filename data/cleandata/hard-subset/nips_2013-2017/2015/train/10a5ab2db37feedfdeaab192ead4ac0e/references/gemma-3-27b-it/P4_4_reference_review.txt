This paper addresses the challenge of deploying deep learning models trained with backpropagation onto energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware, which typically utilizes spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity. The authors present a learning rule and network topology that reconciles this apparent incompatibility by training a network offline with continuous values constrained to [0, 1], representing probabilities of spiking or synaptic activation. The network is designed with progressively expanding connectivity and is demonstrated on the TrueNorth chip, achieving state-of-the-art power efficiency for digit recognition.

The work builds upon prior research in several areas. Backpropagation has been previously applied to spiking neural networks with high-precision weights [9, 10, 11, 12], and networks with low-precision synapses [13]. Probabilistic backpropagation techniques exist for networks with binary neurons and synapses [14, 15]. This paper differentiates itself by simultaneously addressing spiking neurons, low synaptic precision (less than trinary), and constrained connectivity, alongside demonstrating real-time operation and superior power efficiency on dedicated hardware. The comparison to existing work is well-placed within the introduction.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of probabilistic backpropagation, constrained connectivity, and low-precision synapses for neuromorphic deployment is a significant contribution.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate excellent accuracy on MNIST (99.42%) and establish a new state-of-the-art in power efficiency on TrueNorth (4 µJ per classification). The comparison to other low-power approaches [16] is compelling.
*   **Hardware Demonstration:** The successful implementation and evaluation on the TrueNorth chip strengthens the practical relevance of the work.
*   **Clear Explanation of Network Topology:** The progressive mixing approach for connectivity is well-described and justified.
*   **Detailed Training Methodology:** The paper provides a comprehensive explanation of the training procedure, including the probabilistic representation of neuron and synapse states, loss function, and gradient calculation. The approximations made to simplify gradient calculations are clearly stated and motivated.
*   **Network Initialization:** The approach to initialize synapse strengths to maximize information transfer and minimize redundancy is a thoughtful and valuable addition.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The method involves numerous details in training and mapping to hardware. While clearly explained, the overall complexity might hinder easy adoption by researchers unfamiliar with neuromorphic computing.
*   **Limited Dataset:** The results are primarily demonstrated on MNIST. Evaluating the method on more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) would significantly strengthen the findings.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices (e.g., learning rate, momentum, dropout). A more detailed analysis of these parameters could provide valuable insights.
*   **Approximations in Gradient Calculation:** While justified, the approximations made in the gradient calculation (dropping the right term in Equation 4) may introduce some error. A quantitative assessment of the impact of these approximations would be beneficial.
*   **Ensemble Size and Scalability:** The best performance is achieved with a large ensemble (64 members). The scalability of the approach with increasing ensemble size and the associated computational cost are not fully addressed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound solution to a critical problem in neuromorphic computing.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements in power efficiency.
*   The hardware demonstration adds practical value and validates the approach.
*   The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the methodology.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the method might limit its adoption.
*   The evaluation is limited to a relatively simple dataset (MNIST).
*   The potential impact of approximations in the gradient calculation is not fully quantified.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a significant challenge in the field of neuromorphic computing. The authors provide a novel and well-supported solution with compelling empirical results. While some aspects could be further investigated, the contributions are significant and warrant acceptance. The clarity of the writing and comprehensive detail of the methods are commendable.



**Recommendation:**

Accept.
