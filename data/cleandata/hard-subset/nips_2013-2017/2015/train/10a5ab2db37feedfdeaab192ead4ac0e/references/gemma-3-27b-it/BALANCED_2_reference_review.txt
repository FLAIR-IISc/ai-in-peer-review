This paper addresses the important problem of reconciling the efficiency of neuromorphic computing with the training power of backpropagation. The authors demonstrate a learning rule and network topology that allows for training with continuous values, which are then mapped to spiking neurons with low-precision synapses and constrained connectivity suitable for deployment on neuromorphic hardware, specifically the TrueNorth chip. The core idea – constraining the training to a probabilistic representation compatible with the hardware – is clever and effectively bridges the gap between algorithm and implementation.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a compelling argument for its approach. The authors relate their work to existing efforts in both spiking neural networks and neuromorphic hardware (e.g., [4, 6, 7, 8, 9, 11, 13, 14, 15]), and clearly delineate the novelty of their contribution: a complete methodology for backpropagation-based training of spiking networks with constrained connectivity and low synaptic precision, achieving state-of-the-art MNIST accuracy and significantly improved power efficiency on TrueNorth. The experimental results are convincing, showcasing a substantial improvement in power efficiency compared to existing low-power approaches [16]. The detailed explanation of the network initialization strategy, optimizing for efficient hardware implementation, is particularly valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of backpropagation, constrained training values (probabilities), low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity is novel and addresses a key challenge in deploying deep learning on neuromorphic hardware.
*   **Strong Results:** The MNIST accuracy (99.42%) is competitive, exceeding previous results on spiking networks. The power efficiency demonstrated on TrueNorth (4 µJ per classification) is a significant improvement over existing methods.
*   **Clarity and Thoroughness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology, including network training, mapping to the deployment network, and hardware implementation.
*   **Practicality:** The focus on TrueNorth as a deployment platform and the detailed description of the hardware considerations make the work highly relevant and potentially impactful.
*   **Justification:** The “constrain-then-train” philosophy is well-reasoned and presented with convincing arguments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The evaluation is currently limited to the MNIST dataset. While a strong result, it would be beneficial to demonstrate the approach on more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, or even a small subset of ImageNet) to assess its generalizability.
*   **Approximations:** Several approximations are made during the backpropagation process (e.g., Equation 4), and while justified, a more rigorous analysis of their impact on the training process and final accuracy would be beneficial. The sensitivity to the assumptions made in deriving those approximations is not explored.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper briefly mentions the hyperparameters used for training. More details about the hyperparameter tuning process and the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings would strengthen the work.
*   **Ensemble Size Impact:** The results show a trade-off between accuracy and energy consumption with increasing ensemble size. A more detailed analysis of the benefits of ensembling, and the diminishing returns as ensemble size grows, could be insightful.
*   **Generalizability to other neuromorphic architectures**: While the authors mention the approach could be generalized to other architectures, a discussion of potential challenges in doing so, or adaptations that would be required, would add value.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective solution to a significant challenge in neuromorphic computing.
*   The experimental results are strong and demonstrate a substantial improvement in power efficiency.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains a complex methodology.
*   The work is highly relevant to the NIPS community, bridging the gap between deep learning and specialized hardware.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (MNIST).
*   Some approximations are made during backpropagation, and their impact is not fully analyzed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. The proposed approach is novel, the results are compelling, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. While the limited scope of the evaluation and the approximations made during backpropagation are potential weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Consider expanding the evaluation to include more complex datasets.
2.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of the approximations made during backpropagation.
3.  Elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process and sensitivity analysis.
4.  Discuss the generalizability of the approach to other neuromorphic architectures.
