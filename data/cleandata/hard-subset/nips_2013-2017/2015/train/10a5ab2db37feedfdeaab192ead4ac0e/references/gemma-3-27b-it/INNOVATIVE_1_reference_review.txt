This paper presents a compelling and novel approach to bridging the gap between the efficiency of neuromorphic hardware and the training power of backpropagation. The core idea – constraining a backpropagation-trained network to parameters directly mappable to spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity – is genuinely insightful and addresses a significant bottleneck in deploying deep learning models on specialized hardware. The demonstration of 99.42% accuracy on MNIST, surpassing existing spiking network results, is impressive, and the real-time performance and power efficiency figures on TrueNorth are particularly striking. This isn't an incremental improvement; it’s a fundamentally different way to think about the training-deployment pipeline.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The “constrain-then-train” philosophy is well-articulated and justified. The network topology, using progressive mixing, appears well-suited for the hardware constraints. The detailed explanation of the training methodology, including the probabilistic approximations and the handling of low-precision synapses, is commendable. The network initialization strategy, aimed at maximizing information transfer and minimizing redundancy, shows a deep understanding of neuromorphic principles.  Furthermore, the experimental validation on TrueNorth lends substantial credibility to the claims. The inclusion of power efficiency numbers alongside accuracy is also a crucial strength, demonstrating practical impact.

However, the paper isn't without limitations. While the MNIST results are strong, the generalizability to more complex datasets isn’t fully explored. The discussion on adapting this approach to convolutional networks is brief and lacks specific details. The assumptions made during the gradient derivation (µ ≈ 0, small ñj, c̃ij, and σ) should be more rigorously justified. While the authors acknowledge these, a more quantitative analysis of their impact would be valuable. The choice of hyperparameters (learning rate decay schedule, dropout) feels somewhat arbitrary – a more systematic exploration of these parameters might reveal further performance gains.  The rationale for choosing specific values for R, S, and F in the network architecture could be better explained. A deeper analysis of the trade-offs between network size (number of cores) and performance (accuracy, energy) would also be beneficial.  The treatment of the covariance term approximation, while reasonable, feels a bit hand-wavy and could benefit from more rigorous mathematical justification or empirical verification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** This is a genuinely novel approach to a critical problem in neuromorphic computing.
*   **Significance:** The demonstrated power efficiency on TrueNorth is a major step towards practical, energy-efficient deep learning deployments.
*   **Strong Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art MNIST accuracy with spiking neurons is a substantial accomplishment.
*   **Well-Articulated Approach:** The “constrain-then-train” philosophy is clearly explained and logically sound.
*   **Practical Demonstration:** Validation on actual hardware (TrueNorth) strengthens the credibility of the work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is primarily limited to MNIST.
*   **Assumptions in Derivation:** The approximations made in the gradient derivation require further justification.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters lacks a systematic exploration.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor limitations, the originality and potential impact of this work are undeniable. The paper introduces a compelling and practical solution to a long-standing problem in neuromorphic computing. I believe the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and the novelty of the approach justifies overlooking some of the incomplete validations. The work is a clear advance in the field and has the potential to inspire further research. I **strongly recommend acceptance**.  This paper aligns perfectly with NIPS's focus on pushing the boundaries of machine learning and neural computation.  I would suggest the authors address the limitations in a future work, but it does not detract from the value of the current submission.
