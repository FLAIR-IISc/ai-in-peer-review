

# Review of "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing"

This paper proposes a method to train neural networks using backpropagation that can directly map to neuromorphic hardware with spiking neurons and low-precision synapses. The authors claim to resolve the incompatibility between backpropagation (which requires continuous-valued neurons and high-precision weights) and neuromorphic hardware (which uses spiking neurons and low-precision synapses). The core idea is to train a network with continuous values constrained to [0,1] representing probabilities, which can then be mapped to a hardware implementation with binary synapses.

The paper claims several novel contributions: (i) offline training with backpropagation for networks with spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity; (ii) state-of-the-art MNIST accuracy (99.42%) compared to other spiking neural network approaches; and (iii) impressive energy efficiency (4 µJ per classification at 95% accuracy) on the TrueNorth chip.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide detailed descriptions of their training methodology, network topology, and hardware mapping. The energy efficiency claims are compelling, and the TrueNorth implementation demonstrates a practical application of the method.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's primary weakness is insufficient validation of the core technical claims. The Gaussian approximation used in the training process (Equation 3) is questionable under the stated assumptions. The paper claims that "µ, ñi and c̃ij to be much less than σ" but provides no empirical evidence to support this assumption or to demonstrate how the approximation affects training convergence and final performance. The authors should provide a sensitivity analysis showing how deviations from these assumptions impact results.

The claimed state-of-the-art MNIST accuracy (99.42%) requires careful scrutiny. The comparison to "networks with spiking neurons, even with high precision synapses (99.12%)" [12] is problematic because the cited reference [12] (Diehl & Cook, 2015) reports 99.12% accuracy on MNIST using spiking networks with high-precision weights, but the authors don't provide a fair comparison. The paper doesn't report results for the same network architecture and training procedure used in [12], making it impossible to determine if the 0.3% improvement is due to the proposed method or other architectural differences.

The energy efficiency claims (4 µJ per classification) are impressive but lack sufficient context. The paper compares to "other low power approaches (6 mJ per classification at 95% accuracy running 50 images per second)" [16], but doesn't provide details about the specific hardware, network architecture, or implementation details for the comparison. Without this information, it's impossible to assess whether the claimed efficiency is genuinely superior or merely a result of different implementation choices.

The novelty claim is overstated. The paper cites previous work that has explored subsets of the elements proposed here: backpropagation with spiking neurons but high-precision weights [9-12], networks with trinary synapses but continuous output neurons [13], and probabilistic backpropagation for binary neurons/synapses [14-15]. The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how their approach fundamentally differs from these prior works or why their combination represents a significant advance.

**Quality Assessment:** The paper lacks sufficient experimental validation to support its main claims. The Gaussian approximation used in training is not rigorously justified, and the claimed improvements in accuracy and energy efficiency are not sufficiently validated against appropriate baselines. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods, including ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each proposed component.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clear. However, the technical details of the Gaussian approximation and its impact on training performance could be clarified with additional analysis.

**Originality:** The paper combines several existing ideas but doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant advance over prior work. The claimed novelty is overstated, and the paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how it differs from previous approaches.

**Significance:** The potential significance of bridging backpropagation and neuromorphic computing is high, but the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method achieves this goal. Without rigorous validation of the core technical claims, the significance of the work is limited.

**Recommendation:** While the problem addressed is important, the paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by evidence. I recommend rejection, as the authors have not provided adequate validation of their core technical claims. The paper would benefit from significantly more thorough experimental validation, including sensitivity analyses of the Gaussian approximation, fair comparisons with appropriate baselines, and detailed implementation details for the energy efficiency claims.

The paper makes an interesting attempt to bridge backpropagation with neuromorphic hardware, but it falls short of providing the rigorous validation required for a NIPS publication. The authors should substantially strengthen their experimental validation before resubmitting.
