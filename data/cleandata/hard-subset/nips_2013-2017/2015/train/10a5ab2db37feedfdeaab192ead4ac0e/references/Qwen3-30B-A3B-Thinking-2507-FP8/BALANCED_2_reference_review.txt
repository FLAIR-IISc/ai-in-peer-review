

# Review: Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing

This paper proposes a novel training methodology that bridges backpropagation with neuromorphic hardware constraints, specifically spiking neurons, low-precision synapses (less than 2 bits), and constrained connectivity. The authors introduce a "constrain-then-train" approach where they first define a network topology compatible with neuromorphic hardware, then train a probabilistic version of this network using backpropagation. The training network represents inputs, neuron outputs, and synaptic weights as probabilities constrained to [0,1], which directly maps to the hardware deployment. The authors demonstrate their approach on MNIST using the TrueNorth chip, achieving 99.42% accuracy (best reported for spiking networks) and 4 µJ per classification (best energy efficiency reported for digit recognition on neuromorphic hardware).

The paper relates well to previous work in neuromorphic computing (e.g., [4]-[7]), spiking neural networks (e.g., [9]-[12]), and binary neural networks (e.g., [13]-[15]). The key novelty is the integration of all these elements into a single framework with the probabilistic training approach, specifically addressing the incompatibility between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware.

**Strengths:**
1. The "constrain-then-train" approach is a significant conceptual advance over "train-then-constrain" methods, providing a direct correspondence between training error and deployment error.
2. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating the first reported spiking network achieving >99% accuracy on MNIST and the best energy efficiency to date for digit recognition on neuromorphic hardware.
3. The network initialization method (Section 3.4) is well-motivated and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the hardware implementation.
4. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good figures supporting the main claims.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The theoretical justification for the Gaussian approximation (Equation 3) and the gradient simplification (Equation 4) is insufficient. The paper states they assume "µ to be close to zero" but doesn't provide evidence or justification for this assumption in the context of their specific network architectures.
2. The paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate why their specific approach (probabilistic representation with Gaussian approximation) is better than alternatives. For example, how would the approach perform with a different approximation method?
3. The results are limited to MNIST, which is a relatively simple dataset. The paper should discuss how the approach might scale to more complex datasets like CIFAR-10 or ImageNet.
4. The paper doesn't thoroughly compare with other recent approaches to neuromorphic training. For instance, how does their method compare to the work in [22] on "Spiking deep convolutional neural networks" or [23] on "Scalable energy-efficient, low-latency implementations of spiking deep belief networks on SpiNNaker"?
5. The claim about "best accuracy to date" needs to be more rigorously verified against all relevant prior work on spiking networks. The paper cites [12] and [15] but doesn't explicitly compare with other recent spiking network approaches.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing by providing a practical framework for training networks that can be directly deployed on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware. The conceptual innovation of the "constrain-then-train" approach is valuable, and the experimental results on MNIST are impressive. However, the paper would benefit from stronger theoretical justification for key approximations, more thorough comparisons with recent work, and exploration of the method's applicability to more complex datasets.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS in terms of technical quality and significance, but the weaknesses noted above should be addressed before acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the theoretical foundations and broaden the experimental validation.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Provide stronger justification for the Gaussian approximation and gradient simplification, potentially by including empirical evidence that the assumptions hold in practice (e.g., showing that µ is indeed close to zero in their trained networks).
2. Add ablation studies comparing different approximation methods or training strategies to demonstrate the necessity of their specific approach.
3. Include results on at least one additional dataset (e.g., CIFAR-10) to better demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.
4. Expand the related work section to more thoroughly compare with recent approaches in neuromorphic training, particularly those published in the last 2 years.
5. Clarify the claim about "best accuracy to date" by explicitly comparing with all relevant prior work on spiking networks.
