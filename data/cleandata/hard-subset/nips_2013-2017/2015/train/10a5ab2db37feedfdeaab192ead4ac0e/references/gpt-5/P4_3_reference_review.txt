Summary
The paper proposes a “constrain-then-train” method to bridge backpropagation with energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware that uses spiking neurons, binary synapses, and constrained connectivity (TrueNorth). The training network operates in a continuous, differentiable probabilistic domain by representing inputs, neuron outputs, and synaptic connections as values in [0,1], interpreted as probabilities of spikes or synapse-on states. The forward pass uses a Gaussian approximation for weighted sums of Bernoulli inputs to compute firing probabilities; backpropagation is adapted by differentiating through the Gaussian CDF and updating synaptic connection probabilities c̃ij and biases, while synaptic strengths sij are fixed a priori to a balanced ±1 pattern designed to reduce pairwise redundancy on each core. After training, deployment samples synapse binaries and spikes from the learned probabilities; ensembles of sampled networks can be averaged to improve performance.

A progressive sliding-window topology respects TrueNorth’s 256-input/core constraint by selecting a limited R×R×F input window per core, with feature subsampling and non-shared weights across spatial locations. On MNIST, a 30-core, 4-layer network with a 64-member ensemble achieves 99.42% accuracy; a 5-core network attains 92.70% at 0.268 µJ/classification. The paper claims state-of-the-art MNIST accuracy among spiking or binary-synapse networks and demonstrates real-time deployment on TrueNorth with favorable energy/throughput trade-offs.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself relative to: (i) spiking networks trained with backprop but using high-precision weights; (ii) networks with discrete weights but continuous activations; and (iii) probabilistic backpropagation for binary neurons/weights with full connectivity. The novelty asserted is the combination of spiking neurons, sub-trinary synaptic precision (binary on/off with shared-strength blocks), and constrained connectivity, trained offline by backprop in a way that maps directly to hardware. The work is related to weight-quantized/binary networks and to DNN-to-SNN conversion methods; some more recent or concurrent discrete/binary training works could be more thoroughly discussed. The hardware demonstration on TrueNorth is a notable distinguishing aspect.

Quality (technical soundness)
Strengths:
- The probabilistic surrogate model (Gaussian approximation of weighted Bernoulli sums) provides a differentiable training objective aligned with the sampling-based deployment, avoiding post-hoc discretization.
- The careful initialization of ±1 synaptic strengths to minimize pairwise correlations on-core is principled and matches hardware constraints; the derivation linking inner-product minimization to uncorrelated summed inputs is reasonable under stated assumptions.
- The topology design is consistent with TrueNorth’s restricted fan-in and demonstrates a viable path to scale receptive fields via progressive mixing.

Concerns:
- The Gaussian approximation and independence assumptions (for inputs and synapse activations) are strong and not empirically validated (e.g., by comparing exact vs. approximate firing probabilities, or by measuring induced correlations after training). The network uses dropout and random connectivity, but it would help to quantify approximation error.
- The gradient simplification for ∂ñj/∂c̃ij drops the σ−3 term under assumptions (µ≈0, small c̃ij, etc.). While plausible, no ablation shows whether keeping or dropping this term affects training stability or accuracy.
- The statement “corresponding gradient with respect to the bias term equals one” appears imprecise; mathematically ∂µj/∂bj=1, but ∂E/∂bj still depends on ∂E/∂ñj and ∂ñj/∂µj.
- The energy numbers are inconsistent across sections: the Introduction cites 4 µJ/classification at 95% accuracy, while Results report 0.268 µJ at 92.7% and 108 µJ at 99.42%. Please reconcile measurement methodology, settings (ensemble size, throughput), and what is included (active vs. leakage) to enable fair comparison to prior work.
- Only MNIST is evaluated. Given the claims of generality and hardware relevance, at least one additional dataset (e.g., Fashion-MNIST, simple vision tasks) or a convolutional variant would strengthen the case.
- Lack of comparisons to strong post-2014 low-precision/binary training baselines beyond those cited (even if not spiking). A head-to-head accuracy/energy/latency comparison to SNN conversion methods would be informative.

Clarity
- Overall well organized, but several equations contain typos/ambiguities (Eq. 3 formatting; Eq. 4 readability; switching between S and W without defining W). The role of the 4 axon types and the 9-bit strength LUT vs. the ±1 strength pattern should be clarified.
- More detail on mapping class outputs (number of neurons per class, averaging protocol) and on feature subsampling constraints would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The combination of probabilistic backprop for synapse-on probabilities, binary synapses, spiking neurons, and constrained hardware connectivity, along with a principled strength initialization and a demonstration on TrueNorth, appears novel.
- The conceptual “constrain-then-train” stance is a useful contrast to train-then-quantize/SNN-conversion approaches.

Significance
- Practical significance is high for neuromorphic deployment: the method provides a direct training path matched to hardware constraints and demonstrates real-time, low-energy inference.
- Scientific significance would be stronger with broader datasets, ablations, and clearer comparisons to alternative low-precision/spiking approaches. The reliance on large ensembles to reach top accuracy may limit immediate applicability in resource-constrained deployments.

Pros
- Clear hardware-aligned training approach avoiding ad hoc discretization.
- Demonstrated deployment on silicon with measured energy/throughput.
- Principled synaptic strength initialization for low-precision, constrained-connectivity cores.
- Strong MNIST accuracy for spiking/binary-synapse settings.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on approximations (Gaussian, independence, gradient simplification) without quantitative validation.
- Limited evaluation scope (MNIST only) and lack of comprehensive comparisons/ablations.
- Inconsistencies in energy metrics; unclear fairness of comparisons across systems.
- Some notation and equation clarity issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Validate the Gaussian/independence approximations empirically and report their impact on accuracy.
- Provide ablations: with/without σ−3 term; ensemble size vs. accuracy/energy; effect of strength initialization vs. alternative schemes.
- Reconcile and standardize energy reporting; include leakage, active, clock rates, and per-core utilization; compare to prior work under matched operating points if possible.
- Add at least one additional dataset or a convolutional variant to demonstrate generality.
- Clarify the interaction between the TrueNorth LUT/axon types and the fixed ±1 strengths, and standardize notation.

Recommendation
On balance, the paper presents a technically interesting, hardware-relevant training methodology and a compelling on-chip demonstration. The main weaknesses are limited evaluation, unvalidated approximations, and metric inconsistencies. If addressed, this work would be a strong contribution to neuromorphic learning. As is, it is borderline but promising for NeurIPS.
