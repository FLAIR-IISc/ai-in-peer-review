- Proposes a "constrain-then-train" probabilistic surrogate to train networks with spiking neurons, 1-bit synapses, per-neuron LUTs, and limited fan-in for IBM TrueNorth.
- Uses continuous [0,1] variables for spike/synapse probabilities and a Gaussian CLT surrogate to enable backpropagation through sums of Bernoulli inputs.
- Learns synaptic connection probabilities and biases while fixing synaptic strengths to a balanced ±1 initialization chosen to minimize inter-neuron correlations.
- Deploys by sampling binary synapses/inputs from learned probabilities and using ensembles of sampled networks to boost accuracy.
- Employs progressive mixing receptive windows (like non-shared convolutions) to respect TrueNorth core-to-core constraints.
- Reports MNIST results: 99.42% with 30 cores and 64-network ensemble (108 µJ/classification) and 92.7% with 5 cores (0.268 µJ/classification), claiming SOTA for spiking accuracy and power efficiency on digits.
- Gaussian surrogate and gradient derivations are reasonable under CLT/independence but assumptions may break in deeper correlated layers and lack empirical validation.
- Dropping the σ^−3 term in gradients is heuristically justified but not empirically validated.
- Fixing strengths to ±1 matches hardware but may be suboptimal; no ablation compares learning strengths via axon-type LUTs.
- Mapping via independent sampling introduces variance; ensembles mitigate this but at significant core/energy cost.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to MNIST with no confidence intervals, random seeds, or comprehensive ablations.
- Energy measurement methodology is high-level; important details (leakage scaling, run variance) and a claimed 4 µJ/95% figure are unclear or unsupported by presented data.
- Comparisons to prior work may be unfair due to ensemble use, data augmentation, and a highly parameterized, non-shared-topology; matched baselines are missing.
- Paper is generally well written but contains equation typos/notation ambiguities and lacks detailed topology/parameter counts and reproducibility tables.
- Novelty lies in jointly handling spiking neurons, binary connectivity, and constrained connectivity with backprop and a hardware-aware redundancy-minimizing initialization.
- Could cite/compare more explicitly to discrete-variable gradient estimators (e.g., straight-through, REINFORCE, local reparameterization) and L0/structured sparsity work.
- Potentially significant for energy-efficient neuromorphic deployment if generalized to convolutional architectures and larger datasets, but current scope is narrow.
- Strengths: clear hardware-aligned training–deployment bridge, elegant probabilistic surrogate, hardware-aware initialization, real hardware energy measurements, competitive MNIST accuracy.
- Weaknesses/questions: reliance on ensembling, narrow empirical scope, missing ablations, unvalidated independence/CLT assumptions, unclear energy reporting, and reproducibility gaps.
- Suggested experiments: ensemble-size vs accuracy/energy ablation, single-sample vs majority vote, threshold vs sampling, full vs approximated gradient, learned strengths vs fixed, validate Gaussian/independence assumptions, and compare to matched CPU/GPU baselines and convolutional spiking conversions.
- Minor fixes: correct Eq.3 CCDF/erf formatting, clarify whether s_ij use four axon-type values or ±1, and fully specify LUT/initialization and parameter counts.
- Pros for acceptance: timely, practical, novel hardware-aligned training framework with demonstrated energy benefits and clear broader impact potential.
- Cons for acceptance: narrow evaluation, heavy ensembling, unvalidated approximations, missing ablations, and clarity/reproducibility issues.
- Recommendation: Weak accept conditional on clarifying measurements, adding ensemble/gradient ablations, and fixing presentation/reproducibility issues.
