Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a “constrain-then-train” method to train spiking networks with binary on/off synapses and severe connectivity limits so that they map directly to neuromorphic hardware (TrueNorth). The key idea is to represent inputs, neuron outputs, and synaptic connections as probabilities in [0,1], and to train with backpropagation in this differentiable probabilistic domain. Synaptic weights are factored into a binary connection variable and a low-precision strength selected from a small, balanced set; only the connection probabilities and neuron biases are learned. The forward model uses a Gaussian approximation to the sum of weighted Bernoulli variables to compute spike probabilities; the backward pass differentiates the resulting erf-based expression, with further simplifications. The topology uses a progressive mixing/sliding-window scheme compatible with TrueNorth core-to-core constraints. A hardware-friendly initialization minimizes pairwise redundancy by balancing weights and approximately decorrelating neurons. On MNIST, the authors report up to 99.42% with a 30-core network using an ensemble of 64 samples, and strong energy efficiency on TrueNorth (down to 0.268 µJ/classification at 92.7% with a 5-core network), claiming state-of-the-art accuracy among spiking/binary constrained systems and large energy gains over prior low-power implementations.

Relative to prior work: prior spiking networks often retained high-precision weights [e.g., 9–12], or used binary/trinary weights with full inter-layer connectivity [14,15]. This paper appears to be the first to combine spiking neurons, sub-trinary synapse precision (binary connectivity plus small lookup-based strengths), and strict connectivity constraints, trained end-to-end with backprop and deployed on silicon. It also contributes a specific topology and initialization aligned with TrueNorth constraints, and presents system-level energy/throughput measurements.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The probabilistic forward model (Gaussian approximation) and its derivatives are plausible under the stated assumptions (independence, large fan-in, balanced positive/negative weights). The simplification of the gradient by dropping the σ^−3 term is reasonable when σ is sufficiently large; however, empirical validation of the approximation quality would strengthen the work (e.g., correlation between analytic firing probabilities/gradients vs Monte Carlo estimates across layers).
- Experimental support: Results on MNIST are strong within the neuromorphic regime and include real hardware measurements. The paper reports accuracy/energy trade-offs and demonstrates real-time throughput. However, evaluation is limited to MNIST; no ablations quantify the contributions of progressive mixing, the weight initialization, ensemble size, or the approximation choices. Comparisons to prior spiking/binary work are cited but not systematically reproduced under matched conditions.
- Completeness and honesty: The authors clearly describe constraints, training heuristics, and deployment choices. A notable gap is the seeming inconsistency between the 4 µJ/classification at 95% accuracy claim (abstract) and the reported 0.268 µJ at 92.7% and 108 µJ at 99.42% in the main results—this should be reconciled and measurement methodology clarified.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. The probabilistic model, training updates, and topology are described clearly, with reasonable notation. The intuition behind initialization is helpful.
- However, the text references figures/tables (Fig. 1–3, Table 1) that are critical for understanding the topology and initialization but are not included here; ensure these provide full architectural specifics (R/S/F per layer, fan-in per neuron, number of neurons per core).
- Reproducibility would benefit from a more explicit algorithmic summary (forward/backward pseudo-code), details of the loss computation (pk definition and implementation), exact s-value lookup tables and axon-type assignments, and a precise account of which parameters are learned vs fixed.

Originality:
- High. The integration of backprop in a probabilistic domain with spiking neurons, binary synapses, and strict hardware connectivity, plus a tailored topology and weight initialization for TrueNorth, is novel. The ensemble sampling of learned connection probabilities for deployment is an interesting twist that leverages the probabilistic representation.

Significance:
- For neuromorphic computing and energy-efficient ML, the contribution is significant: it offers a practical pathway to bring backprop-learned models onto highly constrained spiking hardware with demonstrated energy/throughput gains. The real-silicon results are valuable.
- Broader ML impact is somewhat limited by the use of MNIST only and by reliance on ensembles to reach top accuracy (with substantial core usage). Showing applicability to more challenging datasets (e.g., SVHN, CIFAR-10) or to convolutional structures, as suggested in the discussion, would increase impact.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear, principled training framework that directly respects hardware constraints.
- Novel combination of spiking neurons, ultra-low precision synapses, and constrained connectivity trained by backprop.
- Practical topology and initialization aligned with hardware; plausible theoretical justification.
- Strong results on hardware, with compelling energy efficiency and throughput.
- Advances state of the art within the spiking/neuromorphic regime and bridges a gap between deep learning and neuromorphics.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Evaluation limited to MNIST; no harder datasets or convolutional variants.
- Heavy reliance on ensembles to match top accuracy; single-model performance not fully explored.
- Lack of ablation studies to isolate contributions (initialization, progressive mixing, Gaussian gradient simplification).
- Approximation assumptions not empirically validated.
- Potential inconsistency in reported energy numbers; measurement methodology needs clarification.
- Reproducibility could be improved with more implementation detail.

Questions and suggestions for authors:
- Please reconcile the “4 µJ at 95% accuracy” claim with the detailed results and specify measurement setup (active vs leakage power, time window, per-core accounting, parallelization across ensemble members).
- Provide ablations: effect of ensemble size on accuracy/energy; effect of the balanced s-values initialization; comparison to learning s-values; impact of the σ^−3 term omission.
- Validate the Gaussian approximation: compare analytic vs sampled firing probabilities/gradients at different layers/fan-in.
- Report single-member accuracy for the 30-core network and the shape of accuracy vs ensemble size curves.
- Clarify mapping to convolutional settings and whether weight sharing can be accommodated within the proposed framework.
- Add a concise algorithm box and release training code, if possible.

Recommendation:
Weak accept. The paper presents a timely and substantive contribution at the intersection of deep learning and neuromorphic hardware, with a sound method, hardware deployment, and strong energy results. Broadening the empirical evaluation and clarifying measurement details would make this an even stronger submission.
