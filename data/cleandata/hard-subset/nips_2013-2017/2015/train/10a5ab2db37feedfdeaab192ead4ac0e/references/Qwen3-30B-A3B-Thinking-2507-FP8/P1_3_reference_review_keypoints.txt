- Introduces a "constrain-then-train" methodology that designs hardware-constrained networks (spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, limited connectivity) and trains them probabilistically with backpropagation.  
- Represents network components as probabilities in [0,1], uses Gaussian approximations for neuron firing probabilities, and maps trained probabilistic networks to hardware via sampling.  
- Demonstrated on MNIST using the TrueNorth chip, achieving 99.42% accuracy and 4 µJ per classification (state-of-the-art for spiking networks and best reported power efficiency).  
- Combines elements previously studied separately (spiking backprop, low-precision synapses, probabilistic training with binary units) into a single coherent framework.  
- Gaussian approximation and gradient derivation are well-motivated and technically sound, with clear equations and justifications.  
- Lacks rigorous theoretical guarantees or error analysis for the Gaussian approximation.  
- Experimental results are comprehensive for MNIST and include relevant baselines; energy-efficiency measurements are convincing.  
- Experimental scope is limited to MNIST; evaluation on harder benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10) is needed to demonstrate generalizability.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; figures effectively illustrate topology and results.  
- Assumes familiarity with TrueNorth; a brief explanation of hardware specifics (e.g., 9-bit synaptic strength LUT) would improve accessibility.  
- Novelty: the constrain-then-train approach fundamentally differs from train-then-constrain and yields better preservation of training performance when mapping to hardware.  
- Significance: addresses practical energy-efficiency challenges, demonstrates real-time operation (1000 images/s), and is adaptable to other architectures with sparsity constraints.  
- Strengths: first successful combination of targeted elements, clear methodology, state-of-the-art MNIST accuracy for spiking networks, best reported power efficiency, and well-explained mapping to deployment.  
- Weaknesses: limited dataset scope, lack of theoretical guarantees for approximation, some missing hardware-specific details, and no exploration of ensemble size vs. accuracy/energy trade-offs.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept for NIPS — technically solid, significant contribution with compelling results despite the noted limitations.
