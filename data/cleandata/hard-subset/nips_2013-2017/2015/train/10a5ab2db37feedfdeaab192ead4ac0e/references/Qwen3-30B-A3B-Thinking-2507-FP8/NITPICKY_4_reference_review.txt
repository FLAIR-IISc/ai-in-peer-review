

# Review of "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing"

This paper proposes a "constrain-then-train" approach to bridge backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware, claiming to demonstrate the first offline training methodology using backpropagation for networks with spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity. The authors report 99.42% accuracy on MNIST (best to date for spiking networks) and 4 µJ per classification energy efficiency on TrueNorth (claimed as best published).

## Technical Issues

The paper contains several significant technical inconsistencies that undermine its claims. The most glaring is the discrepancy between the abstract's claim of "4 µJ per classification at 95% accuracy" and the results section's actual measurement of "108 µJ per classification" for 99.42% accuracy. This is a factor of 27 difference that cannot be reconciled. The authors should either correct the abstract or provide a clear explanation for the discrepancy, as this undermines the paper's energy efficiency claims.

Mathematically, Equation 3 contains a critical error: "1 - 1 + erf θ - µj√ σ2j" is not a valid expression. The correct form should involve the standard normal cumulative distribution function, not an erroneous combination of error functions. Similarly, the derivation of Equation 4 appears to contain formatting errors and mathematical inconsistencies. The approximation "dropping the right term" in Equation 4 is unjustified - the paper claims the right term is "much smaller" but provides no theoretical or empirical justification for this assumption.

The paper's description of the learning rule is confusing. It claims to use "continuous-valued input, neuron output, and synaptic weights" constrained to [0,1] but then refers to "binary synapses" throughout. The actual mechanism uses probabilities (c̃ij) for synapse states, which should be clearly distinguished from binary representations. The paper states "synaptic weights are represented as probabilities" but later refers to "binary synapses" - this terminology inconsistency creates confusion about the actual implementation.

## Experimental and Presentation Issues

The experimental results section lacks crucial details. The paper reports a "30 core trained network using a 64 member ensemble" achieving 99.42% accuracy, but provides no energy measurements for the ensemble sizes below 64. The claim that "the lowest energy was achieved by the 5 core network operating in an ensemble of 1" is unsupported by energy measurements for this configuration. The paper also fails to explain how the 1000 images per second throughput was achieved given the TrueNorth chip's 4096 cores, with only 5 cores used for the smallest network.

The paper's network initialization section claims to "minimize redundancy between neurons on a core" by ensuring "the absolute value of the inner product between columns of W is minimized," but the derivation in Equations 5-6 is flawed. The assumption that "E[xixq] = γ" for all i≠q is not justified for image data, and the paper provides no evidence that this initialization actually improves performance.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:** The paper identifies a genuine challenge in neuromorphic computing (the incompatibility between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware) and proposes a potentially useful approach. The "constrain-then-train" paradigm is conceptually sound, and the MNIST results do represent a modest improvement over some prior work.

**Weaknesses:** The technical errors (particularly the mathematical ones) are substantial and undermine the credibility of the approach. The energy efficiency claims are inconsistent and poorly supported. The paper fails to properly distinguish between probability representations and binary implementations, creating confusion about the actual method. The experimental section lacks necessary details to evaluate the claims.

## Significance and Originality

While the paper's overall concept has merit, the execution contains too many technical flaws to be considered a solid contribution. The claimed novelty (combining spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity) is partially supported, but the paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from prior work like [15] (Training binary multilayer neural networks for image classification using expectation backpropagation), which appears to be doing similar work.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper contains multiple fundamental technical errors, significant inconsistencies in its claims, and insufficient experimental details to verify its results. While the problem being addressed is important, the paper's execution fails to deliver a credible solution. The mathematical errors in key equations (3 and 4) are particularly troubling as they form the basis of the proposed training method. The energy efficiency claims are undermined by the 4 µJ vs. 108 µJ discrepancy, which suggests either a major error in the paper or a misleading claim.

The paper would require substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication. As it stands, the technical flaws outweigh the potential significance of the approach.
