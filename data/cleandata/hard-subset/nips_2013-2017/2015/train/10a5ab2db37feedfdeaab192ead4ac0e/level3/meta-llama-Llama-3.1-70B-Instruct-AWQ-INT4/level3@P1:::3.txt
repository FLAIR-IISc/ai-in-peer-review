This paper presents a novel backpropagation training method that accounts for the hardware constraints of a spiking-neuron, discrete-synapse platform. The proposed approach is timely and interesting, as it addresses the need for efficient training algorithms that can be implemented on emerging neuromorphic hardware.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on the TrueNorth (TN) chip, a dedicated neuromorphic design that uses spikes to provide event-based computation and communication. The TN chip is an efficient substrate for neural network operation, with low power consumption and high speed. However, its design choices introduce an apparent incompatibility with the backpropagation algorithm, which uses continuous-output neurons and high-precision synapses.

The authors' main contribution is a learning rule and network topology that reconciles this incompatibility. They propose a probabilistic representation of the deployment network, where synaptic connections are represented as probabilities, and neuron outputs are represented as probabilities of spiking. This allows the use of standard backpropagation for training, while providing a direct representation of the deployment system.

The paper is well-written and easy to read, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its advantages. However, there are some minor issues with the presentation. For example, the claims in Section 1 are worded in a way that may wrongly imply novelty of using spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses. The authors should reword these claims to avoid giving the impression that they are introducing new concepts.

Additionally, the demonstration on the TN hardware should be presented as validation of the method, rather than as a separate novelty claim. The authors should clarify what "0.15 bits per synapse" means in Section 2, as this is not immediately clear.

The network topology is not clearly described in a single, consolidated place, which makes it difficult to understand the authors' design choices. The authors should provide a clear and concise description of the network topology, including the number of layers, the number of neurons in each layer, and the connectivity between layers.

The authors should also report how much longer training takes due to probabilistic synaptic connection updates. This is an important consideration, as it affects the overall efficiency of the method.

Furthermore, the authors should explain why the single ensemble 30-core network performs much worse than the 5-core network. This is a surprising result, and the authors should provide some insight into why this might be the case.

The authors should also explain why different ensembles of the 5-core network yield similar results. This is an interesting finding, and the authors should provide some discussion of the implications of this result.

Additionally, the authors should provide input spike rates during testing and clarify whether inputs are spike trains. This is an important consideration, as it affects the overall performance of the method.

The authors should also note that Ref. 12 enforces constraints (e.g., bias=0) during training, so it is not purely a train-then-constrain approach. The authors should fix the "approach approach" typo in the text.

Finally, the authors should add a discussion/comparison with other spiking backprop rules, such as SpikeProp. This would provide a more complete understanding of the proposed method and its advantages.

In terms of significance, the paper makes an important contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. Hardware-aware training is crucial for efficient implementation of neural networks on emerging hardware platforms. However, the TN-specific constraints may not generalize to other platforms, and using a 64-ensemble increases hardware resource needs for target accuracy. The authors should discuss these limitations and provide some insight into how their method might be adapted to other platforms.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and timely contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing.
* The proposed method is well-suited to emerging hardware platforms, and provides a promising approach for efficient training of neural networks.
* The authors provide a clear and concise description of their method, and demonstrate its effectiveness on a challenging task.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper has some minor issues with presentation, including unclear claims and lack of clarity on network topology.
* The authors do not provide a complete comparison with other spiking backprop rules, which would be useful for understanding the advantages of their method.
* The TN-specific constraints may not generalize to other platforms, which limits the broader applicability of the method.

Overall, I believe that the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing, and provides a promising approach for efficient training of neural networks on emerging hardware platforms. With some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above, I believe that the paper would be suitable for publication in NIPS.
