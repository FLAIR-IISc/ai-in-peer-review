## Review of “Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing”

This paper presents a method for training spiking neural networks using backpropagation, specifically tailored for deployment on neuromorphic hardware like the TrueNorth chip. The core idea – treating spikes and discrete synapses as probabilities to enable standard backpropagation – is timely and addresses a critical challenge in bridging the gap between the algorithmic success of deep learning and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing. The work is well-written and easy to follow, clearly articulating the motivation and approach.

The strength of this paper lies in its practical focus. The authors don’t just propose a theoretical method; they demonstrate it on actual hardware, achieving impressive results on the MNIST dataset. This demonstration provides valuable validation of the proposed training scheme for constrained, spiking neuron platforms. The paper effectively highlights the trade-offs between accuracy and energy efficiency by showcasing results with different ensemble sizes. 

However, several points require clarification and refinement. The introductory claims regarding novelty should be toned down. While the combination of techniques is valuable, the paper risks overstating its originality by framing the use of spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses as a new concept. The demonstration on TrueNorth should be presented as a validation of the *method*, rather than a separate novelty in itself. 

Several technical details need elaboration. The meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" in Section 2 is unclear and requires further explanation. A consolidated description of the network topology is missing; currently, it’s scattered throughout the text. The paper should also report the computational overhead introduced by the probabilistic synaptic updates during training – how much longer does training take compared to standard backpropagation? 

Regarding the results, the significant performance difference between the 30-core and 5-core networks warrants a detailed explanation. Similarly, the consistency of results across different ensembles of the 5-core network should be justified. Providing input spike rates during testing and clarifying whether the inputs are spike trains would also strengthen the evaluation. 

Finally, the paper would benefit from a discussion of related spiking backpropagation rules, such as SpikeProp, and a more nuanced comparison. While hardware-aware training is crucial, the TN-specific constraints may limit the generalizability of the approach, and the reliance on a 64-ensemble to achieve high accuracy raises concerns about hardware resource requirements.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant challenge in neuromorphic computing.
*   Provides a practical and well-validated training method.
*   Demonstrates strong results on a real neuromorphic platform.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Overstated novelty claims in the introduction.
*   Lack of clarity in certain technical details.
*   Unexplained performance differences in results.
*   Limited discussion of related work.



Overall, this is a promising contribution with the potential to advance the field of neuromorphic computing. Addressing the points raised above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
