This paper addresses the critical challenge of bridging the gap between the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing and the performance of backpropagation-trained deep neural networks. The authors propose a method to train networks with spiking neurons and low-precision synapses using a modified backpropagation algorithm, where spikes and synapses are treated as probabilities. This allows for a direct mapping of the trained network onto neuromorphic hardware like the TrueNorth chip. The approach is demonstrated on the MNIST dataset, achieving competitive accuracy with significantly reduced energy consumption compared to existing methods.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of recent advances in both deep learning and neuromorphic computing, appropriately citing relevant work in each domain (e.g., [1, 7, 8]). The introduction clearly articulates the problem and motivates the proposed solution. The description of the training methodology (Section 3) is detailed and well-structured, and the network topology design (Section 4) is justified in terms of hardware constraints and efficient information flow. The experimental results (Section 5) are particularly compelling, demonstrating a clear trade-off between accuracy and energy efficiency, and achieving state-of-the-art power performance on the TrueNorth chip.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Experimental Results:** The reported accuracy and energy efficiency numbers on the TrueNorth chip are very impressive and represent a significant contribution. Achieving 99.42% accuracy with 108 μJ per image and 92.7% accuracy with 0.268 μJ per image showcases the potential of this approach.
* **Practical Focus:** The work is grounded in the practical constraints of neuromorphic hardware, specifically the TrueNorth chip. The design choices reflect a clear understanding of the hardware’s limitations and capabilities.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning. The inclusion of figures and tables helps to illustrate the key concepts and results.
* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Reconciling backpropagation with neuromorphic hardware is a crucial step towards deploying deep learning models in energy-constrained environments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Overstated Novelty:** The algorithmic contribution of this work is somewhat overstated. Section 3.2’s algorithm is deeply similar to, and effectively a hybrid of, approaches presented in references [14] and [15], utilizing identical approximations. This connection should be explicitly acknowledged. Furthermore, the discussion’s opening paragraph suggests the “constrain-then-train” methodology is novel, despite being previously explored in [15]. 
* **Misleading Novelty Claim (i):** The first novelty claim in the introduction (i) is misleading. Reference [15]'s "expectation backpropagation" is, in fact, a backpropagation update using expectation propagation for binary neurons and weights, and *can* accommodate constrained connectivity.
* **Incorrect Prior Art:** The authors cite incorrect prior state-of-the-art performance. Reference [15] achieved the best known results for binary weights/neurons on MNIST *without* data augmentation, a detail not highlighted in the paper.
* **Limited Generalizability:** While the results on MNIST are promising, it remains unclear how well this approach will generalize to more complex datasets and network architectures. The chosen network topology and parameters appear tailored to MNIST.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. The experimental results are strong and demonstrate the feasibility of training high-performance networks for deployment on energy-efficient hardware. However, the authors overstate the novelty of their algorithmic contributions and misrepresent existing prior art. While the presented method builds on existing techniques, its effective combination and application to the TrueNorth chip are noteworthy.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong experimental results demonstrating significant energy efficiency gains.
* Practical relevance to the deployment of deep learning models on neuromorphic hardware.
* Clear and well-organized presentation of the method.
* Addresses a challenging and important problem.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Overstated novelty and lack of sufficient acknowledgement of related work ([14, 15]).
* Misleading claim about prior state-of-the-art performance.
* Limited discussion of the generalizability of the approach to more complex problems.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the concerns regarding novelty by explicitly acknowledging the relationship to references [14] and [15] and revising the introductory claims accordingly. They should also correct the statement regarding prior state-of-the-art performance.  Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and clarify its contribution to the field. The strong experimental results warrant acceptance despite these shortcomings.
