- Proposes a novel training method that treats spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities to enable backpropagation for neuromorphic hardware.  
- Reconciles backpropagation with spiking neurons and discrete synapses, addressing energy-efficient deployment on embedded systems.  
- Paper is well-written and explanations of the probabilistic treatment are clear and easy to follow.  
- Demonstrated on IBM TrueNorth, achieving strong results (99.42% on MNIST at 108 μJ/image) showing both performance and energy efficiency.  
- Section 1 may overstate novelty by implying that using spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses is itself new.  
- The TrueNorth demonstration should be presented as validation of the method, not an independent novelty claim.  
- Network topology is not described in a single consolidated place; readers need clearer architecture description.  
- Experimental parameters are insufficiently detailed and should be reported more completely.  
- Ambiguous terminology (e.g., “0.15 bits per synapse”) needs clarification.  
- The approach requires a customized training rule, which may limit compatibility with other architectures.  
- Paper provides useful comparisons to prior work and demonstrates advantages over existing methods.  
- The probabilistic treatment of spikes/synapses is an original contribution with potential significance for low-power, scalable neuromorphic systems.  
- Recommendation: accept pending revisions to tone novelty claims, reframe hardware demo as validation, and add clearer topology/parameter details.
