

This paper proposes a method to bridge the gap between backpropagation-based deep learning and neuromorphic hardware by treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities. The authors present a "constrain-then-train" approach where they first constrain the network to match neuromorphic hardware specifications (spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, limited connectivity) and then train using standard backpropagation on the probabilistic representation. The key innovation is mapping continuous probabilities to discrete hardware components through sampling, allowing the trained network to run efficiently on TrueNorth chips with impressive energy efficiency metrics.

The paper makes a strong contribution in demonstrating practical implementation of this approach, achieving 99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ per image on MNIST using a 64-member ensemble, and 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image with a single network. These results represent significant improvements over previous neuromorphic implementations, particularly the 0.268 μJ per image for the single-network configuration, which is more than 100x more energy efficient than previous low-power approaches.

However, the paper's claims of novelty require significant clarification. The core algorithm described in Section 3.2 is deeply similar to and effectively a hybrid of the expectation backpropagation method in [15] (Cheng et al., 2015), which already addressed binary neurons and weights with constrained connectivity using identical probabilistic approximations. The "constrain-then-train" methodology is not novel as claimed in the discussion section, as it was explicitly suggested and tested in [15]. The paper's novelty claim (i) in the introduction is therefore misleading, as [15] already demonstrated a backpropagation method for networks with spiking neurons, binary weights, and constrained connectivity.

The paper also cites incorrect prior state-of-the-art: [15] achieved 97.88% accuracy on MNIST with binary weights/neurons without data augmentation, which is higher than the 92.7% accuracy reported for their single-network configuration. The paper's claim that they "achieve the best accuracy to date on MNIST (99.42%) when compared to networks that use spiking neurons" is misleading, as [12] achieved 99.12% with high-precision weights (not binary), and [15] achieved 97.88% with binary weights. The authors' claim of 99.42% is accurate for their ensemble approach, but this should be clearly distinguished from prior binary-weight work.

Quality: The experimental results are strong and well-documented, with careful energy measurements on TrueNorth hardware. The network initialization method (Section 3.4) is well-motivated by information theory. However, the algorithmic contribution is overstated, and the paper fails to properly acknowledge prior work that directly addresses the core methodology.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The connection between the probabilistic training network and the discrete deployment network is well-explained. The experimental setup and results are presented with appropriate detail.

Originality: The paper's main contribution is the practical implementation and demonstration of the approach on TrueNorth hardware, not the core algorithmic method. The algorithm itself is not novel, as it builds directly on [15]. The paper would be significantly strengthened by properly contextualizing this work within the existing literature on probabilistic backpropagation for low-precision networks.

Significance: The demonstrated energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image) represents a major practical advance for embedded neural network applications. The approach provides a viable path for deploying deep learning models on energy-constrained neuromorphic hardware, which has significant potential for real-world applications in mobile and IoT devices. The experimental results are compelling and represent a meaningful contribution to the field of energy-efficient AI.

Pro: Strong experimental results demonstrating unprecedented energy efficiency on TrueNorth hardware; clear presentation of the probabilistic mapping approach; practical implementation of a viable solution for neuromorphic deployment.

Con: Overstated novelty claims; failure to properly acknowledge prior work that directly addresses the core methodology; misleading state-of-the-art comparison; the algorithmic contribution is not as novel as presented.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable practical contribution to the field of energy-efficient AI by demonstrating a working implementation that bridges backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware. The experimental results are strong and significant, and the approach has clear potential for real-world deployment. However, the novelty claims require substantial revision to properly acknowledge prior work. The core algorithmic contribution is not as novel as the paper suggests, and the authors should clearly differentiate their contribution from [15] and other prior work. With proper revision to address these concerns, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS. As written, it has significant merit but requires clarification of its relationship to prior work. The experimental results are strong enough to warrant acceptance, but the novelty claims must be corrected.
