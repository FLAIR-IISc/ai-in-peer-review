This paper proposes a novel approach to reconciling the incompatibility between high-precision backpropagation and spike-based neuromorphic devices. The authors treat analog variables as probabilities that are sampled to produce binary synapses, and adapt backpropagation to be compatible with this probabilistic sampling strategy for spiking networks. The method is validated on the MNIST dataset and mapped onto the TrueNorth chip, with a focus on optimizing for both size and accuracy.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and methodology. The manuscript quality and clarity are very high, and the work is original and broadly useful across platforms. The authors provide a thorough explanation of their approach, including the mathematical derivations and the network topology used in their experiments.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to bridge the gap between high-precision backpropagation and spike-based neuromorphic devices. The authors demonstrate that their approach can be used to train networks that naturally map to neuromorphic hardware, and that these networks can achieve high accuracy and low power consumption.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. First, the authors could provide more context and background information on the significance and practical deployment scenarios for TrueNorth. While the paper mentions that the TrueNorth chip is a low-power substrate for processing sensory data, it would be helpful to provide more details on which specific applications would benefit from this approach and how it enables practical deployment.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from a clearer comparison of the advantages of the proposed method over alternative methods. The authors mention that previous efforts have shown success with subsets of the elements they bring together, but it would be helpful to provide a more detailed comparison of the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.

The results presented in the paper are convincing, but some implementation details are missing. For example, the authors do not provide details on how inputs are encoded as spikes, or what parameter and frequency values were used in their experiments. Providing these details would be helpful for reproducibility and would allow other researchers to build on the authors' work.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing and provides a novel approach to bridging the gap between high-precision backpropagation and spike-based devices. With some minor revisions to address the areas mentioned above, the paper has the potential to be a valuable resource for researchers in the field.
