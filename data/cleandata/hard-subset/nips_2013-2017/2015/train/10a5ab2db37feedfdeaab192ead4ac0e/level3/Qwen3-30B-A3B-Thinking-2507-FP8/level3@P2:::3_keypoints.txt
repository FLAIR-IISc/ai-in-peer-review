- Addresses deploying deep learning on energy-constrained neuromorphic hardware by bridging continuous backpropagation and spiking/discrete synapse platforms.  
- Core method: treat spikes and synaptic states as probabilities to enable backpropagation, then map to hardware by sampling probabilities and ensemble-averaging networks.  
- Demonstrated on TrueNorth with MNIST: 99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ/image (ensemble of 64) and 92.7% at 0.268 μJ/image (single network).  
- Technically sound constrain-then-train approach that links training error to deployment error.  
- Results are strong and state-of-the-art for spiking-neuron MNIST experiments, outperforming prior work.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized.  
- Energy-efficiency results are impressive and significant for low-power applications.  
- Novelty claims are overstated; prior work has combined related elements (e.g., spiking + constraints), so phrasing should be corrected.  
- Several technical details need clarification (meaning of "0.15 bits/synapse", consolidated network topology, training time overhead from probabilistic updates).  
- Counterintuitive experimental results are unexplained (30-core single ensemble much worse than 5-core ensemble; why different 5-core ensembles yield similar results).  
- Insufficient discussion of limitations and generalizability beyond TrueNorth, and practicality of ensemble approach given increased hardware resources.  
- Missing comparison to other spiking backpropagation methods (e.g., SpikeProp) to better position the contribution.  
- Overall assessment: promising and valuable contribution but requires revisions to clarify novelty, technical details, comparisons, and limitations.  
- Recommendation: accept contingent on revisions to reword novelty claims, clarify technical points, consolidate topology description, add comparisons, and expand limitations discussion.
