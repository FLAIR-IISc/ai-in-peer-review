This paper proposes a novel approach to reconcile high-precision backpropagation with spike-based devices by treating analog variables as probabilities sampled to produce binary synapses. The authors adapt backpropagation to be compatible with the probabilistic sampling strategy for spiking networks and validate their method on the MNIST dataset, achieving state-of-the-art performance. They also demonstrate the feasibility of their approach by mapping trained networks onto the TrueNorth chip, optimizing for size and accuracy.

The manuscript quality and clarity are very high, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and methodology. The work is original and broadly useful across platforms, as it provides a new perspective on how to bridge the gap between deep learning and neuromorphic computing.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to demonstrate the practicality of the proposed approach by providing chip-measured performance figures for two different hardware architectures. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy and energy efficiency, making their approach appealing for real-world applications.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. Firstly, the authors do not sufficiently highlight the significance or practical deployment scenarios for TrueNorth. It would be beneficial to provide more context on how this method enables practical deployment and which specific applications would benefit from it.

Additionally, the paper lacks a clear comparison of the advantages of the proposed method over alternative methods. The authors mention previous work, but a more detailed comparison would help to better understand the novelty and impact of their approach.

Furthermore, while the results are convincing, some implementation details are missing, such as input-to-spike conversion, spike frequencies, shared weight values, and neuron models. Providing more information on these aspects would enhance the reproducibility of the results.

Lastly, it would be beneficial to clarify how inputs are encoded as spikes and provide parameter/frequency details for reproducibility. This would make it easier for other researchers to build upon the authors' work and explore the proposed approach further.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing and deep learning. With some minor revisions to address the mentioned areas, it has the potential to become a seminal work in the field.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* Original and broadly useful approach to bridge the gap between deep learning and neuromorphic computing
* High-quality manuscript with clear and concise writing
* State-of-the-art performance on the MNIST dataset
* Practical demonstration of the approach on the TrueNorth chip

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* Lack of clear comparison with alternative methods
* Missing implementation details
* Limited context on practical deployment scenarios for TrueNorth
* Need for clarification on input encoding and parameter/frequency details
