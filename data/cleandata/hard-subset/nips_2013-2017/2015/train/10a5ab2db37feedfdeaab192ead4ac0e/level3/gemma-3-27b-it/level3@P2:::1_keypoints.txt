- Paper proposes training spiking-neuron networks with low-precision synapses via a modified backpropagation treating spikes/synapses as probabilities, enabling direct mapping to neuromorphic hardware (TrueNorth) and demonstrated on MNIST.  
- Demonstrated competitive accuracy with major energy savings on TrueNorth (e.g., 99.42% at 108 μJ/image; 92.7% at 0.268 μJ/image).  
- Builds on recent deep learning and neuromorphic work and cites relevant prior literature.  
- Introduction clearly motivates the problem; methodology (Section 3) is detailed and well-structured.  
- Network topology design (Section 4) justified by hardware constraints and efficient information flow.  
- Experimental results (Section 5) convincingly show an accuracy vs. energy trade-off and state-of-the-art power performance on TrueNorth.  
- Strength: very strong experimental results demonstrating significant energy-efficiency gains.  
- Strength: practical, hardware-grounded focus tailored to TrueNorth constraints.  
- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation with helpful figures and tables.  
- Strength: tackles an important problem of aligning backprop-trained models with neuromorphic hardware.  
- Weakness: novelty is overstated; algorithm closely resembles and hybridizes methods in [14] and [15] using the same approximations.  
- Weakness: the claimed novelty about “constrain-then-train” is misleading since prior work ([15]) already explored similar ideas.  
- Weakness: incorrect or incomplete citation of prior state-of-the-art—[15] achieved best-known binary results on MNIST without augmentation but this isn’t acknowledged.  
- Weakness: limited demonstrated generalizability—unclear performance on more complex datasets or architectures beyond MNIST.  
- Overall: valuable contribution with strong experiments but overstated novelty and misrepresented prior art; effective combination/application to TrueNorth is noteworthy.  
- For acceptance: strong experimental evidence, practical relevance, clear presentation, and importance of the problem argue for acceptance.  
- Against acceptance: overstated novelty, insufficient acknowledgment of related work, misleading prior-art claims, and limited generalizability discussion argue against.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—explicitly acknowledge relationship to [14]/[15] and correct the prior state-of-the-art claim.
