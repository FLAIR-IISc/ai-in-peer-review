This paper presents a method for training deep neural networks for deployment on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware, such as the TrueNorth chip. The core idea is to reconcile the continuous nature of backpropagation with the discrete, spiking nature of neuromorphic systems. This is achieved by treating synaptic connections and neuron activations as continuous probabilities during an offline training phase. The trained probabilistic network is then mapped to the hardware by sampling from these distributions, allowing for the creation of single or ensemble networks. This work sits at the critical intersection of deep learning algorithms and specialized hardware, addressing the important challenge of running high-performance models under strict power constraints.

The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive experimental results. The authors demonstrate a compelling trade-off between accuracy and power efficiency on the MNIST dataset, achieving a state-of-the-art 99.42% accuracy with an ensemble network and remarkable energy efficiency (0.268 μJ per image) with a single network. The successful deployment and measurement on the TrueNorth chip represent a significant engineering achievement and provide a valuable data point for the field of neuromorphic computing. These results are of high significance and are likely to be of great interest to both researchers and practitioners in the field.

However, the paper's claims regarding methodological novelty are overstated, particularly concerning its relationship to prior work. The core algorithm presented in Section 3.2 appears to be a hybrid of the methods in Zhao et al. [14] and Cheng et al. [15], derived using identical approximations, a connection that the authors should explicitly acknowledge. Furthermore, the "constrain-then-train" methodology is presented as a new concept in the discussion, despite having been proposed and demonstrated in [15]. Similarly, the novelty claim (i) in the introduction is misleading, as the "expectation backpropagation" method in [15] is a backpropagation update designed for binary neurons and weights that can also accommodate constrained connectivity. Finally, the paper cites an incorrect prior state-of-the-art; the work in [15] achieved better results for binary networks on MNIST without data augmentation than the prior work cited by the authors.

In summary, while the experimental validation and the performance achieved on physical hardware are novel and highly significant, the paper's algorithmic contributions are not as original as claimed. Acceptance should be conditioned on a major revision that accurately situates the methodology within the context of existing literature, particularly [15], and tempers its claims of novelty accordingly.
