

# Review of "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing"

This paper proposes a training methodology to bridge the gap between backpropagation-based deep learning and neuromorphic hardware constraints. The authors introduce a "constrain-then-train" approach where networks are trained with continuous-valued probabilities (representing spike probabilities or synapse states) constrained to [0,1], enabling direct mapping to hardware with spiking neurons, discrete synapses, and limited connectivity. The authors demonstrate their approach on the TrueNorth chip, achieving 99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ per image and 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image.

The paper builds upon prior work in probabilistic neural networks and hardware-aware training. Previous efforts have explored subsets of the elements presented here: backpropagation with spiking neurons but high-precision weights [9-12], networks with binary/trinary synapses but continuous output neurons [13], and probabilistic backpropagation for binary neurons/synapses with full connectivity [14,15]. The authors' main contribution appears to be combining these elements for a specific hardware platform.

**Strengths:** The experimental results are strong and well-documented. The paper provides clear metrics for both accuracy and energy efficiency on a real neuromorphic platform, demonstrating the practical viability of the approach. The mapping procedure from training to deployment networks is well-explained and practical. The hardware integration with TrueNorth is compelling, with the 0.268 μJ per image result representing a significant efficiency improvement over previous low-power approaches.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's novelty claims are problematic and require significant revision. The "constrain-then-train" methodology described in the discussion is not novel, as ref [15] explicitly proposed and tested this exact approach. Ref [15] ("Training binary multilayer neural networks for image classification using expectation backpropagation") already demonstrated a backprop update using expectation propagation for binary neurons and weights that can accommodate constrained connectivity. 

The algorithm in Section 3.2 is deeply similar to and effectively a hybrid of refs [14] and [15], derived using identical approximations. The Gaussian approximation in Equation (2) and the subsequent gradient derivation in Equation (4) are virtually identical to those in ref [15], with only minor differences in implementation details. This constitutes a significant oversight in the literature review.

The paper's novelty claim (i) is misleading: "we demonstrate for the first time an offline training methodology using backpropagation to create a network that employs spiking neurons, synapses requiring less bits of precision than even trinary weights, and constrained connectivity." Ref [15] already achieved this exact combination, as stated in their abstract: "We present a novel training algorithm called expectation backpropagation for training binary weight and binary activation neural networks." The paper also incorrectly cites ref [15] as achieving "97.88% accuracy" on MNIST, but ref [15] actually achieved 97.88% on MNIST without data augmentation, which was the best-known result at the time.

**Quality:** The experimental results are technically sound and well-supported, but the theoretical contributions are not sufficiently novel. The authors are not careful in acknowledging prior work, which undermines the paper's scientific integrity.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good explanations of the mapping between training and deployment networks. The hardware details are particularly well-explained.

**Originality:** The core algorithmic contribution is not original, and the novelty claims are overstated. The paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from ref [15], which is a significant oversight.

**Significance:** The demonstrated energy efficiency is impressive and practically significant for embedded systems. The approach could be valuable for real-world neuromorphic applications, but the contribution is diminished by the lack of novelty in the training methodology.

**Recommendation:** The paper should be rejected due to significant issues with novelty and literature review. The experimental results are strong and novel, but the methodology is not. The authors should substantially revise the paper to acknowledge the prior work in ref [15] and properly contextualize their contribution relative to existing literature. Without these revisions, the paper risks misleading readers about the novelty of its approach.

Pro: Strong experimental results, clear mapping between training and deployment, practical demonstration on TrueNorth hardware.
Con: Overstated novelty claims, failure to properly acknowledge prior work, incorrect state-of-the-art claims, algorithmic contributions are not novel.
