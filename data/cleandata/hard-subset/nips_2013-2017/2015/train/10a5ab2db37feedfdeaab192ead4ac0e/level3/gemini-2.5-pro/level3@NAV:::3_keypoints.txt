- Presents a novel "constrain-then-train" backprop method that treats spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities for hardware-mappable training.
- Validated on TrueNorth with MNIST and demonstrates impressive energy efficiency on real neuromorphic hardware.
- Practical and important contribution to hardware-aware machine learning by integrating hardware constraints into training.
- Well-written and easy to follow, with the real-hardware demonstration a significant strength.
- Rephrase Section 1 to avoid implying spiking neurons or low-precision synapses are themselves novel; emphasize integrated training novelty.
- Present the TrueNorth demonstration as validation of the method, not as a separate novelty claim.
- Consolidate the network topology description into a single location and clarify technical terms (e.g., "0.15 bits per synapse").
- For reproducibility, report input spike rates during testing and the computational/training-time overhead of probabilistic updates.
- Provide deeper analysis to explain why the single-ensemble 30-core network performs much worse than the 5-core network and why different 5-core ensembles give similar results.
- Compare with other spiking backpropagation rules (e.g., SpikeProp) and correct the characterization of Ref. [12], which does enforce some constraints during training.
- Note that the specificity of the constraints may limit generalizability across platforms.
- Highlight the significant hardware trade-off: reliance on a 64-member ensemble to reach top accuracy.
