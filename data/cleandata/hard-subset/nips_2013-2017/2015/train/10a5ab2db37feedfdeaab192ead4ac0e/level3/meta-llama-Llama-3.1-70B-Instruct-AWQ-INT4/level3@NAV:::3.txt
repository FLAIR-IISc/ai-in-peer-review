**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper proposes a backpropagation training method for energy-efficient neuromorphic computing. The authors address the incompatibility between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware by treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities. This approach allows training a network using standard backpropagation, which can then be mapped to neuromorphic hardware. The authors demonstrate their method on the MNIST dataset using the TrueNorth chip, achieving high performance and energy efficiency.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

Strengths:

* The paper presents a timely and interesting approach to reconciling backpropagation with neuromorphic hardware constraints.
* The proposed training method is useful for new multi-neuron hardware platforms with constraints.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses:

* The claims in Section 1 may imply novelty in using spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses, which is not entirely accurate.
* The demonstration on the TrueNorth hardware should be presented as a validation of the method, rather than a separate novelty claim.
* The network topology is not clearly described in a single, consolidated place.
* The paper lacks a clear explanation of the "0.15 bits per synapse" concept in Section 2.
* The authors do not report how much longer training takes due to probabilistic synaptic connection updates.
* The performance difference between the single ensemble 30-core network and the 5-core network is not adequately explained.
* The paper does not provide a clear discussion of the significance of the results, particularly in terms of generalizability to other hardware platforms.

**Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance**

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their method. However, some claims may be overstated, and the paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of certain concepts.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to follow, but some sections could be improved for clarity. For example, the network topology description is scattered throughout the paper.

Originality: The paper presents a novel approach to reconciling backpropagation with neuromorphic hardware constraints. However, the authors could provide more context and comparisons to other spiking backpropagation rules, such as SpikeProp.

Significance: The paper addresses an important problem in neuromorphic computing, and the results are significant in terms of energy efficiency and performance. However, the authors could provide more discussion on the generalizability of their approach to other hardware platforms and the potential impact on the field.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and timely approach to neuromorphic computing.
* The results are significant in terms of energy efficiency and performance.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The claims in Section 1 may be overstated.
* The paper lacks a clear explanation of certain concepts, such as the "0.15 bits per synapse" concept.
* The authors do not provide a clear discussion of the significance of the results, particularly in terms of generalizability to other hardware platforms.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of the paper, but with revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above.
