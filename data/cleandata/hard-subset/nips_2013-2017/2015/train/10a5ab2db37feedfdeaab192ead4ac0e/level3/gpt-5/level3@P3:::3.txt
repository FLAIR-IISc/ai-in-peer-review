Summary
This paper proposes a backpropagation-based training method for spiking neuromorphic hardware that uses spike and synapse probabilities to reconcile spiking neurons, discrete synapses, and constrained connectivity with gradient-based optimization. During training, neuron outputs and synaptic connections are treated as continuous probabilities in [0,1], enabling a differentiable forward pass via Gaussian approximations and a corresponding backward pass. After training, discrete deployment networks are obtained by sampling these probabilities; multiple independent samples can be ensembled to improve accuracy. The method is demonstrated on IBM’s TrueNorth (TN) with MNIST, reporting 99.42% accuracy using a 64-member ensemble (108 μJ/image) and 92.7% with a single network (0.268 μJ/image). The work relates to prior spiking backprop and low-precision weight literature (e.g., Diehl et al. 2015; binary/binarized networks; probabilistic backprop), and contributes a hardware-aware, constrained training procedure that directly maps to TN’s constraints (binary crossbar, axon types, limited fan-in).

Strengths
- Timely and interesting: a practical, hardware-aware training pipeline that accounts for spiking neurons, discrete synapses, and constrained connectivity.
- Clear and well-written; the probability-based formulation and mapping to TN are easy to follow.
- Demonstrates compelling energy–accuracy tradeoffs and real-time operation on silicon.
- Separation of learning “what’s on/off” from fixed signed synaptic strengths (with balanced ±1 and axon-type LUTs) is simple and effective.
- Ensemble sampling from a single trained probabilistic network is elegant and empirically effective.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification/improvement
- Novelty framing: Some statements in Section 1 conflate “using spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses” with novelty. The novelty is in the specific probability-based backprop under TN-like constraints and the end-to-end mapping, not in the use of spikes or low precision per se. Please reword to avoid implying that spikes/low-precision weights are novel.
- Position the TN demo as validation. Running on TN is a strong validation/use case; it should not be claimed as a separate core novelty beyond the training method and mapping procedure.
- “0.15 bits per synapse” (Sec. 2) needs a concrete explanation. Suggest: Each neuron has a 4-entry LUT of signed 9-bit strengths (36 bits total) that are shared by its 256 incoming synapses (one of 4 axon types). Amortized LUT overhead per synapse = 36/256 ≈ 0.1406 bits, rounded to 0.15 bits. Combined with 1 on/off bit gives ~1.15 bits/synapse effective memory.
- Consolidate network topology. Please provide a single table (or paragraph) listing, for each network (5-core; 30-core): number of layers, cores per layer, neurons/core, fan-in per neuron, R/F/S per layer, strides, and class-neuron replication. Currently these details are scattered across Sections 3–5 and Figure 3.
- Training cost overhead. The forward pass computes per-neuron Gaussian mean/variance and CDF terms; the backward pass uses corresponding derivatives. Please report wall-clock training time relative to a comparable deterministic network (same sparsity/topology) and break down extra cost due to variance/CDF computations. Also report how many epochs and total hours were needed for each network.
- Ensemble analysis: 
  - Explain why a single-sample 30-core network performs much worse than the 5-core network. A plausible mechanism is higher variance from deeper/larger constrained sampling (binary connections, balanced ±1 weights) that accumulates across layers; ensemble averaging reduces this variance substantially. Please quantify accuracy vs ensemble size for both networks and analyze variance reduction.
  - Explain why different ensembles of the 5-core network yield similar results. Likely the shallower network is bias-limited (capacity-constrained), so variance across samples is small and ensembles yield diminishing returns. Please support with variance-of-predictions across ensemble members.
- Input spiking during testing. Please state explicitly: Are inputs Bernoulli spikes with P(spike)=normalized pixel intensity in a single 1 ms time step (i.e., at most one spike per axon)? Provide the average input spike rate per image, and clarify if any multi-timestep spike trains were used.
- Related work: discuss spiking backprop rules such as SpikeProp (Bohte et al., 2002) and related spike-timing–based gradient methods (e.g., ReSuMe/SPAN). Contrast your probability-domain surrogate with timing-based surrogates, learning signals, and hardware compatibility.
- Train-then-constrain comparison (Ref. 12). Note that Diehl et al. enforce constraints during training (e.g., threshold/bias balancing), so it is not purely train-then-constrain. Also fix the “approach approach” typo.
- Report the slowdown/extra iterations due to probabilistic synapse updates (snapping/clipping) and any instability mitigations (e.g., learning rate schedules near c̃ ∈ {0,1}).
- Claims of “first” and SOTA. Please temper “for the first time” claims to reflect prior spiking backprop with high-precision weights and binarized/probabilistic networks. Emphasize the combination of spiking neurons, <2-bit effective synapses, and constrained connectivity trained jointly with backprop and validated on silicon.

Quality
- Generally sound and well-supported by experiments on real hardware. The Gaussian approximation and gradient simplification are reasonable given the large fan-in and balanced weights; an ablation showing sensitivity to these approximations would strengthen the technical case. Please include numerical stability considerations and any checks that the dropped σ^−3 term is indeed negligible in practice.

Clarity
- Very readable. Add a consolidated topology description, define the 0.15 bits/synapse, and detail training/runtime settings (batch size, epochs, wall-clock).

Originality
- Moderate-to-high. The combination of probability-domain backprop with TN-style constraints and sampling/ensembling is a useful contribution; however, elements (spikes, low-precision, probabilistic training) have precedents.

Significance
- Hardware-aware training is important and of interest to NIPS. The constraints are TN-specific and the need for a 64-member ensemble to reach top accuracy raises resource scaling concerns; discuss generalization to other platforms and accuracy–energy–resource tradeoffs explicitly.

Suggested rewording (examples)
- Section 1 novelty: “We present a hardware-aware training method that treats spikes and discrete synapses as probabilities, enabling backpropagation under spiking, low-precision, and constrained-connectivity assumptions, with a direct mapping to neuromorphic hardware. While spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses are well established, our contribution is a probability-based backprop formulation and deployment procedure tailored to these constraints.”
- TN demo framing: “We validate the approach on the TrueNorth chip, demonstrating real-time operation and state-of-the-art energy efficiency on MNIST.”

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, well-motivated hardware-aware learning with clear benefits in energy efficiency
- Clean probabilistic formulation and direct mapping to deployed spiking hardware
- Strong empirical results and real-time hardware demo
- Easy-to-read paper; method likely reusable on other constrained neuromorphic systems

Cons for acceptance
- Novelty somewhat overstated; needs more careful positioning vs prior spiking backprop and binarized/probabilistic networks
- Generality limited by TN-specific constraints and weight parameterization
- Heavy reliance on large ensembles for top accuracy increases hardware resources
- Missing details: training-time overhead, consolidated topology, input spike-rate specifics, variability analysis

Minor comments
- Fix “approach approach”.
- Provide confidence intervals or multiple runs for reported accuracies, especially across ensembles.
- Consider reporting per-core energy and scaling with ensemble size to make resource tradeoffs explicit.
