This paper addresses the critical challenge of bridging the gap between the algorithmic success of deep learning with backpropagation and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic hardware. The authors present a novel training methodology that allows backpropagation to be used to train networks compatible with spiking neurons, discrete synapses, and constrained connectivity – characteristics of neuromorphic chips like TrueNorth. This is a timely and interesting contribution, as it tackles a key bottleneck in deploying deep learning models on energy-constrained embedded systems.

The core idea is to treat spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, enabling the use of standard backpropagation. The trained network can then be mapped to neuromorphic hardware by sampling these probabilities to create an ensemble of networks, which are averaged for improved performance. The authors demonstrate this approach on the MNIST dataset, achieving strong results on the TrueNorth chip, showcasing both high accuracy and impressive energy efficiency.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically sound, leveraging the probabilistic framework to reconcile continuous backpropagation with discrete neuromorphic hardware. The mathematical formulation, particularly in Section 3.2 and 3.3, is well-defined and justified.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the proposed solution. The organization is logical, with dedicated sections for hardware, training, network topology, and results.
* **Significance:** Hardware-aware training is a significant contribution. Enabling the use of backpropagation on neuromorphic hardware opens the door to leveraging the strengths of both approaches. The demonstrated energy efficiency on TrueNorth is particularly compelling.
* **Novelty:** The combination of probabilistic backpropagation with constrained connectivity, low-precision synapses, and direct mapping to neuromorphic hardware is novel. The authors successfully demonstrate a complete pipeline from training to deployment.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Claimed Novelty:** The claims in Section 1 regarding the novelty of using spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses are overstated. Prior work (e.g., [9, 12, 13, 15]) has explored these areas. The authors should reword these statements to emphasize the *combination* of these elements within their specific probabilistic framework as the core novelty.
* **TN Demonstration as Novelty:** The demonstration on TrueNorth hardware, while valuable, should be framed as a *validation* of the method rather than a separate novelty claim. The primary contribution lies in the training methodology itself.
* **Missing Details:** The explanation of the “0.15 bits per synapse” in Section 2 is unclear and requires clarification. What does this refer to beyond the on/off state?
* **Network Topology Description:** The network topology is described in a fragmented manner. A consolidated description, potentially with a diagram, would enhance clarity.
* **Training Time Overhead:** The paper does not discuss the computational overhead introduced by the probabilistic synaptic connection updates. Reporting the training time compared to a standard backpropagation implementation would be beneficial.
* **Ensemble Performance Discrepancy:** The significantly lower performance of the single ensemble 30-core network compared to the 5-core network is not adequately explained. An analysis of this discrepancy is crucial.
* **Ensemble Consistency:** The consistently similar results across different ensembles of the 5-core network suggest a limited exploration of the probability space. More detailed analysis here would improve understanding.
* **Input Representation:** The paper lacks specifics about the input spike rates during testing. Furthermore, it's unclear whether inputs are provided as single spikes per time step or as spike trains.
* **Related Work Nuance:** The discussion of Ref. [12] incorrectly characterizes it as a purely “train-then-constrain” approach. It enforces constraints *during* training, which alters the learning process. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a discussion and comparison with other spiking backpropagation rules, such as SpikeProp.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a critical problem in neuromorphic computing.
* The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated.
* The results on TrueNorth are promising and demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Overstated claims of novelty.
* Lack of clarity regarding specific implementation details.
* Unexplained performance discrepancies.
* Limited discussion of related work.
* Generalizability of the TN-specific constraints remains a concern.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. While some weaknesses exist, the core idea is innovative, the technical execution is strong, and the results are promising. Addressing the weaknesses, particularly clarifying the novelty claims and providing more detailed analysis of the results, would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the points raised above to improve the clarity, accuracy, and impact of their work.
