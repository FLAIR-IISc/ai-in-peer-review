

This paper presents a "constrain-then-train" methodology for training networks compatible with neuromorphic hardware (TrueNorth), using probabilistic representations of spiking neurons and discrete synapses to bridge backpropagation and energy-efficient deployment. The core idea—treating connectivity as learnable probabilities constrained to [0,1]—is applied to MNIST classification, achieving 99.42% accuracy (64-ensemble) at 108 μJ/image and 92.7% (single-ensemble) at 0.268 μJ/image.  

**Strengths**: Experimental results are robust and novel in demonstrating state-of-the-art accuracy/energy tradeoffs on neuromorphic hardware. The deployment methodology is well-motivated, and the TrueNorth implementation provides concrete, impactful validation. The network topology (progressive mixing) and ensemble approach are clearly articulated.  

**Weaknesses**: The novelty claims are significantly overstated. Section 3.2’s algorithm is mathematically identical to the "expectation backpropagation" in ref [15] (Cheng et al., 2015), which explicitly handles constrained connectivity for binary neurons/weights. The discussion’s opening paragraph incorrectly presents "constrain-then-train" as novel, yet ref [15] both proposed and tested this approach. The novelty claim (i) in the introduction is misleading: ref [15] achieved 97.88% MNIST accuracy with binary weights/neurons *without data augmentation*, contradicting the paper’s assertion that their method "achieves the best accuracy to date." The paper also misstates ref [15] as lacking constrained connectivity (it explicitly accommodates it).  

**Originality**: The core methodology is not novel (ref [15] covers identical concepts). The paper fails to properly contextualize its work against prior probabilistic backpropagation efforts, especially ref [15]. While the deployment results are strong, the algorithmic contribution is derivative.  

**Significance**: The energy efficiency results (0.268 μJ/image) are compelling and practically significant for neuromorphic systems. However, the overstated novelty diminishes the perceived contribution to the field.  

**Recommendation**: Accept with major revisions. The authors must: (1) fully acknowledge ref [15]’s foundational work in Section 3.2 and the introduction; (2) correct the MNIST accuracy comparison; (3) clarify that "constrain-then-train" was first proposed in ref [15]. The experimental merits are strong, but the paper’s scientific integrity requires proper attribution to prior work. Without these changes, the paper risks misrepresenting the literature.
