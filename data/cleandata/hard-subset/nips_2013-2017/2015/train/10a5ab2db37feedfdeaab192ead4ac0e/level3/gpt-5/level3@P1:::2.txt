This paper proposes a principled bridge between high-precision backpropagation and spike-based neuromorphic hardware by relaxing spikes and discrete synapses into continuous probabilities during training, then sampling to instantiate binary synapses and spike trains at deployment. The forward model approximates the weighted sum of Bernoulli inputs with a Gaussian, enabling differentiable computation of spiking probabilities and an adapted backpropagation rule (including a computationally motivated gradient approximation). The topology is matched to TrueNorth’s constrained core-to-core connectivity via sliding windows and progressive mixing of receptive fields. A core-level weight-initialization scheme balances positive and negative s-values and minimizes pairwise inner products of effective weights to reduce redundancy. At deployment, the trained probabilistic network is sampled once or multiple times; ensembles are averaged to trade energy for accuracy. On MNIST, a 30-core network reaches 99.42% with an ensemble of 64 (108 μJ/image), while a 5-core network achieves 92.7% with a single sample (0.268 μJ/image), both running at 1 kHz.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself between (i) backprop-trained spiking networks with high-precision weights [9–12], (ii) binary/trinary networks with continuous activations [13–15], and (iii) neuromorphic hardware like TrueNorth [7]. Its constrain-then-train approach contrasts with train-then-constrain SNN conversions (e.g., Diehl et al. [12]; Cao et al. 2015), and the authors report superior MNIST accuracy among spiking systems. The weight-initialization strategy is related in spirit to infomax [19]. To better contextualize contributions, the paper should compare more explicitly to contemporary low-precision/binarized training methods and SNN conversions (see Suggested additions).

Quality: The methodology is technically sound overall. The Gaussian approximation for sums of weighted Bernoulli inputs and the simplification that drops the σ^−3 term in ∂ñ/∂c̃ are reasonable under the stated balancing assumptions and large in-degree, but would benefit from an ablation (e.g., accuracy and convergence with/without the approximation). The mapping from probabilities to binary synapses via independent sampling is simple and compatible with TrueNorth’s 1-bit crossbar and shared s-value lookups. Hardware results are measured on chip and report active plus pro-rated leakage energy, lending credibility. However, several implementation details are missing or too terse to allow reproduction or fair comparison:
- Input encoding: exact mapping from pixel intensities to spike probabilities, timing (1 ms mentioned), and whether rate or single-sample stochastic coding is used per input line and per ensemble member.
- Neuron model and parameters: leak/threshold/floor settings (beyond qualitative description), refractory behavior, reset mode, time constants.
- Synaptic details: the exact s-value tables per axon type, how axon types are assigned, how “blocks” are defined and shared, and whether s-values differ across layers.
- Ensemble deployment: whether 64-member ensembles are time-multiplexed on one chip or spread across cores/chips; latency/throughput trade-offs and memory footprint.
- Power methodology: absolute power numbers, breakdown of active vs leakage, and sensitivity to batch size, duty cycle, and clock.
- Comparison baselines: same input encoding and hardware constraints when comparing to [9–12]; if not, clearly state differences.

Clarity: The manuscript is very well written and organized. The training equations are clearly derived and the topology description is intuitive. A few places need clarification: Equation (3) notation has minor formatting issues; define pk earlier and state explicitly how the class-averaging variance is computed; specify all training hyperparameters and data augmentation ranges in one place; add a figure/table with all hardware parameters (neuron model, s-values, axon types, spike rates). A short reproducibility appendix or supplementary with parameter tables would greatly help.

Originality: The probabilistic relaxation that simultaneously handles spiking outputs, 1-bit synapses, and constrained connectivity—while preserving standard backprop—is novel relative to prior SNN conversions and binary/trinary approaches. The ensemble-by-sampling strategy from a single trained probabilistic network is also an elegant twist. The weight-initialization method that minimizes pairwise inner products under TrueNorth constraints is a thoughtful hardware-aware contribution.

Significance: Demonstrating near state-of-the-art MNIST accuracy for SNNs together with compelling energy figures on real hardware is important for neuromorphic computing and energy-constrained ML. The approach appears portable to other spiking platforms and could be extended to convolutional structures. The paper would be stronger if it articulated concrete deployment scenarios (e.g., always-on vision, keyword spotting, on-sensor inference) and discussed how accuracy/energy/latency scale beyond MNIST.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled method that unifies backprop with spiking, low-precision, and limited connectivity.
- Strong hardware results with energy measurements on silicon; compelling accuracy–energy trade-offs.
- Hardware-aware topology and initialization; method likely transferable across platforms.
- Good writing and sound derivations.

Cons / concerns:
- Missing implementation details limit reproducibility and make comparisons less definitive.
- Limited discussion of practical deployment scenarios and system-level implications of ensembles.
- Comparisons to alternative SNN training/conversion methods are brief; ablations are absent.

Suggestions to improve the paper:
- Add citations to related neuromorphic/sampling/binary training work, e.g., Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015), and arXiv:1506.05427, and discuss differences.
- Provide a detailed appendix covering: input-to-spike encoding, spike frequencies per layer, neuron parameters, s-value tables and axon-type assignments, and power measurement methodology.
- Include ablations: effect of the gradient approximation; ensemble size vs accuracy/energy; alternative s-value blockings; topology hyperparameters (R, S, F); and “train-then-constrain” baselines under matched constraints.
- Clarify ensemble deployment and its practicality (time-multiplexing vs parallel hardware; throughput/latency).
- Expand on practical applications and how the reported operating points map to real-time tasks.

Recommendation: Accept (clear contribution at the intersection of deep learning and neuromorphic hardware; excellent hardware results) provided the authors add the requested implementation details and stronger comparisons.
