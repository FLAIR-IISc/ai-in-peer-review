This paper proposes a probabilistic backpropagation framework that enables training of spiking neural networks with discrete synapses and constrained connectivity, and demonstrates deployment on IBM’s TrueNorth neuromorphic hardware. The key idea is to represent spikes and binary synapses as continuous probabilities in [0,1] during training, using a Gaussian approximation to compute neuron firing probabilities and their gradients. The trained parameters (connection probabilities and biases) are sampled to produce one or more spiking networks for deployment; multiple sampled networks are ensembled by averaging outputs. The network topology uses locally connected “sliding windows” (akin to locally-connected layers without weight sharing) to match TrueNorth’s core connectivity, and the synaptic strength table s_ij is not learned but initialized to balanced ±1 patterns designed to reduce pairwise correlations. On MNIST, a 30-core network with an ensemble of 64 achieves 99.42% at 108 μJ/image; a 5-core network with ensemble size 1 achieves 92.7% at 0.268 μJ/image, both at 1 kHz throughput.

Relation to prior work: The forward/backward equations in Section 3.2 follow the same Gaussian approximation used in expectation backpropagation for binary neurons/weights (ref [15]) and stochastic bit-stream networks (ref [14]): a CLT-based Gaussian for the pre-activation, a probit/logit-like mapping to firing probability, and gradients via the Gaussian CDF (erf). The approximation ∂n/∂c ≈ (∂n/∂μ)(∂μ/∂c) after dropping the 1/σ^3 term mirrors the derivations in [15]. The proposed “constrain-then-train” philosophy—training directly in the discrete/binary probabilistic domain rather than training then quantizing—has been advocated and empirically validated in [15]. Prior spiking deployments (e.g., [11,12,16]) pursued train-then-constrain or conversion, often with high-precision weights. The present work’s distinct contributions lie less in the learning rule per se and more in: (a) mapping to TrueNorth’s axon-type/lookup-table synapse model, (b) a hardware-aware locally connected topology and initialization scheme, and (c) a thorough energy/performance characterization on real hardware.

Quality: The system is carefully engineered and the empirical results are strong. The energy measurements (including active plus proportional leakage) and real-time throughput are compelling. The Gaussian/probit training approximation is standard and reasonable given the fan-in and balanced ±1 design; however, the paper should more explicitly acknowledge its derivational lineage to [14,15]. The experimental methodology would benefit from ablations: (i) accuracy vs. ensemble size for the 30-core network (report ensemble=1, 4, 16), (ii) effect of data augmentation, (iii) sensitivity to the balanced s_ij initialization vs. alternatives, and (iv) the contribution of the sliding-window sparsity relative to fully connected layers with matched parameter counts. Claims of SOTA should be normalized for augmentation, ensembling, and architecture size.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and provides many deployment details (TrueNorth neuron settings, batch size, momentum, dropout, augmentation). Clarify: (1) that only c̃_ij and biases are learned; s_ij are fixed by the initialization strategy, (2) the exact mapping from class-wise multi-neuron outputs to loss and to hardware votes, and (3) how feature sub-selection across cores avoids conflicts and preserves coverage. The novelty positioning should be revised to reflect [14,15].

Originality: The algorithmic core in Sec. 3.2 is very close to [15] (expectation backprop) and [14], using identical approximations; the “constrain-then-train” message is not new. The noteworthy original aspects are the system integration: a training-to-TrueNorth mapping with constrained connectivity, the s_ij initialization tailored to axon types and decorrelation, and the ensemble sampling scheme tied to hardware deployment. Novelty claims (i) and in the Discussion should be tempered to credit [14,15], and the statement that prior work assumed full connectivity should be revised, as EP can in principle accommodate structured sparsity.

Significance: High for neuromorphic/low-power ML. Demonstrating sub-μJ per MNIST classification and 1 kHz throughput on TrueNorth is impactful, and the 99.42% accuracy with a hardware-feasible ensemble is competitive. For the broader NIPS audience, the methodological advance is incremental relative to [14,15], but the hardware-validated system contribution is substantial and likely to influence future work at the ML–hardware boundary.

Specific issues and recommendations:
- Acknowledge that the probabilistic forward/backward derivation closely follows [15] (EP) and is conceptually related to [14]; explain concrete differences (e.g., learning only connectivity probabilities, fixed s_ij, TrueNorth-specific constraints).
- Revise novelty claim (i) and the “constrain-then-train” framing to credit [15], which trained binary neurons/weights with backprop-style updates and can support constrained connectivity.
- Correct/clarify SOTA comparisons. Report apples-to-apples MNIST numbers: single model vs. ensemble, with/without data augmentation, and binary vs. spiking with high-precision weights. If [15] achieves higher accuracy under comparable settings, state this.
- Provide ablations: ensemble size, augmentation, initialization, and sparsity pattern. Report variance across random seeds for sampling c_ij and inputs.
- Discuss generalization to convolutional weight sharing (or articulate why locally-connected layers are preferable for TrueNorth), and potential extensions beyond MNIST.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong hardware results: excellent energy–accuracy trade-off and real-time operation on a large neuromorphic chip.
- Clear, pragmatic mapping from probabilistic training to spiking deployment, including topology and initialization aligned with hardware constraints.
- Thorough system perspective and relevance to embedded, low-power ML.

Cons for acceptance:
- Overstated novelty; Section 3.2 closely mirrors [14,15], and “constrain-then-train” is not new.
- SOTA comparisons are not strictly controlled for ensemble size and augmentation.
- Limited empirical scope (MNIST only) and missing ablations.

Overall: I lean toward acceptance for NIPS based on the strength and significance of the hardware-backed results and the well-executed end-to-end system, with the caveat that the paper should be revised to accurately position its algorithmic contribution relative to [14,15] and to present fair, controlled comparisons.
