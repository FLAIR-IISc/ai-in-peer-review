This paper proposes a novel approach to training neural networks for neuromorphic hardware using backpropagation. The authors introduce a probabilistic representation of the deployment network, allowing for the use of standard backpropagation to train the network. The trained network can then be mapped to the neuromorphic hardware by sampling the probabilities to create one or more networks, which are merged using ensemble averaging.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the background and related work, and the experimental results are strong and novel. The paper demonstrates the potential of the proposed approach for achieving high performance and energy efficiency on neuromorphic hardware.

However, there are some concerns regarding the originality and novelty of the approach. The algorithm presented in Section 3.2 is deeply similar to and effectively a hybrid of refs [14] and [15], derived using identical approximations. The authors should acknowledge this and provide a clearer explanation of how their approach differs from previous work.

Additionally, the discussion's first paragraph gives the impression that the "constrain-then-train" methodology is novel, despite it being suggested and tested in ref [15]. The novelty claim (i) in the introduction is also misleading, as ref [15]'s "expectation backpropagation" is a backprop update using expectation propagation for binary neurons and weights and can accommodate constrained connectivity.

The paper also cites incorrect prior state-of-the-art, as ref [15] achieved the best-known results for binary weights/neurons on MNIST without data augmentation. The authors should correct this and provide a more accurate representation of the current state-of-the-art.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, providing enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the methodology and results, and the paper is easy to follow.

Regarding originality, while the approach is not entirely novel, the authors do provide a unique combination of familiar techniques. The use of a probabilistic representation of the deployment network and the mapping of the trained network to the neuromorphic hardware are innovative and contribute to the field.

In terms of significance, the results are important, and the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The approach has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in neuromorphic computing and provide a unique solution for achieving high performance and energy efficiency.

Overall, the paper is strong, but the authors should address the concerns regarding originality and novelty. With some revisions to acknowledge previous work and provide a clearer explanation of the contributions, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
