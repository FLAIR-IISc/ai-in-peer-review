This paper proposes a novel method for reconciling backpropagation with the constraints of energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware, specifically addressing the incompatibility between continuous-valued backpropagation and the discrete, spiking nature of devices like TrueNorth. The core idea is to treat analog variables (synaptic weights, neuron outputs) as probabilities, enabling the application of standard backpropagation while naturally mapping to spiking networks via sampling. The authors demonstrate this approach on MNIST, achieving strong accuracy and impressive energy efficiency when deployed on the TrueNorth chip, presenting chip-measured performance for two network sizes.

This work builds upon a significant body of research at NIPS and elsewhere focused on bridging the gap between deep learning and neuromorphic computing. Prior work has explored backpropagation with spiking neurons but typically relied on high-precision weights [9, 10, 11, 12], or utilized binary/trinary synapses with continuous outputs [13]. The paper distinguishes itself by simultaneously addressing spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity, offering a more holistic solution. The approach shares similarities with probabilistic backpropagation methods [14, 15] but extends them to incorporate constrained connectivity, a crucial aspect of neuromorphic efficiency.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically well-founded. The probabilistic interpretation of analog variables provides a mathematically coherent framework for adapting backpropagation. The derivation of the gradient updates (Equation 4) and the approximations made are justified.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The concepts are explained in a straightforward manner, and the diagrams are helpful. The notation is consistent, and the flow of the argument is logical.
* **Originality:** The combination of probabilistic backpropagation, constrained connectivity, and hardware mapping to TrueNorth represents a novel and significant contribution. The “constrain-then-train” approach is a compelling strategy.
* **Significance:** Achieving high accuracy and energy efficiency on TrueNorth is a notable accomplishment. The potential for deploying deep learning models on low-power hardware is a crucial step towards real-world applications.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results on MNIST are convincing, demonstrating competitive accuracy and superior energy efficiency compared to existing approaches on TrueNorth. The chip-measured performance figures add significant credibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Application Context:** While the technical results are strong, the paper lacks a clear articulation of *where* this approach provides a substantial advantage beyond the MNIST benchmark. The discussion mentions mobile devices and automobiles, but doesn't convincingly demonstrate specific applications where this method is uniquely suited. The significance section could be strengthened by outlining realistic deployment scenarios.
* **Comparative Analysis:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with alternative methods for training spiking neural networks, beyond referencing existing work in passing. A dedicated table comparing performance metrics (accuracy, energy, training time) with relevant approaches (e.g., spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP), surrogate gradient methods) would be valuable. References [9]-[12] are mentioned, but a side-by-side comparison is missing.
* **Implementation Details:** The manuscript lacks crucial implementation details necessary for reproducibility. Specifically, the input-to-spike conversion method is not fully described. More information is needed on the spike frequencies used, the specific shared weight values employed, and the precise neuron model implemented in TrueNorth (beyond the basic equation provided).
* **Missing Citations:** The reviewers’ suggestion to include citations to Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015 and arXiv:1506.05427 is well-taken and should be addressed. These papers are relevant to the field and could provide valuable context.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and original solution to a critical problem in neuromorphic computing.
* The results are compelling, demonstrating high accuracy and energy efficiency on a real hardware platform.
* The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clear.
* The work has the potential to significantly advance the field of energy-efficient deep learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper lacks a strong justification for the practical relevance of TrueNorth and a clear articulation of the applications where this method excels.
* The lack of detailed implementation information hinders reproducibility.
* The comparative analysis with alternative methods is insufficient.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The technical contribution is substantial, and the writing quality is excellent. However, addressing the weaknesses related to application context, comparative analysis, and implementation details would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing these points in a revised version. Specifically, a more detailed discussion of potential applications, a table comparing performance metrics with relevant alternatives, and a clearer description of the implementation details (input encoding, spike frequencies, neuron model) are crucial. Incorporating the suggested citations is also important.
