- Presents a training-time proxy that represents discrete spikes/synapses as continuous probabilities to enable backpropagation for SNNs targeted at constrained neuromorphic hardware.
- Demonstrates sampling from learned probability distributions to create deployable networks and evaluates trade-offs between accuracy and energy on IBM TrueNorth.
- Achieves 99.42% accuracy with a 64-network ensemble at 108 µJ/image and 92.7% accuracy with a single network at 0.268 µJ/image.
- Timely and relevant work addressing training for energy-efficient, resource-constrained neuromorphic platforms.
- Novel “constrain-then-train” probabilistic approach elegantly handles hardware discreteness and stochasticity within gradient-based optimization.
- Strong experimental validation via deployment and evaluation on real TrueNorth hardware, showing practical utility.
- Paper is generally clear and well-written, with core concepts explained accessibly.
- Novelty claims should be reworded to emphasize the specific combination of constraints (binary synapses + lookup table) and the joint probabilistic backpropagation, not isolated elements.
- Missing reproducibility details: consolidate network topology (layers, cores, neurons, R/F/S) into a single description/table.
- Clarify input representation: whether testing uses single binary spike patterns or spike trains, and report typical input spike rates.
- Report training time and computational overhead compared to conventional networks, especially for Gaussian-approximation updates.
- Explain “0.15 bits per synapse” and the TrueNorth shared lookup table scheme for non-specialist readers.
- Provide analysis of puzzling results: why the 30-core single network performs worse than the 5-core network, and why 5-core ensemble sampling is so robust.
- Strengthen comparison to prior SNN backprop/gradient methods (e.g., SpikeProp), correct characterization of Ref. [12], and fix typos.
- Significance: practical hardware-aware training methodology for neuromorphic co-design, but generality may be limited to TrueNorth-like constraints.
- Important trade-off highlighted: high accuracy can require large ensembles (e.g., 64 networks), multiplying hardware resources and limiting embedded applicability.
