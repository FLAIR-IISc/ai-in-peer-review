- Paper proposes backprop-based training for spiking neuromorphic hardware treating neuron outputs and synapses as continuous probabilities, using Gaussian approximations for a differentiable forward/backward pass.  
- After training, discrete networks are obtained by sampling probabilities and ensembling multiple samples to improve accuracy.  
- Demonstrated on IBM TrueNorth with MNIST: 99.42% accuracy with 64-member ensemble (108 μJ/image) and 92.7% with a single network (0.268 μJ/image).  
- Method is hardware-aware and directly maps to TN constraints (binary crossbar, axon-type LUTs, limited fan-in).  
- Strength: timely, practical pipeline that accounts for spikes, discrete synapses, and constrained connectivity.  
- Strength: clear writing and an easy-to-follow probability-based formulation and mapping to TN.  
- Strength: demonstrates compelling energy–accuracy tradeoffs and real-time silicon operation.  
- Strength: separating on/off learning from fixed signed strengths (±1 and axon-type LUTs) is simple and effective.  
- Strength: ensemble sampling from one probabilistic network is elegant and empirically effective.  
- Weakness: novelty framing overstates contributions — spikes and low-precision weights are not new; novelty is the specific probability-based backprop and mapping.  
- Weakness: positioning the TN demo as a validation rather than a separate novelty is recommended.  
- Weakness: “0.15 bits per synapse” needs concrete explanation (amortized LUT overhead: 36 bits/256 ≈ 0.1406 → ~0.15 bits plus 1 on/off bit ≈1.15 bits/synapse).  
- Weakness: network topology details are scattered; request a consolidated table/paragraph listing layers, cores, neurons/core, fan-in, R/F/S, strides, and class replica counts.  
- Weakness: report wall-clock training time and overhead relative to comparable deterministic networks, and break down extra cost due to variance/CDF computations; include epochs and total hours per network.  
- Weakness: ensemble analysis missing — explain why single-sample 30-core network underperforms 5-core and quantify accuracy vs ensemble size and variance reduction for both networks.  
- Weakness: explain why different ensembles of 5-core network yield similar results, e.g., bias-limited shallow network with low sample variance; support with prediction-variance metrics.  
- Weakness: clarify input spiking during testing (are inputs Bernoulli spikes with P=normalized pixel intensity in a single 1 ms step?), report average input spike rate and whether multi-timestep trains were used.  
- Weakness: expand related work to include SpikeProp and timing-based gradient methods (ReSuMe, SPAN) and contrast probability-domain surrogate with timing-based surrogates and hardware compatibility.  
- Weakness: clarify train-then-constrain comparison (Diehl et al. enforces constraints during training) and fix typos (e.g., “approach approach”).  
- Weakness: report slowdown/extra iterations from probabilistic synapse updates (snapping/clipping) and any instability mitigations (e.g., learning-rate schedules near c̃ ∈ {0,1}).  
- Weakness: temper “first” and SOTA claims to acknowledge prior spiking backprop and binarized/probabilistic networks; emphasize the combination of elements and TN validation.  
- Quality: experiments on real hardware support claims; Gaussian approximation and gradient simplification are reasonable given large fan-in and balanced weights.  
- Quality request: include ablation on sensitivity to approximations and numerical stability checks (e.g., verify dropped σ^−3 term is negligible).  
- Clarity: readable paper but add consolidated topology, define 0.15 bits/synapse, and detail training/runtime settings (batch size, epochs, wall-clock).  
- Originality: moderate-to-high — useful combination of probability-domain backprop with TN constraints, but components have precedents.  
- Significance: important hardware-aware training work, but TN-specific constraints and need for large ensembles raise generality and resource-scaling concerns; discuss applicability to other platforms.  
- Suggested rewording: frame contribution as a hardware-aware probability-based backprop formulation with direct mapping to neuromorphic hardware and validate on TrueNorth.  
- Pros for acceptance: practical, well-motivated, energy-efficient, reusable method with real-hardware validation and clear probabilistic formulation.  
- Cons for acceptance: novelty somewhat overstated, TN-specific generality limited, reliance on large ensembles increases resources, and missing experimental/training details.  
- Minor: fix “approach approach”, provide confidence intervals/multiple runs for accuracies, and report per-core energy and scaling with ensemble size.
