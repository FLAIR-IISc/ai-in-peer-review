- Core contribution: probabilistic formulation of spikes and discrete synapses enabling standard backpropagation for neuromorphic hardware.  
- Motivation and clarity: well-motivated, clearly written, and easy to follow.  
- Practical validation: demonstrated on TrueNorth with MNIST, showing competitive accuracy and reduced energy consumption, especially using ensembles.  
- Framing/novelty: introduction overstates novelty; emphasize the combination of spiking neurons, reduced-precision synapses, and hardware-aware backprop as the main contribution.  
- TrueNorth claim: TrueNorth implementation should be presented as validation of the method, not a separate novelty claim.  
- "0.15 bits per synapse": the meaning and calculation of this metric need explicit explanation.  
- Network description: topology is presented piecemeal and should be consolidated into a single diagram or section.  
- Training overhead: report the computational/training time cost of probabilistic synaptic updates to assess practicality.  
- Core-size performance discrepancy: explain why the 30-core network performs much worse than the 5-core network.  
- Ensemble consistency: report consistency and variance of results across different ensembles of the 5-core network.  
- Related work comparison: explicitly compare to other spiking backpropagation rules (e.g., SpikeProp).  
- Generalizability: heavy reliance on TrueNorth-specific constraints may limit applicability to other neuromorphic platforms.  
- Resource cost: using a 64-ensemble increases hardware requirements and may reduce practical benefits for resource-constrained applications.  
- Acceptance pros: addresses an important challenge, clear presentation, and promising hardware results.  
- Acceptance cons: novelty overstated, unclear implementation details (synapse precision, training time), and limited discussion of related work and generalizability.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions after addressing the highlighted clarifications and comparisons.
