Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a principled way to reconcile gradient-based training with spike-based neuromorphic hardware that has binary synapses and constrained connectivity (TrueNorth). The key idea is to interpret all continuous quantities during training—input, neuron output, and synaptic connectivity—as probabilities in [0,1]. The training network thus uses continuous, differentiable probabilistic surrogates: synaptic connections are represented by continuous probabilities c̃ij (with binary deployment states sampled at inference), inputs are Bernoulli spike probabilities, and neuron outputs are spike probabilities derived from a Gaussian approximation to the weighted sum and a threshold nonlinearity. Backpropagation is adapted to this probabilistic forward model, including an approximation to the gradient that drops higher-order terms under balanced-input assumptions. Synaptic strengths are fixed a priori in balanced ±1 patterns per core to minimize pairwise neuron redundancy (via inner-product minimization), while only the synapse on/off probabilities and biases are learned.

The trained network maps directly to hardware by sampling the learned probabilities to realize one or multiple binary networks; multiple samples are ensembled by averaging outputs, reducing variance without retraining. The topology is a sliding-window, constrained-connectivity feedforward architecture that respects TrueNorth’s core-level input fan-in and shared input sets (akin to locally connected networks without weight sharing).

Compared to prior work: earlier “spiking backprop” approaches typically assumed high-precision synapses [e.g., 9–12], while binary/trinary networks with probabilistic training often assumed full connectivity and non-spiking neurons [14–15]. This paper is novel in jointly handling spiking neurons, sub-2-bit synapses, and hardware-constrained connectivity, with a concrete real-time deployment on TrueNorth and strong energy measurements. It would benefit from situating against related neuromorphic deployment/training pipelines such as Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015, and arXiv:1506.05427 (please cite and compare).

Empirical results on MNIST demonstrate 99.42% accuracy with an ensemble of 64 (30-core network; 108 μJ/image) and 92.7% with a single instance of the smallest feasible network (5 cores; 0.268 μJ/image), both at 1000 images/s. The paper also claims 4 μJ at 95% accuracy; the relationship among these measurements should be clarified.

Quality
Strengths:
- Technically sound formulation that makes backprop compatible with spiking, low-precision, constrained-connectivity hardware via probabilistic relaxation and sampling.
- Clear derivation of the neuron firing probability using a Gaussian approximation and a practical gradient approximation; the initialization strategy to minimize inter-neuron redundancy on-core is thoughtful and hardware-aware.
- Real hardware measurements on TrueNorth with detailed energy accounting (active + proportional leakage) and real-time throughput.

Weaknesses / questions:
- Approximations: The validity of the Gaussian/independence assumptions and the gradient simplification (dropping the σ−3 term) is argued qualitatively (balanced ±1 weights, 256 inputs), but no empirical ablation validates their impact on accuracy or convergence. Sensitivity to deviations (e.g., fewer inputs, unbalanced s-values, correlated inputs) should be quantified.
- Fixed synaptic strengths: Only connection probabilities and biases are learned; strengths are fixed. A comparison to learning block-wise s-values (even with straight-through estimators) would clarify the trade-off in accuracy/energy/complexity.
- Comparisons: While the paper cites prior SNN conversions and high-precision spiking training [9–12], it lacks a direct, controlled comparison under matched constraints (e.g., same topology, connectivity, and hardware-compatible quantization). This makes it harder to isolate the gain from the probabilistic training vs. topology/ensemble size.
- Reproducibility details are missing (see below).

Clarity
The manuscript is very well written and organized. The probabilistic forward model and mapping to hardware are explained clearly. However, several implementation details needed for reproducibility and fair comparison are underspecified:
- Input encoding: Precisely define the mapping from pixel intensity to spike probability (scaling, normalization, biasing), whether a single Bernoulli sample is used per input line per 1 ms step (as implied), and whether any rate coding or temporal averaging is used.
- Neuron model and parameters on TrueNorth: exact leak/threshold/reset/floor settings, refractory periods, and how biases bj are quantized and mapped (including handling of fractional portions).
- Synaptic strengths: the distribution across axon types and lookup table s-values; the exact ±1 assignment patterns per core and how axon types are balanced.
- Ensemble details: how outputs are averaged across members and output neurons per class; whether ties or thresholds are applied; whether ensemble members share random seeds.
- Training schedule: number of epochs, precise learning rate decay schedule, and any weight decay/regularization beyond dropout.
- Hardware measurement protocol: reconcile the 0.268 μJ, 108 μJ, and “4 μJ at 95%” claims, and specify whether I/O, host interface, and inactive-core leakage are included.

Originality
The combination of:
- probabilistic backprop compatible with spiking neurons,
- binary synapses (<2 bits) with block constraints,
- topology that respects strict hardware connectivity, and
- demonstration on silicon with strong energy numbers
is original and timely. Please discuss and cite related neuromorphic deployment/training efforts (e.g., Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015; arXiv:1506.05427) and clarify differences to expectation backprop/binary networks and recent low-precision training methods.

Significance
The work advances an important practical goal: training models that map faithfully to highly energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware. The measured energy/throughput are impressive and suggest immediate relevance for embedded inference. However, the empirical evaluation is limited to MNIST and locally connected topologies; significance would be stronger with results on a more challenging dataset (e.g., CIFAR-10) or a convolutional variant using the same training principle. The paper would also benefit from clarifying practical deployment scenarios for TrueNorth (e.g., always-on keyword spotting, IoT sensing) and how the proposed method enables them relative to alternatives.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled method bridging backprop and neuromorphic constraints.
- Real hardware deployment with best-in-class energy per inference and competitive MNIST accuracy.
- Broadly applicable idea (probabilistic relaxation + sampling + ensembling) likely to generalize to other neuromorphic platforms.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited evaluation scope (MNIST only) and reliance on large ensembles for SOTA accuracy.
- Missing experimental comparisons to alternative training-to-SNN pipelines under matched constraints.
- Incomplete implementation details limit reproducibility and independent validation.

Requests to authors
1) Provide all missing implementation details listed under Clarity for reproducibility.
2) Add controlled comparisons to at least one alternative (e.g., conversion-based SNN [11–12]) on the same topology/connectivity and report accuracy/energy differences.
3) Empirically validate the gradient approximation (with/without σ−3 term) and the Gaussian assumption (e.g., comparing probit vs. exact Bernoulli convolution or Monte Carlo).
4) Clarify deployment scenarios and discuss why this approach is preferable (accuracy/energy/latency) to train-then-constrain baselines in those settings.
5) Add related work and citations: Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015; arXiv:1506.05427, and discuss differences.
6) If feasible, include a small convolutional experiment or a second dataset to demonstrate broader applicability.

Overall evaluation
A strong and well-written contribution with a clean idea, solid engineering, and compelling hardware measurements. With strengthened comparisons and fuller implementation details, this would be a compelling NIPS paper bridging machine learning and neuromorphic systems. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing reproducibility and comparison gaps.
