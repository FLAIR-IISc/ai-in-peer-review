This paper addresses the crucial challenge of bridging the gap between the algorithmic power of backpropagation and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing. The authors propose a novel approach to train spiking neural networks using backpropagation by representing spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities. This allows leveraging established backpropagation techniques while maintaining compatibility with neuromorphic hardware constraints. The method was successfully applied to the MNIST dataset and deployed on the TrueNorth chip, demonstrating strong performance with varying levels of accuracy and energy efficiency.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in both deep learning and neuromorphic computing. Backpropagation (Rumelhart et al., 1986) has become the dominant training paradigm for deep neural networks, achieving state-of-the-art results across various domains (LeCun et al., 1998; Hinton & Salakhutdinov, 2006). Simultaneously, neuromorphic chips like TrueNorth (Merolla et al., 2014), SpiNNaker (Painkras et al., 2013), and Neurogrid (Benjamin et al., 2014) offer significant energy advantages through event-driven computation and low-precision synapses. Previous attempts to combine these paradigms have focused on training spiking networks with high-precision weights (Diehl et al., 2015; Cao et al., 2015) or adapting continuous networks to binary synapses (Cheng et al., 2015; Zhao et al., 1996). The authors differentiate their work by simultaneously addressing all constraints – spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and limited connectivity – within a backpropagation framework. This is a significant step towards realizing the full potential of neuromorphic computing for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is well-motivated and technically sound. The probabilistic representation of spikes and synapses provides a clever solution to adapt backpropagation to the discrete nature of neuromorphic hardware. The derivation of the gradient update rule appears correct and the use of Gaussian approximations is justified.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The explanation of the method, network topology, and experimental setup is detailed and easy to follow. Figures and tables are effectively used to illustrate key concepts and results.
*   **Originality:** The combination of probabilistic backpropagation, constrained connectivity, and deployment on a neuromorphic chip is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field. The “constrain-then-train” approach is a thoughtful and potentially advantageous strategy.
*   **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. Enabling efficient training of spiking neural networks unlocks a pathway to deploying deep learning models on energy-constrained platforms. The demonstrated results on the TrueNorth chip showcase the feasibility and potential of this approach. 

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Deployment:** While the paper demonstrates impressive results, it lacks a thorough discussion of the practical applications and deployment scenarios that would particularly benefit from this approach. The significance section could be strengthened by outlining specific use cases where TrueNorth’s unique capabilities are crucial.
*   **Insufficient Comparison to Alternatives:** The paper mentions related work but doesn’t provide a sufficiently detailed comparison to existing methods, such as those cited in [9]–[12]. A more nuanced analysis of the trade-offs between different approaches would be beneficial.  The authors should consider citing recent work like Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015) and arXiv:1506.05427 which explore related concepts.
*   **Lack of Implementation Details:**  Reproducibility is hindered by the lack of crucial implementation details. The encoding of inputs into spikes (beyond a mention of sampling) needs to be described more clearly. Details on spike frequencies, the precise shared weight values used, and the specific neuron models employed are essential for allowing others to replicate the results. 
*   **Insufficient Parameter Details:** The network initialization procedure, particularly the choice of synaptic strength values, deserves more detailed explanation. How was the balance between positive and negative weights ensured, and what impact does this have on performance?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for training spiking neural networks using backpropagation.
*   The results on MNIST and TrueNorth demonstrate the feasibility and potential of the approach.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, making it accessible to a broad audience.
*   This work addresses a significant challenge in the field of neuromorphic computing and has the potential to enable a new generation of energy-efficient intelligent systems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of detailed implementation information hinders reproducibility.
*   The paper does not fully articulate the practical applications and benefits of this approach beyond demonstrating performance on MNIST.
*   The comparison to existing methods is not comprehensive enough to fully assess the contributions of this work.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and demonstrates promising results. The clarity of the writing and the well-defined experimental setup are particularly commendable. I recommend **acceptance**, but with the strong encouragement that the authors address the identified weaknesses, particularly by providing more implementation details and elaborating on the practical implications of their work. Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
