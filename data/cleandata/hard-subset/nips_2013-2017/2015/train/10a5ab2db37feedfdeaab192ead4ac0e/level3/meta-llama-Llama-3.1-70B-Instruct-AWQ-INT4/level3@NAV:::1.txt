The paper "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing" presents a novel approach to train neural networks that can be directly mapped to neuromorphic hardware with spiking neurons and low-precision synapses. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the MNIST dataset, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy and energy efficiency on the TrueNorth chip.

The main idea of the paper is to treat spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, allowing the use of standard backpropagation for training. The authors propose a "constrain-then-train" methodology, where the network is first constrained to provide a direct representation of the deployment system, and then trained within those constraints.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach. The results are impressive, with the highest overall performance of 99.42% accuracy achieved with a 30-core trained network using a 64-member ensemble.

However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty and originality of the approach. The algorithm presented in Section 3.2 is deeply similar to and effectively a hybrid of refs [14] and [15], derived using identical approximations. The authors should acknowledge this and provide a clearer explanation of how their approach differs from previous contributions.

Additionally, the discussion's first paragraph gives the impression that the "constrain-then-train" methodology is novel, despite it being suggested and tested in ref [15]. The novelty claim (i) in the introduction is also misleading, as ref [15]'s "expectation backpropagation" is a backprop update using expectation propagation for binary neurons and weights and can accommodate constrained connectivity.

The paper also cites incorrect prior state-of-the-art, as ref [15] achieved the best-known results for binary weights/neurons on MNIST without data augmentation.

Despite these concerns, the experimental results (accuracy and power) are strong and novel, and the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. The authors' approach has the potential to be adapted to other gradient-based learning methods and can be used with other spiking neuromorphic hardware.

In conclusion, while the paper has some limitations regarding novelty and originality, it provides a significant contribution to the field and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. I would recommend accepting the paper, but with revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The discussion's first paragraph gives the impression that the "constrain-then-train" methodology is novel, despite it being suggested and tested in ref [15].
* The novelty claim (i) in the introduction is misleading.
* The paper cites incorrect prior state-of-the-art.

Recommendations for revisions:

* Acknowledge the similarity between the proposed algorithm and refs [14] and [15].
