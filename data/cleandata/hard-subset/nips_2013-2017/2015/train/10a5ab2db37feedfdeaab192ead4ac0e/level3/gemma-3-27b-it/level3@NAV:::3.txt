## Review of "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing"

This paper presents a backpropagation training method tailored for spiking neural networks deployed on hardware platforms with discrete synapses, specifically the TrueNorth chip. The core idea – treating spikes and discrete synapses as probabilities to enable standard backpropagation – is timely and addresses a crucial challenge in bridging the gap between the algorithmic advantages of deep learning and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and generally easy to follow.

The proposed method is particularly valuable given the increasing availability of new neuromorphic hardware platforms with inherent constraints. Focusing training to account for these constraints, rather than attempting to map an already-trained network onto the hardware, is a sensible approach. The authors demonstrate reasonable performance on the MNIST dataset, achieving competitive accuracy with significantly reduced energy consumption, especially when considering ensembles of varying sizes. 

However, several points require clarification and refinement. The introductory framing in Section 1 risks misrepresenting the novelty of the work; using spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses is not a new concept, and the paper should emphasize the *combination* of these elements with a hardware-aware backpropagation training method as the primary contribution. The demonstration on TrueNorth should be presented as a validation of the training method, not a separate claim of novelty. 

Furthermore, the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" in Section 2 needs elaboration. The network topology is described piecemeal throughout the paper and should be consolidated into a single, easily referenced diagram or section. Reporting the training time overhead incurred by the probabilistic synaptic connection updates is crucial for assessing practical applicability. The significantly worse performance of the 30-core network compared to the 5-core network warrants a detailed explanation, as does the consistency of results across different ensembles of the 5-core network. 

The paper would benefit from explicitly discussing and comparing the proposed method to other spiking backpropagation rules, such as SpikeProp. Finally, while hardware-aware training is important, the heavy reliance on TrueNorth-specific constraints may limit generalizability. The use of a 64-ensemble increases the hardware resource requirements, potentially diminishing the practical benefits for resource-constrained applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant challenge in neuromorphic computing.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Demonstrates promising results on a relevant hardware platform.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Overstated novelty claims in the introduction.
*   Lack of clarity regarding certain implementation details (e.g., synapse precision, training time).
*   Limited discussion of related work and generalizability.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. Addressing the highlighted weaknesses would strengthen the manuscript and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
