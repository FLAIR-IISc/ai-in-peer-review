- Presents a method to train networks directly mappable to spiking neuromorphic hardware with low-precision synapses.  
- Treats spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities to enable standard backpropagation.  
- Proposes a "constrain-then-train" methodology: constrain the network to deployment specs, then train within those constraints.  
- Paper is well written with clear, detailed explanations.  
- Strong empirical results on MNIST, reporting 99.42% accuracy using a 30-core, 64-member ensemble on TrueNorth with high energy efficiency.  
- Experimental accuracy and power measurements are novel and a significant contribution to neuromorphic computing.  
- Method has potential to be adapted to other gradient-based learning methods and other spiking hardware.  
- Major novelty concerns: Section 3.2 algorithm is highly similar to and effectively a hybrid of refs [14] and [15] using identical approximations.  
- The paper fails to sufficiently acknowledge and distinguish its approach from prior work, especially refs [14] and [15].  
- The "constrain-then-train" methodology is presented as novel but was suggested and tested in ref [15].  
- Intro novelty claim (i) is misleading because ref [15]'s expectation backprop already provides similar backprop updates for binary neurons/weights and constrained connectivity.  
- Paper cites incorrect prior state-of-the-art: ref [15] reportedly already achieved best-known binary-weights/neurons MNIST results without augmentation.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions to address novelty and citation issues.  
- Required revisions: acknowledge similarity to refs [14]/[15], clarify differences, correct discussion of "constrain-then-train" novelty, and fix incorrect prior-state citations.
