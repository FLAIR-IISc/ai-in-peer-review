Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a training methodology that reconciles backpropagation with neuromorphic deployment constraints (spiking neurons, low-precision/binary synapses, limited fan-in). The key idea is to represent spikes and synaptic connectivity as continuous probabilities in [0,1], train these parameters offline via backprop by using a Gaussian/probit approximation to the thresholded sum of Bernoulli inputs, and then map to hardware by sampling binary synapses and input spikes, optionally forming an ensemble by sampling multiple times and averaging outputs. The network topology respects TrueNorth core-level constraints via sliding windows with sparse, progressive mixing of inputs. The authors demonstrate two MNIST systems (5-core and 30-core), report accuracy/energy trade-offs across ensemble sizes, and achieve compelling energy efficiency on TrueNorth.

This approach closely relates to probabilistic/backprop treatments of binary neurons/weights, notably “stochastic bit stream neural networks” [14] and “expectation backpropagation” (EBP) for binary multilayer networks [15]. Section 3.2’s forward pass uses a Gaussian approximation to sums of Bernoulli variables and a probit nonlinearity (erf/CCDF), and the backward pass differentiates that approximation to obtain gradients—this is essentially the EBP/EP-derived surrogate-gradient recipe used in [15] (and is conceptually very similar to [14]). The novelty here lies less in the learning rule and more in (i) tailoring the network topology and weight-blocking to TrueNorth constraints, (ii) a practical mapping and ensembling procedure that yields excellent accuracy/energy trade-offs on hardware, and (iii) a careful weight-initialization scheme that equalizes s-values and balances columns to reduce redundancy.

Strengths
- Strong experimental results on real neuromorphic hardware, with clear and impressive energy numbers (e.g., 0.268 μJ/classification at 92.7%; 4 μJ/classification at 95% at 1 kHz throughput), and competitive accuracy when using ensembles.
- A complete pipeline from training to deployment, including constrained topology, initialization under hardware weight-blocking, and a practical mapping/ensembling strategy.
- Clear articulation of the constrain-then-train perspective and empirical evidence that it works well under TrueNorth constraints.
- Sensible engineering choices (balanced s-values, progressive mixing topology) that respect hardware limitations.

Weaknesses and points for correction
- Overstated novelty of the learning algorithm. The Section 3.2 derivation (Gaussian approximation to weighted Bernoulli sums, probit activation, differentiating the CCDF) and the idea of training continuous probabilities that are sampled to binary connectivity are deeply similar to EBP [15] and closely related to [14]. Please explicitly acknowledge this connection, clarify what is new relative to those works, and tone down claim (i) in the Introduction and the first paragraph of the Discussion.
- The paper gives the impression that “constrain-then-train” is novel; however, a closely related philosophy and backprop-compatible probabilistic treatment with binary neurons/weights was suggested and empirically tested in [15], and can accommodate constrained connectivity. Please revise the Discussion accordingly.
- Prior state-of-the-art comparisons need to be corrected/clarified. For binary neurons/weights on MNIST, [15] reported strong results; your stated SOTA baselines and attributions are not fully accurate. Also, your best accuracy (99.42%) leverages ensembling and data augmentation; comparisons to prior single-model, no-augmentation results should be separated and labeled clearly. Please report single-model accuracy (no ensemble), with/without augmentation, and compare like-for-like protocols.
- Methodological details that merit further analysis: (a) justification of dropping the σ^3 term in Eq. 4; an ablation showing the impact of this approximation on training would strengthen the technical case, (b) sensitivity to the independence and Gaussian assumptions (e.g., what happens at small fan-in or with correlated inputs?), (c) the effect of learning vs. fixing s-values; a constrained co-optimization experiment would be informative, (d) calibration of the probit/CCDF loss vs. empirical spike sampling at test time.
- The network topology is reasonable and hardware-aware, but comparisons to standard convolutional/topologies with similar parameter budgets (within TrueNorth constraints) would help understand whether the accuracy gains are due to ensembling or architectural choices.

Quality
- The system is well-engineered and technically sound overall. The gradient approximation is standard in this literature, but its specific simplifications should be empirically justified. The hardware measurements appear careful (including leakage allocation). The experimental section would benefit from ablations (ensemble size vs. accuracy variability, effect of data augmentation, effect of initialization scheme), and clearer apples-to-apples baselines.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. It would help to explicitly name the nonlinearity as a probit surrogate and to make the relation to EBP/EP explicit in Section 3.2. Please tidy the equations for readability (e.g., Eq. 3 formatting) and specify whether dropout is disabled at test. Provide confidence intervals or standard deviations over multiple sampling runs.

Originality
- Algorithmic originality is limited relative to [14,15]; the main novelty is the integration with TrueNorth constraints, initialization under weight-blocking, and the hardware demonstration with compelling energy metrics. Please revise claims accordingly.

Significance
- High for the neuromorphic computing community: this is a strong demonstration that backprop-trained probabilistic binary networks can be deployed efficiently on spiking hardware with state-of-the-art energy. The results are likely to influence practitioners seeking deployable training recipes for constrained neuromorphic platforms.

Suggestions for improvement
- Acknowledge and precisely delineate differences to [14,15]; adjust novelty claims (Intro claim (i), Discussion).
- Correct and clarify SOTA comparisons; report single-model/no-augmentation numbers and ensemble/augmentation numbers separately.
- Provide ablations on: ensemble size; inclusion/exclusion of data augmentation; effect of initialization; impact of the σ^3 term; learned vs. fixed s-values (even if on a smaller network).
- Add comparisons to alternative surrogate-gradient methods (e.g., straight-through estimator) if feasible.
- Discuss portability to other neuromorphic platforms beyond TrueNorth.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, novel hardware results with excellent energy efficiency and solid accuracy.
- End-to-end, practically useful pipeline bridging backprop and spiking neuromorphic constraints.
- Clear relevance to NIPS audiences interested in efficient ML and neuromorphic computing.

Arguments against acceptance
- Overstated novelty of the core learning method; insufficient acknowledgment of close prior work [14,15].
- Some experimental comparisons are not apples-to-apples; limited ablation/analysis of key approximations.
