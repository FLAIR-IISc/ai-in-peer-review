This paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks using backpropagation that is tailored to the constraints of spiking-neuron, discrete-synapse platforms. The authors propose a learning rule and network topology that reconciles the apparent incompatibility between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware. The essence of the learning rule is to train a network offline with hardware-supported connectivity, as well as continuous-valued input, neuron output, and synaptic weights, but values constrained to the range [0, 1]. These values correspond to probabilities of a spike occurring or of a particular synapse being "on", providing a means of mapping the training network to the deployment network, while providing a continuous and differentiable space for backpropagation.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it a pleasure to read. The authors provide a clear and concise overview of the proposed approach and its advantages. The demonstration of the method on the TrueNorth (TN) hardware is presented as a validation of the approach, which is a good way to show the practical applicability of the method.

However, there are some points that need clarification or improvement. The claims in Section 1 are worded in a way that may wrongly imply novelty of using spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses. The authors should reword these claims to avoid giving a misleading impression. Additionally, the explanation of the network topology is scattered throughout the paper and could be consolidated into a single, clear description.

The authors report impressive results on the MNIST dataset, achieving 99.42% accuracy with a 64-ensemble network. However, they do not provide information on how much longer training takes due to probabilistic synaptic connection updates. This information would be useful in understanding the trade-offs of the proposed approach. Furthermore, the authors do not explain why the single ensemble 30-core network performs much worse than the 5-core network, or why different ensembles of the 5-core network yield similar results.

The authors also do not provide input spike rates during testing and do not clarify whether inputs are spike trains. This information is important in understanding the behavior of the network and how it processes inputs. Additionally, the authors mention Ref. 12 as an example of a train-then-constrain approach, but do not note that this reference enforces constraints (e.g., bias=0) during training, making it not purely a train-then-constrain approach.

The paper could also benefit from a discussion or comparison with other spiking backprop rules, such as SpikeProp. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach and its place in the broader context of spiking neural networks.

In terms of significance, the paper highlights the importance of hardware-aware training, which is a crucial aspect of developing efficient and effective neural networks. However, the TN-specific constraints may not generalize to other platforms, and using a 64-ensemble increases hardware resource needs for target accuracy. Therefore, the authors should discuss the implications of these constraints and the potential limitations of the proposed approach.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and interesting approach to training neural networks using backpropagation that is tailored to the constraints of spiking-neuron, discrete-synapse platforms. While there are some points that need clarification or improvement, the paper is well-written and provides a clear and concise overview of the proposed approach.
