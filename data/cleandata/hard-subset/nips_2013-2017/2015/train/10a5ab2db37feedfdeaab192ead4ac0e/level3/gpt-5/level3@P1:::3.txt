This paper proposes a “constrain-then-train” backpropagation method that treats spikes and discrete synapses as probabilities in a continuous, differentiable space, enabling training with standard backprop while respecting neuromorphic hardware constraints (spiking neurons, binary synapses, limited fan-in/out, and core-level connectivity). The learned probabilities are sampled to produce one or more binary spiking networks that map directly to TrueNorth (TN), and ensemble averaging across sampled networks improves accuracy. The authors also propose a progressive-mixing topology compatible with TN’s core-to-core communication. On MNIST, they report 99.42% with a 30-core network using a 64-member ensemble (108 μJ/image) and 92.7% with a 5-core single-network configuration (0.268 μJ/image), both running at 1 kHz.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself against (i) train-then-constrain pipelines for spiking conversion and low-precision weights, (ii) spiking networks trained with backprop variants but typically with higher-precision synapses, and (iii) binary/trinary synapse/neurons with full connectivity. Compared to Diehl et al. [12] and related conversion methods, the present work enforces constraints during training and optimizes directly in the probabilistic domain for the exact hardware topology and precision. It differs from SpikeProp and timing-based spiking backprop (e.g., Bohte et al., 2002) by modeling firing probabilities rather than precise spike times, which aligns with TN’s discrete-time, rate-coded operating point. The combination of spiking neurons, sub-trinary synapse precision (binary crossbar + amortized lookups), and constrained connectivity trained end-to-end for a specific neuromorphic substrate is the main contribution.

Strengths
- Timely, clear formulation that bridges deep learning backprop with energy-efficient neuromorphic execution via a principled probabilistic surrogate.
- Hardware-aware training that respects connectivity, precision, and neuron dynamics, with a clean mapping to TN.
- Solid engineering: progressive mixing topology; initialization that balances weights and minimizes pairwise redundancy; gradient approximation justified and efficient.
- Real-time on-chip results with compelling energy-accuracy trade-offs; careful measurement methodology.
- Readable and well-organized overall.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Novelty claims in Section 1 can be read as implying first use of spiking neurons and low-precision synapses with backprop. Please reword to emphasize the specific combination (spikes + sub-trinary synapses + constrained connectivity + end-to-end backprop for TN), and acknowledge prior constrained training (e.g., [12] is not purely train-then-constrain; it enforces constraints like bias/threshold balancing during training).
- Present the TN deployment as validation of the method rather than an independent novelty claim; the key advance is the training approach.
- “0.15 bits per synapse” (Sec. 2) should be explicitly described as amortized: each neuron stores 4 signed 9-bit s-values (36 bits) used by 256 incoming synapses via axon-type indexing, adding ~36/256 ≈ 0.14–0.15 bits per synapse in addition to the 1-bit crossbar.
- Network topology is scattered across sections/figures. Provide a single consolidated description (e.g., a small table) listing R, S, F, fan-in, neuron counts per layer/core, and feature selection policy.
- Report training overhead: wall-clock training time and epochs to convergence relative to a comparable unconstrained baseline, and the impact of the gradient approximation (dropping the σ^−3 term) on convergence/accuracy.
- The accuracy-energy plot suggests anomalous behavior: explain why the 30-core single ensemble performs worse than the 5-core single ensemble (if this is the case in your experiments). Hypotheses include higher sampling variance in larger, deeper probabilistic networks, mismatch between learned probabilities and sampled binaries without ensembling, or overfitting; please diagnose with ablations (e.g., variance across multiple samplings, calibration of c̃ij, depth sensitivity).
- Relatedly, explain why different ensemble sizes for the 5-core network yield similar accuracy. Is this due to limited model capacity, low diversity across sampled models (e.g., correlated sampling or shared topology constraints), or saturation from rate-coded single-tick inputs? Reporting variance across ensembles (mean±std over seeds) would help.
- Input encoding: specify the mapping from pixel intensity to spike probability per tick, the effective input spike rate, and whether inputs are single-tick Bernoulli spikes or spike trains over multiple ticks. Clarify if any temporal integration is used in neurons beyond the thresholding at 1 ms.
- Add discussion of spiking backprop variants (e.g., SpikeProp/timing-based rules, surrogate gradient methods) and articulate trade-offs vs your probability-based surrogate (e.g., ease of mapping to rate-coded hardware, precision requirements, gradient stability).
- Minor: fix the “approach approach” typo; ensure consistent terminology for deployment vs training networks.

Quality
- Methodologically sound with clear derivations, sensible approximations, and a consistent training-to-deployment mapping. The initialization rationale (inner-product minimization, balance) is thoughtful. More empirical analysis of the gradient approximation, training overhead, and sampling variance would strengthen the technical case.

Clarity
- Generally well-written. Consolidating the topology, clarifying the amortized bits, input encoding, and reframing novelty claims would further improve readability. Include a brief algorithm box for training/mapping.

Originality
- The probabilistic backprop formulation tailored to binary synapses, spiking neurons, and constrained TN connectivity in a single end-to-end pipeline is novel in combination and execution, though individual ingredients have precedents. The emphasis on sub-trinary effective precision is noteworthy.

Significance
- Hardware-aware training for neuromorphic substrates is important. The demonstrated energy/throughput numbers are compelling; however, reliance on large ensembles to reach top accuracy increases resource needs and may limit applicability in embedded settings. Discuss generalization to other neuromorphic platforms and to convolutional/topologically different networks; adding a non-MNIST dataset would further bolster impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled bridge between backprop and neuromorphic constraints with strong on-chip validation and energy efficiency.
- Practical recipe (training, initialization, mapping) likely to be adopted by the community working on spiking hardware.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical scope limited to MNIST; dependence on large ensembles for state-of-the-art accuracy raises resource concerns.
- Some claims overstate novelty; several clarifications and ablations are missing.

Overall: A solid and timely contribution. With clarified novelty framing, added analysis of training overhead/sampling variance, and improved presentation of topology and input encoding, I lean toward acceptance.
