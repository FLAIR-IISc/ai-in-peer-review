This paper proposes a principled bridge between high-precision backpropagation and spike-based neuromorphic hardware by treating activations and synaptic connections as probabilities that are sampled to produce binary spikes and synapses at deployment. The authors adapt backpropagation to operate on a Gaussian-probabilistic surrogate of spiking dynamics and learn connection probabilities and biases under TrueNorth-like constraints (binary synapses, low-precision shared s-values, limited fan-in). Trained networks are mapped to hardware via independent sampling and optionally ensembled. The method is validated on MNIST, with two TrueNorth network configurations trading accuracy for energy, and chip-measured energy/throughput figures reported. Relative to prior “train-then-constrain” conversions to SNNs (e.g., Diehl et al.; Cao et al.) and binary/stochastic networks (e.g., Cheng et al.; Zhao et al.; Müller & Indiveri), the novelty lies in an end-to-end “constrain-then-train” probabilistic formulation that simultaneously embraces spiking neurons, binary synapses, and constrained connectivity, and in reporting on-chip measurements.

Quality: The technical development is sound and the Gaussian approximation is well-motivated. Results on MNIST are strong and supported by chip measurements. However, important implementation details are missing for reproducibility and fair comparison: precise input-to-spike encoding (rate vs. temporal coding, number of time steps, spike frequencies), neuron model parameters (leak, threshold, reset, refractory), s-value tables/axon-type assignments and shared-weight schemes, and whether ensemble members share parameters besides sampled connectivity. A clearer head-to-head against refs [9]–[12] (same dataset, comparable model size, with energy and accuracy) would strengthen claims.

Clarity: The manuscript is exceptionally well written and organized. Please add citations to Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015) and arXiv:1506.05427, and explicitly position against those works.

Originality: High. The probabilistic backprop formulation for binary synapses on spiking hardware with constrained connectivity is a notable advance and is broadly applicable across neuromorphic platforms.

Significance: High potential, but the paper underplays the practical deployment story. Please articulate concrete application scenarios where TrueNorth’s latency/throughput/energy regime is decisive (e.g., always-on sensing, mobile/edge). If you have chip-measured data on a second architecture, clarify and include; otherwise state that results are TrueNorth-only.

Pros:
- Clear, elegant probabilistic training compatible with spiking hardware constraints
- Strong MNIST accuracy/energy trade-offs with on-chip measurements
- Broad applicability beyond a single platform

Cons:
- Missing implementation details impede reproducibility
- Limited comparative evaluation versus alternatives [9]–[12]
- Practical deployment scenarios and significance on TrueNorth insufficiently discussed

Recommendation: Accept, contingent on addressing the missing details and strengthening comparisons and deployment context.
