- Proposes training DNNs for IBM TrueNorth by treating neuron activations and synapses as probabilities in [0,1], using modified backprop and sampling to obtain spiking network instances; ensembles improve performance.

- Addresses the mismatch between continuous backprop (high-precision activations/weights) and spiking, low-precision neuromorphic hardware.

- Core probabilistic training maps to hardware by sampling deterministic spiking networks, enabling deployment on TrueNorth.

- Relates closely to prior work on binary stochastic neurons and expectation backprop (e.g., Zhao et al. 1996; Cheng et al. 2015).

- Strength — significance: tackles an important ML and computer-architecture problem enabling low-power, embedded deep learning.

- Strength — experiments: strong practical demonstration on TrueNorth with state-of-the-art hardware results (e.g., 99.42% MNIST with ensemble; 0.268 μJ/image at 92.7% with a single network).

- Strength — clarity: well-written, with an intuitive "constrain-then-train" presentation and supportive figures/tables.

- Weakness — novelty overstated: the probabilistic backprop derivation and Gaussian approximation closely mirror existing expectation backprop methods.

- Weakness — misrepresentation of prior work: claims of being first for offline backprop with spiking, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity are misleading given [15].

- Weakness — incorrect comparisons: the paper cites an inaccurate state-of-the-art MNIST baseline and fails to fairly compare with results from [15].

- Weakness — framing/completeness: primary contribution is strong engineering/applicative work for hardware rather than a novel training algorithm; paper should be reframed accordingly.

- For acceptance: provides a complete, influential, energy-efficient system demonstration on real neuromorphic hardware.

- Against acceptance: serious omissions in citation and overclaiming of algorithmic novelty undermine the scientific contribution unless corrected.

- Recommendation: accept for its systems-level results but revise to properly acknowledge related work, tone down novelty claims, and focus the paper on its engineering contributions.
