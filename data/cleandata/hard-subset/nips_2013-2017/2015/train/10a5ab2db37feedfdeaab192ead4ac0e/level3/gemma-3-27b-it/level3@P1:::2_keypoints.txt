- Problem & contribution: Proposes training spiking neural networks with backprop by representing spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, enabling compatibility with neuromorphic hardware.  
- Experimental validation: Method applied to MNIST and deployed on the TrueNorth chip, showing strong performance and energy-efficiency trade-offs.  
- Novelty: Simultaneously addresses spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and limited connectivity within a backpropagation framework; introduces a “constrain-then-train” approach.  
- Technical soundness: Probabilistic representation and gradient derivation are well-motivated and appear correct; Gaussian approximations justified.  
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and uses figures/tables effectively.  
- Significance: Work could enable energy-efficient deployment of deep models on neuromorphic platforms and has substantial potential impact.  
- Limited deployment discussion: Paper lacks detailed discussion of practical applications and scenarios that uniquely benefit from TrueNorth.  
- Insufficient comparisons: Related work comparisons are not detailed enough; missing citation of relevant recent papers (e.g., Qiao 2015, arXiv:1506.05427).  
- Reproducibility gaps: Key implementation details are missing (input spike encoding, spike frequencies, shared weight values, neuron models).  
- Parameter detail gaps: Network initialization and synaptic strength choices (including balance of positive/negative weights) are insufficiently explained.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, technically sound approach with demonstrated feasibility on hardware and clear presentation.  
- Arguments for rejection: Reproducibility concerns, limited practical application discussion, and inadequate comparison to alternatives.  
- Overall recommendation: Recommend acceptance contingent on addressing weaknesses—provide implementation details and elaborate practical implications to strengthen impact.
