

This paper presents a compelling approach to bridge the gap between backpropagation-based deep learning and neuromorphic hardware by treating spike probabilities and discrete synapses as continuous values during training. The authors propose a "constrain-then-train" methodology where they first design a network topology compatible with neuromorphic hardware (spiking neurons, discrete synapses, limited connectivity), then train using standard backpropagation on continuous-valued representations of these probabilistic elements. The key innovation lies in modeling neuron activations using Gaussian approximations of Bernoulli sums, enabling backpropagation through the probabilistic representation. The trained networks are then mapped to hardware by sampling the continuous probabilities to create binary synapses and spike patterns, with ensemble averaging improving performance.

The work builds on previous efforts in probabilistic backpropagation (e.g., [14,15]) and spiking neural network training (e.g., [9-12]), but makes several significant advances. First, it demonstrates the first end-to-end training methodology compatible with spiking neurons, ultra-low-precision synapses (effectively <2 bits), and constrained connectivity. Second, it achieves state-of-the-art MNIST accuracy (99.42%) for spiking networks, surpassing previous approaches using high-precision synapses (99.12%) [12] or binary synapses (97.88%) [15]. Third, it provides hardware-measured performance on TrueNorth, achieving 108 μJ per image (99.42% accuracy) and 0.268 μJ per image (92.7% accuracy) - significantly better than other low-power implementations (e.g., 4 μJ per classification at 95% accuracy [16]).

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The training methodology is well-motivated and thoroughly explained, with clear mathematical derivations for the Gaussian approximation and gradient calculations. The network topology design (using a sliding window approach with progressive mixing) elegantly accommodates TrueNorth's connectivity constraints. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, with detailed explanations of the hardware mapping procedure, initialization strategy for synaptic weights, and experimental setup. The results are convincing, with comprehensive energy-accuracy tradeoff analysis across different ensemble sizes.

However, the paper could strengthen its significance claims and comparative analysis. While it correctly notes that the "constrain-then-train" approach offers advantages over "train-then-constrain" methods, it doesn't sufficiently elaborate on why this matters for practical deployment. The authors should clarify which specific real-world applications (beyond MNIST) would benefit from this approach, and how the energy efficiency gains translate to actual embedded systems with constrained power budgets. The significance section would be greatly improved by discussing concrete deployment scenarios like edge devices for autonomous vehicles or mobile health monitoring.

The paper also lacks sufficient comparison with alternative methods. While it references [9-12], it doesn't explicitly compare the proposed method's energy efficiency and accuracy tradeoffs with these approaches. A table summarizing key metrics (accuracy, energy per image, hardware requirements) across different methods would strengthen the argument for the paper's contribution. The authors should address how their approach differs from Qiao et al. [Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015] and arXiv:1506.05427, which the reviewer recommends citing.

Implementation details critical for reproducibility are missing. The input-to-spike conversion method, spike frequency parameters, and exact neuron models used in TrueNorth deployment aren't sufficiently explained. The paper mentions "independent random number draws" for sampling, but doesn't specify the distribution or how this affects performance. Details about the specific TrueNorth configuration (e.g., axon types, synaptic strength values) would help readers understand the hardware constraints.

The paper's originality is strong, as it successfully integrates multiple challenges (probabilistic training, constrained connectivity, ultra-low-precision synapses) in a novel way. The significance is substantial for the neuromorphic computing community, though the authors could better articulate its broader impact. The quality is excellent, with rigorous theoretical grounding and thorough experimental validation.

Strengths: Novel "constrain-then-train" methodology; state-of-the-art MNIST results for spiking networks; comprehensive hardware validation; high-quality manuscript; clear mathematical derivations.

Weaknesses: Insufficient discussion of practical deployment scenarios; limited comparison with related work; missing implementation details for reproducibility; underdeveloped significance argument.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to bridging the algorithmic and hardware gaps in neuromorphic computing. While the significance could be better articulated, the technical contribution is strong and well-executed. I recommend acceptance with the following minor revisions: 1) Add citations to Qiao et al. (2015) and arXiv:1506.05427; 2) Elaborate on practical deployment scenarios and comparative advantages; 3) Provide more details about input encoding, spike frequencies, and hardware configuration parameters. The work represents an important step toward energy-efficient brain-inspired computing systems.
