This paper presents a backpropagation training method for spiking neural networks designed with the constraints of neuromorphic hardware, specifically the TrueNorth chip, in mind. The core idea of treating spikes and discrete synapses as probabilities to enable standard backpropagation is timely and interesting, addressing a key challenge in bridging the gap between algorithmic advances in deep learning and energy-efficient hardware implementations. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

However, the presentation of novelty could be improved. Section 1 frames the work as a fundamentally new approach, potentially overstating its originality. While the specific combination of techniques and focus on hardware-awareness is valuable, the paper would benefit from acknowledging the existing body of work on backpropagation with spiking neurons (e.g., [9, 10, 11, 12]) and reduced-precision synapses (e.g., [13, 14, 15]) more explicitly. Presenting the demonstration on the TrueNorth chip as a validation of the proposed method, rather than an independent novelty, would strengthen this aspect. 

Several technical points require clarification. In Section 2, the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" requires more detail – is this an average over all synapses, and what is the specific breakdown of bit allocation? The network topology, while described in parts, lacks a consolidated, high-level description. A single figure or table summarizing the layer sizes, connectivity patterns (R, S values), and neuron counts would significantly improve readability. 

The impact of the probabilistic synaptic connection updates on training time is not reported. Providing this information is crucial for assessing the practical feasibility of the method. Further analysis is needed to explain the significant performance drop observed when scaling the ensemble from 5 cores to 30 cores. Understanding the cause of this degradation is vital. Conversely, the consistency of results across different ensembles of the 5-core network also warrants explanation – why do they yield similar performance? 

Regarding experimental details, the input spike rates during testing should be reported, along with clarification on whether the inputs are presented as spike trains or single spikes per time step. Additionally, a minor correction is needed: Ref. 12 does not solely “train-then-constrain”; it enforces constraints (e.g., bias=0) *during* training. The “approach approach” typo should also be fixed.

The paper would also benefit from a discussion of related spiking backpropagation rules, such as SpikeProp [22, 23], and how this work differentiates itself in terms of computational cost, hardware compatibility, and performance.

The significance of this work lies in its hardware-aware training approach, a critical step towards realizing the full potential of neuromorphic computing. However, the TN-specific constraints may limit the generalizability of the findings. Furthermore, the need for a 64-ensemble to achieve high accuracy increases hardware resource requirements, potentially offsetting some of the energy efficiency gains. A discussion of these trade-offs would provide a more balanced assessment of the method’s practicality.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical challenge in neuromorphic computing – bridging the gap between backpropagation and spiking neuron hardware.
*   Demonstrates successful training of a spiking network on a real neuromorphic chip (TrueNorth).
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Potentially significant for energy-efficient embedded systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Overstates the novelty of the approach.
*   Lacks clarity on key technical details (e.g., "0.15 bits per synapse," network topology, training time).
*   Requires further analysis to explain performance discrepancies (5-core vs. 30-core networks).
*   Generalizability limited by TN-specific constraints.
*   High ensemble size impacts hardware resource usage.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. While the presentation of novelty could be improved and some technical details require clarification, the core idea is sound and the experimental results are promising. Addressing the concerns outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
