- Proposes a "constrain-then-train" method training continuous weights/neurons in [0,1] interpreted as spiking/synapse probabilities and mapping to TrueNorth via sampling.  
- Demonstrates strong MNIST accuracy and state-of-the-art power efficiency on the TrueNorth chip.  
- Ensemble averaging boosts performance and contributes to the impressive results.  
- Network topology and mapping procedure are clearly and coherently presented.  
- Concrete implementation on TrueNorth increases credibility and practical relevance.  
- Addresses an important problem: reconciling backprop-trained networks with neuromorphic hardware for low-power computing.  
- Core methodology and algorithmic contributions strongly resemble prior work, especially references [14] and [15].  
- Section 3.2’s algorithm is highly similar to approaches in [14,15], using probabilistic binary neurons/synapses and identical gradient approximations.  
- Authors fail to adequately acknowledge the lineage to [14,15] and should explicitly state differences.  
- The paper incorrectly frames "constrain-then-train" as novel despite prior exploration of the idea in [15].  
- Claim (i) about novelty is misleading because [15] already provides an expectation backpropagation update compatible with constrained connectivity.  
- The paper inaccurately represents prior state-of-the-art: [15] achieved best published binary results on MNIST without augmentation and should be cited correctly.  
- Lacks a thorough comparison to other probabilistic backpropagation methods beyond [15].  
- Discussion of generalizability to other neuromorphic hardware is brief and needs deeper analysis of challenges.  
- Strengths justify acceptance: strong experimental results, practical demonstration on hardware, clear presentation, and relevance to an important problem.  
- Reasons against acceptance: overstated novelty, inadequate attribution to prior work, and incorrect citation of prior SOTA.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on acknowledging [14]/[15], correcting citations, moderating novelty claims, and adding more comparisons and discussion.
