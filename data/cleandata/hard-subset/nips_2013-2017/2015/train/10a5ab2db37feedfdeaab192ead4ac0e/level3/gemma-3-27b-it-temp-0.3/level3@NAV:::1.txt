## Review of "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing"

This paper addresses the critical challenge of bridging the gap between the performance of backpropagation-trained deep networks and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic hardware. The authors propose a “constrain-then-train” approach, training a network with continuous values constrained to [0,1] representing probabilities of spiking and synaptic activation, which can then be directly mapped to a spiking neuromorphic platform (TrueNorth). They demonstrate strong results on MNIST, achieving high accuracy with impressive energy efficiency, particularly with ensembles of varying sizes.

The core idea of representing discrete neuromorphic elements as probabilities and training with standard backpropagation is a reasonable approach to enable the deployment of powerful networks on energy-constrained hardware. The experimental validation on TrueNorth is a significant strength, demonstrating the practical feasibility of the proposed methodology. The reported accuracy and power numbers are compelling and represent a valuable contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing. 

However, the paper significantly overstates its novelty. Section 3.2’s algorithm is remarkably similar to, and effectively a hybrid of, the approaches presented in Refs [14] and [15], utilizing identical approximations for the Gaussian approximation of neuron activation. This connection *must* be explicitly acknowledged. Furthermore, the discussion’s opening paragraph incorrectly positions the “constrain-then-train” methodology as novel; Ref [15] already explored and tested this approach. The claim of novelty (i) in the introduction is also misleading. Ref [15]’s “expectation backpropagation” is, in fact, a backpropagation update using expectation propagation for binary neurons and weights, and is explicitly capable of handling constrained connectivity. 

Additionally, the paper incorrectly cites prior state-of-the-art results. Ref [15] achieved the best-known results for binary weights/neurons on MNIST *without* data augmentation, surpassing the performance cited in the introduction. While the authors’ experimental results are strong, the overall methodology and algorithmic contributions are overstated. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of its relationship to existing work and a more accurate portrayal of its contributions.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Strong experimental results demonstrating high accuracy and low power consumption on a real neuromorphic platform.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Overstated novelty, lack of acknowledgement of closely related prior work, inaccurate citation of prior state-of-the-art.

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The experimental results are valuable, but the paper needs to be significantly revised to accurately reflect the existing literature and present a more honest assessment of its contributions. Addressing the points raised regarding novelty and related work is crucial.
