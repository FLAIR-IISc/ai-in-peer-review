

# Review: Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing

This paper presents an interesting approach to training spiking neural networks for neuromorphic hardware platforms like TrueNorth. The authors propose a "constrain-then-train" method where they treat spike probabilities and discrete synapses as continuous values during backpropagation training, then map the trained network to hardware by sampling these probabilities. The method is demonstrated on MNIST with impressive energy efficiency results.

The paper makes a timely contribution to the growing intersection of deep learning and neuromorphic computing. Previous work has explored subsets of the elements presented here: backpropagation with spiking neurons (e.g., [9-12]), or networks with low-precision synapses (e.g., [13]), but this paper is novel in combining all these elements (spiking neurons, extremely low-precision synapses, constrained connectivity) within a single training framework. The authors correctly position their work as building on prior efforts while highlighting the integration of multiple constraints that haven't been addressed together before.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and easy to read, with clear explanations of the probabilistic training approach.
- The method provides a direct mapping from training to hardware deployment, avoiding the need for post-training constraint application.
- The energy efficiency results (0.268 μJ per image for 92.7% accuracy) are impressive and demonstrate real-world applicability.
- The network topology design effectively accommodates TrueNorth's architectural constraints.

**Weaknesses:**

The main weaknesses concern clarity, novelty claims, and completeness. The novelty claims in Section 1 are overstated - the paper doesn't present the first use of spiking neurons or reduced-precision synapses (as these have been explored in prior work), but rather the first integration of all these elements with a backpropagation-compatible training method. The phrasing should be revised to clarify that the novelty lies in the *combination* of these elements within a single training framework.

The TrueNorth demonstration should be framed as validation of the method rather than a separate novelty claim. The paper's current presentation of the hardware results as novel (e.g., "achieve by far the best published power efficiency") overstates the contribution, as the hardware demonstration serves to validate the training method rather than represent a new hardware innovation.

The paper needs several clarifications: 
- The "0.15 bits per synapse" claim in Section 2 requires explanation (presumably referring to the additional bits needed for the lookup table scheme beyond the 1 bit for synapse on/off state).
- The network topology description is scattered throughout the paper; a consolidated description would improve readability.
- The paper fails to report how much longer training takes due to the probabilistic synaptic updates compared to standard backpropagation.
- The performance difference between the 30-core single ensemble (lower accuracy) and 5-core single ensemble (higher accuracy) needs explanation. The authors should clarify why a larger network with fewer ensembles performs worse than a smaller network with the same ensemble size.
- The similar results across different ensembles of the 5-core network require explanation (are these due to the robustness of the training method or the small network size?).
- Input spike rates during testing and whether inputs are spike trains need clarification.
- Reference 12 does not represent a pure "train-then-constrain" approach as claimed, since it enforces constraints during training (e.g., bias=0), and the text contains a typo ("approach approach").

The paper would benefit from a comparison with other spiking backpropagation methods like SpikeProp (e.g., [14]) to contextualize the contribution. The significance section should acknowledge that while hardware-aware training is important, the TN-specific constraints may not generalize to other neuromorphic platforms, and using 64 ensembles (for highest accuracy) increases hardware resource needs significantly (1920 cores total) compared to the 5-core network.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a technically sound and interesting approach with clear potential for real-world applications. The core contribution of combining multiple hardware constraints into a single training framework is valuable, but the current presentation overstates novelty and lacks several important details. With revisions to clarify the novelty claim, provide missing details about training time, network behavior, and input representation, and properly contextualize the work against prior art, this would be a strong contribution to the field.

**Overall assessment:** The paper is technically sound with promising results, but needs significant revisions to clarify its novelty, provide missing details, and properly contextualize the work. The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the TN-specific constraints, but the approach could be valuable for other neuromorphic platforms. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the points above.
