- Proposes a “constrain-then-train” probabilistic backprop method that treats spikes and binary synapses as continuous probabilities to train networks respecting TrueNorth (TN) hardware constraints.
- Samples learned probabilities to produce one or more binary spiking networks that map directly to TN; ensemble averaging improves accuracy.
- Introduces a progressive-mixing topology compatible with TN core-to-core communication.
- Reports MNIST results: 99.42% with a 30-core network using a 64-member ensemble (108 μJ/image) and 92.7% with a 5-core single-network (0.268 μJ/image) at 1 kHz.
- Positions work relative to prior train-then-constrain conversion methods and spiking/backprop variants, emphasizing end-to-end constrained training in the probabilistic domain.
- Main contribution is the combined pipeline: spiking neurons + sub-trinary (binary crossbar + amortized lookups) synapses + constrained TN connectivity trained end-to-end.
- Strength: timely, clear probabilistic formulation bridging backprop and energy-efficient neuromorphic execution.
- Strength: hardware-aware training that cleanly maps to TN while respecting connectivity, precision, and neuron dynamics.
- Strength: solid engineering (progressive mixing topology, balanced initialization, efficient gradient approximation) and careful on-chip energy-accuracy measurements.
- Strength: readable, well-organized presentation overall.
- Weakness: novelty claims overstate first-use of elements; should reframe as specific combination and acknowledge prior constrained training (e.g., Diehl et al.).
- Weakness: TN deployment should be presented as validation of the training approach, not an independent novelty.
- Weakness: clarify “0.15 bits per synapse” as amortized storage (36 bits shared across 256 synapses ≈ 0.14–0.15 bits) in addition to 1-bit crossbar.
- Weakness: network topology description is scattered; provide a consolidated table/list of R, S, F, fan-in, neuron/core counts, and feature selection policy.
- Weakness: report training overhead (wall-clock time, epochs to converge) and impact of the gradient approximation (dropping σ^−3) on convergence and accuracy.
- Weakness: anomalous accuracy-energy behavior—explain why 30-core single-network underperforms 5-core single-network and provide ablations (sampling variance, calibration, depth effects).
- Weakness: explain why varying ensemble sizes for 5-core yields similar accuracy—investigate model capacity, sampling diversity, and rate-coded input saturation; report variance over seeds.
- Weakness: clarify input encoding (pixel→spike probability mapping, input spike rate, single-tick vs multi-tick, temporal integration beyond 1 ms).
- Weakness: add discussion of spike-timing backprop and surrogate-gradient methods, comparing trade-offs with the probability-based surrogate.
- Minor: fix typos (“approach approach”) and ensure consistent terminology for deployment vs training networks.
- Quality: methodologically sound with clear derivations and sensible approximations; initialization rationale is thoughtful.
- Clarity: generally well-written; consolidating topology, amortized bits, input encoding, and adding an algorithm box would improve readability.
- Originality: novel in combining probabilistic backprop, binary synapses, spiking neurons, and TN constraints end-to-end, though components have precedents.
- Significance: important hardware-aware training for neuromorphic substrates with compelling energy/throughput results, but reliance on large ensembles may limit embedded applicability.
- Acceptance arguments: principled bridge between backprop and neuromorphic constraints, strong on-chip validation, and practical recipe likely useful to the community.
- Rejection arguments: limited empirical scope (MNIST) and dependence on large ensembles; several clarifications and ablations are missing.
- Overall: solid and timely contribution that would benefit from clarified novelty, additional analysis of training overhead and sampling variance, and improved presentation of topology and input encoding.
