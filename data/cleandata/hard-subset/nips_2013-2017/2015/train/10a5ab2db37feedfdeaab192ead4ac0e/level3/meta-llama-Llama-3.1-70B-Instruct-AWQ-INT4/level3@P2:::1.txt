This paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks for energy-efficient neuromorphic computing using backpropagation. The authors propose a method to reconcile the incompatibility between backpropagation, which uses continuous-output neurons and synaptic weights, and neuromorphic designs, which employ spiking neurons and discrete synapses. They achieve this by treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, allowing training the network using standard backpropagation. The trained network naturally maps to neuromorphic hardware by sampling the probabilities to create one or more networks, which are merged using ensemble averaging.

The authors demonstrate their approach by training a sparsely connected network that runs on the TrueNorth chip using the MNIST dataset. They achieve a high performance network (ensemble of 64) with 99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ per image, and a high efficiency network (ensemble of 1) with 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image. These results are strong and novel, showcasing the potential of their approach for energy-efficient neuromorphic computing.

However, the authors' claims of novelty in their methodology and algorithmic contributions are overstated. The algorithm presented in Section 3.2 is deeply similar to and effectively a hybrid of refs [14] and [15], derived using identical approximations. The authors should acknowledge this and provide a clearer comparison to existing work. Furthermore, the discussion's first paragraph gives the impression that the "constrain-then-train" methodology is novel, despite it being suggested and tested in ref [15]. The novelty claim (i) in the introduction is also misleading, as ref [15]'s "expectation backpropagation" is a backprop update using expectation propagation for binary neurons and weights and can accommodate constrained connectivity.

Additionally, the paper cites incorrect prior state-of-the-art, as ref [15] achieved the best-known results for binary weights/neurons on MNIST without data augmentation. The authors should correct this and provide a more accurate comparison to existing work.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach. The experimental results are strong, and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the trade-offs between accuracy and power efficiency. However, the paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the limitations and potential applications of their approach.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. The figures and tables are well-organized and help to illustrate the key concepts. However, some of the sections could be better organized, and the authors could provide more guidance for the reader to follow the flow of the paper.

In terms of originality, while the authors' approach is not entirely novel, they do provide a unique combination of existing techniques and demonstrate their effectiveness in a new context. The paper provides a significant contribution to the field of energy-efficient neuromorphic computing and demonstrates the potential of backpropagation for training neural networks in this domain.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of energy-efficient neuromorphic computing. The authors demonstrate a novel approach to training neural networks that can be applied to a wide range of applications, from image recognition to speech recognition. The paper provides a significant contribution to the field and has the potential to inspire new research directions.
