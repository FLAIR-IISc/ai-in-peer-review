This paper presents a timely and interesting method for training spiking neural networks that accounts for the constraints of neuromorphic hardware, such as spiking neurons and discrete-valued synapses. The core contribution is a "constrain-then-train" backpropagation approach that treats discrete events (spikes) and connections (synapses) as continuous probabilities during offline training. This allows for gradient-based optimization while ensuring direct map-ability to a target hardware platform. This work builds upon previous efforts to train spiking networks and networks with low-precision weights but is novel in integrating these severe, hardware-specific constraints directly into the training framework from the start. The method is validated on the TrueNorth chip using the MNIST dataset, demonstrating impressive energy efficiency.

The paper's primary strength is its practical and important contribution to the field of hardware-aware machine learning. By designing the training process around the hardware's limitations, the authors provide a principled way to bridge the gap between high-performance deep learning algorithms and energy-efficient neuromorphic systems. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, and the demonstration on a real hardware platform is a significant strength.

However, the manuscript could be strengthened by addressing several points.
**Clarity and Positioning:** The claims in Section 1 should be rephrased to avoid wrongly implying that using spiking neurons or reduced-precision synapses is novel in itself; the novelty is the integrated training approach. Similarly, the TrueNorth demonstration should be presented as a validation of the method, not a separate novelty claim. The network topology should be described in a single, consolidated location, and technical terms like "0.15 bits per synapse" need clarification.

**Experimental Rigor and Analysis:** For reproducibility, the authors should provide input spike rates during testing and report the computational overhead (training time) incurred by the probabilistic updates. The analysis of results also needs more depth. The paper should explain the counter-intuitive result where the single-ensemble 30-core network performs much worse than the 5-core network and why different ensembles of the 5-core network yield such similar results.

**Related Work:** The discussion should include a comparison with other spiking backpropagation rules (e.g., SpikeProp) and correct the characterization of Ref. [12], which does enforce some constraints during training.

Overall, the significance of this work is high. However, the specificity of the constraints may limit generalizability, and the reliance on a 64-member ensemble to achieve top accuracy highlights a significant hardware resource trade-off. Addressing these issues would greatly improve the quality and impact of this promising paper.
