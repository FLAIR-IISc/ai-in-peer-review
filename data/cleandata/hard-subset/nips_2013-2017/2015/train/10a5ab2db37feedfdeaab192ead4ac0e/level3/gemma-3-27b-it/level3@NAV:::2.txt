## Review of “Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing”

This paper presents a compelling approach to reconciling backpropagation with the constraints of spiking neuromorphic hardware, specifically the TrueNorth chip. The core idea—treating analog variables as probabilities sampled to produce binary synapses—is innovative and addresses a significant challenge in deploying deep learning models on energy-efficient hardware. The authors demonstrate strong results on MNIST, achieving competitive accuracy with remarkably low energy consumption when mapped onto TrueNorth, showcasing both high-performance and high-efficiency configurations. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clear, demonstrating a strong understanding of both backpropagation and neuromorphic computing principles. This work is undoubtedly original and has the potential to be broadly useful across various neuromorphic platforms.

However, while the technical execution is strong, the paper falls short in fully articulating the significance of this work beyond a demonstration on TrueNorth. The paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight *why* TrueNorth is the chosen platform or what practical deployment scenarios would particularly benefit from this methodology.  The authors mention potential applications in mobile devices but lack concrete examples.  A clearer articulation of the specific advantages of this method over existing approaches, such as those referenced in [9]–[12] – particularly regarding the trade-offs between precision, sparsity, and energy efficiency – is needed.  For instance, how does this probabilistic approach compare to binarized neural networks regarding performance and training complexity? 

Further, reproducibility is hampered by a lack of implementation details. Crucially, the method for input-to-spike conversion, typical spike frequencies, specific shared weight values employed in TrueNorth, and a more detailed description of the neuron model used (beyond the basic thresholding equation) are missing.  Clarifying how inputs are encoded as spikes would be beneficial.  

Finally, while the authors correctly cite related work, including the important contributions by Qiao et al. [Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015 and arXiv:1506.05427], the introduction and discussion could benefit from a more thorough contextualization of their approach within the wider landscape of spiking neural network training methods.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to bridging backpropagation and neuromorphic computing.
*   Strong empirical results on MNIST with impressive energy efficiency on TrueNorth.
*   Exceptionally well-written and clear manuscript.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of a compelling argument for the specific relevance of TrueNorth and practical deployment scenarios.
*   Insufficient comparison to alternative methods.
*   Missing implementation details hinder reproducibility.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential. Addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding application relevance and reproducibility, would substantially strengthen the contribution and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
