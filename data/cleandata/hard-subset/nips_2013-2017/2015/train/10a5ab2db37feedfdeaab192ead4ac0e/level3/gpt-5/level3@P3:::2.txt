Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a principled way to reconcile high-precision backpropagation with spiking neuromorphic hardware that offers only binary synapses and limited fan-in/out. The core idea is to reinterpret continuous activations and weights as probabilities: neuron outputs are firing probabilities, and synapses are “on” probabilities. Under a Gaussian approximation to weighted Bernoulli inputs, the authors derive smooth surrogates for neuron outputs and their gradients, enabling standard backpropagation to update synapse on-probabilities and biases while keeping the network exactly within hardware constraints (“constrain-then-train”). After training, the network is sampled into one or more binary/spiking instantiations; multiple samples are ensembled to trade hardware resources for accuracy. The trained networks are mapped to IBM’s TrueNorth with a topology that respects its core structure (256 shared inputs, binary crossbar, 4 axon types with shared s-values) and is designed to progressively mix inputs across layers.

Relative to prior work, this unifies strands that were previously separate: backprop for spiking neurons but with high-precision synapses [9–12], and binary/trinary synapses but continuous units or full connectivity [13–15]. The authors argue and demonstrate that training directly under the chip’s constraints avoids the brittleness of train-then-constrain pipelines (e.g., threshold balancing or weight rounding). Empirically, on MNIST they report 99.42% with a 64-member ensemble and very low energy per inference; with a small 5-core network and no ensemble they achieve 92.7% at 0.268 μJ/image. They provide chip-measured performance on TrueNorth and contextualize against published measurements on an alternative platform (SpiNNaker) to illustrate energy/throughput trade-offs.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The probabilistic relaxation and resulting gradients are clearly derived. The approximation that drops the variance-derivative term is reasonable under their balanced-weight assumption and large fan-in; an ablation to quantify its impact would strengthen the case.
- Empirical support: Results are carefully measured end-to-end on silicon, with accuracy–energy trade-offs demonstrated across two network sizes and ensemble sizes. The MNIST accuracy for spiking SNNs is state-of-the-art for the time, and the energy/throughput numbers are excellent.
- Missing details that limit reproducibility: It is hard to reproduce the deployment without specifics on input-to-spike encoding, spike rates per layer, neuron model parameters on hardware, and the exact mapping of axon types/lookup-table s-values. See detailed requests below.

Clarity
- The manuscript is very well written, well organized, and easy to follow. The constrain-then-train narrative and the mapping procedure are clearly explained. Figures and the network topology description are helpful.
- To fully inform expert readers, please add concrete implementation details (encoding, neuron parameters, shared weight values), as listed below.

Originality
- Conceptually novel combination: treating both spikes and binary synapses as probabilities during training, with gradients derived under a Gaussian approximation, while respecting TrueNorth constraints (limited connectivity, shared s-values). The progressive-mixing topology tailored to core-level constraints is also a useful design contribution.
- Related work is largely cited; however, please add (1) Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015, and (2) arXiv:1506.05427, which are relevant to low-precision/binary training and neuromorphic deployment.

Significance
- Practical impact: The method is broadly useful beyond TrueNorth because the training objective directly matches a class of spiking, low-precision substrates. The accuracy–energy trade-off via ensembling is a pragmatic knob for deployment.
- The paper would be even stronger with clearer articulation of deployment scenarios and how the proposed method enables them on TrueNorth (e.g., always-on sensing, embedded vision/audio). The manuscript underplays the system-level significance and constraints (latency, memory/area impact of ensembles, toolchain, and I/O).

Specific comments and requests
- Practical deployment and applications: Please discuss concrete use cases where one would choose (a) the 5-core “high-efficiency” setting and its achievable accuracy/latency envelope, and (b) the larger ensemble settings. Address how ensembles affect area and memory footprint on TrueNorth, and provide guidance on choosing ensemble size under power/latency budgets.
- Comparison to alternatives: Clarify advantages over train-then-constrain pipelines (e.g., Diehl & Neil; discretization/rounding methods [9–12]). A controlled comparison on the same topology/hardware would be ideal. Also: compare to binary/trinary training methods (e.g., arXiv:1506.05427) in terms of accuracy and mapping fidelity under the same connectivity constraints.
- Reproducibility details:
  - Input encoding: How are pixel intensities mapped to x̃i? Linear scaling to [0,1]? Any per-image normalization? Are inputs sampled once per image or resampled per ensemble member? Provide expected spike counts per input and per image (mean/variance).
  - Timing and rates: You state 1 ms per image. Please report layer-wise average firing rates (Hz) and sparsity during inference, and quantify the effect of longer windows (if any) on accuracy/energy.
  - Neuron model: Provide the exact TrueNorth parameters used (leak, threshold, reset/floor, refractory, stochasticity if any) and how the bias term maps to leak when quantized (what happens to the fractional part?).
  - Synaptic strengths and sharing: Specify the four axon types’ 9-bit s-values chosen, how they are distributed, and how block sharing is enforced across synapses/neurons. Include the random seed strategy for sampling c̃ij and inputs across ensemble members.
  - Training protocol: Provide precise schedules (epochs, decay milestones), dropout mask placement, data augmentation distributions, and any regularizers used.
  - Initialization: The orthogonalization/balancing procedure is a nice touch—please include pseudocode for generating S and any constraints used when cores are partially utilized.
- Analysis/ablations:
  - Quantify the error incurred by dropping the σ^−3 term in the gradient; report whether keeping it changes convergence or accuracy.
  - Separate the gains from ensembling vs. single-sample performance: e.g., show variance reduction curves and how many members are needed to saturate accuracy.
  - Report sensitivity to violations of the independence/Gaussian assumptions (e.g., with correlated inputs).
- Hardware metrics:
  - The chip-measured methodology is appreciated. Please detail the measurement setup (counters, sampling interval), define how leakage is apportioned, and report 95% CIs across runs.
  - When contrasting with other architectures (e.g., SpiNNaker), clarify which numbers are measured by you vs. taken from the literature, and note differences in task, latency, and voltage/frequency.

Requested additional citations
- Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience (2015).
- arXiv:1506.05427.

Pros
- Elegant, principled training method that exactly matches spiking, binary, limited-connectivity hardware.
- State-of-the-art MNIST accuracy for SNNs and excellent energy/throughput; chip-measured results.
- Clear writing; topology and initialization insights are practical and generalizable.

Cons
- Practical deployment scenarios and system-level significance for TrueNorth are under-emphasized.
- Missing implementation details hinder reproduction on hardware.
- Limited head-to-head comparisons with strong alternative pipelines.

Recommendation
Accept. Strong, original contribution with clear hardware relevance and convincing results. Addressing the requested details and comparisons would further increase impact and reproducibility.
