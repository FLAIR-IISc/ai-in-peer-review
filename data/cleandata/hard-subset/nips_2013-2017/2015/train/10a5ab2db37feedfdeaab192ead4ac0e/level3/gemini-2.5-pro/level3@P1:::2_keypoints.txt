- Proposes a novel "constrain-then-train" probabilistic method that maps continuous backpropagation training to discrete spiking hardware.  
- Represents continuous synaptic weights and neuron outputs as probabilities for deployment on binary spiking hardware.  
- Uses modified backpropagation on the probabilistic training network, then samples deployable spiking networks (optionally as an ensemble) for higher accuracy.  
- Targeted and validated on IBM TrueNorth, with direct on-chip deployment and measured energy/performance results.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art MNIST accuracy (99.42%) for a spiking network and very low energy per image (as low as 0.268 μJ).  
- Technical quality is high: probabilistic formulation and computational approximations are well-reasoned and supported by experiments.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized; distinctions between training vs. deployment networks are well explained.  
- Original contributions include the unified treatment of spiking neurons, 1-bit synapses, hardware connectivity constraints, and a novel apriori weight initialization to decorrelate core activity.  
- The apriori initialization scheme (minimizing inner products of effective weight vectors) is a clever, hardware-aware contribution.  
- Misses some relevant recent literature (e.g., Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015 and arXiv:1506.05427) that should be cited and discussed.  
- Paper could better articulate advantages of "constrain-then-train" over common "train-then-constrain" approaches.  
- Lacks emphasis on concrete deployment scenarios and practical applications where the energy efficiency is enabling (e.g., always-on keyword spotting, wearable sensors).  
- Reproducibility issues: missing implementation details on input spike encoding (x̃i mapping), typical spike frequencies, exact synaptic strength lookup values, and specific TrueNorth neuron parameters.  
- Recommends adding missing implementation details (e.g., appendix) to improve reproducibility.  
- Strengths for acceptance: highly novel method, solves key reconciliation between training and neuromorphic hardware, validated on real hardware, broadly applicable, and very well written.  
- Weaknesses against acceptance: incomplete reproducibility details, omitted relevant citations, and insufficient discussion of broader practical deployment contexts.  
- Overall recommendation: accept, provided authors address reproducibility and citation gaps in the final version.
