This paper addresses the critical challenge of bridging the gap between the high performance achieved with backpropagation-trained deep neural networks and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing hardware. The authors propose a novel approach of treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities, allowing for standard backpropagation to be used for training networks that can then be mapped onto spiking neuromorphic chips like TrueNorth. This is a significant contribution, as it circumvents the incompatibility between the continuous-valued operations of backpropagation and the discrete nature of neuromorphic systems.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It effectively introduces the problem, outlines the proposed solution, and presents convincing experimental results. The authors appropriately contextualize their work within existing literature on both deep learning and neuromorphic computing, referencing relevant papers from NIPS and other venues. The methodology is clearly described, and the results are presented in a concise and informative manner. The use of the TrueNorth chip for validation adds significant weight to the findings. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of probabilistically mapping a backpropagation-trained network to a spiking neuromorphic platform is a novel and clever solution to a long-standing problem.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the probabilistic model and the adaptation of backpropagation appear technically sound. The authors address potential approximations and their impact on computational efficiency.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The validation on the MNIST dataset, coupled with the deployment on the TrueNorth chip, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The reported accuracy and energy efficiency numbers are impressive.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with neuromorphic computing.
*   **Significance:** The work has the potential to accelerate the deployment of deep learning models on energy-constrained devices. It addresses a fundamental limitation in the practical application of neuromorphic hardware.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Deployment:** While the paper demonstrates the feasibility of the approach, it lacks a detailed discussion of the specific applications that would most benefit from this method and how it enables practical deployment scenarios beyond MNIST.
*   **Insufficient Comparison to Alternatives:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to existing methods for training spiking neural networks, such as those referenced in [9]-[12]. A more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between different approaches would strengthen the contribution. The reviewer notes the suggestion to include citations to Qiao et al., *Frontiers in Neuroscience* 2015 and arXiv:1506.05427 in this discussion.
*   **Missing Implementation Details:** Key implementation details are missing, hindering reproducibility. Specifically, the method for converting analog inputs into spike trains is not fully explained. Furthermore, details regarding the specific spike frequencies used, the shared weight values, and the precise neuron models employed in the TrueNorth implementation would be valuable.
*   **Encoding Details:** More elaboration on how inputs are encoded as spikes would improve understanding and reproducibility. Providing parameter settings and frequency details would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound solution to a significant problem in the field of neuromorphic computing.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.
*   The work has the potential to accelerate the development and deployment of energy-efficient deep learning systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks a detailed discussion of practical deployment scenarios and the specific applications that would benefit from this approach.
*   The comparison to existing methods is limited, and a more thorough analysis of trade-offs is needed.
*   The lack of implementation details hinders reproducibility and limits the ability of other researchers to build upon this work.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant impact on the field of neuromorphic computing. The proposed approach is innovative, technically sound, and well-validated. While some weaknesses exist, particularly regarding the lack of detailed implementation information and the limited discussion of practical applications, these can be addressed in a revision. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by providing more detailed implementation details, expanding the discussion of practical deployment scenarios, and conducting a more thorough comparison to existing methods. Specifically, details regarding input-to-spike conversion, spike frequencies, shared weight values, and neuron models are crucial. Addressing these points will significantly enhance the value and impact of this work.
