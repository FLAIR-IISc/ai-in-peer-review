- Presents a novel hardware-aware backprop method for spiking-neuron, discrete-synapse platforms.  
- Demonstrates the approach on IBM TrueNorth (TN) neuromorphic hardware.  
- Highlights TN’s event-based efficiency but notes apparent incompatibility with standard backprop (continuous outputs, high-precision synapses).  
- Main technical contribution: probabilistic representation of synapses and spiking outputs enabling standard backprop training for deployment on discrete hardware.  
- Paper is well-written and clear overall.  
- Section 1 wording may wrongly imply novelty of using spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses; reword to avoid misleading claims.  
- TN hardware demonstration should be framed as validation, not a separate novelty claim.  
- Clarify the meaning of “0.15 bits per synapse” in Section 2.  
- Network topology is not consolidated in one place—provide clear details (layers, neuron counts, connectivity).  
- Report extra training time incurred by probabilistic synaptic updates.  
- Explain why the single-ensemble 30-core network performs much worse than the 5-core network.  
- Explain why different ensembles of the 5-core network yield similar results and discuss implications.  
- Provide input spike rates during testing and clarify whether inputs are spike trains.  
- Note that Ref. 12 enforces constraints during training (e.g., bias=0), so it is not purely train-then-constrain.  
- Fix the “approach approach” typo.  
- Add discussion/comparison with other spiking backprop rules (e.g., SpikeProp).  
- Significance: important contribution to neuromorphic, hardware-aware training for efficient deployment.  
- Limitations: TN-specific constraints may not generalize; achieving target accuracy with 64 ensembles increases hardware resource needs—discuss adaptability to other platforms.  
- Pros for acceptance: timely novel contribution, suitable for emerging hardware, effective demonstration on a challenging task.  
- Cons for acceptance: presentation issues, incomplete comparison to other spiking backprop methods, limited generalizability beyond TN.
