

# Review for NIPS 2015 Paper: "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing"

This paper proposes a methodology to bridge the gap between backpropagation training and neuromorphic hardware deployment by treating spikes and discrete synapses as continuous probabilities. The authors train networks with continuous-valued inputs, neuron outputs, and synaptic weights constrained to [0,1] (representing probabilities), then map these to neuromorphic hardware by sampling the probabilities to create ensembles of networks. The approach is demonstrated on the TrueNorth chip using MNIST, achieving 99.42% accuracy at 108 μJ per image (ensemble of 64) and 92.7% accuracy at 0.268 μJ per image (ensemble of 1).

The work builds on prior efforts in probabilistic backpropagation for spiking networks (e.g., [9-12]) and binary weight networks (e.g., [13-15]). However, the paper's novelty claims require careful examination in relation to recent NIPS work. The core methodology bears significant similarity to the "expectation backpropagation" approach in [15], which uses expectation propagation for binary neurons and weights with constrained connectivity. The authors' algorithm in Section 3.2 appears to be a hybrid of [14] and [15], using identical approximations to derive their gradient update rules.

**Strengths:**
- The experimental results are strong and demonstrate significant energy efficiency improvements over existing approaches. The 0.268 μJ per image at 92.7% accuracy represents a substantial improvement over prior low-power implementations.
- The methodology is well-motivated and addresses a real-world problem in deploying neural networks on energy-constrained neuromorphic hardware.
- The demonstration on TrueNorth is well-executed and provides concrete evidence of the approach's viability.
- The network topology with progressive mixing and constrained connectivity is well-designed for the target hardware.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty claims are overstated and inaccurate. The paper incorrectly states that "we demonstrate for the first time an offline training methodology using backpropagation to create a network that employs spiking neurons, synapses requiring less bits of precision than even trinary weights, and constrained connectivity." In fact, [15] already demonstrated expectation backpropagation for binary neurons and weights with constrained connectivity, which is exactly what the authors describe.
- The paper misrepresents the prior state-of-the-art: [15] achieved 97.88% accuracy on MNIST with binary weights/neurons without data augmentation, contradicting the paper's claim that the authors achieved "the best accuracy to date on MNIST (99.42%) when compared to networks that use spiking neurons, even with high precision synapses (99.12%) [12], as well as networks that use binary synapses and neurons (97.88%) [15]."
- The "constrain-then-train" methodology is presented as novel, but [15] explicitly uses this approach (training within hardware constraints rather than post-hoc discretization).
- The paper fails to properly acknowledge the significant overlap with [14] and [15] in the introduction and discussion sections.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-executed in terms of implementation and evaluation. The experimental results are well-documented and show clear advantages in energy efficiency. However, the theoretical foundation is not sufficiently novel, as the core algorithm is a hybrid of existing approaches with minimal additional contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with good figures and explanations of the hardware constraints. The mapping procedure from training to deployment is well-explained.

**Originality:** The core methodology is not original, with significant overlap to prior work. The paper's novelty claims are misleading, as the approach is substantially similar to [14] and [15], which should be properly acknowledged. The paper fails to clearly distinguish its contributions from existing work.

**Significance:** The practical significance is high, as the approach enables deployment of neural networks on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware with strong performance. However, the theoretical significance is limited due to the lack of novelty in the core methodology.

**Arguments pro and con acceptance:**
Pro: Strong experimental results demonstrating state-of-the-art energy efficiency for MNIST classification on neuromorphic hardware. The practical contribution to bridging the gap between deep learning and neuromorphic computing is valuable for real-world applications.

Con: The core methodology is not novel, with significant overlap to prior work that should have been properly acknowledged. The novelty claims are overstated and inaccurate, which undermines the paper's contribution. The authors' failure to properly contextualize their work relative to recent NIPS publications is a serious weakness.

**Overall Recommendation:**
While the experimental results are strong and the practical application is valuable, the paper's novelty claims are overstated and inaccurate. The core methodology is substantially similar to prior work in [14] and [15], which should have been properly acknowledged. The paper would be significantly improved by revising the introduction and discussion to accurately position their work relative to these prior contributions. As currently written, the paper's claims of novelty are misleading, which is a significant concern for a NIPS submission.

Given the importance of the problem domain and the strong experimental results, I recommend a borderline decision. If the authors could properly acknowledge the prior work and clarify the distinctions between their approach and [14] and [15], the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. However, as written, the novelty claims are a major weakness that prevents a clear acceptance recommendation. I would lean toward rejection with the possibility of acceptance upon significant revision addressing the novelty concerns.
