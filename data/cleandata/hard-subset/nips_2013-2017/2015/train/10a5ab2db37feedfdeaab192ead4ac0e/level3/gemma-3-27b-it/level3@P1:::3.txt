This paper presents a novel backpropagation training method designed to bridge the gap between the algorithmic strengths of deep learning and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic hardware, specifically targeting spiking neural networks with discrete synapses. This is a timely and important contribution, as deploying deep learning models on embedded systems necessitates both high performance and low power consumption. The approach elegantly addresses the incompatibility between continuous-valued backpropagation and the discrete nature of neuromorphic systems by treating spikes and synaptic states as probabilities, enabling standard backpropagation to be used for training.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in both deep learning and neuromorphic computing. Backpropagation (Rumelhart et al., 1986) remains the cornerstone of deep learning training, while neuromorphic chips like TrueNorth (Merolla et al., 2014) and SpiNNaker (Painkras et al., 2013) offer significant energy efficiency advantages. Previous attempts to combine these approaches have focused on either training spiking networks with high-precision weights (Cao et al., 2015) or continuous-output networks with binary synapses (Cheng et al., 2015). This paper’s novelty lies in successfully reconciling both constraints – spiking neurons, low-precision synapses *and* constrained connectivity – within a single, trainable framework. 

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The core idea of representing discrete elements as probabilities is well-motivated and allows for a relatively straightforward application of standard backpropagation. The authors demonstrate strong performance on the MNIST dataset, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on TrueNorth. The clarity of the writing is also commendable, making the complex concepts accessible. The inclusion of detailed experimental results and comparisons to existing work strengthens the validity of the findings.

However, there are also several weaknesses that need addressing. The introductory claims in Section 1 regarding the novelty of combining spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses are somewhat overstated. The authors should reword these statements to acknowledge prior work in these areas and to more accurately position their contribution as a novel *combination* of established techniques with a specific focus on hardware constraints. The demonstration on TrueNorth should be presented as a validation of the proposed training methodology, rather than a separate claim of novelty.

Section 2 lacks clarity regarding the meaning of “0.15 bits per synapse”. A more detailed explanation is needed to understand the efficiency gains provided by the lookup table scheme. The network topology, described in Section 4, is somewhat fragmented. A consolidated diagram or more explicit description would improve understanding.

Several experimental details are missing. The authors should report the computational cost – specifically, how much longer training takes – due to the probabilistic synaptic connection updates. The performance disparity between the 30-core and 5-core networks is not adequately explained. Why does the larger network perform significantly worse? Further, an explanation of why different ensembles of the 5-core network yield consistently similar results would be insightful.  Providing input spike rates during testing and clarifying whether the inputs are spike trains or rate-coded would add to the reproducibility of the work.

The discussion of related work could also be expanded. A comparison with other spiking backpropagation rules, such as SpikeProp, would be beneficial. Furthermore, a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the TrueNorth-specific constraints and how they might impact generalizability would strengthen the paper.  The authors correctly point out (in Ref. 12) that constraining the network during training is not a purely "train-then-constrain" approach; the existing text has a minor “approach approach” typo.

Regarding significance, while hardware-aware training is undoubtedly important, the strong reliance on TrueNorth’s specific constraints may limit the broader applicability of the method. The requirement for a 64-ensemble to achieve high accuracy also raises concerns about the scalability and resource requirements of the approach. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to bridging the gap between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware.
*   Strong experimental results on TrueNorth, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy.
*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   Addresses a critical challenge in deploying deep learning on embedded systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Overstated claims of novelty regarding spiking neurons and low-precision synapses.
*   Lack of clarity in certain experimental details and explanations.
*   Potential limitations in generalizability due to TrueNorth-specific constraints.
*   Scalability concerns related to ensemble size.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While there are several areas for improvement, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised in this review, particularly regarding the clarity of the claims, experimental details, and discussion of limitations.
