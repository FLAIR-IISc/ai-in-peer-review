This paper proposes a novel approach to reconcile high-precision backpropagation with spike-based devices by treating analog variables as probabilities sampled to produce binary synapses. The authors adapt backpropagation to be compatible with the probabilistic sampling strategy for spiking networks. They validate their method on the MNIST dataset and map trained networks onto the TrueNorth chip, optimizing for size and accuracy.

The manuscript quality and clarity are very high, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and understand the proposed approach. The work is original and broadly useful across platforms, as it addresses a significant challenge in the field of neuromorphic computing.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by achieving state-of-the-art performance on the MNIST dataset with a high-performance network (ensemble of 64) and a high-efficiency network (ensemble of 1). They also present chip-measured performance figures for two different hardware architectures, showcasing the energy efficiency of their approach.

However, there are some areas that require improvement. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison of the advantages of the proposed approach over alternative methods, such as those cited in references [9]–[12]. Additionally, the authors should provide more implementation details, such as input-to-spike conversion, spike frequencies, shared weight values, and neuron models, to facilitate reproducibility.

Furthermore, the paper could be strengthened by clarifying how inputs are encoded as spikes and providing parameter/frequency details. It would also be beneficial to discuss the significance of the results and the practical deployment scenarios for TrueNorth, highlighting which specific applications would benefit from this method and how it enables practical deployment.

In terms of related work, the authors should consider adding citations to Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and arXiv:1506.05427, as suggested by the reviewer.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing, and with some revisions to address the mentioned concerns, it has the potential to be a highly impactful publication.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* Original and broadly useful approach to reconciling high-precision backpropagation with spike-based devices
* State-of-the-art performance on the MNIST dataset
* Energy efficiency demonstrated through chip-measured performance figures
* High-quality manuscript with clear writing and well-organized structure

Arguments con acceptance:

* Lack of detailed comparison with alternative methods
* Insufficient implementation details for reproducibility
* Unclear significance of results and practical deployment scenarios for TrueNorth
* Missing citations to relevant work (Qiao et al. and arXiv:1506.05427)

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide a detailed comparison of the proposed approach with alternative methods
* Include implementation details for reproducibility
* Clarify how inputs are encoded as spikes and provide parameter/frequency details
* Discuss the significance of the results and practical deployment scenarios for TrueNorth
* Add citations to relevant work (Qiao et al. and arXiv:1506.05427)
