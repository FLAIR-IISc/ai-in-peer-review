This paper addresses the critical challenge of bridging the gap between the energy efficiency of neuromorphic computing and the performance of backpropagation-trained deep neural networks. The authors propose a method to train networks using backpropagation with continuous values constrained to [0, 1], interpreting these values as probabilities of spikes or synaptic connections being ‘on’. This allows for a direct mapping to neuromorphic hardware like the TrueNorth chip, enabling efficient deployment of trained models. The work is presented with a clear motivation and demonstrates promising results on the MNIST dataset, achieving high accuracy with impressive energy efficiency.

The paper builds upon a rich body of work in both deep learning and neuromorphic computing. The use of backpropagation for training spiking neural networks is not entirely new, with prior efforts focusing on high-precision weights ([9-12]). Similarly, networks with binary synapses have been explored ([13-15]). However, the authors distinguish their approach by simultaneously addressing spiking neurons, low-precision synapses, and constrained connectivity – a combination not previously demonstrated.  The reliance on TrueNorth as a deployment platform is also a strength, providing a concrete example of the potential benefits.

A significant strength of this work lies in the experimental results. The achieved accuracy of 99.42% with a 64-member ensemble and 92.7% with a single network on the TrueNorth chip, coupled with the reported energy consumption (108 μJ and 0.268 μJ respectively), represent substantial advancements in energy-efficient digit recognition. These numbers are demonstrably superior to previously published results ([16]), offering a compelling case for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The detailed description of the network topology and the initialization strategy, specifically designed to leverage TrueNorth’s architecture, is also commendable.

However, several aspects of the paper warrant critical examination. The novelty claims, particularly regarding the learning rule, are overstated. Section 3.2’s algorithm is remarkably similar to, and effectively a hybrid of, approaches outlined in references [14] and [15], utilizing the same key approximations. This connection should be explicitly acknowledged. Moreover, the discussion in the first paragraph of Section 6 suggests that the “constrain-then-train” methodology is a novel contribution, which is inaccurate, as a similar approach was investigated in reference [15].  Claim (i) in the introduction, stating the demonstration of an offline training methodology is “for the first time,” is also misleading; reference [15] demonstrated expectation backpropagation, a backpropagation update rule accommodating binary neurons and weights with constrained connectivity. 

Furthermore, the citation of prior state-of-the-art results is inaccurate. Reference [15] actually achieved superior performance for binary weights and neurons on the MNIST dataset *without* data augmentation. This discrepancy undermines the claim of achieving “the best accuracy to date” on MNIST for networks employing spiking neurons.  While the reported results are excellent, framing them in the context of an accurate comparison with prior work is crucial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong experimental results demonstrating high accuracy and low energy consumption on the TrueNorth chip.
*   A well-defined and clearly explained methodology for bridging backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware.
*   A detailed description of the network topology and initialization strategy, tailored for efficient hardware deployment.
*   The work addresses a significant challenge in the field of neuromorphic computing and has the potential to impact real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Overstated novelty claims regarding the learning rule and training methodology.
*   Insufficient acknowledgement of related work, particularly reference [15].
*   Inaccurate citation of prior state-of-the-art results.
*   The algorithmic contributions, while effective, are evolutionary rather than revolutionary.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neuromorphic computing, particularly due to its strong experimental results. However, the authors need to revise the paper to address the concerns regarding overstated novelty and inaccuracies in the literature review.  Acknowledging the existing work more fully and presenting the contributions as an effective application and refinement of existing techniques, rather than a completely novel approach, would significantly strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon addressing these points. Specifically, the introduction and discussion sections need to be revised to provide a more accurate and nuanced portrayal of the work’s relationship to prior research.
