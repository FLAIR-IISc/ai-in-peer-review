

This paper presents a novel backpropagation training method for spiking neural networks that accounts for hardware constraints of discrete-synapse neuromorphic platforms. The authors treat spike probabilities as continuous values for training, then map the trained network to hardware by sampling these probabilities to create ensembles. The approach is timely as it bridges the gap between conventional backpropagation and energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware.

The paper's main strengths include its clear presentation, practical demonstration on TrueNorth hardware, and the useful insight that training within hardware constraints (constrain-then-train) produces better deployment results than post-training constraint application (train-then-constrain). The method achieves strong results (99.42% accuracy on MNIST with 64-ensemble) while operating at low energy (108 μJ/image).

However, several weaknesses require attention. The novelty claims in Section 1 incorrectly imply that spiking neurons and reduced-precision synapses are novel contributions, when prior work has explored these elements. The demonstration on TrueNorth should be framed as validation rather than a separate novelty claim. The paper must clarify the meaning of "0.15 bits per synapse" in Section 2, and consolidate the network topology description into a single, clear location. The authors should report training time comparisons with standard backpropagation, explain why the single-ensemble 30-core network underperforms the 5-core network, and justify why different 5-core ensembles yield similar results. Input spike rates during testing need specification, and the paper should clarify whether inputs are spike trains.

Additionally, the discussion should compare with other spiking backpropagation methods like SpikeProp, and the paper must correct the "approach approach" typo while noting that Ref. 12 enforces constraints during training, making it not purely a train-then-constrain approach. While the hardware-aware training is significant, the TN-specific constraints may not generalize, and using a 64-ensemble increases hardware resource requirements for target accuracy.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field with practical implications, but requires careful revision to address the identified weaknesses before acceptance.
