## Review of "Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing"

This paper tackles the important problem of bridging the gap between the performance of backpropagation-trained deep networks and the energy efficiency of neuromorphic hardware. The authors propose a “constrain-then-train” approach, training a network with continuous values representing probabilities of spiking and synaptic activity, enabling a direct mapping to a discrete neuromorphic implementation (TrueNorth). They demonstrate strong results on MNIST, achieving competitive accuracy with significantly improved power efficiency, particularly in a single-ensemble member configuration. 

The core idea of representing discrete elements as probabilities for backpropagation is logical and potentially valuable for deploying deep learning models on neuromorphic platforms. The experimental results, particularly the power measurements on TrueNorth, are the primary strength of this work, showcasing a tangible benefit of the proposed approach. The detailed description of the network topology and initialization procedure is also commendable.

However, the paper suffers from several issues related to novelty and appropriate attribution. Section 3.2’s algorithm is remarkably similar to a hybrid of references [14] and [15], utilizing identical approximations. This critical connection is not acknowledged. Furthermore, the discussion’s opening paragraph incorrectly presents the “constrain-then-train” methodology as novel; this was explicitly explored in ref [15].  The claim of novelty (i) in the introduction is misleading. Ref [15] already demonstrates backpropagation updates using expectation propagation for binary neurons/weights, accommodating constrained connectivity. 

Additionally, the paper misattributes the state-of-the-art.  Ref [15] achieved superior results on MNIST *without* data augmentation compared to the previously cited work [12] for binary weights/neurons. This inaccurate benchmarking undermines the claim of achieving “the best accuracy to date.”

**Arguments for acceptance:** The experimental results demonstrating strong accuracy and impressive power efficiency on TrueNorth are novel and significant. This work offers a practical pathway for deploying deep learning on energy-constrained hardware.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The overstated claims of novelty, lack of acknowledgement of closely related prior work ([14, 15]), and inaccurate state-of-the-art benchmarking are significant weaknesses. The methodology itself builds heavily upon existing work, and the contributions are incremental rather than groundbreaking.

**Overall Assessment:** While the experimental findings are valuable, the paper’s overall presentation exaggerates the novelty of the approach. A major revision addressing the points raised regarding attribution and claims of originality is necessary before this work would be suitable for acceptance. The paper should be resubmitted after acknowledging prior work and accurately benchmarking against existing methods.
