This paper presents a novel and effective method for reconciling high-precision training algorithms like backpropagation with the constraints of energy-efficient, spike-based neuromorphic hardware. The core idea is to treat continuous analog variables during training as probabilities, which are then sampled to produce the binary synapses and spiking neuron activations required by hardware like the TrueNorth chip. This "constrain-then-train" approach adapts backpropagation to be directly compatible with the target hardware's topology and constraints. The work builds upon previous efforts to apply backpropagation to spiking networks or networks with low-precision weights, but it is novel in successfully combining spiking neurons, extremely low-precision binary synapses, and constrained connectivity within a single training framework, validated with impressive chip-measured performance figures.

**Strengths:**
*   **Quality and Significance:** The work is technically sound and the proposed method is elegant. Validating the approach on the MNIST dataset and mapping the trained networks directly onto TrueNorth hardware is a significant strength. The paper presents convincing results demonstrating a clear trade-off between high accuracy (99.42%) and extreme energy efficiency (0.268 μJ/image), advancing the state-of-the-art in deploying deep networks on neuromorphic platforms.
*   **Originality:** The probabilistic adaptation of backpropagation is a novel contribution that provides a principled bridge between the continuous domain of deep learning and the discrete, event-driven domain of spiking hardware.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and presents its complex ideas in an accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Context and Significance:** The paper does not sufficiently highlight the practical significance of its TrueNorth deployment. It would be strengthened by discussing specific applications that would benefit from this level of energy efficiency and how this method enables their practical use.
*   **Reproducibility:** A key weakness is the lack of implementation details needed for reproducibility. The authors should clarify how inputs are encoded as spike probabilities, the specific spike frequencies used, details on shared weight values, and the exact neuron model parameters.
*   **Comparison and Related Work:** The paper lacks a clear comparison of the advantages of its method over the alternatives mentioned in references [9]–[12]. Furthermore, the authors should cite and discuss related work from Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015 and arXiv:1506.05427) to better position their contribution.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses, particularly by adding the missing implementation details to ensure reproducibility.
