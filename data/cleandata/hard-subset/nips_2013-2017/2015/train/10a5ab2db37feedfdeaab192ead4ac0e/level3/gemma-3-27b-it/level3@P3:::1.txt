This paper addresses the crucial challenge of bridging the gap between the energy efficiency of neuromorphic hardware and the performance of backpropagation-trained deep neural networks. The authors propose a “constrain-then-train” methodology, training a network with continuous-valued weights and neurons constrained to [0,1], interpreting these as probabilities of spiking and synapse activation. This trained network can then be mapped to a spiking neuromorphic platform like TrueNorth via sampling. The results demonstrate strong performance on MNIST, achieving state-of-the-art power efficiency on the TrueNorth chip.

However, while the experimental results are compelling, the paper overstates its novelty. The core algorithmic contributions and overall methodology are deeply rooted in prior work, specifically references [14] and [15], and the authors fail to adequately acknowledge this connection. 

The most significant concern is the similarity between Section 3.2’s algorithm and the approaches in [14, 15]. Both utilize probabilistic representations of binary neurons and synapses and employ backpropagation for training. The approximations used to derive the gradients are identical. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this lineage and detail the specific differences. The paper's claim of novelty feels diminished without this attribution.

Furthermore, the discussion's opening paragraph incorrectly positions the "constrain-then-train" methodology as novel. Ref [15] explicitly explores and tests this approach, training a network with constraints before deployment. While the authors' specific implementation differs, the fundamental concept isn’t new.

The novelty claim (i) in the introduction is also misleading. Ref [15]'s “expectation backpropagation” *is* a backpropagation update rule utilizing expectation propagation for binary neurons and weights, and, crucially, can inherently handle constrained connectivity. The authors' approach isn't fundamentally different in this regard, simply applying it within the specific [0,1] probabilistic framework.

Finally, the paper inaccurately cites prior state-of-the-art performance. Ref [15] achieved the best published results for binary weights/neurons on MNIST *without* data augmentation. The authors’ claim of surpassing this with 99.42% accuracy, while true, should be contextualized by acknowledging this prior work accurately.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Experimental Results:** The reported accuracy and energy efficiency on the TrueNorth chip are impressive and demonstrate the practical potential of the approach. The ensemble averaging strategy seems effective in boosting performance.
* **Clear Presentation of the System:** The description of the network topology and mapping procedure is relatively clear and well-structured.
* **Concrete Implementation:** The use of the TrueNorth chip provides a tangible instantiation of the proposed method, enhancing its credibility.
* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Bridging the gap between backpropagation and neuromorphic hardware is a significant challenge with important implications for low-power computing.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Overstated Novelty:** The primary weakness is the overemphasis on novelty. The method is a logical extension and hybridization of existing techniques, particularly [14] and [15], which isn’t sufficiently acknowledged.
* **Inaccurate Citation:** The misrepresentation of prior state-of-the-art performance (ref [15]) diminishes the impact of the reported results.
* **Lack of Thorough Comparison:** A more detailed comparison to other probabilistic backpropagation methods beyond [15] would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Generalizability Discussion:** While acknowledging the potential for generalization to other neuromorphic hardware, the paper doesn't delve into the challenges and considerations for doing so.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The experimental results are strong and represent a significant advance in energy-efficient neuromorphic computing.
* The paper addresses an important and timely problem.
* The work is well-executed and provides a practical demonstration of the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The claims of novelty are overstated and misleading.
* The lack of acknowledgement of related work (particularly [14] and [15]) is a significant oversight.
* The inaccurate citation of prior state-of-the-art weakens the paper's credibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents valuable experimental results that demonstrate the feasibility of training networks for efficient neuromorphic deployment. However, the overstated claims of novelty and lack of adequate attribution to prior work are serious concerns. If the authors address these issues by acknowledging the contributions of [14] and [15], correcting the citation of [15], and moderating the novelty claims, the paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS. As it stands, I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions** contingent on the requested clarifications and corrections. The core scientific contribution, while not entirely novel, is still valuable, and the experimental results are impactful.
