This paper presents a novel and effective method for training spiking neural networks that can be deployed on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware, such as the IBM TrueNorth chip. The core contribution is a "constrain-then-train" methodology that reconciles the continuous nature of backpropagation with the discrete, spiking dynamics of the target hardware. The authors achieve this by framing the training process in a probabilistic domain: continuous values for synaptic connections and neuron outputs in the training network represent the probabilities of a synapse being "on" or a neuron firing a spike in the deployment network. This allows for standard gradient-based optimization using a modified backpropagation algorithm. After offline training, one or more deployable networks are generated by sampling from these learned probabilities, enabling an ensemble approach for higher accuracy.

The work is situated within the growing field of neuromorphic computing, which seeks to bridge the gap between the algorithmic successes of deep learning and the hardware efficiency of brain-inspired architectures. Previous efforts have tackled parts of this problem, such as training spiking networks with high-precision weights (e.g., [11], [12]) or training networks with low-precision weights but continuous-valued neurons (e.g., [13]). This paper is novel in its unified approach that simultaneously addresses spiking neurons, extremely low-precision binary synapses (1-bit connectivity), and hardware-imposed connectivity constraints.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The probabilistic formulation of the backpropagation algorithm is well-reasoned, and the approximations made for computational efficiency seem justified. The claims are strongly supported by experimental results on the MNIST dataset, and crucially, these results are not from simulation but from direct deployment and measurement on the TrueNorth chip. This provides convincing evidence for the method's efficacy and the reported energy efficiency. The work is presented as a complete system, from the theoretical framework and initialization strategy to the hardware mapping and performance characterization.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The core idea of treating discrete events as continuous probabilities is explained clearly. The distinction between the "training network" and the "deployment network" is well-articulated, and Figure 1 provides a helpful overview. The "constrain-then-train" framing in the discussion is particularly effective at situating the work and highlighting its advantages.

**Originality:** The proposed approach is highly original. The concept of training a probabilistic proxy network and then sampling from it to generate hardware-compatible spiking networks is an elegant solution to a challenging problem. The apriori weight initialization scheme, designed to decorrelate neuron activity on a core by minimizing the inner product between effective weight vectors, is also a clever and novel contribution that addresses hardware-specific constraints from first principles.

However, the authors appear to have missed some relevant recent work. For instance, Qiao et al. ("A Reconfigurable Neuromorphic Processor with Memristive Synapses," Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015) and a related preprint ("Training a Spiking Deep Network with Joint-optimization of Firing Rates and Synaptic Weights," arXiv:1506.05427) also explore backpropagation-based training for spiking networks, and it would be beneficial to discuss the relationship between the methods.

**Significance:** The results presented are highly significant. Bridging the gap between state-of-the-art training algorithms and ultra-low-power hardware is a critical challenge for the practical deployment of neural networks in embedded and mobile systems. The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art accuracy (99.42% on MNIST) for a spiking network, along with unprecedented energy efficiency (as low as 0.268 μJ per image) measured on real hardware. This work is likely to be influential and useful for researchers working with a variety of neuromorphic platforms, not just TrueNorth.

A weakness in this area is that the paper does not sufficiently highlight the practical importance or specific deployment scenarios for these results. While the energy figures are impressive, the discussion could be strengthened by providing concrete examples of applications where this level of efficiency is an enabling factor (e.g., always-on keyword spotting, visual-wake systems, or wearable sensor processing). Furthermore, while the paper contrasts its approach with prior work ([9]-[12]), it could more clearly articulate the advantages of its "constrain-then-train" method over the more common "train-then-constrain" alternatives.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Reproducibility:** The paper lacks several key implementation details that would be necessary for another researcher to reproduce the results. Specifically:
    *   How are the continuous-valued grayscale pixels of MNIST converted into input spike probabilities (x̃i)? Is it a simple linear mapping?
    *   What are the typical spike frequencies observed in the network during operation?
    *   The paper mentions that synaptic strengths (sij) are fixed apriori. While the initialization principle is described, the exact values used (e.g., the "lookup table of s-values") are not provided.
    *   The specific neuron model parameters used on TrueNorth (leaks, thresholds) beyond the general description would be helpful.
    Providing these details, perhaps in an appendix, would significantly improve the paper's quality and utility.

2.  **Missing Citations:** Please consider citing and discussing the work of Qiao et al. (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2015, and arXiv:1506.05427) as it is highly relevant to training spiking deep networks with backpropagation.

3.  **Strengthening the Discussion:** To improve the paper's impact, I suggest expanding the discussion to include potential real-world applications where the demonstrated energy/performance trade-off would be critical. This would help ground the significance of the work beyond a benchmark dataset.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and elegant method to train spiking neural networks using backpropagation.
*   Solves the important and difficult problem of reconciling continuous training algorithms with discrete, energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on MNIST, with performance figures measured on the actual TrueNorth chip, providing strong validation.
*   The work is broadly applicable to other neuromorphic platforms.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and clear.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper is missing key implementation details, which significantly hinders reproducibility.
*   The literature review is incomplete, omitting some highly relevant recent work.
*   The discussion on the broader significance and practical application of the results could be stronger.

On balance, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The originality and significance of the contribution are very high. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the issues of reproducibility and missing citations in the final version.
