## Review of “Backpropagation for Energy-Efficient Neuromorphic Computing”

This paper presents a compelling approach to reconcile the benefits of backpropagation-trained deep networks with the energy efficiency of spiking neuromorphic hardware, specifically the TrueNorth chip. The core idea – treating analog variables as probabilities sampled to produce binary synapses – is a clever way to bridge the gap between continuous-output backpropagation and the discrete nature of neuromorphic systems. The authors demonstrate this by training a network on MNIST and successfully mapping it onto TrueNorth, reporting both accuracy and energy consumption figures for different ensemble sizes. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clear, presenting a novel and potentially broadly useful contribution.

The strengths of this work lie in its originality and practical demonstration. The authors not only propose a theoretical framework but also validate it with chip-measured results, a significant accomplishment. Achieving 99.42% accuracy with a 64-member ensemble and 92.7% with a single member on TrueNorth is impressive. The “constrain-then-train” philosophy is well-articulated and provides a clear rationale for their approach. 

However, the paper lacks sufficient context regarding the significance of TrueNorth in the broader landscape of neuromorphic computing and potential real-world applications. While the energy efficiency numbers are presented, it’s unclear *why* deploying on TrueNorth is particularly advantageous compared to other low-power solutions. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of specific applications where this method could be impactful. 

Furthermore, the comparison to existing methods (refs [9]–[12]) feels superficial. A more thorough analysis of the advantages and disadvantages of their approach relative to alternatives is needed. The results are convincing, but crucial implementation details are missing. Specifically, information on the input-to-spike conversion method, spike frequencies, shared weight values, and the precise neuron model used would greatly enhance reproducibility and allow for a more informed assessment of the work. The authors should clarify how inputs are encoded as spikes and provide a more detailed parameter breakdown. 

Finally, I recommend adding citations to Qiao et al., Frontiers in Neuroscience 2015 and arXiv:1506.05427, as these works are relevant to probabilistic spiking neural networks and would enrich the related work section.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to bridging backpropagation and neuromorphic computing.
*   Strong experimental validation on TrueNorth with impressive results.
*   Excellent manuscript quality and clarity.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clear justification for the choice of TrueNorth and its practical applications.
*   Insufficient comparison to alternative methods.
*   Missing implementation details hindering reproducibility.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would substantially strengthen the contribution and increase its impact.
