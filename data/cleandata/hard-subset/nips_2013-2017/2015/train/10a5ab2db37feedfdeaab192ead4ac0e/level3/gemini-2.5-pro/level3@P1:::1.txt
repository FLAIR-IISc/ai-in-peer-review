This paper presents a method to train spiking neural networks with low-precision, discrete synapses for deployment on energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware, specifically the IBM TrueNorth chip. The central problem addressed is the incompatibility between the backpropagation algorithm, which requires continuous activations and weights, and the event-driven, discrete nature of neuromorphic systems. The authors' proposed solution is to frame the training problem probabilistically. They treat synaptic connections and neuron activations as continuous probabilities in the range [0, 1] during training. This allows them to use a modified backpropagation algorithm, where the gradient is computed based on a Gaussian approximation of the summed inputs to a neuron. After this offline training, one or more deployment networks are created by sampling from these learned probabilities. The final system can use an ensemble of these sampled networks to improve performance. This approach connects the powerful training framework of deep learning with the exceptional energy efficiency of neuromorphic hardware. The authors demonstrate this method by training networks for the MNIST dataset and deploying them on TrueNorth, reporting impressive results in both accuracy and energy consumption.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Practical Results:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its experimental results and demonstration on real hardware. Achieving 99.42% accuracy on MNIST with a spiking neural network is a state-of-the-art result. More importantly, the authors provide a compelling trade-off between accuracy and energy efficiency, demonstrating a 92.7% accuracy network that consumes only 0.268 µJ per image. This is a remarkable achievement and a significant step towards deploying useful neural networks in power-constrained embedded systems. The successful deployment on the TrueNorth chip makes this a complete and convincing piece of systems-level work.

2.  **Sound and Well-Articulated Methodology:** The "constrain-then-train" philosophy is a logical and effective way to bridge the gap between training algorithms and hardware constraints. By building the hardware limitations directly into the training framework, the authors ensure that improvements during training correspond directly to improvements in the deployed network. The probabilistic interpretation of spikes and synapses is an elegant way to create a differentiable system for backpropagation.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The methodology, including the deployment network, the training network formulation, and the mapping process, is described clearly and in sufficient detail. The motivation is clear, and the results are presented effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Overstated Algorithmic Novelty:** The main weakness of the paper is its framing of the algorithmic contributions as being more novel than they are. The derivation of the backpropagation update rule in Section 3.2 is deeply similar to prior work. The use of a Gaussian approximation for the sum of weighted binary inputs and the resulting gradient calculation closely mirrors the methods in Zhao et al. [14] and, more recently, the "expectation backpropagation" algorithm of Cheng et al. [15]. These papers also train networks with binary or discrete components by using a probabilistic, continuous relaxation amenable to gradient-based optimization. The paper would be significantly stronger if it explicitly acknowledged and discussed the relationship to these works, rather than presenting the update rule as a novel derivation.

2.  **Misleading Claims of "First":** Related to the point above, several claims of novelty are not entirely accurate.
    *   Introduction claim (i) states this is the "first time an offline training methodology using backpropagation to create a network that employs spiking neurons, synapses requiring less bits of precision..., and constrained connectivity." However, the method in Cheng et al. [15] is also a backpropagation-style update for binary neurons and weights and can accommodate constrained connectivity.
    *   The discussion's framing of the "constrain-then-train" approach as a new paradigm is also misleading, as this is precisely the philosophy employed in [15]. The authors should revise these claims to more accurately position their work relative to existing literature.

3.  **Inaccurate Comparison to Prior Art:** In Introduction claim (ii), the authors compare their 99.42% accuracy to the 97.88% from Cheng et al. [15] for binary networks. This comparison is not on equal footing. The authors of the present work employ significant data augmentation (rotations, shifts, scaling), a standard technique for boosting performance. The result from [15] was achieved *without* data augmentation. While the authors' accuracy is still excellent, the context of the comparison should be made clear to provide a fair assessment of the advance over prior work.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The experimental results are strong, well-supported, and demonstrate a complete piece of work from algorithm design to hardware deployment. The evaluation of strengths (accuracy, power) is thorough.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The methods and results are easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** This is the weakest aspect. While the combination of these techniques and their application to the TrueNorth hardware for such impressive results is novel and significant, the core algorithmic ideas (probabilistic backpropagation with Gaussian approximations) are not. The paper overstates its originality and does not adequately position itself with respect to closely related prior work [14, 15].
*   **Significance:** The significance of the work is very high. Bridging the gap between high-performance deep learning and ultra-low-power neuromorphic hardware is a critical problem. The demonstrated results provide a compelling proof-of-concept and a practical pathway for others to build upon. Researchers and practitioners in both neuromorphic engineering and applied machine learning are likely to find this work influential.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art accuracy for a spiking neural network on MNIST.
*   Presents groundbreaking energy efficiency results on real neuromorphic hardware.
*   Provides a practical and complete methodology for training and deploying networks on constrained hardware like TrueNorth.
*   The overall systems-level contribution is highly significant and impactful.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper overstates the novelty of its core training algorithm, which is very similar to previously published methods.
*   Fails to properly cite and discuss the most relevant prior work, particularly Cheng et al. [15].
*   Comparisons to prior art are not entirely fair due to differences in experimental setup (e.g., data augmentation).

**Recommendation:**
This is a borderline paper. The experimental results and their significance are strong enough to warrant acceptance. However, the issues with the claims of novelty and the discussion of related work are serious and must be addressed. I would recommend acceptance on the condition that the authors thoroughly revise the introduction and discussion to accurately position their algorithmic contributions with respect to prior work, particularly [14] and [15], and to qualify their comparisons to the state-of-the-art. The strength of the hardware demonstration and the energy-efficiency results ultimately make this a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
