- Paper proposes nonspectral likelihood inference for DPPs by directly parameterizing the L-ensemble and deriving determinant bounds for finite and continuous domains using inducing inputs Z.
- Bounds are built via Q = L_YZ L_Z^{-1} L_ZY and Seiler–Simon inequalities, enabling variational lower bounds and a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) sampler that tightens bounds by increasing |Z| and re-optimizing Z.
- Continuous-domain extension uses closed-form Ψ integrals for Gaussian base measures/kernels to bound Fredholm determinants without eigen-expansions.
- Novelty: clean, nonspectral determinant-bounding technique for DPPs, particularly valuable for continuous DPPs.
- Generality/simplicity: avoids eigendecompositions, works with generic kernels, and naturally supports inducing-input optimization.
- Practicality: scalable variational bound when m ≪ n and a well-motivated retrospective MH scheme adapted to nonspectral bounds.
- Empirical indication: diabetic neuropathy experiment yields coherent overdispersion estimates and reproduces known class differences.
- Missing baselines: lacks quantitative comparisons to exact small-n methods, spectral approximations (e.g., Affandi et al. 2014), Nyström approaches, and Bornemann Fredholm quadrature.
- Limited analysis of approximation quality: no systematic study of bound tightness versus m and Z or diagnostics linking bound gap to inference/parameter error.
- Variational inference (VI) failure modes: VI shows large parameter errors in a 1D example and identifiability of κ is acknowledged but not addressed (no reparameterizations or priors).
- Exposition issues: uneven presentation, insufficient primer on K vs L, repetitious discussion, inconsistent notation/equation numbering.
- Missing or unclear assumptions for the continuous case (trace-class, integrability) should be stated clearly with examples.
- Originality: determinant bounds and adaptation of sparse GP inducing ideas to DPP likelihoods appear novel and nontrivial.
- Significance: methods could broaden DPP applicability to large finite and continuous problems; retrospective MH is practically useful.
- Request 1: provide complexity analysis and wall-clock timings, detailing per-iteration costs (e.g., O(n m^2), O(m^3)) and typical number of Z refinements in MH.
- Request 2: add baselines—finite: exact/spectral/Nyström comparisons; continuous: compare to high-precision Fredholm quadrature (Bornemann) where feasible.
- Request 3: analyze when VI succeeds/fails, consider reparameterizing L (expected cardinality/repulsion) and informative priors on κ to improve identifiability.
- Request 4: report ablations for inducing inputs Z (Z = observations, base-measure samples, k-means, maximin) and the effect of optimizing vs not optimizing Z.
- Request 5: give guidance on choosing m and Z (rule-of-thumb using bound gap tolerance, initialization and refinement strategies) and empirically characterize how gaps decrease with m.
- Pros for acceptance: elegant nonspectral bounds, practical inference recipes, and potential impact for large-scale and continuous DPPs.
- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical evaluation and baselines, insufficient guidance on VI/identifiability, and exposition needs tightening.
- Overall assessment: promising and original work; recommend acceptance conditional on stronger empirical validation, diagnostics, and clearer presentation.
