

This paper addresses a key challenge in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the intractable normalizing constant arising from the Fredholm determinant in continuous domains or large determinants in finite domains. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on these determinants that do not require spectral decomposition, enabling more efficient variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods. This work builds upon recent NIPS contributions on DPPs (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013; Gillenwater et al., 2012) but addresses a significant computational bottleneck that has limited the applicability of likelihood-based methods in practice.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it establishes theoretical bounds on the likelihood for both finite and continuous DPPs that avoid spectral decomposition, relying instead on inducing inputs (pseudo-inputs) to approximate the kernel. These bounds are computationally efficient, with complexity O(nm²) for n data points and m inducing inputs, compared to O(n³) for direct determinant computation. Second, the authors demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into existing inference paradigms: variational inference (with complexity O(nm²)) and retrospective MCMC (with moderate computational cost). Third, the paper provides empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets, including a diabetic neuropathy dataset that shows clear separation between disease progression stages.

The paper excels in originality and significance. The approach directly addresses a fundamental computational limitation in DPP inference that has been acknowledged but not adequately resolved in prior work. The nonspectral bounds represent a novel technique that could benefit applications beyond DPPs, particularly in kernel-based models where spectral decomposition is prohibitive. The significance is further reinforced by the demonstration of the method's effectiveness on a real-world dataset showing meaningful biological insights.

The quality of the paper is generally strong, with sound theoretical foundations and reasonable experimental validation. The bounds derived in Propositions 1 and 2 are technically correct and well-motivated by connections to sparse Gaussian processes. The experimental section provides evidence that the proposed methods can recover meaningful parameters in real-world settings, particularly for the MCMC approach. However, the evaluation could be more thorough in several aspects.

The main weaknesses lie in clarity and completeness of the evaluation. The description of the variational inference method (Section 3.1) is somewhat brief, particularly regarding the optimization procedure for the inducing inputs. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the inducing inputs are optimized and why they are effective. Additionally, the experimental results in Section 5.1 raise important questions about the limitations of the variational approach. The authors note that variational inference produces highly variable parameter estimates (κ varies over several orders of magnitude), while retrospective MCMC provides more consistent results. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed analysis of when the variational approach is expected to succeed versus fail, and why. For instance, it would be valuable to understand whether the poor performance of variational inference is due to the specific parameterization, the nature of the likelihood landscape, or the particular choice of inducing inputs.

The paper also lacks a comprehensive comparison with alternative methods. While the authors mention that previous work (Affandi et al., 2013) requires spectral decomposition, they do not provide a thorough comparison of computational complexity and approximation quality with other approaches. A table comparing the time complexity, approximation accuracy, and implementation requirements of the proposed methods versus existing alternatives (including spectral-based methods and other approximate inference techniques) would be highly valuable for the community.

Regarding clarity, the paper would benefit from a more concise discussion of the relationship between the kernels K and L. The paper repeatedly mentions the interpretability trade-offs of directly learning L instead of K, but this discussion is somewhat tangential to the main contribution of developing nonspectral bounds. A more focused presentation would improve the paper's flow.

The authors' choice to directly parametrize L rather than K is reasonable given the computational constraints, but the paper could better address why this approach is preferable for inference purposes despite the interpretability loss. This is particularly relevant in the context of the diabetic neuropathy application, where the authors report clear separation between disease stages using the L parametrization.

In terms of recommendations, I would suggest the authors:
1. Provide a more detailed analysis of the conditions under which variational inference succeeds versus fails
2. Include a comprehensive comparison table of computational complexity and approximation quality versus existing methods
3. Clarify the optimization procedure for inducing inputs and provide guidelines for selecting the number of inducing points
4. Streamline the discussion of K versus L to focus on the main contribution

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the DPP literature by providing computationally efficient inference methods that avoid spectral decomposition. The theoretical bounds are novel, the experimental validation is promising, and the approach has clear potential for broader application in kernel-based models. While the current evaluation could be more thorough, the paper's core contribution is significant and addresses an important gap in the literature.

Pro: Novel nonspectral bounds that avoid computationally expensive spectral decomposition; Demonstrated effectiveness on real-world data; Clear connection to existing sparse GP techniques; Theoretical foundations well-supported.

Con: Limited comparison with existing methods; Variational inference evaluation insufficiently detailed; Some tangential discussion about kernel parameterization; Need for clearer guidelines on inducing input selection.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the evaluation and presentation issues noted above. The paper has strong technical merit and addresses a meaningful challenge in DPP inference, making it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
