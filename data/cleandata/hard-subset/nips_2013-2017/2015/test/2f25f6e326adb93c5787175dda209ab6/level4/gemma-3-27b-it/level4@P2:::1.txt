Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have gained significant attention in machine learning due to their ability to model repulsive interactions between points in a realization. This repulsion is governed by a positive definite kernel, and a key challenge lies in learning the kernel’s parameters from observed point configurations. This estimation is complicated by an intractable normalization constant, effectively requiring summation over all possible subsets. Previous work [7] addressed this by leveraging spectral estimation to provide incremental upper and lower bounds, enabling a provably correct retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method for kernel parameter estimation. This paper presents an alternative bounding approach that avoids eigenvalue estimation, offering a potential computational advantage. This method also facilitates variational inference and draws insightful connections to inducing-point techniques used for scalable Gaussian process models.

This is a technically strong and well-written paper. I was previously unaware of the determinant inequality underpinning Proposition 1, and it appears particularly well-suited to this problem. The authors cleverly circumvent the spectral estimation issues of [7]. The connections to Gaussian process inducing points and the potential for variational inference are particularly appealing. Overall, this paper was a pleasure to read. 

However, I have two technical concerns. First, while I appreciate the approach, I question whether replacing power iterations with potentially complex, high-dimensional nonlinear optimization problems represents a substantial computational win. The cost of optimizing over the inducing points – especially as their number increases to tighten the bounds – warrants further scrutiny. Second, it's not clear whether simply increasing the number of inducing points guarantees convergence of the bounds. The optimization problem is likely highly non-convex, and a local minimum might prevent accurate bounding of the acceptance probability in MCMC, hindering progress. This point needs more direct discussion. Is there a strategy to ensure a global minimum, or a stopping criterion based on bound tightness?

Furthermore, there are minor presentation issues. Figure 1 on page 7 lacks axis labels. The proof of Proposition 1, which constitutes a central contribution, would be more impactful if included in the main body of the paper rather than relegated to the supplementary material. Finally, on page 7, line 361, the phrase "the variational lower is..." appears to be incomplete.

In summary, this paper presents a clever and technically sound method for bounding the partition function of a DPP, potentially leading to faster inference and establishing connections to related models. The key strengths are the novel bound, its avoidance of spectral decomposition, and the resulting connections to variational inference and Gaussian process methodology. The primary weaknesses relate to the computational cost of the optimization required to tighten the bounds, and a lack of discussion surrounding the convergence properties of this optimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivation and proposed bounds appear rigorous and well-justified.
*   **Novelty:** The approach of using non-spectral bounds is a significant contribution, offering a different perspective on DPP inference.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The explicit link to inducing-point methods in Gaussian processes provides valuable context and potential for further research.
*   **Potential for Improvement:** The method could offer computational advantages over existing approaches, particularly in settings where spectral decomposition is expensive.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally very well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The practical benefits of replacing power iteration with nonlinear optimization are not fully demonstrated or convincingly argued.
*   **Convergence Concerns:** The convergence properties of the optimization over inducing points are not thoroughly addressed. A local minimum could severely impede MCMC performance.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the experiments are useful, they do not fully address the computational efficiency gains relative to spectral methods.



I believe this paper is currently a strong submission. Addressing the concerns regarding computational cost and convergence would significantly strengthen the work and increase my confidence in its practical impact. I would rate this as a **weak accept**. I encourage the authors to address the reviewer’s concerns thoroughly in a rebuttal.
