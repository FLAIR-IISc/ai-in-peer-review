- Paper develops nonspectral upper/lower bounds on log det(I+L) for DPPs, enabling variational and MCMC inference without eigenvalues.  
- Authors parameterize the L-kernel and use an inducing-point construction inspired by sparse GPs for finite domains.  
- Approach is extended to continuous DPPs by bounding the Fredholm determinant with inducing points and a tractable cross-covariance for Gaussian L and base measure.  
- Bounds drive a variational lower bound objective and a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings procedure that refines bounds until decisions are provable.  
- Determinant inequalities (Seiler–Simon) used to construct bounds are appropriate and novel in this context.  
- Finite-domain results link cleanly to inducing-point GP ideas and the continuous extension is practical for Gaussian kernels/measures.  
- Retrospective MH construction parallels prior spectral methods but avoids eigenvalue computations.  
- Synthetic 1D example validates correctness against known spectrum and determinant.  
- Real spatial dataset (diabetic neuropathy) shows stable overdispersion estimates and bound convergence with increasing inducing points.  
- Limited head-to-head comparisons with spectral bounding methods; scalability versus n, m, and dimensionality not fully quantified.  
- Continuous-case requires trace-class conditions and computable Ψ; paper outlines cases (e.g., Gaussian–Gaussian) but needs clearer summary of assumptions.  
- Paper is generally well written; moving a proof sketch into the main text would aid understanding.  
- Replacing spectral approximations with nonspectral determinant bounds and extending to continuous DPPs is original and well motivated.  
- Method broadens applicability of DPP inference when spectra are unavailable or costly and enables variational learning.  
- Strength: elegant, theoretically grounded determinant bounds that eliminate spectral computations.  
- Strength: unifying inducing-point GP perspective and practical variational objective.  
- Strength: continuous DPP extension with tractable cases and retrospective MH matching ideal MH in validation.  
- Weakness: computational advantage needs clearer empirical evidence, including wall-clock times and complexity vs. m.  
- Weakness: bound convergence tied to nonconvex optimization of inducing points; MH could stall if local optima prevent tightening.  
- Weakness: identifiability issues when parameterizing L directly; need guidance on priors/reparameterizations/constraints.  
- Weakness: continuous-case assumptions and scope (tr(L)=∫L(x,x)dµ and Ψ computability) need more explicit conditions beyond Gaussian–Gaussian.  
- Requests: provide complexity/memory profiles for variational training and retrospective MH as functions of n, m, d, and compare to spectral baselines.  
- Requests: report how often MH needed to increase m, by how much, acceptance rates, and total runtime.  
- Requests: explore robustness to inducing-point initialization, random restarts, and simple placement heuristics.  
- Requests: quantify bound tightness empirically (gap vs. m) and relate to effective sample size or MH acceptance.  
- Presentation fixes: add axis labels/units to Figure 1, move a proof sketch of the central determinant inequality to main text, and fix typo on p.7.  
- Overall assessment: strong, timely submission with novel nonspectral bounds and useful continuous extension; authors should address computational trade-offs, optimization robustness, and continuous-case assumptions; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
