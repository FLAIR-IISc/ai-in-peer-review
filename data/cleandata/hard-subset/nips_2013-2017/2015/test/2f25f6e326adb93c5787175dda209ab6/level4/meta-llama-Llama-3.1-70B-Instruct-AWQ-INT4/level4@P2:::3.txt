This paper proposes novel, cheap-to-evaluate, nonspectral bounds on the determinants arising in the likelihoods of determinantal point processes (DPPs), both finite and continuous. The authors demonstrate how to use these bounds to infer the parameters of a DPP and show their use for expensive-but-exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and cheap-but-approximate variational inference.

The main contribution of the paper is to derive bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that do not depend on the spectral decomposition of the operator L. These bounds are cheap to evaluate since they do not rely on approximating the spectrum of a large matrix or an operator. The authors show that these bounds can be used in a variational approach or an MCMC algorithm.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of inference for DPPs without spectral knowledge is clearly explained. The authors provide a thorough review of the relevant literature and clearly explain the advantages and limitations of their approach.

However, I found the method description rather rough and the empirical evaluation not thorough enough. A few points that may help clarify:

1. How does the computational complexity and overall compute time of the two proposed methods compare?
2. How do the proposed methods compare to other alternatives, both approximate and exact, in terms of quality of approximation and computation time?
3. Empirical results on the toy data in Section 5.1 show that parameter estimation error using variational inference is too large, and resorts to approximate MCMC instead. In what settings does the proposed variational inference expected to work and when is it expected to fail? Why?
4. A 13-point sample is generated for the toy example. How do the methods perform when the inducing inputs are actually these sampled points?
5. Is there a rule of thumb to follow when selecting a) the number of inducing points, b) placing the initial inducing points?

Minor comments:

* The matrices K and L are referred to throughout the introduction without explaining what they are. Briefly mentioning the marginal kernel and L-ensembles may make it easier to follow for an uninformed reader.
* There is discussion about the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters throughout the paper, but this is not the approach taken. I believe some of it could be cut back since it does not contribute to the rest of the paper.

Overall, I believe there is merit to this paper, but I encourage the authors to improve the write-up and address the above points to make the paper more clear and thorough.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to inference for DPPs without spectral knowledge.
* The authors provide a thorough review of the relevant literature.
* The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of inference for DPPs without spectral knowledge is clearly explained.

Weaknesses:

* The method description is rather rough.
* The empirical evaluation is not thorough enough.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other alternatives.

Originality:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to inference for DPPs without spectral knowledge.
* The authors derive new bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that do not depend on the spectral decomposition of the operator L.

Quality of writing:

* The paper is well-written, but the method description could be improved.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the matrices K and L.

Impact:

* The paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of machine learning and statistics.
* The proposed approach could be useful for a wide range of applications where DPPs are used.

Acceptance:

* I would accept the paper, but I strongly encourage the authors to improve the write-up and address the above points.
