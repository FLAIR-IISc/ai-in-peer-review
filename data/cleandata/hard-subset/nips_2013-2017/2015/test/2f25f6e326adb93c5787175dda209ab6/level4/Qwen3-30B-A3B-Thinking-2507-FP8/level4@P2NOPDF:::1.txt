

This paper introduces a novel approach for learning kernel parameters in determinantal point processes (DPPs), addressing a key challenge in DPP inference: the intractable normalization constant. The authors propose an elegant bound for the partition function that avoids eigenvalue estimation—unlike prior work [7]—enabling efficient MCMC inference and variational learning. The central insight, a new inequality providing a lower bound (Proposition 1), is technically sophisticated and perfectly suited to the problem. The connections to Gaussian process inducing-point methods for scalable inference are particularly valuable, offering a fresh perspective on DPP optimization. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and represents a significant contribution to the literature.

I have two technical concerns that require clarification:

First, while the variational approach is promising, the claim that replacing power iterations (in [7]) with cardinality-increasing nonlinear optimization constitutes a "huge computational win" is not compelling. The complexity of solving increasingly large nonlinear programs (with dimension growing as the cardinality increases) may offset any gains from avoiding eigenvalue estimation. The authors should provide concrete computational comparisons to substantiate this advantage.

Second, the convergence of the bounds as cardinality increases is not guaranteed. The optimization problem is highly non-convex, and without ensuring global minima (e.g., via multiple restarts or convex relaxations), local minima could prevent the bounds from adequately bracketing the partition function. This is critical because MCMC steps require bounds that straddle the Bernoulli threshold for valid sampling. The paper should explicitly address how global optimality is achieved or how local minima are mitigated to ensure the procedure remains valid.

The proof of Proposition 1 is the paper’s cornerstone insight and should be included in the main body, not the appendix. Its omission weakens the presentation. Additionally, the following presentation issues should be addressed:
- Page 7, Figure 1: Add axis labels to all subfigures.
- Page 7, Line 361: Correct typo ("the variational lower is" → "the variational lower bound is").

The paper’s core innovation—avoiding eigenvalue estimation via a tight partition function bound—holds significant promise for accelerating DPP inference. The variational framework and GP connections are well-executed and broadly applicable. With the above clarifications, this would be a strong contribution to NIPS. The technical merits outweigh the concerns, and the paper is a pleasure to read.
