The paper introduces approximate inference methods for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that circumvent the need for spectral analysis of the defining kernel. The approach leverages inducing inputs to bound the likelihood, thereby reducing computational cost. Both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) are presented, and their empirical efficiency is demonstrated.

While the paper presents a valuable contribution, I found the methodological descriptions somewhat lacking in detail and the empirical evaluation insufficiently comprehensive. To enhance clarity and assess the methods more fully, I suggest addressing the following points:

1) What is the computational complexity and overall runtime of the two proposed methods relative to each other?
2) How do the proposed methods compare with existing approximate and exact inference techniques for DPPs in terms of both approximation quality and computational efficiency?
3) The results in Section 5.1 reveal substantial parameter estimation error with variational inference, leading to a reliance on approximate MCMC. Under what conditions is the proposed variational inference expected to perform well, and when is it likely to fail – and why?
4) In the toy example, a 13-point sample is generated. How do the methods perform when these sampled points are used as the inducing inputs?
5) Are there guidelines or heuristics for selecting a) the number of inducing points and b) their initial placement?

Minor comments:

The introduction references matrices K and L without defining them. Briefly explaining their roles as the marginal kernel and relating them to L-ensembles would improve accessibility for readers unfamiliar with the background. 
The paper discusses the potential benefits of directly learning marginal kernel parameters, but this approach is not implemented. I recommend removing or significantly shortening this discussion as it appears tangential to the core contributions.
