In machine learning literature, Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) have recently gained considerable attention due to their ability to model the repulsion within the realities of a set of points. This repulsion is specified through a positive definite kernel function. However, a challenge lies in learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel, given the example point realities. This estimation is difficult due to a hard-to-compute generalization constant, which, in reality, sums over all possible cardinality and point configurations.

A recent proposal [7] has provided a way to do this by observing that the spectrum can be estimated and used to provide incremental upper and lower bounds, resulting in a proven-correct scheme for "backward" Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) of kernel parameters.

The current paper proposes a different type of bound that can also be used for MCMC estimation, but does not require the estimation of eigenvalues. This approach is also suitable for a variance approach to learning, and the paper establishes a connection with induced-point methods for scalable estimation in Gaussian process models.

This is a technically robust and well-written paper. I was unaware of the inequality that provides the lower bound in proposal 1, and it seems an excellent fit for this problem. This is a clever way to avoid estimation problems [7]. I like the connection with GP induced points, as well as the ability to now estimate variance. Overall, this paper was a pleasant read.

However, I have two technical concerns that I would like to see addressed. First, although I like this approach very much, I do not find it so appealing that it is a huge computational victory to replace power iterators with non-linear optimization problems of increasing dimension.

Second, it is not clear to me that a simple increase in cardinality guarantees convergence of the bounds, as this is a coupled optimization problem. Although I am not sure, I believe that this optimization is highly non-convex and therefore requires increasing the cardinality and ensuring that a global minimum is achieved.

This is something that should be addressed directly, as it seems that a local minimum process can prevent the Bernoulli threshold from binding and making it impossible to take the MCMC step.
