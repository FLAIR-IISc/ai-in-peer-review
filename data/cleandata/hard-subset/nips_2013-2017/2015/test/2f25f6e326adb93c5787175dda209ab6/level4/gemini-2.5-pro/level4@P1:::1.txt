This paper addresses the challenging problem of parameter inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). DPPs are powerful models for capturing repulsion or diversity in a set of points, with the repulsion structure defined by a positive definite kernel. A primary obstacle to learning the kernel's parameters from data is the intractable normalizing constant in the likelihood, which involves computing a large determinant. A recent approach [7] tackled this by using estimates of the kernel matrix's spectrum to form iterative upper and lower bounds on the likelihood, enabling a provably correct "retrospective" Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling scheme for the parameters.

The main contribution of this work is to introduce a different class of non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood. These bounds are derived from a determinantal inequality and are parameterized by a set of "inducing points," drawing a clear and insightful analogy to sparse or inducing-point approximations in Gaussian process (GP) models. A key advantage of this approach is that it avoids the need for eigenvalue computations. Furthermore, the proposed bounds are suitable not only for MCMC inference but also for a much faster, approximate variational inference scheme. The authors demonstrate the utility of these methods for DPPs defined on both finite and continuous domains.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The application of the determinantal inequality from [14] to derive non-spectral bounds for the DPP likelihood is a clever and elegant contribution. It provides a principled way to approximate the intractable term without resorting to costly spectral methods.

2.  **Connection to Gaussian Processes:** The connection drawn between this method and inducing-point approximations for scalable GPs is particularly insightful. This link helps to contextualize the work within the broader machine learning literature and may open up avenues for cross-pollination of ideas between the two fields.

3.  **Expanded Inference Capabilities:** A significant advantage of this work over previous methods is that it enables variational inference for DPPs. This provides a valuable trade-off between computational cost and accuracy, offering a fast, scalable, though approximate, alternative to MCMC for large-scale problems.

4.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically sound. The ideas are presented clearly, building from the finite to the continuous case, making the material accessible. The experimental validation is solid and convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses**

While the approach is compelling, there are two technical points that would benefit from further clarification.

1.  **Computational Trade-offs:** The paper suggests that this method offers a computational advantage over the spectral approach of [7]. However, the proposed algorithm replaces power iterations with a series of non-linear optimization problems to find the optimal locations for the inducing points. The dimensionality of this optimization problem grows with the number of inducing points required to tighten the bounds. It is not immediately obvious that this is a significant computational win in all scenarios. A more direct comparison of computational complexity or empirical runtime against the method in [7] would be needed to substantiate this claim fully.

2.  **Convergence of Bounds and Non-Convexity:** A more significant concern relates to the convergence of the bounds within the retrospective MCMC sampler. The procedure relies on successfully tightening the upper and lower bounds by increasing the number of inducing points and re-optimizing their locations. The objective function for this optimization is likely non-convex, meaning a standard optimizer may only find a local minimum. This raises a critical question: could a local minimum prevent the bounds from converging sufficiently to make an MCMC acceptance decision (i.e., to trap the log-uniform draw outside the acceptance ratio interval)? If the optimization can fail to find a sufficiently good configuration of inducing points, the sampler could become stuck, compromising its efficiency or even its correctness. The authors should address this possibility and discuss whether this is a problem in practice or why local optima are sufficient to guarantee progress.

**Minor Comments**

*   The proof of Proposition 1 is central to the paper's contribution. While space constraints are understood, including a brief proof sketch or the core intuition in the main text would greatly benefit the reader's understanding.
*   Figure 1 is missing axis labels, which would improve its clarity.
*   There is a minor typo on page 7, line 361: "the variational lower is..." should be corrected.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and valuable technique for inference in DPPs. It cleverly sidesteps the need for spectral decomposition and, in doing so, creates a strong link to scalable Gaussian processes and enables efficient variational inference. Addressing the concerns about the computational trade-offs and the impact of non-convexity would further strengthen this already impressive contribution.
