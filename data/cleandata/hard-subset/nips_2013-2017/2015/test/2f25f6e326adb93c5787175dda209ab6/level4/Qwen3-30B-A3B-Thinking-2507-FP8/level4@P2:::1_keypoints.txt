- Proposes novel nonspectral bounds on DPP likelihoods that remove the need for spectral decomposition, addressing a major computational bottleneck.
- Bounds depend only on pseudo-inputs (inducing points) and avoid eigenvalue estimation entirely.
- Applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs and integrated into variational inference and MCMC frameworks.
- Establishes a new determinant inequality (Proposition 1) providing tight, computationally efficient bounds on the normalizing constant.
- Draws clear, insightful connections to sparse Gaussian process inducing-point methods.
- Demonstrates effectiveness on a toy Gaussian continuous DPP and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset.
- Theoretical contributions (Propositions 1 and 2) are carefully developed and experimentally validated.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and makes complex concepts accessible.
- Originality is high: departs from prior spectral approaches and reuses GP techniques in a novel context.
- Significance: enables more scalable DPP inference and extends applicability to continuous domains, potentially enabling new applications.
- Strengths: elegant theory, clear GP connections, solid experiments, practical applicability, and strong exposition.
- Weaknesses: unclear computational advantage over spectral methods as optimizing pseudo-inputs may offset gains, especially with many pseudo-inputs.
- Weaknesses: convergence guarantees for the bound-refinement optimization are unclear; potential nonconvexity and local minima concerns need analysis.
- Weaknesses: central proof (Proposition 1) is in the supplement and should appear in the main text; minor presentation issues (missing axis labels in Figure 1, a typo).
- Overall recommendation: high-quality paper, recommended for acceptance provided the authors address convergence and computational-complexity concerns and fix presentation issues.
