

The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid requiring spectral knowledge of the defining kernel. Instead, it leverages inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood, reducing computational cost. Both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are presented, with empirical efficiency demonstrated.

While the paper has merit, the method description is somewhat rough, and the empirical evaluation lacks thoroughness. The following points require clarification:

1.  **Computational Complexity:** How do the computational complexities and overall runtime of the proposed variational inference and approximate MCMC methods compare?
2.  **Comparative Performance:** How do the proposed methods compare to both approximate and exact alternatives in terms of approximation quality and computational time?
3.  **Variational Inference Limitations:** The empirical results in Section 5.1 show that variational inference yields large parameter estimation errors on the toy data, necessitating a switch to approximate MCMC. Under what specific settings is the proposed variational inference expected to succeed, and when is it expected to fail? Why?
4.  **Inducing Points Selection:** How do the methods perform when the inducing inputs are exactly the 13-point sample used in the toy example (Section 5.1)?
5.  **Inducing Points Guidelines:** Is there a practical rule of thumb for selecting (a) the number of inducing points and (b) the initial placement of these points?

**Minor Comments:**
*   The introduction refers to matrices $K$ and $L$ without definition. Briefly clarifying the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would improve accessibility for less specialized readers.
*   The paper repeatedly discusses the advantages of directly learning marginal kernel parameters, yet this approach is not adopted. This discussion appears tangential and could be shortened or removed, as it does not contribute to the core methodological contribution.
