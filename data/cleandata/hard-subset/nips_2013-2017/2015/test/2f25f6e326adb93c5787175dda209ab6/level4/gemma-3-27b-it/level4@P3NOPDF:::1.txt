## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission

Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have recently gained significant attention in machine learning due to their ability to model repulsion between samples, a property governed by a positive definite kernel function. A central challenge lies in learning the parameters of this kernel from observed point realizations. This estimation is complicated by a computationally intractable normalization constant that requires summation over all possible cardinalities and point configurations. A recent work [7] addressed this through spectral estimation, enabling provably-correct retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for kernel parameter inference. 

This paper presents an alternative bounding strategy for MCMC inference that bypasses eigenvalue estimation. Furthermore, this approach naturally lends itself to variational inference and establishes connections to inducing-point methods commonly used for scalable inference in Gaussian process (GP) models. Overall, this is a technically strong and well-written contribution. I was previously unaware of the inequality leveraged in Proposition 1, and it appears particularly well-suited for tackling this problem. The proposed method offers a clever workaround to the estimation difficulties encountered in [7]. I particularly appreciate the insightful connections to GP inducing points and the potential for variational inference enabled by this work. The paper was a pleasure to read.

However, I have two technical concerns. First, while I find the approach conceptually appealing, it is not immediately clear that replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension represents a substantial computational improvement. The complexity of these optimization problems warrants further discussion and justification.

Second, the convergence of the bounds resulting from incrementally increasing the cardinality is not fully convincing. The optimization problem involved appears highly non-convex, and simply increasing the cardinality does not guarantee convergence to a global minimum. This is a critical point that needs to be addressed. A local minimum could prevent the procedure from adequately bounding the Bernoulli threshold, potentially hindering MCMC steps. Providing a theoretical analysis or empirical evidence supporting convergence, or outlining strategies to mitigate the risk of converging to a local minimum, would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Presentation Issues:**

*   Page 7, Figure 1: Please include axis labels for clarity.
*   The proof of Proposition 1 is the cornerstone of this work and represents a central insight. Moving it from the appendix to the main body of the paper would enhance readability and highlight its importance.
*   Page 7, Line 361: “… the variational lower is …” – minor grammatical correction needed.

In summary, this paper presents a clever and theoretically sound method for bounding the partition function of a DPP, potentially enabling faster inference and forging connections to other established modeling techniques. Addressing the concerns regarding computational complexity and convergence guarantees would further solidify this contribution and make it even more impactful. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
