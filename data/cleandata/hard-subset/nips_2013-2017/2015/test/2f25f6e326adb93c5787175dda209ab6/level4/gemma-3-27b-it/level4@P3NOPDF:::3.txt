This paper introduces approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that circumvent the need for spectral knowledge of the kernel function defining the DPP. The core idea is to leverage inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood, thereby reducing computational cost. The authors present both a variational inference (VI) approach and an approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method, and demonstrate their efficacy through empirical evaluation.

I find the approach presented in this paper to be potentially valuable, but believe the current manuscript would benefit from a more rigorous presentation of the methodology and a more comprehensive empirical analysis. While the core contribution—avoiding explicit spectral decomposition for DPP inference—is promising, the details surrounding the method and its practical limitations require further clarification.

Specifically, I have the following major concerns and questions:

1) **Computational Comparison:** The paper details two distinct inference methods (VI and approximate MCMC). A crucial aspect missing is a clear comparison of their computational complexity and overall runtime. How do the scaling properties of these methods differ? Providing a breakdown of the computational cost for each approach, even in terms of Big-O notation, would be highly beneficial.

2) **Benchmarking Against Alternatives:** The empirical evaluation would be significantly strengthened by a comparison to existing DPP inference methods. This should include both approximate inference techniques *and*, where feasible, exact inference methods.  The comparison should explicitly address both the quality of the approximation (e.g., log-likelihood, KL divergence) and the computational time required.  Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess the practical advantages of the proposed methods.

3) **Variational Inference Limitations:** The results presented in Section 5.1 indicate substantial parameter estimation error with the variational inference approach, leading the authors to resort to approximate MCMC. This suggests a limited applicability of the VI method. The paper needs to clearly delineate the settings in which the proposed variational inference is expected to perform well, and more importantly, *why* it fails in other scenarios.  What characteristics of the data or kernel function lead to inaccurate VI approximations? A deeper analysis of the VI’s failure modes is essential.

4) **Inducing Point Selection & Performance with Sampled Points:** In the toy example, a 13-point sample is used. It would be informative to evaluate the performance of the proposed methods when the inducing inputs *are* precisely these sampled points. This would provide a baseline understanding of how well the methods can recover the true DPP distribution given a reasonable set of inducing points.

5) **Guidance for Inducing Point Selection:** A practical consideration for applying these methods is the selection of inducing inputs.  The paper would benefit from providing some guidance, even in the form of rules of thumb, regarding: a) the appropriate number of inducing points to use, and b) strategies for initially placing these inducing points.  Are there any heuristics based on the kernel function or the expected data distribution that can aid in this selection process?

Minor Comments:

*   Throughout the introduction, the matrices *K* and *L* are referenced without initial definition. Briefly mentioning the marginal kernel (associated with *K*) and L-ensembles (related to *L*) would improve readability for readers unfamiliar with the specific DPP formulation.
*   The paper discusses the potential advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters, but ultimately does not pursue this approach. While exploring alternative directions is valuable, the discussion of this unpursued avenue feels somewhat tangential and could be trimmed to focus on the core contribution of the paper. 
*   The introductory paragraph is somewhat repetitive, stating the core idea twice.



In summary, this paper presents a potentially useful contribution to the field of DPP inference. However, the current presentation lacks sufficient detail and a thorough empirical evaluation. If accepted, I strongly recommend that the authors revise the manuscript to address the concerns outlined above, specifically focusing on clarifying the method’s details, providing a comprehensive comparison to existing methods, and analyzing the limitations of the variational inference approach.
