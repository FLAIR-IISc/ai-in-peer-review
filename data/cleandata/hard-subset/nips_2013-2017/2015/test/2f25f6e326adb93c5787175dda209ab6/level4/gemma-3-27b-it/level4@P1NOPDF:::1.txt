Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have recently gained significant attention in machine learning due to their ability to model repulsion between points in a set. This repulsion is governed by a positive definite kernel function. A key challenge, however, lies in learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel from example point realizations. This estimation is difficult due to a computationally intractable normalization constant that requires summing over all possible cardinalities and point configurations. A recent work [7] addressed this by demonstrating that the kernel’s spectrum can be estimated to provide incremental upper and lower bounds, leading to a provably correct scheme for “retrospective” Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) of the kernel parameters.

The present paper proposes an alternative bounding strategy for MCMC inference that avoids explicit eigenvalue estimation. This approach also naturally lends itself to variational inference, and the authors draw insightful connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models. This is a technically strong and well-written paper. I was previously unaware of the inequality underpinning the lower bound in Proposition 1, and it appears to be an excellent fit for this problem. It provides a clever way to circumvent the estimation difficulties of [7]. I particularly appreciate the connections to GP inducing points and the resulting ability to perform variational inference. Overall, this paper was a pleasure to read. 

However, I have two technical concerns that I would like the authors to address. First, while I find the approach appealing, I am not convinced that replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension represents a substantial computational advantage. Second, it is not clear to me that simply increasing the cardinality guarantees convergence of the bounds, given the coupled nature of the optimization problem. I suspect this optimization is highly non-convex, and therefore increasing cardinality alone may not be sufficient; one also needs to ensure a global minimum is achieved. This point should be addressed directly, as a local minimum could prevent the procedure from effectively bounding the Bernoulli threshold, thereby hindering MCMC step acceptance.

- The proof of Proposition 1 is central to the paper’s contribution and its core insight. I would have preferred to see it included in the main body of the paper, rather than in the appendix.

In summary, this is a compelling paper that uses a clever trick to bound the partition function of a determinantal point process, potentially leading to faster inference and connections to related models.
