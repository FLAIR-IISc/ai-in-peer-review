This paper introduces approximate inference methods for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that circumvent the need for spectral information of the kernel function. The approach leverages inducing inputs to bound the likelihood, thereby reducing computational expense. The authors detail both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques, and demonstrate their empirical performance.

While the work presents a valuable contribution, I found the methodological descriptions somewhat lacking in detail and the empirical evaluation insufficiently comprehensive. To strengthen the manuscript, I recommend addressing the following points:

1) Please provide a comparative analysis of the computational complexity and overall runtime for both proposed inference methods.
2) How do the proposed methods stack up against existing approximate and exact inference techniques with respect to both the quality of the approximation achieved and the computational time required?
3) The results in Section 5.1 reveal substantial parameter estimation error with variational inference, leading to a reliance on approximate MCMC. It would be beneficial to delineate the conditions under which the proposed variational inference is expected to succeed, and conversely, when it is likely to fail, along with a justification for these expectations.
4) In the toy example, a 13-point sample is generated. How do the methods perform specifically when these sampled points are used as the inducing inputs?
5) Can you offer practical guidance, such as a rule of thumb, for selecting a) the number of inducing points and b) their initial placement?

Minor comments:

Throughout the introduction, matrices K and L are referenced without initial definition. Briefly introducing the concepts of the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would improve readability for those less familiar with the field. 

The paper discusses the potential benefits of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters, but this approach is not ultimately implemented. I suggest streamlining the manuscript by removing this discussion, as it does not directly contribute to the presented work.

In summary, the paper proposes a promising approach, but would benefit from a more rigorous and detailed presentation. If accepted, I strongly recommend the authors prioritize a substantial revision to improve clarity and completeness.
