

Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have garnered significant attention in machine learning for modeling repulsion within point sets, specified via a positive definite kernel function. A key challenge lies in learning the kernel parameters from example realizations, complicated by a normalization constant that sums over all possible cardinalities and configurations—a difficult-to-compute quantity. A recent approach [7] addresses this via spectral estimation to derive incremental upper and lower bounds, enabling provably correct retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for kernel parameter inference. 

The present paper proposes an alternative bound for MCMC inference that avoids eigenvalue estimation, while also enabling variational learning. The authors draw compelling connections to inducing-point methods for scalable Gaussian process inference, which is a notable strength. The paper is technically strong, well-written, and presents a clever solution. I was particularly impressed by the inequality establishing the lower bound in Proposition 1—it is particularly well-suited to this problem and elegantly circumvents the estimation challenges in [7]. The connections to GP inducing points and the extension to variational inference are excellent contributions.

However, I have two technical concerns requiring clarification:  
1. The computational advantage of replacing power iterations with increasing-dimensional nonlinear optimization is not clearly established. It is not evident that this shift constitutes a substantial computational win over the spectral approach.  
2. The convergence guarantee for the bounds via increasing cardinality is unclear. The coupled optimization over cardinality and kernel parameters likely involves a highly non-convex problem. Without explicit guarantees that global minima are achieved (e.g., via multiple restarts or convex relaxations), local minima could prevent the bounds from sufficiently distancing themselves from the Bernoulli threshold, thereby blocking MCMC steps. This critical issue must be addressed.
