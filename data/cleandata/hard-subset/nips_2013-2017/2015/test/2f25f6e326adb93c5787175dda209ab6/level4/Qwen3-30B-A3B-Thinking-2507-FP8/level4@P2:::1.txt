

This paper addresses a significant challenge in probabilistic modeling: efficient inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), which are widely used to model repulsive point patterns. The authors propose novel, nonspectral bounds on the likelihood of DPPs that eliminate the need for spectral decomposition, a major computational bottleneck in existing approaches. While previous work [7] used spectral approximations to enable retrospective MCMC inference, this paper derives bounds that depend only on a set of pseudo-inputs (inducing points) and avoid eigenvalue estimation entirely. The bounds are applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs, and the authors demonstrate how they can be integrated into variational inference and MCMC frameworks, drawing insightful connections to sparse Gaussian process methods.

The paper makes a strong technical contribution by establishing a novel determinant inequality (Proposition 1) that provides tight, computationally efficient bounds on the intractable normalizing constant. This is a significant theoretical advance that has practical implications for DPP inference. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through two experiments: a toy Gaussian continuous DPP and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset. Their results show that the proposed bounds enable accurate inference while avoiding the spectral decomposition bottleneck.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical contribution (Propositions 1 and 2) is carefully developed and verified through experimental results. The authors provide a clear connection between their bounds and existing variational inference techniques for Gaussian processes, demonstrating how the proposed approach can be integrated into established frameworks. The experimental validation is thorough, with both synthetic and real-world data, and the results align with expectations from the theoretical framework.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of complex concepts. The connections to Gaussian process literature make the contribution accessible to a broad audience. The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to experimental validation. The main theoretical contribution (Proposition 1) is particularly elegant, though it would benefit from being presented in the main text rather than relegated to the supplementary material.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by providing bounds that do not require spectral knowledge, which is a significant departure from previous approaches [7]. The connection to inducing-point methods in Gaussian processes is insightful and demonstrates how techniques from one domain can be fruitfully applied to another. The authors carefully contextualize their work within the DPP literature, noting how their approach differs from previous parametrization strategies that favor tractability over interpretability.

**Significance:** This work addresses a fundamental challenge in DPP inference with practical implications for numerous applications, from news summarization [3] to spatial modeling of tree distributions [4]. By eliminating the need for spectral decomposition, the authors enable more scalable inference, particularly for large datasets. The extension to continuous domains (Section 4) broadens the applicability of their method to a wider range of problems. The proposed approach is not just a technical improvement but enables new applications where spectral decomposition would be prohibitively expensive.

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical contribution with elegant determinant inequality
- Clear connections to established Gaussian process methods
- Effective experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets
- Demonstrates practical applicability through two distinct case studies
- Well-written with excellent organization and explanation

**Weaknesses:**
- The computational advantage over spectral methods [7] is not fully convincing. Replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization of pseudo-inputs may not yield significant gains, especially as the number of pseudo-inputs increases. The paper should more thoroughly analyze the computational complexity trade-offs between the two approaches.
- The convergence guarantees for the bound refinement procedure are unclear. The authors optimize over both the pseudo-inputs and the kernel parameters, creating a highly non-convex optimization problem. It's not obvious that increasing the number of pseudo-inputs alone will guarantee convergence to tight bounds, as local minima could prevent the procedure from bounding away the Bernoulli threshold required for MCMC steps. The paper should provide more detailed analysis of this convergence behavior.
- The proof of Proposition 1, which is central to the paper's contribution, is relegated to the supplementary material. Given its importance, it should be presented in the main text.
- Some presentation issues: Figure 1 lacks axis labels, and there's a typo ("variational lower is" on page 7, line 361).

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**
- *Pro:* Strong theoretical contribution with practical implications, well-written, significant extension to continuous DPPs, clear connections to Gaussian process literature, thorough experimental validation.
- *Con:* Unclear computational advantage over existing methods, insufficient discussion of convergence guarantees for the bound refinement procedure, presentation issues with figure labels and proof location.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling. The theoretical insight is elegant and valuable, and the practical applications are well-demonstrated. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the convergence concerns and providing a more detailed computational complexity analysis. The presentation issues are minor but should be corrected. Given the paper's technical merit and relevance to NIPS, I recommend acceptance.
