- Paper proposes approximate DPP inference that avoids spectral decompositions by using inducing inputs to bound the likelihood and presents variational and approximate MCMC methods with empirical efficiency demonstrations.

- Major concern 1: Need detailed computational complexity and runtime comparison of the two proposed methods, ideally as a function of data points and inducing points.

- Major concern 2: Require direct comparisons to existing DPP inference methods (approximate and exact, e.g., power iteration) in both approximation quality and computation time.

- Major concern 3: Variational inference shows large parameter estimation error in the toy example; clarify when VI is expected to work or fail and explain underlying reasons for its limitations.

- Major concern 4: In the toy example, evaluate performance when inducing inputs are chosen as the sampled points to provide a baseline for inducing point optimization.

- Major concern 5: Provide practical guidance or rules of thumb for selecting the number of inducing points and initial placement strategies.

- Minor comment: Matrices K and L are introduced without sufficient explanation; briefly define K as the marginal kernel and relate to L-ensembles for accessibility.

- Minor comment: Discussion about directly learning marginal kernel parameters is lengthy but not pursued; streamline or remove tangential discussion.

- Argument for acceptance: Addresses practical challenge of avoiding costly spectral decompositions in DPP inference.

- Argument for acceptance: Proposed bounds enable promising approximate inference methods (variational and MCMC).

- Argument for acceptance: Empirical results, though limited, show feasibility on synthetic and real datasets.

- Argument for acceptance: Links to sparse Gaussian process approximations connect to existing literature.

- Argument against acceptance: Method description lacks sufficient detail and clarity in places.

- Argument against acceptance: Empirical evaluation is not comprehensive and misses strong comparisons to existing methods.

- Argument against acceptance: Limitations of the variational approach are not fully explored or explained.

- Argument against acceptance: Presentation could be more focused by removing nonessential discussions.

- Overall assessment: Potentially valuable contribution; reviewer leans weak accept conditional on substantial revisions addressing methodology clarity, empirical comparisons, and discussion of limitations.
