This paper introduces novel and computationally efficient upper and lower bounds on the determinant of a matrix, with a specific application to improving likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The core idea—leveraging pseudo-inputs to approximate the determinant—is straightforward yet insightful, offering a practical approach to a challenging problem in DPP modeling. The work is well-executed and has the potential to facilitate advancements in the field.

DPPs are increasingly used to model diversity, appearing in applications ranging from recommendation systems to spatial statistics. A key challenge in applying DPPs is learning the kernel parameters from data, complicated by the need to compute determinants that are often intractable. The authors address this by deriving bounds on the likelihood that avoid explicit spectral decomposition, a significant improvement over existing methods. This approach opens avenues for more scalable variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The proposed method is broadly applicable, extending beyond DPPs to scenarios requiring determinantal computations. The experimental validation, including a compelling demonstration of the limitations of variational inference and the effectiveness of MCMC, effectively illustrates the benefits of the proposed approach. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, building logically from the introduction of DPPs and the associated challenges to the presentation of the novel bounds and their application to inference. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work, referencing key papers in the fields of point processes, Gaussian processes, and numerical linear algebra. The inclusion of both theoretical results and empirical evaluation strengthens the paper's contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed bounds appear theoretically well-motivated and the derivations are clearly presented (with more detailed proofs available in the supplementary material).
*   **Computational Efficiency:** Avoiding spectral decomposition represents a significant practical advantage, making DPP inference more tractable for large datasets.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with all the nuances of DPPs.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods and highlight their advantages over existing techniques.
*   **Generality:** The approach has potential applications beyond DPPs.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Focus on K Parameterization:** While the abstract and introduction emphasize the issue of parameterizing the kernel *K* versus *L*, the paper primarily focuses on bounding the likelihood *given* a parameterization of *L*. A discussion of methods to directly bound the determinant in terms of *K* (even if preliminary) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Supplementary Material:** While a supplementary material is planned, the description suggests it will be an extended version of the paper rather than a concise collection of proofs and additional details. A more standard supplementary format would be preferable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound solution to a challenging problem in DPP modeling.
*   The proposed bounds offer significant computational advantages over existing methods.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach and provide valuable insights into the trade-offs between different inference techniques.
*   The work has the potential to impact a range of applications that rely on DPPs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough exploration of the implications of parameterizing *K* versus *L* and potential approaches to address this issue directly.
*   The supplementary material format should be improved for clarity and conciseness.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a valuable contribution to the field of determinantal point processes, providing a practical and efficient solution to a long-standing challenge. The authors have clearly articulated the problem, presented a novel and well-motivated solution, and demonstrated its effectiveness through rigorous experimentation. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper, but the current version is already of high quality and suitable for publication at NIPS.
