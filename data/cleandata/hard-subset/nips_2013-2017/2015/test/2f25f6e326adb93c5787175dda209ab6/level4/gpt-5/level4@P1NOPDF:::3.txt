This paper proposes approximate inference methods for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid requiring spectral knowledge of the kernel. The key idea is to use inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood, thereby reducing computational cost. The authors develop both a variational inference (VI) approach and an approximate MCMC scheme, and demonstrate their efficiency empirically.

I believe the paper has merit, but the method description is rough in places and the empirical evaluation is not sufficiently thorough. I have several questions and suggestions that could help clarify and strengthen the work:

1) Complexity and runtime: How do the computational complexity and wall-clock time of the two proposed methods compare across representative settings (e.g., varying dataset size, number of inducing points, and kernel hyperparameters)?

2) Baselines: How do the proposed methods compare to existing alternatives, both approximate and exact, in terms of approximation quality and computation time? A systematic comparison would help contextualize the gains and trade-offs.

3) VI reliability: In the toy experiment in Section 5.1, the parameter estimation error under VI is large, motivating a switch to approximate MCMC. In what regimes is the proposed VI expected to work well, and when is it likely to fail? Please provide intuition or analysis (e.g., dependence on the number/placement of inducing points, kernel smoothness, or data sparsity) to explain the observed behavior.

4) Choice of inducing inputs: For the toy example with a 13-point sample, how do the methods perform if the inducing inputs are chosen to be exactly these sampled points?

5) Practical guidance: Is there a rule of thumb for selecting (a) the number of inducing points and (b) their initial placement? Any sensitivity analysis or heuristics would be helpful for practitioners.

Minor comments:
- The matrices K and L are referenced in the introduction without definition. Briefly introducing the marginal kernel K and L-ensembles would make the paper more accessible to readers new to DPPs.
- There is substantial discussion of the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters, but this is not the approach the paper ultimately takes. Consider trimming this discussion to focus the narrative on the proposed methods and their implications.

Overall, the direction is promising and the idea of bounding the DPP likelihood with inducing inputs is interesting. However, clearer methodological exposition and more comprehensive empirical comparisons are needed to fully assess the contribution. If accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to improve the write-up.
