Determinantal point processes (DPPs) are gaining prominence in machine learning due to their capacity to model repulsive interactions between points in a set. This repulsion is governed by a positive definite kernel function, and a key challenge lies in estimating the parameters of this kernel from observed point realizations. This estimation is complicated by a computationally intractable normalization constant that requires summation over all possible cardinalities and point configurations. A recent work [7] addressed this by leveraging spectral estimation to derive incremental upper and lower bounds, enabling provably-correct retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for kernel parameter inference. 

This paper introduces an alternative bounding strategy for MCMC inference that avoids eigenvalue estimation. Furthermore, the proposed approach naturally extends to variational learning and establishes connections with inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models. The paper is technically sound and exceptionally well-written. The inequality underpinning the lower bound presented in Proposition 1 was novel to me and appears particularly well-suited to this problem, offering a clever alternative to the estimation challenges of [7]. I particularly appreciate the links to Gaussian process inducing points and the resulting possibility of variational inference. Overall, this work is a pleasure to read.

However, I have two technical concerns. First, while I find the approach appealing, I am not fully convinced that replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimensionality constitutes a substantial computational improvement. Second, the convergence of the bounds resulting from simply increasing the cardinality is not immediately clear, given the coupled nature of the optimization problem. This optimization appears potentially highly non-convex, and thus increasing cardinality alone may not guarantee convergence to a global minimum. This point requires further investigation, as a local minimum could hinder the ability to effectively bound the Bernoulli threshold and prevent successful MCMC steps.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a clever technique for bounding the partition function of a determinantal point process, potentially leading to faster inference and connections to related modeling frameworks.
