This paper introduces approximate inference methods for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid relying on the spectral decomposition of the kernel. The key idea is to use inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood, reducing computational cost. The authors present both a variational inference approach and an approximate MCMC method, and provide empirical evidence of their efficiency.

The paper addresses an important problem and the approach is promising. However, the methodological exposition is rough in places, and the empirical evaluation is not sufficiently thorough to assess when each method is preferable. Clarifications that would strengthen the paper include:

- Computational complexity and runtime: Please provide a clear comparison of the theoretical time and memory complexity of the variational and MCMC approaches (as functions of data size, number of inducing inputs, and kernel hyperparameters), along with wall-clock times on the same hardware. A breakdown of dominant costs (e.g., matrix factorizations, gradient steps, sampling steps) would be helpful.

- Comparison to alternatives: How do the proposed methods compare with existing exact and approximate inference techniques for DPPs, both in approximation quality (e.g., likelihood, parameter error, predictive performance) and compute time? Including baselines such as spectral methods where feasible would contextualize the gains.

- Behavior of variational inference: In Section 5.1, variational inference yields large parameter estimation error, leading you to favor approximate MCMC. Please characterize the regimes where variational inference is expected to succeed or fail (e.g., as a function of kernel smoothness, data size, number and placement of inducing inputs, or model misspecification). If the bound is loose in certain settings, explain why and whether tighter bounds or alternative variational families would help.

- Choice of inducing inputs: For the toy example with a 13-point sample, how do the methods perform when these sampled points are used directly as inducing inputs? More broadly, please report sensitivity to the number and placement of inducing inputs, ideally with ablations.

- Practical guidance: Provide rules of thumb for (a) selecting the number of inducing points (e.g., as a fraction of dataset size or based on spectrum decay or desired approximation error), and (b) initializing their locations (e.g., k-means, k-DPP, farthest-point heuristics, or optimizing inducing locations jointly with kernel parameters). Discuss trade-offs between accuracy and cost.

Minor comments:

- The introduction uses K and L without definition. A brief explanation of the marginal kernel K and L-ensembles would make the paper more accessible.

- The paper repeatedly emphasizes the advantages of directly learning marginal kernel parameters, yet this is not the adopted approach. Consider trimming or relocating this discussion to avoid distracting from the main contributions.

Overall, the paper has merit and addresses a relevant challenge with a potentially impactful idea. If accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to revise the exposition for clarity, expand the empirical comparisons, and provide practical guidance on complexity, runtime, and inducing-point selection.
