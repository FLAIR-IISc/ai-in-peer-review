

Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have gained significant traction in machine learning for modeling repulsion within point sets, specified via a positive definite kernel. A key challenge lies in learning the kernel parameters from example realizations, complicated by a normalization constant requiring summation over all possible point configurations and cardinalities. Recent work [7] addresses this via spectral estimation to derive provable upper and lower bounds for retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference.  

This paper proposes an alternative bound that enables MCMC inference without eigenvalue estimation, while also supporting a variational learning approach. The authors cleverly connect this to inducing-point methods in Gaussian process models, offering a promising path toward scalable DPP inference. The technical contributions are strong and well-executed. I was particularly impressed by the novel inequality establishing the lower bound in Proposition 1—it’s a precise fit for the problem and effectively sidesteps the estimation challenges in [7]. The linkage to GP inducing points is insightful, and the extension to variational inference is a valuable addition. Overall, this is a well-written, technically robust paper that’s a pleasure to read.  

I have two technical concerns that merit clarification:  
1. The computational advantage of replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization over increasing cardinalities is not clearly established. While the approach is elegant, it’s unclear whether the overhead of solving larger optimization problems constitutes a net win over [7]’s spectral method, especially as dimension grows. The paper should explicitly compare computational complexity or provide empirical evidence of speedup.  
2. The convergence of the bounds via increasing cardinality requires careful justification. Given the coupled optimization problem, the non-convexity of the objective (which appears likely) means local minima could trap the procedure. If a local minimum prevents the bounds from sufficiently separating from the partition function threshold, MCMC steps may fail. The authors should address how global convergence is ensured or discuss practical safeguards against this scenario.
