The Determinantal Point Process (DPP) has recently gained considerable attention in the field of machine learning because it is capable of modeling repulsion within the implementation of point sets.

This repulsion is specified through a positive definite kernel function.

However, a challenge is learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel given, for example, point implementations.

This is difficult to assume because there is a complex normalization constant that adds to virtually all possible cardinality and point configurations.

A recent proposal [7] has provided a way to do this. The proposal observes that the spectrum can be estimated and used to provide increasing upper and lower bounds, thereby creating a provably-correct scheme for the "retrospective" Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) of kernel parameters.

The present article proposes a different type of bound that can also be used for MCMC estimation, but does not require the estimation of eigenvalues.

This method is also suitable for a variational approach to learning, and this article connects with the inducing-point method for scalable estimation in Gaussian process models.

This is a technically strong and well-written article.

I was not aware of the inequality that provides the lower bound in proposal 1, and it seems like an excellent solution to this problem.

[7] It is a clever way to avoid the estimation problems.

I liked the connection with GP inducing points and now the ability to perform variational estimation.

Overall, this article was enjoyable to read.

However, I have two technical concerns that I would like to address.

Firstly, although I like this method very much, replacing power iterations with increasing degrees of non-linear optimization is a huge computational victory, but it does not seem very convincing to me.

Secondly, it is not clear to me that a general increase in cardinality will ensure the convergence of the bounds, as it is a coupled optimization problem.

Although I am not sure, I think this optimization is highly non-convex, and therefore it is necessary to increase the cardinality and ensure a global minimum is achieved.

This is something that should be addressed directly, as it seems that a local minimum method could prevent the Bernoulli threshold from binding and making it impossible to take the MCMC step.
