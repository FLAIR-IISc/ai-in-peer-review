Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have been receiving significant attention in the machine learning literature due to their ability to model repulsion within realizations of a set of points. This repulsion is specified via a positive definite kernel function. One challenge, however, is learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel, given example point realizations. This estimation is difficult due to a difficult-to-compute normalization constant that, in effect, sums over all possible cardinalities and point configurations.

The paper addresses this challenge by proposing novel bounds on the likelihood of DPPs, both for finite and continuous domains. These bounds do not rely on approximating the spectrum of a large matrix or an operator, making them cheaper to evaluate. The authors draw inspiration from bounds used for variational inference of Gaussian processes and extend these bounds to DPPs over continuous domains.

The paper is technically strong and well-written, and the proposed approach is a clever way to avoid the estimation issues of previous methods. The connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models are also well-drawn. The ability to perform variational inference is an added advantage.

However, there are two technical concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, it is not clear whether replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension is a significant computational win. Secondly, it is not obvious that simply increasing the cardinality of the bounds is guaranteed to result in convergence, due to the coupled optimization problem. This optimization problem appears to be highly non-convex, and it is essential to ensure that a global minimum is achieved to prevent the procedure from getting stuck in a local minimum.

To improve the presentation, it would be helpful to include axis labels for the figures on page 7. Additionally, the proof of the proposition, which is the central insight of the paper, could be included in the main body of the paper instead of the supplementary material.

Overall, the paper is well-written and proposes a clever approach to bounding the partition function of a DPP. The connections to other models and the ability to perform variational inference are significant advantages. Addressing the technical concerns and improving the presentation will further strengthen the paper.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to bounding the likelihood of DPPs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The approach is technically strong and well-written.
* The connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models are well-drawn.
* The ability to perform variational inference is an added advantage.
