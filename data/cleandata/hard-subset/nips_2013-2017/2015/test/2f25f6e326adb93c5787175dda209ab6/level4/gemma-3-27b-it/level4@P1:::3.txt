The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that do not require spectral knowledge of the kernel defining the DPP. Instead, inducing inputs are utilized to bound the likelihood, which reduces the computational cost. Both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques are described, and their efficiency is demonstrated empirically.

I believe there is merit to this paper, but I found the method description somewhat rough and the empirical evaluation not thorough enough. Here are some points that could help clarify the work and strengthen the presentation:

1) How does the computational complexity and overall compute time of the two proposed methods compare? Providing a more detailed analysis of the computational cost – perhaps relating it to the number of data points and inducing points – would be valuable.

2) How do the proposed methods compare to other alternatives, both approximate and exact, in terms of quality of approximation and computation time? A more direct comparison to existing DPP inference methods (e.g., those based on power iteration) would better highlight the advantages of this approach.

3) Empirical results on the toy data in Section 5.1 show that parameter estimation error using variational inference is too large, leading the authors to resort to approximate MCMC. In what settings is the proposed variational inference expected to work, and when is it expected to fail? What are the underlying reasons for these limitations?

4) In the toy example, a 13-point sample is generated. How do the methods perform when the inducing inputs are specifically chosen to be these sampled points? This would provide a useful baseline for evaluating the effectiveness of the inducing point optimization.

5) Is there a rule of thumb to follow when selecting a) the number of inducing points, and b) placing the initial inducing points? Providing guidance on these practical aspects of the method would be helpful for practitioners.

Minor comments:

The matrices K and L are referred to throughout the introduction without sufficient explanation. Briefly mentioning that K represents the marginal kernel and connecting it to the concept of L-ensembles might make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.

There is discussion throughout the paper regarding the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters, but this approach is ultimately not taken. I believe some of this discussion could be streamlined, as it doesn't directly contribute to the core contribution of the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a practical challenge in DPP inference: avoiding costly spectral decompositions.
*   The proposed bounds offer a promising approach to approximate inference, enabling both variational and MCMC methods.
*   The empirical results, while needing improvement, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   The connection to sparse Gaussian process approximation provides a valuable link to existing literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The method description lacks sufficient detail and clarity in places.
*   The empirical evaluation is not comprehensive enough, and lacks a strong comparison to existing methods.
*   The limitations of the variational inference approach are not fully explored or explained.
*   The paper could benefit from a more focused presentation, removing tangential discussions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of DPP inference. While the current presentation has some weaknesses, the core ideas are sound and the results are promising. If the authors address the concerns raised above – particularly by providing a more detailed methodological description, more comprehensive empirical evaluation, and a clearer discussion of the limitations of the approach – this paper could be a significant addition to NIPS. I currently lean towards a **weak accept**, contingent on substantial revisions addressing the points outlined above.
