The paper presents inference procedures for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that sidestep spectral decompositions. The key idea is to introduce inducing (pseudo-)inputs to obtain computable bounds on the DPP likelihood, thereby lowering computational cost. These bounds are then used within a variational objective and an approximate Metropolis–Hastings sampler via retrospective acceptance. The authors provide empirical demonstrations of efficiency.

I find the contribution promising, but the methodological exposition feels under-specified and the empirical evaluation insufficiently comprehensive. Clarifications that would strengthen the paper include:
1) A direct comparison of computational complexity and wall-clock runtime between the two proposed approaches (variational vs. MCMC), including how costs scale with n and the number of inducing points m.
2) A quantitative comparison against existing alternatives (both approximate and exact/spectral), reporting approximation quality and computational time on shared benchmarks.
3) In Section 5.1, variational inference exhibits large parameter errors and the paper falls back to approximate MCMC. Under what regimes (data size, kernel smoothness, identifiability, m) is the variational approach expected to succeed or struggle, and why?
4) The toy experiment uses a 13-point realization. How do the methods behave if the inducing set is chosen to be these observed points?
5) Practical guidance would be helpful: (a) heuristics for selecting m, and (b) strategies for initializing/placing inducing inputs.

Minor comments:
- K and L are used throughout the introduction before being clearly defined. A brief early explanation of the marginal kernel K and L-ensembles would aid readers unfamiliar with DPP conventions.
- The manuscript repeatedly motivates learning parameters of the marginal kernel, but ultimately operates in the L-ensemble parameterization. Some of that discussion could be trimmed or refocused to better align with the chosen approach.
- Inducing inputs are introduced to bound the DPP likelihood and lower computation, and both the variational and approximate MCMC instantiations are empirically illustrated. The paper has merit, but the presentation would benefit from tightening and additional experimental depth. If accepted, I recommend a substantial revision to improve clarity.
