Review- This paper introduces approximate inference methods for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that obviate the need for spectral information about the defining kernel. The approach uses inducing points to bound the likelihood, thereby reducing computational cost. The authors present both a variational inference procedure and an approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method, and provide empirical demonstrations of their efficiency.
I see value in this work, but the methodological description is somewhat rough and the empirical study is not sufficiently comprehensive. Clarifications that would help include:
1) How do the two proposed methods compare in terms of computational complexity and overall wall-clock time?
2) How do the proposed approaches compare to alternative methods—both approximate and exact—in terms of approximation quality and runtime?
3) In the toy experiment of Section 5.1, variational inference exhibits large parameter estimation error and the paper switches to approximate MCMC. Under what conditions is the proposed variational method expected to succeed, and when is it likely to fail? Why?
4) The toy example uses a 13-point sample. How do the methods perform if the inducing inputs are chosen to be exactly these sampled points?
5) Is there a practical rule of thumb for (a) selecting the number of inducing points and (b) initializing their locations?

Minor comments:
- The matrices K and L are referenced throughout the introduction without definition. Briefly introducing the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would aid readers unfamiliar with the terminology.
- The paper frequently discusses the benefits of directly learning marginal-kernel parameters, yet this is not the adopted approach. Consider trimming this discussion if it does not inform the proposed methods.

Overall, the paper leverages inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood for computational gains and presents both variational and approximate MCMC solutions with empirical evidence. The contribution is promising, but the method presentation feels rough and the evaluation not thorough enough. If accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to improve the write-up.
