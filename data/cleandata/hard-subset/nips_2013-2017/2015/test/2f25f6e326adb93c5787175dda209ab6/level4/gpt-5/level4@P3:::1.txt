Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes nonspectral upper and lower bounds on the DPP likelihood for both finite and continuous domains. The bounds replace the intractable determinant det(I + L) with terms based on an inducing-point approximation Q = LYZ L−1Z LZY and a trace correction, inspired by variational sparse GP methods (Titsias, AISTATS 2009). The approach enables two inference schemes: (i) a variational lower bound for approximate maximum likelihood, and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler that tightens bounds on demand until an accept/reject decision can be made, analogous in spirit to the spectral-bounds MCMC in Affandi et al. (ICML 2013) but without estimating eigenvalues. For continuous DPPs, the paper provides analogues that use Fredholm determinants, with computable Ψ terms for common Gaussian/Gaussian settings. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian toy example (where the spectrum is known) and a diabetic neuropathy dataset, demonstrating both the retrospective MCMC and the variational approach.

Strengths
- Originality: A clean, general-purpose set of nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods, building on an inequality among determinants (Seiler & Simon, PNAS 1975), and extended to continuous DPPs. The link to inducing points in GPs is elegant and timely.
- Quality: The bounds are theoretically well-motivated, easy to evaluate, and lead to practical inference procedures. The retrospective MCMC is a correct-by-construction adaptation using interval tests on the acceptance probability. The continuous-domain extension is carefully framed with appropriate operator-theoretic assumptions.
- Significance: Removing spectral computations broadens the applicability of DPP inference to settings where eigenvalue estimation is slow or infeasible, and opens the door to scalable approximate training. The continuous setting is particularly valuable for spatial statistics applications.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written, and the connections to GP variational methods improve intuition about the role of inducing points.

Weaknesses and concerns
1) Computational advantage claims. While avoiding power iterations is appealing, the proposed method introduces nonlinear optimization over an increasing number of inducing points. It is not obvious that this is a net win across regimes. The paper reports reasonable runtimes, but a head-to-head comparison with the spectral-bounds approach (e.g., wall-clock time per MH accept/reject decision vs. dataset size, and as a function of bound tightness) would quantify the trade-off. An ablation showing time per iteration and convergence of the bound with m, as well as sensitivity to the number of Z-optimization steps, would help substantiate the computational benefits.

2) Convergence of bounds under optimization. The retrospective MH procedure assumes that tightening the bounds by enlarging Z will eventually separate log u from the interval. With optimized Z, the objective is nonconvex, and local minima could, in principle, stall the process. Two clarifications would mitigate this concern:
- Finite case: note explicitly that if Z is expanded to Y, then Q = L and the bounds become equalities, guaranteeing termination. Discuss practical strategies (e.g., nested Z, multiple restarts, jitter/regularization of LZ, or warm-starting from previous Z) to avoid poor local optima during intermediate m.
- Continuous case: provide a theorem or citation establishing convergence of the bounds as m increases and Z becomes dense in X under mild conditions (the paper hints at this via GP posterior variance arguments). If a proof is out of scope, empirical evidence showing monotone tightening for nested Z and multiple initializations would be useful.

3) Identifiability and parameterization. The 1D example nicely illustrates that κ is weakly identified when parameterizing L directly. It would be helpful to discuss practical guidance: e.g., informative priors on κ, reparameterizations that separate intensity and repulsion, or diagnostics indicating when VI is unreliable relative to MCMC.

4) Assumptions and numerical details. For the continuous case, clearly state when Ψ is available in closed form (e.g., Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures) and provide pointers for numerical evaluation otherwise. In the finite case, mention the need for a numerically stable inverse of LZ (e.g., adding jitter).

Quality assessment (per NIPS criteria)
- Technical soundness: Strong. The bounds rest on a solid determinant inequality, and the retrospective MH adaptation is standard and correct provided the bounds can be tightened.
- Clarity: Good overall; see presentation suggestions below.
- Originality: High. Nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods and their continuous analogue appear novel.
- Significance: Good to high. The approach offers practical advantages and extends to continuous DPPs, which are common in spatial applications.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a runtime comparison with [7] across a range of n, m, and accuracy targets, including the average number of inducing points required per MH decision and acceptance rates.
- For retrospective MH, describe a practical strategy for increasing m (e.g., doubling) and optimizing Z (e.g., limited LBFGS steps, warm starts, multiple random restarts), and report how often restarts are needed.
- Include a plot of bound tightness vs. m for nested Z (finite and continuous cases), and report whether monotonic tightening is observed.
- Move at least the finite-domain proof (Proposition 1) to the main text, or provide a more detailed proof sketch; the inequality is central to the contribution.
- Discuss initialization of Z (e.g., k-means on Y, sampling from the base measure) and its impact on convergence.
- Consider releasing code or detailed pseudocode for reproducibility.

Presentation issues
- Figure 1 (p.7): Please add axis labels.
- The proof of the key proposition is central; consider moving it from the appendix to the main body or expanding the sketch.
- p.7, l.361: “the variational lower is ...” → “the variational lower bound is ...”.
- Ensure consistent notation for submatrices (e.g., LYZ vs. LYZ) and define all terms (e.g., Ψ) where first used.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel, simple, and effective nonspectral bounds applicable in both finite and continuous settings.
- Enables both variational training and correct retrospective MCMC without eigenvalue computations.
- Clear connections to GP inducing-point methods; good empirical demonstrations.

Cons:
- Limited quantitative comparison to spectral-bounds baselines; computational advantage not fully substantiated.
- Convergence under nonconvex Z-optimization could be fragile without further guidance or guarantees.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The contribution is technically sound and potentially impactful, particularly for continuous DPPs. Strengthening the empirical comparison, clarifying convergence behavior, and elevating the core proof would make the paper even stronger.
