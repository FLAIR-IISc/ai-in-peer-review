This paper addresses the challenging problem of parameter inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The primary difficulty in likelihood-based inference for DPPs is the computation of the normalizing constant, which involves the determinant of a large matrix (in the finite case) or a Fredholm determinant (in the continuous case). While previous work has tackled this by approximating the kernel's spectrum, this can be computationally demanding and is often limited to finite domains.

The main contribution of this work is the derivation of novel upper and lower bounds on the DPP likelihood that do not require any spectral information. Inspired by sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes, the authors introduce a set of "inducing points" to construct these bounds. The key advantage is that the bounds are significantly cheaper to compute than the exact likelihood or its spectral approximations. The authors demonstrate how these non-spectral bounds can be integrated into two standard inference frameworks: a fast, approximate variational inference (VI) scheme and a more computationally intensive but exact Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm using retrospective sampling. The proposed methods are developed for DPPs on both finite sets and continuous domains.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of using inducing points to create non-spectral bounds for the DPP likelihood is novel and significant. It provides a valuable alternative to existing spectral-based approximation techniques [7], potentially offering a better trade-off between computational cost and accuracy in certain settings. This could make DPP inference more accessible for larger-scale problems.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the bounds, which relies on a determinant inequality from [14], appears to be correct. The application of these bounds to construct both a variational lower bound and a retrospective MCMC sampler is methodologically sound and well-motivated.

3.  **Generality:** The framework is developed for both discrete and continuous DPPs, which broadens its applicability. The continuous case, in particular, is often more challenging, and providing a practical inference method for it is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

While the core idea is strong, the paper could be significantly improved in its methodological description and, most importantly, its empirical evaluation. The current experiments demonstrate that the method works but do not provide sufficient context or analysis to fully understand its advantages and limitations.

**1. Clarity and Methodological Details:**

*   For readers not deeply familiar with DPPs, the introduction could be made more accessible by briefly defining the roles of the kernels `K` (the marginal kernel, which is more interpretable) and `L` (the L-ensemble kernel, which is more tractable) upfront.
*   The paper spends considerable time discussing the benefits of parameterizing `K` but ultimately parameterizes `L`, following common practice. To improve focus, the introduction could be streamlined to more quickly motivate the choice of parameterizing `L` and the computational bottlenecks that this paper solves.

**2. Empirical Evaluation:**

The empirical evaluation is the weakest part of the paper and needs to be strengthened to substantiate the claims of the method's utility.

*   **Lack of Baselines:** The paper does not compare its proposed methods against any alternatives. A crucial comparison would be against the spectral-bound MCMC approach of Affandi et al. [7]. How does the proposed non-spectral MCMC compare in terms of wall-clock time, convergence speed, and the quality of the posterior estimates? Such a comparison would clearly position the contribution of this work.
*   **Insufficient Internal Comparison:** The paper proposes two distinct methods (VI and MCMC) but does not compare them. What is the computational complexity and typical runtime difference between them on the experimental tasks? The experiments show VI failing on the toy problem, forcing a switch to MCMC. This is an important finding but is not analyzed. The authors should investigate and discuss *why* VI fails in this setting. Is the variational bound too loose? Is the joint optimization over kernel parameters and inducing points unstable? Understanding the failure modes of the VI approach is critical for a user to know when to apply it.
*   **Guidance for Practitioners:** The performance of the method depends heavily on the number and placement of the inducing points (`m` and `Z`). The paper shows that results converge as `m` increases but provides little practical guidance.
    *   How should one choose the number of inducing points, `m`, in practice? Is there a principled way or a useful heuristic?
    *   How sensitive is the method to the initial placement of the inducing points? The paper mentions optimizing their locations but doesn't discuss the initialization strategy.
*   **Additional Analysis:**
    *   On the toy example in Section 5.1, a 13-point sample is generated from the true DPP. A simple and insightful experiment would be to fix the inducing points to be these 13 observed data points. How does the resulting likelihood bound and parameter inference compare to when the inducing point locations are optimized? This could provide insight into the nature of the optimal inducing points.

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel and promising approach for approximate inference in DPPs by using non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds on the likelihood. The idea is original and addresses a key computational bottleneck in the field. However, the paper in its current form is weakened by an empirical evaluation that lacks comparisons to relevant baselines and a deeper analysis of the proposed methods' properties and failure modes.

I believe the paper has merit and the core contribution is valuable. I would be willing to raise my score if the authors can substantially strengthen the experimental section by including comparisons to prior work (especially [7]), providing a more thorough analysis of the trade-offs between their proposed VI and MCMC methods, and offering more practical guidance on the choice and placement of inducing points.
