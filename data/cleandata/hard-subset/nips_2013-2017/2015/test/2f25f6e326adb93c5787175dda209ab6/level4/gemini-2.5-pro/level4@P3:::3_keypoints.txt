- Addresses scalable likelihood-based inference for DPPs by bounding the normalizer det(L + I), avoiding spectral computations.  
- Proposes novel non-spectral bounds using inducing inputs (inspired by sparse GP approximations).  
- Develops two inference algorithms from the bounds: a fast variational inference (VI) method and an exact-in-the-limit retrospective MCMC sampler.  
- Framework is extended to both finite-set and continuous-domain DPPs.  
- Original and significant: offers an efficient alternative to partial eigendecomposition approaches (e.g., Affandi et al. 2014) with an elegant GP connection.  
- Theoretical contributions appear sound, with bounds founded on a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon 1975).  
- Major empirical weakness: evaluation lacks rigor and adequate comparative analysis.  
- No baselines included—must compare to spectral-bound MCMC (Affandi et al. 2014) to quantify speed/accuracy trade-offs.  
- VI performs poorly/ inconsistently on a toy problem; the paper fails to analyze why (looseness of bound, optimization issues, local minima, etc.).  
- Experiments are limited (one synthetic, one real) and insufficient to demonstrate general robustness.  
- Clarity issues: introduction could better explain K vs L for non-experts and streamline discussion of marginal-kernel interpretability.  
- Needs practical guidance on inducing inputs: how to choose m, initialization/placement strategies, and relation to Nyström/subset-based methods.  
- Recommendation: add empirical baseline comparisons, deeper analysis of VI failure modes, and practical guidance on inducing-point choices.  
- Overall: promising and original, but practical impact unclear without stronger empirical support; reviewer would reconsider if concerns are addressed.
