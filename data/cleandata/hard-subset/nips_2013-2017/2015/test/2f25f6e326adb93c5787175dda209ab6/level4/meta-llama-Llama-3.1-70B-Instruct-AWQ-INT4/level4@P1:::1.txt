Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have been receiving significant attention in the machine learning literature due to their ability to model repulsion within realizations of a set of points. This repulsion is specified via a positive definite kernel function. One challenge, however, is learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel, given example point realizations. This estimation is difficult due to a difficult-to-compute normalization constant that, in effect, sums over all possible cardinalities and point configurations.

The authors address this challenge by proposing a novel approach to bounding the likelihood of a DPP. Unlike previous work, their bounds do not require the estimation of eigenvalues, making them more computationally efficient. The authors draw inspiration from sparse approximations to Gaussian processes using inducing variables and propose a lower and upper bound on the likelihood of a DPP. These bounds can be used for both variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods.

The paper is technically strong and well-written, making it a pleasure to read. The connection to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models is particularly noteworthy. The authors' use of a clever inequality to provide the lower bound in Proposition 1 is an excellent fit for this problem, avoiding the estimation issues of previous work.

However, there are two technical concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, while the authors' approach may be more computationally efficient than previous work, it is not entirely clear whether replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension is a significant computational win. Secondly, the coupled optimization problem may be highly non-convex, and increasing the cardinality of the bounds may not guarantee convergence. The authors should address these concerns directly and provide more insight into the optimization process.

Additionally, there are a few presentation issues that need to be addressed. Figure 1 on page 7 is missing axis labels, and the proof of the proposition, which is the central insight of the paper, is relegated to the supplementary material. It would be beneficial to include the proof in the main body of the paper.

Overall, the paper proposes a novel and efficient approach to bounding the likelihood of a DPP, making it a valuable contribution to the field of machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and efficient approach to bounding the likelihood of a DPP.
* The approach avoids the estimation issues of previous work and makes connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models.
* The paper is technically strong and well-written.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The computational efficiency of the approach is not entirely clear.
* The coupled optimization problem may be highly non-convex, and increasing the cardinality of the bounds may not guarantee convergence.
* The presentation could be improved, particularly with regards to the proof of the proposition and the axis labels in Figure 1.
