Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have recently received significant attention in the machine learning literature for their ability to model repulsion among items in a selected subset. This repulsion is specified via a positive definite kernel function. A key challenge, however, is learning the parameters of this kernel from examples of realized sets. This estimation is difficult because it requires computing a normalization constant that involves a sum over all possible subset cardinalities and configurations.

A recent method [7] addresses this by estimating the kernel's spectrum to derive incremental upper and lower bounds on the normalization constant. This results in a provably-correct "retrospective" Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) scheme for sampling the kernel parameters. The present paper proposes a different type of bound that can also be used for MCMC inference but does not require estimating the eigenvalues. This approach also facilitates a variational framework for learning and draws insightful connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models.

This is a technically strong and well-written paper. The inequality used to derive the lower bound in Proposition 1 is a clever and excellent fit for this problem, elegantly avoiding the eigenvalue estimation issues present in [7]. The connections drawn to GP inducing points are insightful, and the extension to variational inference is a significant contribution. Overall, this paper was a pleasure to read.

Despite the strengths, I have two technical concerns that I would like to see addressed.

First, the claim of a significant computational advantage is not entirely convincing. The proposed method replaces the power iterations of [7] with a series of nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension. It is not immediately clear that this trade-off results in a substantial net gain, and a more thorough comparison is warranted.

Second, the convergence of the bounds as the cardinality of the inducing set increases is not rigorously established. This optimization problem appears to be highly non-convex, which raises concerns about finding the global optimum required to guarantee convergence. This issue warrants direct discussion, as getting stuck in a local minimum could prevent the bounds from tightening sufficiently to surpass the Bernoulli threshold, thereby stalling the MCMC sampler. A discussion of the practical impact of local optima on the inference procedure would strengthen the paper.
