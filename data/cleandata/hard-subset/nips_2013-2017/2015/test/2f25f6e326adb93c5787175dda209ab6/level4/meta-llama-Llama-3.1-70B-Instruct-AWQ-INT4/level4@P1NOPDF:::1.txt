Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have garnered significant attention in the machine learning literature due to their ability to model repulsion within realizations of a set of points, which is specified via a positive definite kernel function. However, learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel from example point realizations poses a challenge. The primary difficulty lies in estimating a normalization constant that sums over all possible cardinalities and point configurations, making it computationally intractable.

A recent proposal [7] has addressed this issue by estimating the spectrum and using it to derive incremental upper and lower bounds, resulting in a provably-correct scheme for retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference of the kernel parameters. In contrast, the present paper proposes a different type of bound that can be used for MCMC inference without requiring eigenvalue estimation. This approach also lends itself to a variational method for learning and draws connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models.

The paper is technically strong and well-written, making it a pleasure to read. The inequality used to derive the lower bound in Proposition 1 is particularly noteworthy, as it provides an excellent fit for this problem and cleverly avoids the estimation issues present in [7]. The connections to Gaussian process (GP) inducing points, as well as the ability to perform variational inference, are also commendable.

However, I have two technical concerns that I would like to see addressed. Firstly, while I appreciate the proposed approach, I am not convinced that replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension is a significant computational win. Secondly, it is unclear whether simply increasing the cardinality guarantees convergence of the bounds, given the coupled optimization problem. I suspect that this optimization problem is highly non-convex, and therefore, increasing the cardinality alone may not ensure global optimality. This is a crucial point, as a local minimum could prevent the procedure from bounding away the Bernoulli threshold, making it impossible to take an MCMC step.

Presentation-wise, I have a few suggestions:

- Figure 1 on page 7 is missing axis labels, which should be included for clarity.
- The proof of the proposition, which is the central insight of the paper, would be more accessible if it were included in the main body of the paper rather than the appendix.
- On page 7, line 361, the phrase "the variational lower is" could be rephrased for better clarity.

Overall, the paper presents a clever trick for bounding the partition function of a DPP, leading to potentially faster inference and connections to other models.
