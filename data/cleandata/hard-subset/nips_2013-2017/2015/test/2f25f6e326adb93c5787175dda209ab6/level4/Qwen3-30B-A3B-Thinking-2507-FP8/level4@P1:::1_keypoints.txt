- Problem: DPP learning is hard because the likelihood normalization requires computing large determinants or Fredholm determinants.  
- Contribution: Derives novel non-spectral bounds on these determinants that avoid eigenvalue computations and spectral decompositions.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on Affandi et al. (ICML 2013) but offers a non-spectral computational pathway.  
- Main technical result: Proposition 1 provides tight lower and upper determinant bounds via a determinant inequality.  
- Practical use: Bounds are efficient to evaluate and integrate into variational inference and MCMC algorithms.  
- Extension: Presents an analogous bound for continuous DPPs (Proposition 2).  
- Insight: Draws a clear and useful connection to inducing-point methods in Gaussian processes.  
- Writing and presentation: Paper is technically strong, well-written, and carefully considers computational implications and pseudo-input optimization.  
- Experiments: Validated on synthetic and real datasets (including diabetic neuropathy), showing effectiveness and convergence as pseudo-inputs increase.  
- Strengths summary: Novel theoretical contribution, practical applicability, clear exposition, and strong experiments.  
- Major concern 1: Computational efficiency — optimizing pseudo-inputs may scale poorly and might not offer a clear advantage over spectral methods; needs concrete complexity comparisons for large-scale problems.  
- Major concern 2: Convergence guarantees — unclear whether bounds reliably tighten as pseudo-inputs increase given non-convex optimization; need guarantees or clarification for MCMC acceptance.  
- Minor issues: Proof of Proposition 1 is in the supplement but should be in the main paper; Figure 1 lacks axis labels; fix typo "the variational lower is" → "the variational lower bound."  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address computational-efficiency comparisons and presentation issues.
