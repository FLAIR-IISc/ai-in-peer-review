The paper "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge" presents a novel approach to learning the parameters of determinantal point processes (DPPs), a class of point processes that naturally encode diversity between points. The authors address the challenge of estimating the normalization constant, which is difficult to compute due to its dependence on the spectral decomposition of the kernel operator. The proposed method avoids this issue by deriving bounds on the likelihood that do not require spectral knowledge.

The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to DPPs, their properties, and the challenges associated with likelihood-based inference. They also review existing methods, including the approach proposed in [7], which estimates the spectrum to provide incremental upper and lower bounds for Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The current paper proposes a different type of bound that can be used for MCMC inference without requiring eigenvalue estimation.

The technical contribution of the paper is the derivation of nonspectral bounds on the likelihood, which is achieved through a nontrivial inequality on determinants. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by applying it to both finite and continuous DPPs. They also draw connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its advantages. The experimental results, including the application to a real-world dataset, demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel approach to learning the parameters of DPPs without requiring spectral knowledge.
* The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to DPPs and their properties.
* The technical contribution of the paper is significant, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments.
* The connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models are valuable.

Weaknesses:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly in comparison to existing approaches.
* The optimization problem involved in the proposed method is highly non-convex, and the authors could provide more insight into how to ensure convergence to a global minimum.
