

The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that do not require spectral knowledge of the defining kernel. Instead, inducing inputs are employed to bound the likelihood, reducing computational cost. Both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are presented, with empirical efficiency demonstrated. While the paper has merit, the method description is somewhat superficial and the empirical evaluation is not sufficiently thorough. Key clarifications are needed:  
1) What is the computational complexity and actual runtime comparison between the two proposed methods?  
2) How do the proposed methods compare to other approximate and exact alternatives in approximation quality and runtime?  
3) In Section 5.1, variational inference yields large parameter estimation errors on toy data, necessitating a switch to approximate MCMC. Under what conditions does variational inference succeed or fail, and why?  
4) How do the methods perform when inducing inputs are exactly the 13 sampled points used in the toy example?  
5) Are there practical guidelines for selecting (a) the number of inducing points and (b) their initial placement?  

Minor comments:  
- Matrices $K$ and $L$ are referenced without definition in the introduction; briefly clarifying the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would aid readability.  
- The paper emphasizes advantages of directly learning marginal kernel parameters but does not adopt this approach; some related discussion could be omitted as it does not support the core contribution.  
The statement "Inducing inputs are utilized to bound the DPP likelihood..." is redundant with the earlier description and should be removed. I believe there is merit to this work, but if accepted, the authors must substantially improve the write-up and empirical validation.
