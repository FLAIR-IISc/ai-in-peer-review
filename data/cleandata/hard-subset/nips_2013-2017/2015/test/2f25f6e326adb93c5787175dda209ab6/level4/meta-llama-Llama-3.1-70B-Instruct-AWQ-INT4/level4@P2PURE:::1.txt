Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have garnered significant attention in the machine learning literature due to their ability to model repulsion within realizations of a set of points, which is specified via a positive definite kernel function. However, learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel from example point realizations poses a challenge, primarily due to the difficult-to-compute normalization constant that sums over all possible cardinalities and point configurations.

A recent proposal [7] addressed this issue by estimating the spectrum to provide incremental upper and lower bounds, resulting in a provably-correct scheme for "retrospective" Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) of the kernel parameters. In contrast, the present paper proposes a different type of bound that can also be used for MCMC inference without requiring eigenvalue estimation. This approach lends itself to a variational approach to learning and draws connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models.

The paper is technically strong and well-written. The inequality used to provide the lower bound in Proposition 1 is an excellent fit for this problem and offers a clever way to avoid the estimation issues of [7]. The connections to GP inducing points and the ability to perform variational inference are notable strengths.

However, I have two technical concerns that I would like to see addressed. Firstly, while I appreciate this approach, I am not convinced that replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension is a significant computational win. Secondly, it is unclear to me whether simply increasing the cardinality guarantees convergence of the bounds due to the coupled optimization problem. I suspect that this optimization problem is highly non-convex, and therefore, increasing cardinality alone may not be sufficient to achieve a global minimum. This is a critical issue, as a local minimum could prevent the procedure from bounding away the Bernoulli threshold, making it impossible to take an MCMC step.

Presentation Issues:

* Figure 1 on page 7 is missing axis labels.
* The proof of the proposition, which is the central insight of the paper, would be more accessible if it were included in the main body rather than the appendix.
* On page 7, line 361, the phrase "...the variational lower is..." could be rephrased for clarity.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that uses a clever trick to bound the partition function of a determinantal point process, leading to potentially faster inference and connections to other kinds of models.
