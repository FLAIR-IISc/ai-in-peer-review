### Review Summary

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the parameters of a determinantal point process (DPP) from data, which is hindered by an intractable normalizing constant in the likelihood function. Building on recent work that uses spectral approximations to bound this term [7], the authors propose a novel and elegant alternative: non-spectral bounds derived from a determinantal inequality, inspired by inducing-point methods in sparse Gaussian processes. This new formulation has two significant consequences: it enables a provably-correct "retrospective" Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampler that avoids costly eigenvalue computations, and it introduces the first variational inference framework for DPP parameter learning. The proposed methods are developed for DPPs on both finite and continuous domains and are validated on synthetic and real-world data.

### Quality and Originality

This is a technically strong, well-written, and original paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on probabilistic modeling with DPPs.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Bounding Technique:** The core contribution—using an inequality from Seiler & Simon [14] to derive non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood—is a clever and powerful idea. It provides a distinct and compelling alternative to the spectral bounds proposed in [7], avoiding the need to store a large kernel matrix and run iterative methods like power iteration to estimate its spectrum.

2.  **Enabling Variational Inference:** A major advantage of this work is that it opens the door to variational inference for DPPs. This provides a new, scalable, and approximate inference paradigm that is complementary to the more exact but computationally intensive MCMC methods. The experiments on the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrate the practical utility of this approach.

3.  **Insightful Connection to Gaussian Processes:** The connection drawn between the proposed method and inducing-point methods for sparse Gaussian processes (GPs) [11] is particularly insightful. This analogy makes the approach highly intuitive for a large part of the NIPS audience and elegantly bridges two important areas of machine learning.

4.  **Generality:** The framework is thoughtfully developed for both DPPs on finite sets and on continuous domains, broadening its applicability.

**Weaknesses / Questions for the Authors:**

Despite the paper's many strengths, there are two primary technical concerns that I believe should be addressed to solidify the contribution.

1.  **Computational Trade-offs:** The paper argues that the proposed method is a computational win by replacing power iterations with a non-linear optimization problem. However, this optimization is performed over the locations of the `m` pseudo-inputs, and its difficulty increases with `m`. It is not immediately obvious that this is always more efficient than the spectral approach of [7], whose cost depends on the number of eigenvalues needed. The paper would be more compelling with a direct discussion or empirical comparison of these computational trade-offs. Under what conditions (e.g., spectral gap of the kernel matrix, required tightness of the bound) is the proposed optimization-based approach expected to be significantly faster?

2.  **Robustness of the MCMC Retrospective Sampler:** A more critical concern relates to the convergence of the bounds within the MCMC sampler. The optimization of the lower bound with respect to the pseudo-inputs `Z` is likely a non-convex problem, meaning that a standard optimizer may only find a local minimum. If this local optimum is poor, the bounds on the log acceptance probability may fail to tighten sufficiently as the number of pseudo-inputs `m` is increased. This could cause the sampler to stall indefinitely, unable to make an acceptance decision. This is a potential failure mode for the "provably-correct" claim.
    *   Could the authors please comment on this issue? Are there any theoretical guarantees or practical strategies (e.g., better initialization, multiple restarts) to mitigate this risk?
    *   In the experiments, did the MCMC sampler ever struggle to make a decision within a reasonable number of pseudo-inputs, and how was the optimization of `Z` performed at each step?

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the notation is consistent, and the progression from the finite to the continuous case is logical. The connection to sparse GPs is explained well and greatly aids understanding.

**Minor Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Figure 1:** The plots in Figure 1 are missing axis labels, which makes them difficult to fully interpret.
*   **Location of Proof:** The proof of Proposition 1 is the central technical insight of the paper. While space constraints are understood, the authors might consider including a brief proof sketch in the main body to make the paper more self-contained, rather than deferring it entirely to the supplement.
*   **Typo:** Page 7, Line 361: "the variational lower is" should be corrected.

### Significance

The work is highly significant. DPPs are gaining considerable traction in machine learning for modeling diversity, and robust and scalable inference methods are critically needed. By introducing a new class of bounds that enables both a different style of MCMC and a fast variational approach, this paper substantially advances the practical usability of parametric DPPs. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by other researchers in the field.

### Overall Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, clever, and significant contribution to an important and growing area of machine learning. It is technically sound and clearly communicated. While I have raised some questions about the practical computational trade-offs and the robustness of the MCMC scheme, I believe these points can be addressed by the authors. I strongly recommend acceptance.
