This paper develops nonspectral bounds on the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs) that enable both variational and MCMC inference without computing eigenvalues. The authors parameterize the L-kernel (rather than K) and derive upper and lower bounds on log det(I + L) for finite domains using an inducing-point construction inspired by sparse Gaussian processes. They extend the approach to continuous DPPs by bounding the Fredholm determinant, again with inducing points and an analytically tractable cross-covariance term when L and the base measure are Gaussian. These bounds can drive a variational lower bound objective or a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings procedure that refines bounds until an accept/reject decision is provable, paralleling but avoiding the spectral machinery in Affandi et al. (ICML ’13) [7]. Experiments include a synthetic 1D Gaussian example (where the spectrum and determinant are known) and a real spatial dataset (diabetic neuropathy), showing agreement with ideal MH and meaningful separation between classes via overdispersion metrics.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The determinant inequalities used to construct the bounds (via Seiler–Simon) are appropriate and, to my knowledge, novel in this context. The finite-domain result is clear and connects neatly to inducing-point GP ideas; the continuous-domain extension is appealing and practically computable for Gaussian kernels and measures. The retrospective MH construction mirrors prior spectral approaches but with the proposed bounds.
- Empirical support: The synthetic example validates correctness against a known ground truth. The real-data experiment demonstrates stability of overdispersion estimates as the number of inducing points grows and shows upper/lower bound convergence. However, head-to-head cost/tightness comparisons with spectral bounding methods (when feasible) are limited, and scalability results versus n, m, and dimensionality are not fully quantified.
- Assumptions: The continuous case requires trace-class conditions and computable Ψ. The paper outlines when this holds (e.g., Gaussian–Gaussian), but a clearer summary of assumptions and practical cases where Ψ is closed-form or efficiently approximable would strengthen the contribution.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, with a clear link to GP variational methods. A few presentation issues remain (listed below), and moving at least a sketch of the key proof into the main text would help readers understand why the bounds behave as observed.

Originality
- Replacing spectral approximations with nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP inference is, to my knowledge, new. The inducing-point perspective and extension to continuous DPPs are original and well motivated. The connection to retrospective MH without eigenvalues is especially compelling.

Significance
- By avoiding eigenvalue computations, the method broadens the applicability of DPP inference to settings where spectra are unavailable or costly (large discrete sets, continuous domains). The ability to do variational learning is practically important. If the computational benefits hold broadly, this can materially impact how DPPs are trained in ML and spatial statistics.

Strengths
- Elegant, theoretically grounded determinant bounds that eliminate spectral computations.
- Unifying view with inducing-point GP methods; practical variational objective.
- Extension to continuous DPPs with clear tractable cases.
- Retrospective MH without eigenvalues, matching “ideal” MH on a case where ground truth is known.
- Empirical evidence of bound tightening with m and stable overdispersion estimates.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Computational advantage needs clearer evidence. Optimizing an increasing number of inducing variables is a nonconvex problem; the paper should report wall-clock comparisons, complexity vs. m, and sensitivity to initialization relative to power iterations or Nyström-based spectral bounds when those are available.
- Convergence of bounds as m increases is coupled to optimization quality. The retrospective MH step could, in principle, stall if local optima prevent sufficient tightening. Please discuss practical safeguards (restarts, annealing, randomized initializations, adaptive placement heuristics) and whether any monotonicity/tightness guarantees hold under mild conditions.
- Identifiability when parameterizing L directly is highlighted by the κ example. Guidance on priors, reparameterizations, or constraints that improve identifiability in practice would be useful, especially for continuous DPPs.
- Continuous-case assumptions and scope: more explicit conditions ensuring tr(L) = ∫ L(x, x) dµ(x) and computability of Ψ, beyond Gaussian–Gaussian, would help practitioners.

Requests for clarification or additional analysis
- Provide practical complexity and memory profiles for variational training and retrospective MH as functions of n, m, and d, and compare to spectral-bounding baselines when feasible.
- Report how often retrospective MH needed to increase m and by how much, and include acceptance rates and total runtime.
- Explore robustness to inducing-point initialization and show variability across random restarts; consider simple placement heuristics (e.g., k-means, sampling from estimated marginals).
- Quantify bound tightness empirically (gap between upper and lower bounds) as a function of m, and relate this to effective sample size or acceptance decisions in MH.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated bounds that remove a major practical bottleneck in DPP inference.
- Solid theoretical underpinning and clear link to established variational machinery.
- Demonstrated applicability to continuous DPPs, not just finite domains.
- Clean experiments that validate correctness and practical utility.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited head-to-head runtime comparisons with spectral methods and incomplete characterization of optimization difficulties.
- Lack of theoretical guarantees on bound tightening under nonconvex optimization of inducing locations.
- Some assumptions in the continuous case could be articulated more explicitly.

Presentation issues
- Figure 1 (p. 7): Please add axis labels and units where appropriate.
- The proof of the central determinant inequality is critical to the contribution; consider moving a self-contained proof sketch into the main text, with full details in the supplement.
- Typo on p. 7, line 361: “the variational lower is …” → “the variational lower bound is …”

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely submission that introduces a clever and practically useful alternative to spectral methods for DPP inference, with thoughtful extensions and experiments. Addressing the concerns about computational trade-offs, optimization robustness, and continuous-case assumptions would make the paper even stronger. I lean toward acceptance.
