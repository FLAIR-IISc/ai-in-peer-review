### **Summary**

This paper addresses the significant challenge of performing likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), where the primary bottleneck is the computation of the normalizing constant, `det(L + I)`. The authors propose a novel method for bounding this term without requiring any spectral information of the kernel matrix `L`. Drawing inspiration from sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes, the method utilizes a set of "inducing inputs" to construct tractable upper and lower bounds on the likelihood. These bounds are then leveraged to develop two inference algorithms: a fast, approximate method based on variational inference (VI), and a more computationally intensive but exact (in the limit) Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling scheme using retrospective sampling. The proposed framework is developed for both DPPs on finite sets and on continuous domains.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Originality and Significance**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its novel approach to approximating the DPP likelihood. Prior work, such as Affandi et al. (2014), has relied on bounds derived from partial eigendecompositions of `L`, which can still be computationally demanding. By introducing non-spectral bounds based on inducing inputs, this work presents a genuinely new and potentially much more efficient alternative. The connection to sparse Gaussian processes is elegant and provides a strong theoretical underpinning. If effective, this method could significantly broaden the applicability of DPPs to larger-scale problems where spectral methods are prohibitive. The development of both a fast variational method and an exact MCMC sampler from the same set of bounds is also a significant contribution.

#### **Quality**

The theoretical contributions of the paper appear to be sound, with the proposed bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) resting on a non-trivial determinant inequality from Seiler & Simon (1975). The extension of these ideas from the finite to the continuous case is well-handled and adds to the completeness of the work.

However, the empirical evaluation is a significant weakness. While the experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods can be applied, they lack the necessary rigor and comparative analysis to fully support the paper's claims.
1.  **Lack of Baselines:** The paper does not compare its performance against any existing methods. A direct comparison with the spectral-bound MCMC approach of Affandi et al. (2014) would be crucial to quantify the trade-offs between computational speed and accuracy. Without this, it is difficult to assess the practical advantage of the proposed non-spectral approach.
2.  **Analysis of Variational Inference:** The experiments in Section 5.1 reveal that the VI method yields "poorly consistent results" for parameter estimation on a simple toy problem, forcing a switch to the MCMC approach. This is a critical finding that is not adequately explored. The paper should provide a deeper analysis of why the VI method fails here. Is the variational lower bound too loose? Is the joint optimization over kernel parameters and inducing points prone to poor local minima? Understanding the limitations of the VI approach is essential for any practitioner hoping to use it.
3.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The evaluation is based on one synthetic dataset and one real-world dataset. While these are illustrative, a more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of problems would be needed to establish the general utility and robustness of the proposed methods.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and structured logically. However, its clarity could be improved in a few areas:
1.  **Introduction for Non-Experts:** The introduction quickly delves into the distinction between the marginal kernel `K` and the L-ensemble kernel `L`. For readers not deeply familiar with DPP literature, this distinction and its importance might be unclear. A brief sentence or two clarifying that `K` governs marginal probabilities (interpretability) while `L` appears in the likelihood (tractability) would make the motivation more accessible.
2.  **Focus of the Narrative:** The paper repeatedly highlights the interpretability benefits of parameterizing the marginal kernel `K`, but ultimately adopts the standard, more tractable approach of parameterizing `L`. While this provides important context, some of this discussion could be streamlined to maintain a clearer focus on the paper's core contribution, which is the inference method for a given `L`-parameterized model.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Empirical Comparison:** I strongly encourage the authors to include an empirical comparison to a relevant baseline, such as the spectral method in Affandi et al. (2014). This comparison should address both the quality of inference (e.g., accuracy of posterior estimates) and the computational cost (e.g., wall-clock time). This would provide a much clearer picture of the practical benefits of this work.

2.  **Analysis of VI Failure:** Please provide a more thorough discussion on the failure of the variational method in the toy example. This analysis would be extremely valuable, as it would help characterize the settings in which the proposed VI is a reliable tool versus when the more expensive MCMC approach is necessary.

3.  **Guidance on Inducing Inputs:** The paper would benefit from more practical guidance regarding the inducing inputs `Z`.
    *   How should one choose the initial number of inducing points, `m`?
    *   Are there best practices for their initial placement (e.g., random, on a grid, or a subset of the data)? The paper notes that optimized points form neat grids, which is an interesting observation that could be expanded upon.
    *   The paper considers inducing inputs that are not part of the data. What is the connection to, and performance relative to, Nyström-based methods where inducing points are a subset of the data?

### **Conclusion**

This paper introduces a novel and promising approach for scalable inference in DPPs using non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds on the likelihood. The core idea is original and significant. However, the manuscript in its current form is weakened by an insufficiently thorough empirical evaluation that lacks comparisons to existing methods and fails to properly analyze the limitations of the proposed variational inference scheme. While there is clear merit to the work, these issues prevent a confident assessment of its practical impact. I would be willing to reconsider my rating if the authors can address these points, particularly by adding a meaningful experimental baseline.
