This paper addresses the challenging problem of parametric inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The main difficulty in likelihood-based inference for DPPs stems from an intractable normalizing constant, which takes the form of a large determinant. The authors' primary contribution is the derivation of novel upper and lower bounds on this determinant for DPPs on both finite and continuous domains. Unlike previous work [7], which relies on estimating the spectrum of the kernel matrix or operator, these new bounds are non-spectral and depend instead on a set of "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points." This approach draws a clear and insightful parallel to variational sparse approximations in Gaussian processes. The proposed bounds are then leveraged to develop two inference schemes: a cheap, approximate variational inference method and a more computationally intensive but exact retrospective MCMC sampler.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Originality and Quality:** The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a well-known problem in the DPP literature. The core idea of using a determinant inequality [14] to derive non-spectral bounds is both clever and elegant. This work provides a compelling alternative to the spectral estimation techniques proposed in prior work [7].
*   **Significance:** The proposed methods represent a significant contribution to the practical application of DPPs. By enabling variational inference, the authors open up DPPs to larger-scale problems where MCMC may be infeasible. The connection to inducing point methods in Gaussian processes is valuable, as it situates the work within a broader context of scalable inference for kernel-based models.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the technical contributions are presented logically, and the extension from the finite to the continuous case is handled smoothly.

**Arguments against acceptance / Questions for the authors:**

*   **Computational Trade-offs:** The paper argues that its method is computationally advantageous by avoiding costly power iterations. However, it replaces this with a non-linear optimization problem over the locations of the pseudo-inputs, which must be solved repeatedly and with an increasing number of variables. A more thorough discussion of the computational trade-offs would strengthen the paper. It is not immediately obvious that this new procedure represents a significant computational win in all scenarios.
*   **Convergence of Bounds for MCMC:** A critical aspect of the retrospective MCMC sampler is the guarantee that the bounds on the acceptance probability can be tightened sufficiently to make a decision. The optimization over the pseudo-input locations `Z` is likely a non-convex problem. This raises a concern: could the optimization get stuck in a poor local minimum, preventing the bounds from converging and effectively stalling the MCMC chain? The paper would benefit from addressing this potential issue, as it is central to the correctness of the proposed MCMC algorithm.

**Minor Issues:**

*   The proof for Proposition 1 is a cornerstone of this work. While acknowledging space limitations, including a high-level proof sketch in the main paper, rather than relegating it entirely to the supplementary material, would improve the paper's self-containedness.
*   In Figure 1, the plots are missing axis labels.
*   There appears to be a typo on Page 7, Line 361: "the variational lower is..."

In summary, this paper introduces a clever and valuable technique for bounding the partition function in DPPs, leading to new inference algorithms and insightful connections to other scalable kernel methods.
