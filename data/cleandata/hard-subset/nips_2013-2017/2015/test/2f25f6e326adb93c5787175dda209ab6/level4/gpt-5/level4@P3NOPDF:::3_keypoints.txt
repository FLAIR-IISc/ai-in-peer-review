- Summary: Proposes inducing-input based approximate inference for DPPs to avoid kernel eigendecompositions, with variational and approximate MCMC methods and empirical efficiency claims.
- Strength: Removes need for spectral information, addressing a key computational bottleneck in DPPs.
- Strength: The inducing-input construction is appealing and potentially widely applicable.
- Strength: Providing both VI and MCMC variants is useful for practitioners.
- Concern: Method descriptions are terse, hindering reproducibility and assessment of assumptions.
- Concern: Empirical evaluation is limited and does not clearly compare accuracy vs. cost against strong exact/approximate baselines.
- Concern: VI shows large parameter error on the toy example, raising questions about its practical scope and guidance for choosing methods.
- Request (complexity): Provide explicit per-iteration and overall complexities in n, m, and key kernel ops, plus wall-clock times, memory usage, and iterations to reach target accuracy.
- Request (comparisons): Compare to exact eigendecomposition on small/medium problems and to strong approximate baselines (Nyström, spectral truncation, other scalable DPPs).
- Request (metrics): Report held-out log-likelihood, kernel reconstruction error, parameter error, sample diversity, runtime, and accuracy tradeoffs.
- Request (VI behavior): Characterize when VI succeeds/fails—dependence on kernel conditioning, inducing/data mismatch, m and placement, initialization, optimization settings, and bound looseness.
- Request (VI diagnostics): Provide bound tightness vs. m, convergence curves, and ablations on initialization/hyperparameters.
- Request (inducing = sampled points): Evaluate performance when inducing set equals sampled points, and when it is a subset/superset.
- Request (heuristics): Offer practical guidance for selecting m and initializing inducing points (rules of thumb tied to length scales, effective rank; k-means, FPS, leverage scores, DPP sampling).
- Method detail: Precisely define the likelihood bound—direction, validity conditions, monotonicity in m, and theoretical guarantees or consistency as m→n.
- VI detail: State whether gradients are exact or stochastic, whether estimates are unbiased, and any variance reduction or regularization used to avoid pathological solutions.
- MCMC detail: Describe the proposal, acceptance computation using the bound, any bias corrections (delayed-acceptance, pseudo-marginal), and mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, ESS).
- Method detail: Discuss identifiability and whether different parameterizations of L or K yield similar approximated likelihoods.
- Empirical suggestion: Expand experiments to synthetic datasets with varying rank/repulsion, standard real datasets, multiple m values, and multiple initializations with confidence intervals.
- Empirical suggestion: Provide accuracy vs. runtime plots, ablations on inducing-point selection, and report variability over runs.
- Presentation: Early define K and L and their relationship to help readers unfamiliar with notation.
- Presentation: Re-focus or trim the discussion on marginal-kernel learning if not used; add clear algorithmic summaries, pseudocode, explicit computational costs, and reproducibility details.
- Presentation: Proofread to remove repetitive text between introduction and conclusion.
- Overall assessment: Interesting and relevant inducing-point approach with both VI and MCMC, but needs clearer methods, stronger baselines, and deeper empirical and practical guidance before acceptance.
