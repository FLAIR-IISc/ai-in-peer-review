The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that do not require spectral knowledge of the kernel defining the DPP. Instead, inducing inputs are utilized to bound the likelihood, which reduces the computational cost. Both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques are described, and their efficiency is demonstrated empirically.

I believe there is merit to this paper, however, I found the method description to be somewhat rough around the edges, and the empirical evaluation could be more thorough. To clarify and strengthen the paper, I suggest the authors address the following points:

1. A detailed comparison of the computational complexity and overall compute time of the two proposed methods would be beneficial. How do they stack up against each other in terms of efficiency?

2. It would be helpful to compare the proposed methods to other alternatives, both approximate and exact, in terms of quality of approximation and computation time. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of their strengths and weaknesses.

3. The empirical results on the toy data in Section 5.1 show that the parameter estimation error using variational inference is too large, and the authors resort to approximate MCMC instead. It would be useful to discuss the settings in which the proposed variational inference is expected to work and when it is expected to fail, along with the underlying reasons for this.

4. A 13-point sample is generated for the toy example. I am curious to know how the methods perform when the inducing inputs are actually these sampled points.

5. Are there any rules of thumb to follow when selecting a) the number of inducing points, and b) placing the initial inducing points? Guidance on these practical considerations would be valuable for readers implementing the methods.

Minor comments:

The matrices K and L are referred to throughout the introduction without explanation. Briefly mentioning the marginal kernel and L-ensembles may make it easier for uninformed readers to follow.

There is a discussion about the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters throughout the paper, but this is not the approach taken. I believe some of this content could be cut back, as it does not contribute to the rest of the paper.

If accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to improve the write-up, addressing the method description and empirical evaluation. With some revisions, this paper has the potential to make a more significant impact in the field.
