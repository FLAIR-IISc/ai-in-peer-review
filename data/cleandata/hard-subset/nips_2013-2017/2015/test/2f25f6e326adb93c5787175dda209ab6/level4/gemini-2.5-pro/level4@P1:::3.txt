This paper introduces a method for approximate inference in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) that avoids the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix, a significant computational bottleneck. Drawing inspiration from sparse Gaussian Process models, the authors use inducing points to derive novel lower and upper bounds on the DPP likelihood. These bounds are then leveraged to develop two inference schemes: a variational inference approach for approximate maximum likelihood estimation and a more exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method using retrospective sampling. The methods are developed for both discrete and continuous DPPs and demonstrated on synthetic and real-world data.

While the core idea is meritorious, I found the description of the methods and the empirical evaluation to be insufficient for a complete assessment. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the following points.

**Major Comments**

1.  **Analysis and Comparison of Proposed Methods:** A key weakness is the lack of a clear analysis of the trade-offs between the proposed variational and MCMC methods. The empirical results in Section 5.1 demonstrate a case where variational inference performs poorly, forcing a switch to MCMC. However, the paper does not investigate *why* the VI approach failed or offer guidance on when each method should be preferred. A more thorough discussion of their relative computational complexity, performance guarantees, and failure modes would be essential for a practitioner to choose between them.

2.  **Comparison to Alternative Methods:** The experimental evaluation is missing a comparison against relevant alternative methods. To properly contextualize the contribution, it would be valuable to see how the proposed techniques compare to other approximate or exact DPP inference schemes (e.g., the spectral bound approach of Affandi et al., 2014, or Nyström-based methods) in terms of both approximation quality and wall-clock time. Without these baselines, it is difficult to judge the practical significance of the proposed methods.

3.  **Practical Guidance on Inducing Points:** The paper could provide more practical guidance on the selection and use of the inducing points, which are central to the method.
    *   **Number of Inducing Points:** Is there a principled way or a reliable heuristic for choosing the number of inducing points, `m`? The experiments suggest increasing `m` until the results stabilize, but this may not always be practical.
    *   **Placement of Inducing Points:** How sensitive are the methods to the initial placement and subsequent optimization of the inducing points?
    *   **Relationship to Data:** For the 13-point toy example, a simple but informative experiment would be to use the observed data points themselves as the inducing points. This could provide valuable insight into the nature of the approximation.

**Minor Comments**

*   **Clarity in Introduction:** The introduction could be made more accessible to readers less familiar with DPPs. The kernels `K` and `L` are mentioned without a clear initial definition of their roles (e.g., `K` as the marginal kernel and `L` defining the L-ensemble). A brief explanation at the outset would improve readability for a broader audience.
*   **Focus of the Narrative:** The paper spends considerable time discussing the interpretability benefits of parametrizing the `K` kernel, but ultimately proceeds by parametrizing `L`. To improve the paper's focus, some of this discussion could be streamlined, as it is not central to the proposed methodology.

In summary, this paper presents a promising and novel idea for scalable DPP inference. The core contribution of non-spectral likelihood bounds is significant. However, the paper in its current form is weakened by an insufficient empirical evaluation and a lack of detailed analysis regarding the practical application and trade-offs of the proposed methods. If the authors can address the points above—particularly by adding baseline comparisons and a more thorough analysis of the VI vs. MCMC approaches—the paper's contribution would be much stronger.
