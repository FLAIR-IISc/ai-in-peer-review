

This paper presents a novel approach to learning kernel parameters in determinantal point processes (DPPs), addressing a key challenge in their application: the intractable normalization constant. The authors propose a new bound for the partition function that avoids the eigenvalue estimation required in prior work [7], enabling both MCMC inference and variational learning. The connection to inducing-point methods in Gaussian processes is particularly insightful, offering a promising pathway for scalable DPP inference. The technical contribution is strong, with Proposition 1 providing a clever and effective lower bound derived from a novel inequality. The paper is well-written and clearly motivated, making it a pleasure to read.

The core innovation—replacing spectral estimation with a variational bound—effectively sidesteps a significant bottleneck in [7]. The proposed bound is elegant and well-justified, and the resulting variational framework seamlessly integrates with existing scalable GP inference techniques. This synergy with Gaussian process literature is a major strength, suggesting broader applicability beyond DPPs.

However, two technical concerns require clarification before acceptance:

First, the computational advantage of the proposed method over [7] is not fully convincing. While the approach avoids power iterations for eigenvalue estimation, it replaces them with high-dimensional non-convex optimization problems whose complexity grows with the cardinality of the inducing set. The claim that this constitutes a "huge computational win" needs stronger justification. Specifically, the paper should quantify the trade-off: for a given problem size and desired accuracy, how does the per-iteration cost and convergence rate of the proposed optimization compare to power iterations? Without empirical or theoretical analysis of this trade-off, the computational benefit remains speculative.

Second, the convergence guarantee for the bound as the inducing set cardinality increases is unclear. The optimization problem (Equation 11) is highly non-convex, and the paper does not address how local minima might affect the bound's tightness. Crucially, if the optimization converges to a local minimum rather than the global optimum, the lower bound may fail to "bound away" from the Bernoulli threshold required for valid MCMC steps (as noted in Section 4.2). The authors should explicitly discuss: (a) the risk of local minima, (b) strategies to mitigate this (e.g., multiple restarts, initialization schemes), and (c) whether convergence to the global optimum can be guaranteed under reasonable assumptions. Without addressing this, the MCMC scheme's correctness remains questionable.

Presentation-wise, the following improvements are needed:

- **Figure 1 (Page 7)**: Add clear axis labels to both subplots to ensure interpretability without reference to the text.
- **Proof of Proposition 1**: This is the paper's central technical contribution. The proof should be included in the main body (e.g., as an appendix section) rather than relegated to the index. Its omission weakens the paper's accessibility and credibility.
- **Typo**: Page 7, Line 361: "the variational lower" → "the variational lower bound".

Overall, this is a technically strong paper with significant potential to advance DPP inference. The core idea is novel and well-executed, and the connections to Gaussian process methods are compelling. Addressing the computational trade-off and convergence concerns—particularly the risk of local minima undermining the MCMC procedure—will be essential for the paper's impact. The minor presentation issues are straightforward to resolve. With these clarifications, this work would make a valuable contribution to the literature.
