The authors propose novel upper and lower bounds on the matrix determinant, with applications to bounding the likelihood of a determinantal point process. The main idea is simple, but the paper is well executed and enlightening, and paves the way for several potential improvements. 
The authors propose a creative use of pseudo-inputs to bound functionals of positive definite kernels. The problem addressed is timely and will be of interest to many in the community.
Such an approach is quite general, with potential applications beyond determinantal point processes. The experimental evaluation is well constructed to elucidate several key points, including limitations of a variational approximation under certain conditions. I found the selected application area interesting and highly relevant to the studied problem.

Minor comments:
- It’s unclear why the issue of parameterizing K (instead of L) features so prominently in the abstract and throughout the paper, as the authors primarily describe the problem and do not attempt to address it.
- If accepted, I suggest the authors prepare a standard supplement, rather than simply uploading an extended version of the paper as the supplement.

Suggestions for future work:
- The discussion of the interpretability of the K parameterization makes a direct approach to bounding the determinant in terms of K even more appealing. Could the authors consider such an approach? What would be the scalability of this approach if combined with the pseudo-input method? 

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper addresses an important practical challenge in inference for determinantal point processes – avoiding expensive spectral decompositions. The authors provide novel bounds on the likelihood that circumvent this issue, enabling both variational and MCMC inference. The theoretical results are clearly presented, and the experimental validation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods. 

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically sound, with clear derivations and a solid theoretical foundation. The connection to existing work on variational inference for Gaussian processes is well-established.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the key ideas are explained clearly.
* **Originality:** The proposed bounds are a novel contribution to the field, offering a practical solution to a significant computational bottleneck.
* **Significance:** The results are important as they make DPP inference more tractable, potentially broadening the applicability of these models. The demonstration on both a synthetic and a real dataset strengthens this claim. The potential for generalization beyond DPPs also adds to the significance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of K Parameterization:** While the paper highlights the difficulties of directly parameterizing K, it doesn’t explore potential solutions or provide a clear justification for focusing solely on L. 
* **Experimental Setup:** While the experiments are well-executed, a more thorough comparison with existing methods (beyond those mentioned) would further strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to a significant problem.
* Clear presentation and good experimental validation.
* Potential for broad impact within the DPP community and beyond.
* Addresses a key limitation of existing DPP inference methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper doesn't attempt to resolve the issue of K parameterization, despite highlighting its importance.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.



I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and recommend acceptance. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. Addressing the minor comments regarding the supplement and exploring the K parameterization further could further enhance the paper.
