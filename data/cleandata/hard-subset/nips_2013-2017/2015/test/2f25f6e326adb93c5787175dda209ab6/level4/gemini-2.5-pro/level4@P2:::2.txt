This paper introduces novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds for the determinant term that arises in the likelihood of a determinantal point process (DPP). The key challenge in likelihood-based inference for DPPs is the computational cost of this determinant, `det(L + I)`, which typically requires an expensive spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix L. Drawing inspiration from sparse approximations in Gaussian processes, the authors propose using a set of "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" to construct tractable bounds. These bounds are then leveraged in two distinct inference schemes: a fast, approximate variational inference method based on maximizing the evidence lower bound, and an exact-but-more-costly MCMC method using retrospective sampling. The proposed framework is developed for both finite (discrete) and continuous DPPs and is validated on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—a non-spectral, pseudo-input-based bound for the DPP likelihood—is both novel and significant. It provides a practical and scalable alternative to previous methods that relied on partial or full eigendecompositions (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013). This work has the potential to make parametric DPP models accessible to a wider range of applications where the number of items `n` is large. The general technique for bounding matrix determinants could also be of interest beyond the DPP community.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed bounds are derived rigorously, and the connections to variational methods for sparse Gaussian processes are clearly articulated. The experimental evaluation is particularly strong and well-designed. It not only demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods but also honestly assesses their limitations. For instance, the authors show a case where the variational approach fails to identify parameters correctly, thereby motivating the need for the more robust MCMC approach. This thoughtful analysis adds considerable credibility to the work.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It introduces the concepts methodically, starting with the simpler finite case before extending the ideas to the more technical continuous domain. This structure makes the paper's main contributions easy to follow, even for readers not deeply familiar with operator theory.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Framing of the K vs. L Parameterization Problem:** The abstract and introduction prominently feature the issue of parameterizing the kernel K (which has an interpretable link to repulsion) versus the kernel L (which is more tractable but less interpretable). While this is an important problem in the field and provides useful context, the paper does not actually address it. The proposed methods operate entirely on the L parameterization. This framing could be slightly confusing, as it sets up an expectation that is not met. It might be beneficial to reframe this point more explicitly as a key motivation for future work rather than a central focus of the current paper.

2.  **Extending the Method to the K Parameterization:** A natural and highly impactful extension of this work would be to investigate whether the pseudo-input approach could be adapted to work directly with the K matrix. The authors correctly note the benefits of the K parameterization for model interpretability. If bounds could be derived for the likelihood in terms of K, it would represent a major step forward for DPP modeling. A discussion of the potential challenges and scalability of such an approach would be a valuable addition, perhaps in the conclusion.

**Minor Comments:**

*   For the camera-ready version, it is suggested that the authors prepare a standard supplement containing only the proofs and additional details, rather than providing an extended version of the main paper. This is a more common and reader-friendly format.

Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on determinantal point processes. The central idea is elegant, the technical development is solid, and the experimental validation is convincing. It tackles a timely problem and opens up promising avenues for future research. I recommend acceptance.
