This paper addresses the significant challenge of likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), a class of models useful for capturing repulsion and diversity. The primary bottleneck in learning DPP parameters is the computation of a large determinant term in the likelihood function, which typically requires a costly spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. This is a well-known problem, previously tackled by methods that approximate the spectrum [7].

The main contribution of this work is the derivation of novel upper and lower bounds on this determinant term, which, crucially, do not require any spectral information. Inspired by variational methods for sparse Gaussian processes [11], the authors introduce a set of "pseudo-inputs" to construct a low-rank approximation of the kernel matrix, leading to bounds that are computationally efficient to evaluate. The paper presents these bounds for DPPs on both finite sets and continuous domains. Based on these bounds, the authors develop two inference schemes: a fast, approximate method based on variational inference, and a more computationally intensive but exact MCMC sampler using retrospective sampling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The proposed non-spectral bounds are a novel and elegant solution to a key computational bottleneck in DPP inference. By avoiding expensive eigendecompositions, this work significantly broadens the applicability of parametric DPPs to larger-scale problems. The approach is general and could potentially find applications beyond DPPs where similar determinant computations are required.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the bounds is well-grounded, and their application to both variational inference and MCMC is clearly explained. The experimental evaluation is well-designed, using both a synthetic toy problem with a known ground truth and a real-world dataset. The authors are commendably transparent, showing not only the success of their methods but also a case where the variational approach fails due to parameter identifiability issues, thereby demonstrating the value of the more robust MCMC approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. It effectively builds intuition by first presenting the method for the simpler discrete case before tackling the more technical continuous case. This structure makes the core ideas accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The abstract and introduction place significant emphasis on the interpretability challenge of parametrizing the kernel L instead of the more natural kernel K. While this provides excellent motivation, the paper's contributions do not directly address this issue, focusing instead on inference for a given L-parametrization. Clarifying this focus earlier could prevent a mismatch in reader expectations.
*   A minor point for the final version: the authors should consider preparing a standard supplement containing only additional material, rather than submitting an extended version of the main paper.

**Suggestions for Future Work:**

Given the paper's insightful discussion on the K vs. L parametrization, a compelling direction for future work would be to investigate if the pseudo-input framework could be adapted to bound the likelihood directly in terms of the K matrix. This would be a significant step towards combining the computational benefits of this work with the modeling benefits of the more interpretable K parametrization. An analysis of the scalability of such an approach would be of great interest to the community.
