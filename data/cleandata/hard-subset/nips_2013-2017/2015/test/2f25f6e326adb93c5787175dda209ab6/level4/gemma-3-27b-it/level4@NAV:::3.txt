This paper introduces approximate inference methods for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that circumvent the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The approach leverages inducing inputs to provide likelihood bounds, thereby reducing computational expense. The authors detail both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) procedures, and present empirical validation of their efficacy.

While the work holds promise, I found the methodological descriptions somewhat lacking in detail and the empirical evaluation insufficiently comprehensive. To strengthen the manuscript, I recommend addressing the following points:

1) A comparison of the computational complexity and runtime performance of the two proposed methods would be valuable.
2) It would be beneficial to benchmark the proposed methods against existing approximate and exact inference techniques for DPPs, evaluating both approximation quality and computational cost.
3) The results in Section 5.1 indicate significant parameter estimation error with variational inference, leading to a reliance on approximate MCMC. Clarification is needed regarding the conditions under which the proposed variational inference is expected to succeed or fail, and a rationale for these expectations.
4) Given the 13-point sample used in the toy example, an analysis of method performance using these sampled points as inducing inputs would be informative.
5) Providing guidance on selecting the number of inducing points and their initial placement—perhaps in the form of a heuristic—would enhance the practical applicability of the methods.

Minor comments:

The introduction references matrices K and L without defining them. Briefly introducing the concepts of the marginal kernel and L-ensembles could improve readability for a broader audience. 

The paper discusses the potential benefits of directly learning marginal kernel parameters, but does not implement this approach. Some of this discussion could be streamlined as it doesn’t directly contribute to the presented work.

Overall, the approach of utilizing inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood and facilitate approximate inference is promising. However, if accepted, I strongly recommend the authors revise the manuscript to provide a more detailed and rigorous presentation of the methods and results.
