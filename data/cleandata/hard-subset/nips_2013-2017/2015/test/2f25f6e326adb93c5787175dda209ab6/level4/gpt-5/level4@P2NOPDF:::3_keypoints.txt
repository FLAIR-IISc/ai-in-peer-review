- Proposes approximate DPP inference that avoids spectral decomposition by using inducing inputs to bound the likelihood, with variational and approximate MCMC methods and empirical speedups.

- Addresses an important problem and presents a promising idea, but methodological exposition is rough in places.

- Empirical evaluation is insufficient to determine when variational vs. MCMC methods are preferable.

- Request clear theoretical time and memory complexity comparisons for variational and MCMC methods (as functions of data size, number of inducing inputs, kernel hyperparameters), plus wall-clock times and a breakdown of dominant costs.

- Ask for comparisons to existing exact and approximate DPP inference methods (e.g., spectral methods) in both approximation quality and compute time.

- Request characterization of when variational inference fails or succeeds (dependence on kernel smoothness, data size, inducing-input count/placement, model misspecification) and explanations for loose bounds or remedies.

- Ask for experiments using sampled data points as inducing inputs in the toy example and broader sensitivity studies/ablations on number and placement of inducing inputs.

- Request practical guidance: rules of thumb for choosing the number of inducing points and initializing their locations, and discussion of accuracy vs. cost trade-offs.

- Minor: introduce and define K (marginal kernel) and L-ensembles for accessibility.

- Minor: trim or move the discussion about directly learning marginal kernel parameters since it distracts from the paper’s main contributions.

- Overall: paper has merit and potential impact; should revise exposition, expand empirical comparisons, and provide practical complexity/runtime and inducing-point selection guidance.
