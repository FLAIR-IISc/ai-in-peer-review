Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have been receiving significant attention in machine learning due to their ability to model repulsion within realizations of a set of points. This repulsion is specified via a positive definite kernel function, and a key challenge lies in learning the parameters of this kernel given example point realizations. This estimation is difficult due to a computationally intractable normalization constant. A recent approach [7] addresses this by leveraging spectral estimation and providing incremental upper and lower bounds, enabling a provably-correct retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) scheme for kernel parameter estimation. This paper proposes an alternative bounding strategy that avoids eigenvalue estimation, also facilitating MCMC inference and enabling a variational approach, drawing connections to inducing-point methods used for scalable inference in Gaussian process models. 

This is a technically strong and well-written paper. I was unaware of the determinant inequality utilized in Proposition 1, and it appears particularly well-suited for this problem. The approach of circumventing eigenvalue estimation represents a clever and compelling alternative to [7]. The connections to Gaussian process inducing points and the resulting ability to perform variational inference are significant strengths. Overall, the paper was a pleasure to read.

However, I have two technical concerns. First, while the avoidance of power iterations is appealing, it’s not entirely clear that replacing them with potentially high-dimensional nonlinear optimization problems constitutes a substantial computational win. The cost of optimizing over the inducing points, particularly as their number increases, should be more thoroughly discussed. Second, simply increasing the cardinality of the inducing point set doesn’t guarantee convergence of the bounds. The optimization problem is likely highly non-convex, and achieving a global minimum is not assured. A local minimum could prevent the bounds from sufficiently narrowing the Bernoulli acceptance threshold, potentially halting MCMC progress. This aspect requires more detailed consideration and justification.

The paper demonstrates a solid understanding of related work in DPPs, Gaussian processes, and determinant estimation. The authors correctly situate their contribution within this landscape and appropriately reference relevant publications. The originality of the work lies in the novel application of the determinant inequality to provide tractable bounds for DPP likelihoods, circumventing the need for spectral decomposition. The significance of this work stems from the potential for more efficient inference in DPP models, particularly for large datasets where spectral methods become prohibitive. The results appear important and address a challenging problem in a novel and potentially impactful way. The provision of both variational and MCMC inference methods further enhances the contribution. The experimental validation in Section 5, including a comparison to existing methods, is valuable and provides initial support for the approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and mathematically rigorous.
*   Novel approach to bounding DPP likelihoods, avoiding spectral decomposition.
*   Clear connections to related work and established inference techniques (variational inference, MCMC, Gaussian process inducing points).
*   Potentially significant improvements in computational efficiency for large-scale DPP inference.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational benefits relative to existing methods are not fully demonstrated and may be problem-dependent.
*   The convergence properties of the bounding procedure (increasing inducing point cardinality) are not fully addressed and may be susceptible to local optima.
*   The practical impact of the parameterization choice (directly parameterizing L instead of K) isn't fully explored.

**Recommendations and minor points:**

*   **Proposition 1 Proof:** While the reference to [14] is sufficient, briefly outlining the core idea of the proof within the main body of the paper would enhance clarity. It's a central contribution and deserves more prominence.
*   **Figure 1 (P7):** Axis labels are missing and should be added for clarity.
*   **P7 L361:** “...the variational lower is…” – Correct to “…the variational lower bound is…”.
*   Further discussion of the computational complexity of the optimization over the inducing points would be beneficial.
*   A discussion of potential strategies for mitigating the risk of local optima during the inducing point optimization would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. Despite the minor concerns regarding computational complexity and convergence, the technical strength, novelty, and potential impact of the work are substantial. The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and offers a promising alternative for inference in determinantal point processes. Addressing the reviewer’s concerns in a revised version would further strengthen the paper.
