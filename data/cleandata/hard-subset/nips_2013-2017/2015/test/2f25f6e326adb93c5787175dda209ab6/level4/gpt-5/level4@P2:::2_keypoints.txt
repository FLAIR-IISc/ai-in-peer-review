- Introduces non-spectral upper and lower determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods on finite and continuous domains using low-rank approximations with pseudo-inputs Z.  
- Bounds avoid eigendecompositions by approximating kernel L with Q = LZY L−1Z LZY and applying a determinant inequality.  
- Proposes two inference strategies: a variational lower bound optimized over kernel params and Z (O(n m^2)), and a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler that tightens acceptance by increasing m and optimizing Z.  
- Extends results to continuous domains using computable integrals Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x), available in Gaussian–Gaussian settings.  
- Builds on inducing-variable sparse GP methods (Titsias, 2009) and adapts Seiler & Simon’s determinant inequality; contrasts with spectral and Nyström DPP methods.  
- Retrospective MH mirrors spectral-bounding samplers but is adapted to the non-spectral setting; connection to Bornemann’s quadrature noted as future work.  
- Empirical validation: 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP shows close agreement between retrospective MH and an “ideal” MH.  
- Empirical validation: diabetic neuropathy 2D dataset shows variational method recovers stable overdispersion with increasing m and separates classes consistent with prior analyses.  
- Strength: conceptually simple, general, and removes spectral bottlenecks.  
- Strength: applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs; continuous extension is a notable contribution.  
- Strength: provides practical algorithms — scalable variational bound and exact retrospective MH sampler.  
- Strength: clear empirical demonstrations including a candid failure case (parameter identifiability) and convergence of bounds as m increases.  
- Strength: pseudo-input optimization is effective and interpretable.  
- Weakness: discussion of parameterizing K versus L is prominent but lacks methods or links without spectral info.  
- Weakness: continuous-case depends on closed-form Ψ; outside Gaussian cases this may require numerical integration and limit generality.  
- Weakness: limited large-scale comparisons to spectral or Nyström baselines and limited exploration in high-dimensional continuous domains.  
- Weakness: no theoretical guidance on choosing m or characterizing bound tightness as functions of m and Z; relies on heuristics.  
- Weakness: variational optimization details (initialization, gradient methods, sensitivity to Z) and stability are only briefly addressed.  
- Quality: theoretical claims are grounded in known inequalities; proofs in supplement; experiments support claims but broader benchmarks needed.  
- Clarity: generally well-written and organized, but some overemphasis on K vs L without actionable outcomes; minor clarifications needed.  
- Originality: novel adaptation of inducing-point bounds to DPP determinants and extension to Fredholm determinants; retrospective MH is a useful extension.  
- Significance: broadens DPP inference where spectra are unavailable, especially for continuous domains; applicable to other probabilistic models needing determinant bounds.  
- Suggested experiments/details: clarify Ψ computation strategies (quadrature, random features), provide guidance on choosing m and Z, and include ablations and runtime–accuracy trade-offs.  
- Suggested comparisons: add head-to-head benchmarking with spectral/partial-eigenvalue methods, including memory and wall-clock times.  
- Suggested theory: discuss empirical monotonicity of bounds with m and conditions guaranteeing convergence; offer pathways to operate on K directly.  
- Supplement requests: include full proofs, algorithmic details, derivations of Ψ for common kernels, pseudo-code, and reproducible code for MH schedules.  
- Acceptance pros: removes spectral computation barrier, solid general methodology, extends to continuous domains, and includes validating experiments.  
- Acceptance cons: narrow empirical evaluation lacking strong large-scale baselines, heuristic guidance on m and Z, heavy emphasis on K vs L without tools, and continuous-case limited by computable Ψ.  
- Minor fixes: correct typos/notation, suggest remedies for identifiability (priors, reparameterizations, fixing scale), and include code or detailed reproduction instructions.
