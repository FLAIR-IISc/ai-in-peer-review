This paper proposes novel upper and lower bounds on the matrix determinant, with applications to bounding the likelihood of a determinantal point process (DPP). The core idea is elegantly simple, yet the paper is well-executed and provides a valuable contribution, opening avenues for further improvements in the field. The authors’ creative use of pseudo-inputs to bound functionals of positive definite kernels is particularly noteworthy. The problem addressed is timely and will likely be of considerable interest to the NIPS community. This approach exhibits generality and possesses potential applications extending beyond DPPs. The experimental evaluation is thoughtfully designed to highlight key insights, including limitations of a variational approximation under specific conditions. The selected application area – modeling diversity – is both interesting and highly relevant to the presented problem.

The paper begins with a clear introduction to DPPs and the challenges associated with likelihood-based inference, specifically the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel. The authors effectively motivate their work by outlining the trade-offs between parameterizing the kernel K versus L, and correctly identify the computational bottleneck of evaluating the likelihood as the determinant calculation. They then present their key contribution: bounds on the likelihood that avoid explicit spectral decomposition, leveraging inspiration from variational Gaussian process inference. The extension to continuous domains is also well-presented.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed bounds appear technically sound, with a clear connection to existing work on Gaussian process approximation. The proofs, while deferred to the supplementary material, are referenced appropriately.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a reasonably accessible manner. The inclusion of connections to existing methods (e.g., variational inference, MCMC) helps to contextualize the contribution.
*   **Originality:** The non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood represent a novel approach to a challenging problem. While the core idea of using bounds is not entirely new, its application to DPPs in this way, and the extension to continuous domains, is a valuable contribution.
*   **Significance:** The proposed bounds have the potential to significantly improve the scalability of DPP inference, making it feasible to apply these models to larger and more complex datasets. The demonstrated applications to both synthetic and real-world data highlight the practical relevance of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of K vs. L Parameterization:** The paper extensively discusses the issue of parameterizing K versus L, particularly in the introduction and abstract. However, the authors do not propose or explore any methods for directly addressing this challenge. While acknowledging it is beyond the scope of the paper is reasonable, the sustained focus on the problem without offering potential solutions feels somewhat incomplete.
*   **Supplementary Material:** The authors state they will provide an extended version of the paper as supplementary material. It would be preferable to have a standard supplement with additional details, proofs, and potentially more extensive experimental results, rather than a complete duplicate of the manuscript.
*   **Experimental Depth:** While the experiments are well-constructed, they could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing DPP inference methods, particularly those that rely on spectral approximations. A more quantitative analysis of the computational cost and accuracy trade-offs would also be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in DPP inference.
*   The proposed bounds are novel and technically sound.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical potential of the approach.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper does not offer a solution to the issue of parameterizing K directly.
*   The supplementary material format is not ideal.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of DPP modeling. The proposed bounds are a valuable technical innovation that addresses a key bottleneck in DPP inference. While the paper has some minor weaknesses, the strengths outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend acceptance, with the request that the authors prepare a standard supplement containing detailed proofs and additional experimental analysis. A minor revision addressing the supplementary material format would strengthen the submission.
