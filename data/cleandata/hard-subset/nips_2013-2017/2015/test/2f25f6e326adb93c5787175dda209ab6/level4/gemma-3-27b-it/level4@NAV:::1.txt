Determinantal point processes (DPPs) are gaining prominence in machine learning due to their ability to model repulsive interactions between points, governed by a positive definite kernel. A core challenge lies in estimating kernel parameters from observed point realizations, complicated by a computationally intractable normalization constant involving sums over all possible cardinalities and configurations. Recent work [7] addresses this via spectral estimation and bounding, enabling provably-correct retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for kernel parameters. 

This paper introduces an alternative bounding strategy for MCMC inference that circumvents the need for eigenvalue estimation. This approach also facilitates a variational learning framework and establishes links to inducing-point methods commonly used for scalable Gaussian process inference. 

The paper is technically rigorous and clearly presented. The inequality underpinning the lower bound in Proposition 1 was novel to me and appears particularly well-suited to this problem, offering an elegant solution to the estimation challenges of [7]. I especially appreciate the connections drawn to Gaussian process inducing points and the resulting capability for variational inference. Overall, this work represents a substantial contribution and was a pleasure to read.

However, I have two technical concerns. First, while the proposed method is appealing, it's unclear whether substituting power iterations with potentially complex, increasing-dimensional nonlinear optimization problems truly constitutes a significant computational advantage. Second, the convergence of the bounds through simple cardinality increases is not guaranteed due to the coupled optimization nature of the problem. This optimization appears highly non-convex, necessitating strategies to ensure global minimum attainment. This point requires direct attention, as a local minimum could hinder effective bounding and obstruct MCMC steps.

In summary, this is a strong paper that cleverly bounds the partition function of a DPP, potentially enabling faster inference and forging connections to related modeling paradigms.
