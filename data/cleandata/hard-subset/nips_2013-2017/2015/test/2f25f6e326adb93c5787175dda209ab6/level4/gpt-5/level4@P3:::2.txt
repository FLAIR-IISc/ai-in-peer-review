Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes nonspectral upper and lower bounds on determinants that appear in the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs), enabling likelihood-based inference without computing eigenvalues. For DPPs on finite sets, the authors introduce bounds on det(L + I) using inducing inputs Z and a low-rank proxy Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, inspired by variational sparse Gaussian processes [11]. For continuous DPPs, they provide analogous bounds for the Fredholm determinant det(I + L), expressed in terms of LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(z, x)L(x, z′) dμ(x), which are tractable for Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measure. These bounds are then used to develop (i) a variational lower bound for efficient approximate maximum likelihood, and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) sampler that adaptively tightens bounds by increasing |Z| until an accept/reject decision is possible.

The work is positioned against prior spectral methods that require computing the leading eigenpairs of L for finite domains [7], or that are unavailable in closed form for continuous domains. By avoiding spectral decompositions, the proposed approach reduces memory/time burdens and generalizes naturally to continuous settings. The paper also clarifies the connection to sparse GP techniques and extends these ideas to DPP likelihoods, which to my knowledge has not appeared previously in this form.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and clearly motivated. The finite-domain bounds (Proposition 1) and the continuous-domain bounds (Proposition 2) follow from a determinant inequality [14]; while full proofs are deferred to the supplement, the statements are plausible and consistent with the GP literature. The computational profile is attractive: the variational objective can be evaluated in O(n m^2) given m inducing points, and in the continuous case Ψ is closed-form for Gaussian–Gaussian settings.

The experimental section is thoughtful and illustrative. The 1D Gaussian–Gaussian experiment exploits a known spectral decomposition to validate that the retrospective MH with nonspectral bounds matches an “ideal” MH that uses the exact determinant. It also highlights identifiability issues (e.g., κ) under L-parameterization, where the variational method can perform poorly but MCMC remains reliable. The real-data study on spatial nerve fibers replicates prior findings on overdispersion differences between classes [7, 22] and demonstrates monotone tightening of bounds as m increases.

Some aspects could be strengthened: (i) comparisons to spectral approximations in finite domains, both in accuracy and wall-clock, (ii) guidance on initializing/optimizing inducing inputs Z, and (iii) a clearer articulation of conditions ensuring tightness/monotonicity of bounds as m grows (the empirical convergence plots are helpful, but a brief theoretical discussion would increase confidence).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The exposition of finite-domain results before the continuous extension is effective. A few suggestions:
- The paper repeatedly emphasizes the tension between K- and L-parameterizations, but the contributions do not address this problem directly. Consider moving this discussion to the limitations/future work sections or adding a short experiment that empirically explores mapping between K and L in a constrained setting.
- State the assumptions for Proposition 2 more prominently (trace-class operator, computability of Ψ), with concrete examples beyond Gaussian–Gaussian.
- If accepted, please provide a standard supplementary document with complete proofs and additional experimental details, rather than only an extended version of the paper.

Originality
The core novelty is the derivation and use of inducing-point determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods that do not rely on eigenvalues, extending sparse GP ideas [11] to both finite and continuous DPPs. Adapting retrospective MH to these nonspectral bounds is natural but valuable. Relative to [7], which uses spectral truncations, this work opens a complementary path that applies when spectra are expensive or unavailable. I view the originality as solid.

Significance
The results are practically useful for training DPPs in settings where spectral methods are infeasible or too costly, particularly on continuous domains. The approach offers a new tool for both approximate and exact inference (via MH) and could have impact beyond DPPs wherever log-determinant terms arise. The ability to optimize inducing inputs to tighten bounds is a strong practical lever. The real-data results and the synthetic validation support the method’s credibility.

Strengths
- Nonspectral, cheap-to-evaluate determinant bounds applicable to finite and continuous DPPs.
- Clear connection to variational sparse GPs; sensible complexity O(n m^2) with m ≪ n.
- Practical algorithms: variational training and retrospective MH using the same bounds.
- Thoughtful experiments that validate correctness and highlight when variational methods can fail.
- Inducing inputs are optimized and empirically placed in informative regions.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Overemphasis on K vs. L parameterization without proposing a resolution. Given L + I = (I − K)^{-1} in the finite case, det(L + I) = det(I − K)^{-1}; exploring analogous inducing-point bounds directly for det(I − K) (with PSD K constrained to have spectrum in [0,1)) could make K-parameterization feasible while preserving interpretability. Even a short analysis or negative result would strengthen the narrative.
- Limited empirical comparison to spectral methods in finite domains (e.g., [7]) in terms of runtime and approximation quality for fixed accuracy.
- Sensitivity and robustness of Z: initialization strategies, regularization, and optimization stability are not fully discussed. An ablation on Z optimization and m-selection (beyond convergence plots) would be valuable.
- Assumptions in the continuous case: clarify when Ψ is tractable, discuss numerical strategies (quadrature/low-rank structure) in non-Gaussian settings, and comment on scaling to higher dimensions.
- Reproducibility: please release code and scripts for both finite and continuous cases.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important bottleneck in DPP learning with a broadly applicable, elegant method.
- Demonstrates both approximate and exact inference with the same machinery.
- Extends to continuous DPPs without spectral knowledge, which is genuinely useful.
- Clear empirical validation and insightful analysis of identifiability.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing direct empirical comparison to leading spectral baselines in finite domains.
- The K vs. L discussion is not matched by a technical contribution.
- Theoretical tightness/monotonicity of bounds with m is not formalized (though suggested).

Questions for the authors
- Can you characterize conditions under which the bounds tighten monotonically with increasing m or with optimized Z?
- In the finite case, could your inequality be used to bound det(I − K) directly and thereby enable K-parameterization with inducing points, perhaps with additional constraints on K?
- How sensitive is performance to Z initialization? Any heuristics (e.g., sampling from estimated marginals or k-means on data locations) that you recommend?
- For non-Gaussian kernels/base measures, how do you propose computing Ψ efficiently in higher dimensions?

Overall evaluation
This is a well-executed paper with a simple, powerful idea that removes a key obstacle in DPP inference, with convincing experiments and clear practical value. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying assumptions, trimming the K vs. L emphasis, and adding a brief empirical comparison to a spectral baseline in the finite case.
