- Proposes nonspectral determinant bounds for DPPs (finite and continuous) using inducing inputs Z, yielding Q = LYZ LZ−1 LZY to avoid eigen decomposition.  
- Provides upper/lower bounds on det(L+I) (finite) and Fredholm determinants (continuous), enabling variational inference and retrospective MH.  
- Positions work against spectral DPP methods and connects to inducing-variable sparse GP approaches.  
- Experiments: 1D toy with exact spectral quantities and a diabetic neuropathy dataset.  

Strengths:
- Novel: nonspectral determinant bounds for finite and continuous DPPs appear original and useful.  
- Practical: enables a cheap variational objective and an exact-but-approximate retrospective MH sampler.  
- Clear intuition: inducing-input construction and sparse-GP analogy are conceptually straightforward.  

Weaknesses / Questions:
- Method description is terse, especially around Z optimization and VI vs MH computational trade-offs.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited in datasets, baselines, and does not demonstrate clear superiority in accuracy, scalability, or runtime.  
- Variational method appears unstable on the toy example (large errors/high variability) with little guidance on applicability.  

Quality:
- Theoretical bounds are based on known determinant inequalities and are clearly stated, but practical guidance on tightness vs m and Z optimization is limited.  
- Requests explicit complexity analysis for VI and MH (time/memory in n, m, T) including cost of optimizing Z and computing Ψ in continuous case.  
- Requests clarification on whether Z is state-specific or shared in retrospective MH, initialization/re-optimization protocol, and effects on mixing/acceptance.  

Clarity:
- Ask to define K, L and their relationship early, and motivate parameterizing L versus K.  
- Suggest trimming repeated discussion of K vs L and focus on practical implications (identifiability/interpretability).  
- Request full VI objective, optimization steps (what is fixed vs optimized), gradient availability, and stopping criteria.  
- For MH, request a summarized algorithm with retrospective acceptance, how m is increased, and when Z is optimized.  

Originality:
- Nonspectral bounds and extension to continuous DPPs are likely new; connection to inducing-variable approximations is well made.  

Significance:
- If bounds are tight with moderate m and Z can be optimized efficiently, approach could significantly reduce DPP inference costs; current experiments hint at promise but don’t establish clear advantage.  

Empirical clarification requests:
- Provide compute-time and memory comparisons for VI vs MH, including Z optimization overhead, acceptance rates, distribution of m in MH, and end-to-end training times.  
- Compare against strong baselines: spectral methods (Affandi et al. 2014), EM/fixed-point L-ensemble methods (Gillenwater et al. 2014; Mariet & Sra 2015), and Nyström approximations where applicable.  
- Diagnose when VI works: sensitivity to m, Z initialization, identifiability issues, and scenarios favoring VI vs MH.  
- Test using observed points as inducing inputs (e.g., toy with 13 points): assess VI and MH performance and stability.  
- Provide heuristics for choosing m and initializing Z (k-means, sampling from base measure/marginal intensity) and a criterion to grow m (e.g., bound-gap threshold).  

Minor comments:
- Define K/L and terms like “marginal kernel” and “L-ensembles” at first use.  
- Reduce discussion on learning K if not used; focus on identifiability (κ) when parameterizing L.  
- Include reproducibility details: gradient computation for Z, optimizers, learning rates, stopping criteria, restarts, and initialization strategies.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, general, and impactful method to avoid spectral decompositions in DPP inference; extends to continuous domains and unifies VI and MH approaches.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient empirical comparisons, limited datasets/scale, unclear guidance on when VI is reliable, and incomplete methodological clarity for reproducibility.
