Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have recently attracted considerable attention in machine learning for their ability to model repulsion among points in a set. This repulsion is encoded by a positive definite kernel. A central challenge is learning the kernel parameters from observed point sets. Estimation is hard because the normalization constant requires summing over all possible cardinalities and configurations. A recent approach [7] addresses this by estimating the spectrum to construct incremental upper and lower bounds, yielding a provably correct “retrospective” MCMC scheme for kernel parameter inference.

This paper proposes a different class of bounds that enable MCMC inference without estimating eigenvalues. The approach also supports a variational learning method and draws connections to inducing-point techniques for scalable Gaussian process models. The paper is technically strong and well written. I was unfamiliar with the inequality underpinning the lower bound in Proposition 1; it appears to be an excellent fit here and is a clever way to avoid the estimation challenges in [7]. I also appreciate the connections to GP inducing points and the resulting capability for variational inference. Overall, it was a pleasure to read.

I have two technical concerns:
1) While I like the approach, I am not convinced it is a clear computational win to replace power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems whose dimension grows.
2) It is not obvious that simply increasing the cardinality guarantees convergence of the bounds given the coupled optimization. The objective seems highly non-convex, so one must both increase cardinality and ensure attainment of a global minimum. Otherwise, a local minimum could prevent bounding away the Bernoulli threshold, making it impossible to accept or reject an MCMC step. This should be addressed directly.

Presentation issues:

- The proof of the key proposition is the central insight; it should appear in the main text rather than the appendix.
- P7, L361: “… the variational lower is …” (typo).

In sum, this is a strong paper that uses a clever bound on the DPP partition function, enabling potentially faster inference and connections to other modeling frameworks.
