This paper studies likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), which model repulsion/diversity via a positive definite kernel. While DPPs admit attractive analytic properties, learning kernel parameters is challenging because the likelihood normalization involves a large determinant (finite case) or a Fredholm determinant (continuous case), typically requiring spectral decompositions. Prior work [7] addresses this by estimating leading eigenvalues to form tight upper and lower bounds and enable “retrospective” MCMC. In contrast, this paper proposes nonspectral bounds that avoid eigenvalue computation entirely. The bounds are constructed via inducing inputs, in the spirit of sparse Gaussian process approximations, and can be used for both variational inference and exact (but more expensive) MCMC via retrospective acceptance tests. The paper treats both finite and continuous domains, the latter with Fredholm determinants, and presents empirical results on synthetic and real data.

Strengths:
- Technical contribution: Proposition 1 provides tight, computationally cheap bounds based on an inequality among determinants that is very well suited to this problem. I was not previously aware of this inequality; it is a clever and elegant idea that cleanly circumvents the spectral estimation burden of [7].
- Scope: The framework handles both finite and continuous DPPs and unifies variational and MCMC inference; the extension to continuous DPPs with computable Ψ is valuable.
- Connections: The analogy to inducing-point methods in sparse Gaussian processes is insightful and helps position the work within scalable probabilistic inference.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and a pleasure to read.

Concerns and questions:
1) Computational trade-offs. While the approach removes power iterations, it introduces a sequence of nonlinear optimization problems over the inducing inputs Z (with increasing cardinality) and kernel parameters. It is not clear that this is always a net win computationally. Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison—both theoretical complexity and empirical wall-clock—between (a) power iterations plus spectral bounds [7] and (b) the proposed optimization-based tightening of nonspectral bounds, across regimes of n and target accuracy? In particular, how do time and memory scale with m and the number of optimization restarts required?

2) Convergence of retrospective MCMC with optimized bounds. The retrospective scheme requires tightening bounds until the Bernoulli accept/reject decision can be made. The manuscript argues this can be done by increasing |Z| and re-optimizing Z. However, because Z-optimization is coupled and (presumably) highly nonconvex, simply increasing m does not guarantee convergence of the bounds to the true likelihood unless global optima are consistently found. This raises the risk that the acceptance probability cannot be bounded away from the threshold for some proposals, potentially stalling the chain. Could the authors discuss:
- Conditions under which the optimized bounds provably converge as m→∞ (beyond the GP variance analogy), and what role local minima might play?
- Practical safeguards (e.g., multiple restarts, annealing, structured initializations) and diagnostics to detect when the optimization fails to tighten sufficiently?
- Any empirical evidence that the retrospective decisions always resolve within reasonable m across datasets?

Presentation issues:
- p.7, Figure 1: Please include axis labels for all subplots.
- The proof of the key determinant inequality application (Proposition 1) is central to the paper’s contribution. I recommend moving it from the appendix to the main text (or at least providing a more detailed sketch) so readers can fully appreciate the insight.
- p.7, line 361: “the variational lower is …” → “the variational lower bound is …”

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, elegant nonspectral bounds that broaden the applicability of likelihood-based DPP inference.
- Unification of variational and MCMC approaches with clear connections to sparse GP methodology.
- Empirical demonstrations on both synthetic and real datasets; continuous-domain treatment is a plus.

Cons for acceptance:
- Unclear computational advantage in regimes where spectral methods are feasible; more thorough comparisons would strengthen the case.
- Lack of guarantees (or a more thorough discussion) about convergence of the optimized bounds within the retrospective MCMC framework, given nonconvexity.

Overall evaluation:
A strong and interesting paper that introduces a clever technique to bound the DPP partition function without spectral information, enabling both variational and retrospective MCMC inference and forging useful links to inducing-point GPs. Addressing the computational trade-offs and the convergence concerns for the optimized bounds would further solidify the contribution.
