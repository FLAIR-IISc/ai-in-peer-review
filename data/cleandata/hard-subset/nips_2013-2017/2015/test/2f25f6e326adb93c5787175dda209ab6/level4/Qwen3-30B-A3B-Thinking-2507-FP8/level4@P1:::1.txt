

This paper addresses a significant challenge in learning determinantal point processes (DPPs): the computational difficulty of evaluating the normalization constant in the likelihood function, which involves large determinants or Fredholm determinants. The authors make an important contribution by deriving novel, non-spectral bounds on these determinants that do not require eigenvalue computations or spectral decompositions. This work builds upon and extends the recent approach by Affandi et al. (ICML 2013), who proposed spectral-based bounds for DPP inference, but the current paper's bounds offer a different computational pathway that avoids spectral estimation entirely.

The paper's main technical contribution is Proposition 1, which provides tight lower and upper bounds on the determinant of interest using a clever application of a determinant inequality. These bounds are computationally efficient to evaluate and can be directly incorporated into both variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be used to develop a variational inference method that avoids spectral computations, and they extend the approach to continuous DPPs with an analogous bound (Proposition 2). The connections to inducing-point methods in Gaussian processes are particularly insightful, drawing a clear parallel between the two fields that should help readers understand the novelty of the approach.

The paper is technically strong, well-written, and makes a valuable contribution to the literature on DPPs. The authors have carefully considered the practical implications of their approach, including the computational complexity of the bounds and how to optimize the pseudo-inputs that parameterize the bounds. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets (including the diabetic neuropathy dataset) demonstrates the effectiveness of their methods and shows convergence as the number of pseudo-inputs increases. The connection to Gaussian process inference is especially compelling and well-explained.

The main strengths of this paper include:
- Novel theoretical contribution: The non-spectral bounds are technically sound and represent a new approach to DPP inference.
- Practical applicability: The bounds lead to efficient variational inference methods and exact MCMC procedures that avoid spectral computations.
- Clear exposition: The paper is well-organized and explains the connections to related work (particularly Gaussian processes) effectively.
- Strong experimental validation: The experiments on both synthetic and real data demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.

The main concerns I have are:
1. Computational efficiency: While the paper claims these bounds avoid spectral computations, the optimization over pseudo-inputs (which increases in dimension as the number of pseudo-inputs grows) may not represent a significant computational advantage over spectral methods. The authors should provide a more concrete comparison of computational complexity between their approach and spectral methods, particularly for large-scale problems where spectral methods become expensive.

2. Convergence guarantees: The paper does not address whether the bounds converge as the number of pseudo-inputs increases, and how this relates to the optimization of the pseudo-inputs. Given that the optimization over pseudo-inputs is highly non-convex, it's unclear whether the bounds will reliably tighten as the number of pseudo-inputs increases. The authors should clarify whether they can guarantee convergence of the bounds to the true value as the number of pseudo-inputs grows, and how to ensure the optimization finds a sufficiently tight bound for MCMC acceptance decisions.

I also have minor presentation concerns:
- The proof of Proposition 1 is critical to the paper but is relegated to the supplementary material. Given the importance of this technical contribution, it would be beneficial to include the proof in the main paper.
- Figure 1 (P7) lacks axis labels, which makes it difficult to interpret the results.
- The phrase "the variational lower is" (P7, L361) should be corrected to "the variational lower bound."

Overall, this is a technically strong paper that makes an important contribution to the DPP literature. The non-spectral bounds are novel, well-motivated, and effectively applied to both variational inference and MCMC. While the computational advantages need to be more clearly articulated, the paper is well-written, the experiments are thorough, and the connections to Gaussian process inference are particularly compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the computational efficiency concerns and presentation issues.
