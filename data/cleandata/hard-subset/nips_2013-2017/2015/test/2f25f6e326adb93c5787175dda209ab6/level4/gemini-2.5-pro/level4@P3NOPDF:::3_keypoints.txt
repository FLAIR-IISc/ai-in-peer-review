- Introduces inducing-input-based approximate inference for DPPs to avoid spectral decomposition and reduce computational cost, with VI and approximate MCMC algorithms and experiments on synthetic and real data.  
- Methods are underdeveloped and empirical evaluation is insufficient to fully assess the contribution.  
- Major concern: lacks clear theoretical and empirical comparison between the VI and MCMC algorithms (trade-offs, computational complexity, wall-clock times).  
- Major concern: benchmarking is incomplete—needs comparisons to exact DPP inference (where feasible) and to other approximate DPP methods for both accuracy and cost.  
- Major concern: variational inference shows large parameter estimation error in a toy experiment; the paper fails to analyze why VI fails and under what conditions it can be trusted.  
- Major concern: selection and placement of inducing inputs is not addressed—no guidance, sensitivity analysis, or ablation studies (e.g., using sampled points as inducing inputs).  
- Minor comment: introduction uses matrices K and L without definition; brief explanations of marginal kernel and L-ensembles would improve readability.  
- Minor comment: discussions on learning marginal kernel parameters distract from the main inducing-point approach and should be condensed or removed.  
- Overall: promising, well-motivated approach but requires substantial revisions—clarer methods, stronger experiments, and guidance on inducing points—to be a valuable contribution.
