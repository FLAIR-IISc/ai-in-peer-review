Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have been receiving significant recent attention in the machine learning literature, due to their ability to model repulsion within realizations of a set of points. This repulsion is specified via a positive definite kernel function. One challenge, however, is learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel, given example point realizations. This estimation is difficult due to a difficult-to-compute normalization constant that, in effect, sums over all possible cardinalities and point configurations. A recent work [7] has provided a way to address this by observing that the spectrum can be estimated and used to provide incremental upper and lower bounds, resulting in a provably-correct scheme for "retrospective" Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) of the kernel parameters. The present paper proposes a different type of bound that can also be used for MCMC inference, but that does not require estimation of the eigenvalues. This approach also lends itself to a variational approach to learning, and the paper draws connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models.

This is a technically strong and well-written paper. I was not previously aware of the inequality that provides the lower bound in Proposition 1, and it appears to be an excellent fit for this problem. It represents a clever way to avoid the estimation issues of [7]. I particularly appreciate the connections to GP inducing points, as well as the ability to now perform variational inference. Overall, this paper was a pleasure to read. 

I have two technical concerns that I would like to see addressed. First, while I find this approach promising, I am not entirely convinced that it represents a substantial computational win to replace power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension. Second, it is not obvious to me that simply increasing the cardinality of the inducing points is guaranteed to result in convergence of the bounds, given the coupled optimization problem. Although I am not certain, I believe this optimization is highly non-convex, and thus one needs to ensure a global minimum is achieved when increasing cardinality. This is a crucial point that should be directly addressed, as a local minimum could prevent the procedure from being able to bound the Bernoulli threshold, hindering MCMC steps.

Regarding presentation, I have the following suggestions:

*   P7, Figure 1: Please include axis labels for the figures.
*   The proof of Proposition 1 is central to the paper's contribution and key insight. I would have preferred to see it in the main body of the paper, rather than in the supplementary material.
*   P7, L361: “…the variational lower is…” – please correct the grammar.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in DPP inference – avoiding spectral decomposition.
*   The proposed bounds are novel and mathematically sound, supported by a non-trivial inequality.
*   The connection to inducing point methods and variational inference provides practical algorithmic benefits.
*   The paper is well-written and clear.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical computational advantages over existing methods are not definitively demonstrated.
*   The convergence properties of the optimization problem involved in refining the bounds require further investigation and justification.



I believe this is a valuable contribution, and with minor revisions addressing the concerns above, it would be a strong addition to the conference.
