### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of parameter inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). DPPs are elegant models for capturing repulsion or diversity in a set of items, with the interaction strength governed by a kernel function. A primary obstacle to likelihood-based inference for DPPs is the computation of the normalizing constant—the determinant of `L + I` in the discrete case or a Fredholm determinant in the continuous case—which is generally intractable for large-scale problems.

The authors build upon recent work, such as Affandi et al. [7], which introduced MCMC methods that use bounds on the likelihood derived from spectral approximations of the kernel matrix. The key contribution of this paper is a novel set of upper and lower bounds on this determinant that, crucially, do not require any spectral information (i.e., eigenvalues). Instead, the bounds are derived using a low-rank approximation based on a set of "inducing points," drawing a strong and insightful analogy to sparse approximation methods in Gaussian Processes (GPs).

This new bounding strategy enables two main methodological advances:
1.  A retrospective MCMC sampler for Bayesian inference of the kernel parameters that avoids the need for iterative eigenvalue solvers.
2.  A variational inference framework for approximate inference, which is a new and significant development for DPP models.

The proposed methods are developed for both discrete and continuous DPPs and are validated on synthetic and real-world datasets.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core technical contribution—using an inequality from Seiler & Simon [14] to derive non-spectral bounds via inducing points—is both clever and highly effective for this problem. It provides a new and compelling alternative to the spectral methods of prior work. The introduction of a variational inference framework for DPPs is a significant practical advance that could substantially broaden their applicability to larger datasets where MCMC may be too slow.

2.  **Clarity and Quality of Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and a pleasure to read. The authors clearly motivate the problem, situate their work within the existing literature, and explain their technical contributions with precision. The connection drawn to inducing point methods for sparse GPs is particularly valuable, as it grounds the work in a familiar context for the machine learning community and illuminates the role of the variational parameters (the inducing points Z).

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper appears to be technically solid. The derivations are presented logically, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's validity. The 1D Gaussian-Gaussian experiment, where the true likelihood is computable, serves as an excellent sanity check, showing that the proposed MCMC sampler correctly recovers the posterior.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

While this is a strong paper, there are two main technical points that I believe warrant further discussion.

1.  **Practical Computational Trade-offs:** The paper frames the avoidance of power iterations as a major computational advantage. However, this is replaced by the need to solve a non-linear, and likely non-convex, optimization problem to tighten the bounds by positioning the inducing points `Z`. It is not immediately obvious that this trade is always a definitive computational win, particularly as the number of inducing points `m` increases to achieve tighter bounds. Could the authors provide more detailed comments on the practical complexity? How does the cost of optimizing over `Z` (which must be done whenever the bounds are insufficient for an MCMC decision) compare to the cost of computing a few leading eigenvalues?

2.  **Guarantees for the MCMC Sampler:** The correctness of the retrospective MCMC sampler relies on the ability to arbitrarily tighten the bounds by increasing `m` and re-optimizing over `Z`. However, the optimization of the lower bound with respect to `Z` is non-convex, and standard optimizers may only find a local minimum. It seems possible that for a given `m`, a poor local minimum could prevent the bounds from converging sufficiently to make an acceptance decision, effectively stalling the Markov chain. Does the procedure remain provably correct under this non-convexity? Is there a guarantee that for a sufficiently large `m`, the bounds will always successfully bracket the acceptance threshold, regardless of the quality of the solution found for `Z`? This seems to be a crucial point for the robustness of the MCMC algorithm.

### **Minor Comments and Presentation Issues**

*   **Figure 1:** The plots in Figure 1 are missing axis labels, which would improve their readability.
*   **Proof of Proposition 1:** The proof of the central proposition is relegated to the supplementary material. While I understand the space constraints, the paper's core insight relies on this result. The authors might consider including a brief proof sketch or at least the key intuition in the main body to make the work more self-contained.
*   **Typo:** On page 7, line 361, there is a minor typo: "...the variational lower is..." should likely be "...the variational lower bound is...".

### **Overall Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the literature on inference for determinantal point processes. By introducing non-spectral bounds based on inducing points, the authors have developed a powerful new approach that enables both an alternative MCMC scheme and a much-needed variational inference framework. The work is technically strong, clearly presented, and the connection to sparse GPs is particularly insightful. Despite the questions raised about computational trade-offs and MCMC convergence guarantees, the strengths of the paper are substantial. I recommend acceptance.
