Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have received significant recent attention in machine learning for modeling repulsion among points, specified via a positive definite kernel. A core challenge is learning the kernel parameters from observed point realizations, which is difficult due to an intractable normalization constant that effectively sums over all cardinalities and configurations. A recent proposal [7] addressed this by estimating the spectrum to obtain incremental upper and lower bounds, enabling provably correct “retrospective” MCMC over kernel parameters. This paper proposes a different class of bounds that avoid eigenvalue estimation, can be used for MCMC inference, and naturally yield a variational learning approach. The work also draws appealing connections to inducing-point methods for scalable Gaussian process models.

This is a technically strong and well-written paper. I was not aware of the inequality that underlies the lower bound in Proposition 1; it appears to be an excellent fit for this setting and provides a clever way to avoid the estimation issues in [7]. I particularly like the connections to GP inducing points and the ability to perform variational inference. Overall, this paper was a pleasure to read.

Technical concerns to address:
1) Computational advantages: While the approach is elegant, I am not yet convinced it represents a major computational win to replace power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension. Please clarify the expected complexity trade-offs and provide empirical evidence (e.g., wall-clock time vs. accuracy) comparing your optimization-based bounds to power-iteration-based spectral bounds such as in [7].
2) Convergence of bounds under increasing cardinality: It is not obvious that simply increasing the cardinality guarantees convergence of the bounds given the coupled optimization problem. My understanding is that the optimization is highly non-convex, so increasing cardinality alone may not suffice unless one can also ensure the global optimum is reached. This seems important to address directly, because converging only to a local minimum could prevent the procedure from bounding away the Bernoulli threshold and thereby make it impossible to execute an MCMC step. Please clarify: (a) any theoretical conditions under which the bounds converge as cardinality increases, (b) how optimization is performed to avoid poor local minima (e.g., guarantees or practical strategies), and (c) how failure to reach the global minimum would impact correctness or mixing of the retrospective MCMC.

Presentation issues:
- Page 7, Figure 1: Please include axis labels.
- The proof of the key proposition is the central insight that enables the paper. I would have preferred to see it in the main body rather than relegated to the appendix.
- Page 7, line 361: Typo—“… the variational lower is …” should read “… the variational lower bound is …”.

Overall assessment: A very nice paper that uses a clever trick to bound the partition function of a DPP, enabling potentially faster inference and illuminating connections to other models. Addressing the two concerns above—especially the computational trade-offs and the convergence/optimization issue—would significantly strengthen the contribution.
