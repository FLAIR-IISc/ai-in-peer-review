

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), proposing novel non-spectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid the expensive spectral decompositions required in previous approaches. The authors build upon the well-established connection between DPPs and Gaussian processes, extending variational inference techniques from GP literature [11] to DPPs. Their key contribution is deriving bounds on the likelihood that depend only on a set of pseudo-inputs (inducing points) rather than requiring spectral decomposition, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, progressing logically from finite to continuous domains. The theoretical development is sound, with careful derivations and proofs that are well-supported by references to relevant literature. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating the approach on both synthetic and real-world datasets, including a diabetic neuropathy dataset that showcases the practical relevance of DPPs for modeling spatial patterns. The authors effectively show that their bounds yield meaningful improvements in computational efficiency without sacrificing accuracy, as evidenced by the convergence of variational bounds to the ideal likelihood as the number of pseudo-inputs increases.

Regarding the four key criteria:

Quality: The technical contributions are solid and well-justified. The bounds are theoretically sound and practically useful, with careful attention to the computational complexity of the proposed methods. The authors provide clear comparisons with existing approaches [7] and demonstrate the computational advantages of their method. The experimental results are thorough and well-justified, with appropriate statistical analysis.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors make excellent use of the "pseudo-input" framework from GP literature to build intuition for their approach. The presentation of the finite and continuous cases is clear, with the latter being presented as a natural extension. The paper would benefit from slightly more explicit discussion of the relationship between the K and L parametrizations (which is mentioned but not resolved), but this is a minor point.

Originality: The main contribution—non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihoods—represents a meaningful advancement over existing methods. While the authors draw inspiration from GP literature, their application to DPPs is novel and addresses a specific bottleneck in the DPP literature. The paper demonstrates significant technical innovation in adapting GP techniques to the DPP setting.

Significance: The proposed approach has the potential to significantly broaden the applicability of DPPs in machine learning, particularly for large-scale problems where spectral decomposition is prohibitively expensive. The authors demonstrate this through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that their method can be applied to meaningful problems in spatial statistics and pattern analysis.

The paper has two minor weaknesses that should be addressed. First, the authors emphasize the interpretability issue of parametrizing L instead of K throughout the paper, but do not provide any theoretical or empirical analysis of how this affects inference. This is a notable gap, as the paper's main contribution is presented as an alternative to the spectral-based approaches that avoid this issue. Second, while the authors discuss the scalability of their method, they do not provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity of their approach versus existing spectral-based methods.

The authors' claim that their approach is "quite general with potential applications beyond determinantal point processes" is well-supported by their theoretical framework, though they could strengthen this by briefly discussing specific potential applications in other areas.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
1. Novel and technically sound contribution that addresses a significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference
2. Well-executed theoretical development with careful proofs and clear presentation
3. Comprehensive experimental evaluation that demonstrates practical utility
4. Clear connection to established GP literature, making the approach accessible to a broad audience

Con arguments for acceptance:
1. Does not resolve the interpretability issue of parametrizing L instead of K, despite highlighting it as a key concern
2. Limited discussion of computational complexity comparison with spectral-based methods
3. Some experimental results (like the variational approach's poor consistency) could be more thoroughly analyzed

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in DPP inference. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a meaningful problem with practical significance. The minor weaknesses noted above are easily addressable in a revision, and the paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. The authors' approach has the potential to significantly broaden the applicability of DPPs in machine learning, particularly for large-scale applications where spectral decomposition is prohibitive.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the interpretability discussion and provide more detailed computational complexity analysis. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling, with immediate relevance to practitioners working with DPPs.
