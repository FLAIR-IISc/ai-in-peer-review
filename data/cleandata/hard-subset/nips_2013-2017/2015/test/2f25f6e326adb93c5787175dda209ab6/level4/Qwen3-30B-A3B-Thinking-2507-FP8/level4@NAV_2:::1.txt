

The paper addresses the challenging problem of likelihood-based parameter inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), which model repulsive point patterns through positive definite kernels. While DPPs have gained significant traction in machine learning for diversity modeling, their inference is complicated by intractable normalization constants that require spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix or operator. Previous work [7] overcame this by using spectral approximations to provide bounds for retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The authors make a key contribution by deriving novel nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood that avoid spectral decomposition entirely, instead leveraging a determinant inequality that allows for efficient variational inference and exact MCMC sampling. These bounds connect naturally to inducing-point methods used in Gaussian process inference, extending the applicability of these techniques to DPPs. The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and presents a clever solution to a persistent challenge in DPP inference.

Strengths:
- The paper makes a significant technical contribution with Proposition 1, providing a novel inequality that enables efficient inference without spectral knowledge.
- The connections to Gaussian process inducing-point methods are insightful and well-executed, demonstrating the broader applicability of the approach.
- The paper is well-written with clear explanations of both the finite and continuous DPP cases.
- The experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed methods.
- The approach provides a viable alternative to spectral-based methods, particularly for large-scale or continuous domains where spectral decomposition is intractable.

Weaknesses:
- The computational advantage over spectral-based methods [7] is not clearly established. Replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization over inducing points (which grows with the number of pseudo-inputs) may not yield significant computational savings, especially when considering the iterative optimization required to tighten the bounds.
- The paper does not adequately address the convergence properties of the bound refinement procedure. The optimization over inducing points is highly non-convex, and it's unclear how the authors ensure that the procedure converges to a global minimum rather than a local minimum, which could prevent the MCMC acceptance step from being determined.
- Presentation issues: Figures lack axis labels (e.g., Figure 1), which hinders interpretation; the proof of the key inequality should be included in the main text rather than relegated to the appendix, as it represents the central insight of the paper; and there's a typo in line 361 ("variational lower is" should be "variational lower bound").

Pro-acceptance arguments: The paper presents a technically sound, novel solution to a well-known problem in DPP inference. The nonspectral bounds are both theoretically elegant and practically useful, with clear connections to established methods in Gaussian processes. The experimental validation demonstrates the approach's effectiveness across different domains.

Con-acceptance arguments: The computational advantages are not convincingly established, and the paper does not sufficiently address the convergence concerns of the optimization procedure. These technical issues could limit the practical applicability of the method, especially for large-scale applications where convergence guarantees matter.
