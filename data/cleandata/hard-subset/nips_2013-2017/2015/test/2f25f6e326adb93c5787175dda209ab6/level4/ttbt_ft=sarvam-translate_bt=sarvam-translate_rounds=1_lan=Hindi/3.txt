This paper proposes approximate estimation techniques for the deterministic point process (DPP), which does not require spectral knowledge of the kernel defining the DPP. Instead, motivated inputs are used to constrain the probability, which reduces computational costs. Both variational estimation and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques are described, and their efficiency is empirically demonstrated.

I believe this paper has some merit, although I found the method description to be somewhat rough and the empirical evaluation not thorough enough. Some points that could help clarify:

1) How to compare the computational complexity and overall computation time of the two proposed methods?
2) How do the proposed methods compare in terms of approximation quality and computation time compared to other options, both approximate and exact?
3) Section 5.1 shows empirical results on toy data that the parameter estimation error is very large using variational estimation, and instead relies on approximate MCMC. In what settings is the proposed variational estimation expected to work, and when is it expected to fail? Why?
4) A 13-point sample is generated for the toy example. How do the methods perform when the motivated inputs are actually these sampled points?
5) What are the rules to follow when selecting the number of motivated points, b) when placing the initial motivated points?

Minor Comments:

The entire introduction mentions the matrices K and L without explaining what they are. A brief mention of the kernel and L-ensemble may make it easier for an uninformed reader to follow.

The entire paper discusses the benefits of directly learning the kernel parameter, but these are not the approaches adopted. I believe some of this could be reduced as it does not contribute to the rest of the paper.
