This paper presents a novel approach to inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral knowledge of the kernel. The authors derive bounds on the likelihood of a DPP, both for finite and continuous domains, which can be used for variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The bounds are cheap to evaluate and do not rely on approximating the spectrum of a large matrix or an operator.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of inference for DPPs is clearly explained. The authors provide a thorough review of the existing literature and clearly state the contributions of their work. The technical content of the paper is sound, and the proofs of the propositions are provided in the supplementary material.

The paper is well-organized, and the structure is easy to follow. The authors start by introducing the problem of inference for DPPs and explaining the challenges associated with it. They then present their approach, which involves deriving bounds on the likelihood of a DPP using inducing inputs. The authors explain how these bounds can be used for variational inference and MCMC methods and provide experimental results to demonstrate the efficiency of their approach.

The experimental results are convincing, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both synthetic and real-world data. The results show that the proposed methods can provide accurate estimates of the parameters of the DPP and that the variational inference method can be used for approximate maximum likelihood training.

However, I do have some minor comments and suggestions for improvement. Firstly, the introduction could be improved by briefly explaining what the matrices K and L are, as they are referred to throughout the introduction without explanation. Additionally, some of the discussion about the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters could be cut back, as it does not contribute to the rest of the paper.

In terms of the method description, I found it to be somewhat rough and lacking in detail. For example, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the computational complexity and overall compute time of the two proposed methods compare. Additionally, the authors do not provide a thorough comparison of their methods to other alternatives, both approximate and exact, in terms of quality of approximation and computation time.

Furthermore, the empirical results on the toy data in Section 5.1 show that parameter estimation error using variational inference is too large, and the authors resort to approximate MCMC instead. However, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of in what settings the proposed variational inference is expected to work and when it is expected to fail.

Finally, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how to select the number of inducing points and how to place the initial inducing points. A rule of thumb or some guidance on this would be helpful.

Overall, I believe that this paper has merit, but it requires some improvements to the method description and empirical evaluation. With some revisions to address these issues, I believe that this paper could make a valuable contribution to the field.
