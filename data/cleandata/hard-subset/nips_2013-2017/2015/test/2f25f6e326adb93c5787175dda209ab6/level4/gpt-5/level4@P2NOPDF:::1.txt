Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have gained significant attention for modeling repulsion among points via a positive definite kernel. A core challenge is learning the kernel parameters from observed point sets, since the likelihood involves a normalization constant that sums over all cardinalities and configurations and is expensive to compute. Prior work [7] addressed this by estimating the kernel spectrum to construct incremental upper and lower bounds, enabling a provably correct “retrospective” MCMC scheme for parameter inference. This paper proposes an alternative family of bounds that avoids eigenvalue estimation, supports MCMC inference, and naturally yields a variational learning approach. The resulting method also connects to inducing-point techniques for scalable Gaussian process models.

This is a technically strong and clearly written paper. The inequality underlying Proposition 1 was new to me and seems exceptionally well suited to DPPs, providing a clever way around the spectral estimation issues in [7]. I particularly appreciate the GP inducing-point perspective and the ability to perform variational inference. Overall, the paper was a pleasure to read.

I have two technical concerns:

1) Computational trade-offs. Although the approach is appealing, I am not yet convinced it offers a substantial computational advantage over [7]. Replacing power iterations with a sequence of nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension may be costly in practice. Please provide theoretical and/or empirical evidence clarifying when your optimization is faster or more stable than spectrum estimation, including wall-clock comparisons, scaling with dataset size and effective rank, and sensitivity to initialization.

2) Convergence of bounds with increasing cardinality. It is not obvious that simply enlarging the inducing set guarantees convergence of the bounds, given the coupled, apparently non-convex optimization. Convergence may require both increased cardinality and reliably attaining a global minimum at each step. Please discuss conditions ensuring global optimality (or why local optima suffice), potential convex relaxations or certificates, and practical strategies—such as warm starts, continuation, multiple restarts, or diagnostics—to avoid poor local minima. I am particularly concerned that suboptimal solutions could fail to separate from the Bernoulli acceptance threshold, potentially stalling retrospective MCMC updates.

In sum, this is a strong paper that leverages a clever inequality to bound the DPP partition function, enabling potentially faster inference and insightful connections to related models.
