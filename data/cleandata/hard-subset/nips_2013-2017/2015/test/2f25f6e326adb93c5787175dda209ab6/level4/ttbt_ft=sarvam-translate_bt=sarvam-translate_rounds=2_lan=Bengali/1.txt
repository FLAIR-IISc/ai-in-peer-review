The Determinantal Point Process (DPP) has recently gained significant attention in the field of machine learning because it is capable of modeling repulsion within the implementation of point sets.

This repulsion is specified by a positive definite kernel function.

However, a challenge lies in learning the appropriate parameters of this given kernel, for example, the point implementation.

This is difficult to predict because there is a complex normalization constant that adds to virtually all possible cardinality and point configurations.

A recent proposal [7] has provided a way to do this. The proposal observes that the spectrum can be estimated and used to provide increasing upper and lower bounds, thereby creating a provably-correct scheme for the "retrospective" Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) of kernel parameters.

The present article proposes a different type of boundary that can also be used for MCMC estimation, but does not require the estimation of eigenvalues.

This method is also suitable for a variational approach to learning, and this article links it to the induction-point approach for scalable estimation in Gaussian process models.

This is a technically strong and well-written article.

I was unaware of the inequality providing lower bounds in proposal 1, and it seems like an excellent solution to this problem.

[7] This is a clever way to avoid estimation problems.

I liked the connection with GP induction points and now the ability to variational estimation.

Overall, this article was enjoyable to read.

However, I have two technical concerns that I would like to address.

Firstly, although I like this method very much, increasing non-linear optimization instead of power iterations is a huge computational victory, but it does not seem very convincing to me.

Secondly, it is not clear to me that a simple increase in cardinality will ensure the convergence of the bounds, as this is a connected optimization problem.

Although I am not sure, I think this optimization is extremely non-convex, and therefore it is necessary to increase the cardinality and ensure a global minimum is achieved.

This is something that should be addressed directly, as it seems that a local minimum approach could bind the Bernoulli threshold and make it impossible to take the MCMC step.
