

This paper introduces novel upper and lower bounds for matrix determinants, with applications to bounding the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs). The core idea—leveraging pseudo-inputs to bound functionals of positive definite kernels—is both simple and insightful. The work is well-executed, timely, and addresses a problem of significant interest to the machine learning community, particularly for its potential to generalize beyond DPPs. The experimental evaluation is thorough and effectively demonstrates key insights, including the limitations of variational approximations under specific conditions. The chosen application domain is highly relevant and well-motivated, enhancing the paper's impact.

The primary strength lies in the generality of the approach and its clear exposition. The bounds are derived with mathematical rigor, and the connection to practical DPP likelihood estimation is compelling. The authors successfully bridge theoretical contributions with empirical validation, making the results accessible and applicable.

**Minor concerns:**
1.  The paper emphasizes parameterizing the kernel matrix $K$ (rather than its Cholesky factor $L$) in the abstract and throughout, yet the focus remains on problem formulation without addressing this choice. It is unclear why this parameterization is highlighted as a central issue when the proposed bounds do not resolve it. Clarifying the rationale or explicitly addressing this distinction would strengthen the narrative.
2.  Should the paper be accepted, the authors are encouraged to submit a standard supplementary material document rather than simply uploading an extended version of the main paper. This would improve accessibility and align with common NeurIPS practices.

**Future work suggestions:**
The discussion on the interpretability of $K$-parameterization is particularly compelling. It would be valuable to explore whether the determinant can be bounded directly in terms of $K$, potentially simplifying implementation. Additionally, the scalability of the pseudo-input method for large-scale determinant bounding merits investigation—how does computational cost scale with problem size, and what approximations might be viable for massive datasets?

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the understanding of determinant computation in kernel methods. The results are well-supported, the writing is clear, and the work opens promising avenues for future research. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's substantial merits. I recommend acceptance.
