- Addresses intractable normalizing constants in DPPs by proposing nonspectral bounds that avoid spectral decomposition.  
- Derives theoretical nonspectral bounds for finite and continuous DPPs using inducing (pseudo-)inputs to approximate the kernel.  
- Computational complexity reduced to O(n m^2) for n data points and m inducing inputs versus O(n^3) for direct determinants.  
- Bounds enable integration into variational inference and retrospective MCMC, with practical computational costs.  
- Empirical validation on synthetic and real datasets, including diabetic neuropathy, showing meaningful parameter recovery and disease-stage separation.  
- Novel and significant technique with potential applicability beyond DPPs to other kernel-based models where spectral methods are prohibitive.  
- Propositions 1 and 2 are theoretically sound and well-motivated by connections to sparse Gaussian processes.  
- Variational inference description is brief and lacks details on the optimization of inducing inputs and their effectiveness.  
- Variational approach shows highly variable parameter estimates (e.g., κ varies orders of magnitude), while MCMC gives more consistent results.  
- Paper lacks analysis of when variational inference succeeds or fails and why (parameterization, likelihood landscape, or inducing input choices).  
- Insufficient comprehensive comparison with existing methods; needs a table comparing time complexity, approximation accuracy, and implementation requirements.  
- Discussion of kernels K versus L is tangential and should be streamlined; authors should better justify parametrizing L despite interpretability loss.  
- Recommended revisions: analyze variational failure modes, add comparison table, clarify inducing input optimization and selection guidelines, and tighten K vs L discussion.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions—strong technical merit and promising experiments but evaluation and presentation need improvement.
