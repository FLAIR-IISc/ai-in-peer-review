Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have been receiving significant attention in the machine learning literature, due to their ability to model repulsion within realizations of a set of points. This repulsion is specified via a positive definite kernel function. One challenge, however, is learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel, given example point realizations. This estimation is difficult due to a difficult-to-compute normalization constant that, in effect, sums over all possible cardinalities and point configurations. A recent proposal [7] has provided a way to address this by observing that the spectrum can be estimated and used to provide incremental upper and lower bounds, resulting in a provably-correct scheme for "retrospective" Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) of the kernel parameters. The present paper proposes a different type of bound that can also be used for MCMC inference, but that does not require eigenvalue estimation. This approach also lends itself to variational inference, and the paper draws connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models. 

This is a technically strong and well-written paper. I was not aware of the inequality that provides the lower bound in Proposition 1, and it seems like an excellent fit for this problem. It is a clever way to avoid the estimation issues of [7]. I particularly appreciate the connections to GP inducing points, as well as the ability to now perform variational inference. Overall, this paper was a pleasure to read. 

I have two technical concerns that I would like to see addressed. First, while I appreciate this approach, I find it less compelling that replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension constitutes a substantial computational win. Second, it is not obvious to me that simply increasing the cardinality of the inducing points is guaranteed to result in convergence of the bounds, due to the coupled optimization problem. Although I am not certain, I believe this optimization is highly non-convex, and therefore one needs to not only increase cardinality but also ensure a global minimum is achieved. This should be addressed directly, as a local minimum could prevent the procedure from bounding the Bernoulli threshold and making an MCMC step impossible.

In summary, this is a nice paper that uses a clever trick to bound the partition function of a determinantal point process, potentially leading to faster inference and connections to other types of models.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and novel approach to DPP inference.
*   Avoids the computationally expensive spectral decomposition required by previous methods.
*   Clear connections to existing techniques in Gaussian process modeling.
*   Well-written and easy to follow.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness through experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational benefit over existing methods is not fully convincing.
*   The convergence properties of the proposed bound refinement strategy (increasing cardinality of inducing points) are not fully established.
*   Potential for getting stuck in local optima during the optimization of inducing points.
