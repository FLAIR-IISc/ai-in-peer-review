This paper proposes novel, cheap-to-evaluate, nonspectral bounds on the determinants arising in the likelihoods of determinantal point processes (DPPs), both finite and continuous. The authors demonstrate how to use these bounds to infer the parameters of a DPP and explore their application in expensive-but-exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and cheap-but-approximate variational inference.

The paper provides a clear and concise summary of the main ideas and relates them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors effectively explain the technical details of their approach, including the derivation of bounds on the likelihood of a DPP and the application of these bounds in variational inference and MCMC. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with the topic.

The strengths of the paper include the novelty of the approach, the thoroughness of the technical details, and the empirical evaluation of the methods. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity and overall compute time of the proposed methods, which is essential for understanding their practicality. The comparison with other alternatives, both approximate and exact, is also a significant contribution.

However, there are some areas for improvement. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the settings in which the proposed variational inference is expected to work and when it is expected to fail. The authors mention that the variational inference approach may not be suitable for certain cases, but a more detailed analysis of the limitations of the approach would be helpful.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from more minor revisions to improve clarity and readability. For example, the introduction could be improved by briefly explaining the marginal kernel and L-ensembles, which are referred to throughout the paper without explanation. Some of the discussion about the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters could also be cut back, as it does not contribute significantly to the rest of the paper.

Overall, I believe there is merit to this paper, and with some revisions to address the mentioned concerns, it has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field.
