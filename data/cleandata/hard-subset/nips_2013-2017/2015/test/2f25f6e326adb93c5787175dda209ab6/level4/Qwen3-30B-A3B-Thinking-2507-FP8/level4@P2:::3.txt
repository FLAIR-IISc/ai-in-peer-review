

This paper addresses a significant challenge in determinantal point process (DPP) inference: the computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition required for likelihood evaluation. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on DPP likelihoods that avoid spectral decomposition by utilizing inducing inputs (pseudo-inputs), enabling both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. This approach directly extends work on sparse Gaussian processes to DPPs, offering a computationally efficient alternative to existing methods that rely on spectral approximations [7].

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality through rigorous derivations of the bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) and establishes their connection to existing variational methods for Gaussian processes. The authors make a clear contribution to the field by addressing a fundamental limitation in DPP inference—spectral decomposition—which has been a barrier to applying DPPs at scale. Their bounds are computationally cheaper than previous approaches that require eigendecomposition of large matrices or operators, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications like the news corpus example in [3].

The significance of this work is evident in its potential to make DPPs more practical for real-world applications where spectral decomposition is intractable. The paper successfully demonstrates this through empirical validation on both synthetic and real datasets (the diabetic neuropathy dataset), showing that the proposed methods can achieve results comparable to spectral-based approaches with reduced computational cost. The experiments also reveal important insights about parameter identifiability when using the L-parametrization, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

However, several aspects of the paper require improvement. The empirical evaluation is insufficiently thorough. While the authors compare their method to spectral-based approaches in terms of computational efficiency, they do not provide a comprehensive comparison with alternative approximate inference methods for DPPs (e.g., [8, 9]). The comparison should include metrics such as accuracy of parameter estimation, convergence rates, and trade-offs between computational cost and inference quality.

The paper does not adequately address when variational inference is expected to work versus when MCMC is necessary. In Section 5.1, the authors note that variational inference yields inconsistent results for the toy example, requiring the use of MCMC instead. The authors should provide more concrete guidance about the conditions under which variational inference would be appropriate versus when MCMC is necessary, including specific characteristics of the data or model parameters that would determine this choice.

The paper lacks clear guidelines for selecting the number of inducing inputs. While the authors mention that they increase m when acceptance decisions cannot be made in MCMC, they do not provide a principled rule for choosing the initial number of inducing points or for placing them. For practitioners, this is a critical implementation detail that would benefit from more thorough discussion.

The writing would be significantly improved by more precise notation and clearer explanations of the kernel relationships. The paper frequently refers to K and L without clearly defining their relationship to the marginal kernel and L-ensembles. A brief explanation of these concepts in the introduction would help readers unfamiliar with DPPs follow the technical developments more easily.

The paper's focus on the interpretability trade-offs of directly parametrizing L rather than K is somewhat tangential to the main contribution. While this discussion is relevant to DPP modeling more broadly, it doesn't contribute to the specific inference methods proposed and could be shortened to improve focus.

The paper's main strengths are its theoretical contribution (novel nonspectral bounds), practical significance (making DPP inference more scalable), and clear demonstration of the method's applicability. The weaknesses include insufficient empirical comparison with alternatives, lack of guidance for practitioners on key implementation details, and some unnecessary tangents.

Arguments for acceptance: 
- Novel theoretical contribution addressing a fundamental limitation in DPP inference
- Practical computational advantages over existing methods
- Clear demonstration of the method's utility through empirical validation
- Significant potential to advance the application of DPPs in real-world scenarios

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incomplete empirical comparison with alternative inference methods
- Insufficient guidance for practitioners on key implementation choices
- Some tangential discussion that detracts from the main contribution
- Inadequate explanation of key technical concepts for readers unfamiliar with DPPs

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling, and with the suggested improvements to the empirical evaluation and exposition, it would be a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings. The authors should provide a more thorough comparison with alternative methods, clarify the practical implementation guidelines, and streamline the discussion of kernel parametrization to focus on the main contribution.
