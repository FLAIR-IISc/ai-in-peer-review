- Core contribution: proposes scalable DPP inference that avoids spectral decomposition using inducing inputs, with VI and approximate MCMC methods.  
- Positive: avoiding spectral knowledge for DPPs is valuable and aligns with scalable probabilistic modeling trends.  
- Major concern: computational complexity and runtime comparisons between VI and MCMC are missing; need time-complexity analysis (scaling with m vs n) and wall-clock experiments.  
- Missing baselines: no benchmarks against exact spectral DPP inference or established approximate methods for accuracy (log-likelihood, diversity) and speed.  
- VI reliability: toy example shows large parameter errors from VI; paper fails to explain when VI succeeds or fails (model misspecification, initialization, or variational family limits).  
- Inducing point choice: unclear performance when inducing points equal data points; current independent selection may not reflect realistic use cases.  
- No practical guidance: lacks rules of thumb for selecting number m of inducing points or initialization/placement strategies (e.g., scaling laws, k-means).  
- Empirical evaluation weak: needs comparative benchmarks, sensitivity analyses over m, and clearer narrative on method applicability.  
- Presentation issues: matrices K and L are not defined early; discussions about "directly learning marginal kernel parameters" are misleading/redundant.  
- Recommendation: promising direction but requires substantial revisions—add rigorous empirical comparisons, computational analysis, and diagnostics of VI failure modes; recommend rejection pending major revisions.
