This paper introduces novel, non-spectral bounds for the determinants that appear in the likelihood function of determinantal point processes (DPPs), for both finite and continuous domains. The core idea is to leverage a set of "pseudo-inputs," in a manner analogous to inducing variables in sparse Gaussian processes, to construct computationally cheap upper and lower bounds on the intractable determinant term. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be effectively integrated into standard inference frameworks, enabling both an approximate variational inference scheme and an exact Metropolis-Hastings MCMC sampler. The main idea is simple, but the paper is well-executed, enlightening, and paves the way for several potential improvements.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Elegance:** The use of pseudo-inputs to create non-spectral bounds is a creative and elegant approach to a well-known bottleneck in DPP inference. The connection drawn to sparse approximations in Gaussian processes is insightful.
*   **Significance and Timeliness:** The problem of scalable inference for DPPs is highly relevant. This work provides a practical tool that will likely be of significant interest to researchers working on point processes and related models.
*   **Generality:** The proposed bounding technique is quite general and could potentially be applied to other problems involving large determinants, beyond DPPs.
*   **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed. It is particularly commendable that the authors demonstrate not only the success of their MCMC method but also the potential pitfalls of the variational approach, providing valuable insights into the model's parameter identifiability issues. The chosen application is interesting and highly relevant.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The paper's motivation heavily emphasizes the superior interpretability of parameterizing the `K` kernel, as opposed to the more common but less intuitive `L` kernel. However, the proposed technical contribution works exclusively with the `L` kernel and does not address this `K` vs. `L` challenge. This creates a slight disconnect between the stated problem and the delivered solution. While the work is a strong contribution to inference in `L`-parameterized DPPs, the framing could be clarified to manage reader expectations regarding the interpretability issue.

**Minor Comments:**

*   If the paper is accepted, I would recommend that the authors prepare a standard, self-contained supplementary document rather than simply an extended version of the main paper.

**Suggestions for Future Work:**

*   Given the paper's discussion on the benefits of the `K` parameterization, an exciting direction for future work would be to extend this bounding approach to work directly with the `K` kernel. Could the authors comment on the potential challenges and scalability of applying the pseudo-input method in that setting?
