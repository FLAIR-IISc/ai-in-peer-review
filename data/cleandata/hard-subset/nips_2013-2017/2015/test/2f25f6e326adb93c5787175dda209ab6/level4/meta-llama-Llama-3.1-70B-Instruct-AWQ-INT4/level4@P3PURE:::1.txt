Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have garnered significant attention in the machine learning literature due to their ability to model repulsion within realizations of a set of points, specified via a positive definite kernel function. One challenge, however, is learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel, given example point realizations. This estimation is difficult due to a difficult-to-compute normalization constant that, in effect, sums over all possible cardinalities and point configurations.

A recent proposal [7] has provided a way to address this challenge by observing that the spectrum can be estimated and used to provide incremental upper and lower bounds, resulting in a provably-correct scheme for "retrospective" Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) of the kernel parameters. The present paper proposes a different type of bound that can also be used for MCMC inference, but that does not require estimation of the eigenvalues. This approach also lends itself to a variational approach to learning, and the paper draws connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models.

This is a technically strong and well-written paper. The inequality that provides the lower bound in Proposition 1 is an excellent fit for this problem, and it is a clever way to avoid the estimation issues of [7]. The connections to GP inducing points, as well as the ability to now perform variational inference, are particularly noteworthy. Overall, this paper was a pleasure to read.

However, I have two technical concerns that I would like to see addressed. First, although I appreciate the proposed approach, I am not convinced that it is a huge computational win to replace power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension. Second, it is not obvious to me that simply increasing the cardinality is guaranteed to result in convergence of the bounds, due to the coupled optimization problem. I believe this optimization is highly non-convex, and therefore, one needs to increase cardinality and also ensure that a global minimum is achieved. This is something that should be addressed directly, as a local minimum could prevent the procedure from being able to bound away the Bernoulli threshold and make it impossible to take an MCMC step.

Overall, this is a nice paper that uses a clever trick to bound the partition function of a determinantal point process, leading to potentially faster inference and connections to other kinds of models.
