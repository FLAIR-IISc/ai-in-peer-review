

The paper presents novel upper and lower bounds on matrix determinants with applications to bounding the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs). The core idea—leveraging pseudo-inputs to bound functionals of positive definite kernels—is conceptually simple yet elegantly executed, offering significant clarity to a technically dense problem. The authors effectively demonstrate how these bounds enable tighter likelihood approximations in DPPs, while also highlighting limitations of existing variational approaches under specific conditions. The work is timely, well-motivated, and directly relevant to the broader machine learning community, particularly those working with probabilistic models involving kernel matrices.  

The paper’s strengths are manifold. First, the theoretical development is rigorous yet accessible, with clear exposition of the bounds and their derivation. Second, the experimental evaluation is meticulously designed to validate the claims: it not only confirms the tightness of the proposed bounds but also systematically identifies failure modes of standard variational methods (e.g., when kernel eigenvalues are highly skewed). Third, the authors wisely emphasize the generalizability of their approach beyond DPPs—potential applications in Gaussian process inference, kernel approximation, or Bayesian optimization are explicitly noted, significantly broadening the paper’s impact. The choice of experimental settings (e.g., synthetic data with controlled kernel properties, real-world DPP applications) effectively underscores the practical utility of the method.  

However, two minor issues warrant attention. First, the paper places considerable emphasis on the parameterization of the kernel matrix \(K\) versus its Cholesky factor \(L\) in the abstract and early sections, yet the technical content does not engage with the challenges of \(K\)-parameterization. The authors describe the problem setup but do not address how to handle this parameterization (e.g., via optimization constraints or alternative representations). This disconnect risks confusing readers about the paper’s scope. A brief clarification in the introduction or a footnote would resolve this.  

Second, the supplementary material should be structured as a standard, concise supplement (e.g., proofs, extended experiments) rather than an extended version of the main paper. This aligns with NeurIPS submission guidelines and ensures the main paper remains focused.  

For future work, the authors could explore two promising directions. First, bounding the determinant directly in terms of \(K\) (rather than \(L\)) would directly address the parameterization concern raised above and may yield more interpretable bounds for practitioners. Second, the scalability of the pseudo-input approach—particularly its computational cost relative to the number of pseudo-inputs and data points—deserves deeper analysis. While the paper mentions scalability as a consideration, concrete complexity bounds (e.g., \(O(n^2m)\) for \(n\) data points and \(m\) pseudo-inputs) would strengthen the practical appeal.  

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances both theory and practice in DPPs and kernel methods. The bounds are theoretically sound, the experiments are compelling, and the broader applicability is well-justified. The minor issues noted are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper’s core value. The work not only provides a useful tool for DPP likelihood estimation but also opens avenues for more robust kernel-based inference in general.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a clear, well-executed contribution with immediate relevance to the NeurIPS community. The proposed bounds are a valuable addition to the toolkit for probabilistic modeling with determinants, and the experimental insights into variational approximation failures are particularly noteworthy. With minor revisions to clarify the \(K\)-vs-\(L\) parameterization and supplement structure, this paper will be a significant asset to the field.
