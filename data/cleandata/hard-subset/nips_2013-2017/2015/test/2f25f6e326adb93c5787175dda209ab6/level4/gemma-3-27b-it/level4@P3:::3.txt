This paper addresses the challenging problem of inference in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) by proposing novel bounds on the likelihood that avoid costly spectral decompositions. The core idea is to leverage inducing inputs to approximate the DPP likelihood, enabling both variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The authors correctly identify a key bottleneck in DPP inference – the need for spectral information – and offer a pragmatic solution. The paper is well-motivated, situating the work within the broader context of DPP modeling and inference techniques.

The paper's strengths lie in its technical contribution – the derivation of tractable bounds on the DPP likelihood – and its potential to broaden the applicability of DPPs to larger datasets where spectral methods become prohibitive. The connection to variational inference for Gaussian Processes (GPs) is clearly drawn, providing intuition for the proposed approach. The empirical validation, though limited, demonstrates the feasibility of the proposed methods. The authors also acknowledge and discuss the trade-offs involved in directly parametrizing the kernel L, which demonstrates careful consideration of the modeling choices.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The methodological description feels somewhat terse, lacking sufficient detail to fully reconstruct the implementation. The empirical evaluation, while present, is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods across a range of scenarios. Specifically:

1) **Computational Complexity & Runtime:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and runtime of both variational inference and the proposed MCMC algorithm, relative to existing methods (e.g., the power iteration approach mentioned in the introduction). A table comparing runtime on different dataset sizes would be particularly valuable.

2) **Comparison to Existing Methods:** A more thorough comparison to alternative DPP inference methods is needed. How do the proposed bounds compare to existing approximation techniques in terms of accuracy and computational cost?  Are there situations where existing methods outperform the proposed approach? 

3) **Variational Inference Limitations:** The observation in Section 5.1 that variational inference yields poor parameter estimates and necessitates switching to MCMC raises concerns about its practical utility. The authors should provide a clearer explanation of the conditions under which variational inference is expected to succeed or fail, and the factors influencing its performance. A discussion of sensitivity to initialization and hyperparameter tuning would also be helpful.

4) **Inducing Point Placement:** The choice of inducing points significantly impacts the accuracy of the approximation. The paper briefly mentions optimizing their locations, but lacks guidance on initialization strategies. Investigating the performance with inducing points selected directly from the data (as suggested by question 4 in the original review) could be insightful.  Providing a rule of thumb or a heuristic for choosing the number and placement of inducing points would increase the paper’s practical value.

5) **Kernel Explanation:** The initial introduction of kernels K and L could be improved with a brief, intuitive explanation of their roles within the DPP framework. While the authors correctly reference relevant literature, a concise definition would aid readers less familiar with the area.

6) **Redundant Discussion:** The repeated discussion of the trade-offs between parametrizing K versus L feels somewhat repetitive and detracts from the core contribution. Streamlining this section would improve the paper’s clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a significant challenge in DPP inference – avoiding spectral decomposition.
*   The proposed bounds are technically sound and offer a practical alternative to existing methods.
*   The empirical results, although limited, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.
*   The connection to GP variational inference provides valuable intuition.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The methodological description lacks sufficient detail for easy replication.
*   The empirical evaluation is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed methods.
*   The limitations of variational inference are not adequately addressed.
*   The paper lacks clear guidance on practical aspects such as inducing point selection.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of DPP modeling. However, the current presentation lacks the polish and thoroughness expected of a NIPS publication. I recommend **conditional acceptance** pending significant revisions that address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should: 1) provide a more detailed methodological description, 2) conduct a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, including comparisons to existing methods, 3) thoroughly analyze the limitations of variational inference, and 4) offer practical guidance on inducing point selection.  Improving these aspects will substantially enhance the paper's clarity, impact, and overall quality.
