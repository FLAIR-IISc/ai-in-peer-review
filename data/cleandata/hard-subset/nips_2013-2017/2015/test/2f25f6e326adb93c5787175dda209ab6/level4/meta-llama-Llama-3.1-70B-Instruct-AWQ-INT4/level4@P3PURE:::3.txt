The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that do not require spectral knowledge of the kernel defining the DPP. Instead, inducing inputs are utilized to bound the likelihood, which reduces the computational cost. Both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques are described, and their efficiency is demonstrated empirically.

I believe there is merit to this paper; however, I found the method description to be somewhat rough and the empirical evaluation not thorough enough. To clarify and strengthen the paper, the authors may want to address the following points:

1. A comparison of the computational complexity and overall computation time of the two proposed methods would be beneficial. How do these methods compare in terms of computational efficiency?
2. It would be helpful to compare the proposed methods to other alternatives, both approximate and exact, in terms of the quality of approximation and computation time. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the methods' strengths and weaknesses.
3. The empirical results on the toy data in Section 5.1 show that the parameter estimation error using variational inference is too large, and the authors resort to approximate MCMC instead. It would be useful to discuss the settings in which the proposed variational inference is expected to work and when it is expected to fail, along with the underlying reasons for these expectations.
4. A 13-point sample is generated for the toy example. It would be interesting to investigate how the methods perform when the inducing inputs are actually these sampled points.
5. Providing a rule of thumb for selecting a) the number of inducing points and b) the placement of the initial inducing points would be helpful for practitioners.

Minor comments:

The matrices K and L are referred to throughout the introduction without explanation. Briefly mentioning the marginal kernel and L-ensembles may make it easier for uninformed readers to follow.

The discussion about the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters is repeated throughout the paper, but this approach is not taken. Some of this content could be removed, as it does not contribute to the rest of the paper.

Overall, I believe there is merit to this paper; however, I found the method description to be somewhat rough and the empirical evaluation not thorough enough. If accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to improve the write-up to address these concerns.
