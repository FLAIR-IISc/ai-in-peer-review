- Addresses the computational bottleneck of DPP likelihood inference by avoiding spectral decomposition.  
- Proposes novel likelihood bounds using inducing inputs to approximate the DPP likelihood.  
- Enables both variational inference and MCMC using the derived bounds.  
- Significant contribution because spectral decomposition is a practical barrier for large-scale DPPs.  
- Technical derivations (Propositions 1 and 2) are sound and inspired by Gaussian process techniques.  
- Methods are flexibly applicable, offering a speed–accuracy tradeoff between variational and MCMC approaches.  
- Paper situates itself well within existing DPP and related GP literature.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited; comparisons to existing methods are insufficient.  
- Toy example shows variational inference can yield poor parameter estimates without explaining why.  
- Method descriptions lack clarity, especially regarding optimization procedures.  
- Provides little practical guidance on choosing, initializing, or placing inducing inputs.  
- Motivation for parametrizing L rather than K is weak and leaves interpretability issues unresolved.  
- Requests for authors: report computational complexity and wall-clock scaling with data and inducing points.  
- Requests benchmarking against other approximate and exact DPP inference methods for accuracy and efficiency.  
- Requests analysis of when variational inference succeeds or fails and factors affecting its accuracy.  
- Requests investigation of inducing input placement sensitivity and heuristics for selecting their number.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, impactful approach with sound theoretical bounds and promising feasibility.  
- Rejection arguments: insufficient empirical validation, unclear method details, and lack of practical guidance.  
- Overall: promising idea but needs clearer presentation and substantially stronger experiments; reviewer leans weak accept pending revisions.
