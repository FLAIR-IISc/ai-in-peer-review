Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have emerged as a powerful tool in machine learning for modeling repulsion, where the repulsive behavior is encoded in a positive definite kernel. A key challenge in applying DPPs is learning the kernel parameters from data, a task complicated by the intractable normalization constant (or partition function) of the DPP likelihood, which involves a sum over all possible subsets of items. Recent work [7] addressed this by developing a retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) scheme that uses estimates of the kernel's spectrum to construct progressively tighter bounds on the likelihood.

This paper introduces an alternative method for bounding the DPP partition function. The proposed bounds, derived from a novel inequality, cleverly avoid the need for eigenvalue estimation. This new approach not only enables an alternative MCMC inference scheme but also facilitates a variational inference framework for learning DPP parameters, drawing an elegant connection to inducing point methods used for scalable Gaussian process inference.

This is a technically strong and well-written paper that was a pleasure to review. The central contribution is a novel inequality (Proposition 1) that provides a lower bound on the partition function. This insight is an excellent fit for the problem and offers a clever alternative to the eigenvalue-based estimation required by prior work [7]. The authors' derivation of a variational inference framework from this bounding strategy is particularly compelling, as is the connection they establish to inducing point methods in Gaussian processes. These contributions significantly advance the toolbox for DPP parameter estimation.

However, I have two technical concerns that I would like the authors to address:

1.  The paper argues that its approach is computationally advantageous by avoiding the power iterations required for eigenvalue estimation. However, it replaces this step with a sequence of non-linear optimization problems of increasing dimensionality. The authors should provide a more detailed comparison to justify why this trade-off represents a significant computational win, perhaps by discussing the typical convergence properties or computational complexity of both sub-problems in practice.

2.  A second, more significant concern relates to the convergence of the proposed bounds. The iterative tightening of the bounds relies on solving an optimization problem whose dimensionality increases with the cardinality of the inducing set. This optimization problem appears to be non-convex. If the optimization routine finds a poor local minimum, it is not clear that the bounds are guaranteed to tighten sufficiently with increasing cardinality. This could potentially stall the retrospective MCMC sampler, preventing it from "bounding away" the Bernoulli threshold and making a sampling decision. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this issue, addressing whether convergence of the bounds can be guaranteed or how the non-convexity might impact the robustness of the inference procedure in practice.

Finally, there are a few minor presentation issues that could be addressed:

*   Figure 1 (Page 7) is missing axis labels.
*   The proof of Proposition 1 is central to the paper's contribution. While page limits are a concern, the authors might consider moving a proof sketch or the key steps into the main body to highlight this core insight, rather than relegating it entirely to the appendix.
*   There is a minor typo on Page 7, Line 361: "...the variational lower is..." should likely be "...the variational lower bound is...".
