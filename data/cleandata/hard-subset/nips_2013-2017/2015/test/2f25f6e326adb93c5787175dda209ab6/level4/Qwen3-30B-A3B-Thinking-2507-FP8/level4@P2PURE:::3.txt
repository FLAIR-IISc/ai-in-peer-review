

The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid requiring spectral knowledge of the defining kernel. Instead, it employs inducing inputs to bound the likelihood, reducing computational cost. Both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are presented, with empirical efficiency demonstrated.  

While the paper has merit, the method description is somewhat rough and the empirical evaluation lacks thoroughness. The following points require clarification:  
1) What is the computational complexity and overall runtime comparison between the two proposed methods?  
2) How do the proposed methods compare to other approximate and exact alternatives in terms of approximation quality and computational time?  
3) In Section 5.1, parameter estimation error using variational inference is large on the toy data, necessitating approximate MCMC. Under what settings is variational inference expected to succeed or fail, and why?  
4) The toy example uses 13 sampled points. How do the methods perform when the inducing inputs are exactly these sampled points?  
5) Is there a rule of thumb for selecting (a) the number of inducing points and (b) the initial placement of inducing points?  

Minor comments:  
- The introduction refers to matrices K and L without explanation. Briefly defining the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would aid readability for less familiar readers.  
- The paper discusses advantages of directly learning marginal kernel parameters but does not adopt this approach. Some of this discussion could be shortened, as it does not contribute to the core methodology.  

I believe the paper has merit, but the write-up requires significant improvement. If accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to address these points.
