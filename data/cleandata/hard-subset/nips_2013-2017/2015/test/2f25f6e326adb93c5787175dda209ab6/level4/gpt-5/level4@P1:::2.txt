This paper introduces simple, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on determinants that yield tractable bounds on DPP likelihoods for both finite and continuous domains. The key idea is to use inducing (pseudo-)inputs Z to form a low-rank proxy Q = LYZ LZ−1 LZY and derive determinant bounds that avoid eigenvalue computations. These bounds drive two inference schemes: a fast variational approach and a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler. The methods are demonstrated on a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP, where ground-truth spectra are available, and on a real spatial dataset of epidermal nerve fibers in diabetic neuropathy.

Relation to prior work: The paper positions itself against spectral approaches that require partial eigendecompositions (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014), which are costly and limited to finite domains. The construction of bounds is inspired by sparse GP variational methods using inducing variables (e.g., Titsias, 2009), adapted here to determinants in DPP likelihoods. For continuous domains, the work leverages Fredholm determinants and is complementary to numerical quadrature approaches (e.g., Bornemann, 2010). Within the DPP literature (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar; Lavancier et al.), this paper contributes a new nonspectral route to likelihood-based inference.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-executed idea: translating inducing-point approximations into determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods.
- Broad applicability: the bounds do not require spectra and extend naturally from finite to continuous domains.
- Practical impact: enables both efficient variational training and exact-but-expensive MCMC via retrospective acceptance tests.
- Experiments are thoughtfully chosen to make key points: the toy 1D case validates the MCMC against an “ideal” likelihood; the neuropathy data showcase practical utility and convergence of bounds with increasing m.
- The paper is enlightening and likely to stimulate further developments, including beyond DPPs.

Weaknesses and notes:
- The paper repeatedly raises the tension between parameterizing K versus L (interpretability versus tractability) but does not attempt to resolve it; this emphasis feels disproportionate given that the proposed methods operate entirely in L.
- The supplement should be a standard, self-contained appendix (proofs, additional experiments), not merely an extended version of the paper.

Suggestions for future work:
- Given the appeal of K for interpretability, can similar inducing-point determinant bounds be derived directly in terms of K (e.g., by bounding det(I + L) with K = L(I + L)−1 or via identities linking K and L)? What is the computational profile and scalability of such bounds when pseudo-inputs are used?
- More broadly, the determinant-bounding framework may have applications beyond DPPs; exploring these directions could amplify impact.

Pros and cons for acceptance:
Pros:
- Novel, simple, and general bounding technique that removes a major practical bottleneck (spectral computations).
- Solid experimental evidence illustrating both capabilities and limits (e.g., variational failures under non-identifiability).
- Relevance to active areas (DPPs, scalable inference, kernels).
- Clear exposition and constructive connections to sparse GP ideas.

Cons:
- The K-versus-L parameterization issue is highlighted but not addressed; readers may expect some concrete progress or guidance.
- The paper does not explore bounds directly in K, which could improve interpretability—though this is appropriately left for future work.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound. The determinant inequalities are grounded in known results; finite and continuous cases are handled carefully. The retrospective MH construction is appropriate. Experiments are convincing.
- Clarity: Generally very clear and well organized. The narrative around K vs L could be tightened to match the actual scope of contributions. A conventional supplement would help reproducibility.
- Originality: The nonspectral determinant bounds with inducing inputs for DPP likelihoods are a fresh and useful contribution, extending ideas from sparse GPs to a new setting.
- Significance: High potential impact. These bounds enable practical variational and MCMC inference for DPPs without eigendecompositions and may generalize to other kernel-based models.

Minor comments:
- Please clarify why the K-versus-L discussion is emphasized in the abstract and introduction if no new technique is proposed to bridge them.
- If accepted, provide a standard supplementary document containing all proofs, implementation details, and extended results, rather than an extended paper.
