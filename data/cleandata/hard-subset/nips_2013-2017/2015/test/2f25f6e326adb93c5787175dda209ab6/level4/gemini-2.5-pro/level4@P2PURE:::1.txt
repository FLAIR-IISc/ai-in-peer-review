This is a technically strong and well-written paper that addresses the challenging problem of learning kernel parameters for determinantal point processes (DPPs). DPPs have recently gained attention for their ability to model repulsion using a positive definite kernel. However, parameter estimation is hindered by an intractable normalization constant (the partition function), which involves a sum over all possible point configurations and cardinalities.

A recent method [7] tackled this by using estimates of the kernel's spectrum to form incremental bounds for a provably-correct retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) scheme. This paper proposes an alternative bounding strategy that achieves a similar goal for MCMC inference but cleverly avoids the need to estimate eigenvalues. Furthermore, this approach naturally facilitates a variational inference framework and draws insightful connections to inducing-point methods for scalable Gaussian process models.

The inequality used to derive the lower bound in Proposition 1 is an excellent fit for this problem and provides an elegant way to circumvent the estimation challenges present in [7]. The connections to GP inducing points and the extension to variational inference are also significant contributions. Overall, the paper is a pleasure to read.

However, I have two technical concerns that I would like the authors to address:

1.  The claim of a significant computational advantage is not entirely convincing. The proposed method replaces the power iterations of [7] with a series of nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimensionality. It is not immediately clear that this trade-off results in a substantial net gain in efficiency, and this should be discussed more thoroughly.

2.  It is not obvious that simply increasing the cardinality of the inducing set guarantees convergence of the bounds. The associated optimization problem is likely non-convex, meaning that convergence would require finding a global minimum at each step. This issue warrants direct discussion, as getting stuck in a local minimum could prevent the bounds from becoming tight enough to resolve the Bernoulli test, thereby halting the MCMC sampler.
