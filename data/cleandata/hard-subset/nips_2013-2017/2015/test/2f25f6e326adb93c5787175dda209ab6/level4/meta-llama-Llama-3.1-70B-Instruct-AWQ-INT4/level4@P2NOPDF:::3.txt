The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that eliminate the need for spectral knowledge of the kernel defining the DPP. Instead, inducing inputs are utilized to bound the likelihood, which reduces the computational cost. The authors describe both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques and demonstrate their efficiency empirically.

I believe there is merit to this paper, but I found the method description to be somewhat rough and the empirical evaluation not thorough enough. To clarify and strengthen the paper, I suggest addressing the following points:

1. A comparison of the computational complexity and overall compute time of the two proposed methods would be beneficial. This would provide insight into the trade-offs between the two approaches and help readers understand their applicability in different scenarios.

2. It would be helpful to compare the proposed methods to other alternatives, both approximate and exact, in terms of quality of approximation and computation time. This would allow readers to better understand the strengths and limitations of the proposed methods.

3. The empirical results on the toy data in Section 5.1 show that parameter estimation error using variational inference is too large, and the authors resort to approximate MCMC instead. It would be beneficial to discuss the settings in which the proposed variational inference is expected to work and when it is expected to fail, along with the underlying reasons for this behavior.

4. The toy example generates a 13-point sample. I would like to see an evaluation of the methods when the inducing inputs are actually these sampled points. This would provide insight into the methods' performance in a more realistic setting.

5. Providing a rule of thumb for selecting the number of inducing points and placing the initial inducing points would be helpful for practitioners looking to apply these methods.

Minor comments:

The introduction refers to matrices K and L without explaining their meaning. Briefly mentioning the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would make the text more accessible to uninformed readers.

The paper discusses the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters, but this approach is not taken. I suggest removing some of this discussion, as it does not contribute to the rest of the paper.

Overall, I believe the paper has potential, but the method description and empirical evaluation need improvement. If accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to revise the write-up to address these concerns.
