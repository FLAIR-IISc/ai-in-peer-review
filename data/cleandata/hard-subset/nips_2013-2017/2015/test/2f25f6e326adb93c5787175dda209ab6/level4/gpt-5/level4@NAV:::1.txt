Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have recently garnered substantial attention in the machine learning community because they naturally capture repulsion among points in a set. This repulsion is encoded via a positive definite kernel.

A key challenge is learning the kernel parameters from observed point configurations. This is difficult due to the intractable normalization constant, which effectively sums over all possible cardinalities and configurations.

A recent method [7] addresses this by estimating the spectrum and using it to construct incremental upper and lower bounds, leading to a provably correct retrospective MCMC procedure for the kernel parameters.

This paper introduces an alternative class of bounds that also support MCMC inference but do not require eigenvalue estimation. The approach further enables a variational learning framework and draws connections to inducing-point methods for scalable Gaussian process inference.

This is a technically solid and well-written paper. I was unfamiliar with the inequality underpinning the lower bound in Proposition 1, and it appears to be an excellent match to this setting. It is a neat way to sidestep the estimation issues in [7]. I appreciate the links to GP inducing points, as well as the ability to perform variational inference. Overall, the paper was a pleasure to read.

I have two technical concerns I would like to see addressed. First, while I like the approach, I am not convinced that replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension represents a substantial computational gain. Second, it is not clear that simply increasing the cardinality guarantees convergence of the bounds given the coupled optimization; I suspect the optimization landscape is highly non-convex, implying one must both grow the cardinality and ensure attainment of a global minimum. This should be discussed directly, as a poor local minimum could prevent pushing the bounds past the Bernoulli threshold and thereby block an MCMC transition.
