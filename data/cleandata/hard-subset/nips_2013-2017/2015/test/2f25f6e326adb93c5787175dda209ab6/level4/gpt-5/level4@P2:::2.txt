Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces non-spectral upper and lower bounds on determinants that appear in DPP likelihoods, for both finite and continuous domains. The key idea is to approximate the kernel L with a low-rank object built from “pseudo-inputs” Z: Q = LYZ L−1Z LZY, and then use a determinant inequality to bound det(L + I) (finite case) and the Fredholm determinant det(I + L) (continuous case). These bounds are cheap to evaluate and avoid any eigendecomposition.

The bounds lead to two inference strategies: (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood that can be optimized jointly over kernel parameters and pseudo-inputs, with O(n m^2) cost; and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that tightens acceptance bounds by increasing m and optimizing Z. The continuous-domain extension relies on computable integrals Ψij = ∫ L(zi, x)L(x, zj)dµ(x), which are available in common Gaussian–Gaussian settings.

This work is closely related to inducing-variable sparse GP methods (Titsias, 2009) and adapts the determinant inequality of Seiler & Simon. It contrasts with prior DPP learning approaches that rely on spectra or Nyström approximations (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML/AISTATS; Gillenwater et al., NIPS; Mariet & Sra, NIPS) by removing the need to compute large eigendecompositions. The retrospective MH construction mirrors earlier spectral-bounding samplers for DPPs, adapted here to the non-spectral setting. The connection to Bornemann’s quadrature for Fredholm determinants is noted as future work.

Empirically, the paper validates the approach on: (a) a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where the true spectrum and Fredholm determinant are known, showing close agreement between the retrospective MH sampler and an “ideal” MH; and (b) a diabetic neuropathy dataset in 2D, where the variational method recovers stable overdispersion measures as m increases and separates classes in line with prior analyses.

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple, general, and well-motivated approach that removes spectral bottlenecks.
- Applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs; the continuous case is a notable contribution.
- Practical algorithms: a scalable variational bound and an exact (but more costly) retrospective MH sampler.
- Clear empirical demonstrations, including a candid example where the variational method struggles (parameter identifiability), and convergence of bounds with increasing m.
- Pseudo-input optimization is effective and interpretable; the observed layouts provide intuition.

Weaknesses:
- The discussion of parameterizing K versus L is prominent, but the paper does not offer methods for K-parameterization or links between the two without spectral knowledge.
- The continuous-case applicability hinges on computable Ψ; outside Gaussian settings, this may limit generality or require numerical integration.
- Limited large-scale comparisons to spectral or Nyström-based baselines in the finite case (e.g., runtime/accuracy trade-offs versus partial eigensolvers) and limited exploration of high-dimensional continuous domains.
- No theoretical guidance on choosing m or characterizing bound tightness as a function of m and Z; practical heuristics are used.
- Variational optimization details (initialization, gradients vs black-box, sensitivity to Z) and stability are only briefly discussed.

Quality:
- Theoretical claims are grounded in known determinant inequalities; proofs are deferred to the supplement. Algorithms are sensible. Experiments support the main claims, though broader benchmarks would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and organized. Some sections overemphasize K vs L without actionable outcomes. Minor clarifications (see below) could further improve readability.

Originality:
- Adapting inducing-point-style bounds to DPP determinants and extending them to Fredholm determinants is novel. The retrospective MH adaptation is a natural but useful extension.

Significance:
- The method can broaden DPP inference to settings where spectra are unavailable or expensive, especially on continuous domains. The ideas may be useful for bounding determinants in other probabilistic models.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
- Clarify the computational requirements for Ψ in the continuous case and discuss strategies when closed forms are not available (e.g., quadrature, random features), including the impact on complexity and accuracy.
- Provide guidance on choosing m and initializing/optimizing Z (gradient-based vs CMA-ES, sensitivity, convergence diagnostics). Include ablations on m, Z initialization, and runtime–accuracy trade-offs.
- Add head-to-head comparisons with spectral/partial-eigenvalue methods where feasible (finite case), including memory and wall-clock.
- Tightness: discuss empirical monotonicity of the bounds with m and any known conditions guaranteeing convergence.
- K vs L: either streamline the discussion or add a concrete pathway (e.g., bounds that operate on K directly via K = L(I + L)−1, or approximate mappings) to make the emphasis actionable.
- Supplement: please provide a standard supplementary document with full proofs, algorithmic details, derivations of Ψ for common kernels/measures, and pseudo-code; avoid only linking to an extended version.

Pros for acceptance:
- Removes a major practical barrier (spectral computations) for DPP inference.
- Solid, general methodology with both variational and MCMC instantiations.
- Extends naturally to continuous domains with demonstrable utility.
- Clear experiments that validate the approach and highlight limitations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is relatively narrow and lacks strong baselines on large finite problems.
- Practical guidance on m and Z is heuristic; no bound-tightness theory beyond asymptotics.
- Heavy emphasis on K vs L without delivering tools for K-parameterization.
- Continuous-case applicability depends on computable Ψ, which may restrict general use.

Minor comments:
- Fix minor typos and notation consistency (e.g., indices and set notation).
- When discussing identifiability issues for L-parameterization, suggest remedies (priors, reparameterizations, fixing scale via κ, or learning in γd).
- Include code or sufficient detail to reproduce experiments, especially the retrospective MH refinement schedule.
