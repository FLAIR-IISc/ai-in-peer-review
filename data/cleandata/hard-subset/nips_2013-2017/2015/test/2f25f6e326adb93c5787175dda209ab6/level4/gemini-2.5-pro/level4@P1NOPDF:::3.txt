This paper presents approximate inference techniques for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) that do not require knowledge of the kernel's eigenspectrum. The proposed approach leverages inducing inputs to construct a bound on the likelihood, thereby reducing the computational cost of inference. The authors develop and empirically demonstrate both a variational inference (VI) and an approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method.

While the core idea of this work has merit, I found the description of the methodology to be unclear in places and the empirical evaluation not sufficiently comprehensive. If accepted, I would strongly encourage the authors to improve the write-up. The following points are intended to help clarify the work and strengthen its contributions.

1.  A direct comparison of the computational complexity and empirical runtime for the two proposed methods (VI and MCMC) is missing. This analysis is crucial for understanding the trade-offs between them.

2.  The evaluation would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison to other alternative methods, including both exact and approximate DPP inference techniques. This comparison should assess both the quality of the approximation and the computational time.

3.  The empirical results in Section 5.1 show that the VI approach leads to large parameter estimation errors on the toy dataset, prompting the authors to use the approximate MCMC method instead. This apparent failure of the VI approach warrants a more detailed discussion. Under what conditions is the proposed VI method expected to perform well, and when is it expected to fail? A theoretical or practical discussion of the VI method's limitations would be very valuable.

4.  For the toy example, a 13-point sample is generated. A potentially insightful experiment would be to use these exact 13 sampled points as the inducing inputs. This could provide a useful baseline for evaluating the quality of the approximation.

5.  The paper would be more practical if it offered guidance on selecting the inducing points. What rules of thumb or principled methods can be used to determine a) the number of inducing points required, and b) their initial placement for a given problem?

**Minor Comments:**

*   In the introduction, the matrices K and L are mentioned without being defined. For readers not deeply familiar with DPPs, a brief explanation of the marginal kernel (K) and L-ensembles (L) would improve readability.
*   The paper frequently discusses the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters, yet this is not the approach ultimately taken. This discussion feels tangential and could be condensed to improve the paper's focus on the proposed methods.
