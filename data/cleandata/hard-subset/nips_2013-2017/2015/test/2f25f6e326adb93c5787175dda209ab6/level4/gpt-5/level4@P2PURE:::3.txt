The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that do not require spectral knowledge of the DPP kernel. Instead, inducing inputs are used to bound the likelihood, reducing computational cost. The authors describe both a variational inference method and an approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) scheme, and demonstrate their efficiency empirically.

I believe the paper has merit, but the methodological presentation is rough and the empirical evaluation is not sufficiently thorough. Clarifications that would strengthen the work include:
1) How do the computational complexities and wall-clock times of the two proposed methods compare?
2) How do the proposed methods compare with other alternatives—both approximate and exact—in terms of approximation quality and computation time?
3) In the toy data experiment in Section 5.1, variational inference yields large parameter-estimation errors, leading the authors to rely on approximate MCMC instead. In which settings is the proposed variational inference expected to succeed or fail, and why?
4) The toy example uses a 13-point sample. How do the methods perform when the inducing inputs are chosen to be exactly these sampled points?
5) Is there a rule of thumb for selecting (a) the number of inducing points and (b) the initial placement of inducing points?

Minor comments:
- The matrices K and L are referenced throughout the introduction without definition. Briefly introducing the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would help readers unfamiliar with DPPs.
- The paper repeatedly discusses the advantages of directly learning the marginal-kernel parameters, but this is not the approach actually taken. Consider trimming this discussion since it does not contribute to the main development.
- If accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to refine the exposition and improve the write-up.
