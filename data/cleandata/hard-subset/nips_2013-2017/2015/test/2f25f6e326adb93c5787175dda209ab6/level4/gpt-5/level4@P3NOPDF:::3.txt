Summary
The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid eigendecompositions of the kernel. The key idea is to introduce inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood, reducing computational cost. Two inference approaches are developed: a variational method based on the bound and an approximate MCMC scheme. The authors present empirical demonstrations of efficiency.

Strengths
- Tackles a well-known computational bottleneck in DPPs by removing the need for spectral information.
- The inducing-input construction is appealing and potentially broadly applicable.
- Presenting both VI and MCMC variants is valuable, giving practitioners options.

Main concerns
- The method descriptions are terse in places, making it hard to reproduce or fully assess the algorithms and their assumptions.
- The empirical evaluation is limited. It does not clearly position the proposed approaches against strong exact or approximate baselines in terms of accuracy vs. cost.
- On the toy example, the variational estimator shows large parameter error, and the paper defaults to approximate MCMC. This raises questions about the practical scope of the VI method and guidance on when to use which approach.

Requests for clarification
1) Computational complexity and runtime:
- Please provide explicit per-iteration and overall complexity for both methods in terms of n (data size), m (inducing points), and key kernel operations. For example, what are the leading terms (e.g., O(n m^2), O(m^3)) for computing the bound, its gradients, and the MCMC acceptance steps?
- Include wall-clock times and memory usage as n and m vary, ideally with fixed hardware, and report the number of iterations required to reach a target accuracy.

2) Comparisons to alternatives:
- Compare against exact DPP inference (via eigendecomposition) on small to medium problems to quantify approximation quality.
- Include strong approximate baselines, e.g., low-rank/Nyström approximations, spectral truncation, and other scalable DPP methods where applicable.
- Report metrics such as held-out log-likelihood, kernel reconstruction error, parameter estimation error (when ground truth is known), and sample diversity metrics, in addition to runtime.

3) Variational inference behavior:
- On the toy data (Section 5.1), VI exhibits large parameter error. Please characterize when the VI approach is expected to succeed or fail. For example:
  - Dependence on the conditioning of the kernel and on the mismatch between inducing and data locations.
  - Sensitivity to the number and placement of inducing points, initialization, and optimization hyperparameters (learning rates, natural gradients vs. standard gradients).
  - Whether the bound can become too loose in regimes with strong repulsion or when the effective rank is high.
- Provide diagnostics: bound tightness as a function of m, convergence curves, and ablations on initialization.

4) Using sampled points as inducing inputs:
- For the toy example with a 13-point sample, please evaluate performance when the inducing set is exactly these sampled points and when it is a superset/subset. This would illuminate the effect of colocating inducing points with observed items.

5) Heuristics for inducing points:
- Offer practical guidance for selecting m and initializing inducing points. For example:
  - Rules of thumb relating m to the kernel length scales, intrinsic dimensionality, or effective rank.
  - Initialization via k-means, farthest-point sampling, ridge leverage scores, or DPP sampling over candidate locations.
  - Sensitivity analysis showing how performance degrades when m is too small and how quickly it plateaus as m increases.

Methodological details to clarify
- Precisely define the likelihood bound: its direction (upper/lower), conditions for validity, and whether it is monotonic in m. If possible, provide theoretical guarantees on approximation error or consistency as m → n.
- For VI:
  - Are the gradients of the bound exact or estimated? If stochastic estimates are used, are they unbiased and do you employ variance reduction (e.g., reparameterization, control variates)?
  - Any constraints or regularization to prevent pathological solutions when the bound is loose?
- For MCMC:
  - Describe the proposal, acceptance ratio computation using the bound, and any corrections for bias introduced by approximation (e.g., delayed-acceptance, pseudo-marginal schemes).
  - Provide mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, ESS) and sensitivity to step sizes or temperature.
- Discuss identifiability and whether different parameterizations of L or K yield similar likelihoods under the approximation.

Empirical evaluation suggestions
- Expand experiments to include:
  - Synthetic datasets with known ground truth across varying ranks and repulsion strengths.
  - Real datasets used in prior DPP work, with standardized splits and metrics.
- Provide comprehensive plots of accuracy vs. runtime for multiple m values and multiple initializations.
- Include an ablation on inducing-point selection strategies.
- Report confidence intervals over multiple runs.

Presentation and minor comments
- Early in the paper, define K (marginal kernel) and L (L-ensemble) and briefly recall their relationship and the DPP likelihood; this will help readers unfamiliar with the notation.
- The paper discusses advantages of directly learning marginal-kernel parameters, but this is not the approach taken here. Consider trimming or refocusing that discussion to avoid distracting from the main contribution.
- Improve the write-up in the methods section with a clear algorithmic summary for both VI and MCMC, explicit computational costs, and implementation details sufficient for reproduction (e.g., pseudocode, hyperparameters).
- Proofread for repetition; the introduction and conclusion currently restate some points verbatim.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important scalability challenge in DPP inference and proposes an interesting inducing-point framework with both VI and MCMC instantiations. However, the current presentation lacks sufficient methodological clarity and comparative empirical evaluation to fully assess the contribution. With stronger analyses of complexity, tighter benchmarking against exact and approximate baselines, guidance on inducing-point selection, and a clearer account of when VI works vs. fails, this work could be a solid contribution. If accepted, I strongly encourage substantial revisions to improve clarity and empirical rigor.
