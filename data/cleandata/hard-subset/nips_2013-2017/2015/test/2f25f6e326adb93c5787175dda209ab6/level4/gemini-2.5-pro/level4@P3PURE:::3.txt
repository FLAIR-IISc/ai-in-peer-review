This paper introduces approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The core idea is to use inducing inputs to form a bound on the likelihood, thereby reducing computational complexity. The authors present two methods based on this approach: one using variational inference (VI) and another using approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC), and demonstrate their performance empirically.

This paper addresses an important problem, and the proposed approach is promising. However, I found the manuscript in its current form to be rough, with a method description that lacks clarity and an empirical evaluation that is not sufficiently thorough. If the paper is accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to improve the write-up.

My main concerns are outlined below:

1.  **Comparison of Proposed Methods:** A direct comparison between the two proposed methods (VI and approximate MCMC) is missing. The authors should provide a clear analysis of their respective computational complexities and a direct empirical comparison of their runtimes on the same tasks.

2.  **Comparison to Alternatives:** The experiments lack comparisons to relevant alternative methods. To properly contextualize the contribution, the proposed techniques should be benchmarked against other approximate and/or exact DPP inference methods in terms of both approximation quality and computational cost.

3.  **Limitations of Variational Inference:** The empirical results in Section 5.1 demonstrate that the VI approach yields large parameter estimation errors, leading the authors to use the MCMC method instead. This raises a critical question: under what conditions is the proposed VI method expected to perform well, and when is it likely to fail? The paper would be much stronger with a discussion of the theoretical or practical limitations of the VI approximation and the reasons for its failure in this specific setting.

4.  **Inducing Points as Sampled Points:** In the toy example, a 13-point sample is generated from the DPP. A valuable diagnostic experiment would be to set the inducing points to be these exact 13 sampled points. How does the model behave in this scenario? This could provide insight into the tightness of the bound and the behavior of the approximation.

5.  **Guidance on Inducing Points:** The choice of inducing points (both their number and initial placement) appears to be a critical hyperparameter. The paper would benefit from practical guidance or a rule of thumb for these selections. Is there a principled way to choose the number of inducing points, and how sensitive is the performance to their initial placement?

**Minor Comments:**

*   The introduction refers to matrices K and L without defining them. For readers not deeply familiar with DPPs, a brief explanation of the marginal kernel (K) and L-ensembles (L) would significantly improve readability.
*   The paper repeatedly discusses the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters, but this is not the approach ultimately taken by the proposed methods. This discussion could be streamlined or reduced, as it may confuse the reader about the paper's actual contribution.
