Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have garnered significant attention in the machine learning literature due to their ability to model repulsion within realizations of a set of points. This repulsion is specified via a positive definite kernel function. One challenge, however, is learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel, given example point realizations. This estimation is difficult due to a difficult-to-compute normalization constant that, in effect, sums over all possible cardinalities and point configurations.

A recent proposal [7] has provided a way to address this challenge by estimating the spectrum and using it to provide incremental upper and lower bounds, resulting in a provably-correct scheme for "retrospective" Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) of the kernel parameters. The present paper proposes a different type of bound that can also be used for MCMC inference, but that does not require estimation of the eigenvalues. This approach also lends itself to a variational approach to learning, and the paper draws connections to inducing-point methods for scalable inference in Gaussian process models.

This is a technically strong and well-written paper. The inequality that provides the lower bound in Proposition 1 is an excellent fit for this problem, and it is a clever way to avoid the estimation issues of [7]. The connections to GP inducing points, as well as the ability to now perform variational inference, are particularly noteworthy. Overall, this paper was a pleasure to read.

However, I have two technical concerns that I would like to see addressed. Firstly, although I appreciate the approach, I am not convinced that it is a significant computational win to replace power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension. Secondly, it is not obvious to me that simply increasing the cardinality is guaranteed to result in convergence of the bounds, due to the coupled optimization problem. I believe this optimization is highly non-convex, and therefore, one needs to increase the cardinality while ensuring that a global minimum is achieved. This is a crucial point that should be addressed directly, as a local minimum could prevent the procedure from being able to bound away the Bernoulli threshold and make it impossible to take an MCMC step.

Regarding presentation issues, I have a few suggestions. On page 7, Figure 1 would benefit from axis labels for clarity. Additionally, I would have liked to see the proof of the proposition in the main body of the paper, rather than in the appendix, as it is the central insight that makes this paper possible. Finally, on page 7, line 361, the phrase "the variational lower is" could be rephrased for better clarity.

In conclusion, this is a nice paper that uses a clever trick to bound the partition function of a determinantal point process, leading to potentially faster inference and connections to other kinds of models. With some revisions to address the technical concerns and presentation issues, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
