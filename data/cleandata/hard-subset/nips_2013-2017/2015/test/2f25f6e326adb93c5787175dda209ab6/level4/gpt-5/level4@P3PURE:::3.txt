This paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid requiring spectral information about the kernel. Instead, the authors introduce inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood, which reduces computational cost. They describe both a variational inference (VI) approach and an approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method, and present empirical results demonstrating efficiency.

Overall, the paper has merit, but the method description is rough and the empirical evaluation is not sufficiently thorough. The following clarifications would strengthen the work:

- How do the computational complexity and wall-clock time of the two proposed methods compare, both theoretically and empirically?
- How do the proposed methods compare to existing alternatives—both approximate and exact—in terms of approximation quality and computation time?
- In Section 5.1 (toy data), parameter estimation with VI exhibits large error, and the approach falls back to approximate MCMC. In what settings is the proposed VI expected to succeed, and when is it expected to fail? Why?
- For the toy example, a 13-point sample is generated. How do the methods perform when the inducing inputs are chosen to be exactly these sampled points?
- Is there a practical rule of thumb for selecting (a) the number of inducing points and (b) the initialization/placement of inducing points?

Minor comments:
- The matrices K and L are referenced early in the introduction without definition. Briefly recalling the marginal kernel and L-ensemble formulations would help readers unfamiliar with DPP background.
- There is substantial discussion of the advantages of directly learning marginal kernel parameters, yet this is not the approach taken in the paper. Consider trimming this discussion, as it does not feed into the presented methods or experiments.
- If accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to improve the write-up for clarity and completeness.
