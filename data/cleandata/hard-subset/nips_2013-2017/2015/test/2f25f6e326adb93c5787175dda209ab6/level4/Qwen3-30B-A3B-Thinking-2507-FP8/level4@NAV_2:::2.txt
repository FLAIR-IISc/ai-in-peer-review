

The paper introduces novel nonspectral bounds on determinants arising in determinantal point process (DPP) likelihoods, addressing a key computational bottleneck in parametric DPP inference. The authors extend variational and MCMC inference frameworks for DPPs by leveraging pseudo-inputs (inducing variables) to avoid spectral decompositions, which were required in prior work like Affandi et al. (ICML 2013) and Gillenwater et al. (NIPS 2012). This approach directly builds upon Titsias' (AISTATS 2009) variational GP bounds while adapting them to DPPs, making it highly relevant to recent NIPS contributions on scalable inference (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar, NIPS 2011; Affandi et al., NIPS 2013).

**Strengths:**  
- *Originality & Significance:* The nonspectral bounds (Propositions 1–2) are a significant technical advance over spectral approximation methods (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2013), enabling scalable DPP inference without eigendecomposition. The work clearly differentiates from prior NIPS papers by avoiding spectral bottlenecks while maintaining theoretical rigor.  
- *Quality:* The proofs are sound (supplementary material), and experiments (Sections 5.1–5.2) convincingly demonstrate the approach’s efficacy: retrospective MCMC yields consistent posterior estimates (Fig. 1), and variational inference converges to meaningful overdispersion measures (Fig. 3). The diabetic neuropathy dataset application (Section 5.2) validates practical utility.  
- *Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-structured, with clear progression from finite to continuous domains (Section 2 → 4). Key contributions (e.g., pseudo-inputs as optimization variables) are motivated effectively.  
- *Pro:* Avoids the "interpretability vs. tractability" pitfall (Section 1) by directly parametrizing L (standard in ML literature), and the bounds’ scalability (O(nm²) vs. O(n³) for spectral methods) is thoroughly validated.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- *Minor Clarity Issue:* The discussion of parameterizing K instead of L (Section 1) is slightly overemphasized relative to the paper’s focus on L-based inference. While the trade-off is acknowledged, the abstract and introduction could more concisely clarify that the authors *accept* this interpretability cost as a necessary step for tractability (consistent with ML conventions, e.g., [3,7]).  
- *Limited Scope:* The continuous-domain extension (Section 4) relies on Mercer’s theorem assumptions; the paper could briefly discuss limitations for non-Mercer kernels (e.g., heavy-tailed L).  

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Groundbreaking bounds that eliminate spectral bottlenecks; strong theoretical foundation; compelling experiments across synthetic and real datasets; directly applicable to high-impact DPP applications (e.g., search engines [7], spatial modeling [4]).  
*Con:* The K/L parameterization discussion, while accurate, slightly overshadows the main contribution.  

**Verdict:** This is a high-quality, original contribution with clear significance for scalable probabilistic modeling. The technical execution is rigorous, and the experimental validation is thorough. The minor clarity issue does not undermine the paper’s core advances. I recommend *acceptance*.
