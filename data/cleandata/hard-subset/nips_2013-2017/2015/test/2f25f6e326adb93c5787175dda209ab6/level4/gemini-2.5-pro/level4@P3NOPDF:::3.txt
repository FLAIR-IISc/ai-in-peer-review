This paper introduces novel approximate inference methods for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) that circumvent the need for spectral decomposition of the DPP kernel. The core idea is to use a set of inducing inputs to construct a lower bound on the likelihood, which significantly reduces the computational complexity. The authors then develop two inference algorithms based on this bound: one using variational inference (VI) and another based on approximate Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). The effectiveness of these methods is demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world data.

While the core idea of using inducing inputs for scalable DPP inference is promising, I found the description of the methods to be underdeveloped and the empirical evaluation insufficient to fully assess the contribution. The paper has merit, but requires substantial revision to clarify its claims and validate its performance.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison of Proposed Methods:** The paper presents two distinct algorithms (variational inference and MCMC) but fails to provide a clear comparison between them. For a practitioner to adopt these methods, it is crucial to understand the trade-offs. The authors should include a detailed analysis of their respective computational complexities, both theoretically and in terms of empirical wall-clock time on the tested datasets.

2.  **Lack of Comprehensive Benchmarking:** The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently thorough. The proposed methods should be benchmarked against a wider range of existing alternatives. This comparison should include:
    *   **Exact Methods:** On datasets where it is feasible, comparison against exact DPP inference is essential to quantify the approximation error introduced by the inducing point framework.
    *   **Other Approximate Methods:** How do the proposed VI and MCMC approaches compare to other approximate DPP inference techniques in the literature? A proper comparison should be made on both the quality of the approximation and the computational cost.

3.  **Unexplained Failure of Variational Inference:** The paper needs a more in-depth discussion on the limitations of the variational inference approach. The toy data experiment in Section 5.1 shows that VI yields a large parameter estimation error, forcing a switch to the MCMC method. This is a critical finding that requires further investigation. The authors should provide a clear explanation for why the VI approximation fails in this setting and characterize the conditions under which it is expected to perform well or poorly. Without this analysis, the utility of the VI method is unclear.

4.  **Guidance on Inducing Inputs:** The selection of inducing inputs is central to the proposed method, yet the paper provides little to no guidance on this critical aspect. The following questions need to be addressed:
    *   **Selection and Placement:** Is there a rule of thumb or a principled method for selecting the number of inducing points (M) and their initial placement? How sensitive are the results to these choices?
    *   **Ablation Study:** For the toy example, a 13-point sample is generated. An interesting and informative experiment would be to set the inducing inputs to be these exact 13 sampled points. How does the method perform in this scenario? This could provide valuable insight into the behavior of the likelihood bound.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity in Introduction:** The matrices K and L are referred to throughout the introduction without being defined. Briefly introducing the concepts of the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would significantly improve readability for an audience not already expert in DPPs.
*   **Focus of Discussion:** The paper contains several discussions on the advantages of directly learning marginal kernel parameters. Since this is not the approach ultimately taken, these sections detract from the main contribution and could be condensed or removed to improve the paper's focus and flow.

In summary, this paper presents a promising and well-motivated approach for scalable DPP inference. However, the current manuscript suffers from a lack of clarity in its methodological description and an insufficiently rigorous empirical evaluation. If the major concerns regarding experimental comparison, analysis of the VI method's limitations, and guidance on inducing points are addressed, I believe the paper could be a valuable contribution. I strongly encourage the authors to revise the write-up to improve its clarity and strengthen the experimental section.
