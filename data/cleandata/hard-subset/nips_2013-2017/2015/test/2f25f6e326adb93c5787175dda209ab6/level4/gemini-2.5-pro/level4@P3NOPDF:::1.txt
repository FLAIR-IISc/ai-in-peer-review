This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel parameters of a determinantal point process (DPP) from observed data. DPPs are a powerful class of models for capturing repulsion in point patterns, but inference is hampered by an intractable partition function that involves a sum over all possible point configurations and cardinalities.

Recent work [7] introduced a retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) scheme for parameter learning by using eigenvalue estimates to construct incremental upper and lower bounds on the likelihood. The present paper introduces a novel alternative. Instead of relying on eigenvalue estimation, the authors derive a new type of bound on the partition function. This bound facilitates a similar exact MCMC inference scheme but avoids the need for spectral estimation. Furthermore, the proposed framework is elegantly extended to develop a variational inference method for learning DPP parameters, drawing insightful connections to inducing-point methods for scalable Gaussian Processes.

**Strengths and Significance:**

This is a technically strong and exceptionally well-written paper that was a pleasure to review. The primary contributions are significant and well-motivated.

1.  **Novel Bounding Technique:** The paper's technical core rests on a powerful inequality (Proposition 1) that provides a tight lower bound on the partition function. This appears to be an excellent and novel application of this mathematical result to the problem of DPP inference. This approach cleverly sidesteps the computational and estimation challenges associated with the spectral methods used in [7].

2.  **New Inference Modalities:** A key strength is the extension of this bounding technique to enable not only MCMC but also variational inference. The development of a variational learning scheme is a particularly valuable contribution, as it expands the applicability of DPPs to larger-scale problems where MCMC may be too slow.

3.  **Insightful Connections:** The connections drawn to Gaussian process inducing-point methods are highly insightful and open up promising avenues for cross-pollination between these fields. This framing helps to situate the work within a broader context of scalable kernel methods.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

While I am very enthusiastic about this work, I have two technical concerns that I would like the authors to address.

1.  **Computational Trade-offs:** The authors argue that replacing the power iterations of [7] with a sequence of nonlinear optimization problems represents a significant computational advantage. However, it is not immediately obvious that this trade-off is universally beneficial, as solving nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension can be demanding. A more detailed discussion or empirical analysis comparing the practical runtime complexities of the two approaches would substantially strengthen this claim.

2.  **Convergence and Non-Convexity:** My second concern relates to the convergence of the bounds within the MCMC sampler. The proposed scheme relies on incrementally increasing the cardinality of the inducing set to tighten the bounds until a sampling decision can be made. However, this is coupled with a highly non-convex optimization problem to find the optimal inducing points. It is plausible that the optimization could converge to a local minimum, which would prevent the bounds from tightening sufficiently. This could stall the MCMC sampler, making it impossible to bound the Bernoulli acceptance threshold away from the current value. The paper would be improved by directly addressing this potential issue, perhaps by discussing initialization strategies or providing theoretical or empirical evidence that this is not a practical concern.

**Presentation Issues:**

-   Page 7, Figure 1: Please add axis labels to all plots for clarity.
-   The proof of Proposition 1 is the central technical insight of the paper. While I understand the space constraints, its placement in the appendix somewhat diminishes its visibility. I would encourage the authors to consider moving at least a proof sketch or the key steps into the main body of the paper.
-   Page 7, Line 361: There is a typo, "...the variational lower is..." should be corrected.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a clever and effective method for bounding the DPP partition function. This innovation facilitates both MCMC and variational inference, broadens the connection between DPPs and other kernel methods, and represents a significant advance in the field. I recommend acceptance, provided the authors can satisfactorily address the technical questions raised above.
