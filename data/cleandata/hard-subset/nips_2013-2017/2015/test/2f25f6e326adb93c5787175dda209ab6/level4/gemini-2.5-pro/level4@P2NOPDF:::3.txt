This paper introduces two approximate inference methods for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): one based on variational inference (VI) and another on Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). The central contribution is the use of inducing inputs to construct a lower bound on the DPP likelihood. This approach circumvents the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix, thereby reducing the computational complexity that typically limits the scalability of DPPs.

The paper addresses an important problem, and the core idea of using inducing inputs for approximate DPP inference is promising. However, the current manuscript suffers from a lack of clarity in the methodological description and a limited empirical evaluation. These weaknesses prevent a full appreciation of the proposed methods' contributions and limitations. For the paper to be compelling, significant improvements are needed in both the exposition and the experimental validation.

My main concerns are detailed below:

1.  **Comparison of Proposed Methods:** A direct comparison between the two proposed methods (variational inference and MCMC) is missing. The paper would be substantially strengthened by a detailed analysis of their respective computational complexities and a direct empirical comparison of their wall-clock times on the same tasks. This would help readers understand the trade-offs between the two approaches.

2.  **Comparison to Existing Alternatives:** The experimental evaluation lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing baselines. It is crucial to benchmark the proposed VI and MCMC approaches against other state-of-the-art approximate inference techniques for DPPs, as well as exact methods on smaller datasets where they are tractable. Comparisons should evaluate both the quality of the approximation (e.g., in terms of log-likelihood, parameter estimation error, or sample quality) and the computational runtime.

3.  **Analysis of VI Failure Mode:** The results on the toy dataset (Section 5.1) reveal a significant weakness in the variational inference approach for parameter estimation, which motivates the authors to use MCMC instead. This finding requires a more thorough investigation. The authors should provide a deeper analysis of *why* the VI method fails in this setting. What properties of the data, model, or variational approximation lead to this poor performance? Conversely, what are the conditions under which the proposed VI method is expected to succeed? A clear characterization of the limitations and failure modes of the VI approach is essential for understanding its practical utility.

4.  **Sensitivity to Inducing Point Placement:** Regarding the toy example, it would be insightful to understand the model's behavior in a specific, well-defined scenario. For instance, what is the performance if the set of inducing points is chosen to be the set of 13 sampled data points themselves? This experiment could shed light on the sensitivity of the method to the choice and placement of inducing inputs.

5.  **Guidance on Selecting Inducing Points:** The choice of inducing points—both their number and initial locations—appears to be a critical hyperparameter for the model. The paper would be more impactful if it provided practical guidance or heuristics for setting these parameters. Is there a principled way to select the number of inducing points, perhaps based on dataset characteristics? How sensitive is the performance to their initial placement? An ablation study exploring these questions would be highly valuable for future users of this method.

Minor Comments:

*   The introduction refers to matrices `K` and `L` without defining them. To make the paper more accessible to readers not deeply familiar with DPPs, it would be helpful to briefly introduce these as the marginal kernel and the L-ensemble kernel, respectively, upon their first mention.
*   The paper includes several discussions on the benefits of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters. Since this is not the approach ultimately taken, this discussion feels extraneous and could be condensed or removed to improve the paper's focus and clarity.

In summary, the paper presents a promising direction for scalable DPP inference. However, the execution in its current form is not fully convincing due to an unclear methodological description and an insufficient empirical evaluation. If the authors can thoroughly address the concerns raised above, particularly by providing a more comprehensive experimental validation and a clearer analysis of their methods, the paper's contribution would be significantly strengthened.
