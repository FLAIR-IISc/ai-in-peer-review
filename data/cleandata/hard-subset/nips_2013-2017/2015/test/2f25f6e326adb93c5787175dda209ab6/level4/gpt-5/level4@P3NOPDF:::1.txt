This paper develops new bounds for the normalization constant of determinantal point processes (DPPs) that enable both MCMC-based inference and a variational learning approach without requiring eigenvalue estimation. The key idea is a lower bound based on a nontrivial inequality (Proposition 1), paired with an upper bound, yielding a practical alternative to retrospective MCMC schemes that rely on spectral approximations [7]. The approach also reveals tight conceptual links to inducing-point methods for scalable Gaussian processes (GPs), thereby unifying ideas across these areas.

Strengths
- Technical novelty and fit: The lower bound in Proposition 1 is elegant and appears to be an excellent match for the DPP partition function, providing a clean way to avoid the spectrum-estimation machinery of [7]. I was not previously aware of this inequality; using it here is both clever and consequential.
- Broader impact: The resulting framework supports variational inference for DPPs and clarifies connections to GP inducing-point schemes. This is a valuable bridge for scaling and for transferring intuition and tools between DPPs and GPs.
- Clarity: The paper is well written overall and was a pleasure to read. The narrative is coherent, and the methodological contributions are clearly motivated.

Main concerns and questions
1) Computational trade-offs versus spectral methods:
- The paper argues that avoiding power iterations is advantageous, but it is not yet compelling that replacing them with a sequence of nonlinear optimization problems (whose dimensionality increases with the number of inducing points) yields a clear computational win in practice.
- Please provide quantitative complexity comparisons and wall-clock results against [7] across problem sizes and kernel families. In particular:
  - Per-iteration complexity (and memory) of your bound optimization versus power-iteration-based spectral estimation.
  - Sensitivity to the number of inducing points and how that number scales with dataset size and kernel smoothness.
  - Empirical accuracy of the bounds (tightness) versus runtime for both approaches.
- It would help to include ablations that separate the effect of the bound type from optimization heuristics (e.g., initialization, step sizes, and stopping tolerances).

2) Convergence and reliability of bound tightening:
- The proposed scheme appears to rely on monotonically tightening bounds as the inducing-point set grows. However, because bound construction is coupled with a nonconvex optimization, it is unclear that simply increasing the cardinality guarantees improved bounds in practice.
- Please clarify:
  - Under what conditions does increasing the inducing set yield monotone improvement of the lower/upper bounds if the inner optimization is solved to global optimality?
  - How do you certify or approximate global optimality in practice? Are there convex subproblems, dual gaps, or certificates that can be computed?
  - What is the observed effect of local minima on the Bernoulli-threshold decision in retrospective MCMC? Can a poor local optimum indefinitely prevent acceptance/rejection and stall mixing?
- Practical mitigations would be helpful: warm starts, multiple restarts, trust-region methods, annealing or continuation strategies on kernel hyperparameters, or incremental updates that preserve feasibility while guaranteeing nondecreasing lower bounds.
- If a theoretical guarantee is out of reach, please provide empirical diagnostics (e.g., bound progress versus restarts, variance across seeds) and recommendations for robust settings.

Additional suggestions
- Provide a concise statement comparing the statistical and computational regimes in which your method is preferable to [7]. For example, when kernel spectra decay slowly, when eigenvalue estimation is costly or unstable, or when variational learning is required.
- Discuss how inducing-point selection is performed (greedy, k-DPP, leverage-score-like, or learned jointly) and how this choice impacts both bound tightness and runtime.
- Clarify whether the variational objective is amenable to stochastic mini-batching and, if so, whether the bounds remain valid under subsampling.

Presentation issues
- Figure 1 (p.7): Please add axis labels and units where appropriate.
- Proof placement: The proof of Proposition 1 is central to the paper’s contribution. Consider moving it to the main text (possibly in a condensed form), with extended details in the appendix.
- Typo (p.7, line 361): “the variational lower is …” → “the variational lower bound is …”
- Minor: Briefly summarize the inequality underlying Proposition 1 in the main text to aid readers who may not consult the appendix immediately.

Overall assessment
This is a technically strong and thoughtfully executed paper that introduces a clever, well-suited bound for the DPP partition function. It avoids the spectrum-estimation challenges of prior retrospective MCMC work and opens the door to variational learning while forging useful connections to GP inducing-point methods. My main reservations concern the practical computational benefits relative to [7] and the reliability of bound tightening in the presence of nonconvex inner optimization. If the authors can substantiate the claimed efficiency gains and provide clearer guidance or guarantees regarding convergence and robustness, I would be strongly in favor of acceptance. As it stands, I lean positive and view these clarifications as important to maximize the paper’s impact.
