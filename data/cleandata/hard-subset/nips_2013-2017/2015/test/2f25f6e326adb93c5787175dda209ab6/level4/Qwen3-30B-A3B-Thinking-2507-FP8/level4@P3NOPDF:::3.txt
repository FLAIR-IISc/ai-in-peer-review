

The paper proposes approximate inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) using inducing inputs to bound the likelihood without requiring spectral knowledge of the kernel, thereby reducing computational cost. It introduces variational inference (VI) and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques, demonstrating empirical efficiency. The core contribution—avoiding spectral decomposition for scalable DPP inference—is valuable, and the approach aligns with broader trends in scalable probabilistic modeling. However, the method description requires significant refinement, and the empirical evaluation lacks depth, preventing a clear assessment of the method's strengths and limitations. The following concerns must be addressed for the paper to be suitable for publication.

First, the computational complexity and runtime of the proposed VI and approximate MCMC methods are not compared. The paper claims reduced cost but omits concrete analysis of time complexity (e.g., scaling with inducing point count *m* versus full kernel size *n*), making it impossible to evaluate efficiency claims. A quantitative comparison of wall-clock times for both methods across varying *m* and *n* is essential.

Second, the paper fails to benchmark against key alternatives. How do the proposed methods compare to exact DPP inference (e.g., via spectral decomposition) or established approximate methods (e.g., [cite relevant works]) in terms of approximation quality (e.g., log-likelihood, diversity metrics) and speed? Without such comparisons, the paper cannot establish whether the trade-offs are justified. For instance, if exact methods are feasible for moderate *n*, the value of approximation is unclear.

Third, Section 5.1’s toy example reveals a critical flaw: variational inference yields large parameter estimation errors, forcing reliance on approximate MCMC. The paper does not clarify *when* VI is expected to succeed or fail. Is this due to model misspecification, poor initialization, or inherent limitations of the variational family? Without this analysis, the practical utility of VI remains ambiguous. The authors should provide theoretical or empirical conditions under which VI is viable.

Fourth, the toy example uses 13 sampled points as inducing inputs. How do the methods perform when the inducing points *are* these samples (i.e., the true data points)? This would test if the approach leverages the data structure effectively. The current setup (inducing points chosen independently) may not reflect realistic use cases.

Fifth, no guidelines are provided for selecting the number of inducing points *m* or their initial placement. A rule of thumb (e.g., *m* scaling with *n*^α, or placement via k-means) is crucial for reproducibility. Without it, the method appears ad hoc.

Minor issues require attention. The introduction references matrices *K* and *L* without defining them (e.g., as the marginal kernel and L-ensemble). A brief clarification early on would improve accessibility. Additionally, the paper repeatedly emphasizes "directly learning marginal kernel parameters" but does not adopt this approach, rendering these discussions redundant. These sections should be condensed or removed.

In summary, the paper addresses a relevant problem with a promising direction, but its current form lacks rigor in methodology and evaluation. The proposed techniques are not sufficiently justified against alternatives, and key practical questions (runtime, scalability, failure modes) remain unanswered. The empirical section needs expansion to include comparative benchmarks, sensitivity analyses (e.g., varying *m*), and a clearer narrative on when each method is appropriate. The minor editorial points also detract from clarity.

The work has potential but requires substantial revision. If the authors address these concerns—particularly providing a thorough empirical comparison, clarifying computational trade-offs, and diagnosing VI’s failure modes—the paper could make a meaningful contribution to scalable DPP inference. As is, the current evaluation is insufficient to support the claims of efficiency and applicability. I recommend *rejection* pending major revisions addressing these points.
