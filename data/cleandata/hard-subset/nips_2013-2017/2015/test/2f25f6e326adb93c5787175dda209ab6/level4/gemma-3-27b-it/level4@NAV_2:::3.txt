The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that circumvent the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel. The core idea is to leverage inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood, thereby reducing computational cost. Both variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are presented, and their performance is evaluated empirically.

This work addresses an important challenge in DPP inference – the computational burden of spectral calculations – and offers a promising approach based on readily computable bounds. The proposed methods have the potential to broaden the applicability of DPPs, particularly to larger datasets and continuous domains where exact inference is intractable. The connection to variational inference in Gaussian processes is also well-motivated and provides intuition for the proposed bounding technique.

However, the presentation of the methodology lacks some clarity and the empirical evaluation could be significantly strengthened. Specifically, the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity and runtime of the proposed variational and MCMC methods. It is also crucial to benchmark these approaches against existing DPP inference algorithms, both approximate and exact, with respect to both approximation quality and computational efficiency. 

The empirical results in Section 5.1 reveal substantial parameter estimation error with variational inference, necessitating a switch to MCMC. A deeper discussion is needed regarding the conditions under which the variational approach is expected to succeed or fail, and the underlying reasons for these limitations. It would also be informative to assess the performance of the methods when the inducing inputs are initialized to be the data points themselves, in the context of the toy example. Finally, providing practical guidelines for selecting the number of inducing points and their initial placement would greatly enhance the usability of the proposed techniques.

Minor points: The introduction briefly mentions kernels K and L without sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with DPPs. A concise explanation of the marginal kernel and L-ensembles could improve accessibility. The discussion regarding the advantages of directly learning parameters of the marginal kernel appears somewhat tangential, as the paper ultimately parametrizes L directly. Streamlining this discussion would enhance focus.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
*   Provides novel bounds on the DPP likelihood that avoid spectral decomposition.
*   Offers viable approximate inference methods (variational and MCMC).
*   Demonstrates the feasibility of the approach on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Methodological description is somewhat unclear and lacks sufficient detail.
*   Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks comprehensive benchmarking against existing methods.
*   The conditions for successful application of variational inference are not clearly identified.
*   Practical guidance for parameter selection (number and placement of inducing points) is missing.

Overall, while the paper presents a valuable contribution, addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly improve its clarity, impact, and ultimately, its suitability for publication at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
