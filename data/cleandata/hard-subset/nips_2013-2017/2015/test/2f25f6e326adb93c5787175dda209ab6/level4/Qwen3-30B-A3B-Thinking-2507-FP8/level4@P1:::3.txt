

This paper presents a novel approach to likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoids the computationally expensive spectral decomposition typically required. The authors derive nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood for both finite and continuous domains using inducing inputs, which significantly reduce computational complexity compared to previous methods. The key innovation lies in replacing spectral decomposition with efficient determinant bounds that depend only on a subset of inducing points, enabling practical variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods.

The paper builds on prior work in DPP inference [7, 8, 9] by addressing the computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition. Unlike [7], which relies on approximating eigenvalues through power iteration, the proposed method provides cheap-to-evaluate bounds that do not require spectral knowledge. The approach extends variational sparse GP techniques [11] to DPPs, making it applicable to both finite and continuous domains with minimal additional complexity. The authors demonstrate their method on a toy Gaussian continuous experiment and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset, showing its practical utility.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. The theoretical contribution of deriving nonspectral bounds that avoid spectral decomposition is significant and well-motivated.
2. The extension to continuous domains (Section 4) demonstrates the method's generality beyond previous finite-domain approaches.
3. The experimental validation shows the method works well on real data, with clear separation between classes in the diabetic neuropathy dataset.
4. The connection to variational sparse GP methods provides a familiar framework for the ML community.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing:
1. The empirical evaluation lacks sufficient comparison to alternative methods. The paper does not provide a clear comparison between the proposed bounds and existing spectral-based approaches [7] in terms of both approximation quality and computational efficiency. A table comparing runtime, approximation error, and convergence rates would greatly strengthen the empirical claims.

2. The explanation for why variational inference fails on the toy example (Section 5.1) is insufficient. The authors note that variational inference produces inconsistent results while MCMC works better, but don't provide guidance on when to use which method. A more detailed analysis of the optimization landscape and conditions under which variational inference is likely to succeed would be valuable.

3. The paper does not address an important practical concern: how the number of inducing points affects both accuracy and computational cost. While the authors incrementally increase the number of inducing points in the MCMC method, they don't provide a systematic analysis of the trade-off between computational complexity and approximation quality. A convergence analysis showing how the bounds tighten with increasing m would be helpful.

4. The toy experiment (Section 5.1) uses only 13 points, which is small for the DPP model. It would be valuable to see how the method performs with larger datasets where the computational advantage would be most apparent. The paper also doesn't test whether using the actual data points as inducing inputs would yield better results.

5. The paper contains some confusing statements about kernel parametrization. While the authors acknowledge that parametrizing L instead of K sacrifices interpretability, they don't clearly explain how this choice affects their specific inference methods. This could be clarified to help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

The writing could be improved in several places. For instance, the introduction should briefly define K and L as the kernel and marginal kernel (L-ensemble) to help readers unfamiliar with DPPs. The discussion of interpretability issues in Section 2.1 is somewhat tangential to the main contribution and could be condensed.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to DPP inference by providing computationally efficient bounds that avoid spectral decomposition. The theoretical results are sound, and the experimental validation demonstrates practical applicability. However, the empirical evaluation would benefit from more thorough comparisons to existing methods and deeper analysis of the method's limitations.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily to strengthen the empirical evaluation and provide clearer guidance on when to use variational inference versus MCMC. Specifically, the authors should:
1. Add a comparative analysis of their method against spectral-based approaches [7] in terms of both accuracy and runtime
2. Provide a more detailed explanation of the conditions under which variational inference succeeds or fails
3. Include a convergence analysis showing how bound tightness relates to the number of inducing points
4. Clarify the kernel parametrization discussion to better connect with the main contribution

The paper addresses a meaningful challenge in DPP inference with a technically sound approach that has practical implications for real-world applications. With the suggested revisions, it would make a strong contribution to the NIPS community.
