This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the calculation of the normalizing constant, which involves an intractable determinant of a large matrix (in the finite case) or a Fredholm determinant of an operator (in the continuous case). Previous work often relied on expensive spectral approximations to handle this term.

The central contribution of this work is the derivation of novel, computationally efficient upper and lower bounds on this determinant that do not require any spectral knowledge. The approach is inspired by sparse Gaussian Process methods, using a set of "inducing points" or "pseudo-inputs" to form a low-rank approximation of the kernel matrix. The authors show how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference frameworks: a fast, approximate variational inference (VI) scheme and a more computationally intensive but exact MCMC algorithm using retrospective sampling. The methods are developed for both finite and continuous DPPs and are validated on synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known and important problem in the DPP literature. The proposed non-spectral bounds represent a significant step forward in making DPP inference more scalable and practical. The idea of adapting inducing point methods from the Gaussian Process literature to bound the DPP likelihood is both creative and elegant, opening a new avenue for approximate inference in this model class.

2.  **Strong Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and well-executed. The bounds are rigorously derived, and their application to VI and MCMC is clearly explained. The experimental evaluation is particularly strong and provides compelling evidence for the method's utility. The authors include a toy 1D experiment where the ground truth is known, allowing for a direct comparison that validates their MCMC sampler.

3.  **Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed to highlight key aspects of the method. The authors are commendably transparent about the limitations of the VI approach, showing a case where it struggles with parameter identifiability, thereby motivating the need for the more robust MCMC method. The application to the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrates the method's practical relevance on a real-world problem and shows that the quality of the approximation can be controlled by increasing the number of inducing points, with results converging gracefully.

4.  **Excellent Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It clearly articulates the core problem, motivates the proposed solution, and walks the reader through the technical details for both finite and continuous domains. The connection to sparse GPs provides a helpful anchor for readers familiar with that area.

**Weaknesses (Points for Improvement)**

1.  **Mismatch in Framing vs. Contribution:** The abstract and introduction place significant emphasis on the modeling challenge of parameterizing the `L` kernel versus the more interpretable `K` kernel. This framing creates an expectation that the paper will address this issue. However, the proposed method ultimately works with the standard `L`-parametrization and does not offer a solution to the interpretability problem. While this is a pragmatic choice to focus on the computational aspect, the extensive setup feels somewhat disconnected from the paper's core contribution. It would be clearer if the paper either toned down this discussion or more explicitly stated early on that it focuses solely on the computational bottleneck of the `L`-kernel likelihood.

**Minor Comments & Questions for the Authors**

*   The discussion on the interpretability of the `K` parameterization is interesting and highlights an important challenge for practitioners. As a point of curiosity and for future work, have the authors considered whether their bounding technique could be applied directly to the `K`-based formulation of the DPP? The existence of a DPP is governed by the eigenvalues of `K`, and some formulations involve terms like `det(I-K)`. It seems this might also be amenable to similar bounding strategies. What new challenges would arise in that setting?

*   A minor logistical point: if the paper is accepted, I would recommend formatting the supplementary material as a standalone document rather than an extended version of the main paper for easier navigation.

**Overall Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that makes a novel and valuable contribution to the literature on inference for determinantal point processes. The proposed non-spectral bounds are a significant practical and conceptual advance. The paper is well-written, technically solid, and supported by a thorough and insightful experimental evaluation. Despite a minor issue with the framing of the `K` vs. `L` kernel problem, the core contribution is clear and impactful. I recommend acceptance.
