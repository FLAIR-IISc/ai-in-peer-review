

This paper proposes novel bounds on the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid the computationally expensive spectral decompositions typically required for likelihood-based inference. The authors derive both upper and lower bounds on the determinant terms in the DPP likelihood that can be evaluated without spectral knowledge, making the bounds cheap to compute and applicable to both finite and continuous domains. The main contribution is a clever use of "pseudo-inputs" (or inducing points) to construct these bounds, which can then be directly incorporated into variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods for DPP parameter estimation.

The paper is well-motivated by the challenges of likelihood-based inference for DPPs, which typically requires either spectral decomposition (computationally prohibitive for large datasets) or parametrization of the kernel L rather than K (sacrificing interpretability). The authors correctly note that the machine learning community has largely adopted the L parametrization for computational convenience, and they build upon this established practice. The theoretical development is sound, with the bounds extending from finite to continuous domains in a principled way. The derivation of Proposition 1 and its continuous counterpart (Proposition 2) follows from nontrivial determinant inequalities, and the supplementary material provides sufficient detail for verification.

The paper's strengths are numerous: the bounds are theoretically well-founded, computationally efficient (O(nm²) versus O(n³) for spectral methods), and they enable practical inference methods for DPPs. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-constructed, demonstrating the approach on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The toy Gaussian experiment validates the MCMC approach against exact inference, while the diabetic neuropathy dataset shows the method's applicability to real-world problems. The authors also provide insightful analysis of the pseudo-inputs, noting their tendency to form grids that reflect the marginal distribution of the DPP.

The paper addresses the core technical challenges of DPP inference effectively, and the writing is clear and accessible to readers familiar with Gaussian processes and point processes. The connection to sparse Gaussian process approximations is well-articulated, making the approach familiar to many in the machine learning community. The experimental results convincingly show that the proposed bounds lead to meaningful inference for DPPs, with the variational approach providing fast approximate inference and the MCMC approach delivering exact inference when needed.

A minor weakness is the limited discussion of scalability for extremely large datasets. While the O(nm²) complexity is better than O(n³), the paper doesn't explore how the method scales with very large n (e.g., n > 10⁵) or how the choice of m (number of pseudo-inputs) affects performance in such regimes. The authors could also more explicitly discuss how their approach compares to other approximate DPP inference methods, such as those based on Nyström approximation.

The draft review's claim that "the authors mainly focus on describing the problem, and make no efforts to address [the K vs L parameterization issue]" is inaccurate. The paper properly acknowledges this interpretability trade-off (as seen in Section 2), noting that parametrizing L directly sacrifices interpretability but gains computational tractability, which is the standard approach in the ML literature (citing [3, 7]). The authors do not claim to solve this interpretability issue, but rather work within the established ML convention of parametrizing L. The paper's contribution is correctly framed as addressing the computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference, not resolving the parameterization trade-off.

The paper's significance is strong: DPPs are increasingly used for modeling diversity in applications ranging from news summarization to spatial statistics, and the authors provide a practical inference method that overcomes a major computational hurdle. The bounds are general enough to be potentially applicable to other kernel-based models, which adds to the paper's broader impact.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with clear exposition of the problem, solution, and experiments. The theoretical contributions are solid, the experimental validation is thorough, and the writing is accessible to the NIPS audience. The authors have clearly demonstrated that their bounds are both theoretically justified and practically useful.

In summary, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of point process modeling by providing computationally efficient inference methods for DPPs. The bounds are novel, well-derived, and effectively applied to both finite and continuous DPPs. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the approach's utility across different applications. The paper is of high quality, well-executed, and makes a meaningful contribution to the NIPS community.

Strengths: Novel theoretical contribution; clear exposition; well-constructed experiments; practical relevance to several applications; good connection to related work in Gaussian processes.

Weaknesses: Limited discussion of scalability for extremely large datasets; could better compare with alternative approximate inference methods.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, makes a significant contribution to the field, and would be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
