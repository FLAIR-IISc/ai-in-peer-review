The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that do not require spectral knowledge of the kernel defining the DPP. Instead, inducing inputs are utilized to bound the likelihood, reducing the computational cost. Both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques are described, and their efficiency is demonstrated empirically.

I believe there is merit to this paper, as it addresses a significant challenge in DPP inference. However, I found the method description to be somewhat rough around the edges, and the empirical evaluation could be more thorough. To strengthen the paper, I suggest the authors address the following points:

1. A comparison of the computational complexity and overall compute time of the two proposed methods would be beneficial. How do they scale with the size of the input data, and what are the implications for large-scale applications?
2. It would be helpful to compare the proposed methods to other approximate and exact alternatives in terms of quality of approximation and computation time. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of their strengths and weaknesses.
3. The empirical results on the toy data in Section 5.1 show that parameter estimation error using variational inference is too large, leading the authors to resort to approximate MCMC instead. It would be useful to discuss the settings in which the proposed variational inference is expected to work and when it is expected to fail, along with the underlying reasons for these expectations.
4. A 13-point sample is generated for the toy example. I am curious to know how the methods perform when the inducing inputs are actually these sampled points. This would provide insight into the robustness of the methods to different input configurations.
5. Finally, are there any rules of thumb or guidelines for selecting a) the number of inducing points and b) the placement of the initial inducing points? This information would be valuable for practitioners seeking to apply these methods in practice.

Minor comments:

* The matrices K and L are referred to throughout the introduction without explanation. Briefly mentioning the marginal kernel and L-ensembles may make the text more accessible to uninformed readers.
* The discussion about the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters is not entirely relevant to the approach taken in the paper. Some of this content could be condensed or removed to improve the overall focus and flow of the paper.

Overall, I believe the paper has potential, but the method description and empirical evaluation require refinement. If accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to improve the write-up to address these concerns.
