- Addresses DPP inference bottleneck by introducing nonspectral likelihood bounds using inducing inputs, enabling variational inference and approximate MCMC without eigendecomposition.  
- Novel extension of sparse Gaussian process methods to DPPs, offering a computationally efficient alternative to spectral approximations.  
- Strong theoretical contribution with rigorous derivations (Propositions 1 & 2) and connections to variational GP literature.  
- Demonstrates practical scalability and comparable performance to spectral methods on synthetic and real (diabetic neuropathy) datasets.  
- Empirical evaluation is insufficiently thorough—lacks comprehensive comparisons to other approximate DPP inference methods (e.g., refs [8,9]) and missing metrics like parameter accuracy and convergence rates.  
- Unclear guidance on when variational inference is appropriate versus when MCMC is necessary; toy example shows VI can be inconsistent.  
- No principled rule for selecting or placing the number of inducing inputs; current practice of increasing m ad hoc during MCMC is inadequate.  
- Notation and exposition need improvement—K and L relationships, marginal kernel and L-ensembles are not clearly defined for nonexperts.  
- Discussion of interpretability trade-offs for L vs K is somewhat tangential and should be shortened to maintain focus.  
- Main strengths: novel nonspectral bounds, practical scalability, and clear empirical demonstration.  
- Main weaknesses: incomplete empirical comparisons, lack of practitioner guidance, some tangential discussion, and insufficient explanation for newcomers.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—add thorough comparisons, clarify implementation guidelines (inducing inputs, VI vs MCMC), and streamline kernel parametrization discussion.
