- Proposes nonspectral, inducing-point-based upper and lower bounds on DPP normalizing constants for finite and continuous domains, avoiding eigenvalue computations.  
- Introduces pseudo-inputs Z and a low-rank approximation Q to derive tractable bounds on det(I+L) and the Fredholm determinant.  
- Develops two inference strategies: (i) variational inference maximizing a lower bound over kernel params and Z, and (ii) a retrospective MCMC scheme using the bounds for exact accept/reject decisions.  
- Builds on sparse GP inducing-point methodology and sidesteps spectral/Nyström and eigenvalue-truncation approaches used in prior DPP work.  
- The determinant inequalities and their adaptation to DPP likelihoods appear technically sound, with proofs in the supplement.  
- The retrospective MH is principled for exact inference, but the main text should clarify conditions guaranteeing almost-sure decision resolution and risks of biased stopping.  
- For continuous DPPs, trace-class and integrability assumptions are given, but more guidance is needed on when Ψ is available beyond Gaussian–Gaussian cases.  
- Empirical evaluation is informative but limited in scope and lacks breadth of comparisons and scalability tests.  
- Exposition needs clearer upfront definitions of K (marginal kernel) and L (L-ensemble) and streamlined discussion of parameterizing K vs L.  
- Notation is mostly consistent but would benefit from reminders (submatrix indices, measure assumptions) and explicit computational complexity/memory costs for all steps.  
- Using inducing-point-based determinant bounds to avoid spectral decompositions is novel and conceptually appealing.  
- If broadly applicable, the bounds could make likelihood-based DPP inference more practical, especially for continuous domains where spectra are unavailable.  
- Strength: simple, general, computationally attractive bounds that avoid eigenvalue computations.  
- Strength: unified treatment of finite and continuous DPPs with connections to variational sparse GPs.  
- Strength: retrospective MH adaptation enabling exact posterior sampling using bounds.  
- Strength: insightful discussion on identifiability when parameterizing L.  
- Weakness: missing explicit asymptotic time/space complexity for bound evaluation, gradients, VI and MH iterations, and Z-optimization as functions of n, m, T.  
- Weakness: lack of wall-clock times, memory footprints, and time breakdowns (bound evaluation vs Z-optimization) for experiments.  
- Weakness: absence of comparisons with spectral/Nyström methods, EM/fixed-point L-ensemble learners, and truncated-eigenvalue baselines where spectra exist.  
- Weakness: need characterization of when VI performs well or fails (identifiability, parameter coupling, bound tightness vs m, curvature).  
- Weakness: should report empirical gaps between the variational bound and true log-likelihood and how they scale with m and Z-optimization.  
- Weakness: retrospective MH needs quantification of how often m increases, typical final m, per-iteration cost, acceptance rates, and ESS; clarify theoretical guarantee on almost-sure resolution and bias absence.  
- Weakness: evaluate using Z equal to observed points and compare to optimized Z and Nyström choices; provide practical guidance for selecting m, initializing Z, stopping rules, and regularization/prior use to avoid Z overfitting.  
- Weakness: continuous-case practicality beyond Gaussian–Gaussian—clarify when Ψ is tractable and whether quadrature approximations preserve the nonspectral advantage; compare to Fredholm quadrature methods.  
- Minor: define K and L in the introduction, shorten the K-learning discussion, state the determinant inequality and conditions in the main text, and proofread for duplicates/typos.  
- Pro for acceptance: conceptually clean, broadly applicable bounds removing a major practical bottleneck and concrete continuous-domain examples.  
- Con against acceptance: limited empirical evaluation, insufficient practical guidance on computational trade-offs and inducing points, and clarity gaps in algorithmic/details.  
- Overall: promising and original work with practical potential that would be substantially strengthened by stronger empirical comparisons, clearer complexity/runtime reporting, and more guidance for practitioners.
