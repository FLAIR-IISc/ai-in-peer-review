This paper introduces approximate inference techniques for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) that circumvent the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix, a significant computational bottleneck. The core idea is to leverage inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood, thereby reducing computational cost. The authors present both a variational inference (VI) approach and an approximate Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method, demonstrating their empirical efficiency.

While the work has merit, I found the methodological descriptions somewhat lacking in detail and the empirical evaluation insufficiently comprehensive. Several points require clarification to fully assess the contribution.

First, a comparative analysis of the computational complexity and runtime of the proposed VI and MCMC methods is needed. Understanding their relative costs is crucial for practical application.

Second, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to existing DPP inference techniques – both approximate and exact – regarding both the quality of approximation achieved and computational time. Positioning the proposed methods within the broader landscape of DPP inference is essential.

Third, the results presented in Section 5.1 reveal substantial parameter estimation error with VI, leading to a reliance on the more computationally expensive approximate MCMC. It's critical to delineate the conditions under which the proposed VI approach is expected to perform well, and conversely, when it is likely to fail, along with a theoretical or intuitive explanation for these limitations.

Fourth, the toy example utilizes a 13-point sample. It would be valuable to investigate the performance of the proposed methods specifically when these sampled points are used as the inducing inputs. This provides a direct benchmark against a straightforward, albeit potentially suboptimal, inducing point selection strategy. 

Fifth, practical guidance on selecting the inducing inputs is absent. Providing a rule of thumb or heuristic for determining a) the optimal number of inducing points and b) their initial placement would greatly enhance the usability of the proposed methods.

Several minor points could improve the paper’s clarity. The introduction refers to matrices *K* and *L* without initial definition. Briefly introducing the concepts of the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would improve accessibility for readers less familiar with DPP theory. Furthermore, the paper discusses the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters, yet this approach is not implemented. Reducing or eliminating this discussion would streamline the presentation and focus on the core contributions.

In summary, while the proposed approach to approximate DPP inference is promising, the paper requires substantial refinement. The method descriptions need greater clarity, and the empirical validation must be more thorough and comparative. If accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to address these points to improve the quality and impact of the work.
