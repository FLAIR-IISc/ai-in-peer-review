

This paper addresses a core challenge in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the intractable normalizing constant in the likelihood, which typically requires spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors propose novel, nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid spectral computation by leveraging pseudo-inputs (inspired by sparse Gaussian processes [11]), enabling efficient variational inference and exact MCMC for both finite and continuous DPPs. This work directly extends prior NIPS contributions on DPP inference (e.g., [7] on spectral bounds for MCMC, [4] on DPP modeling in spatial data), but crucially bypasses the spectral bottleneck that limits scalability.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The technical contribution is sound and well-validated. The bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) are derived rigorously using determinant inequalities [14], and the experimental evaluation demonstrates clear utility. The finite-domain variational inference (Section 3.1) achieves significant speedups over spectral methods [7] (e.g., O(nm²) vs. O(n³) for eigendecomposition), while the retrospective MCMC (Section 3.2) provides exact inference without spectral approximations. The diabetic neuropathy experiment (Section 5.2) convincingly shows convergence of overdispersion metrics (γₐ) as pseudo-inputs increase, validating the approach on real data. Crucially, the authors carefully acknowledge limitations—e.g., the L-parametrization sacrifices interpretability (Section 1) and κ remains poorly identified (Section 5.1)—demonstrating honesty in evaluation.

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-organized. The finite-domain derivation (Section 2) builds intuition before extending to continuous domains (Section 4), with clear connections to Gaussian processes. Figures 1–4 effectively illustrate key insights (e.g., pseudo-inputs aligning with marginal densities, convergence of bounds). The supplementary material provides necessary technical details without cluttering the main text. Minor suggestions: the continuous-domain setup (Section 4.1) could briefly clarify why Fredholm determinants are used over alternative likelihood formulations, and the discussion of identifiability (Section 5.1) might explicitly link κ’s non-identifiability to the L-parametrization trade-off.

*Originality*: This is a significant step beyond prior NIPS work. While [7] used spectral bounds for MCMC (requiring eigenvalue approximations), the authors’ nonspectral bounds avoid all spectral computation, enabling broader applicability (e.g., to large finite sets or continuous domains where spectral methods fail). The extension to continuous DPPs (Section 4) is novel and generalizes sparse GP techniques [11] to a new context. Related work [3, 4, 8] focuses on modeling *with* DPPs but not scalable inference, making this work a key enabler for future applications.

*Significance*: The contribution has immediate impact for practitioners. DPPs are increasingly used in diversity modeling (news [3], search [7], ecology [4]), but likelihood-based inference has been impractical for large datasets. This work unlocks scalable, principled inference—e.g., the diabetic neuropathy experiment (Section 5.2) uses 1,200 pseudo-inputs to distinguish disease stages efficiently. The bounds also generalize beyond DPPs (e.g., to other likelihoods with intractable determinants), suggesting broader relevance to NIPS topics like probabilistic modeling and spatial statistics.

**Weaknesses**  
The paper does not address the K-parametrization issue (as mistakenly noted in the draft review), but this is a deliberate choice: the authors explicitly adopt L-parametrization for tractability (Section 1), acknowledging the interpretability cost. This is not a weakness but a well-justified design decision. The only minor gap is the limited discussion of how the pseudo-inputs (Z) are initialized or optimized in practice—though Section 3.1 and Figure 4 show they converge to meaningful locations, a brief note on initialization strategies would strengthen reproducibility. The continuous-domain experiments (Section 5.1) rely on a special case with known spectral decomposition; while this validates correctness, future work should test on less tractable kernels (e.g., non-Gaussian).

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*: Novel nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods; scalable inference for finite/continuous domains; rigorous theory + strong experiments; clear connection to prior NIPS work; addresses a critical bottleneck in DPP applications.  
*Con*: L-parametrization sacrifices interpretability (acknowledged); continuous-domain extension is technically dense for some readers; no experiments on very large-scale datasets (e.g., n > 10⁴).  

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a high-impact contribution to scalable probabilistic modeling. The bounds are theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and validated on both synthetic and real-world data. The work directly advances the state of the art in DPP inference beyond [7] and enables practical applications cited in [3, 4, 7]. The minor weaknesses do not undermine the core contribution, and the clarity of presentation meets NIPS standards. I strongly recommend acceptance.  

*Note on draft review correction*: The draft incorrectly stated the paper "makes no efforts to address [K vs L]"; the paper explicitly *chooses* L-parametrization (Section 1) and discusses its interpretability trade-off, making this critique invalid. The authors’ focus on L is a deliberate, well-motivated design choice, not an oversight.
