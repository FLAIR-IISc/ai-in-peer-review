Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have recently gained considerable attention in machine learning due to their ability to model repulsive interactions between samples. This repulsion is governed by a positive definite kernel function, and a key challenge lies in learning the parameters of this kernel from observed point realizations. Traditional estimation methods are hampered by a computationally intractable normalization constant, requiring summation over all possible subset cardinalities and configurations. A recent work [7] addressed this by estimating the kernel’s spectrum and deriving incremental upper and lower bounds, enabling provably correct retrospective Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for kernel parameter estimation.

This paper presents an alternative bounding strategy for MCMC inference that circumvents the need for spectral estimation. Furthermore, the proposed approach naturally lends itself to variational inference and establishes connections to inducing-point methods commonly used for scalable inference in Gaussian process (GP) models. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and introduces a valuable contribution to the field. I was previously unaware of the inequality underpinning Proposition 1, which appears particularly well-suited for this problem, and appreciate the elegant way it avoids the estimation difficulties of [7]. The links to GP inducing points and the possibility of variational inference are especially exciting. Overall, this work is a pleasure to read.

However, I have two technical concerns. First, while the proposed method is appealing, I am not fully convinced that replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension represents a substantial computational advantage. The potential benefits require stronger justification. Second, the convergence of the bounds achieved by simply increasing the cardinality is not immediately clear, given the coupled nature of the optimization problem. This optimization appears highly non-convex, and simply increasing cardinality does not guarantee convergence to a global minimum. This issue should be directly addressed, as a local minimum could prevent effective bounding of the Bernoulli threshold, thereby hindering MCMC step acceptance.

Regarding presentation, I have a few suggestions:

*   Page 7, Figure 1: Please include axis labels to improve clarity.
*   The proof of Proposition 1 is central to the paper’s contribution and core insight. I recommend moving it from the appendix to the main body of the paper for increased visibility.
*   Page 7, Line 361: The phrase “… the variational lower is …” appears incomplete and should be revised.

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces a clever technique for bounding the partition function of a DPP, potentially leading to faster inference and bridging connections to other modeling frameworks. Addressing the concerns regarding computational cost and convergence would further strengthen the contribution.
