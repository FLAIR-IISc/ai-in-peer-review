Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have recently garnered considerable interest in the machine learning community for their capacity to model repulsive interactions within point sets. This repulsion is defined through a positive definite kernel function. A significant challenge, however, is learning the parameters of this kernel from example realizations of point sets. This estimation is complicated by an intractable normalization constant, which involves a summation over all possible set cardinalities and configurations.

A prior method [7] addressed this by estimating the kernel's spectrum to construct incremental upper and lower bounds, enabling a provably correct "retrospective" Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm for the kernel parameters. The current work introduces an alternative bounding technique, also suitable for MCMC inference, which crucially does not depend on estimating eigenvalues. This framework is also amenable to a variational learning approach, and the authors draw insightful connections to inducing-point methods used for scalable inference in Gaussian process models.

This is a technically sound and well-articulated paper. The inequality that forms the basis of the lower bound in Proposition 1 is a particularly effective tool for this problem and appears to be a novel application in this context. It provides an elegant way to circumvent the estimation difficulties associated with the approach in [7]. The connections drawn to GP inducing points are highly valuable, as is the new capability for performing variational inference. Overall, the manuscript was a pleasure to review.

However, I have two technical points that I would like the authors to address.

First, while the proposed method is appealing, it is not entirely clear that it offers a substantial computational advantage. The trade-off involves replacing power iterations with a sequence of nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimensionality, and the benefit of this exchange is not fully substantiated.

Second, the convergence of the bounds as cardinality increases is not guaranteed, given the coupled optimization problem. This optimization appears to be highly non-convex, meaning that simply increasing the cardinality may be insufficient if a global minimum is not found. This issue should be discussed directly, as convergence to a local minimum could prevent the bounds from sufficiently tightening around the Bernoulli threshold, thereby stalling the MCMC sampler and making it impossible to accept a step.
