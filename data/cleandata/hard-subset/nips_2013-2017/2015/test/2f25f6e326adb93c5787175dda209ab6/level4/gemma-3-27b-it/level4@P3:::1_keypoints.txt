- Paper proposes a novel determinant-inequality-based bounding strategy for DPP likelihoods that avoids eigenvalue estimation.  
- Approach enables retrospective MCMC inference and a variational method, linking DPP inference to Gaussian process inducing-point techniques.  
- The determinant inequality used in Proposition 1 is novel and well-suited to the problem.  
- Avoiding power-iteration eigenvalue estimation is a clever and compelling alternative to prior work [7].  
- Connections to inducing points and variational inference are significant strengths for scalable DPP inference.  
- The paper is technically strong, mathematically rigorous, and well-written.  
- Experimental validation (Section 5) compares to existing methods and provides initial empirical support.  
- Major concern: replacing power iterations with high-dimensional nonlinear optimization over inducing points may not be a clear computational win.  
- The cost of optimizing many inducing points as their number grows needs more thorough discussion and complexity analysis.  
- Increasing inducing-point cardinality does not guarantee convergence of bounds; the optimization is likely highly non-convex.  
- Local minima in inducing-point optimization could prevent bounds from narrowing enough to progress MCMC acceptance, possibly halting inference.  
- The practical impact of parameterizing L directly instead of K is not fully explored.  
- Recommend briefly outlining the core idea of Proposition 1’s proof in the main text for clarity.  
- Minor fixes: add axis labels to Figure 1 (P7) and correct typo “the variational lower is…” to “the variational lower bound is…” (P7 L361).  
- Suggest discussing strategies to mitigate local optima in inducing-point optimization and providing more evidence of computational tradeoffs.  
- Overall recommendation: accept, contingent on addressing computational complexity and convergence concerns in a revision.
