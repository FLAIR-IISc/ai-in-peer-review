- Paper proposes nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods using inducing inputs, avoiding eigenvalue computation, applicable to both finite and continuous domains and usable in variational inference and retrospective MCMC.

- Technical strength: Proposition 1 gives a tight, cheap determinant inequality-based bound that circumvents spectral estimation.

- Scope strength: Framework handles finite and continuous DPPs and unifies variational and MCMC approaches; continuous-domain treatment with computable Ψ is valuable.

- Connection strength: Clear analogy to inducing-point sparse Gaussian process methods, situating the work in scalable probabilistic inference.

- Clarity: Manuscript is generally well written and easy to follow.

- Main computational concern: Removing power iterations trades them for nonlinear optimization over inducing inputs Z and kernel parameters; unclear net computational benefit.

- Request for evaluation: Need detailed theoretical complexity and empirical wall-clock comparisons between spectral methods [7] and the proposed optimization-based tightening across n, m, target accuracy, and restart counts.

- Scaling question: How do time and memory scale with m and with the number of optimization restarts required?

- Convergence concern for retrospective MCMC: Optimizing Z is nonconvex, so increasing m does not guarantee bounds converge to the true likelihood, risking unresolved accept/reject decisions and stalled chains.

- Request for guarantees: Ask for conditions under which optimized bounds provably converge as m→∞ and discussion of the role of local minima beyond the GP variance analogy.

- Practical safeguards: Request discussion of mitigation strategies (multiple restarts, annealing, structured initializations) and diagnostics to detect failed tightening.

- Empirical evidence: Ask for demonstrations that retrospective decisions reliably resolve within reasonable m across datasets.

- Presentation issues: Add axis labels to Figure 1 subplots; move or sketch the central proof of Proposition 1 into the main text; fix typo “variational lower is …” → “variational lower bound is …”.

- Pros for acceptance: Novel nonspectral bounds, unification of variational and MCMC, links to sparse GPs, and empirical demonstrations including continuous-domain cases.

- Cons for acceptance: Unclear computational advantage in regimes where spectral methods are feasible and insufficient guarantees/discussion on convergence of optimized bounds in retrospective MCMC.

- Overall evaluation: Strong, interesting, and clever contribution that should be solidified by addressing computational trade-offs and convergence concerns.
