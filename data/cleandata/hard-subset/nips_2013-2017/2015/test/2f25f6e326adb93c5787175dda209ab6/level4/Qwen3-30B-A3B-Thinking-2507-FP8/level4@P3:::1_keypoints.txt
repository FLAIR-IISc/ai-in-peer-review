- Addresses DPP inference challenge by avoiding spectral decomposition and deriving non-spectral bounds on the normalization constant using pseudo-inputs (inducing points).
- Main theoretical contribution (Proposition 1): elegant determinant-based bounds enabling variational inference for DPPs while preserving retrospective MCMC guarantees.
- Connects DPP inference to Gaussian process inducing-point methods, providing an insightful and well-developed framing within scalable kernel methods.
- Extends the bounding technique to continuous DPPs, applying to Fredholm determinants without spectral knowledge.
- Experimental: toy Gaussian example—retrospective MCMC recovers true parameters; variational approach is unstable, showing complementarity of methods.
- Experimental: diabetic neuropathy dataset—clear separation of disease progression classes; variational estimates converge as number of pseudo-inputs increases.
- Computational efficiency: variational approach demonstrates practical speed (e.g., 24 minutes for m=1200), impressive given problem complexity.
- Exposition is clear and well-organized; presenting finite case first and linking to GP literature enhances accessibility.
- Concern: claimed computational advantage is unclear—replacing O(n^3) power iterations with O(n m^2) pseudo-input optimization may not scale if m must grow with n; needs precise complexity analysis.
- Concern: convergence of bounds as m increases lacks strong theoretical guarantees; non-convex optimization may yield local minima that undermine MCMC acceptance decisions.
- Recommendation: move proof of Proposition 1 to main text and add axis labels to figures for clarity.
- Overall judgment: significant, novel, and technically strong contribution to DPP inference; recommend acceptance pending clarifications on computational scaling and convergence guarantees.
