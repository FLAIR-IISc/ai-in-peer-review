- Proposes novel non-spectral upper and lower bounds on DPP likelihoods using inducing points, avoiding expensive spectral computations.  
- Bounds are inspired by sparse GP approximations and rely on a determinant inequality, making them cheaper to compute than exact likelihoods or spectral approximations.  
- Methods are integrated into two inference frameworks: a fast approximate variational inference (VI) and an exact retrospective MCMC sampler.  
- Framework is developed for both finite (discrete) and continuous DPPs, increasing applicability, especially for continuous domains.  
- The core idea is original and significant, offering a potentially better cost–accuracy trade-off for large-scale DPP inference.  
- The theoretical derivation appears technically sound and well-motivated.  
- Empirical evaluation is weak and does not sufficiently substantiate claimed advantages or limitations.  
- No comparisons to relevant baselines (notably the spectral-bound MCMC of Affandi et al. [7]) are provided.  
- The paper lacks an internal comparison between the proposed VI and MCMC methods, including runtime and complexity analyses.  
- The observed failure of VI on a toy problem is reported but not analyzed—reasons (loose bound, joint optimization instability) are not investigated.  
- Dependence on number and placement of inducing points (m and Z) is underexplored; practical guidance and heuristics are missing.  
- Sensitivity to inducing point initialization and the strategy for optimizing their locations are not discussed.  
- Suggested additional experiment: fix inducing points to observed data locations to compare bounds and inference performance versus optimized inducing points.  
- Overall, the contribution is valuable but requires substantially strengthened experiments (baselines, VI vs MCMC analysis, practitioner guidance) to validate utility.
