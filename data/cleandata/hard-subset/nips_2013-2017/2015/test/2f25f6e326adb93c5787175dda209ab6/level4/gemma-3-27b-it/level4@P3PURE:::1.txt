Determinantal point processes (DPPs) have recently gained significant attention in the machine learning literature due to their ability to model repulsion within realizations of a set of points. This repulsion is specified via a positive definite kernel function. A key challenge, however, lies in learning the appropriate parameters of this kernel given example point realizations. This estimation is difficult due to a computationally intractable normalization constant that effectively sums over all possible cardinalities and point configurations. A recent work [7] addresses this by observing that the spectrum can be estimated and used to provide incremental upper and lower bounds, resulting in a provably-correct scheme for “retrospective” Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) of the kernel parameters. The present paper proposes an alternative bounding strategy that also enables MCMC inference, but without requiring eigenvalue estimation. This approach further lends itself to variational inference, and the paper establishes connections to inducing-point methods, offering potential for scalable inference in Gaussian process models.

This is a technically strong and well-written paper. I was previously unaware of the inequality that provides the lower bound in Proposition 1, and it appears to be an excellent fit for this problem. It represents a clever way to circumvent the estimation issues associated with [7]. I particularly appreciate the connections to GP inducing points, as well as the possibility of performing variational inference. Overall, this paper was a pleasure to read.

However, I have two technical concerns that I would like the authors to address. First, while I find this approach promising, I am not entirely convinced that replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization problems of increasing dimension constitutes a significant computational advantage. Second, it is not clear to me that simply increasing the cardinality guarantees convergence of the bounds, given the coupled nature of the optimization problem. I suspect this optimization is highly non-convex, and thus increasing cardinality alone may not suffice; ensuring a global minimum is reached is crucial. This point requires direct discussion, as a local minimum could prevent the procedure from bounding away the Bernoulli threshold and, consequently, impede MCMC steps.
