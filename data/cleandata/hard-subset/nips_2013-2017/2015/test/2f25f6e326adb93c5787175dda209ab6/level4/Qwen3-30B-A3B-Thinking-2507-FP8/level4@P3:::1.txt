

This paper addresses a significant challenge in the inference of determinantal point processes (DPPs), which are valuable for modeling repulsive point patterns across various applications including news summarization, search engine results, and spatial ecology. The authors tackle the core difficulty in DPP parameter learning: the intractable normalization constant that appears in the likelihood. Previous work by Affandi et al. [7] circumvented this by approximating the spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix, enabling retrospective MCMC inference but requiring computationally expensive power iterations. The present paper makes a novel contribution by deriving non-spectral bounds on the normalization constant that avoid spectral decomposition entirely, instead using pseudo-inputs (akin to inducing points in Gaussian processes) to construct tight, efficiently computable bounds.

The paper is technically strong and well-executed. The main theoretical contribution (Proposition 1) provides elegant bounds on the likelihood that leverage a non-trivial determinant inequality, offering a fresh perspective on DPP inference. This is particularly valuable because it enables variational inference for DPPs—a capability previously unavailable—while maintaining the theoretical guarantees of retrospective MCMC. The connections to Gaussian process inducing point methods are insightful and well-developed, demonstrating how the DPP inference problem can be framed within the broader context of scalable kernel methods. The authors effectively extend their approach to continuous DPPs (Section 4), showing how the same bounding technique applies to Fredholm determinants without spectral knowledge.

The experimental validation is thorough and compelling. In the toy Gaussian example (Section 5.1), the retrospective MCMC method successfully recovers the true parameters for the continuous DPP, while the variational approach exhibits instability—demonstrating the complementary value of both methods. The diabetic neuropathy dataset (Section 5.2) shows clear separation between disease progression classes using the inferred kernel parameters, with the variational approach converging to consistent estimates as the number of pseudo-inputs increases. The computational efficiency of the variational approach (24 minutes for m=1200 inducing points) is particularly impressive given the problem's complexity.

The paper excels in clarity despite its mathematical depth. The exposition is well-organized, with the finite case presented first followed by the continuous extension. The connection to Gaussian process literature makes the approach accessible to the broader machine learning community. However, I have two specific concerns regarding quality that require clarification:

First, while the paper demonstrates computational efficiency compared to spectral methods, the replacement of power iterations (O(n^3) per iteration) with nonlinear optimization over pseudo-inputs (O(nm^2) per iteration) is not clearly a computational win. For large n, m must scale with n to achieve tight bounds, potentially negating any advantage. The authors should provide a more precise complexity analysis showing when their approach is superior.

Second, the convergence of the bounds as m increases requires stronger theoretical justification. The optimization over pseudo-inputs is non-convex, and the authors' current approach (increasing m until the bounds are tight enough) does not guarantee convergence to the true likelihood. The paper should clarify whether the optimization procedure can reliably find global minima or if local minima could prevent the MCMC acceptance decision from being made. This is a critical issue for the theoretical soundness of their MCMC method.

In terms of originality, this work makes a significant contribution by avoiding spectral decomposition entirely, which is a fundamental limitation in previous DPP inference approaches. The connection to GP inducing points is novel in the DPP context and represents a meaningful bridge between two important classes of probabilistic models. The significance of this work is high, as it enables more scalable inference for DPPs and opens the door to using DPPs in larger-scale applications.

The paper's clarity is excellent, though I recommend moving the proof of Proposition 1 to the main text (as suggested in the draft review) to better highlight this key contribution. The figures would also benefit from axis labels (as noted in the draft review).

Pro-con acceptance summary:
- Pro: Novel non-spectral bounds that enable variational inference for DPPs, strong theoretical foundation, excellent experimental validation, clear connections to Gaussian processes.
- Con: Computational advantage not clearly established for large-scale problems, convergence guarantees for the optimization procedure need strengthening.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the inference of determinantal point processes. The technical contribution is solid, the writing is clear, and the experimental validation is convincing. The concerns regarding computational advantage and convergence are important but not fundamental to the paper's contribution. With minor clarifications addressing the two points above, this paper would be an excellent addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
