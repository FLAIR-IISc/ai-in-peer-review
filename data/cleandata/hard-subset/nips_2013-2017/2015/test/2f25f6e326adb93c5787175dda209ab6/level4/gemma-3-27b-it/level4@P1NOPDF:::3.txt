The paper proposes approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that do not require knowledge of the kernel’s spectrum. Instead, inducing inputs are used to bound the likelihood, reducing the computational cost. Both variational inference and approximate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques are described, and their efficiency is demonstrated empirically.

I believe this paper has merit, but I found the method descriptions somewhat unclear and the empirical evaluation insufficiently thorough. To help clarify the work, I have the following questions and suggestions:

1) How does the computational complexity and overall runtime compare between the two proposed methods?

2) How do the proposed methods compare to other alternatives – both approximate and exact – in terms of approximation quality and computation time? 

3) The empirical results on the toy data in Section 5.1 show a significant parameter estimation error when using variational inference, leading to a switch to approximate MCMC. In what settings is the proposed variational inference expected to perform well, and when is it expected to fail? What are the reasons for these differences in performance?

4) A 13-point sample is generated for the toy example. How do the methods perform when the inducing inputs are specifically chosen to be these sampled points?

5) Are there any guidelines or rules of thumb for selecting a) the number of inducing points, and b) their initial placement?

Minor comments:

The matrices K and L are referenced throughout the introduction without prior definition. Briefly mentioning the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would improve readability for readers unfamiliar with these concepts.

The paper discusses the advantages of directly learning the marginal kernel parameters, but this approach is not ultimately taken. I suggest reducing this discussion, as it doesn’t directly contribute to the core arguments of the paper.
