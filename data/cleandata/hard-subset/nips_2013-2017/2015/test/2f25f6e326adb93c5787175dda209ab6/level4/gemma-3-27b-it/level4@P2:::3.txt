This paper addresses the computational challenge of likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) by proposing novel bounds on the likelihood that circumvent the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel. The core idea is to leverage inducing inputs to approximate the likelihood, enabling both variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. This is a valuable contribution, as spectral decomposition is often a bottleneck in DPP inference, particularly for large datasets.

The paper begins by clearly outlining the problem – the intractability of the likelihood due to the determinant calculation – and positions its approach within the existing literature on DPP learning, acknowledging previous work on direct kernel parameterization. The technical presentation of the bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) appears sound, drawing inspiration from related techniques in Gaussian process literature. The proposed inference methods are logically presented, building upon the derived bounds.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a significant computational bottleneck:** Avoiding spectral decomposition makes DPP inference feasible in settings where it was previously impractical.
*   **Novel bounds:** The proposed bounds provide a new approach to approximating the DPP likelihood.
*   **Flexibility:** The methods can be used with both variational inference and MCMC, offering a trade-off between speed and accuracy.
*   **Connections to existing work:**  The paper appropriately situates itself within the broader context of DPP modeling and inference, and draws inspiration from related work on Gaussian processes.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited empirical evaluation:** The experimental section, while present, feels somewhat incomplete. The comparisons to existing methods are lacking, and the analysis of the toy example highlights limitations of the variational approach without fully explaining *why* those limitations occur.
*   **Method description could be clearer:** While the core ideas are present, the description of the methods feels a bit rough around the edges. More detailed explanations, particularly regarding the optimization procedures, would be beneficial.
*   **Practical guidance lacking:** There's little discussion of practical considerations for applying the methods, such as how to choose the number of inducing inputs or initialize them.
*   **Motivation for direct L-parametrization:** The paper spends time discussing the trade-offs of parametrizing L versus K, but ultimately commits to L without a particularly compelling justification, especially given the observed difficulty in interpreting the parameters. Some of this discussion could be streamlined.

**Specific Questions/Suggestions for the Authors:**

1.  **Computational Complexity:**  A more detailed comparison of the computational complexity and wall-clock time of the proposed methods (variational and MCMC) would be valuable. How do these scale with the number of data points and inducing inputs?
2.  **Benchmarking:** How do the proposed methods compare to other existing DPP inference techniques, both approximate (e.g., those based on sparse approximations) and exact (when feasible), in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency?
3.  **Variational Inference Limitations:** The toy example demonstrates that variational inference can suffer from poor parameter estimation. What are the conditions under which the variational approach is expected to perform well, and when is it likely to fail? What factors influence the accuracy of the variational approximation?
4.  **Inducing Input Placement:**  Would using the sampled data points themselves as inducing inputs (in the toy example) improve performance? How sensitive are the results to the initialization and placement of the inducing inputs? Are there any heuristics for selecting good inducing points?
5.  **Number of Inducing Inputs:** What guidance can you provide for selecting the number of inducing inputs? Is there a rule of thumb or a method for determining an appropriate value based on the dataset size and complexity?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to a significant computational challenge in DPP modeling.
*   The theoretical contributions (the derived bounds) appear sound and well-motivated.
*   The empirical results, though limited, demonstrate the feasibility of the proposed methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation is not thorough enough to fully assess the performance of the methods.
*   The method description could be clearer and more detailed.
*   The lack of practical guidance limits the usability of the proposed techniques.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has merit and the idea is promising. However, the current presentation requires improvement. If the authors address the weaknesses outlined above – particularly by providing a more comprehensive empirical evaluation and clearer explanations – the paper would be significantly strengthened. I lean towards a **weak accept** contingent on substantial revisions.
