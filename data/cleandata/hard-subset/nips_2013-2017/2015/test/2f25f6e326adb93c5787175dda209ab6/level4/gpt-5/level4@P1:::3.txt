Summary
This paper proposes nonspectral bounds on the determinant terms that appear in DPP likelihoods, for both finite sets and continuous domains. The bounds are constructed using inducing inputs (pseudo-inputs) Z, in the spirit of sparse Gaussian processes, yielding an approximation Q = LYZ LZ−1 LZY that avoids computing eigenvalues. The upper and lower bounds on det(L + I) (finite case) and on det(I + L) (continuous case via Fredholm determinants) enable two inference strategies: (i) a variational lower bound that is optimized jointly over kernel parameters and Z; and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler that refines bounds by increasing m = |Z| only when needed to make acceptance decisions. The work is positioned against prior DPP inference that relies on spectral information (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2014), and connects to variational inducing-variable GP methods (Titsias, AISTATS 2009). Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian toy problem (where exact spectral quantities are available) and a diabetic neuropathy dataset (R2).

Strengths
- Novelty: Providing determinant bounds that do not require spectral decompositions for either finite or continuous DPPs appears original and practically useful. Extending the technique to continuous domains is particularly valuable.
- Practicality: The approach enables both a cheap variational objective and an exact-but-approximate MCMC with retrospective acceptance, potentially broadening DPP applicability.
- Conceptual clarity: The inducing-input construction and the analogy to sparse GPs are intuitive and likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Weaknesses and questions
- Method description is terse in places, especially around optimization over Z and computational trade-offs between VI and MH.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and baselines. It demonstrates feasibility but not clear superiority in accuracy, scalability, or runtime over existing methods.
- The variational approach appears unstable on the toy example (large errors, high variability), with limited guidance on when it should be expected to work.

Quality
- The theoretical bounds rely on a known determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon), and the finite/continuous cases are clearly stated. However, practical guidance on tightness versus m and optimization of Z is limited.
- Please provide a more explicit complexity analysis:
  1) For VI and for one MH iteration, detail asymptotic time and memory in n, m, and number of observations T. Include the cost of optimizing Z (per iteration, number of steps), and the cost of computing Ψ in the continuous case.
  2) In retrospective MH, clarify whether Z is state-specific or shared, how Z is initialized and re-optimized after proposals, and its effect on mixing and acceptance.

Clarity
- Early in the introduction, define K, L, and their relationship (K = L(I + L)−1, L-ensembles), and briefly motivate why many ML works parameterize L rather than K.
- The paper repeatedly contrasts parameterizing K and L, but since you ultimately parameterize L, consider trimming that discussion and focusing on the concrete implications for identifiability and interpretability observed in your experiments.
- Spell out the full VI objective and optimization steps (what is fixed vs optimized, gradients available, stopping criteria).
- For MH, summarize the full algorithm with the retrospective acceptance step, including how m is grown and when optimization of Z is triggered.

Originality
- The nonspectral determinant bounds and their extension to continuous DPPs are, to my knowledge, new. The connection to inducing-variable approximations is well drawn.

Significance
- If the bounds are tight with moderate m and Z can be optimized efficiently, the approach could materially reduce the cost of DPP inference in both discrete and continuous settings. The current experiments suggest promise but do not yet establish advantages over strong baselines or on larger-scale problems.

Requests for empirical clarification and additions
1) Complexity and wall-clock: Compare the compute time and memory of VI vs MH on your datasets, including the overhead of optimizing Z. Report acceptance rates, distribution of m used by MH, and end-to-end training times.
2) Baselines: Compare to spectral approaches (e.g., Affandi et al. ICML’14), EM/fixed-point methods for L-ensembles (Gillenwater et al. NIPS’14; Mariet & Sra NIPS’15), and Nyström-type approximations when applicable. Report likelihoods (or tight bounds), parameter estimation error, and runtime.
3) When does VI work? The toy example shows large errors and variability for VI. Provide diagnostics and guidance: sensitivity to m, initialization of Z, and parameter identifiability (e.g., κ). Clarify scenarios where VI performs well (e.g., larger T, stronger priors, constrained parameterizations) and when MH is recommended.
4) Using the observed points as inducing inputs: In the toy example with 13 points, how do VI and MH perform if Z is initialized to or fixed at the observed sample? Does this tighten bounds or improve stability?
5) Rules of thumb for Z: Provide practical heuristics for selecting m and initializing Z (e.g., k-means on pooled points, sampling from the base measure, or from the marginal intensity), and a criterion to grow m (bound gap threshold).

Minor comments
- Define K and L, and briefly mention “marginal kernel” and “L-ensembles,” at first use in the introduction to aid non-experts.
- Consider reducing discussion on directly learning K if it is not used in your methods; focus instead on the identifiability and interpretability implications when parameterizing L (e.g., the κ issue).
- Provide implementation details needed for reproducibility: gradient computation for Z, optimizers, learning rates, stopping criteria, number of restarts, and initialization strategies.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, general, and potentially impactful method to avoid spectral decompositions in DPP inference.
- Extends to continuous domains with implementable integrals for common kernels/measures.
- Enables both variational and MH inference within a unified framework.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient empirical comparison to strong baselines; limited datasets and scale.
- Lack of clear guidance on when VI is reliable and how to set m and Z in practice.
- Some aspects of the method description and assumptions could be clarified to improve reproducibility.
