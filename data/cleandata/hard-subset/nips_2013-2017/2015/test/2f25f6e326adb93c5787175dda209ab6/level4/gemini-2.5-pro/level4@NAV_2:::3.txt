This paper addresses the significant challenge of parameter inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), where the likelihood calculation is often intractable due to a large determinant term that typically requires spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix or operator. The main contribution is the derivation of novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the DPP likelihood for both finite and continuous domains. These bounds are inspired by the use of inducing variables in sparse Gaussian process approximations. The authors then demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference paradigms: a computationally efficient but approximate variational inference (VI) framework, and a more costly but asymptotically exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) scheme using retrospective sampling.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The work tackles a well-known and important bottleneck in learning DPPs. Bypassing the need for spectral knowledge makes DPP inference more scalable and applicable to a wider range of kernels where eigendecomposition is not analytically or computationally feasible.
2.  **Originality:** The application of inducing-point-based bounds, adapted from the Gaussian process literature, to the DPP likelihood is a novel and clever idea. The extension of these bounds from the finite to the continuous case is a solid technical contribution.
3.  **Utility:** The paper provides two distinct inference methods (VI and MCMC), offering a practical trade-off between computational cost and accuracy. This flexibility is valuable for practitioners with different computational budgets and modeling needs.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

The core ideas of the paper are promising, but the method description and particularly the experimental evaluation could be substantially improved to provide a clearer picture of the methods' practical performance and limitations.

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Comparison:** The experiments demonstrate that the methods work, but they lack a comparative analysis.
    *   How do the proposed VI and MCMC methods compare against each other in terms of computational complexity, wall-clock time, and the quality of the parameter estimates?
    *   How do these methods compare to relevant existing alternatives, such as the spectral-bound MCMC of Affandi et al. (2014) or Nyström-based approximations? A comparison on both approximation quality and runtime is essential to position the contribution's significance.

2.  **Analysis of Variational Inference:** The paper notes that VI yields "poorly consistent results" on the toy dataset and resorts to MCMC. This is a critical finding that warrants a deeper investigation.
    *   Under what conditions is the proposed variational approach expected to perform well, and when is it likely to fail? An analysis of the properties of the variational lower bound or the optimization landscape could provide valuable insight. Without this, it is difficult for a reader to know when to trust the VI approach.

3.  **Practical Guidance on Inducing Inputs:** The performance of the method relies on the choice of inducing inputs (Z). The paper lacks clear guidance on this crucial aspect.
    *   Is there a recommended heuristic for selecting the number of inducing points, *m*? The experiments suggest increasing *m* until results stabilize, which is reasonable, but more formal guidance would be helpful.
    *   How should the initial locations of the inducing points be chosen? Are the final results sensitive to this initialization?
    *   In the 1D toy example with 13 data points, what is the performance if the inducing inputs are simply set to be the observed data points? This seems like a natural baseline to explore.

**Minor Comments on Clarity:**

*   The introduction refers to the kernels K and L before they are formally defined. For readers less familiar with the DPP literature, briefly explaining the distinction between the marginal kernel (K) and the L-ensemble kernel (L) at the outset would improve accessibility.
*   The paper repeatedly motivates the desire to learn the more interpretable parameters of kernel K, but then proceeds by parametrizing L. While this choice is well-justified by tractability, some of the introductory discussion on K could be condensed to better focus the reader on the paper's actual contributions regarding inference for L-ensembles.
