Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decompositions. The authors directly parameterize the L-ensemble and derive nonspectral upper and lower bounds on determinants that appear in DPP likelihoods, both for finite domains (det(L+I)) and continuous domains (Fredholm determinant det(I+L)). The bounds are constructed via inducing inputs Z, yielding Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY and a pair of determinant inequalities derived from Seiler–Simon. These bounds underpin two inference schemes: (i) a variational lower bound enabling approximate maximum likelihood, and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler that adaptively tightens acceptance bounds by increasing |Z| and re-optimizing Z. The approach is positioned as an alternative to spectral methods (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2014) and connects to variational sparse GP methods (Titsias, AISTATS 2009), but adapted to DPP determinants rather than GP marginal likelihoods. The continuous-domain extension uses closed-form Ψ integrals for Gaussian base measures and kernels, providing practical bounds for Fredholm determinants without eigen-expansions, complementing numerical determinant quadrature (e.g., Bornemann, 2010).

Strengths
- Novelty: A clean, nonspectral bounding technique for both finite and continuous DPPs that is, to my knowledge, new in the DPP literature and leverages a classical determinant inequality. The extension to continuous DPPs is particularly valuable.
- Generality and simplicity: The approach obviates eigendecompositions, applies with generic kernels, and naturally supports inducing-input optimization.
- Practicality: The retrospective MH scheme is well-motivated and adapts the idea from spectral methods to nonspectral bounds. The variational bound is computationally attractive and scalable when m ≪ n.
- Empirical indications: On the diabetic neuropathy data, the method yields coherent overdispersion parameter estimates across increasing m and reproduces known class differences.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
Quality
- Missing baselines: The paper does not quantitatively compare against exact methods (small n) or established spectral approximations (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2014), nor against non-spectral alternatives like Nyström approximations for finite DPPs. For the continuous case, a comparison to high-accuracy Fredholm determinant quadrature (Bornemann, 2010) would strengthen claims.
- Limited analysis of approximation quality: There is no systematic study of bound tightness as a function of m and Z, nor diagnostics relating the bound gap to inference quality (likelihood error, parameter error).
- Variational failure modes: On the 1D Gaussian–Gaussian example, VI exhibits large parameter errors, yet there is no analysis of when VI should succeed or fail. The identifiability issues for κ when parameterizing L are acknowledged but not addressed methodologically (e.g., priors, reparameterizations, constraints).

Clarity
- Exposition is uneven in places. K and L are used early without a succinct primer; a short, self-contained description of marginal kernels vs L-ensembles and their relation would help non-experts.
- Some repetition and meandering discussion (e.g., about preferring K vs L) could be trimmed since the paper ultimately adopts an L-ensemble parameterization.
- Notation and equation numbering appear inconsistent in a few places; check for typos and ensure all assumptions for the continuous case (trace-class, integrability) are stated once and clearly with examples.

Originality
- The determinant bounds and their use for DPP inference without spectral knowledge appear original. The link to sparse GP inducing methods is well placed but the adaptation to DPP likelihoods is nontrivial.

Significance
- If validated more thoroughly, these methods could broaden applicability of DPPs to larger finite problems and continuous domains where spectra are unavailable. The retrospective MH adaptation is a practically useful contribution.

Specific requests and questions to improve the paper
1) Complexity and timings: Please provide a clear complexity analysis and wall-clock comparisons for VI and MH. For finite DPPs, detail per-iteration costs and memory: O(n m^2) for computing tr(L−Q) and log det(Q+I), plus O(m^3) for LZ operations; for MH, include the typical number of Z refinements per acceptance decision.
2) Baselines: 
- Finite case: compare against exact determinants for small n and against spectral methods (e.g., top-m eigenvalues with power iterations) with matched accuracy, reporting run time and parameter errors.
- Continuous case: compare bound-based MH/VI to a high-precision determinant obtained via Fredholm quadrature (Bornemann) where feasible.
3) VI behavior: Analyze when VI is expected to work (e.g., well-identified length scales, informative data, moderate κ) and when it may fail (weakly identified cardinality parameters, strong posterior correlations). Consider reparameterizing L in terms of expected cardinality and repulsion scales, and using informative priors on κ to address identifiability.
4) Inducing inputs: Report results when Z is set to the observed points (finite case) or drawn from the base measure (continuous case), and compare to optimized Z. Include an ablation showing the effect of Z initialization (k-means, maximin, base-measure sampling) and of not optimizing Z.
5) Guidance on choosing m and Z: Offer a rule of thumb based on the bound gap (upper–lower difference) and target accuracy for acceptance decisions. For example, increase m until the gap falls below a tolerance relative to log-likelihood scale; initialize Z via k-means or maximin over the base measure and refine by gradient-based optimization. Empirically characterize how bound gaps decrease with m on several datasets.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Conceptually elegant, nonspectral bounds applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Practical inference recipes (VI and retrospective MH) with promising empirical behavior.
- Potentially impactful for large-scale and continuous-domain DPP applications.

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks strong baselines and systematic accuracy/runtime analyses.
- Unclear guidance on when VI is reliable; identifiability issues insufficiently addressed.
- Exposition needs tightening and a clearer introduction to K vs L.

Overall assessment
This is a promising and original contribution that could meaningfully advance practical DPP inference. I lean toward acceptance contingent on significantly strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying the methodology and presentation as suggested above. Even if some experiments must remain modest due to space, targeted comparisons and diagnostic analyses would greatly increase confidence in the approach and its recommended use.
