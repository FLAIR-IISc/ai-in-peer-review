- Core challenge: DPP likelihoods have an intractable normalizing constant that normally requires spectral decompositions, limiting scalability.  
- Main contribution: novel nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood using pseudo-inputs (inspired by sparse GPs) to avoid spectral computation.  
- Inference methods: enables efficient variational inference on finite domains and exact retrospective MCMC without spectral approximations.  
- Theoretical soundness: Propositions 1 and 2 are rigorously derived using determinant inequalities.  
- Computational advantage: finite-domain variational inference reduces cost (e.g., O(n m^2) vs. O(n^3) for eigendecomposition).  
- Continuous extension: approach generalized to continuous DPPs (using Fredholm determinants), transferring sparse GP ideas to this setting.  
- Empirical validation: experiments, including a diabetic neuropathy case, show convergence of metrics as pseudo-inputs increase and practical utility.  
- Clarity and presentation: well-organized paper with helpful figures and supplementary technical details; only minor clarifications suggested.  
- Originality: significant advance over prior spectral-bound methods, enabling inference where spectral approaches fail.  
- Significance: makes likelihood-based DPP inference practical for larger/continuous problems and may apply to other determinant-based likelihoods.  
- Identifiability/parametrization: authors deliberately choose L-parametrization for tractability, acknowledging loss of interpretability and poor identification of κ.  
- Minor weaknesses: limited discussion of pseudo-input initialization/optimization and continuous-domain experiments rely on kernels with known spectra.  
- Acceptance pros: novel nonspectral bounds, scalability, rigorous theory, strong experiments, clear relation to prior work.  
- Acceptance cons: interpretability trade-off (L-parametrization), dense continuous-domain technicalities, lack of very large-scale (n>1e4) experiments.  
- Recommendation: strong recommendation for acceptance—high-impact, technically sound, and well-validated work.  
- Draft correction: reviewer corrects earlier mistake—paper explicitly chooses L-parametrization and discusses its trade-offs, so the critique in the draft was invalid.
