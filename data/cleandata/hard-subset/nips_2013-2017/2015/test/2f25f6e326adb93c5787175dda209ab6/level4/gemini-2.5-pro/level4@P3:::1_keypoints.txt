1. Paper proposes non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihoods using a determinantal inequality, avoiding costly eigenvalue computations.  
2. Introduces a retrospective MCMC sampler that sidesteps eigenvalue calculations.  
3. Presents the first variational inference framework for DPP parameter learning.  
4. Method applies to both finite and continuous-domain DPPs and is validated on synthetic and real data.  
5. Core idea is novel, technically strong, and well-written.  
6. Non-spectral bounding technique is clever and avoids storing large kernel matrices and power iterations.  
7. Enables scalable, approximate inference complementary to exact MCMC methods; demonstrated on a real dataset.  
8. Insightful connection to inducing-point methods in sparse Gaussian processes, improving interpretability.  
9. General framework broadens applicability across DPP settings.  
10. Concern: computational trade-offs unclear—optimizing pseudo-input locations may become costly as m grows versus spectral methods.  
11. Request for empirical or theoretical comparison on when the optimization approach outperforms spectral methods (e.g., dependence on spectral gap, bound tightness).  
12. Critical concern: MCMC retrospective sampler may stall if optimization of pseudo-inputs is non-convex and yields poor local optima, undermining the "provably-correct" claim.  
13. Asks for discussion of guarantees or practical mitigations (initialization, restarts) and empirical behavior of the sampler/optimization in experiments.  
14. Paper is exceptionally clear; notation and exposition are strong.  
15. Minor fixes: add axis labels to Figure 1, include a brief proof sketch of Proposition 1 in the main text, and correct a typo on Page 7, Line 361.  
16. Significance: substantial advance for scalable DPP inference likely to be influential.  
17. Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the raised computational and robustness concerns.
