Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes nonspectral, inducing-point-based bounds on the normalizing constants that appear in DPP likelihoods, for both finite and continuous domains. By introducing a set of pseudo-inputs Z and defining a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, the authors derive tractable upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) (finite case) and on the Fredholm determinant (continuous case). These bounds enable two inference strategies: (i) a variational approach that maximizes a lower bound on the log-likelihood jointly over kernel parameters and inducing inputs; and (ii) an “exact” MCMC scheme using retrospective acceptance tests driven by the bounds. The work draws inspiration from sparse GP methodology (e.g., variational inducing point methods) and sidesteps the need for eigenvalue computations required by prior DPP inference methods, notably spectral/Nyström approaches and eigenvalue-truncation-based samplers (e.g., Affandi et al., Gillenwater et al., Mariet & Sra). For continuous DPPs, the paper gives analogous bounds under trace-class assumptions and demonstrates applicability for Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The determinant inequalities and their adaptation to DPP likelihoods appear technically sound, with proofs deferred to the supplement. The retrospective MH scheme is a principled way to obtain exact inference conditional on resolving accept/reject decisions; however, the main text should more clearly state the conditions under which decisions are almost surely resolved and whether any stopping criterion could introduce bias. For the continuous case, assumptions (trace class, integrability) are stated, but guidance on when Ψ is available in closed form beyond Gaussian–Gaussian settings would strengthen the contribution. Empirical evaluation is informative but limited in scope and comparative breadth.
- Clarity: The exposition would benefit from defining K (marginal kernel) and L (L-ensemble) up front, clarifying their relationship, and streamlining the discussion about the pros/cons of parameterizing K versus L. Notation is mostly consistent, though some reminders (e.g., submatrix indices, measure assumptions) would aid readability. Providing explicit computational complexity and memory costs for all steps (finite and continuous settings) would improve transparency.
- Originality: Using inducing-point-based determinant bounds to avoid spectral decompositions in DPP inference is novel and conceptually appealing, bridging sparse GP ideas with DPPs in both discrete and continuous domains.
- Significance: If broadly applicable, the bounds could make likelihood-based DPP inference more practical, especially for continuous domains where spectra are unavailable. That said, the current experiments (a 1D toy with known spectrum and a moderate-size real dataset) are not sufficient to fully establish scalability, robustness, and comparative performance versus strong baselines.

Strengths:
- Simple, general, and computationally attractive bounds that avoid eigenvalue computations.
- Unified treatment of finite and continuous DPPs, with clear connections to variational sparse GPs.
- Retrospective MH adaptation that leverages the bounds for exact posterior sampling.
- Insightful discussion about identifiability when parameterizing L.

Weaknesses and questions for the authors:
- Complexity and runtime:
  - Please provide explicit asymptotic time and space complexity for evaluating the bounds, their gradients, and one iteration of VI and MH (including Z-optimization), as functions of n, m, and T (number of realizations).
  - Include wall-clock times and memory footprints for all experiments, and breakdowns of time spent in bound evaluation vs. Z-optimization.
- Comparisons:
  - Add comparisons with spectral/Nyström-based methods (e.g., Affandi et al.) and parameter-learning algorithms for L-ensembles (EM, fixed-point), reporting approximation quality (log-likelihood gap, predictive tasks) and runtime.
  - For the finite case, compare against truncated-eigenvalue baselines on problems where spectra are available.
- Variational inference behavior:
  - In the 1D toy, VI shows large parameter errors while MH succeeds. Please characterize when VI is expected to perform well or fail (e.g., identifiability of κ, coupling of scale/length parameters, tightness of bounds as a function of m, curvature issues).
  - Report the empirical gap between the variational bound and the true log-likelihood (where available), and how it scales with m and Z-optimization.
- Retrospective MH details:
  - Quantify how often m must be increased to resolve accept/reject decisions, the typical final m, and the cost per iteration. Provide acceptance rates and effective sample sizes.
  - Clarify the theoretical guarantee that the decision is resolved almost surely (or specify conditions). Confirm that no early stopping introduces bias.
- Inducing inputs:
  - Evaluate the case where Z equals the observed points in the toy example (and perhaps in the finite case more broadly). How does this compare to optimized Z and Nyström-style choices?
  - Provide practical guidance for selecting m and initializing Z (e.g., k-means, sampling from the base measure, using marginals if available). Offer a stopping rule (increase m until upper–lower bound gap or parameter estimates stabilize).
  - Discuss the risk of overfitting via Z-optimization and whether regularization or priors over Z help.
- Continuous case:
  - Beyond Gaussian–Gaussian settings, when is Ψ tractable? Can quadrature-based approximations be used without losing the nonspectral advantage? Empirically compare against Bornemann-style Fredholm quadrature where applicable.

Minor comments:
- Define K and L (marginal kernel versus L-ensemble) in the introduction, with a brief explanation of their relationship and implications for interpretability/constraints.
- The discussion on the benefits of learning K directly could be shorter, since the paper ultimately parameterizes L; focus on how your approach mitigates the trade-offs.
- State the determinant inequality used and any conditions (PSD, trace-class) in the main text for reader orientation.
- Please proofread for duplicated sentences and minor typos.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean, broadly applicable bounds that remove a major practical bottleneck (spectral computation).
- Extension to continuous domains with concrete examples.
- Potentially impactful for practitioners seeking scalable DPP inference.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited empirical evaluation, with few datasets and no head-to-head comparisons against strong baselines.
- Insufficient guidance on computational trade-offs, parameter regimes where VI works, and practical selection of inducing points.
- Some clarity gaps in the exposition and algorithmic details (especially for retrospective MH and continuous-case assumptions).

Overall: Promising and original contribution with practical potential. With stronger empirical comparisons, clearer complexity/runtime reporting, and more guidance on using the method in practice, the paper would be significantly strengthened.
