

The paper presents a valuable contribution by introducing approximate inference techniques for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid requiring spectral decomposition of the kernel. Instead, it leverages inducing inputs to bound the likelihood, significantly reducing computational costs. Both variational inference and approximate MCMC methods are proposed and empirically validated. While the core idea holds promise, the paper requires substantial refinement to strengthen its technical and empirical foundations.

The method description lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. Crucially, the authors do not clarify the computational complexity or runtime trade-offs between the two proposed approaches. A comparative analysis of their time/space requirements—particularly regarding the number of inducing points—would be essential for practitioners. Additionally, the empirical evaluation fails to adequately benchmark against relevant alternatives. The authors should compare their methods not only to exact DPP inference (where feasible) but also to established approximate techniques (e.g., greedy sampling, Nyström approximations), reporting both approximation quality (e.g., log-likelihood, diversity metrics) and computational efficiency.

Section 5.1’s toy example reveals a critical limitation: variational inference yields large parameter estimation errors, necessitating a switch to approximate MCMC. The paper does not address *when* variational inference is expected to succeed or fail, nor does it explain *why* it fails in this specific case. This omission undermines the method’s claimed practicality. The authors should provide theoretical or empirical insights into the conditions under which variational inference is valid (e.g., kernel properties, inducing point density) and identify its failure modes.

The toy experiment uses a 13-point sample, but the paper does not test whether the inducing inputs themselves could be these sampled points—a natural baseline. This gap prevents assessing the method’s sensitivity to inducing point selection. Furthermore, the authors offer no practical guidance for hyperparameter choices: there is no discussion of (a) how to select the optimal number of inducing points, or (b) strategies for initializing them (e.g., k-means, random, or kernel-based placement). Such rules of thumb are vital for real-world application.

Minor but important issues require correction. The introduction references matrices **K** and **L** without defining them (as the marginal kernel and Cholesky factor, respectively). Briefly clarifying these terms would improve accessibility. Additionally, the paper repeatedly emphasizes the advantage of directly learning marginal kernel parameters—a point that is never implemented in the proposed framework. This disconnect should be removed to avoid confusion.

In summary, while the core idea of kernel-free DPP inference via inducing inputs is promising, the current manuscript lacks the technical depth and empirical rigor expected for publication. The method description is insufficiently detailed, the evaluation is incomplete (missing key comparisons and failure analysis), and critical implementation guidance is absent. The authors should address all points above before the paper can be considered for acceptance. I recommend rejection pending substantial revisions that resolve these fundamental concerns.
