

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the intractable normalizing constant that requires spectral decomposition. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that eliminate the need for spectral approximations, enabling more efficient variational inference and MCMC methods. This work builds on prior NIPS contributions like Affandi et al. (2013) and Gillenwater et al. (2012), which relied on spectral approximations for DPP inference, and extends them to both finite and continuous domains without requiring spectral knowledge.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality with rigorous bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) supported by detailed proofs in supplementary material. The authors carefully address the trade-off between interpretability (parametrizing K) and tractability (parametrizing L), acknowledging this limitation while providing a practical solution. The experimental validation on synthetic and real diabetic neuropathy datasets effectively demonstrates the approach's utility, showing convergence of overdispersion measures as inducing variables increase.

The clarity of the presentation is excellent, with well-structured sections that make the technical content accessible. The paper appropriately cites relevant NIPS work (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar 2012, Affandi et al. 2013) and clearly distinguishes their contribution from prior art. The continuous DPP extension (Section 4) is particularly significant, as most prior work focused on finite domains.

Originality is strong: the nonspectral bounds represent a clear advancement over spectral approximation methods, with the key innovation being the use of pseudo-inputs to avoid spectral decomposition. This is a meaningful contribution to the DPP literature, as it addresses a persistent computational bottleneck.

Significance is high: the proposed methods make likelihood-based DPP inference feasible for larger datasets, potentially enabling broader applications in areas like recommendation systems (Kulesza & Taskar 2012) and spatial statistics (Lavancier et al. 2015). The paper demonstrates this through practical experiments on real-world data.

Pro: Novel nonspectral bounds, strong theoretical foundation, clear presentation, practical validation on real datasets, addresses a significant bottleneck in DPP inference.

Con: The paper acknowledges the interpretability trade-off with parametrizing L instead of K, which remains a limitation for some applications. The continuous DPP section requires some background in operator theory.

Overall, this is a high-quality, significant contribution that advances the state of the art in DPP inference with practical implications for machine learning practitioners. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a genuine challenge in the field.
