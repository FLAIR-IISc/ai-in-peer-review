- Problem: DPP likelihood inference is hindered by an intractable normalizing constant that normally requires spectral decomposition of the kernel.  
- Contribution: Derives cheap non-spectral bounds on the normalizing constant using pseudo-inputs, avoiding spectral knowledge.  
- Methods: Applies inducing-point-style bounds for finite DPPs and extends them to Fredholm determinants for continuous DPPs.  
- Inference: Enables both variational inference (fast approximate) and retrospective MCMC (exact) without spectral decompositions.  
- Optimization: Bounds can be tightened by optimizing pseudo-input locations, which is crucial for accurate inference.  
- Computational gain: Variational approach runs in O(n m^2) versus O(n^3) for direct determinant evaluation; MCMC avoids power iterations.  
- Experiments: Synthetic Gaussian experiment shows parameter recovery except for non-identifiable scale κ; diabetic neuropathy dataset shows convergence of overdispersion as m increases.  
- Theoretical support: Clear mathematical derivations with proofs in supplementary material; connection to sparse GP methods is well-explained.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written and organized, accessible to readers familiar with Gaussian processes.  
- Originality: Novel non-spectral bounding approach for DPPs and novel extension to continuous domains.  
- Significance: Addresses a practical scalability bottleneck in DPP inference, making likelihood-based methods feasible for larger datasets.  
- Strength: Comprehensive experimental validation on synthetic and real data demonstrating practical efficiency.  
- Strength: Careful discussion of limitations, including identifiability and interpretability trade-offs when parametrizing L.  
- Weakness: Limited exploration of the trade-off between number of pseudo-inputs (m), computational cost, and accuracy.  
- Weakness: Parametrizing L instead of K reduces interpretability and is not fully resolved.  
- Weakness: Theoretical analysis of convergence of bounds as m increases could be more detailed.  
- Pro acceptance: Solves a well-known problem with a practical, novel approach and demonstrates clear computational advantages.  
- Con acceptance: Interpretability issues from parametrizing L and incomplete exploration of m vs. accuracy trade-offs may limit applicability in some scenarios.
