### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the significant computational challenge of likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The main bottleneck is the evaluation of a normalizing constant, which takes the form of `det(L + I)` for DPPs on finite sets and a Fredholm determinant for continuous domains. Computing this term typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix or operator `L`, which is often intractable.

The authors' main contribution is a set of novel upper and lower bounds on this determinant term that, crucially, do not require any spectral information. The approach is inspired by sparse approximations in Gaussian processes (GPs), specifically the use of inducing variables [11]. By introducing a small set of `m` "pseudo-inputs," they construct a low-rank approximation `Q` to the kernel matrix `L` and derive bounds based on this approximation. This method is developed for both finite and continuous DPPs.

These non-spectral bounds are then leveraged to develop two inference schemes:
1.  A fast, approximate variational inference (VI) method where the derived lower bound on the log-likelihood is maximized jointly with respect to the kernel parameters and the locations of the pseudo-inputs.
2.  An asymptotically exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference method using the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm. The bounds are used within a "retrospective sampling" framework [17], similar to Affandi et al. [7], to make acceptance decisions without computing the exact likelihood, by refining the bounds as needed.

The paper validates these methods on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP (where the exact likelihood is computable) and a real-world spatial point process dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) are the core theoretical contribution and appear sound, though the proofs are in the supplementary material. The work is complete, presenting the theory, the resulting algorithms (VI and MCMC), and solid experimental validation. The authors are honest about the limitations of their work, for instance, by reporting the poor performance of the VI method on the toy problem and correctly attributing it to parameter identifiability issues inherent in the direct parametrization of the `L` kernel—an issue that affects the broader field. The experimental design is strong, especially the use of a toy problem with a known ground truth to validate the MCMC sampler's correctness.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very clearly written and well-structured. Introducing the concepts first for the simpler finite case (Section 2) before moving to the more technical continuous case (Section 4) is an excellent pedagogical choice that makes the paper accessible. The connection to the well-known inducing point methods for sparse GPs is explained well and provides helpful intuition for readers familiar with that area. Figures are clear and support the experimental conclusions effectively. The description of how the bounds are integrated into VI and MCMC frameworks is also easy to follow. A minor point for improvement would be to include a brief intuitive sketch of the proof for the bounds in the main text, as relying solely on a citation to a "nontrivial inequality" [14] is somewhat opaque.

**Originality:**
The core idea of non-spectral bounds for the DPP likelihood is novel and significant. Previous work on scalable DPP inference, such as [7], still relied on computing a partial spectrum, which is a major bottleneck that this work successfully removes. While the algorithmic components are inspired by existing work—inducing points from sparse GPs [11] and retrospective sampling for MCMC from [7]—their application to derive and use *non-spectral* bounds for DPPs is a novel and clever combination. The extension of this framework from the finite to the continuous setting is also a non-trivial and valuable contribution.

**Significance:**
The results presented in this paper are highly significant for the machine learning community using DPPs. The intractability of the normalizing constant has been a primary barrier to the widespread application of DPPs, especially in large-scale or continuous-domain problems. By providing a method that bypasses expensive eigendecompositions, this work makes DPP inference much more scalable and practical. The variational approach, despite its limitations in one experiment, offers a compelling speed/accuracy trade-off for large datasets, as demonstrated on the neuropathy data. The exact MCMC method provides a robust alternative when higher accuracy is needed. Researchers and practitioners are likely to adopt these methods, as they represent a substantial improvement in efficiency over the previous state-of-the-art. The work clearly advances the field of probabilistic modeling with DPPs.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Impact:** The paper introduces the first non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihood inference, solving a key computational bottleneck and significantly improving scalability compared to prior art [7].
2.  **Generality:** The proposed framework applies to both finite and continuous DPPs, broadening the scope of practical DPP modeling.
3.  **Completeness:** The paper provides a full suite of tools: sound theory, practical approximate (VI) and exact (MCMC) algorithms, and thorough experimental validation.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand, making a technical contribution accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Robustness of VI:** The variational method showed poor performance on the toy problem. While the authors provide a reasonable explanation (parameter identifiability), this might suggest the VI approach is not universally robust, and the paper could benefit from a deeper analysis of when it is expected to work well.
2.  **Borrowed Concepts:** The high-level ideas of using inducing points and retrospective sampling are not new. The main novelty is in their specific application and the derivation of the resulting bounds for DPPs. This is a minor point, however, as the combination is potent and non-obvious.
3.  **Optimization of Pseudo-inputs:** The performance of the method relies on optimizing the locations of the pseudo-inputs `Z`. The practical challenges of this nested optimization (e.g., local minima, computational cost) are not discussed in depth.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and practical contribution to the literature on determinantal point processes. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the importance of the core ideas. The proposed non-spectral bounds and the associated inference algorithms represent a clear advance over previous methods, making DPPs a more viable tool for large-scale machine learning problems. I strongly recommend acceptance.
