- Problem: Likelihood-based inference for DPPs is bottlenecked by computing the normalizing constant det(L+I) or a Fredholm determinant, which normally requires expensive spectral decomposition.
- Main contribution: Novel upper and lower bounds on the determinant that avoid any spectral information by using inducing-point-style low-rank approximations Q.
- Methodology: Introduce m pseudo-inputs to build Q and derive bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Inference algorithms: Present a fast approximate variational inference (VI) maximizing a lower bound jointly over kernel parameters and pseudo-inputs.
- Inference algorithms: Present an asymptotically exact MCMC using Metropolis–Hastings with retrospective sampling that refines bounds to make acceptance decisions without full likelihoods.
- Experiments: Validate methods on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP with known likelihood and on a real diabetic neuropathy spatial point dataset.
- Quality: High technical quality; core theoretical results (Propositions 1 and 2) appear sound, with complete algorithmic development and experiments.
- Honesty: Authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., VI poor performance on toy problem) and correctly attribute some issues to identifiability in L parametrization.
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, pedagogically structured (finite case first), and explains connection to inducing points in sparse GPs clearly.
- Suggestion: Include an intuitive sketch of the proofs in the main text rather than relying solely on references to nontrivial inequalities.
- Originality: First non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihoods; novel and valuable extension from finite to continuous settings.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on inducing points and retrospective sampling ideas, but removes the need for partial eigendecompositions used in prior scalable DPP methods.
- Significance: Substantially improves scalability of DPP inference, making DPPs more practical for large-scale and continuous-domain problems.
- VI tradeoff: Variational approach offers a useful speed/accuracy trade-off for large datasets despite limitations in some settings.
- MCMC robustness: Exact MCMC provides a reliable alternative when high accuracy is required.
- Argument for acceptance: Novelty/impact, generality across finite and continuous DPPs, completeness of theory+algorithms+experiments, and clarity.
- Argument against acceptance: VI robustness concerns, reuse of high-level ideas (inducing points, retrospective sampling), and insufficient discussion of practical optimization issues for pseudo-input locations.
- Conclusion: Overall excellent paper with minor weaknesses; strong recommendation for acceptance.
