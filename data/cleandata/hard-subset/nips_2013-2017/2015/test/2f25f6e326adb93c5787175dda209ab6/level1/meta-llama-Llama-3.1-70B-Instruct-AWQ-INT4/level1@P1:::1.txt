The paper "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge" presents a novel approach to inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that does not require spectral knowledge. The authors derive bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that can be used for variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to DPPs and their applications.

The main contribution of the paper is the derivation of bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that do not require spectral knowledge. The authors show that these bounds can be used for variational inference and MCMC methods, which are commonly used in machine learning and statistics. The bounds are based on a non-trivial inequality on determinants, and the authors provide a clear and concise proof of this inequality.

The paper also presents experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors apply their method to a toy Gaussian continuous experiment and a real-world dataset of spatial patterns of nerve fibers in diabetic patients. The results show that the proposed approach can be used to infer the parameters of a DPP and to perform approximate maximum likelihood training.
