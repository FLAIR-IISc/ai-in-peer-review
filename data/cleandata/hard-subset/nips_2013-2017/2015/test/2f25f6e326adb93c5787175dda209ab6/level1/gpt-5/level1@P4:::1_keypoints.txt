- Addresses likelihood-based inference for DPPs without spectral decompositions by parametrizing the L kernel directly.
- Main technical contribution: nonspectral upper and lower bounds on the normalizing constant using an inducing-point Nyström-like approximation Q for finite domains.
- Extends analogous determinant bounds to continuous domains via m×m matrices LZ and Ψ, thereby bounding the Fredholm determinant.
- Bounds rely on determinant inequalities (Seiler–Simon) and avoid eigenvalue computations.
- Proposes two inference schemes: (i) a variational lower bound jointly optimizing kernel parameters and inducing locations Z (O(n m^2) when m≪n); (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings that adaptively increases m to bracket acceptance probabilities.
- Approach is inspired by sparse variational Gaussian processes and contrasts with prior spectral/Nyström DPP methods.
- Experiments: (a) 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP validating retrospective MH against exact Fredholm determinant; (b) 2D diabetic neuropathy data showing convergent variational estimates and class separation.
- Technical soundness: bounds are well-founded and complexity claims are reasonable; continuous-case assumptions (trace-class, computable Ψ) are discussed but limit applicability.
- Proofs are deferred to the supplement; tighter conditions, error characterizations, and a main-text proof sketch would improve confidence.
- No formal rates or guarantees on bound tightness or convergence as m increases are provided.
- Clarity: generally well organized with helpful GP connections, but contains minor typos and needs more explicit computational details for reproducibility.
- Originality: novel nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods and a new application of retrospective MH in this context.
- Significance: provides a practical alternative to spectral methods, enabling tunable-accuracy variational inference and adaptive exact inference for continuous or large domains.
- Strengths: general nonspectral bounds, clear link to inducing-point GP methodology, principled retrospective MH, validated on synthetic and real data.
- Weaknesses: limited empirical scope (no large-scale finite-domain benchmarks or runtime/accuracy comparisons), no theoretical tightness guarantees, dependence on Ψ computability, and variational identifiability/instability issues.
- Suggestions: add spectral-baseline comparisons and scalability experiments; ablations on m and Z initialization; provide proof sketch and precise determinant-inequality conditions; discuss k-DPPs and numerical Ψ computation with error control.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, removes a key bottleneck in DPP learning, methodologically clean with promising validation.
- Arguments against acceptance: narrow empirical evaluation, lack of theoretical guarantees on tightness and inducing-point selection, and presentation issues.
- Overall: solid and original contribution with clear potential impact that would be strengthened by broader experiments and guidance on inducing-point selection.
