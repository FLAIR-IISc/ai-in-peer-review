Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decompositions of the kernel operator. DPP likelihoods involve determinants of L+I (finite case) or the Fredholm determinant det(I+L) (continuous case), where L is a positive definite kernel related to the more interpretable marginal kernel K. Following common ML practice, the paper parameterizes L directly to avoid the spectral constraints on K.

The main technical contribution is a pair of nonspectral bounds on the normalizing constant: for finite domains, upper and lower bounds on 1/det(I+L) constructed via an inducing-point Nyström-like approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY (with Z not constrained to be a subset of Y), yielding an upper bound 1/det(Q+I) and a lower bound multiplied by exp(−tr(L−Q)). For continuous domains, analogous bounds are given in terms of the m×m matrices LZ and LZ+Ψ (with Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x)), thus bounding the Fredholm determinant. These bounds rely on determinant inequalities (Seiler–Simon) and avoid eigenvalue computations.

The bounds are used in two inference schemes: (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood that can be maximized jointly over kernel parameters and inducing locations Z, with O(n m^2) scaling when m ≪ n; and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis-Hastings scheme that uses lower/upper likelihood bounds to bracket the acceptance probability and adaptively increases m until the decision is determined. The approach is inspired by sparse variational Gaussian processes (Titsias AISTATS 2009/2010) and contrasts with prior DPP methods that require eigendecompositions or spectral truncations (e.g., Affandi et al. ICML 2013; Affandi et al. AISTATS 2013 for Nyström; Gillenwater et al. NIPS 2014; Mariet & Sra NIPS 2015).

Experiments include: (a) a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian continuous DPP with known spectrum, validating that retrospective MH matches an “ideal” MH using the exact Fredholm determinant; and (b) a real 2D spatial dataset (diabetic neuropathy), where the variational method with increasing m gives convergent estimates of overdispersion measures and separates two clinical classes, in line with prior work that used spectral approximations.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The finite- and continuous-domain bounds are clearly stated and grounded in known determinant inequalities. Using inducing points to construct Q mirrors well-understood GP approximations, and the resulting complexity claims (O(n m^2) for key terms, O(n m) memory to store cross-covariances) are reasonable.
- The variational lower bound is coherent and directly usable; the retrospective MH construction is standard once reliable bounds are available.
- Assumptions for the continuous case (trace-class operator, existence of tr(L) via integrability of L(x,x), computability of Ψ) are discussed and satisfied for Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures; however, practical computability may be restricted to kernels/measures allowing analytic or efficiently approximable Ψ.
- Proofs are deferred to the supplement; correctness appears plausible, but tighter conditions, error characterizations, and a brief sketch in the main text would improve confidence.
- No formal rates on bound tightness or convergence as m increases are provided; the paper notes intuition via GP posterior variance but lacks guarantees.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized, with a clean separation between finite and continuous settings. Connections to sparse GP literature are helpful.
- Some equation numbering and minor referencing typos appear; careful proofreading is needed. More explicit computational details (e.g., how tr(Q) is computed efficiently and memory footprints) would aid reproducibility.
- The discussion of identifiability (e.g., κ in Gaussian–Gaussian DPPs) is clear and properly caveated.

Originality:
- Introducing nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods via inducing-point constructions and extending them to continuous DPPs is novel relative to prior work that relies on eigenspectra or Nyström approximations tied to spectral truncations.
- The adaptation of retrospective MH to these bounds is natural but, to our knowledge, new in the context of nonspectral DPP inference.

Significance:
- The method offers a practical alternative to spectral methods, particularly useful on continuous domains or large finite domains where eigendecompositions are infeasible.
- Variational inference becomes accessible with tunable accuracy via m, and the retrospective MH provides a path to exact inference with adaptive cost.
- Experimental evidence supports feasibility and correctness; however, broader empirical evaluation would strengthen claims about scalability and advantages over spectral approaches.

Strengths:
- Clear, general bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs that avoid spectral computations.
- Conceptually elegant link to inducing-point GP methodology; simple to implement with standard linear algebra.
- Retrospective MH using these bounds is principled and validated on a case with known spectrum.
- Real-data application demonstrates practical utility and convergence behavior as m increases.

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical scope: no large-scale finite-domain benchmarks (e.g., subset selection/summarization) and no head-to-head comparisons in runtime/accuracy vs spectral methods.
- No theoretical tightness guarantees or adaptive schemes for choosing Z beyond heuristic optimization; sensitivity to Z initialization and local optima not analyzed.
- Continuous-case applicability hinges on computing Ψ; guidance for non-Gaussian kernels/measures is limited.
- Variational method showed instability/identifiability issues for some parameters; more discussion on regularization or reparameterization would help.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add comparisons to spectral-bound MH (e.g., Affandi et al. 2013) and to EM/fixed-point methods on finite datasets, reporting accuracy, runtime, and memory.
- Provide ablations on m, Z initialization, and optimization strategies; consider greedy Z selection or inducing-point placement heuristics with guarantees.
- Include a brief proof sketch in the main text and state precise conditions under which the determinant inequalities apply.
- Discuss extensions to k-DPPs (fixed-size) and practical computation of Ψ for broader kernel/measure families; consider numerical quadrature with error control.
- Clarify computational complexity and memory for multi-trajectory (large T) settings and very large n.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, general nonspectral bounds enabling both variational and exact inference for DPPs.
- Addresses an important bottleneck in DPP learning, especially for continuous domains.
- Methodologically clean, with connections to established GP literature.
- Promising empirical validation and practical feasibility.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is relatively narrow, with limited comparisons to strong baselines and limited large-scale demonstrations.
- Lack of theoretical guarantees on bound tightness or inducing-point selection may limit confidence in performance across settings.
- Some clarity and presentation issues remain.

Overall assessment:
A solid and original contribution that removes a key barrier in DPP inference by avoiding spectral computations, with clear potential impact. Strengthening empirical comparisons and providing more guidance on inducing-point selection would further enhance the paper.
