This paper addresses the key computational challenge in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the intractable normalizing constant, `det(L+I)`. The main contribution is a novel set of non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant, applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs. Drawing inspiration from the use of inducing variables in sparse Gaussian Processes (Titsias, 2009), this work circumvents the need for costly eigendecompositions required by previous methods (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014). The authors demonstrate how these bounds facilitate two powerful inference schemes: a cheap, approximate method via variational inference (VI) and an asymptotically exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm using retrospective sampling.

The paper’s strengths are numerous.
**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
1.  **Significance:** The proposed method removes a major bottleneck for DPP inference, potentially making these powerful models for diversity more scalable and widely applicable. The unified treatment of both finite and continuous domains is a particularly strong contribution.
2.  **Originality:** The idea of applying inducing point approximations to bound the DPP likelihood determinant is novel and elegant. It forges a valuable connection between the DPP and sparse GP literature.
3.  **Quality:** The work is technically sound, deriving its bounds from established matrix inequalities. The experimental validation is well-designed; the use of a synthetic problem with a known ground truth to verify the MCMC sampler is convincing, and the real-world neuropathy dataset demonstrates the practical utility and convergence properties of the VI approach as the number of inducing points increases.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The methodology is explained clearly, and the separation of the finite and continuous cases makes the content accessible.

The paper also has some minor weaknesses that could be addressed.
**Con-acceptance arguments:**
1.  The authors report that the VI method yields "poorly consistent results" on the synthetic experiment, attributing this to parameter non-identifiability. While plausible, a more thorough analysis of the VI optimization landscape would be beneficial. Guidance on when to prefer the more expensive MCMC approach would strengthen the paper’s practical impact.
2.  While the computational complexity is favorable, a direct empirical runtime comparison with the spectral-based bounding approach from prior work would better quantify the practical advantages, especially considering the added overhead of optimizing the inducing point locations.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant and original contribution to the machine learning literature on DPPs. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, and the proposed methods are likely to be built upon by future research.
