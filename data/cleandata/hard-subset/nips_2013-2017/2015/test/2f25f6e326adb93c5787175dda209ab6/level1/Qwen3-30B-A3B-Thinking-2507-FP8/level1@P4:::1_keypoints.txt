- Addresses inference challenges for DPPs: intractable spectral decomposition linking L and K, and an intractable normalizing constant (determinant/Fredholm determinant).  
- Main contribution: derives likelihood bounds for finite DPPs using pseudo-inputs (inducing variables) analogous to sparse GPs.  
- Extends those spectral-free likelihood bounds to continuous DPPs with similar determinant and trace-based inequalities.  
- Key bound form (finite): det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L-Q)} ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I), enabling computation without spectral decompositions.  
- Provides methods to use the bounds in variational inference and MCMC, connecting to existing inference paradigms.  
- Theoretical contributions are technically sound and supported by determinant inequalities and proofs.  
- Experiments validate the approach on synthetic and real datasets, including diabetic neuropathy, with clear visualizations.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good background and connections to Gaussian process methods.  
- Originality: avoids spectral decompositions entirely and non-trivially extends results from finite to continuous domains.  
- Significance: makes likelihood-based inference for DPPs more tractable, potentially widening practical applications.  
- Weakness: interpretability trade-off between parametrizing L versus K is acknowledged but not quantified or exemplified.  
- Weakness: computational-complexity claims (e.g., O(nm^2)) lack empirical comparisons showing savings over spectral methods at large scale.  
- Weakness: experimental comparisons are limited, lacking broader baselines beyond referenced spectral methods.  
- Pro: solves an important DPP inference bottleneck with novel, practical, and implementable bounds.  
- Con: technique is incremental over variational GP methods; core methodology is not entirely new, and interpretability issues remain underexplored.  
- Overall recommendation: paper meets criteria for acceptance at NIPS—technically sound, well-written, original in avoiding spectral decompositions, and practically valuable.
