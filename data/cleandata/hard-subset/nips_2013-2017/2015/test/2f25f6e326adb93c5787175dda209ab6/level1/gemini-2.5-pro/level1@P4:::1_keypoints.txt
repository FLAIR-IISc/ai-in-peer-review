- Problem: scalable likelihood-based parameter inference for DPPs is hard because the normalizing constant requires computing det(L+I) or the Fredholm determinant det(I+L).
- Prior work: spectral bounds rely on top eigenvalues, which are computationally expensive and problematic in continuous domains.
- Main contribution: novel non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the DPP log-likelihood using m inducing (pseudo-)inputs and a low-rank approximation Q.
- Complexity: the bounds can be computed in O(n m^2) for finite DPPs without any eigenvalue computations.
- Extension: the bounding technique is rigorously extended from finite matrix determinants to continuous DPPs (Fredholm determinants).
- Variational inference: use the lower bound as an objective, jointly optimizing kernel parameters and pseudo-input locations.
- Exact inference: embed the bounds in a Metropolis-Hastings algorithm with retrospective sampling to obtain exact accept/reject decisions by tightening bounds.
- Technical soundness: derivations are based on a known matrix inequality and appear correct; extension to continuous case is non-trivial.
- Clarity: paper is very well written, logically structured, and experimentally supported on a real-world dataset.
- Originality: applying inducing-point-based bounds (inspired by sparse GPs) to DPP likelihoods and continuous DPPs is novel.
- Significance: removes the need for spectral decompositions, enabling scalable DPP inference for large-scale and continuous problems; provides both fast approximate and exact inference options.
- Weakness: parametrizing kernel L directly (instead of K) reduces interpretability and leads to identifiability issues (e.g., scaling κ).
- Weakness: variational inference showed poor robustness and inconsistent results on a toy problem, indicating optimization difficulties when jointly learning parameters and pseudo-inputs.
- Acceptance summary: pros — novel, efficient, extends to continuous DPPs, clear writing, provides VI and MCMC; cons — VI robustness issues and interpretability/identifiability from L-parametrization.
