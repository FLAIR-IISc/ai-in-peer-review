

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the intractable normalizing constant in the likelihood, which involves a determinant (finite case) or Fredholm determinant (continuous case). The authors propose a novel approach that avoids spectral decomposition by deriving cheap-to-evaluate bounds on these determinants. Unlike previous work that required approximating the spectrum of a large matrix or operator [7], their bounds depend only on a set of "pseudo-inputs" and can be optimized to tighten the bounds. These bounds enable efficient variational inference and moderately expensive exact MCMC inference for DPPs.

The paper builds on the machine learning literature about DPPs, particularly the work by Kulesza and Taskar [3], Affandi et al. [7], and Gillenwater et al. [8]. While these papers have explored DPP inference, they typically require spectral decomposition for likelihood evaluation, which becomes computationally prohibitive for large datasets. The authors' approach directly parametrizes the kernel L (instead of K), which is common in the ML literature [3, 7] despite sacrificing parameter interpretability. The key innovation is deriving nonspectral bounds that avoid this computational bottleneck.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs for the bounds (provided in supplementary material). The authors carefully address the trade-off between computational efficiency and interpretability by acknowledging the parametrization of L instead of K. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world diabetic neuropathy data demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the approach. The bounds are well-justified through connections to variational Gaussian processes, and the MCMC implementation using retrospective sampling is correctly adapted from [7] to the new bounds. The authors are honest about limitations, such as the parameter identifiability issues in the continuous case.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to validation. The connection to variational Gaussian processes is clearly explained, making the contribution accessible to a broad ML audience. The experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including specific parameter settings and implementation choices. The supplementary material contains the necessary theoretical proofs, enhancing the paper's transparency.

**Originality:** This work makes a clear and significant contribution to DPP inference by removing the need for spectral decomposition. Previous NIPS papers on DPP inference [3, 4, 7, 8] all relied on spectral approximations or other computationally intensive methods for likelihood evaluation. The authors' bounds are novel, cheap to evaluate, and can be optimized to tighten the bounds. The extension to continuous DPPs (Section 4) is particularly valuable, as it broadens the applicability to spatial statistics problems like the forest example in [4].

**Significance:** The work addresses a practical bottleneck in DPP applications, making likelihood-based inference feasible for larger datasets. The computational efficiency (O(nm²) vs. O(n³) for eigendecomposition) could significantly impact real-world applications of DPPs in areas like search engine diversity [7], news summarization [3], and spatial statistics [4]. The method provides a viable alternative to approximate inference methods, with the ability to trade off between computational cost (through the number of pseudo-inputs) and inference accuracy.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Solves a well-defined problem in DPP inference with a clean, efficient solution
- Provides theoretical bounds that are both novel and practical
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on both synthetic and real data
- Well-written with clear connections to related work
- Significantly advances the state of the art in DPP inference

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The parametrization of L instead of K sacrifices parameter interpretability, though this is acknowledged as a common trade-off in the ML literature
- The theoretical guarantees are limited to the bounds themselves, not to the inference methods using these bounds
- The comparison with spectral approximation methods is somewhat limited (though [7] is cited as the main alternative)

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling, particularly for point processes. The bounds are elegant, computationally efficient, and directly address a practical bottleneck in DPP inference. The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and well-written. While the interpretability trade-off is acknowledged, the computational benefits significantly outweigh this limitation for many applications. The results are clearly presented and validated through thorough experiments. This work represents a meaningful step forward in the practical application of DPPs and should be accepted for publication at NIPS.
