- Proposes inference for DPPs without spectral decomposition by deriving novel likelihood bounds for finite and continuous domains, enabling variational inference and MCMC.
- Technically sound with correct derivations and strong use of kernel/operator theory; connects well to variational GP literature.
- Continuous-case treatment is more involved but adequately explained.
- Experimental validation is limited (toy + reused real dataset) but shows the methods are viable and competitive.
- Claim that bounds are “cheap” requires qualification: avoiding spectral decomposition helps, but optimizing pseudo-inputs (Z) inside MCMC can be expensive, especially in high dimensions.
- Presentation is generally clear and well-organized, though Section 4’s operator-theoretic notation is dense and could use more intuitive explanation.
- Core idea and application of determinant bounds to DPP inference are novel; extension to continuous domains is a significant contribution.
- Work is significant for enabling DPPs on larger-scale problems and opens avenues for recommendation, summarization, and spatial modeling applications.
- Strengths include careful discussion of parameter identifiability and limitations of direct L-parametrization (Gaussian–Gaussian example).
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a major bottleneck, provides mathematically sound bounds, enables tractable inference, and presents encouraging experiments.
- Arguments against acceptance: pseudo-input optimization cost, limited experiments, accessibility issues for readers without operator-theory background, and the need to temper “cheap” claims.
- Overall recommendation: accept, with a minor revision to discuss computational complexity of pseudo-input optimization in the MCMC algorithm.
