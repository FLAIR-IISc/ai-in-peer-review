This paper addresses the challenging problem of inference in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decompositions, a common bottleneck in practical applications. The authors provide bounds on the DPP likelihood, for both finite and continuous domains, enabling the use of variational inference and MCMC methods without explicit eigenvalue calculations.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

DPPs are increasingly used to model diversity in various applications (summarization, search, spatial statistics). A core challenge is inference, as the likelihood involves determinants that are computationally expensive to evaluate due to the spectral decomposition requirement. Previous work largely circumvented this by directly parameterizing the kernel *L* instead of the more interpretable kernel *K*, or by employing approximations relying on spectral knowledge (e.g., power iteration, which still scales poorly). This paper takes a different approach, offering bounds on the likelihood that avoid spectral decomposition altogether.  The bounds are inspired by variational GP approximations [11] and extended to DPPs. The contribution builds on work in [7] which also addresses spectral computations in MCMC, but aims to improve efficiency by removing the spectral approximation requirement, and extends to continuous domains – a gap in existing approaches. The connection to Fredholm determinants and operator theory for continuous DPPs is established, referencing foundational work [1, 18].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core contribution – non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood – is genuinely novel.  Avoiding spectral decomposition is a significant practical improvement.
* **Generality:** The paper successfully extends the bounds to both finite and continuous DPPs, broadening its applicability.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The bounds are derived using established mathematical results [14], and the theoretical connection to Gaussian process variational inference is well-articulated.
* **Practical Implications:** The derived bounds enable both efficient variational inference and more feasible MCMC sampling, opening up DPPs to larger datasets and more complex models. The experiments demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed methods.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly explained, and the mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient detail (though some are deferred to supplementary material).
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, including a synthetic Gaussian case and a real-world dataset, provide compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness.  The comparison to previous work [7] is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Interpretability Trade-off:** The paper acknowledges the trade-off between tractability and interpretability when directly parameterizing *L* instead of *K*. While unavoidable, a more in-depth discussion of strategies to mitigate this interpretability loss (beyond simply acknowledging it) would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-Input Optimization:**  While avoiding spectral decomposition, the optimization of pseudo-inputs (*Z*) adds another computational layer.  The paper briefly discusses this but could benefit from a more detailed analysis of its computational complexity and scalability, particularly for large datasets. The choice of optimization algorithm (CMA-ES) for variational inference isn't explicitly justified, and other alternatives might be worth considering.
* **Limited Exploration of Continuous DPPs:**  The presentation of the continuous DPP case feels somewhat rushed compared to the finite case. While the theoretical results are presented, the experimental validation is limited. More comprehensive experiments specifically targeting the continuous DPP setting would strengthen the paper.
* **Dependence on Determinant Inequality:** The performance of the bounds, and thus the entire approach, heavily relies on the quality of the determinant inequality used [14]. A discussion on the tightness of this inequality and its potential impact on the accuracy of the inference methods would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of number of pseudo-inputs (*m*) appears crucial for performance. The paper suggests increasing *m* until results converge, but a more systematic study of the relationship between *m*, computational cost, and accuracy is warranted.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant bottleneck in DPP inference—the computational cost of spectral decomposition.
* The proposed non-spectral bounds are a novel and theoretically sound contribution.
* The method is applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs.
* Empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of pseudo-input optimization needs further investigation.
* The interpretability trade-off could be more thoroughly addressed.
* The experimental validation of the continuous DPP case is limited.
*  Potential sensitivity to the choice of *m* requires further exploration.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant advance in DPP inference. The non-spectral bounds are a valuable contribution that addresses a key practical challenge. While some weaknesses remain (related to computational cost, interpretability, and experimental validation), the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns.  I believe this work is suitable for acceptance to NIPS.

**Confidence:** 8/10. (Highly confident that the work is solid and makes a valuable contribution, but some further investigation of the weaknesses mentioned would be beneficial).
