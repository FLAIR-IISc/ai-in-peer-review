## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses the challenge of performing likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decompositions, a common bottleneck in practice. The authors propose bounds on the DPP likelihood – both for finite and continuous domains – that avoid this spectral computation, enabling cheaper Variational Inference (VI) and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The work is well-motivated, as DPPs are increasingly used in machine learning for modeling diversity, but inference can be computationally prohibitive.

The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature on DPPs, clearly outlining the trade-offs between parametrizing the kernel *K* directly (for interpretability) versus *L* (for tractability). The proposed bounds are derived rigorously, building upon existing results from Gaussian Process literature and extending them to DPPs. The authors thoughtfully consider both finite and continuous DPPs, providing a comprehensive treatment of the problem.  The connection to quadrature-based Fredholm determinant approximations, mentioned as future work, is a promising direction.

A significant strength is the provision of both VI and MCMC approaches leveraging the derived bounds. The experimental validation, though limited to a toy example and a real dataset, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed MCMC method and shows the potential of VI. The comparison with existing methods (where applicable) is helpful, and the discussion of parameter identifiability (specifically regarding the scaling parameter κ) is honest and insightful. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The experimental section feels somewhat underdeveloped. While the results are encouraging, more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets and comparison with more sophisticated DPP inference techniques would strengthen the claims.  The explanation of the continuous DPP case and its mathematical details (specifically section 4) could benefit from more intuitive explanations.  Additionally, the notation is dense, and while mathematically precise, it sometimes hinders readability. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practical limitation in DPP inference – the need for spectral decomposition.
*   Presents theoretically sound and novel bounds on the DPP likelihood.
*   Provides viable inference methods (VI and MCMC) based on these bounds.
*   Demonstrates empirical validity, albeit with limited scope.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation is relatively limited.
*   The mathematical exposition in Section 4 may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with operator theory.
*   The notation is dense and could be improved for readability.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the experimental section could be strengthened, the theoretical novelty and practical implications of avoiding spectral decomposition make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the experimental evaluation.
