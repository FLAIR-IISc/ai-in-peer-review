

# Review: Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the intractable normalizing constant that requires spectral decomposition of the kernel. The authors present a novel approach to derive cheap-to-evaluate bounds on this normalizing constant that do not rely on spectral knowledge, enabling both variational inference and exact MCMC methods for DPPs.

The paper begins by establishing the context of DPPs in machine learning, noting their utility in modeling diversity (e.g., in news summarization [3], search engines [7], and spatial statistics [4]). The authors clearly identify two main challenges in likelihood-based inference: (1) the kernel used in the likelihood (L) is related to the more interpretable kernel (K) through an intractable spectral decomposition, and (2) the normalizing constant (determinant/Fredholm determinant) is computationally expensive to evaluate. Previous work [7] has attempted to address these challenges by approximating the spectral decomposition, which becomes computationally prohibitive for large datasets.

The main contribution is the derivation of bounds on the likelihood that avoid spectral decomposition entirely. For finite DPPs, the authors use the concept of "pseudo-inputs" (inducing points) to create bounds on the determinant that resemble those used in sparse Gaussian processes [11]. For continuous DPPs, they extend these bounds to Fredholm determinants. The key insight is that these bounds can be tightened by optimizing over the pseudo-inputs, which is crucial for accurate inference. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be used for both variational inference (cheap, approximate) and retrospective MCMC (more expensive, exact).

The paper is well-structured, with clear mathematical derivations (the proofs are relegated to supplementary material, as is standard for NIPS). The experiments validate their approach on both synthetic and real datasets. In the toy Gaussian continuous experiment, they demonstrate that their retrospective MCMC approach successfully recovers parameters (except for the scale parameter κ, which is non-identifiable when parametrizing L directly, as discussed in Section 1). On the diabetic neuropathy dataset, their variational inference method shows convergence of overdispersion measures as the number of pseudo-inputs increases, matching previous findings [7] while providing computational efficiency.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experiments. The bounds are rigorously derived and shown to be effective in practice. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the interpretability trade-off when parametrizing L instead of K) and provide a clear comparison with prior work. The experimental setup is appropriate, and the results demonstrate clear improvements over previous spectral-based methods in terms of computational efficiency.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, with a clear progression from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The connection to Gaussian process inference (via inducing variables) is well-explained, making the approach accessible to readers familiar with GP methods. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of why spectral decomposition is problematic in practice (beyond stating it's intractable), but the explanation is sufficient for the target audience. The supplementary material provides adequate technical details for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The approach of using non-spectral bounds for DPP inference is novel. Previous work [7] has relied on spectral approximations, which the authors correctly identify as computationally expensive for large-scale problems. The use of pseudo-inputs to create bounds that don't require spectral knowledge represents a significant conceptual advance. The extension to continuous domains is also novel, as most previous work has focused on finite domains. The authors properly cite related work, including [7] (which uses spectral bounds) and [11] (which inspired their finite-domain bounds).

**Significance:** This work addresses a practical bottleneck in DPP applications, making likelihood-based inference feasible for larger datasets. The computational efficiency gains are substantial: their variational approach runs in O(nm²) time compared to O(n³) for direct determinant evaluation, and their MCMC method avoids the need for power iterations. The experiments on real-world datasets (diabetic neuropathy) demonstrate clear practical utility. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP inference by providing methods that are both theoretically sound and practically efficient.

**Strengths:**
- Novel non-spectral bounds that avoid computationally expensive spectral decompositions
- Clear connection to Gaussian process methods, making the approach accessible
- Comprehensive experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets
- Careful discussion of limitations (e.g., parameter identifiability when parametrizing L)
- Demonstrated computational efficiency in practice

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not extensively explore the trade-offs between the number of pseudo-inputs (m) and computational cost versus accuracy
- The limitation of parametrizing L instead of K is acknowledged but not fully addressed (though the authors note this is common in the literature [3,7])
- The theoretical analysis of the convergence of the bounds as m increases could be more detailed

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

Pro:
1. The paper solves a well-known problem in DPP inference with a novel and practical approach.
2. The bounds are cheap to evaluate, enabling both variational and MCMC inference without spectral knowledge.
3. The experimental results demonstrate clear computational advantages over previous methods.
4. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and properly situates the work in the literature.

Con:
1. The limitation of parametrizing L instead of K may limit the interpretability of results for some applications.
2. The paper doesn't fully explore the trade-offs between m (number of pseudo-inputs) and accuracy in all scenarios.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling with DPPs. It addresses a practical bottleneck in DPP inference with a theoretically sound and computationally efficient approach that advances the state of the art. The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and demonstrates clear significance for both practitioners and researchers. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS, and I recommend it for publication.
