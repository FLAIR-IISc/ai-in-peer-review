

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a key challenge in DPP inference: the intractable normalizing constant in the likelihood, which requires spectral decomposition of the kernel L. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid expensive eigendecompositions, enabling more efficient variational inference and MCMC methods. The work builds on the observation that while DPPs are often parametrized through kernel K (with interpretability), the likelihood depends on L = (I-K)^{-1}K. Prior work (e.g., Affandi et al. [7]) circumvents the spectral challenge by directly parametrizing L, but still requires eigendecomposition for likelihood evaluation. The authors' key insight is extending sparse GP approximations (Titsias [11]) to DPPs using "pseudo-inputs" to derive bounds on the determinant that don't require spectral knowledge.

**Quality:** The theoretical contribution is sound. Propositions 1 and 2 provide rigorous bounds using determinant inequalities, with proofs in the supplementary material. The experimental validation is thorough, covering both synthetic and real datasets (diabetic neuropathy), and demonstrates the practical utility of the approach. The paper correctly acknowledges the interpretability trade-off in parametrizing L instead of K. However, the optimization procedure for the inducing points (Z) lacks sufficient justification—while the paper shows that optimizing Z tightens the bounds, it doesn't explain why this is theoretically sound or how to avoid local optima in this optimization. The comparison with Affandi et al. [7] is limited; a direct runtime comparison would strengthen the claims about computational efficiency.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good connections to sparse GP literature. The separation of finite and continuous domains is appropriate, though Section 4 (continuous case) contains technical operator theory that may be challenging for some readers. The experimental setup is well-described, and the figures effectively illustrate results. A minor issue is the lack of explicit computational complexity analysis for the continuous case beyond the bound derivations.

**Originality:** The core contribution—nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods—is genuinely novel. While sparse approximations have been used in GPs, their application to DPPs is new. The paper properly situates itself against prior work (e.g., [7], [3], [4]), and the distinction between spectral vs. nonspectral approaches is clear. The extension to continuous domains is a significant technical contribution that advances the field beyond previous finite-domain DPP work.

**Significance:** This work addresses a critical bottleneck in DPP applications, making likelihood-based inference more accessible for large datasets. The computational efficiency gains (O(nm²) vs. O(n³) for spectral methods) could enable DPPs in new application domains. The experiments show the approach works well on real data, and the MCMC implementation demonstrates that the bounds can yield accurate posterior estimates. The connection to GP literature also helps bridge two important probabilistic modeling frameworks.

**Strengths:** 
- Novel nonspectral bounds that avoid eigendecomposition
- Practical implementation of variational inference and MCMC
- Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real data
- Clear connection to sparse GP literature
- Honest discussion of parametrization trade-offs

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient justification for optimizing inducing points (Z)
- Limited comparison with spectral methods (e.g., runtime measurements)
- Limited discussion of failure cases (e.g., poor choice of Z)
- Continuous domain section is technically dense for a general NIPS audience

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a significant contribution to DPP inference methodology. While the optimization of inducing points could be better justified, the core technical contribution is sound and well-motivated. The work addresses a real computational bottleneck in a field where DPPs are increasingly used for modeling diversity. The experiments demonstrate practical utility, and the approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners working with DPPs. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP inference in a demonstrable way, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.

The authors should address the following in revision:
1. Provide more theoretical justification for optimizing the inducing points
2. Include concrete runtime comparisons with spectral methods
3. Add a brief discussion of failure cases or limitations of the approach
4. Consider simplifying the continuous domain section for broader accessibility

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling. The techniques presented have clear potential for adoption in existing DPP applications and could inspire similar approaches for other models with intractable normalizing constants.
