This paper proposes novel, cheap-to-evaluate, nonspectral bounds on the determinants arising in the likelihoods of determinantal point processes (DPPs), both finite and continuous. The authors provide a thorough introduction to DPPs, their desirable properties, and the challenges associated with likelihood-based inference. They then derive bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that do not depend on the spectral decomposition of the operator L, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the technical details. The use of pseudo-inputs or inducing inputs to approximate the likelihood is a clever idea, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the bounds and their properties. The experimental results are also convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in both synthetic and real-world datasets.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to balance technical rigor with accessibility. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to DPPs and their properties, making the paper accessible to readers who may not be familiar with the topic. At the same time, the technical details are thorough and well-explained, making the paper a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper also raises some interesting questions and potential avenues for future research. For example, the authors mention the possibility of investigating connections between kernels L and K that could be made without spectral knowledge, which could potentially address the issue of replacing L by K. Additionally, the use of pseudo-inputs to approximate the likelihood raises questions about the optimal choice of inducing inputs and the trade-offs between accuracy and computational efficiency.

In terms of weaknesses, one potential criticism is that the paper focuses primarily on the technical aspects of the proposed method, without providing a more comprehensive discussion of the broader implications and applications of DPPs. While the authors do provide some examples of how DPPs can be used in practice, a more detailed discussion of the potential applications and limitations of the proposed method would be helpful.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of DPPs. The authors provide a thorough and well-written analysis of the technical details, and the experimental results are convincing. The paper raises interesting questions and potential avenues for future research, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to DPPs and their properties.
