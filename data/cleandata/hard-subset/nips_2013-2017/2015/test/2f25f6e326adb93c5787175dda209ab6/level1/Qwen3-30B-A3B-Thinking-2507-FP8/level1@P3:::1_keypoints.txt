- Addresses DPP inference bottleneck: intractable normalizing constant requiring eigendecomposition of kernel L.
- Proposes novel nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood using pseudo-inputs, extending sparse GP approximations to DPPs.
- Key insight: derive determinant bounds that avoid spectral knowledge, enabling more efficient variational inference and MCMC.
- Theoretical contribution is sound: Propositions 1 and 2 provide rigorous determinant bounds with proofs in the supplement.
- Experimental validation is thorough: synthetic and real (diabetic neuropathy) datasets show practical utility.
- Computational efficiency claim: nonspectral approach can reduce cost to O(n m^2) versus O(n^3) for spectral methods.
- Properly acknowledges interpretability trade-off when parametrizing L instead of K.
- Optimization of inducing points (Z) tightens bounds but lacks theoretical justification and guidance to avoid local optima.
- Comparison with prior nonspectral work (Affandi et al.) is limited—missing direct runtime comparisons to strengthen efficiency claims.
- Continuous-domain extension is original and significant but technically dense and may be hard for a general audience.
- Clarity is generally good and well-organized, though Section 4 (continuous case) contains challenging operator theory and needs simplification.
- Minor missing analysis: no explicit computational complexity discussion for the continuous case beyond bound derivations.
- Strengths: novel nonspectral bounds, practical VI and MCMC implementation, strong experiments, and clear GP connections.
- Weaknesses: poor justification for Z optimization, limited runtime/spectral comparisons, limited discussion of failure cases, and technical density in continuous section.
- Recommendation: strong candidate for acceptance; authors should (1) justify optimizing inducing points, (2) include runtime comparisons with spectral methods, (3) discuss failure cases/limitations, and (4) simplify the continuous-domain section for accessibility.
