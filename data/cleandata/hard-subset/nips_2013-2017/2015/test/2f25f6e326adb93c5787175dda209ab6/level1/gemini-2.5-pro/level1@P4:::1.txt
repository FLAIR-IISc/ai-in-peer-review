This paper addresses the problem of scalable, likelihood-based parameter inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The main challenge in this setting is the computation of the normalizing constant of the DPP likelihood, which involves evaluating `det(L+I)` for finite DPPs, or a Fredholm determinant `det(I+L)` for continuous DPPs. Previous work, such as Affandi et al. (ICML 2014) [7], has tackled this by developing bounds on the determinant that rely on computing the top eigenvalues of the kernel matrix or operator. This spectral approach can be computationally expensive and is difficult to apply in continuous domains where the spectrum is often unknown.

The main contribution of this work is the derivation of novel, non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood. Drawing an analogy to variational methods for sparse Gaussian Processes [11], the authors introduce a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" `Z` to construct a low-rank approximation `Q` to the kernel matrix `L`. Based on this approximation, they derive both an upper and a lower bound on the log-likelihood. A key advantage of these bounds is that they can be computed efficiently in `O(nm^2)` time (for the finite case) without any eigenvalue calculations. The authors demonstrate that this framework is applicable to both finite DPPs (Section 2) and, significantly, to DPPs on continuous domains (Section 4).

These bounds are then incorporated into two standard inference paradigms. First, a variational inference (VI) scheme is proposed where the derived lower bound on the log-likelihood serves as the objective function, which is maximized jointly with respect to the kernel parameters and the locations of the pseudo-inputs. Second, for exact Bayesian inference, the authors employ the bounds within a Metropolis-Hastings (MH) algorithm using retrospective sampling [17]. This allows for an exact accept/reject decision by adaptively tightening the bounds (e.g., by increasing `m`) until the outcome is certain.

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the bounds is based on a known matrix inequality [14], and its application appears correct. The extension of the bounding technique from the finite case (matrix determinants) to the continuous case (Fredholm determinants) is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. The proposed inference methods (variational inference and MCMC) are standard and appropriate applications of the derived bounds. Experimental results on a real-world dataset [22] show that the method is practical and produces results consistent with previous studies.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The structure, which introduces the simpler finite case before moving to the more complex continuous setting, is effective. The authors clearly explain the motivation for their work, its connection to the sparse Gaussian Process literature, and the limitations of previous spectral-based approaches. The experimental sections are clear and provide good support for the paper's claims.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—the use of inducing-point-based approximations to bound the DPP normalizing constant—is novel. While the technique is inspired by work in Gaussian Processes, its application to DPPs and its rigorous extension to the continuous domain are original. This work provides a new and distinct approach to DPP inference, offering a computationally cheaper alternative to the spectral methods proposed in [7].

**Significance:** This work addresses a critical bottleneck for the broader adoption of DPPs in machine learning: scalable parameter inference. By obviating the need for expensive and often infeasible spectral decompositions, the proposed methods make DPPs more applicable to large-scale problems and continuous domains. The ability to perform both fast, approximate inference (VI) and exact, though more costly, inference (MCMC) within a unified framework is a significant practical advantage. This contribution has the potential to be widely used by researchers and practitioners working with DPPs.

### Weaknesses

The paper follows the common practice in the machine learning literature [3, 7] of directly parametrizing the kernel `L` rather than the more interpretable kernel `K`. The authors acknowledge this, but it remains a limitation. The toy experiment in Section 5.1 demonstrates a practical consequence of this choice: a severe parameter identifiability issue with the scaling parameter `κ`, where the posterior distribution is nearly identical to the prior.

The performance of the variational inference method was reported to be poor on the 1D toy problem, yielding inconsistent results across runs. While the authors use this to motivate the more robust MCMC approach, it raises questions about the general reliability and optimization landscape of the proposed variational objective. A deeper analysis of the potential difficulties in jointly optimizing the kernel parameters and pseudo-input locations would strengthen the paper.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, technically sound, and computationally efficient method for bounding the DPP likelihood, which is a fundamental problem.
*   **Pro:** The work provides both approximate (VI) and exact (MCMC) inference schemes, increasing its practical utility.
*   **Pro:** The extension to continuous DPPs is a significant theoretical and practical step forward for the field.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very clearly written and well-motivated.
*   **Con:** The variational inference approach appears to have robustness issues, as evidenced by the toy experiment.
*   **Con:** The method inherits the known interpretability limitations associated with parametrizing the `L` kernel directly.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty and significance in making DPP inference more scalable, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The proposed non-spectral bounds represent a valuable new tool for the community.
