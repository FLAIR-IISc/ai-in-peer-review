- Problem: Addresses the computational bottleneck in DPP likelihoods caused by evaluating det(I+L) or the Fredholm determinant without spectral decomposition.
- Approach: Derives nonspectral upper and lower determinant bounds using inducing/pseudo-inputs Z and a low-rank proxy Q, based on a Seiler & Simon determinant inequality.
- Computation: Bounds are cheap to evaluate via matrix determinant lemmas, avoiding eigen decompositions; finite-domain complexity roughly O(n m^2) for bound terms.
- Variational bound: Produces a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood analogous to sparse GP bounds, usable for multiple realizations.
- Exact inference: Constructs an exact retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme using bracketing bounds on acceptance probabilities, refining by increasing m and re-optimizing Z.
- Continuous case: Extends bounds to continuous DPPs via operator analogues with Ψ integrals, connecting to Fredholm determinant literature when Ψ is tractable.
- Experiments: Validates methods on a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian toy (compared to ground truth) and a 2D diabetic neuropathy dataset, showing convergence of bounds and class separation.
- Theoretical quality: Development is sound and well-motivated; determinant inequality use is appropriate and derivations are clean.
- Empirical quality: Proof-of-concept experiments are convincing, but broader large-n finite-domain benchmarks are lacking to substantiate practical efficiency claims.
- Clarity: Paper is generally clear and well-organized; suggests presenting bounds on det(I+L) directly and making O(n m^2) computations explicit.
- Originality: Novel nonspectral, inducing-point-based determinant bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, distinct from spectral/Nyström approaches.
- Significance: Potentially broad practical impact by removing spectral bottlenecks and offering both approximate and exact inference routes, especially for continuous domains.
- Applicability limitation: Method requires tractable Ψ integrals, which limits applicability; guidance on families where Ψ is closed-form is needed.
- Z and m selection: Paper gives empirical but not prescriptive guidance on choosing/optimizing Z and m; optimization cost and sensitivity are concerns.
- Identifiability: L-parameterization shows poor identifiability (e.g., κ); remedies (reparameterization, priors, diagnostics) are not provided.
- Retrospective MH practicalities: Re-optimizing Z at each step can be costly; stopping rules, initialization, and safeguards for monotone bound tightening should be discussed.
- Theoretical gaps: No formal rates or propositions on monotonicity/tightness of bounds with increasing m or optimized Z; convergence conditions not provided.
- Suggested experiments: Need large-scale finite-domain comparisons to spectral MH, EM/FP, and Nyström methods on accuracy, runtime, and memory.
- Overall assessment: Strong, novel, and well-founded contribution with promising continuous-domain impact, but requires expanded experiments and more guidance on applicability and Z-optimization to maximize practical impact.
