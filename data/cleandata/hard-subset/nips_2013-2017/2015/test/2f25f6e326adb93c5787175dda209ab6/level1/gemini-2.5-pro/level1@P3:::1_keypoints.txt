- Problem: likelihood-based DPP inference is hindered by the intractable normalizing constant det(L+I) (finite) / det(I+L) (continuous).

- Main contribution: novel non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the determinant using m pseudo-inputs and a determinantal inequality (Seiler & Simon).

- Complexity: proposed bounds are cheaper to compute (O(n m^2)) than spectral methods.

- Inspiration/technique: adapts variational sparse Gaussian Process ideas (Titsias 2009) to construct low-rank approximations Q to L.

- Applicability: bounds apply to both finite and continuous DPPs, extending prior work focused on discrete settings.

- Inference enabled: facilitates a fast approximate variational inference (maximizing the lower bound) and an asymptotically exact retrospective MCMC.

- Theoretical quality: derivations are well-founded and the continuous-case treatment is rigorous.

- Experiments — synthetic: 1D Gaussian-Gaussian experiment validates the MCMC sampler against known ground truth and reveals VI identifiability limits.

- Experiments — real data: 2D diabetic neuropathy analysis shows practical utility, stable results as m increases, and informative pseudo-input visualizations.

- Empirical insight: visualizations of optimized pseudo-inputs suggest connections between variational parameters and process intensity.

- Strength: original, significant contribution that bridges sparse GP methods and scalable DPP inference.

- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation that introduces finite-case intuition before the continuous extension.

- Weakness: lacks a direct empirical runtime/accuracy comparison to the main competing spectral method (Affandi et al. 2014), despite shared datasets.

- Weakness: optimization of pseudo-input locations Z is non-convex and the paper gives limited details on initialization sensitivity and robustness.

- Weakness: interpretability trade-off from parameterizing L directly (rather than K) leaves some parameters (e.g., scaling κ) unidentifiable, warranting more discussion.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel, practical, technically sound approach enabling the first scalable likelihood-based inference framework for continuous DPPs with convincing experiments.

- Arguments against acceptance: missing head-to-head empirical comparison with prior spectral methods and limited exploration of practical optimization issues.

- Conclusion: reviewer strongly recommends acceptance, citing strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses.
