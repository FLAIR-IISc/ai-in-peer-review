## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of performing likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), specifically tackling the computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition required for evaluating the likelihood. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood, both for finite and continuous domains, that circumvent the need for spectral information. This is a significant contribution, as it opens up the possibility of applying DPPs to larger and more complex datasets where spectral decomposition is intractable.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation and technical details. It effectively connects DPPs to related work in Gaussian Processes, highlighting the parallels and adapting existing techniques (like variational inference and MCMC) to the DPP setting. The derivation of the bounds builds upon existing inequalities and is presented in a logical manner. The inclusion of both finite and continuous domain results strengthens the generality of the approach. The experimental validation, including a toy example and a real-world dataset (diabetic neuropathy), provides compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness. The results on the neuropathy dataset are particularly interesting, demonstrating comparable performance to existing methods while avoiding spectral computations.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed bounds. While the authors acknowledge the trade-off between accuracy and computational cost, a more detailed analysis of the bound tightness under different conditions would be valuable. The choice of pseudo-inputs (Z) and its impact on performance is also not fully explored. Furthermore, the discussion of the interpretability issues associated with parametrizing the kernel L instead of K, while present, could be expanded.  The connection to quadrature-based bounds mentioned in the discussion section feels somewhat abrupt and could be elaborated upon.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed bounds offer a significant advancement in DPP inference by removing the spectral decomposition requirement.
*   **Significance:** This work has the potential to broaden the applicability of DPPs to larger and more complex datasets.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivations are mathematically sound and well-justified.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited analysis of bound tightness:** A more detailed analysis of the bounds' accuracy would be beneficial.
*   **Pseudo-input selection:** The impact of pseudo-input selection on performance needs further investigation.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and has the potential to significantly impact the application of DPPs. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh its limitations, and I recommend **acceptance**.
