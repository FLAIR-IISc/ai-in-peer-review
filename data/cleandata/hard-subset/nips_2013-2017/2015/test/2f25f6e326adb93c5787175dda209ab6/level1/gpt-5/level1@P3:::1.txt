Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles a key bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): evaluating det(I+L) (finite case) or the Fredholm determinant det(I+L) (continuous case) without resorting to spectral decompositions. The authors introduce non-spectral upper/lower bounds on these determinants by constructing a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ L−1_Z LZY using m “pseudo-inputs” Z, in the spirit of inducing-point approximations for Gaussian processes (GPs) [Titsias, AISTATS’09]. They provide finite-domain bounds and extend them to continuous domains via operator-theoretic arguments, leading to inexpensive variational objectives and to retrospective MH sampling that refines bounds adaptively in m. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP with known spectrum and a spatial neuropathy dataset (R2), demonstrating both MCMC and variational training.

Relative to prior DPP learning work, this departs from spectral truncation and Nyström approaches that compute leading eigenpairs (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’14; and bounds for acceptance in [7]) and from discrete L-ensemble training via EM and fixed-point iterations (Gillenwater et al., NIPS’14; Mariet & Sra, NIPS’15). It also connects to numerical Fredholm determinant evaluation (Bornemann, 2010) and to delayed/retrospective MH ideas used previously for DPPs with spectral bounds [7], but here without spectra. The contribution is novel in providing general-purpose, nonspectral determinant bounds that are cheap and apply to continuous DPPs.

Quality:
- Theory: The proposed matrix/operator bounds are plausible and in line with Seiler–Simon determinant inequalities; the construction Q yields L−Q ⪰ 0 (via Schur complements) when Z are inducing points in the same RKHS, underpinning monotonicity of the determinant bounds. However, there appears to be a sign inconsistency between Proposition 1 and the variational bound in Eq. (6). From the inequality det(I+L) ≤ det(I+Q) exp(tr(L−Q)), one expects −log det(I+L) ≥ −log det(I+Q) − tr(L−Q), whereas Eq. (6) uses “+ tr(L−Q)”. Please check/correct the signs and ensure consistency between (4) and (6). Similar attention is needed for the continuous case (Prop. 2) to avoid sign slips.
- Assumptions: The continuous-domain result assumes trace-class L and tractability of Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x); the paper states Gaussian–Gaussian covers many practical cases, but use cases beyond that are limited unless quadrature is used (the authors mention this as future work).
- Algorithms: The variational objective is well-motivated and its O(nm2) scaling is attractive. The retrospective MH scheme is sound in spirit, but the paper should more explicitly justify that adaptively increasing m and re-optimizing Z during the accept/reject step preserves detailed balance (cf. delayed acceptance/retrospective MH literature). Providing a precise statement/argument would strengthen the paper.
- Experiments: The 1D synthetic validates MH against ground truth and highlights identifiability issues when parameterizing L (κ unidentifiable), which is insightful. The neuropathy study shows convergence of bounds and stability of overdispersion summaries. However, the empirical section is relatively narrow: no large-scale discrete-domain benchmark (e.g., summarization or recommendation) where the n→ large, nm2 advantage over power iterations would be most compelling; no runtime/accuracy comparison to spectral bound methods [7] or to EM/fixed-point methods on discrete problems; and limited discussion of optimization stability and gradients for Z, θ.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and accessible, with useful context on K vs. L parametrizations. That said:
- Fix the sign inconsistency noted above and clean up equation numbering (some mismatches: e.g., references to (14) vs. (9)).
- Explicitly state the PSD ordering L−Q ⪰ 0 and the conditions under which it holds when Z ∉ Y.
- Provide gradients for F(θ,Z), or at least outline efficient derivatives for tr(L−Q) and det(Q+I), to encourage reproducibility beyond black-box CMA-ES.
- Clarify when the approach applies to discrete DPPs where L is only defined on a finite set: you need a kernel L(x,z) defined for arbitrary z in feature space; otherwise Z ⊄ Y is not applicable.

Originality:
High. Using GP-style inducing-point bounds to derive nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods, including a continuous-domain extension with Fredholm determinants, is new to the DPP literature.

Significance:
Moderate to high. The bounds provide a practical alternative to eigen-based methods, especially valuable for continuous DPPs and for finite problems where leading eigen-computation is prohibitive. The variational and MH recipes are broadly useful. The current experiments demonstrate viability but stop short of a comprehensive empirical case; adding larger-scale discrete tasks and head-to-head comparisons would substantially raise impact.

Pros:
- Novel, elegant nonspectral determinant bounds applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Clear computational benefits (O(nm2) vs. eigendecompositions), with straightforward implementation.
- Retrospective MH leveraging bounds is attractive and validated on a case with known spectra.
- Insightful discussion of L vs. K parametrization trade-offs.

Cons:
- Possible sign error in the variational bound; needs correction.
- Limited empirical breadth and lack of comparisons to spectral-bound MH [7] and discrete DPP learning baselines (EM, fixed-point).
- Continuous-case applicability hinges on closed-form Ψ; otherwise requires numerical quadrature.
- Theoretical convergence of bounds as m→∞ is discussed informally; a theorem/conditions would help.
- Lack of clarity on detailed balance with adaptive m and optimized Z during MH acceptance.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix the determinant/trace sign issue and provide a self-contained proof sketch for Propositions 1–2, emphasizing conditions ensuring L−Q ⪰ 0.
- Add at least one large discrete-domain experiment (n ≥ 10^4) with runtime vs. accuracy comparisons to [7] and to EM/fixed-point methods, highlighting when your method wins.
- Provide gradient expressions for θ and Z; report sensitivity to Z initialization and m; discuss numerical stabilization of LZ (e.g., jitter).
- Formalize convergence of bounds as m→∞ under mild conditions (e.g., Z dense in X, Mercer kernels), or provide a counterexample/limitations.
- Clarify the MH accept/reject correctness with adaptive bounds; cite delayed acceptance/retrospective MH references explicitly and state a proposition.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a novel and practically useful idea with solid potential, but would benefit from fixing the sign issue and strengthening empirical and theoretical components.
