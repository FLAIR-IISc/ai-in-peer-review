This paper addresses the problem of inference in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), focusing on avoiding the computationally expensive spectral decomposition required for likelihood evaluation. The core contribution is deriving bounds on the DPP likelihood – for both finite and continuous domains – that do not rely on spectral knowledge, enabling cheaper variational inference and MCMC methods.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction to DPPs and their applications, motivating the need for more efficient inference methods. It meticulously builds its argument, first handling the finite case, then extending the results to continuous domains. The connection to related work, including Gaussian Processes, is clearly established. The experimental validation, encompassing both a toy example and a real dataset, strengthens the paper’s claims.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivations appear mathematically sound, drawing upon existing results in operator theory and point process analysis. The use of existing bounds for GPs as inspiration is well-motivated.
* **Novelty:** Avoiding spectral decomposition for DPP inference is a significant contribution. While parametrizing L instead of K is a known workaround, the development of bounds that don’t require spectral knowledge provides a new approach.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors make a conscious effort to explain complex concepts and provide necessary background information. The separation of the finite and continuous cases aids comprehension.
* **Significance:**  DPPs are gaining prominence in machine learning, and efficient inference is crucial for wider adoption. This work directly addresses this bottleneck and provides practical tools for parameter estimation. The demonstration on a real dataset further highlights its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments are a good start, they feel somewhat limited. The toy example serves as validation but lacks complexity. The diabetic neuropathy dataset example is a good application, but a comparison to existing methods (even those requiring spectral decomposition) would be beneficial to demonstrate the speedup and accuracy tradeoffs. Specifically, how does the runtime compare to methods in [7]?
* **Interpretability Concerns (Reiterated):** The paper acknowledges the interpretability loss when parametrizing L instead of K, but doesn’t explore potential ways to mitigate this issue. Discussing this limitation further, and perhaps hinting at future work to address it, would be helpful.
* **Proposition 1 & 2 - Tightness of Bounds:** While the bounds are computationally efficient, their tightness isn't thoroughly analyzed. Understanding how loose the bounds are (and how this impacts the accuracy of inference) is important.  Are there scenarios where the bounds are particularly weak?
* **Connection to Existing Quadrature Methods:** The discussion of future work mentioning quadrature-based bounds [23] feels a bit tacked on.  Exploring this connection in more detail, even briefly, would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant bottleneck in DPP inference.
* Provides a novel and mathematically sound approach.
* Clear presentation and well-motivated arguments.
* Demonstrated effectiveness on a real-world dataset.
* Offers practical tools for researchers and practitioners.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited empirical evaluation and lack of comparison to existing methods.
* The inherent interpretability issues of parametrizing L remain a concern.
* The tightness of the derived bounds is not thoroughly investigated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs. The proposed bounds offer a practical solution to the computational challenges of likelihood-based inference. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, and the interpretability concerns should be acknowledged more fully, the technical soundness and novelty of the work are strong enough to warrant acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily focusing on expanding the experimental section and addressing the concerns about bound tightness.
