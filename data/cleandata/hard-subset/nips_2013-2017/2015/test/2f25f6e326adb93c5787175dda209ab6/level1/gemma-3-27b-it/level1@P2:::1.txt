This paper addresses the challenging problem of inference in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decompositions, which are often computationally prohibitive. The authors derive bounds on the DPP likelihood, for both finite and continuous domains, that avoid these decompositions, enabling the use of variational inference and MCMC methods.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly connecting DPPs to diverse applications and highlighting the limitations of existing inference techniques. The introduction does a good job of placing the work within the broader context of DPP research and explicitly points out the trade-off between interpretability and tractability when parameterizing the kernel. The presentation of the mathematical derivations appears sound, although a deeper understanding would require reviewing the supplementary material. The connection to related work, particularly Gaussian processes, is appropriately made.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of bounds on the likelihood appears logically sound, drawing inspiration from existing results in GP approximation. The proposed bounds offer a potentially significant improvement in computational efficiency compared to methods requiring spectral decompositions.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is reasonably clear, and the authors attempt to explain the core ideas in an accessible manner. The separation of finite and continuous cases is helpful.
*   **Originality:** Avoiding spectral decomposition for DPP inference is a valuable contribution. While the idea of bounding the likelihood is not new, applying it specifically to DPPs in a way that circumvents spectral computations represents a novel approach.
*   **Significance:** Efficient inference in DPPs has practical implications for various applications, including diversity modeling, recommendation systems, and spatial statistics. The proposed bounds could unlock the application of DPPs to larger-scale problems. The experimental validation on both a synthetic and real-world dataset strengthens this claim.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost of Bounds:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a major benefit, the proposed bounds involve introducing pseudo-inputs (Z) and optimizing over them, potentially adding a new computational burden. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed analysis of the trade-off between the cost of evaluating the bounds and the cost of spectral decomposition would be helpful.
*   **Identifiability Issues:** The experiments reveal identifiability challenges, specifically with the kernel parameter κ in the Gaussian continuous DPP. While the authors acknowledge this, it underscores a potential limitation of directly parameterizing the kernel L rather than K.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments, while valuable, primarily focus on validating the method's correctness on a synthetic example and demonstrating its applicability to a single real-world dataset. Expanding the experimental evaluation to include more diverse datasets and comparing against existing methods more comprehensively would strengthen the claims.
*   **Connection to Existing Bounds:** The paper states the connection to quadrature-based bounds in the discussion, but it would be more beneficial to discuss this connection earlier in the paper, perhaps when presenting the bounds themselves.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in DPP inference.
*   The proposed bounds offer a practical alternative to spectral decomposition.
*   The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the experimental results are promising.
*   The work is well-motivated and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of optimizing over the pseudo-inputs needs further investigation.
*   Identifiability issues may limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of DPP research. The proposed bounds offer a valuable tool for performing inference without resorting to computationally expensive spectral decompositions. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

*   Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational trade-offs between evaluating the bounds and performing spectral decomposition.
*   Discuss the identifiability issues in more detail and explore potential solutions.
*   Expand the experimental evaluation to include more datasets and comparisons with existing methods.
*   Elaborate on the connection to existing quadrature-based bounds earlier in the paper.
