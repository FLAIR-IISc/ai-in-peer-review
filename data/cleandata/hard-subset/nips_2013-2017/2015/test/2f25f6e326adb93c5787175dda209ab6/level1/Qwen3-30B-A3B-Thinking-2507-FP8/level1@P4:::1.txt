

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), which are increasingly used for modeling diversity in machine learning applications. The authors identify two main challenges: (1) the likelihood involves kernel L related to the more interpretable kernel K through an intractable spectral decomposition, and (2) the likelihood contains an intractable normalizing constant (a determinant in finite domains, Fredholm determinant in continuous domains). Previous work [7] circumvents the first challenge by directly parametrizing kernel L (at the cost of interpretability) but still relies on spectral decompositions for the second challenge. The authors' main contribution is to derive bounds on the likelihood that do not require spectral decomposition, making inference more tractable.

The paper makes two key contributions: (1) for finite DPPs, they derive bounds using pseudo-inputs (inducing variables) that are analogous to those used in sparse Gaussian processes [11], and (2) they extend these bounds to continuous DPPs. The bounds take the form det(Q + I)e^(-tr(L-Q)) ≤ 1/det(L + I) ≤ 1/det(Q + I) for finite domains and similar expressions for continuous domains. Crucially, these bounds are computationally efficient as they avoid spectral decompositions. The authors then demonstrate how to use these bounds for variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods, providing a clear connection to existing inference paradigms.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper presents technically sound theoretical contributions with well-justified proofs that rely on established determinant inequalities. The bounds are carefully derived and validated through both theoretical arguments and experimental results. The authors appropriately address the limitations of their approach (e.g., the trade-off between using kernel L versus K) and provide thorough experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear transitions between theoretical development and practical implementation. The authors provide sufficient background on DPPs and their relevance to machine learning, and they effectively connect their work to related methods in Gaussian processes. The experimental section is particularly strong, with clear visualizations and well-designed experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

*Originality:* The paper makes a clear contribution by avoiding spectral decompositions entirely, which distinguishes it from prior work [7, 8, 9]. The extension from finite to continuous domains is non-trivial and well-executed. The authors properly contextualize their work within the existing literature, noting that previous approaches rely on spectral approximations while their method does not.

*Significance:* DPPs are increasingly important in machine learning for modeling diversity (e.g., in news aggregation, search results, and spatial patterns), but their inference has been limited by computational challenges. This paper provides a practical solution that makes likelihood-based inference more tractable for both finite and continuous DPPs, potentially enabling broader application of DPPs in machine learning. The experimental results on the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrate the approach's practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**

*The paper could better address the interpretability trade-off between kernels K and L. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they do not provide concrete examples or quantitative analysis of how much interpretability is lost when using kernel L instead of K, which might be important for practitioners.

*The computational complexity analysis could be more detailed. While the authors state that their variational approach is O(nm²) and that m can be chosen much smaller than n, they do not provide empirical evidence of computational savings compared to spectral-based methods for large-scale problems.

*The experimental section could better compare against alternative approaches to DPP inference beyond spectral methods. While the paper references [7], a more comprehensive comparison with other recent approaches would strengthen the significance claim.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro: The paper solves a well-defined, important problem in DPP inference with a technically sound approach that avoids the computational bottleneck of spectral decompositions. The bounds are novel, the implementation is straightforward, and the experimental results demonstrate clear practical value. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the literature.

*Con: The paper's main contribution is incremental in the sense that it builds on existing variational techniques for Gaussian processes. While the application to DPPs is novel, the core technique is not entirely new. The interpretability trade-off between kernels K and L is acknowledged but not deeply explored.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling for diversity. The technical approach is sound, the theoretical contributions are well-justified, and the experimental validation demonstrates clear practical utility. The work addresses a significant challenge in DPP inference that has limited the broader adoption of DPPs in machine learning applications. While the core technique builds on existing variational methods, the application to DPPs is novel and well-executed. The paper is well-written and provides a clear path for practitioners to implement the proposed methods. Given the increasing importance of DPPs in machine learning, this work is both timely and significant.

The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS: it is technically sound, clearly written, makes an original contribution by avoiding spectral decompositions, and provides significant practical value for inference with DPPs. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the overall quality and contribution of the work.
