**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the computationally challenging problem of likelihood-based parameter inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The primary bottleneck is the calculation of the normalizing constant, which involves computing `det(L+I)` for finite DPPs and a Fredholm determinant for continuous DPPs. The authors propose a novel method for inference that avoids the costly spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix/operator `L`, which was required by previous approaches.

The core contribution is the derivation of new, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the log-likelihood. These bounds are constructed using a low-rank approximation `Q` of the kernel matrix `L`, based on a small set of `m` "inducing inputs" or "pseudo-inputs" `Z`. This technique is inspired by and directly analogous to methods used for sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes (GPs). The resulting bounds can be computed efficiently in `O(nm^2)` time, a significant improvement over the `O(n^3)` cost of direct determinant calculation.

Based on these bounds, the authors develop two inference schemes:
1.  A cheap, approximate variational inference (VI) method that maximizes a lower bound on the log-likelihood jointly with respect to the model parameters and the locations of the inducing inputs.
2.  A more expensive but asymptotically exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method. This approach uses the bounds within a Metropolis-Hastings sampler, employing a retrospective sampling trick to make acceptance decisions without computing the exact likelihood, tightening the bounds by adding more inducing points only when necessary.

A key advantage of this framework is its applicability to both finite and continuous domain DPPs, extending inference capabilities beyond prior spectral methods which were limited to the finite case. The authors demonstrate their methods on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP, where they show the MCMC sampler correctly recovers posterior distributions, and on a real-world 2D spatial statistics dataset of nerve fibers, where the VI method successfully distinguishes between patient classes.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper tackles an important problem and the proposed high-level idea is elegant and well-motivated. The experimental results, particularly for the real-world dataset, are promising and show that the bounds tighten and parameter estimates converge as the number of inducing points `m` increases. However, there appears to be a critical technical issue in the derivation of the main theoretical result, which casts doubt on the soundness of the variational inference section.

In Proposition 1 and its use in Equation (6), the lower bound on the log-likelihood seems to rely on the inequality `log det(L+I) <= log det(Q+I) - tr(L-Q)`. Standard results based on Jensen's inequality or matrix inequalities (e.g., [14]) suggest the opposite: `log det(L+I) >= log det(Q+I) + tr(L-Q)`, since `L-Q` is positive semi-definite. This apparent contradiction needs to be addressed, as it potentially invalidates the variational lower bound `F(θ,Z)`. If this is a typo, it is a significant one that needs careful correction and verification that the rest of the VI derivation holds. The authors also report that VI performs poorly on the toy example, which, while potentially due to model identifiability, also raises questions about the robustness of the variational objective.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the connection to sparse GPs provides a helpful intuition for readers familiar with that literature. The separation of the finite and continuous cases is a good structural choice that improves readability. The figures are clear and support the experimental claims.

**Originality:** The main idea of applying inducing-point approximations from the GP literature to derive non-spectral bounds for DPP inference is novel and clever. It represents a significant departure from previous spectral-based methods for DPP inference, such as Affandi et al. (2013). This work opens a new and computationally appealing avenue for fitting DPP models.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is high. By providing a cheaper, more scalable, and more general (i.e., applicable to continuous domains) method for DPP inference, this paper could enable the application of DPPs to a wider range of larger-scale problems in machine learning. The proposed methods, if technically sound, would represent a clear advance in the state of the art for DPP inference.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** The core idea is highly original and addresses a key bottleneck in a popular and increasingly important model class.
*   **Computational Advantage:** The proposed non-spectral approach is significantly more efficient than previous methods and scales better to large datasets.
*   **Generality:** The framework's ability to handle both finite and continuous DPPs is a major strength and broadens its applicability.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Potential Technical Flaw:** There is a serious concern regarding the correctness of the variational lower bound presented in Proposition 1 and Equation (6). This is the paper's central technical tool, and if it is incorrect, a major part of the contribution is undermined.
*   **Inconsistent VI Performance:** The variational method is reported to fail on the synthetic experiment, which weakens the claims about its general utility without further analysis.
*   **Limited Experimental Baseline:** The experiments demonstrate the method's properties (e.g., convergence with `m`) but lack a direct comparison to the most relevant prior work (e.g., the spectral-based MCMC of Affandi et al., 2013) in terms of runtime and accuracy.

**Conclusion and Suggestions:**

This paper presents a very promising and original approach to DPP inference. The core idea is elegant and has the potential for significant impact. However, the apparent technical error in the derivation of the variational lower bound is a critical issue that prevents me from recommending acceptance in its current form.

I would strongly urge the authors to double-check the inequalities in Proposition 1 and the resulting variational objective `F(θ,Z)`. Please clarify the derivation or correct it if it is indeed a mistake. If the bound is corrected, please verify that a tractable variational lower bound still results and update the experimental section accordingly. Addressing this point is essential for the paper's publication.

Minor suggestions:
*   Providing more detail on the optimization of the inducing inputs `Z` would be beneficial.
*   A direct empirical comparison to the spectral-bound MCMC method [7] on a shared (finite) problem would significantly strengthen the experimental section by contextualizing the trade-offs in speed and sample quality.
