This paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) when spectral information about the kernel operator L is unavailable. The authors derive nonspectral upper and lower bounds on the normalizing determinant in the DPP likelihood, both for finite domains (det(I+L)) and for continuous domains (Fredholm determinants). The finite-domain bounds are constructed via an inducing-point, low-rank surrogate Q = LYZ L−1Z LZY inspired by sparse GP methods [11], and rely on a determinant inequality of Seiler and Simon [14]. The continuous analogue uses LZ and Ψij = ∫ L(zi, x)L(x, zj)dµ(x) to bound det(I+L). These bounds enable (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood that is optimized jointly over kernel parameters and inducing locations, and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that adaptively tightens accept/reject bounds without eigen-decompositions. The work contrasts with prior DPP learning approaches that depend on spectra or Nyström eigen-approximations (e.g., [7, 8, 9, 13]) and connects to variational inducing-point approximations in GPs [11]. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP with known spectrum, validating the retrospective MH against “ideal” MH, and a diabetic neuropathy dataset where the variational method recovers plausible overdispersion differences between classes, with bounds tightening as m grows.

Quality: The main technical claims are sound and supported by clear propositions with proofs (in the supplement). The inference constructions are correct, and complexity arguments (O(nm2)) are reasonable. Empirical validation is convincing for method correctness but limited in scope and comparative breadth.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and generally readable. Some assumptions for the continuous case (existence and computability of Ψ) could be discussed more explicitly, and guidance on selecting/initializing inducing points would aid practitioners.

Originality: The nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods—especially for continuous domains—appear novel. The import of inducing-point ideas from GP variational inference to DPP normalization is a neat and nontrivial contribution.

Significance: Removing spectral requirements broadens applicability of DPP inference, particularly on continuous spaces. Impact would be strengthened by larger-scale benchmarks and head-to-head comparisons with spectral and fixed-point methods.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, general nonspectral bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Practical inference instantiations (variational and retrospective MH).
- Solid theoretical underpinnings; clear connection to GP variational literature.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited experimental breadth; no direct comparison to [7, 8, 9, 13].
- Practical constraints on Ψ computation not fully explored.
- Limited guidance on inducing-point placement and bound tightness vs. m.
- Identifiability issues when parameterizing L are observed but not deeply analyzed.

Suggestions: Add comparisons on finite datasets to spectral/MH baselines; analyze bound tightness as a function of m and Z; discuss approximate Ψ (quadrature, random features); provide implementation details and code.
