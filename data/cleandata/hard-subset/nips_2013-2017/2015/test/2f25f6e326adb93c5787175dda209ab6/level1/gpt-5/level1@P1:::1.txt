Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a core obstacle in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the normalization term det(I+L) (finite case) or the Fredholm determinant det(I+L) (continuous case), whose evaluation typically requires spectral decompositions that are costly or unavailable. The authors adopt the common machine-learning parameterization of L-ensembles (rather than the more interpretable K, cf. [3,4]) and derive nonspectral upper and lower bounds on these determinants using inducing/pseudo-inputs Z and a low-rank proxy Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY (finite) or its continuous analogue with Ψ = ∫ L(z,x)L(x,z′) dμ(x). The bounds are based on a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon [14]) and are cheap to evaluate via matrix determinant lemmas, avoiding eigenvalue computations.

These bounds enable:
- A variational lower bound on the log-likelihood for multiple realizations, F(θ,Z) = sum_t log det(LYt) − T log det(Q+I) + T tr(L−Q), reminiscent of variational sparse GP bounds (Titsias [11]).
- An exact Metropolis–Hastings scheme via retrospective sampling using bracketing bounds on acceptance probabilities, refining by increasing m and re-optimizing Z (cf. spectral bracketing in Affandi et al. [7]).

For continuous DPPs, the paper provides analogous operator bounds using LZ and Ψ; these apply when Ψ is tractable (e.g., Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures), connecting to numerical Fredholm determinant literature (Bornemann [23]).

Experiments include: (i) a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP with known spectrum to validate the retrospective MH against an “ideal” MH, and (ii) a 2D diabetic neuropathy dataset [22], fitting separate DPPs per class and demonstrating convergence of overdispersion measures γd = σd/ρd with increasing m, and clear class separation comparable to [7].

Quality
- Theoretical development is sound and well-motivated. The use of [14] to obtain tight, computable determinant bounds is appropriate; finite-domain complexity is O(n m^2) for the bound terms via determinant lemma and trace identities, and O(|Yt|^3) for the numerator determinants, which is reasonable when m ≪ n.
- The variational lower bound is derived cleanly, and the retrospective MH construction mirrors established techniques while removing spectral reliance.
- Empirical validation is convincing as a proof-of-concept: the toy continuous setting allows comparison to ground truth; the real dataset reproduces known qualitative findings and shows convergence of bounds with m. However, broader benchmarking (finite-domain, large-n regimes) would strengthen claims about practical efficiency.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized, with a helpful finite→continuous progression. A few notational points could be clarified: (i) present bounds directly on det(I+L) (rather than 1/det(I+L)) to reduce cognitive load; (ii) explicitly state how det(Q+I) and tr(Q) are computed in O(n m^2) using det(I+UV)=det(I+VU) and trace-cycling.
- The conditions ensuring tr(L)=∫ L(x,x) dμ(x) and applicability of the continuous bounds are briefly discussed; adding a concise, self-contained set of sufficient conditions (beyond directing to [19]) would help practitioners.

Originality
- The core contribution—nonspectral, inducing-point-based determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods in both finite and continuous settings—appears novel. Prior ML work uses spectral truncations or Nyström approximations [7,13]; here, bounds avoid eigencomputations entirely and extend to operator settings in a principled manner. The analogy to variational sparse GPs is leveraged in a new context (DPP likelihood normalization rather than GP marginal likelihood).

Significance
- Practically important: DPPs are increasingly used in ML for diversity modeling and spatial statistics; removing the spectral bottleneck can broaden applicability, especially on continuous domains or very large discrete domains where spectrum is infeasible.
- The method provides both approximate (variational) and exact (retrospective MH) inference routes, which is valuable. The continuous Fredholm determinant bounds are especially impactful given the computational challenges in that setting.
- Limitations reduce immediate impact: bounds require Ψ integrals to be available; guidance on choosing/optimizing Z and m is empirical; no formal rate of bound tightening is provided.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical scope: Lacks large-scale finite-domain experiments comparing to spectral MH [7], EM/FP methods [8,9], or Nyström-based approaches [13] in terms of accuracy, runtime, and memory. Such comparisons would substantiate the “cheap-to-evaluate” claim in practice.
- Identifiability: The 1D study highlights poor identifiability of κ under L-parameterization. The paper could offer remedies (reparameterizations separating intensity from repulsion, informative priors, constraints) and diagnostics for when the variational objective is unreliable (e.g., multimodality/flatness).
- Retrospective MH details: While standard in principle, re-optimizing Z at each step introduces nontrivial computational overhead; discuss stopping rules, initialization, and any safeguards to ensure monotone tightening of bounds so that correctness of retrospective tests is maintained.
- Theory: Provide more discussion (or a proposition) on the monotonicity/tightness of bounds as m increases and Z is optimized (e.g., connection to integrated posterior variance decay in GPs), and conditions under which bounds converge to equality.
- Continuous case applicability: Be explicit about families where Ψ is closed-form or cheaply approximable (e.g., RBF kernels with Gaussian base, certain polynomial/exponential families), and discuss numerical quadrature as a fallback with error control.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually novel, elegant, and broadly applicable nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods (finite and continuous).
- Provides both approximate and exact inference schemes; removes a major practical bottleneck.
- Solid theoretical underpinning leveraging a nontrivial determinant inequality; clear computational pathway (O(n m^2)).
- Empirical demonstrations validate correctness and practical promise; continuous-domain contribution is particularly valuable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical comparison to state-of-the-art on finite large-scale problems; practical efficiency claims not fully substantiated.
- Dependence on tractable Ψ integrals may limit applicability; guidance is brief.
- No guarantees on bound tightening rate or convergence with m; optimization over Z may be sensitive and computationally heavy.
- Identifiability and parameterization issues under L are acknowledged but not addressed methodologically.

Overall, this is a strong, well-founded contribution with clear potential impact on DPP inference. With expanded experiments and more guidance on continuous-case applicability and Z-optimization, it would be even more compelling.
