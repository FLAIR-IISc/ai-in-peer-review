### **Review**

**Summary**

This paper addresses a key computational challenge in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the intractable normalizing constant, which involves computing `det(L + I)` for a large matrix L (in the finite case) or the Fredholm determinant `det(I + L)` for an operator (in the continuous case). Previous work, such as Affandi et al. (2014) [7], tackled this by using bounds based on partial spectral decompositions, which can be computationally demanding and are primarily suited for the finite case.

The main contribution of this paper is the introduction of novel, "non-spectral" bounds on this determinant term. The core idea, cleverly inspired by variational sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes (Titsias, 2009) [11], is to use a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" (or inducing points) to construct a low-rank approximation `Q` to the kernel matrix `L`. Based on a determinantal inequality from Seiler & Simon (1975) [14], the authors derive both an upper and a lower bound on `det(L + I)`. These bounds are significantly cheaper to compute (O(nm^2)) than spectral methods and, importantly, are applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs.

The authors demonstrate how these bounds facilitate two standard inference schemes: a fast, approximate variational inference (VI) method based on maximizing the lower bound, and a more costly but asymptotically exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method using retrospective sampling. The utility of the framework is demonstrated on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP, where the known ground truth allows for a thorough validation of the MCMC sampler, and on a real-world 2D spatial point process dataset of nerve fibers, where the VI approach successfully replicates previous scientific findings.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The connection drawn between sparse GP methods and DPP inference is both novel and impactful. It provides a new, principled way to approximate the DPP likelihood that avoids the costs and limitations of spectral methods. This is a significant advance for the field, making DPPs more scalable and applicable to a wider range of problems, especially those involving continuous domains, for which practical inference methods were lacking.

2.  **Technical Quality and Generality:** The paper is technically solid. The derivation of the bounds is well-founded, and their application to both VI and MCMC is clearly explained. A major strength is the extension of the method to continuous DPPs, which represents a substantial contribution over prior art that focused mainly on the discrete setting. The treatment of the continuous case is rigorous and opens up new modeling possibilities.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors wisely introduce the concepts first in the simpler finite setting (Section 2) before moving to the more technical continuous case (Section 4). This structure makes the core ideas accessible to a broad audience. The connection to sparse GPs is used as an effective intuitive guide throughout the paper.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-conceived and provide convincing evidence for the method's effectiveness.
    *   The 1D Gaussian-Gaussian experiment is an excellent sanity check, as the availability of a ground truth allows for a direct comparison with the "ideal" MCMC sampler. The authors are commendably transparent about the limitations of the VI approach in this setting due to parameter identifiability, which is an important and honest finding.
    *   The diabetic neuropathy experiment demonstrates the practical utility on a real-world problem, showing that the method can be used to perform scientific discovery and that the results are stable and converge as the number of pseudo-inputs increases. This provides a clear, practical recipe for setting the key hyperparameter `m`.
    *   The visualizations of the optimized pseudo-inputs (Figs 1a, 4) are insightful, suggesting a deeper connection between the variational parameters and the underlying process intensity.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Direct Comparison to Competing Methods:** The primary motivation for this work is to provide an alternative to the spectral bounding method of Affandi et al. [7]. While the asymptotic complexity analysis suggests the proposed method is superior, the paper lacks a direct empirical comparison in terms of runtime and accuracy on a common task. Since the diabetic neuropathy dataset was also used in [7], a head-to-head comparison on that data would have been highly informative and would have strengthened the claims of practical superiority.

2.  **Optimization of Pseudo-Inputs:** The performance of the method relies on optimizing the locations of the pseudo-inputs `Z`, which is a non-convex optimization problem. The paper gives few details on the practicalities of this optimization (e.g., sensitivity to initialization, potential for local minima). While the results appear good, a more in-depth discussion of the robustness of this optimization step would improve the paper.

3.  **Inherited Interpretability Issues:** The authors correctly note that, for tractability, they adopt the common machine learning practice of parameterizing the kernel `L` directly, rather than the more interpretable "correlation" kernel `K`. While this is a known trade-off in the DPP literature and not a flaw of this work per se, the MCMC experiment vividly illustrates the consequence: the scaling parameter `κ` is completely unidentifiable from the data. The paper could benefit from a slightly deeper discussion of this limitation and whether the proposed inference framework might, in future work, help to make inference with the K-parametrization more feasible.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   Introduces a highly novel and significant approach to DPP inference, bridging the gap with the sparse Gaussian Process literature.
*   Provides the first truly practical and scalable likelihood-based inference framework for continuous DPPs.
*   The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and supported by strong, transparent experiments.
*   The proposed method is likely to become a standard tool for practitioners and a foundation for future research on scalable DPPs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance**
*   The empirical comparison against the most relevant prior work [7] is absent.
*   Some practical aspects, particularly concerning the optimization of variational parameters, could be explored in more detail.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature on determinantal point processes. The proposed non-spectral bounds are an elegant and powerful idea that substantially advances the state of the art in DPP inference, particularly by extending its applicability to continuous domains. The strengths in originality, technical quality, and clarity far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
