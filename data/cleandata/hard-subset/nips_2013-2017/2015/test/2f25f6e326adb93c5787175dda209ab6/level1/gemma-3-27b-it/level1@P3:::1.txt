This paper addresses the challenging problem of inference in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decomposition, a significant computational bottleneck. The authors provide novel bounds on the DPP likelihood, for both finite and continuous domains, enabling tractable variational inference and MCMC methods. This is a valuable contribution, as it removes a major hurdle in applying DPPs to larger-scale problems.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and demonstrates a strong understanding of DPPs, kernel methods, and relevant mathematical tools (Fredholm determinants, operator theory). The derivations appear correct, and the connection to existing work on variational inference for GPs is well-established. The presentation of the continuous case is more involved, but adequately explained. The experimental validation, while limited to a toy example and a real dataset with a setup borrowed from previous work, provides evidence that the proposed methods are viable and competitive. However, the claim of “cheap” bounds needs qualification; while avoiding spectral decomposition is a win, the optimization of pseudo-inputs (Z) within the MCMC loop can still be computationally expensive, especially for high-dimensional data. The authors acknowledge this implicitly.  The paper feels complete, given its scope. The careful discussion of parameter identifiability in the Gaussian-Gaussian example and the limitations of direct L-parametrization are strengths.

**Clarity:** The writing is generally clear and well-organized. The paper follows a logical flow, building from the finite case to the continuous case. However, the mathematical notation can be dense, and readers unfamiliar with operator theory may struggle with Section 4. Providing more intuitive explanations for the key concepts in Section 4 would improve accessibility. The connection between the bounds and the variational/MCMC inference procedures is clearly explained. The experimental section is well-structured and provides sufficient detail to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The core idea of leveraging bounds on the likelihood to circumvent spectral decomposition is novel. While bounds on determinants exist, their application to DPP inference in this manner is new. The extension of the bounds to continuous domains is also a significant contribution. The authors appropriately cite related work, including previous NIPS publications on DPPs and variational inference. The work builds on existing techniques (variational inference, MCMC, GP approximations) but combines them in a novel way to address a specific challenge in DPP modeling. The adaptation of retrospective sampling to this context is a good example of this.

**Significance:**  The work is significant because it expands the applicability of DPPs to problems where spectral decomposition is impractical. This opens up new opportunities for using DPPs in areas such as large-scale recommendation systems, image/video summarization, and spatial modeling.  The proposed methods provide a practical alternative to existing approaches that rely on approximations or spectral truncation. The potential for building on this work is high. Future research could explore more efficient methods for optimizing the pseudo-inputs, developing tighter bounds, and applying the methods to more complex datasets and models.  The fact that the method performs comparably to the more computationally intensive exact methods (in the simple Gaussian case) is encouraging.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant bottleneck in DPP inference.
*   Provides novel, mathematically sound bounds on the likelihood.
*   Enables tractable variational and MCMC inference methods.
*   Experimental results demonstrate the viability of the proposed methods.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation (despite some mathematical density).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of optimizing the pseudo-inputs in MCMC can be significant.
*   Experimental validation is limited, relying on a toy example and a previously studied dataset.
*   The continuous case (Section 4) may be challenging for readers without a strong background in operator theory.
*   The claim of "cheap" bounds requires qualification; it's cheap *relative* to spectral decomposition, but not necessarily cheap in absolute terms.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper. The technical contributions are solid, the presentation is clear, and the potential impact of the work is significant. While the experimental validation could be more extensive, the results presented are encouraging. The limitations of the approach are acknowledged, and the authors provide a clear roadmap for future research. This work represents a valuable addition to the growing body of literature on DPPs and will likely be of interest to a broad range of researchers in machine learning and statistics.  A minor revision request could be to include a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the pseudo-input optimization step in the MCMC algorithm.
