- Paper tackles DPP inference without spectral decompositions by providing non-spectral bounds on the likelihood for finite and continuous domains.  
- Builds on GP variational approximations and prior work to avoid eigenvalue calculations and connects to Fredholm determinants for continuous DPPs.  
- Novelty: non-spectral likelihood bounds are a genuinely new and practically important contribution.  
- Generality: bounds extend to both finite and continuous DPPs, widening applicability.  
- Theoretical soundness: derivations use established results and relate well to variational GP theory.  
- Practical impact: enables variational inference and MCMC without spectral costs, making larger/complex DPPs feasible.  
- Empirical validation: synthetic and real-data experiments show effectiveness and comparisons to prior work are informative.  
- Presentation: paper is well-written, organized, and math is clearly motivated (with some derivations in supplement).  
- Interpretability trade-off: parameterizing L instead of K reduces interpretability and mitigation strategies are under-discussed.  
- Pseudo-input optimization cost: optimizing Z adds computational overhead; scalability and complexity need deeper analysis.  
- Choice of optimizer: use of CMA-ES for variational inference is not justified and alternatives aren’t explored.  
- Limited continuous-DPP experiments: theory for continuous case is stronger than its empirical validation.  
- Dependence on determinant inequality: method’s performance hinges on inequality tightness; impact on inference accuracy is not fully analyzed.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: performance depends on number of pseudo-inputs m; systematic study of m vs. cost/accuracy is lacking.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses major spectral bottleneck, is novel, theoretically sound, applicable to both domains, and empirically effective.  
- Arguments against acceptance: concerns about pseudo-input optimization cost, interpretability loss, limited continuous experiments, and sensitivity to m.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant advance in DPP inference; weaknesses don’t outweigh contributions and merit acceptance.  
- Confidence: reviewer rates 8/10 in confidence.
