- Proposes nonspectral, computationally cheap two-sided bounds on the determinantal normalizer det(I+L) for finite (matrix) and continuous (Fredholm) DPPs using low-rank inducing points Z.  
- Core idea: approximate L by Q built from inducing inputs and apply Seiler–Simon determinant inequalities to obtain tight sandwich bounds.  
- Finite-case bound: det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I); continuous-case bounds use LZ and Ψ = ∫ LZx LxZ dµ(x).  
- Avoids eigenvalue/eigendecomposition computations, enabling practical evaluation on large or continuous domains.  
- Enables two inference methods: a variational lower bound for MLE and a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler using the upper/lower likelihood bounds.  
- Situates work well in DPP literature and draws an insightful analogy to sparse variational GP methods (Titsias).  
- Continuous treatment leverages Fredholm determinant theory and provides computable Ψ for Gaussian–Gaussian settings.  
- Technical soundness: derivations rely on established determinant inequalities and trace-class operator assumptions; proofs are in the supplement.  
- Algorithms: variational objective runs in O(n m^2) with Z optimized to tighten bounds; retrospective MH construction is coherent and well explained.  
- Empirical support: includes 1D Gaussian–Gaussian validation and a neuropathy dataset showing monotone tightening with m; retrospective MH matches ideal MH in known-spectrum case.  
- Empirical limitations: missing large-n finite-domain experiments and limited head-to-head comparisons with spectral and Nyström baselines on accuracy and runtime.  
- Clarity: generally clear and well organized, with heavy but standard notation; GP connection aids intuition.  
- Suggested clarifications: precise conditions for monotonic tightening/equality with m, initialization/optimization guidance for Z, and summary of key proof steps in the main text.  
- Originality: applying known determinant inequalities to produce two-sided, nonspectral DPP likelihood bounds (finite + continuous) and using them for variational and MH inference is novel.  
- Significance: removing spectral dependence is practically important and broadens applicability of DPPs, though impact is limited by current empirical scope.  
- Strengths: simple, broadly applicable nonspectral bounds; two-sided bounds enable both variational and exact inference; continuous extension; empirical validation in controlled settings.  
- Weaknesses: limited large-scale empirical validation, no theoretical rates/monotonicity guarantees for tightness as m increases, minimal practical guidance for choosing/optimizing Z and m, and unresolved identifiability issues in L parametrization.  
- Suggested improvements: add large-n finite experiments with runtime/memory comparisons, provide theoretical statements on monotone tightening/equality, include ablations on Z strategies and adaptive m selection, and discuss extensions beyond Gaussian kernels.  
- Acceptance pros: novel bounds removing spectral dependence, enabling inference with clear computational benefits, sound theory, and reasonable empirical validation.  
- Acceptance cons: empirical evaluation lacks scale and benchmarks, theoretical guarantees on bound tightness are limited, and variational instability/identifiability remain concerns.  
- Overall evaluation: solid, potentially impactful contribution that meets criteria for originality and clarity; leans toward acceptance pending stronger empirical validation and theoretical clarifications.
