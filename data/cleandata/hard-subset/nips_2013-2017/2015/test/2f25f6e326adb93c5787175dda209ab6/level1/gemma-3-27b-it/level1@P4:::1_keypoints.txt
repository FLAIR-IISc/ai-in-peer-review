- Core contribution: Derives likelihood bounds for finite and continuous DPPs that avoid spectral decomposition, enabling cheaper variational inference and MCMC.  
- Paper structure: Well-organized, introduces DPPs, treats finite then continuous cases, and connects to related work like GPs.  
- Experiments: Includes a toy example and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset to validate claims.  

Strengths:
- Technical soundness: Derivations are mathematically solid, leveraging operator theory and point process analysis.  
- Novelty: Provides a new approach to DPP inference by avoiding spectral knowledge rather than relying on L-parametrization alone.  
- Clarity: Clear exposition with helpful separation of finite and continuous cases and background material.  
- Significance: Addresses a major computational bottleneck in DPP inference and demonstrates practical utility on real data.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical evaluation: Experiments are few and lack comparisons to existing (spectral) methods and runtime analyses.  
- Interpretability concerns: Parametrizing L reduces interpretability; the paper notes but does not explore mitigation strategies.  
- Tightness of bounds: The paper does not thoroughly analyze how tight or loose Propositions 1 & 2 are and when they might fail.  
- Weak linkage to quadrature methods: The brief mention of quadrature-based bounds as future work feels underdeveloped.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Tackles an important DPP inference bottleneck with a novel, sound approach and clear presentation.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness on real-world data and offers practical tools for practitioners.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical section is insufficiently comprehensive and lacks direct comparisons and runtime metrics.  
- Remaining interpretability issues and unquantified bound tightness weaken the contribution.  

Overall assessment:
- Solid, novel, and technically strong paper that merits acceptance with minor revisions to expand experiments and analyze bound tightness.
