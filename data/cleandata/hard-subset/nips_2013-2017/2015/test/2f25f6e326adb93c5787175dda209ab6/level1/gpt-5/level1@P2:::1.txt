Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes nonspectral, computationally cheap bounds on the determinantal normalizer det(I+L) that appears in DPP likelihoods, both for finite domains (matrix determinant) and continuous domains (Fredholm determinant). The core technical idea is to approximate L with a low-rank matrix Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY built from “inducing” or “pseudo”-inputs Z, and then use determinant inequalities (Seiler–Simon) to obtain a tight, two-sided bound:
- finite case: det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I);
- continuous case: bounds expressed via LZ and Ψ = ∫ LZx LxZ dµ(x).
These bounds avoid computing spectra/eigendecompositions. They enable: (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood for approximate maximum likelihood learning, and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler using upper/lower likelihood bounds, analogous in spirit to Affandi et al. (ICML’14) but without requiring eigenvalues.

The paper clearly situates itself in the DPP literature: Kulesza & Taskar’s foundational treatment; prior learning methods based on spectral information (Affandi et al. ICML’14) or Nyström approximations (AISTATS’13); fixed-point/EM approaches (Gillenwater et al.; Mariet & Sra). It also draws a tight analogy to sparse variational GP methods (Titsias AISTATS’09) in the construction of Q and trace corrections, which is apt and insightful. For continuous DPPs, the use of Fredholm determinants follows standard operator theory (Bornemann).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The finite- and continuous-domain bounds derive from a well-established determinant inequality and a GP-style inducing construction. The resulting sandwich bounds are plausible and appear correct; proofs are deferred to the supplement. The extension to operators with trace-class assumptions is careful and references appropriate functional analysis background.
- Algorithms: The variational objective is straightforward and has O(n m^2) complexity, with Z optimized to tighten the bound. The retrospective MH construction is standard and well explained; replacing spectral bounds of Affandi et al. with the proposed nonspectral bounds is coherent.
- Empirical support: Experiments include (i) a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where the spectrum and Fredholm determinant are known, allowing validation of the MH results; and (ii) a real neuropathy dataset, reproducing the “overdispersion” separation between classes and showing monotone tightening of upper/lower bounds as m increases. However, finite-domain, large-n experiments (where spectral methods are most costly) are missing, and head-to-head comparisons in accuracy/time with spectral or Nyström baselines are limited.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized: finite case first, then inference methods, then continuous case. The connection to sparse GPs is explicit and helps intuition. Notation is heavy but standard for DPPs. A few aspects could be clarified:
- Provide the precise conditions under which the finite-domain bounds tighten monotonically with m and when equality is achieved (e.g., Z spanning the column space of L).
- Offer guidance on initializing and optimizing Z, and on choosing m adaptively in practice.
- Summarize the proofs’ key steps (beyond the reference) in the main text to aid reader confidence.

Originality
While the determinant inequality itself is known, its application to produce two-sided, nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods—covering both finite and continuous cases—and leveraging them for variational and retrospective MH inference appears novel. The continuous-domain treatment with computable Ψ for Gaussian–Gaussian settings is a useful extension beyond prior work focusing on finite sets or spectral approximations.

Significance
Removing dependence on eigenvalue computations is practically important for DPP learning, especially for large finite domains and continuous DPPs where spectra are unavailable. The proposed bounds are easy to evaluate and integrate smoothly into standard inference paradigms. The approach could broaden applicability of DPPs in machine learning and spatial statistics. That said, the empirical section underplays the potential scalability advantage by not including substantial large-n finite experiments or rigorous benchmark comparisons; stronger empirical evidence would increase impact.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable, nonspectral bounds with a clear GP-variational analogy.
- Two-sided bounds enable both variational and exact (retrospective MH) inference.
- Extension to continuous DPPs with Fredholm determinants, with computable ingredients for popular Gaussian kernels.
- Empirical validation that variational bounds tighten with m and that retrospective MH matches “ideal” MH in a setting with known determinant.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope: no large-scale finite-domain benchmarks; sparse head-to-head comparisons with spectral/Nyström methods on accuracy vs. runtime.
- No theoretical rate or monotonicity guarantees for tightness as m increases (beyond intuition from GP literature).
- Practical guidance on choosing/optimizing Z and m is minimal; sensitivity analyses are limited.
- Identifiability issues when parametrizing L are acknowledged but not mitigated; the variational method can struggle on some parameters (e.g., κ).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add large-n finite experiments (e.g., summarization or recommendation tasks) comparing against spectral and Nyström approximations, reporting wall-clock time, memory, and predictive/performance metrics.
- Provide theoretical statements (or counterexamples) on monotonic tightening, and characterize when equality holds (e.g., Q = L at sufficient m).
- Include ablations on Z initialization strategies, optimization schemes, and automatic m selection (e.g., stopping when bound gap falls below a tolerance).
- Discuss applicability beyond Gaussian kernels, including computability of Ψ and numerical stability.

Pro/con list for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel, elegant bounds removing spectral dependence; applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Enabling technology for variational and MH inference with clear computational benefits.
- Sound theory foundation and reasonable empirical validation.
- Clear exposition and connections to established GP variational methods.

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation lacks large-scale and comparative studies to fully substantiate practical gains.
- Limited theoretical guarantees on bound tightness rates and practical guidance for Z/m.
- Variational instability for some parameters highlights identifiability issues that remain unaddressed.

Overall evaluation
A solid and potentially impactful contribution that addresses a key computational bottleneck in DPP learning with a simple, general technique. With stronger empirical validation and some theoretical clarifications, this could be a very influential paper. As it stands, it meets the NIPS criteria well on originality and clarity, adequately on quality, and moderately on significance due to limited experiments. I lean toward acceptance.
