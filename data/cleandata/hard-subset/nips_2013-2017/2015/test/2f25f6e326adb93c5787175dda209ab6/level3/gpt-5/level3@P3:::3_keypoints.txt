- Proposal: nonspectral inducing-point determinant bounds for DPP normalizers in finite and continuous settings, yielding a variational lower bound and retrospective MH that avoid eigendecompositions.  
- Key idea: introduce inducing inputs Z and use determinant inequalities to bound det(I+L), tightening bounds by increasing m=|Z|.  
- Relation to prior work: adapts sparse GP inducing-point methods to DPP normalizers and contrasts with spectral approaches that use power iterations/eigendecompositions.  
- Strength: conceptual novelty in applying inducing-point ideas and operator-level extension to continuous DPPs.  
- Strength: practical, cheap-to-evaluate bounds (O(n m^2)) that are easy to tighten and enable principled retrospective MH.  
- Strength: unified treatment of finite and continuous cases with clear links to sparse GPs.  
- Strength: empirical promise—indicative speedups and workable inference on spatial data; retrospective MH matches ideal MH on toy case.  
- Weakness: method description is sometimes rough and notation/assumptions (K vs L, Ψ, trace-class) are not introduced early/clearly.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is limited (few datasets, limited baselines, incomplete runtime/accuracy analysis).  
- Weakness: VI appears brittle on the toy example and the paper lacks guidance on when VI should succeed or fail.  
- Quality: technical contributions are sound and grounded in known determinant inequalities, but main-text derivations need tightening for reproducibility.  
- Quality: claims about speed/accuracy require stronger quantitative support and broader comparisons.  
- Clarity: generally readable but needs explicit definitions of marginal kernel K, L-ensembles, relation L=K(I−K)−1, and assumptions for Ψ/Fredholm determinants.  
- Clarity: recommend defining K and L at first appearance and trimming inconsistent discussion about learning K vs L.  
- Originality: non-spectral determinant bounds with inducing inputs and the VI + retrospective MH synthesis for DPPs appear novel.  
- Significance: could broaden DPP applicability to larger finite sets and continuous domains if empirically validated.  
- Major request 1: provide per-iteration and end-to-end complexities for VI and retrospective MH (functions of n, m, T, d), including costs for optimizing Z and increasing m, plus wall-clock vs held-out log-likelihood.  
- Major request 2: add baselines/comparisons: spectral MH with power iterations, Nyström approximations, exact small-n eigendecomposition, EM and fixed-point training; report likelihood gaps, parameter error, and runtimes.  
- Major request 3: characterize when VI works/fails (identifiability, sensitivity to m, local optima, bound tightness vs likelihood curvature) and give practical diagnostics (restarts, bound gaps, synthetic calibration).  
- Major request 4: evaluate using inducing inputs equal to observed points (Z=Y or nearest-grid projection) and compare bound tightness to optimized Z.  
- Major request 5: provide rule-of-thumb guidance for m and Z initialization (e.g., m proportional to expected cardinality or covering numbers; k-means++, farthest-point, or intensity-based seeding).  
- Minor comment: include a 2–3 sentence primer defining K and L and their roles in DPP parametrizations early in the paper.  
- Pros summary: novel, elegant nonspectral bounds; practical VI and MH algorithms tied to sparse GPs; promising speedups and continuous-domain applicability.  
- Cons summary: insufficient empirical evaluation, unclear notation/background, and limited practical guidance on VI reliability and inducing-point choices.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept pending clearer exposition and substantially stronger experimental validation and guidance.
