## Review of “Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge”

This paper addresses a key challenge in applying Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) – efficient likelihood-based inference – by proposing novel bounds on the DPP likelihood that avoid costly spectral decompositions. The core idea is simple yet effective: leveraging bounds inspired by Gaussian process variational inference to circumvent the need for eigenvalue computations, a major bottleneck in existing DPP inference methods. This is a timely and well-executed contribution with clear potential for impact within the NIPS community and beyond.

The paper effectively introduces DPPs, their growing importance across various fields (random matrix theory, machine learning applications like diversity modeling in search results and news summaries), and the challenges associated with learning their parameters. The authors correctly identify the two primary obstacles: the disconnect between parametrizing the kernel *K* (which has clear interpretability) and the kernel *L* appearing in the likelihood, and the computational burden of the spectral decomposition required to evaluate the likelihood. This work directly tackles the latter issue, focusing on providing tractable bounds for the likelihood when the kernel *L* is directly parameterized.

The technical contribution – Propositions 1 and 2 – providing the bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, is significant. The use of pseudo-inputs to refine these bounds is a creative and insightful approach.  The connections drawn to variational sparse Gaussian processes enhance understanding and highlight the relationship between these established methods and the proposed approach.  The paper is careful to note the limitations and the trade-offs involved, particularly regarding the need to optimize the pseudo-inputs.

The experimental validation is well-constructed and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method. The toy Gaussian continuous experiment effectively showcases the MCMC implementation and demonstrates the challenges of parameter identifiability when directly parametrizing *L*. The application to the diabetic neuropathy dataset provides a compelling demonstration of the method’s applicability to a real-world problem, showing convergence of results with increasing numbers of inducing variables, and reproducing findings from previous work.  The inclusion of results using both variational inference and MCMC strengthens the evaluation.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivations appear mathematically sound, building on established results in Gaussian process inference and matrix theory. The provided connections to existing literature (e.g., [11, 14]) are well-placed and strengthen the technical foundation.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and results. The organization is logical, progressing from problem definition to technical contribution, experimental validation, and discussion.
* **Originality:** The proposed bounds represent a novel approach to DPP inference, offering a practical solution to a significant computational bottleneck. The use of pseudo-inputs is a clever and effective technique.
* **Significance:**  The ability to perform efficient inference for DPPs without spectral decomposition unlocks potential applications in various areas, including diversity modeling, recommendation systems, and spatial statistics. The work addresses a real-world challenge and provides a valuable contribution to the field.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameterization Discussion:** While the paper emphasizes the trade-off between interpretability and tractability in choosing to parameterize *L* instead of *K*, this discussion feels somewhat superficial. A deeper exploration of potential avenues for bridging this gap would be beneficial.
* **Supplementary Material:** The authors mention an "extended paper version" as supplementary material. It would be more standard and useful to provide a dedicated supplement in a readily accessible format (e.g., a PDF) containing detailed proofs, additional experimental results, and potentially code.
* **Future Work – K Dependence:** The suggestion to explore bounding the determinant directly in terms of *K* is excellent. It would further strengthen the work to briefly discuss the potential challenges and limitations of such an approach, and whether the proposed pseudo-input method would scale effectively in that scenario.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Strong experimental validation.
* Potential for significant impact in the DPP research community.
* Addresses a practical bottleneck limiting the application of DPPs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The discussion surrounding the choice of kernel parametrization feels incomplete.
* The supplementary material format is non-standard.
* While the results are promising, the paper doesn’t explicitly demonstrate a large-scale application exceeding previous spectral methods in terms of dataset size or complexity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and effective solution. The technical contributions are significant, the presentation is clear, and the experimental validation is compelling. The minor weaknesses are easily addressable through revisions. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting a standard supplemental document and a more in-depth discussion of the parameterization trade-offs. The work is likely to be well-received by the NIPS community and has the potential to stimulate further research in this area.
