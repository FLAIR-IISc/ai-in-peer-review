Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes nonspectral, computable upper and lower bounds on log det(I+L) for DPP likelihoods, both on finite sets and continuous domains. The central idea is to use an inducing-point construction Q = LYZ LZ−1 LZY (with pseudo-inputs Z not constrained to be a subset of Y) and a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975) to obtain bounds det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ det(I+L) ≤ det(Q+I). These are then used to derive tractable bounds for the DPP likelihood, enabling (i) a variational lower bound for approximate maximum likelihood, and (ii) retrospective MH sampling with bracketing of the MH acceptance probability that can be tightened by increasing m and optimizing Z. The approach is extended to continuous DPPs by introducing Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x) and showing analogous bounds involving det(LZ+Ψ), thereby avoiding Fredholm spectrum approximations.

This work builds on variational sparse GP methodology (Titsias, 2009) and contrasts with DPP inference methods relying on spectral decompositions and Nyström approximations (Affandi et al., ICML 2013; Affandi et al., AISTATS 2013), EM/fixed-point methods (Gillenwater et al., NIPS 2014; Mariet & Sra, NIPS 2015), and numerical Fredholm determinant approximations (Bornemann, 2010). The key novelty is to bound DPP normalizers without explicitly approximating the spectrum, and to make the continuous-domain case practical whenever Ψ is tractable (e.g., Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures).

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple, elegant bounds yielding practical algorithms that avoid expensive eigendecompositions, yet retain correctness via retrospective MH.
- Creative and well-motivated use of pseudo-inputs to control the tightness of bounds on functionals of positive definite kernels; clear analogy to inducing-point GPs.
- General framework covering both finite and continuous DPPs; the latter is especially valuable as continuous spectra are often analytically intractable.
- Experiments are well chosen to illustrate behavior: a toy case where the exact spectrum is known validates the MH approach; a real spatial dataset shows useful variational behavior and interpretable overdispersion metrics.
- Discussion is candid about when the variational approximation may fail (e.g., weak identifiability of κ when parameterizing L), and the retrospective MH provides a robust alternative.
- Timely and broadly relevant to ML audiences working with repulsive point processes and diversity modeling.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Tightness/consistency: While the bounds provably tighten with larger m, the paper does not quantify convergence rates or provide practical guidance for choosing m; some empirical diagnostics (e.g., gap vs. m curves across several datasets) would help.
- Dependence on Z: Optimization over pseudo-inputs is crucial, but initialization strategies, optimization stability, and computational costs are not deeply analyzed. Comparing heuristic placements (k-means, k-DPP, grid) against optimized Z would strengthen the case.
- Scope of Ψ: In the continuous case, tractability hinges on computing Ψ. Beyond Gaussian-Gaussian, guidance on when Ψ is available in closed form or how to approximate it (with error control) would broaden applicability.
- Comparisons: Include more direct baselines for finite DPP learning at scale (e.g., Nyström-based spectral bracketing and EM methods) with wall-clock and accuracy comparisons, to better position the gains of nonspectral bounds.
- K vs L: The paper emphasizes interpretability benefits of K but ultimately commits to L throughout; the discussion on linking K and L without spectral knowledge is brief. A small experiment or analysis exploring whether the proposed bounds can be expressed directly in terms of K (and when that is advantageous) would be valuable.
- Practicalities: Numerical stability (e.g., for log-det and trace computations), and complexity for multiple realizations with large |Yt| (cost of det(LYt)) deserve more detailed treatment.
- Minor: Please provide a standard supplementary PDF (proofs, algorithmic details, gradients) in addition to an extended version link, per conference norms.

Quality:
- Theoretical development is sound and well grounded in operator inequalities. The extension to continuous DPPs is carefully stated with appropriate assumptions (trace-class, Mercer conditions).
- Algorithms are correctly derived and the retrospective MH construction is standard and appropriate.
- Experiments validate correctness and illustrate limitations, though broader scaling comparisons would improve the empirical case.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized; the analogy to sparse GPs is clear and helpful.
- Suggestions: add brief intuitive explanation of the determinant inequality; present pseudo-code for variational and MH procedures; provide guidance on choosing m and initializing Z; clarify when Ψ is computable.

Originality:
- New application of determinant inequalities with inducing-point constructions to DPP normalizers, avoiding spectral knowledge. The continuous-domain bounds are particularly novel.
- Distinct from prior spectral and Nyström-based approaches.

Significance:
- Likely to impact practitioners who avoid DPPs due to spectral costs, especially in continuous settings.
- The pseudo-inputs perspective and nonspectral bracketing could extend to other models with log-det normalizers beyond DPPs.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general idea that removes a major barrier (spectral computations) for DPP inference.
- Solid theory and practical algorithms for both variational and MCMC settings.
- Demonstrated on relevant tasks with insightful analysis of when VI can fail.
- Broad applicability and potential extensions beyond DPPs.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited empirical comparisons and scaling evidence versus strong baselines.
- Dependence on tractable Ψ in the continuous case narrows immediate applicability.
- Limited guidance on selecting m and optimizing Z; no convergence-rate analysis of the bounds.

Minor comments:
- Clarify the role and identifiability of κ under L-parameterization; consider reporting posterior correlations between κ and lengthscales.
- Explore inducing-point selection strategies informed by base measure or k-DPP sampling.
- Consider an experiment bounding the determinant directly in terms of K, and discuss computational trade-offs and scalability of the pseudo-input method in that parameterization.

Overall recommendation: I lean toward acceptance. The paper offers a clear, broadly useful contribution that advances DPP inference and is likely to stimulate follow-up work in nonspectral bounding and inducing-point methods for point processes.
