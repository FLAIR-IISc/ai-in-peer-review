## Review of “Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge”

This paper addresses the challenging problem of inference in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), specifically tackling the computational bottleneck arising from the spectral decomposition required for likelihood evaluation. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood, circumventing the need for eigendecomposition and enabling efficient variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The core idea leverages inducing inputs, inspired by variational Gaussian processes, to approximate the likelihood and facilitate tractable inference.

The work builds upon the established literature on DPPs [1, 2, 3, 4, 5], which have gained traction in diverse fields like random matrix theory, combinatorics, and machine learning for modeling diversity. Recent applications include modeling diversity in news summaries [3] and search results [7], highlighting the growing need for efficient inference techniques. The authors correctly identify the key impediment – the computationally expensive spectral decomposition – and propose a pragmatic solution by directly parametrizing the kernel *L* rather than *K*, a common strategy in practice [3, 7] despite sacrificing some interpretability.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant bottleneck:** The paper tackles a core computational issue in DPP inference, making the method more applicable to large-scale datasets.
* **Novel bounds:** Deriving bounds on the likelihood without relying on spectral decomposition is a valuable contribution. The use of inducing inputs, mirroring techniques successful in Gaussian processes [11], is a sensible approach.
* **Two inference paradigms:** Offering both variational inference and MCMC methods provides flexibility for different application scenarios and computational constraints.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The paper is theoretically sound, with proofs provided in the supplementary material (though these are not assessed in detail here).
* **Empirical validation:** The experimental section, while needing strengthening (see below), demonstrates the feasibility of the proposed methods on both a synthetic dataset and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of clarity in method description:** The description of the inference procedures, particularly the retrospective MCMC, feels somewhat rushed. More detail on the optimization process and practical considerations would be beneficial. The connection between the bounds and the actual MCMC acceptance criterion needs to be more clearly explained.
* **Insufficient empirical evaluation:** The experiments, while present, are insufficient to fully assess the effectiveness of the proposed methods. A critical omission is a comparison of computational complexity and wall-clock time between the proposed methods and existing approaches (e.g., [7]).
* **Limited comparison to alternatives:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to other approximate and exact inference techniques in terms of approximation quality and runtime. Without this, it’s difficult to gauge the true benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Variational inference instability:** The experiment on the synthetic data reveals large parameter estimation errors in variational inference. The paper doesn’t adequately address *when* this method is expected to work or fail, and *why* the instability occurs. This limits the confidence in the variational approach.
* **Inducing point sensitivity:** The choice and placement of inducing points are crucial, yet the paper provides limited guidance on this.  How does performance change when inducing points are initialized as the actual sampled points in the toy example?  What rules-of-thumb can be offered for selecting the number of inducing points and their initial placement?
* **Inconsistent discussion:** The discussion of advantages of directly learning marginal kernel parameters appears somewhat contradictory given the paper’s own approach.  This section could be streamlined.
* **Missing definitions:** The paper references matrices *K* and *L* without fully defining them, which may confuse readers unfamiliar with DPPs. A brief explanation of the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would improve accessibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant practical challenge in DPP inference.
* The proposed bounds are a novel and potentially valuable contribution.
* The method offers a practical alternative to computationally intensive spectral decomposition techniques.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The method description is somewhat rough and lacks sufficient detail.
* The empirical evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the approach compared to existing methods.
* The instability of variational inference raises concerns about its general applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to addressing a critical bottleneck in DPP inference. However, the current presentation requires significant improvement. The method description needs to be clarified, the empirical evaluation strengthened with comparisons to existing techniques, and the limitations of the variational inference approach addressed.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **conditional acceptance** with major revisions. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the method description, strengthening the empirical evaluation by including comprehensive comparisons, and addressing the limitations of the variational inference approach. If these revisions are adequately addressed, the paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field. Specifically, the authors need to provide a clear benchmark showing the computational gains relative to existing methods.  More thorough analysis of the impact of inducing point selection would also be beneficial.
