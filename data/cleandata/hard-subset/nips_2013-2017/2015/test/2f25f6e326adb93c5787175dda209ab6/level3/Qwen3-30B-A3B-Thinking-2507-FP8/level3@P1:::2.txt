

This paper addresses a critical bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the intractable normalizing constant involving large matrix or Fredholm determinants. The authors propose novel, nonspectral bounds on these determinants that avoid expensive spectral decompositions, leveraging pseudo-inputs (inducing variables) to create tight, cheap-to-evaluate bounds applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs. This builds directly on recent NIPS work (e.g., Affandi et al., NIPS 2013; Gillenwater et al., NIPS 2012) that struggled with spectral computation costs, while offering a more general solution than prior spectral approximations [7].

**Strengths.** The core contribution—Propositions 1 and 2—is technically sound, elegantly derived from determinant inequalities [14], and well-motivated. The bounds avoid spectral decomposition entirely, enabling efficient variational inference (O(nm²) vs. O(n³) for exact likelihood) and retrospective MCMC sampling, as demonstrated in Sections 3 and 5. The experimental validation is thorough: the synthetic Gaussian experiment (Section 5.1) confirms correctness via comparison with exact spectral methods, while the diabetic neuropathy dataset (Section 5.2) shows practical utility, with overdispersion measures converging as inducing points increase (m=50→1200). Crucially, the paper exposes a key limitation of variational methods (high parameter variability) through empirical failure modes, contrasting with MCMC’s robustness—this nuanced analysis significantly strengthens the contribution. The approach is broadly applicable beyond DPPs (e.g., to GP inference), and the pseudo-input technique offers a fresh perspective on kernel functionals.

**Weaknesses.** The paper briefly mentions the K vs. L parametrization issue (Section 1) but does not resolve it, leaving the interpretability trade-off unaddressed despite its relevance to prior work [4, 7]. While the supplemental proof is provided, the authors should adopt a standard supplement format (not an extended paper) for clarity. The experimental section could better quantify computational savings (e.g., runtime comparisons with spectral methods [7]).

**Originality.** The nonspectral bound framework is novel; prior work relied on spectral approximations [7, 8], which are infeasible for large-scale or continuous domains. The use of pseudo-inputs to tighten bounds is a creative adaptation of GP techniques [11] to DPPs, distinct from Nyström approximations [13]. The continuous-domain extension (Section 4) is a significant contribution, as most DPP inference literature focuses on finite sets. This work clearly differentiates from NIPS 2012–2013 DPP papers by solving the spectral bottleneck without approximation.

**Significance.** DPPs are increasingly used for diversity modeling (e.g., news summarization [3], search results [7]), but likelihood-based inference has been limited by computational barriers. This paper enables scalable, principled inference for large and continuous DPPs, directly addressing a gap noted in [4, 7]. The results are immediately applicable to real-world problems like the diabetic neuropathy analysis here, and the methods could accelerate DPP adoption in computer vision, NLP, and spatial statistics. The bounds’ generality suggests potential impact beyond DPPs (e.g., in GP-based models).

**Quality & Clarity.** The theoretical foundation is rigorous (bounds proven via determinant inequalities [14]), and the experimental design effectively tests both validity and practicality. The paper is well-structured, with clear progression from finite to continuous domains. Minor clarity issues: the motivation for parametrizing L (vs. K) is underdeveloped, and the supplement’s format should be standardized.

**Recommendation.** This paper makes a strong, timely contribution to scalable DPP inference. The technical quality is high, the experimental validation is robust, and the significance for both theory and applications is clear. The minor weaknesses (parametrization discussion, supplement format) do not undermine the core advance. The work aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on principled, scalable ML methods. **Accept.** 

**Pro:** Novel nonspectral bounds; efficient inference (variational/MCMC); rigorous validation; clear experimental insights (e.g., variational failure modes); broad applicability.  
**Con:** Superficial treatment of K vs. L parametrization; non-standard supplement; limited runtime comparisons.
