The paper "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge" presents a novel approach to inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that does not require spectral knowledge. The authors propose new bounds on the likelihood of a DPP, both for finite and continuous domains, which can be used for variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods.

The main contribution of the paper is the derivation of bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that do not depend on the spectral decomposition of the operator L. The authors draw inspiration from sparse approximations to Gaussian processes using inducing variables and propose a new bound that relies on a nontrivial inequality on determinants. The bound is shown to be cheap to evaluate and can be used for variational inference and MCMC methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough introduction to DPPs and their applications, as well as a detailed explanation of the proposed bounds and their derivation. The experimental evaluation is also well-constructed and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address a timely and relevant problem in the field. DPPs are a popular model in machine learning, and the lack of efficient inference methods has been a major limitation. The proposed approach provides a significant improvement over existing methods and has the potential to be widely adopted.

The paper also has some minor weaknesses. For example, the authors emphasize the importance of parameterizing the kernel L instead of K, but do not provide a clear explanation of why this is the case. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for future work.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to inference for DPPs that does not require spectral knowledge. The proposed bounds are new and have not been previously published. The paper also makes connections with variational sparse Gaussian processes, which is a novel contribution.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a major limitation in the field of DPPs. The proposed approach has the potential to be widely adopted and could lead to significant advances in the field. The paper is also well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow and understand.
