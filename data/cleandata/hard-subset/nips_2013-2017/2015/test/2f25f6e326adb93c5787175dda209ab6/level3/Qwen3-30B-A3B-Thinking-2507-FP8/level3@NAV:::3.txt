

This paper proposes novel approximate inference methods for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid spectral decomposition by using inducing inputs to bound the likelihood, presenting both variational inference and approximate MCMC approaches with empirical speedups. The work builds on recent advances in DPP inference (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2013; Gillenwater et al., NIPS 2012) but addresses the computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition in likelihood evaluation.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with a clear theoretical foundation, deriving non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood for both finite and continuous domains. The proposed variational inference approach operates in O(nm²) time, while the MCMC method provides exact sampling with retrospective acceptance. The empirical results on a Gaussian toy example and diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrate computational benefits, though the evaluation requires significant strengthening.

Major weaknesses include insufficient empirical comparison. The paper lacks: (1) computational complexity analysis and wall-clock time comparisons between variational inference and MCMC; (2) comparisons to other approximate/exact methods in terms of both approximation quality and runtime; (3) clarification on when variational inference fails (as evidenced by large parameter estimation errors on toy data) and why it relies on approximate MCMC. The authors should also address how methods perform when inducing inputs are chosen as actual sampled points, and provide practical guidance for selecting the number and placement of inducing points.

Minor issues include undefined matrices K and L (briefly explaining marginal kernel and L-ensembles would help uninformed readers), and an inconsistent discussion of advantages of learning marginal kernel parameters that conflicts with the paper's approach.

While the core idea is promising and the paper has merit, the insufficient empirical evaluation and rough method description prevent it from meeting NIPS standards. The authors should strengthen their evaluation by providing comprehensive comparisons to existing methods, clarifying the scope of variational inference's applicability, and adding practical guidelines for implementing their approach. With these improvements, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the literature on probabilistic modeling and point processes.
