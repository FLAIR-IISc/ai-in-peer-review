

# Review for "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the intractable normalization constant that appears in the likelihood. DPPs are attractive models for capturing diversity in point patterns, but likelihood-based learning is computationally prohibitive due to the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors propose a novel approach to derive bounds on the likelihood that avoid spectral decomposition entirely, drawing inspiration from inducing-point techniques in Gaussian process inference.

The paper makes two main contributions: (1) a new set of nonspectral bounds on the likelihood for DPPs over finite sets, and (2) an extension of these bounds to continuous domains. The key insight (Proposition 1) provides lower and upper bounds on the likelihood using an arbitrary set of "pseudo-inputs" (inducing points), without requiring spectral decomposition. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be used for both variational inference (which is computationally efficient) and Markov chain Monte Carlo inference (which is more expensive but exact). The paper establishes a compelling connection to sparse Gaussian process inference, which enriches the paper's theoretical grounding.

The paper is technically strong and well-written, with clear exposition of the theoretical contributions. The main theoretical result (Proposition 1) is a significant contribution that advances the state of the art in DPP inference. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets (including a diabetic neuropathy spatial pattern dataset) provides convincing evidence of the method's practical utility. The authors demonstrate that their approach can recover meaningful parameters from data and that the variational inference method converges as the number of inducing points increases.

The paper's strengths are numerous: the theoretical contribution is novel and well-justified, the connection to Gaussian process inference is insightful and well-explained, the experimental results are thorough and relevant, and the paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The method's ability to avoid spectral decomposition is particularly valuable, as it eliminates the need for power iterations or other expensive spectral approximation methods.

However, there are two important concerns. First, the paper's claim that the approach is "cheap to evaluate" may not be fully supported. Replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization over the inducing points and their locations could result in similar or higher computational costs, especially as the number of inducing points increases. The paper does not provide a detailed computational complexity analysis comparing the proposed method with [7] in terms of actual runtime.

Second, the paper does not adequately address the potential convergence issues of the bounds. The optimization of inducing points is likely non-convex, and the paper does not provide guarantees that increasing the number of inducing points will always tighten the bounds. This is particularly concerning for MCMC, where valid acceptance decisions require tight bounds, and local minima in the optimization could prevent correct sampling.

Additional minor concerns include: Figure 1 lacks axis labels, which makes it difficult to interpret the results; the key proof of Proposition 1 is relegated to the appendix (it should be in the main text); and there's a minor typo ("the variational lower is" instead of "the variational lower bound is").

In terms of the four criteria:

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions. The bounds are correctly derived and validated through experiments. The authors are careful to discuss limitations of their approach.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and organized. The connection to Gaussian process inference is particularly helpful for readers familiar with that literature. However, the presentation could be improved by moving the key proof to the main text and fixing the minor formatting issues.

**Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution by avoiding spectral decomposition for DPP inference, which distinguishes it from prior work (notably [7]). The connection to inducing-point techniques for Gaussian processes is novel and valuable.

**Significance**: The approach has the potential to significantly speed up inference for DPPs, which are used in diverse applications (news diversity, search engines, spatial modeling). The connection to Gaussian process inference broadens the applicability of the method.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical contribution that avoids spectral decomposition
- Clear connection to Gaussian process inference techniques
- Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets
- Well-written and technically sound

Arguments against acceptance:
- Potential computational cost comparison with prior work is not adequately addressed
- Convergence guarantees for the bounds are not fully established
- Minor presentation issues that could be easily fixed

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling. The theoretical insights are valuable, and the method has clear practical utility. The concerns raised are important but not fatal to the paper's contribution. The authors have made a significant step toward making DPP inference more tractable, and the paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the presentation issues and provide more detailed computational comparisons in the final version.
