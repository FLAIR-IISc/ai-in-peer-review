This paper introduces novel and efficient bounds on the likelihood of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a key computational bottleneck in DPP inference – the intractable determinant calculations. The authors skillfully leverage ideas from variational inference for Gaussian Processes to derive these bounds, which crucially do *not* rely on spectral decomposition, a significant advantage over existing methods. This work is a valuable contribution to the field, offering a practical pathway for learning DPP parameters, and demonstrating a creative use of pseudo-inputs to approximate kernel functionals.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It begins with a comprehensive introduction to DPPs, outlining their applications and the challenges in parametric inference. The problem statement is clearly articulated: while DPPs offer compelling modeling capabilities, likelihood-based inference is hindered by the need for spectral decomposition and the intractable normalizing constant in the likelihood function. The authors position their work within the existing literature, acknowledging the common practice of directly parameterizing the kernel *L* rather than *K* for computational tractability, while also noting the trade-off in interpretability. 

The technical contribution – Propositions 1 and 2 – are central to the paper's strength. The bounds on the determinant, inspired by sparse GP approximations, are presented clearly and justified with appropriate citations. The subsequent sections demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms, offering practical inference methods. The extension to continuous DPPs in Section 4 is well-motivated and adds to the generality of the approach.

The experimental evaluation is robust and insightful. The toy Gaussian experiment effectively validates the MCMC implementation, demonstrating consistency with known results and highlighting the limitations of the variational approach. The application to a real-world dataset of diabetic neuropathy, following the setup of prior work, showcases the practical utility of the proposed method and provides a compelling comparison with existing techniques. The authors transparently discuss the convergence of the variational approach with varying numbers of pseudo-inputs and observe consistent results across different settings. The visualization of optimized pseudo-inputs provides valuable intuition into the method's behavior.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations are clear and well-justified. The bounds are novel and offer a significant computational advantage.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with all the nuances of DPPs.
*   **Originality:** The approach of using bounds on the determinant, inspired by sparse GP approximations, is a novel and insightful contribution. The avoidance of spectral decomposition is a key differentiator.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental challenge in DPP inference, making these models more accessible for practical applications. The generality of the approach suggests potential applications beyond DPPs.

**Weaknesses:**

*   While the paper notes the trade-off between parameterizing *K* versus *L*, this aspect feels somewhat underdeveloped. A more in-depth discussion of the implications of this choice, or potential methods for bridging the gap, could strengthen the work.
*   The supplement is currently presented as an extended version of the paper. While comprehensive, a standard, concise supplemental material document, focusing on proofs and detailed experimental settings, would be more aligned with typical NIPS practice.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and original solution to a significant problem in the field of DPPs.
*   The proposed bounds offer a substantial computational improvement over existing methods.
*   The experimental validation is thorough and demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The clarity of the writing and the well-structured presentation make the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The discussion regarding the parameterization of *K* vs. *L* could be more detailed.
*   The supplemental material format could be improved.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs by providing efficient and practical methods for likelihood-based inference. The clarity of presentation and the thorough experimental evaluation further enhance the quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above – specifically, expanding on the *K* vs. *L* parameterization discussion and providing a standard supplemental material document.
