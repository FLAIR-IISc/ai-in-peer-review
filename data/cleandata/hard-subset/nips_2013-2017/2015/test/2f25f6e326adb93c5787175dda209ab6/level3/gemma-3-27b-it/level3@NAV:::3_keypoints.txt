- Proposes bounding DPP likelihood using inducing inputs to avoid expensive spectral decompositions, enabling variational inference and approximate MCMC.
- Builds on sparse Gaussian process techniques, extending them to the DPP setting.
- Paper is well-motivated and clearly outlines the motivation and advantages of avoiding spectral methods.
- Technical exposition is rough; matrices K and L are introduced without prior definition.
- Connection between variational inference and approximation accuracy is under-explained; toy example shows large parameter estimation error for variational method.
- Reliance on approximate MCMC to "correct" variational errors is not fully justified.
- Needs clear explanation of when and why the variational approach is expected to succeed or fail.
- Empirical evaluation is insufficient and lacks sensitivity analysis to inducing point placement.
- Missing experiments where inducing inputs are the actual sampled points.
- No comparisons to other approximate or exact DPP inference methods in terms of approximation quality and runtime.
- Diabetic neuropathy example lacks quantitative runtime comparison to spectral methods (e.g., [7]).
- Provides no guidance or rule-of-thumb for selecting number of inducing points or their initialization/placement.
- Discussion of learning marginal kernel parameters early in Section 2 feels contradictory to the paper’s main approach and should be streamlined.
- For acceptance: addresses a relevant, challenging problem with potentially impactful methodology.
- Against acceptance: unclear method description, weak empirical validation, and unresolved practical questions.
- Recommendation: reject in current form; revise for clarity, stronger experiments, and answers to open practical issues.
