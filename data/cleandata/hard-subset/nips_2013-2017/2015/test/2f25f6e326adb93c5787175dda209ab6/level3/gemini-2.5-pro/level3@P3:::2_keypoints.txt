- Problem: Likelihood-based inference for DPPs is hampered by an intractable normalizing constant (determinant of L+I or Fredholm det(I+L)).  
- Prior work: Spectral approximations (e.g., Affandi et al. 2014) are computationally expensive and mainly finite-case.  
- Main contribution: New non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the determinant using m pseudo-inputs (inducing points) adapted from sparse GPs.  
- Complexity: Bounds are computationally efficient, scaling as O(n m^2), and can be tightened by increasing m.  
- Inference methods: Enables a fast variational approach (maximize lower bound) and an asymptotically exact MCMC via retrospective sampling.  
- Generality: Framework covers both finite and continuous DPPs and is broadly applicable.  
- Validation: Experiments on synthetic and real data (including 1D Fredholm-computable case and diabetic neuropathy dataset) demonstrate correctness and practicality.  
- Empirical insight: 1D experiment confirms MCMC correctness and reveals a variational failure mode (parameter non-identifiability).  
- Practical guidance: Analysis shows bounds and parameter estimates converge as m increases, assisting users in choosing m.  
- Originality/significance: The approach is novel, elegant, and addresses a major barrier to wider DPP use.  
- Technical quality: Derivation is solid, leveraging a determinant inequality from mathematical physics and extending to continuous settings.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, structured from finite to continuous cases, and clearly connects to sparse GP intuition.  
- Broader impact: Technique of pseudo-input bounds could apply to other models with large kernel determinants or operator traces.  
- Weakness: Emphasis on K vs L parametrization feels unresolved; working with K directly is not addressed.  
- Weakness: Supplementary proofs presentation should be provided as a standard separate supplement for ease of access.  
- Future work: Investigate bounding det(I−K) to enable inference on K and analyze how required m scales with data size n or pattern complexity.
