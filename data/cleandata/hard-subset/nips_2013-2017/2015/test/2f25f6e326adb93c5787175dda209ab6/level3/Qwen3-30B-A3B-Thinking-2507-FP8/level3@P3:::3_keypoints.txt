- Problem: DPP inference is hindered by an intractable normalizing constant requiring spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix.  
- Proposed solution: Use inducing inputs to derive non-spectral bounds on the likelihood, enabling variational inference and approximate MCMC without spectral decomposition.  
- Main contribution: Derivation of non-spectral likelihood bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) that avoid spectral decomposition.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on sparse Gaussian process techniques and extends them to DPPs.  
- Empirical claims: Demonstrated on toy and real datasets (including diabetic neuropathy) and validated MCMC on a Gaussian-Gaussian toy example.  
- Strength: The approach addresses a real computational bottleneck with theoretically sound bounds derived from determinant inequalities.  
- Weakness 1 (method clarity): Paper introduces K and L without defining them or explaining their relationship, causing confusion.  
- Weakness 2 (empirical evaluation): Lacks critical comparisons — need runtime/complexity comparisons between VI and MCMC, comparisons to other approximate and exact methods, and tests with inducing inputs equal to sampled points.  
- Weakness 3 (variational performance): VI shows large parameter estimation variability on toy data (κ varies widely); paper doesn’t clarify when VI succeeds or fails.  
- Weakness 4 (practical guidance): No rules-of-thumb for choosing number or placement/initialization of inducing points; unclear how observed “neat grids” arise or generalize.  
- Weakness 5 (kernel learning discussion): Inconsistent/confusing discussion about learning marginal kernel parameters since the method parametrizes L not K.  
- Recommendation: Paper has merit but requires significant revisions — improve method description (define K/L), expand empirical comparisons, and provide practical implementation guidance.
