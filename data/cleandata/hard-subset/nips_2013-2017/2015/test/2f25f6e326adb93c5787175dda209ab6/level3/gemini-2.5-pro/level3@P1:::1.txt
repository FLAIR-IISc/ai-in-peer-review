This paper addresses the challenging problem of likelihood-based parameter inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The main difficulty in this setting is the computation of the normalizing constant, `det(L+I)`, which involves the determinant of a large matrix in the finite case or a Fredholm determinant in the continuous case. The authors propose a novel method for bounding this quantity without resorting to spectral decompositions. This work builds upon and provides an alternative to recent work by Affandi et al. [7], who used spectral information (eigenvalue estimates) to derive bounds for use within a retrospective MCMC sampler.

The core contribution of this paper is a new set of upper and lower bounds on the DPP likelihood. These bounds are derived by introducing a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points," `Z`, drawing a strong and insightful connection to sparse approximation techniques used in Gaussian Process (GP) regression. By optimizing the locations of these inducing points, the bounds can be tightened. The authors demonstrate how these non-spectral bounds can be plugged into two different inference schemes: 1) an MCMC algorithm using retrospective sampling, similar in spirit to [7] but avoiding eigenvalue computations, and 2) a variational inference (VI) framework, which is a novel and significant contribution for this class of models. The proposed methods are applicable to both DPPs on finite sets and on continuous domains.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The derivation of the bounds, particularly the lower bound in Proposition 1, is non-trivial and represents a solid theoretical contribution. The claims are well-supported by experiments on both a synthetic 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset. The experiments effectively demonstrate the utility of both the MCMC and VI approaches. For instance, the MCMC experiment correctly identifies the parameter non-identifiability issue with the L-kernel parameterization, while the VI experiment shows a clear convergence of the log-likelihood bounds and estimated parameters as the number of inducing points `m` increases.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally very well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated, and the proposed solution is introduced logically, starting with the finite case before moving to the more complex continuous setting. However, there are a few areas for improvement:
*   The proof of Proposition 1 is the technical centerpiece of the paper, yet it is relegated to the supplementary material. Including a proof sketch in the main body would significantly improve the paper's self-containedness and allow readers to better appreciate the core technical insight.
*   Figure 1 is missing axis labels, which hinders its interpretation.
*   There are minor typographical errors (e.g., in the abstract, "moderately expensive exact Markov chain Monte Carlo inference methods").

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The idea of using inducing points to create non-spectral bounds for the DPP partition function is novel. While it borrows concepts from the GP literature, its application to DPPs is new and insightful. This provides a clear and distinct alternative to the spectral methods proposed in prior work. The introduction of a variational inference framework for learning DPP parameters is a particularly novel and impactful contribution. The connection to sparse GPs is a strength, as it links two important areas of machine learning and could facilitate future cross-pollination of ideas.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be influential. By providing a VI framework, the authors open the door to faster, scalable, albeit approximate, inference for DPPs, which could broaden their practical application. The avoidance of direct spectral computation is also appealing. However, two related concerns temper the potential significance:
1.  **Computational Trade-off:** The paper frames the method as a computational win over spectral methods like power iteration. However, it replaces eigenvalue computations with a potentially complex, non-linear optimization problem over the `m` inducing points `Z`. The overall computational cost is not just the `O(nm^2)` complexity of evaluating the bound, but the cost of this optimization, which may require many iterations. It is not definitively established that this is always more efficient than spectral methods, especially as `m` needs to be large to get tight bounds.
2.  **Convergence of Bounds for MCMC:** The validity of the retrospective MCMC sampler depends on the ability to arbitrarily tighten the bounds to make a correct accept/reject decision. The optimization of `Z` and kernel parameters is likely non-convex. It is possible for the optimization to get stuck in poor local minima, preventing the bounds from becoming tight enough. This could stall the MCMC sampler or make it prohibitively slow, undermining the practical utility of the "provably exact" sampling scheme. The authors should acknowledge and discuss this potential issue more directly.

**Summary of Arguments**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and technically sound method for bounding the DPP partition function without spectral knowledge.
*   Introduces the first, to my knowledge, variational inference framework for DPPs, a significant contribution that could enable much faster inference.
*   Draws an elegant and useful connection between DPPs and sparse Gaussian Processes, which is of interest to the broader NIPS community.
*   The approach is well-demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The practical computational advantage over existing spectral methods is not clearly established and depends on the performance of a non-convex optimization routine.
*   The reliability of the retrospective MCMC sampler could be compromised by local minima in the optimization of the inducing points, an issue that is not fully addressed.
*   Minor presentation issues, such as placing the main proof in the appendix and missing axis labels in a key figure, detract from the paper's clarity.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly the novelty of the non-spectral bounds and the introduction of a variational inference scheme for DPPs, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. It is a clever and well-executed piece of work that makes an important contribution to the literature on probabilistic modeling. I recommend acceptance.
