- Proposes novel non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the DPP log-likelihood using inducing inputs, avoiding eigendecomposition.  
- Derives two inference procedures: a fast approximate variational inference (VI) and an asymptotically exact MCMC with retrospective sampling.  
- Framework applies to both finite and continuous DPPs and is inspired by sparse Gaussian Process techniques.  
- Main technical contribution: Propositions 1 and 2 connecting sparse GP ideas to DPP likelihood bounds.  
- Strength: Original and significant idea that could scale DPP inference to larger problems.  
- Strength: General, flexible framework offering both approximate and exact-in-the-limit inference options.  
- Weakness: Paper lacks clarity—key concepts like marginal kernel K vs L-ensemble L are not clearly defined or integrated.  
- Weakness: Empirical evaluation is insufficient and preliminary, leaving practical utility unproven.  
- Weakness: No comparisons to relevant baselines (e.g., spectral-bound MCMC [7], Nyström approximations [13]) on accuracy and runtime.  
- Weakness: No internal comparison or complexity/runtime trade-off analysis between VI and MCMC methods.  
- Weakness: VI performs poorly on a simple 1D toy example with no analysis of failure modes or conditions for success.  
- Weakness: Little practical guidance on selecting, initializing, or placing inducing inputs (choice of m, initialization strategies).  
- Missing experiment: performance when inducing inputs equal observed data points (connection to Nyström) is not evaluated.  
- Argument for acceptance: Elegant, conceptually significant approach that bypasses the main computational bottleneck in DPP inference.  
- Argument against acceptance: Too weak empirical validation, lack of baseline comparisons, unreliable VI results, and missing practical guidance—paper feels preliminary.
