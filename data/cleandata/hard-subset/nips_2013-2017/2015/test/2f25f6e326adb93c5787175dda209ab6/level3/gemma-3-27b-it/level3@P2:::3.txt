This paper proposes novel bounds on the likelihood of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), both for finite and continuous domains, aiming to enable efficient inference without relying on spectral decompositions. The core idea is to utilize inducing inputs to approximate the kernel and derive tractable bounds for variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The authors demonstrate their approach on a toy Gaussian continuous DPP and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work on DPPs, referencing key contributions from Macchi (1952), Hough et al. (2009), Kulesza & Taskar (2012), and others. It correctly identifies a key challenge in DPP inference – the computational cost associated with the spectral decomposition required for likelihood evaluation – and positions itself within the existing trend of directly parameterizing the kernel *L* instead of *K* for tractability (Affandi et al., 2013; Gillenwater et al., 2015). The proposed bounds are inspired by techniques used in sparse Gaussian Processes (Titsias, 2009).

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** The computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition is a significant obstacle to applying DPPs to large-scale datasets. The paper’s focus on circumventing this issue is highly relevant.
*   **Novelty of approach:** The proposed bounds, and their application to both variational inference and MCMC, represent a novel contribution. Leveraging inducing inputs to avoid spectral computation is a clever idea.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The paper provides theoretical justification for the bounds, referencing relevant inequalities on determinants.
*   **Demonstration on real data:** The application to the diabetic neuropathy dataset provides some evidence of the method’s practical utility, and the comparison with prior work (Affandi et al., 2013) is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of clarity in method description:** The description of the variational inference and MCMC procedures feels somewhat rough. The connection between the bounds and the optimization steps is not always clear. A more detailed algorithmic description would be beneficial.
*   **Insufficient empirical evaluation:** The empirical evaluation lacks comprehensive comparisons. While the toy example shows good agreement with exact MCMC, the authors don't rigorously compare the computational complexity (and, crucially, *wall-clock time*) of their proposed methods against existing approaches, including those leveraging power iteration.
*   **Variational inference instability:** The observation that variational inference exhibits large parameter estimation error on the toy data is concerning. The paper doesn't adequately explain *why* this happens, or provide guidance on when variational inference is likely to succeed or fail.  The reliance on approximate MCMC to "rescue" the variational approach raises questions about the overall robustness of the framework.
*   **Inducing point selection:**  The paper does not offer clear guidance on selecting the number of inducing points or their initial placement. The impact of these choices on performance is not thoroughly investigated. Examining the performance when inducing inputs are chosen to be the actual sampled points (in the toy example) would be informative.
*   **Kernel definitions:** The paper introduces matrices K and L without clearly defining their roles for readers unfamiliar with the DPP literature. A brief explanation of the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would improve accessibility.
*   **Inconsistent argument:** The discussion regarding the advantages of directly learning parameters of kernel K feels at odds with the paper’s own focus on parameterizing L.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach, but the lack of detailed experimental analysis and clarity in the method description limit its overall quality. It feels incomplete, lacking the rigor expected of a NIPS publication.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the method description could be significantly improved. The mathematical notation is appropriate, but could be clarified in some places.

**Originality:** The core idea of using inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood is novel and represents a meaningful contribution.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant. Efficient inference for DPPs could broaden their applicability in various fields, including machine learning, statistics, and computational biology. However, the current lack of compelling empirical evidence limits the demonstrable impact.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a computationally challenging problem in DPP inference.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   Demonstrates preliminary results on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently detailed method description.
*   Lack of comprehensive empirical evaluation (missing runtime comparisons).
*   Variational inference instability and lack of explanation.
*   No guidance on inducing point selection.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak reject**. While the paper has merit and addresses an important problem, the current presentation lacks the clarity, rigor, and comprehensive evaluation required for acceptance at NIPS.  If the authors address the weaknesses outlined above – particularly by providing a more detailed method description, strengthening the empirical evaluation with runtime comparisons, and clarifying the conditions under which variational inference is reliable – I would be willing to reconsider my assessment.  Specifically, a comparison to Affandi et al. (2013) in terms of wall-clock time for a given level of accuracy is crucial.
