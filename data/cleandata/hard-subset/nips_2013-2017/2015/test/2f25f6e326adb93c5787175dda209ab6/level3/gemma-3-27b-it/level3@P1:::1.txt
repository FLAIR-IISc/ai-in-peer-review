This paper addresses the challenging problem of inference in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), which are powerful models for encoding diversity but suffer from computational difficulties due to an intractable normalizing constant involving determinants. The authors present novel bounds on the DPP likelihood, both for finite and continuous domains, that crucially *avoid* the need for spectral decomposition – a major bottleneck in previous approaches like that of Affandi et al. [7]. This allows for both cheap variational inference and moderately expensive Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The work is well-motivated, connecting DPPs to applications in diverse fields like news summarization [3] and spatial modeling [4], and clearly articulates the challenges of likelihood-based learning.

The paper's strength lies in its technical soundness and the ingenuity of Proposition 1, which provides the core bounds on the DPP likelihood. Drawing inspiration from variational Gaussian process approximations [11], the authors cleverly extend these concepts to DPPs, bypassing the need for eigenvalue computations. The subsequent application of these bounds to variational inference and MCMC is logical and well-explained. The connection to inducing-point techniques from sparse Gaussian processes further enhances the paper's value, demonstrating a clear link to established methods in the machine learning literature.  The extension to continuous DPPs in Section 4 is a significant contribution, broadening the applicability of the proposed methodology.

However, the practical advantages of the proposed approach relative to existing methods warrant further scrutiny. While avoiding eigenvalue estimation is theoretically appealing, the paper acknowledges that the resulting optimization – particularly when using the retrospective MCMC approach – requires optimizing over the locations of inducing points, potentially increasing computational cost as dimensionality grows. It’s not entirely clear if this trade-off – replacing power iterations with a potentially more complex nonlinear optimization – consistently yields a performance improvement, especially in high dimensions.

A related concern is the convergence of the bounds as the number of inducing points increases. The paper mentions that increasing the cardinality of the inducing set tightens the bounds, but doesn't fully address the potential for local minima in the optimization of inducing point locations.  Without guarantees of convergence, the retrospective MCMC approach could become unreliable, particularly if the bounds become insufficiently tight to drive valid acceptance/rejection decisions. The impact of the choice of proposal distribution in the MCMC algorithm is also not thoroughly discussed.

The presentation contains minor areas for improvement. Figure 1 lacks axis labels, hindering its interpretability.  The proof of Proposition 1, central to the paper's contribution, should ideally be included in the main text rather than relegated to the supplementary material.  Finally, there are minor wording/typo issues, such as “the variational lower is…”, which can be easily addressed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed bounds on the DPP likelihood, avoiding spectral decomposition, represent a significant contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation and justification of Proposition 1 are rigorous.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The extension to continuous DPPs expands the scope of the work.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The clear links to Gaussian process inference and inducing-point methods strengthen the paper’s relevance.
*   **Potential Impact:** The method has the potential to enable faster and more scalable inference for DPPs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational Trade-offs:** The practical advantages over existing methods, particularly in high dimensions, are not definitively established.
*   **Convergence Concerns:** The convergence of the bounds with increasing inducing points is not fully addressed.
*   **Minor Presentation Issues:** Issues with Figure 1 and the placement of the proof detract slightly from the paper's clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor concerns, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP inference. The proposed bounds offer a novel and potentially impactful approach to tackling the computational challenges associated with likelihood-based learning in DPPs. The work is technically sound, well-written, and demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant literature. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the presentation issues and a more thorough discussion of the computational trade-offs and convergence properties.
