### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the parameters of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). DPPs are elegant models for capturing repulsion or diversity in a set of items, defined by a positive-definite kernel `L`. The primary difficulty in likelihood-based inference for DPPs is the intractable normalization constant, `det(L+I)`, which requires summing over all possible subsets of items, a task with exponential complexity.

The authors build upon prior work [7] which tackled this problem by estimating the spectrum of the kernel matrix `L` to derive incremental upper and lower bounds on the log-likelihood. This enabled a provably-correct MCMC algorithm for sampling from the parameter posterior. The key limitation of that approach is its reliance on expensive spectral methods (e.g., power iteration) and its restriction to finite domains.

This paper's main contribution is a novel set of non-spectral bounds on the partition function `det(L+I)`. Drawing an insightful connection to inducing-point methods in Gaussian Process (GP) literature, the authors introduce a set of `m` pseudo-inputs to construct a low-rank approximation `Q` of the kernel matrix `L`. This approximation yields a tractable upper bound and a non-trivial lower bound (Proposition 1) on the likelihood. These bounds avoid eigenvalue computations entirely and are applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be used within two inference frameworks: a fast, approximate variational inference scheme, and a more costly but exact retrospective MCMC sampler.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—the non-spectral bounds on the DPP partition function—is highly original and represents a significant advance for the field. Proposition 1, which establishes the lower bound, is a key theoretical insight. This work successfully moves beyond the spectral estimation paradigm of prior work [7], opening up new avenues for DPP inference.

2.  **Expanded Inference Capabilities:** A major strength of this approach is that it enables variational inference for DPPs. This provides a fast, scalable, and deterministic alternative to MCMC, making DPPs accessible to larger-scale problems where MCMC would be computationally prohibitive. The ability to jointly optimize kernel parameters and variational parameters (the inducing points) is a powerful feature.

3.  **Elegant Connection to Gaussian Processes:** The paper draws a clear and useful connection between DPP inference and sparse/inducing-point methods for GPs. This is an elegant theoretical link that not only provides intuition for the proposed method but also connects two important families of kernel-based models, which could inspire future cross-pollination of ideas.

4.  **Technical Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically strong and generally well-written. The problem is clearly motivated, the proposed bounds are derived rigorously, and the extensions from the finite to the continuous case are handled well. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data effectively demonstrate the correctness and utility of the proposed methods.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Computational Trade-offs:** The paper replaces the power iterations of [7] with a non-linear optimization problem over the `m` inducing points. While this avoids direct spectral computation, it's not immediately clear that this is always a computational win. The optimization of inducing points is likely non-convex, and its cost scales with `m` (e.g., `O(nm^2)` for the variational bound). The paper would be stronger with a more direct discussion or empirical comparison of the computational trade-offs between these two approaches. For instance, under what conditions (e.g., spectral gap, required precision) is one method preferable to the other?

2.  **Robustness of Optimization in MCMC:** The retrospective MCMC scheme relies on tightening the bounds by increasing the number of inducing points, `m`, and re-optimizing their locations. Since the optimization over the inducing points `Z` is non-convex, there is a risk of converging to a poor local minimum. This could prevent the bounds from becoming tight enough to make an acceptance decision, potentially causing the MCMC sampler to stall. A discussion of the practical robustness of this optimization step would be valuable. Do the authors have practical advice for initialization or optimization strategies to mitigate this?

3.  **Presentation Issues:**
    *   The proof for Proposition 1, the paper's central theoretical result, is in the supplementary material. While understandable due to page limits, including a proof sketch or the key insight in the main text would greatly improve the paper's self-containedness and impact.
    *   In Figure 1(a), the axes are unlabeled, making it difficult to interpret the scale and location of the data points and inducing points.
    *   There are several minor typos and referencing errors that detract from the paper's polish. For example, in Section 3.2, the log-likelihood `l(θ)` is stated as being defined in (7), but it is in (5). The same section later refers to bounds in an equation (13), which does not exist. Careful proofreading is recommended.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on inference for Determinantal Point Processes. The proposed non-spectral bounds are a clever and powerful idea that significantly expands the applicability of DPPs by enabling both a new type of exact MCMC and, more importantly, a scalable variational inference framework. The connection to inducing-point methods from the GP community is particularly insightful. While there are some concerns regarding the practicalities of the required non-linear optimization and minor presentation issues, the originality, technical quality, and potential significance of this work are very high. It clearly advances the state of the art and is likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
