This paper tackles the challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), where learning kernel parameters is hindered by an intractable normalization constant. Previous work, notably Affandi et al. [7], addressed this by estimating the kernel spectrum to derive bounds for retrospective MCMC. This paper proposes a significant and novel alternative by deriving bounds that entirely avoid spectral estimation. The core idea is to draw an elegant connection to inducing-point methods from the scalable Gaussian Process literature. This not only provides a new mechanism for MCMC but, crucially, also enables a variational inference framework for DPPs for the first time.

The paper’s strengths are numerous. It is technically sound, clearly written, and the proposed approach is clever and well-motivated. The lower-bound inequality in Proposition 1 is a key novel insight that underpins the entire method. The most significant contribution is the extension to variational inference, which opens the door to faster, approximate learning in large-scale settings where MCMC may be infeasible. The connection established between DPPs and sparse Gaussian Processes is particularly insightful, bridging two important areas of machine learning and broadening the potential impact of this work.

Despite its strengths, there are a couple of concerns. First, it is not clear that replacing power iterations with a nonlinear optimization of increasing dimension is a definitive computational win. A more detailed comparison of the practical trade-offs would strengthen the claims. Second, the joint optimization over kernel parameters and inducing points is likely non-convex. The authors should discuss the potential for local minima to prevent the bounds from tightening sufficiently, which could cause the retrospective MCMC sampler to become inefficient.

Minor presentation issues should also be addressed: Figure 1 lacks axis labels, the crucial proof for Proposition 1 should arguably be in the main text rather than the appendix, and there is a minor typo ("the variational lower is").

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a valuable contribution. The non-spectral bounds offer a new, promising path for DPP inference. The development of a variational approach and the link to GPs are significant advances that are likely to be built upon by other researchers, making this a strong candidate for acceptance.
