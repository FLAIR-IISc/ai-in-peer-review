This paper addresses the challenging problem of inference in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), specifically tackling the computational burden of evaluating the likelihood function which involves intractable determinants. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood, both for finite and continuous domains, that circumvent the need for spectral decomposition – a key bottleneck in previous approaches like [7]. These bounds enable both variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods for DPP parameter learning.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the difficulties of likelihood-based inference for DPPs, particularly the spectral decomposition requirement. It effectively situates the work within the existing literature on DPPs [1, 2, 3, 4, 5] and their applications in diverse fields like modeling diversity in search results [7] and spatial distributions [4]. The connection drawn to variational inference techniques used in Gaussian Processes [11] is insightful, and provides a valuable framing for understanding the proposed approach. The mathematical presentation is generally rigorous, and Proposition 1, the core result providing the non-spectral bounds, appears to be a significant contribution. The extension to continuous DPPs in Section 4 is a welcome addition, demonstrating the generality of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the bounds appears technically sound, relying on established mathematical results [14]. The extension to continuous domains demonstrates a strong theoretical understanding.
* **Originality:** Avoiding spectral decomposition is a novel and significant contribution, addressing a major limitation of existing DPP inference methods. The connection to inducing point techniques is a creative way to leverage existing scalable inference approaches.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the mathematical notation is consistently used. The connections to related work are explicitly highlighted.
* **Significance:** The proposed bounds have the potential to significantly speed up DPP inference, making these models more practical for large-scale applications. The ability to perform both variational and MCMC inference offers flexibility to practitioners. The linkage to Gaussian Process techniques also broadens the potential audience and impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Gains Not Fully Established:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a theoretical advantage, the paper doesn’t provide a convincing argument that the resulting optimization (especially in the MCMC setting involving potentially increasing dimensionality with pseudo-inputs) will *always* be computationally cheaper than existing power iteration methods, particularly for large *n*. The cost of optimizing over the inducing inputs (Z) should be further analyzed.
* **Convergence Concerns:** The retrospective MCMC approach relies on tightening bounds by increasing the cardinality of the inducing set. It's not fully clear whether this process is guaranteed to converge, or whether the optimization landscape will be prone to local minima, hindering the ability to draw valid samples. This is a crucial point for the practical applicability of the MCMC method.
* **Presentation Issues:** Several minor presentation issues detract from the overall polish of the paper. Figure 1 lacks axis labels, making it difficult to interpret. The proof of Proposition 1 is relegated to the supplementary material and should ideally be included in the main text for greater accessibility. There are also minor wording issues (e.g., “the variational lower is…”) that could be refined.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound solution to a significant problem in DPP inference.
* The proposed bounds offer a promising alternative to spectral decomposition, potentially enabling faster and more scalable inference.
* The connection to inducing point techniques and Gaussian Processes strengthens the contribution and broadens its impact.
* The experimental validation, while limited, provides some support for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational advantages over existing methods are not fully demonstrated, and there are potential convergence issues with the MCMC approach.
* The minor presentation issues detract from the overall polish of the paper.
* The impact of replacing K with L in parameterization isn’t fully addressed beyond mentioning interpretability concerns.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP modeling. The proposed non-spectral bounds are a clever and potentially significant advance. While some concerns remain regarding the practical computational gains and convergence properties, the originality, technical soundness, and clarity of the paper are strong. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the presentation issues and provide a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity and convergence behavior of the proposed methods. Specifically, I recommend the authors include the proof of Proposition 1 in the main text, label the axes in Figure 1, and discuss the conditions under which the proposed approach is likely to be more efficient than existing methods. A discussion of strategies for mitigating potential convergence issues in the MCMC algorithm would also be beneficial.
