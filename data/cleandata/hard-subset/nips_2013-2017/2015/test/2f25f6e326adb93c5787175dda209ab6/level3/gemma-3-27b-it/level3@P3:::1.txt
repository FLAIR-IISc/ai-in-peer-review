This paper presents a clever approach to bounding the likelihood of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), avoiding the computationally expensive spectral decomposition required by prior work. The proposed bounds enable both variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling for DPP parameters, and draw valuable connections to inducing-point techniques used in scalable Gaussian Process (GP) inference. The technical details are strong, and the paper is generally well-written. Proposition 1, providing a key lower-bound inequality, is a particularly noteworthy contribution. The variational inference capability and the demonstrated link to GP methods significantly extend the potential impact of this work.

The paper effectively situates itself within the existing DPP literature, referencing relevant work in machine learning, statistics, and probability. It correctly identifies the computational bottleneck of likelihood-based inference – the intractable normalization constant (Fredholm/regular determinant) – and positions its contribution as a solution to this problem, building on the earlier work of Affandi et al. [7] but circumventing the need for eigenvalue estimation via power iteration. The authors clearly articulate the trade-offs between parametrizing the kernel K versus L, and acknowledge the loss of interpretability associated with the latter.  The connection to variational GP inference via inducing points is well-explained and provides a useful intuition for the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The non-spectral bounds are a significant contribution, providing a computationally cheaper alternative to existing methods.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper demonstrates a strong theoretical foundation, with Proposition 1 being a key insight. The proof, while currently in the supplementary material, is crucial and central to the contribution.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation and methodology.  The connection to existing work (particularly GP inference) is well-articulated.
* **Significance:** The ability to perform faster inference on DPPs opens up possibilities for applying these models to larger datasets and more complex problems.  The demonstrated connections to GPs and the potential for hybrid modeling are also highly significant.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, though relatively simple, demonstrate the feasibility of the proposed methods and validate the MCMC approach against an exact solution in a 1D Gaussian case. The diabetic neuropathy dataset experiment demonstrates practical applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While avoiding eigenvalue decomposition, replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization of increasing dimension (optimizing over the pseudo-inputs Z) may not necessarily be a clear computational win, particularly for large-scale problems. The empirical results provide some evidence, but a more thorough complexity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Convergence Concerns:** Increasing the number of inducing points/cardinality does not guarantee convergence of the bounds. The optimization landscape is likely non-convex, and local minima could prevent valid MCMC steps. This is not thoroughly addressed.
* **Presentation Issues:**
    * Figure 1 lacks axis labels, hindering its interpretability.
    * The proof of Proposition 1 is crucial and should be included in the main text, not relegated to the supplementary material.
    * Minor wording issue: "the variational lower is..." should be corrected.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant bottleneck in DPP inference, offering a potentially faster and more scalable alternative to existing methods.
* The theoretical contributions are strong, with a key novel insight in Proposition 1.
* The connections to GP inference and the resulting variational inference capability expand the applicability of the proposed approach.
* The experimental results, while limited in scope, provide initial validation of the methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational advantage over existing methods is not fully demonstrated and requires further analysis.
* The potential for convergence issues due to non-convex optimization is a concern that needs to be addressed.
* The presentation issues, while minor, detract from the overall quality of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of DPP modeling. While some concerns remain regarding computational cost and convergence, the novel contributions, technical soundness, and clear presentation outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the presentation issues and provide a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity and convergence properties. Specifically, moving the proof of Proposition 1 to the main text and labeling the axes in Figure 1 are essential. A brief discussion of the conditions under which the optimization over pseudo-inputs is likely to be well-behaved would also strengthen the paper.
