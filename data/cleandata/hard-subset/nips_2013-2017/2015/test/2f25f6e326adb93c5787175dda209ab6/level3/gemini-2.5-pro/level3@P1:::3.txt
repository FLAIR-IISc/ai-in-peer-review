This paper proposes a novel approach for approximate likelihood-based inference in determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoids the computationally expensive spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The core contribution is the derivation of non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the likelihood, specifically on the intractable `det(L+I)` term. These bounds are obtained by introducing a set of `m` "inducing inputs," drawing a clear parallel to sparse approximations in Gaussian processes. Based on these bounds, the authors present two inference schemes: a fast, approximate method based on variational inference (VI) that maximizes the lower bound, and a more computationally intensive but asymptotically exact MCMC method that uses the bounds for retrospective sampling within a Metropolis-Hastings framework. The methods are developed for both DPPs on finite sets and on continuous domains.

The work is situated in the context of scaling up DPP inference, where previous methods like Affandi et al. (2014) also used MCMC with bounds, but their bounds required partial spectral decomposition. The proposed method's use of inducing inputs is inspired by the GP literature (Titsias, 2009) and represents a novel and promising direction for DPPs.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem of scalable inference for DPPs is highly relevant, and this paper offers a genuinely new approach. The connection drawn between sparse GPs and DPP inference via inducing inputs is insightful and opens up a promising avenue for future research. Moving away from spectral-based approximations is a significant conceptual step.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation of the paper—the derivation of the likelihood bounds in Propositions 1 and 2—appears to be sound, resting on a known determinantal inequality. The extension of these ideas from the finite to the continuous case is also a valuable contribution.

3.  **Potential for Impact:** By providing a framework that avoids `O(n^3)` eigendecomposition, the work has the potential to make DPPs applicable to much larger problems. The proposal of both a fast VI method and a more robust MCMC method provides practitioners with a useful trade-off between speed and accuracy.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

The main weaknesses of the paper lie in its empirical evaluation, which is not thorough enough to fully substantiate the method's practical utility, and in the clarity of the exposition.

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Comparison:** The experiments demonstrate that the methods can be run, but they lack the comparative analysis necessary to understand their advantages and disadvantages.
    *   **Internal Comparison:** The paper proposes two methods (VI and MCMC) but never directly compares them. What is the trade-off in wall-clock time versus parameter accuracy between them? A quantitative comparison on at least one dataset is essential.
    *   **External Baselines:** The proposed methods are not compared against any relevant alternatives. How does the approximation quality and runtime compare to Nyström-based approximations (Affandi et al., AISTATS 2014)? How does the non-spectral MCMC sampler compare to the spectral-bound MCMC sampler (Affandi et al., ICML 2014) in terms of efficiency (e.g., number of iterations to converge, time per iteration)? Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the practical significance of the contribution.

2.  **Unconvincing Variational Inference Results:** The VI method performs poorly on the toy example, with the authors noting that it "yields poorly consistent results" and that parameter `κ` is not identified. The paper then relies on the MCMC method to show that this is an identifiability issue. This raises serious concerns about the reliability of the VI approach.
    *   When is the variational approach expected to work, and when will it fail? Is the failure due to a loose bound, a difficult non-convex optimization landscape, or the inherent identifiability problem? A deeper analysis is needed to give practitioners confidence in this method. Suggesting that one should detect failure by observing high variance across runs is not a satisfactory solution.

3.  **Lack of Practical Guidance:** The method's performance depends critically on the number and placement of the inducing inputs `Z`, yet the paper offers little guidance on these choices.
    *   How should one select the number of inducing points, `m`? The paper suggests increasing `m` until the results stabilize, but a more concrete rule-of-thumb or sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
    *   How should the inducing points be initialized? The experiments show they form neat grids after optimization, but their initialization is not discussed. This is a crucial detail for reproducibility and practical use.
    *   A simple and informative experiment would be to evaluate the method when the inducing inputs are chosen to be the observed data points from a realization (a Nyström-style approximation). How would this compare to jointly optimizing their locations? This would better connect the work to existing approximation schemes.

4.  **Clarity:**
    *   For readers less familiar with DPPs, the distinction between the marginal kernel `K` (which has interpretable parameters) and the L-ensemble kernel `L` (which is used for tractability) is not clearly explained. A few sentences of definition and clarification in Section 2.1 would significantly improve the paper's accessibility.
    *   The introduction discusses the advantages of parametrizing `K`, but the paper then parametrizes `L`. This introductory motivation feels somewhat disconnected from the actual method and could be streamlined to avoid confusion.

**Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a novel and promising idea for scalable DPP inference. However, the current manuscript feels more like a proof-of-concept than a complete piece of work. The empirical evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate the method's practical advantages over existing techniques, and the failure of the variational method on a simple toy problem is concerning without further analysis.

While I believe the core idea has merit, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can significantly strengthen the experimental section by providing the missing comparisons and offer a more thorough analysis of the proposed methods' properties and limitations.
