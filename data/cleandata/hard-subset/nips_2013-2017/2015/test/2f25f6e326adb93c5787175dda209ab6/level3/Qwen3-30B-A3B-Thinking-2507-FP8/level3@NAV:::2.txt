

This paper presents novel upper and lower bounds on matrix determinants with applications to bounding determinantal point process (DPP) likelihoods. The authors address a key challenge in DPP inference: the intractable spectral decomposition required for likelihood evaluation. Their main contribution is a simple yet effective approach using "pseudo-inputs" to derive bounds that avoid spectral computations, enabling efficient variational inference and exact Markov chain Monte Carlo methods. 

The work builds on sparse GP approximations [11] but extends them to DPPs, creating a general framework applicable beyond DPPs. The paper makes significant contributions to a timely problem (DPP inference), which has gained traction in machine learning for modeling diversity (e.g., [3, 6, 7]). The experimental evaluation is thorough, demonstrating the bounds' efficacy on both synthetic and real-world diabetic neuropathy datasets, while revealing limitations of variational methods under certain conditions.

Strengths include the elegant theoretical contribution, clear connection to existing GP work, and well-constructed experiments showing how the bounds enable practical inference. The approach is generalizable and could benefit other kernel-based methods. The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs provided in the supplement, and addresses both strengths and weaknesses of the approach.

Weaknesses are minor: the emphasis on parameterizing K instead of L is not well-justified (Section 1), and the supplement should follow standard formatting rather than being an extended paper. The authors could also explore direct bounds in terms of K, as suggested in the future work.

The work excels in originality (novel bounds for DPPs), significance (enabling practical DPP inference), and quality (solid theory and experiments). It's clearly written and well-organized, with the continuous case well-motivated for readers unfamiliar with operator theory. While the focus on L parameterization is common in the literature [7], the paper would benefit from addressing the interpretability trade-offs more explicitly.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances DPP inference with practical implications. The minor issues don't detract from the paper's substantial technical contribution and relevance to the community. I recommend acceptance.
