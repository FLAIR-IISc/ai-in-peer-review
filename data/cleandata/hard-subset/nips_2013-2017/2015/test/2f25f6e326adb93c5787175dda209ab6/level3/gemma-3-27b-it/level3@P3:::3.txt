This paper proposes approximate inference methods for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), circumventing the need for spectral decomposition by leveraging inducing inputs to bound the likelihood. Both variational inference and approximate MCMC are presented, with empirical demonstrations on both toy and real datasets. While the core idea is promising and addresses a known computational bottleneck in DPP inference, the presentation and evaluation require significant improvement.

The paper is well-motivated; DPPs are increasingly used for modeling diversity, but likelihood-based inference is hampered by the spectral decomposition required for the normalizing constant. The authors correctly identify the parametrization of the kernel *L* directly as a common workaround, acknowledging the trade-off between tractability and interpretability. The proposed bounds, inspired by variational GP approximations, are a sensible approach to tackling the computational complexity. The connection to existing work on sparse GPs is also clearly articulated.

However, the method description is often rough and lacks sufficient detail. For instance, the connection between Proposition 1 (bounds) and the specific implementation of variational inference in Section 3.1 isn’t fully clear – how is the optimization performed jointly over θ and Z in practice? The discussion of the MCMC method also feels somewhat hand-wavy, particularly the retrospective sampling step. A more detailed algorithmic description, potentially with pseudocode, would greatly enhance clarity.

A major weakness lies in the insufficient empirical evaluation. The paper lacks a critical comparison of the computational complexity and wall-clock time between the variational inference and MCMC methods. Which one is faster in practice, and under what conditions? The runtime of both methods relative to exact inference (where feasible) or other approximate methods (e.g., those based on Nyström approximation) is also missing.  Comparing approximation quality (e.g., through log-likelihood values on the test data) is crucial.

The toy example reveals a significant issue with the variational inference approach: large parameter estimation error. This suggests that the variational approximation may be unreliable, or at least highly sensitive to initialization. The paper doesn’t adequately address this; instead, it pivots to the MCMC method.  A clearer explanation is needed: When is variational inference expected to work well, and when will it likely fail? Why is there such a large error in the toy example?  Furthermore, for the toy example, it’s crucial to demonstrate performance when the inducing inputs are chosen to be the actual sampled points, as this represents a best-case scenario for the proposed method. 

Practical guidance for selecting the number of inducing points and their initialization/placement is absent. The paper mentions optimizing the location of the inducing inputs *Z*, but doesn't offer any rules of thumb. How does the performance of the methods change as *m* increases? Is there a point of diminishing returns? Are there strategies for intelligently initializing *Z* beyond random selection? 

Minor points: the matrices *K* and *L* are used without prior definition, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with DPPs. A brief explanation of the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would be helpful. The discussion of advantages of directly learning marginal kernel parameters in the introduction feels somewhat inconsistent with the paper’s subsequent approach of also directly parametrizing L; this section could be streamlined.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant computational challenge in DPP inference.
*   Presents a novel approach based on inducing inputs and likelihood bounds.
*   Demonstrates promising results on a real dataset.
*   Connections to related work on sparse GPs are well-established.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Method description lacks clarity and detail.
*   Empirical evaluation is insufficient, missing crucial comparisons.
*   Variational inference exhibits large parameter estimation errors.
*   Lack of practical guidance for parameter selection (number and placement of inducing points).



**Recommendation:**

I recommend rejection in its current form. However, I believe the paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution if the authors significantly improve the clarity of the method description, strengthen the empirical evaluation (including comparisons with other methods and a detailed analysis of runtime), and address the issues with the variational inference approach. A more thorough investigation of the sensitivity of the results to the choice of inducing points and their initialization is also necessary.
