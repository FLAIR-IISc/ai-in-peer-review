The paper "Inference for Determinantal Point Processes without Spectral Knowledge" proposes a novel approach to inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) that avoids the need for spectral decomposition. The authors derive bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that do not rely on approximating the spectrum of a large matrix or an operator. These bounds are then used to develop variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods for DPPs.

The main contribution of the paper is the derivation of the bounds on the likelihood of a DPP, which is a significant improvement over previous methods that require spectral decomposition. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on both synthetic and real-world data.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the background and motivation for their work. The technical details are also well-explained, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the related work in the field.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. The method description is rough in some places, and the empirical evaluation is insufficient. For example, the authors do not provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity and wall-clock time between the two proposed methods. Additionally, the authors do not provide comparisons to other approximate and exact alternatives in terms of approximation quality and runtime.

Furthermore, the variational inference method shows large parameter estimation error on toy data and relies on approximate MCMC. The authors should clarify when variational inference is expected to work or fail and why. It would also be interesting to see how the methods perform when inducing inputs are chosen to be the actual sampled points.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of machine learning, and the results have the potential to impact a wide range of applications. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results and the potential applications of the method.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with the condition that the authors address the above-mentioned issues and improve the clarity of the method description, empirical evaluation, and discussion of the results.
