This paper addresses the challenging problem of parameter inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). DPPs are elegant models for capturing diversity and repulsion, but learning the parameters of the underlying kernel `L` is hampered by an intractable normalizing constant, `det(L+I)`. This term, known as the partition function or Fredholm determinant in the continuous case, requires summing over all possible subsets, making direct likelihood evaluation computationally infeasible for all but the smallest problems.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel set of upper and lower bounds on this intractable determinant, for both finite and continuous DPPs. Crucially, these bounds are "non-spectral," meaning they do not require computing the eigenvalues of the kernel matrix `L`, which has been the standard approach in prior work (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2014). The proposed method instead introduces a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points," drawing an insightful and powerful connection to variational and sparse inference methods for Gaussian Processes (GPs). The bounds are derived from a low-rank approximation of the kernel matrix constructed from these inducing points.

Based on these bounds, the authors develop two inference schemes:
1.  An approximate but efficient variational inference method that maximizes a lower bound on the log-likelihood jointly with respect to the kernel parameters and the locations of the inducing points.
2.  A provably correct MCMC sampler using the retrospective sampling technique. Here, the bounds are adaptively tightened by increasing the number of inducing points until a Metropolis-Hastings accept/reject decision can be made without ambiguity.

The paper validates these methods on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP and a real-world 2D spatial point process dataset of nerve fibers in diabetic patients.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—the non-spectral bounds based on inducing points—is highly original and significant. It provides a new and compelling alternative to the spectral methods that have dominated recent work on DPP inference. The connection to inducing point methods for GPs is particularly elegant. It not only provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approximation but also opens the door for cross-pollination of ideas and techniques between the GP and DPP communities.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The central results (Propositions 1 and 2) are derived from a non-trivial determinant inequality and appear to be sound. The subsequent application of these bounds to construct both variational and MCMC inference algorithms is well-founded and clearly explained.
*   **Expanded Applicability of DPPs:** The proposed variational inference framework is a major practical contribution. It offers a much faster, albeit approximate, inference route compared to exact MCMC methods. This could significantly broaden the applicability of DPPs to larger-scale problems where MCMC would be prohibitively slow.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. It does an excellent job of motivating the problem, positioning the work relative to prior art, and clearly laying out its contributions. The explicit discussion of both finite and continuous DPPs is comprehensive.

### Weaknesses

*   **Computational Trade-offs:** The paper suggests that the proposed method is computationally cheaper than spectral approaches. While it avoids eigenvalue computations, it introduces a non-linear optimization problem to find the optimal locations of the inducing points. This optimization must be solved repeatedly within the MCMC algorithm whenever the bounds need tightening. The convergence and cost of this optimization step, especially as the number of inducing points `m` increases, are not fully analyzed. It is not immediately obvious that this is always a net computational gain over power iteration methods, and a direct empirical comparison of runtimes would have strengthened this claim.
*   **Convergence and MCMC Validity:** The retrospective sampling MCMC scheme relies on the ability to arbitrarily tighten the bounds by increasing `m` and re-optimizing the inducing point locations. However, the optimization over these locations is likely non-convex. If the optimization gets stuck in a poor local minimum, the resulting bounds may not be tight enough to make an accept/reject decision, potentially stalling the sampler. The authors briefly allude to this in a footnote, but a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of this non-convexity on the sampler's performance and correctness would be valuable.
*   **Presentation:**
    *   The proof for Proposition 1, which is the paper's central theoretical contribution for the finite case, is located in the supplementary material. Including a proof sketch in the main text would improve the paper's self-containedness.
    *   Figure 1 is missing axis labels, which makes it difficult to fully interpret.
    *   The authors report that the variational method performed poorly on the 1D synthetic experiment, which somewhat undermines the strength of that contribution. A brief discussion of why this might be the case (e.g., parameter non-identifiability, multimodality of the posterior) would be helpful for practitioners.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a highly novel, technically sound, and elegant approach to a key bottleneck in DPP inference. The connection to GP methods is a significant conceptual advance. The work enables fast variational inference for DPPs, expanding their practical reach.
*   **Con:** The practical computational advantages over existing spectral methods are not conclusively demonstrated. The reliance on non-convex optimization within the MCMC loop raises potential, though not critical, concerns about robustness.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong and novel contribution to the literature on inference for determinantal point processes. The strengths, particularly the originality of the non-spectral bounds and the connection to the rich literature on sparse GPs, far outweigh the weaknesses. The concerns raised are mainly about practical trade-offs and could be addressed in future work. This work is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community and has the potential to become an influential approach for learning DPP models. I strongly recommend acceptance.
