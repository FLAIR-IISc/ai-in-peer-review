This paper addresses the challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The main bottleneck for this task is the intractable normalizing constant, which involves computing the determinant of a large matrix `L+I` in the finite case, or a Fredholm determinant `det(I+L)` in the continuous case. Previous work, such as Affandi et al. (2014), tackled this by using spectral approximations, which can be computationally expensive and are often limited to the finite case.

The key contribution of this paper is the derivation of novel upper and lower bounds on this determinant. Crucially, these bounds are "non-spectral" and are instead derived by introducing a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" (or inducing points), an idea creatively adapted from the sparse Gaussian Process literature (Titsias, 2009). These bounds are computationally efficient, scaling as O(nm^2), and can be systematically tightened by increasing the number of pseudo-inputs. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be used to enable two inference schemes: a fast, approximate variational inference method by maximizing the lower bound, and an asymptotically exact MCMC inference method via retrospective sampling. The proposed framework is general, covering both finite and continuous DPPs, and is validated on synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and elegant approach to a significant, timely problem in the machine learning community. The intractability of the DPP likelihood has been a major barrier to its wider application. By providing a non-spectral, computationally cheaper alternative to existing methods, this work represents a substantial advance. The core idea is simple yet powerful, and is likely to inspire further research in this area and beyond.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and well-executed. The creative use of pseudo-inputs to bound a matrix determinant is a key insight. The derivation is solid, correctly building on a non-trivial determinant inequality from the mathematical physics literature. The extension of the framework from the finite setting to the more general continuous setting is a valuable and non-trivial contribution that broadens the applicability of the method.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors make an excellent pedagogical choice by first introducing the concepts and bounds in the simpler finite case before moving to the more technical continuous case. This structure makes the core ideas accessible to a broad audience. The connection to sparse GPs is clearly explained and provides helpful intuition.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are thoughtfully constructed and effectively illustrate the properties of the proposed method.
    *   The 1D synthetic experiment is a strong piece of validation, as the exact Fredholm determinant is computable, allowing for a direct comparison that confirms the correctness of the MCMC sampler.
    *   This experiment also astutely highlights a potential failure mode of the variational approach (parameter non-identifiability), demonstrating a careful and honest evaluation of the method's limitations.
    *   The application to the diabetic neuropathy dataset is interesting and highly relevant, demonstrating the practical utility of the method. The analysis showing the convergence of the bounds and parameter estimates as the number of pseudo-inputs increases is very insightful and provides practical guidance for users.

5.  **Generality:** The approach is general and has potential applications beyond DPPs. The technique of using pseudo-inputs to construct tractable bounds for functionals of positive definite kernels could be adapted to other models where large determinants or operator traces are a computational bottleneck.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Parametrization of K vs. L:** The introduction correctly points out that parameterizing the diversity kernel `K` is more interpretable than parameterizing `L`, but the paper then proceeds with the `L` parametrization for tractability, which is standard practice. While this is a reasonable choice, the initial emphasis on this issue feels slightly unresolved. A brief comment in the discussion on potential paths toward working with `K` directly, perhaps enabled by this new bounding technique, would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Supplement Format:** The paper refers to supplementary material containing proofs. While not a major issue, providing this as a standard, separate supplement document, rather than an "extended version" of the paper, is generally preferred for consistency and ease of access within the review process and for future readers.

**Future Work Suggestions:**

The paper opens up several exciting avenues for future work. One direction could be to investigate bounding the `det(I-K)` term directly to allow inference on the more interpretable `K` kernel. Furthermore, while the experiments show the number of pseudo-inputs `m` needed is manageable, a more formal analysis of the scalability of the method would be valuable. How does the required `m` scale with the data size `n` or the complexity of the underlying point pattern to achieve a certain bound tightness?

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a novel, significant, and well-executed contribution to an important problem. The core idea is insightful, the technical work is solid, and the experimental validation is thorough. It clearly advances the state-of-the-art for inference in DPPs.
