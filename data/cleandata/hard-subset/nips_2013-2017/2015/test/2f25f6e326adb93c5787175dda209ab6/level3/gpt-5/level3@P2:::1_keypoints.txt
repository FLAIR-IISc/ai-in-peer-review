- Introduces nonspectral upper and lower bounds on the DPP normalizer using m inducing inputs Z to form a low-rank Q, with inequalities det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I), and extends to continuous domains via Fredholm determinants and Ψ.

- Enables a tractable variational lower bound (replace log det(L+I) with its lower bound) and a retrospective MH scheme that tightens acceptance by increasing m and re-optimizing Z.

- Approach is inspired by sparse GP inducing-variable methods and contrasts with prior spectral or Nyström DPP methods by avoiding eigendecompositions and applying to continuous domains.

- Conceptual advance: provides clean, evaluable determinant inequalities for DPP normalizers in both finite and continuous settings.

- Practicality: supports two complementary inference modes—cheap variational learning and (numerically) exact retrospective MH—without spectral information.

- Leverages sparse GP tooling: inducing-input intuition, optimizing Z, and O(n m^2) computational scaling familiar to the community.

- Experiments: correct on a 1D continuous toy with known Fredholm determinant and useful on a real spatial dataset showing coherent overdispersion trends and convergence with m.

- Writing: generally clear with helpful discussion of L vs K parameterizations and identifiability trade-offs.

- Computational trade-offs unclear: replacing power iterations with nonconvex, growing-dimension Z-optimization may not be a net win and lacks head-to-head runtime/accuracy comparisons to Affandi et al. (2013).

- Missing tightening guarantees: no theorem in main text ensuring convergence or monotonicity of bounds as m increases and Z is optimized, which is crucial for retrospective MH reliability.

- Sensitivity to Z-optimization: local minima could stall MH tightening; paper lacks diagnostics, ablations, and analyses of initialization and optimization choices.

- Practical memory/compute concerns: finite-case still requires O(n m^2) time and full access to L; memory footprint and streaming vs storing L not fully addressed.

- Presentation issues: some figures lack axes, the central Proposition 1 needs a main-text proof sketch, and minor typos remain.

- Quality assessment: derivations are careful and based on solid determinant inequalities, but theoretical guarantees about tightening are informal or deferred.

- Clarity recommendations: add a proof sketch for Proposition 1 and clearer continuous-case assumptions (trace-class, computability of Ψ); clean up figures.

- Originality: high — novel application of inducing-point-style determinant bounds to DPP normalizers and continuous DPPs.

- Significance: potentially high if competitive empirically, as it broadens DPP inference to settings where spectra are costly or unavailable.

- Pros for acceptance: elegant nonspectral bounds, applicability to continuous DPPs, enables variational and exact MH inference, and strong links to sparse GP literature.

- Cons against acceptance: lack of theoretical tightening guarantees, limited empirical comparisons to spectral methods on large finite sets, and potential fragility from nonconvex Z-optimization.

- Suggested improvements: prove or state conditions for bounds tightening as m→∞, add comparisons vs Affandi et al. (2013) with time/memory/accuracy, include Z ablations and MH acceptance diagnostics, move Proposition 1 sketch to main text, state continuous assumptions, and improve figures/typos.

- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — clever and potentially impactful but needs stronger guarantees and broader empirical validation.

- Reviewer confidence: 3/5 (familiar with DPPs and sparse GP methods; did not verify all operator-theoretic details).
