- Paper proposes nonspectral likelihood inference for DPPs by parametrizing the L-kernel and avoiding K-kernel spectral constraints.  
- Main technical contribution: upper and lower inducing-point determinant bounds on det(I+L) for finite and continuous DPPs, avoiding eigencomputations.  
- Bounds constructed via an inducing-point approximation Q = L_·Z LZ^{-1} LZ·, inspired by sparse GP inducing-variable methods.  
- Bounds are cheap to evaluate (O(n m^2) for m inducing points) and applicable to Fredholm determinants in continuous domains.  
- Enables a variational ELBO for approximate learning and retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) that is exact in the limit.  
- Theoretical development leverages established determinant/operator inequalities and extends to continuous DPPs using Fredholm determinant theory.  
- Novelty: replacing spectral/Nyström approximations with inducing-point determinant bounds and connecting DPPs to variational GP methodology.  
- Quality: propositions bounding det(I+L) are mathematically sound and correctly integrated into variational and MH frameworks.  
- Experiments: 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP validates retrospective MH against ground truth; real diabetic neuropathy data reproduces known effects and shows convergence with m.  
- Paper clearly discusses L vs K parametrization tradeoffs and identifiability issues (e.g., κ).  
- Clarity: generally well written; connections to sparse GP methods aid intuition, though some presentation issues remain.  
- Strength: practical applicability to large discrete and continuous domains without eigen-decompositions.  
- Strength: validated convergence and correctness in experiments; transparent about limitations.  
- Weakness: computational advantage over spectral methods not fully established—need wall-clock/runtime comparisons vs power iteration/Lanczos across n and accuracy.  
- Weakness: monotonic tightening of bounds under retrospective MH is not guaranteed when Z is re-optimized; recommend nested Z or formal monotonicity results and fallback strategies.  
- Weakness: ELBO nonconvexity in Z and θ—needs practical guidance on initializing Z, choosing m, restarts, and diagnostics.  
- Weakness: no theoretical rates for bound convergence with m; even qualitative discussion would strengthen claims.  
- Suggested experiments: sensitivity of MH acceptance and runtime to m, mixing diagnostics, and ablation comparing fixed vs optimized Z.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, principled, and broadly applicable bounds with sound theory and useful algorithms likely to influence follow-up work.  
- Rejection concerns: limited empirical benchmarking of efficiency vs spectral methods and potential fragility in retrospective MH when Z optimization stalls.  
- Overall: technically clever and sound contribution that would benefit from stronger empirical comparisons and clarified refinement guarantees; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
