- Addresses the key bottleneck in DPP likelihood inference: computing the normalizing determinant (finite: det(L+I), continuous: Fredholm determinant).  
- Main contribution: novel non-spectral upper and lower bounds for the determinant using m inducing (pseudo-)inputs to build a low-rank kernel approximation.  
- Method avoids costly spectral/eigenvalue solvers and scales as O(n m^2) instead of O(n^3).  
- Bounds apply to both finite and continuous DPPs, broadening applicability beyond finite-domain spectral methods.  
- Integrates bounds into two inference schemes: a fast approximate variational inference (maximize lower bound) and an asymptotically exact MCMC via retrospective sampling.  
- Paper is technically sound, derivations use established matrix inequalities, and claims are well-supported experimentally.  
- Experiments are thorough and candid: toy 1D shows VI can fail to recover a parameter (κ) while MCMC succeeds, illustrating trade-offs.  
- Real-world diabetic neuropathy experiments show bounds tighten and parameter estimates converge as m increases.  
- Clarity and presentation are strong: clear organization, progressive exposition (finite → continuous), consistent notation, and helpful connection to sparse GP literature.  
- Originality: creative and novel application of inducing-point ideas from sparse GPs to derive non-spectral DPP likelihood bounds.  
- Significance: substantially reduces computational cost of DPP inference, likely enabling wider practical use and further research extensions.  
- Minor weakness: initial motivation favoring parameterizing K for interpretability is not resolved—paper still parameterizes L for tractability.  
- Minor presentation suggestion: provide a standard self-contained supplement rather than only an extended version.  
- Acceptance arguments: tackles an important bottleneck, offers elegant novel bounds, insightful GP–DPP connection, and convincing empirical validation.  
- Future directions suggested: automatic selection/adaptation of m and extending the bounding techniques to work directly with the K kernel.
