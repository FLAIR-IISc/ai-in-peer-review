Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes likelihood bounds for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid any spectral decomposition. The main idea is to introduce a low-rank surrogate Q = LYZ L−1 ZZ LZY, built from a set of m inducing inputs Z, and use a determinant inequality (Seiler–Simon) to upper- and lower-bound det(I + L) in the finite case and the Fredholm determinant det(I + L) in the continuous case. These bounds lead to: (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood that can be optimized jointly over kernel parameters and inducing inputs; and (ii) a retrospective MCMC scheme that refines the bounds adaptively by increasing m and re-optimizing Z until an MH accept/reject decision can be made. The approach draws an explicit analogy to sparse GP variational methods (e.g., Titsias, AISTATS’09), and contrasts with prior DPP inference based on spectral information, such as Affandi et al. (ICML’14) who use bounds requiring leading eigenvalues. The paper extends the bounds to continuous DPPs (with trace-class assumptions) by replacing finite-dimensional matrices with operators and an analytically computable Ψ-term for popular Gaussian–Gaussian settings. Empirically, the authors verify correctness on a 1D toy problem with a known spectrum and illustrate scalability on a diabetic neuropathy dataset, reporting convergence of variational estimates as m increases.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: simple nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods, applicable to both finite and continuous domains.
- Practicality: avoids eigen-decompositions and only requires kernel evaluations; complexity O(n m^2) for the variational bound when m ≪ n.
- Generality: unified treatment of finite and continuous DPPs; the continuous case is rarely addressed at scale.
- Inference breadth: both a cheap variational method and a principled, albeit costlier, retrospective MH scheme.
- Empirical sanity checks: retrospective MH matches “ideal” MH on the toy where det(I + L) is computable; inducing inputs exhibit intuitive structure.

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical evaluation is thin. Missing comparisons (accuracy and runtime) to: (a) spectral-bound MCMC (Affandi et al., ICML’14), (b) EM/fixed-point methods for L-ensembles (Gillenwater et al., NIPS’14; Mariet & Sra, NIPS’15), and (c) log-det approximations (e.g., stochastic trace/Chebyshev). Please add head-to-head comparisons with matched hardware, reporting wall-clock time, memory, and accuracy (held-out likelihood or parameter error).
- Complexity vs. wall-clock. Provide a clear complexity table and wall-clock scaling plots versus n and m, for both proposed methods. How expensive is optimizing Z at each MH step relative to likelihood evaluation?
- Variational instability. On the toy example, the variational method exhibits large parameter error (especially κ). Please clarify when you expect the variational approach to work or fail. Is the issue identifiability (scaling of L) or bound looseness? Can tempering or priors stabilize κ? A small ablation varying m and Z initializations would help.
- Inducing inputs selection. Provide rule-of-thumb guidance for choosing m and initializing Z. How do k-means on pooled points, k-DPP, farthest-point/greedy det, or maximizing posterior variance (as in sparse GPs) compare? Report performance when Z is set to the observed sample points in the toy (this is a natural baseline).
- Tightness and diagnostics. Quantify the bound gap (upper–lower) as a function of m and Z, and connect this to inference error. In MH, specify stopping criteria for retrospective refinement (e.g., gap < ε) and report acceptance rates and average m used.
- Clarity around K vs L. Early sections use K and L without a concise primer. For readers new to DPPs, briefly define marginal kernel K (with eigenvalues in [0,1]) and L-ensembles, and explain why most ML work parameterizes L directly.
- Continuous case practicality. Computing Ψ requires an integral; for which kernels/base measures is Ψ closed-form or efficiently approximable? Provide complexity and numerical strategies (quadrature, RFF, inducing measures) and an example beyond Gaussian–Gaussian.
- Scope of proofs. Key determinant inequalities are deferred; a high-level proof sketch and conditions under which equality holds (e.g., Z → dense) would help assess robustness.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The determinant bounds appear correct and well-motivated by Seiler–Simon; the extension to operators is reasonable under trace-class assumptions. The retrospective MH recipe is standard. However, the paper lacks a quantitative analysis of bound tightness and its impact on inference quality, and does not yet demonstrate superiority or parity on standard benchmarks versus existing methods.
- Experimental support: Demonstrations are suggestive but limited. The toy confirms correctness; the neuropathy study shows convergence with m and separation between classes, but without comparisons or wall-clock studies it is hard to assess practical gains.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but the method description could be more self-contained. Please:
  - Add a concise primer on K vs L and L-ensembles early on.
  - Present pseudo-code for (i) variational optimization (including gradient expressions wrt θ and Z) and (ii) retrospective MH (initial m, refinement schedule, stopping).
  - Include a complexity table and a notation table (L, Q, Ψ, Z).
  - Reduce the extended discussion about interpreting K-parameters since the paper commits to L-parameterization.

Originality
- The specific nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods, their operator extension, and their use in retrospective MH appear novel. The connection to inducing-point GP ideas is insightful, though conceptually related.

Significance
- Potentially significant: removing spectral requirements could broaden DPP applicability to large finite and continuous domains. The practical impact hinges on empirical evidence that the bounds are tight enough and deliver competitive accuracy–speed trade-offs versus the best alternatives.

Specific requests for revision
- Add runtime and accuracy comparisons to spectral-bound MCMC (Affandi et al.) and EM/fixed-point methods on finite datasets; include cases where exact log-det is feasible for sanity checks.
- Provide wall-clock scaling plots and a complexity table.
- Analyze bound gaps vs. m and Z; propose and evaluate heuristics for choosing m and initializing Z; include a run with Z equal to observed points on the toy.
- Clarify when variational inference is expected to succeed; discuss identifiability (especially κ) and provide stabilization strategies (priors, constraints, reparameterizations).
- Elaborate on Ψ computation and applicability in the continuous case; include a non-Gaussian example if possible.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, general nonspectral bounds with clear algorithmic consequences (variational + MH).
- Extends to continuous DPPs, which are underserved in ML.
- Simple to implement and potentially widely useful.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient empirical validation and lack of comparisons to strong baselines.
- Limited guidance on choosing/optimizing inducing inputs; variational instability not well explained.
- Some clarity gaps and missing practical details.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and technically sound, but the paper needs stronger empirical evidence, clearer methodological guidance, and improved exposition to meet the bar. If these issues are addressed, this could become a valuable contribution.
