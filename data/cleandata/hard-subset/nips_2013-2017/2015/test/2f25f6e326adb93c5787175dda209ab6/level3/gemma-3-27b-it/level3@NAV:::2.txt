## Review of “Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge”

This paper addresses a key challenge in applying Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) – performing likelihood-based inference without requiring costly spectral decompositions. The authors propose novel upper and lower bounds on the DPP likelihood, avoiding spectral computations and enabling both variational inference and MCMC methods. This is a significant contribution, as spectral decomposition is often a bottleneck for large-scale DPP applications.

The core idea is elegantly simple: leverage bounds inspired by Gaussian process variational inference and extend them to DPPs using pseudo-inputs. This approach is well-executed, and the mathematical derivations appear sound (though relying on the supplement for full proof details). The proposed bounds are general, applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs, extending the applicability beyond typical machine learning settings.  The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers on DPPs and related inference techniques.  The experimental evaluation is well-constructed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed bounds in both a synthetic Gaussian example and a real-world dataset of nerve fiber patterns. Importantly, the experiments highlight scenarios where variational inference may struggle, which is a crucial honesty in evaluation. The chosen application area is also highly relevant, as modeling diversity in spatial data is a significant problem. 

A minor point is the emphasis on parameterizing the kernel *L* instead of *K*. While the authors acknowledge the interpretability trade-off, a deeper discussion of why this parameterization is specifically emphasized would be beneficial. The supplement, being an extended paper rather than a standard supplement, might be less accessible for reviewers wishing for concise proof details.  

Looking ahead, the authors suggest bounding the determinant directly in terms of *K* – this is a compelling direction for future work. Additionally, assessing the scalability of the pseudo-input method with increasing data dimensionality would strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Proposes a new approach to DPP inference avoiding spectral decomposition.
*   **Significance:** Addresses a practical bottleneck in DPP applications.
*   **Soundness:** Theoretically well-grounded and experimentally validated.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the core ideas are clearly explained.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion on the implications of directly parameterizing *L* versus *K*.
*   Supplement format could be improved for accessibility.


**Overall Recommendation:** I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a valuable contribution to the field, offering a practical solution to a significant problem. The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and has the potential to be widely adopted by researchers working with DPPs.
