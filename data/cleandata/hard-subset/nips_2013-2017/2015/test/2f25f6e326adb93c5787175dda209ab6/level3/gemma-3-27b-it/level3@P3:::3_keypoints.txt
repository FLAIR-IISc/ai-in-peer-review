- Proposes approximate DPP inference that avoids spectral decomposition by using inducing inputs to bound the likelihood.  
- Presents both variational inference and approximate MCMC methods with empirical demos on toy and real data.  
- Well-motivated: tackles the computational bottleneck of DPP normalizing constants and links clearly to sparse GP approximations.  
- Core idea is promising and addresses a significant problem in DPP inference.  
- Method description is under-detailed and often rough, impairing reproducibility.  
- Connection between Proposition 1 (bounds) and the practical variational implementation (Section 3.1) is unclear.  
- Unclear how optimization is performed jointly over model parameters θ and inducing locations Z in practice.  
- MCMC method description, especially the retrospective sampling step, is hand-wavy and needs more detail or pseudocode.  
- Empirical evaluation is insufficient: missing wall-clock/runtime comparisons between variational and MCMC methods.  
- Lacks runtime comparison to exact inference (where feasible) and to other approximations (e.g., Nyström).  
- Missing quantitative comparisons of approximation quality (e.g., test log-likelihoods) across methods.  
- Toy example indicates large parameter estimation error for variational inference, suggesting unreliability or sensitivity to initialization.  
- Paper fails to explain when variational inference should work well versus when it will fail, and why the toy error occurs.  
- Should evaluate the toy case with inducing inputs set to the true sampled points as a best-case benchmark.  
- No practical guidance on selecting number of inducing points m, initialization strategies, or diminishing returns as m increases.  
- Claims about learning marginal kernel parameters and direct L parametrization are inconsistently presented and need streamlining.  
- Minor clarity issues: matrices K and L are used without prior definition; brief DPP context (marginal kernel, L-ensembles) needed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a key computational challenge, novel inducing-input likelihood bounds, promising real-data results, solid GP connections.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear method description, weak empirical evaluation, variational errors, and no practical guidance on inducing points.  
- Recommendation: reject in current form but consider resubmission after substantially improving clarity, experiments (including runtimes and comparisons), and analysis of inducing-point sensitivity.
