Summary and relation to prior work: The paper tackles a core obstacle in likelihood-based learning for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the intractable normalization term det(I+L) (or its Fredholm analog), which in prior work (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’14) is bounded via partial spectral information to enable provably correct retrospective MCMC over kernel parameters. The authors propose alternative, nonspectral bounds that avoid eigenvalue estimation by leveraging an inducing-point construction Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY. A key contribution is Proposition 1, which provides tight upper and lower determinant bounds using Q and a trace correction; analogous bounds are given for continuous DPPs through Fredholm determinants. These bounds support both exact retrospective MH and a variational objective, and the construction draws a compelling connection to inducing-point methods for sparse Gaussian processes (Titsias, AISTATS’09).

Quality: Technically strong and largely sound. The determinant inequalities (via Seiler–Simon) are used deftly, and the extension to continuous domains with computable Ψ is valuable. The retrospective MCMC adaptation is principled. Experiments convincingly validate correctness (1D Gaussian-Gaussian, comparison to exact) and illustrate utility on the diabetic neuropathy dataset. A concern is computational: replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization over growing sets of inducing points may not always be a net win, and convergence of the bounds as m increases is not guaranteed in the presence of coupled, non-convex optimization; local minima could stall valid MH decisions.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized, with clear motivation and links to GP literature. However, Figure 1 lacks axis labels; Proposition 1 (the main conceptual leap) should be in the main text; minor wording/typo (“the variational lower is …”).

Originality: High. The nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods and their variational use are, to my knowledge, novel, and the GP-inducing connection is insightful.

Significance: Potentially impactful. Removing spectral requirements broadens applicability to large finite and continuous settings, and the variational route could enable faster approximate learning. The method could influence related kernel models that rely on Fredholm determinants.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clever, general bounds enabling both VI and provably correct retrospective MCMC without spectra.
- Clear GP connections; Proposition 1 is a notable insight.
- Continuous-domain treatment broadens scope.

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical computational tradeoffs vs spectral methods not fully resolved.
- No guarantees that increasing m and optimizing Z monotonically tightens bounds; possible MCMC deadlocks.
- Some presentation issues.

Suggestions: Provide wall-clock and accuracy comparisons to spectral retrospective MCMC; clarify conditions under which bounds tighten with m→∞; discuss optimization strategies for Z (initialization, regularization, stochastic gradients); document failure modes and diagnostics; fix figure labels and move Proposition 1’s proof to main text. Overall, I lean toward acceptance.
