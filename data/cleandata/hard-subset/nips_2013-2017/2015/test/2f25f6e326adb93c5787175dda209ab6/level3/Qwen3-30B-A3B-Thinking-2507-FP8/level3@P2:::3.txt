

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a key challenge in DPP inference: the intractable normalizing constant requiring spectral decomposition. The authors propose a novel approach using inducing inputs to derive bounds on the likelihood that avoid spectral decomposition, enabling variational inference and MCMC. This extends work on GP inference [11] to DPPs, building on previous DPP inference methods like [7] that rely on spectral approximations.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a technically sound contribution with clear theoretical foundations. The bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 are novel for DPP inference and avoid the computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition. The connection to GP inference is well-motivated, and the extension to continuous DPPs is a valuable contribution. The empirical evaluation demonstrates the approach on both a toy Gaussian example and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset, showing the method works in practice. The paper correctly identifies that parametrizing L directly (rather than K) sacrifices interpretability for tractability, which aligns with standard practice in the field [3, 7].

**Weaknesses:**
The paper has significant weaknesses in empirical evaluation and method description. First, the empirical results lack crucial comparisons: there is no comparison to other approximate methods (like [7]) in terms of approximation quality, runtime, or parameter estimation accuracy. The variational inference approach shows large parameter estimation errors (up to 100% for α and σ) on the toy example without explanation of when it works or fails. The paper also fails to provide guidance for selecting the number of inducing points or their initialization—critical practical considerations. The computational complexity analysis is incomplete; the paper claims the method is "cheap" but doesn't compare wall-clock times to alternatives.

The method description is rough in places. The paper uses K and L without defining them as the marginal kernel and L-ensemble (as in [5]), which may confuse readers unfamiliar with DPPs. The discussion about the advantages of learning K parameters is inconsistent with the paper's approach of directly parametrizing L, creating confusion about the trade-offs.

**Quality:** The theoretical results are sound, but the experimental evaluation is insufficient to validate the claims. The paper doesn't demonstrate when the bounds are tight or how they compare to existing methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized but lacks clarity on practical implementation details. The connection between inducing inputs and the likelihood bounds could be better explained.

**Originality:** The bounds are novel for DPP inference without spectral decomposition, and the extension to continuous DPPs is a valuable contribution. This work significantly differs from [7] which relies on spectral approximations.

**Significance:** DPPs have growing applications in machine learning (diversity modeling in search results [7], news summarization [3], spatial statistics [4]). A method avoiding spectral decomposition could enable larger-scale applications, but this potential is not demonstrated through sufficient empirical evidence.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Novel approach to a well-known challenge in DPP inference; sound theoretical contribution; general framework for finite and continuous DPPs; connects to well-established GP inference techniques.

*Con:* Insufficient empirical evaluation without comparisons to existing methods; lacks practical guidance for implementation; variational inference shows large errors without explanation; method description is incomplete.

**Recommendation:** Major revision. The paper has merit but requires significant strengthening of the empirical evaluation and method description. Specifically, the authors should:
1. Add comparisons to existing methods (including [7]) in terms of both approximation quality and runtime
2. Provide clear guidance for selecting the number of inducing points and their initialization
3. Explain the circumstances under which variational inference succeeds or fails
4. Clarify the relationship between K and L to improve accessibility
5. Include wall-clock time comparisons for the methods

Without these revisions, the paper's claims about the efficiency and effectiveness of the proposed method cannot be properly evaluated. The current empirical evidence is insufficient to demonstrate that the method provides a meaningful advance over existing approaches.
