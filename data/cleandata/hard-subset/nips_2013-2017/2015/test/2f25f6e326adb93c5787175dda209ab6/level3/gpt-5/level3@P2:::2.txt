Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes nonspectral upper and lower bounds on determinants that appear in the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs) for both finite and continuous domains. The core idea is to approximate the DPP kernel L with a low-rank object constructed from arbitrary “pseudo-inputs” Z: Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY for the finite case, and to derive determinant bounds of the form det(Q+I) with a trace correction e^{-tr(L−Q)} (finite) and an analogous construction for Fredholm determinants involving LZ and Ψ for the continuous case. These bounds avoid computing eigenvalues of L, in contrast to prior work that relied on spectral decompositions or partial spectra (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’14; Kulesza & Taskar). The approach is conceptually related to inducing-variable sparse variational GP methods (e.g., Titsias, AISTATS’09), but applied to determinants rather than predictive conditionals.

The bounds are used in two inference settings: (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood for fast approximate training, jointly optimizing kernel parameters and pseudo-inputs; and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler that refines the bounds adaptively (by increasing m and optimizing Z) until the accept/reject decision is determined. The continuous-domain extension leverages computable Ψ integrals for Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures and ties to Fredholm determinants (Bornemann, 2010). Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian synthetic where exact spectra are known, and a 2D real dataset on diabetic neuropathy previously used for DPP modeling, showing both the strengths of the MCMC approach and practical convergence of the variational method as m increases.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The determinant inequalities are grounded in Seiler & Simon’s determinant inequality and are applied carefully to yield practical bounds. The MH “retrospective” construction is standard and correctly adapted to nonspectral bounds. The continuous-domain extension is clearly stated with reasonable operator assumptions and demonstrates computable quantities for common kernels/measures.
- Empirical evaluation: The synthetic study validates posterior recovery and highlights an identifiability issue (κ) where variational training struggles but MCMC succeeds—a valuable and honest observation. The neuropathy study shows coherent convergence with m and interpretable separation between classes.
- Limitations: Bound tightness depends critically on Z; there is no quantitative gap control beyond increasing m, and guidance on Z initialization/optimization is limited. Scalability beyond the reported sizes, especially in high dimensions and for very large n, is not fully assessed. For non-Gaussian base measures, Ψ may be expensive or intractable.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized, with intuitive motivation, connections to sparse GP ideas, and careful separation of finite vs. continuous cases. The role and interpretation of L vs. K are discussed, though the paper emphasizes L in practice.
- Suggestions: Provide a brief recipe for choosing/initializing Z, monitoring the bound gap to adapt m, and practical gradient expressions when optimizing Z. A standard supplemental document (rather than only an extended version) would improve accessibility and reproducibility.

Originality
- The use of pseudo-inputs to produce computable nonspectral upper and lower bounds for DPP log-normalizers (determinants/Fredholm determinants) appears novel and distinct from prior spectral or Nyström-based approximations. The adaptation to retrospective MH is natural but impactful given the avoidance of eigendecompositions.
- Relation to prior work is appropriate, and the GP variational analogy is insightful.

Significance
- The approach directly targets a central obstacle to likelihood-based inference for DPPs—computing det(I+L)—and enables both fast variational training and practical exact inference. The bounds are general and may be useful beyond DPPs for other determinant-based objectives. The diabetic neuropathy application underscores practical relevance, and the synthetic study is diagnostic.

Strengths
- Simple, well-motivated, and broadly applicable idea; avoids spectral computations.
- Creative and illuminating use of pseudo-inputs; clear connection to sparse GPs.
- Both variational and MCMC instantiations; retrospective MH is practical and exact.
- Solid experiments highlighting both successes and a failure mode of VI.
- Timely and of high interest for the NIPS community; likely to spur follow-on work.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Bound tightness and convergence: No quantitative guarantees on the gap as a function of m; empirical guidance would help (e.g., stopping rules based on bound gap).
- Choice/optimization of Z: Provide more detail (gradients, initialization heuristics, robustness), especially for continuous domains and higher dimensions.
- Parameterization discussion: The paper repeatedly motivates parameterizing K but ultimately works entirely with L. Either de-emphasize K or add a short section bounding determinants directly for K or providing concrete bridges to K without spectra.
- Scalability: More systematic timing/memory comparisons with spectral methods, Nyström baselines, and randomized log-det estimators would strengthen claims of efficiency.
- Generality of Ψ: Clarify for which kernel–measure pairs Ψ is tractable and provide fallback approximations (e.g., quadrature, low-rank features).

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide empirical curves of the bound gap vs. m and its impact on parameter estimates and predictive tasks? Could this serve as an automatic m-selection criterion?
- How sensitive are results to Z initialization? Do grid initializations generally work better than random?
- For finite DPPs with very large n, can stochastic or mini-batch variants of the variational objective be used?
- Could your determinant bounds be combined with randomized log-det estimators to reduce dependence on Z in very large-scale settings?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled, and practical contribution addressing a core bottleneck for DPP inference.
- Clear writing, good experimental design, and honest discussion of limitations.
- Likely to influence subsequent work on DPPs and determinant-based inference broadly.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical characterization of bound tightness and convergence with m.
- Limited guidance on Z selection/optimization and scalability in high dimensions.
- The discussion of K vs. L is raised but not substantively advanced.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper introduces a simple and powerful idea with clear practical impact, strong connections to established methodology, and careful empirical validation. Minor presentation improvements (expanded supplement, guidance on Z/m, and a tighter narrative on K vs. L) would further strengthen the final version.

Minor suggestions
- Provide a standard supplement with full proofs, gradients, and algorithmic details.
- Consider a short discussion or experiment bounding det(I+L) when starting from a K parameterization, and report preliminary scalability of the pseudo-input method under that setting.
