

This paper presents novel bounds on the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid spectral decompositions, enabling more efficient inference. The authors address two key challenges in DPP inference: the intractable spectral decomposition required for the likelihood's normalizing constant and the need for efficient parameter estimation. They derive bounds on the determinant terms in the DPP likelihood that depend on "pseudo-inputs" (similar to inducing variables in sparse Gaussian processes), rather than spectral information. These bounds enable both variational inference and exact Markov chain Monte Carlo methods for DPPs over finite and continuous domains.

The work builds on prior DPP inference methods such as those in Affandi et al. (ICML 2013) and Gillenwater et al. (NIPS 2012), but significantly improves upon them by avoiding spectral approximations. While previous work required spectral information (e.g., via power iteration), the authors' approach provides bounds that are cheap to evaluate and can be tightened through optimization of the pseudo-inputs.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical results. The bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 are rigorously derived using determinant inequalities, and the experimental validation demonstrates their practical utility. The authors clearly address the trade-offs between approximation quality and computational cost, and their experiments on both synthetic and real data (diabetic neuropathy dataset) provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness. The paper carefully discusses limitations, such as the poor identifiability of the parameter κ in the continuous case, showing appropriate scholarly honesty.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem, and the technical development flows logically from finite to continuous domains. The connection to Gaussian process inference (via pseudo-inputs) is well-explained, making the approach accessible to the broader machine learning community. The experimental section is thorough and provides useful insights into when variational inference succeeds or fails compared to MCMC.

**Originality:** The main contribution—deriving nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods—represents a significant advance over existing methods. While the use of inducing variables for determinants has precedents in Gaussian processes, their application to DPPs without spectral knowledge is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work (e.g., Affandi et al., NIPS 2013) by avoiding spectral approximations entirely. The authors also extend their method to continuous domains, which is less explored in the literature.

**Significance:** This work addresses a key bottleneck in DPP applications—efficient inference—which has been a barrier to wider adoption. The bounds enable more scalable inference, making DPPs more practical for real-world applications like modeling diverse news summaries (Kulesza & Taskar, NIPS 2011) or spatial patterns of nerve fibers (as demonstrated in the diabetic neuropathy experiment). The method's generality (applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs) and potential to extend beyond DPPs (as noted in the discussion) further enhance its significance.

**Strengths:** The paper is conceptually simple yet powerful, with a clean theoretical foundation and strong experimental validation. The authors demonstrate how their bounds can be used to build practical inference algorithms (variational and MCMC), and the experiments clearly show the method's advantages over previous approaches. The choice of the diabetic neuropathy dataset provides a compelling real-world application.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's emphasis on parameterizing K instead of L (the natural kernel for the likelihood) is not fully justified. The authors acknowledge this issue but do not explore it in depth, which could be a more significant contribution. Additionally, the supplement appears to be a long extended paper rather than a standard supplement, which would make it harder for readers to access the key technical details.

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a strong contribution to the DPP inference literature with practical implications for the broader machine learning community. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the method is both novel and broadly applicable. While the focus on parameterizing K instead of L is somewhat underdeveloped, this does not detract from the paper's core contribution. The minor issue with the supplement format can be easily addressed in the final version.

The paper merits acceptance at NIPS for its technical quality, clear exposition, and meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling. The work provides a valuable tool for researchers working with DPPs and demonstrates how techniques from Gaussian processes can be fruitfully adapted to other probabilistic models.
