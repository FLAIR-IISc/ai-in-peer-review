Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes nonspectral upper and lower bounds on determinants that appear in DPP likelihoods for both finite and continuous domains. The key construction introduces pseudo-inputs Z to define a low-rank surrogate Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, yielding bounds on det(I + L) and, in the continuous case, on the Fredholm determinant via computable kernel integrals Ψ. These bounds enable a variational lower bound for approximate learning and a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that circumvents eigenvalue computations. The approach is inspired by inducing-point methods in sparse GPs (Titsias, AISTATS’09) and replaces the spectral bracketing strategy of Affandi et al. (ICML’13), thereby broadening applicability to continuous DPPs and large discrete domains without eigendecompositions. The paper also connects to numerical work on Fredholm determinants (Bornemann) and determinant inequalities (Seiler & Simon).

Quality: The theory is technically sound, with clear finite- and infinite-dimensional statements and proofs outlined (details in the supplement). The variational and MCMC constructions correctly use the bounds, and experiments thoughtfully probe both success and failure modes (e.g., variational failures in the 1D Gaussian–Gaussian example and identifiability issues). Some practical guidance on tightness vs m and optimization of Z is given, but more quantitative diagnostics would help.

Clarity: The exposition is generally clear and well-organized, with a helpful progression from finite to continuous settings. However, the manuscript emphasizes the K vs L parameterization tension early on without addressing it in depth later; either tone this down or provide additional discussion. Also, please provide a standard supplementary PDF with proofs and implementation details rather than only an extended version.

Originality: The determinant bounds via pseudo-inputs for DPP likelihoods are novel and conceptually elegant, extending GP-style variational ideas to DPPs and to Fredholm determinants.

Significance: This is a timely contribution. The method is general, has potential applications beyond DPPs (e.g., bounding functionals of PD kernels), and will likely be used by practitioners who wish to avoid spectral computations.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general, and enlightening idea; well executed.
- Creative pseudo-input mechanism with broad applicability.
- Strong experimental design illustrating both behaviors and failure modes; relevant application (neuropathy data).
- Extends to continuous DPPs without spectral knowledge.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited quantitative guidance on tightness, scalability, and choice/optimization of Z; high-dimensional behavior unclear.
- Comparisons to spectral methods and Nyström baselines could be expanded.
- K vs L discussion underdeveloped; supplement format should be standardized.

Suggestions: (i) Add scaling plots in n, m, and d; report wall-clock times. (ii) Compare against spectral bracketing where feasible. (iii) Explore bounding determinants directly in terms of K and assess the scalability/automation of pseudo-input selection. (iv) Clarify computational costs of Ψ in higher dimensions and provide implementation details.
