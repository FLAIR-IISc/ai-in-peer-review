This paper presents novel bounds on the determinant arising in the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs), both for finite and continuous domains. The core idea is elegantly simple – leveraging pseudo-inputs to bound the determinant – and well-executed, offering a valuable contribution to the field by circumventing the computationally expensive spectral decomposition traditionally required for DPP inference. The work is timely, addressing a significant bottleneck in DPP applications, and possesses a broad potential applicability beyond DPPs themselves.

**Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the challenge of likelihood-based inference in DPPs. Standard DPP likelihood calculations involve determinants that are costly to compute, particularly for large datasets or continuous domains. Prior work (e.g., [7]) attempted to address this using spectral approximations, but these methods still require iterative computations (power iteration) and can be slow. This paper cleverly avoids spectral decomposition by proposing bounds on the determinant using a technique inspired by variational inference for Gaussian processes [11]. The use of pseudo-inputs (or inducing variables) to approximate kernel operators is a known technique (e.g., sparse GPs), but its application to bounding DPP likelihoods and the derived theoretical bounds are novel. The paper appropriately contextualizes the work within the broader landscape of DPP research, citing key papers in the field ([1, 2, 3, 4, 5]) and highlighting applications in diverse areas ([3, 4, 6, 7]).

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed bounds (Propositions 1 & 2) appear theoretically well-founded, building on existing determinant inequalities [14]. The paper clearly outlines the assumptions necessary for these bounds to hold.
* **Originality:** The approach of using pseudo-inputs to directly bound the determinant, circumventing spectral decomposition, is a novel and significant contribution. This distinguishes the work from previous attempts to tackle the DPP likelihood problem.
* **Significance:** Addressing the computational bottleneck of DPP likelihood evaluation is a crucial step towards wider adoption of DPPs in machine learning. The proposed bounds offer a practical pathway to more efficient inference. The potential applications, as highlighted by the authors and demonstrated in the experiments, are broad and important.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the connection to related work (e.g., sparse GPs) is explicitly stated.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental evaluation is well-constructed. The 1D Gaussian example convincingly demonstrates the correctness of the MCMC sampler and illustrates the limitations of parameter identifiability when directly parametrizing L. The application to the diabetic neuropathy dataset shows the practicality of the variational approach and provides empirical validation of the bounds. The comparison to previous work [7] is valuable.
* **Generality:** The authors correctly point out the potential applicability of the approach beyond DPPs, suggesting broader impact.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameterization Choice:** The emphasis on parameterizing *L* rather than *K* is not fully justified. While the authors acknowledge the interpretability trade-off, the paper doesn’t delve deeper into the implications of this choice or explore potential benefits of working with *K*.  A more thorough discussion of this design decision would be beneficial.
* **Supplement Format:** Providing an "extended paper version" as the supplement is less ideal than a standard supplemental document. A dedicated supplement would allow for a more organized presentation of proofs and additional experimental details.
* **Scalability Analysis:** While the experimental section demonstrates the method's feasibility, a more detailed analysis of the scalability with respect to dataset size (n or dimensionality) and the number of pseudo-inputs (m) would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant computational challenge in DPP inference.
* The proposed solution is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.
* The work is clearly written and well-motivated.
* The potential impact of the research is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limitations of directly parametrizing *L* could be more thoroughly discussed.
* The supplement format is sub-optimal.
* A more detailed scalability analysis would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of determinantal point processes. The proposed bounds provide a practical and efficient way to perform inference in DPPs, opening up new possibilities for their application in various domains. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the supplement format and including a discussion of the scalability of the pseudo-input method, potentially with a few additional experiments. Also, elaborating on the parameterization choice of L vs K would be helpful.
