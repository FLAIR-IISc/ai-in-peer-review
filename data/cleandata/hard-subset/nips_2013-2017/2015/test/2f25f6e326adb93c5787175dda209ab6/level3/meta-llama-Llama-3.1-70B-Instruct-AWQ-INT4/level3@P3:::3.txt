This paper proposes novel bounds on the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs) that do not require spectral decomposition, enabling efficient inference for DPPs. The authors derive bounds for both finite and continuous domains, leveraging inducing inputs to approximate the likelihood. These bounds are used to develop variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods for DPPs.

The paper is well-motivated, as DPPs are a useful tool for modeling diversity in various applications. The authors provide a clear overview of the challenges in performing likelihood-based inference for DPPs, particularly the need for spectral decomposition and the intractable normalizing constant. The proposed bounds address these challenges, making likelihood-based inference more feasible.

The technical contributions of the paper are sound, and the authors provide a thorough derivation of the bounds. The use of inducing inputs to approximate the likelihood is a clever approach, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in various experiments.

However, the paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons to existing methods. While the authors mention the work of [7], they do not provide a comprehensive comparison of their approach to other approximate and exact methods. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more discussion on the choice of inducing inputs and their impact on the quality of the bounds.

The experimental evaluation is limited, with only two experiments presented. The first experiment on a toy Gaussian continuous DPP shows that the proposed MCMC method can recover the true parameters, but the variational inference method performs poorly. The second experiment on a real dataset of diabetic neuropathy demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method in distinguishing between different classes of point processes. However, more experiments on various datasets and comparison to other methods would strengthen the paper.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the technical details. However, some minor comments can be made. The notation is sometimes unclear, and the authors could provide more explanation for the uninformed reader. For example, the matrices K and L are used without definition, and a brief explanation of the marginal kernel and L-ensembles would be helpful.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to inference for DPPs, leveraging inducing inputs to approximate the likelihood. While the idea of using inducing inputs is not new, its application to DPPs is a significant contribution. The paper also demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method in various experiments, which is a notable achievement.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in machine learning and statistics, namely, efficient inference for DPPs. The proposed method has the potential to be widely applicable, as DPPs are used in various fields, including computer vision, natural language processing, and spatial statistics.

Overall, the paper has merit, but the method description could be improved, and the empirical evaluation is insufficient. The authors should provide more detailed comparisons to existing methods, discuss the choice of inducing inputs, and present more experiments on various datasets.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to inference for DPPs, leveraging inducing inputs to approximate the likelihood.
* The technical contributions of the paper are sound, and the authors provide a thorough derivation of the bounds.
* The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method in various experiments.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from more detailed comparisons to existing methods.
* The experimental evaluation is limited, with only two experiments presented.
* The paper could benefit from more discussion on the choice of inducing inputs and their impact on the quality of the bounds.

Recommendations:

* Improve the clarity of the method description, particularly with regards to notation and technical details.
* Provide more detailed comparisons to existing methods, including approximate and exact alternatives.
* Present more experiments on various datasets, including comparison to other methods.
* Discuss the choice of inducing inputs and their impact on the quality of the bounds.
* Consider providing more guidance on the selection of inducing inputs, including the number of inducing points and their initialization/placement.
