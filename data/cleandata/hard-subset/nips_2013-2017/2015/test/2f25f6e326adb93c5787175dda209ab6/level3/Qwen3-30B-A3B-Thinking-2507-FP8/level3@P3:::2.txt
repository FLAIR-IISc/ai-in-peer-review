

# Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the intractable normalizing constant involving a determinant (for finite domains) or Fredholm determinant (for continuous domains). The authors propose novel, nonspectral bounds on these determinants that avoid the need for expensive spectral decompositions, enabling more efficient inference methods. The work builds on sparse approximations for Gaussian processes [11] but extends them to DPPs, creating a general framework for bounding determinants without spectral knowledge.

The main contribution is a pair of tight bounds on the determinant of the operator L + I (for finite domains) and the Fredholm determinant (for continuous domains), using a set of "pseudo-inputs" Z. These bounds are computationally efficient, requiring only O(nm²) time where m is the number of pseudo-inputs, compared to O(n³) for spectral methods. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be used in variational inference (yielding a lower bound that can be optimized) and Markov chain Monte Carlo (enabling retrospective sampling without spectral knowledge).

The paper makes a timely contribution to an active area of research. DPPs have gained significant traction in machine learning since their introduction in [3, 6, 7], particularly for modeling diversity in applications like recommendation systems [7], news summarization [3], and spatial point processes [4]. Previous work on DPP inference has largely relied on spectral methods [7], which become computationally prohibitive for large-scale problems. The authors' approach directly addresses this bottleneck while maintaining theoretical guarantees.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The theoretical contribution is sound and well-supported. The bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) are derived from a nontrivial determinant inequality [14], with proofs in the supplementary material. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, showing both the effectiveness of the approach and the limitations of variational methods under certain conditions (e.g., poor identifiability of parameters in the continuous Gaussian example). The comparison with spectral methods in [7] demonstrates clear computational advantages.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear motivation and logical progression from finite to continuous domains. The connection to sparse Gaussian process approximations [11] is well-explained, making the technical content accessible to the broader ML community. The experimental setup is carefully designed to validate both the theoretical claims and practical utility.

*Originality:* While the concept of using pseudo-inputs for approximation is not entirely new, the application to DPP likelihoods without spectral knowledge is novel. The bounds are general enough to apply to a wide range of DPPs, and the paper explicitly discusses how the approach could extend beyond DPPs (e.g., to other kernel-based models requiring determinant computation).

*Significance:* The problem addressed is important for the ML community working with DPPs, which have become increasingly popular for modeling repulsive phenomena. The approach enables more scalable likelihood-based inference, which is crucial for real-world applications. The authors' demonstration of parameter identifiability issues with the L-parametrization (Section 5.1) provides important insights for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

*Minor issue:* The paper emphasizes parameterizing K instead of L, but does not address this distinction in depth. The authors choose to work with L directly (as most prior work does), but the motivation for this choice is unclear given that the paper does not explore K-parameterization. The text states "we also make this choice of parametrizing L directly," but doesn't justify why this is preferable to exploring the K-parameterization that they mention as more interpretable.

*Minor issue:* The supplementary material is provided as an extended paper rather than a standard supplement. A standard supplement with additional figures, proofs, and implementation details would be more accessible to readers.

**Future work suggestion:** The authors could explore bounding the determinant directly in terms of K (rather than L), which would address the interpretability issue they mention. Additionally, assessing the scalability of the pseudo-input method for very large datasets would be valuable.

**Overall assessment:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling. The main idea is simple, well-executed, and enlightening, paving the way for further improvements in DPP inference. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses an important problem with broad relevance. The experimental evaluation convincingly demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed bounds.

The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. It is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), offers a novel approach to a well-known problem (Originality), and addresses a timely problem with significant potential impact (Significance). The minor issues noted do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

**Recommendation:** Accept
