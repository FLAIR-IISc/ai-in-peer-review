- Proposes a novel inference method for DPPs that avoids needing spectral knowledge by deriving likelihood bounds for finite and continuous domains.  
- Bounds are based on a non-trivial determinant inequality, with a clear, detailed proof.  
- Bounds are cheap to evaluate and usable within variational inference and MCMC frameworks.  
- Method draws parallels to inducing-point techniques for scalable Gaussian process inference.  
- Thorough experimental validation on synthetic and real-world data (including a diabetic neuropathy dataset) showing accurate and efficient inference.  
- Paper is well-written and technically strong, with a clear introduction to DPPs and inference challenges.  
- Main theoretical proof (Proposition 1) is placed in the appendix rather than the main text.  
- Minor presentation issues and typographical/wording errors.  
- Figure 1 lacks axis labels.  
- Computational efficiency is unclear: eigenvalue estimation is avoided but replaced by nonlinear optimization of increasing dimension.  
- Method requires increasing the inducing/cardinality, which may not guarantee convergence of the bounds.  
- Coupled, likely non-convex optimizations introduce risk of local minima that could invalidate MCMC steps and harm accuracy.  
- Paper makes a significant contribution with potential broader applicability to other models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, strong theory, clear proofs, and convincing experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear computational gains, optimization/convergence concerns, and minor presentation flaws.
