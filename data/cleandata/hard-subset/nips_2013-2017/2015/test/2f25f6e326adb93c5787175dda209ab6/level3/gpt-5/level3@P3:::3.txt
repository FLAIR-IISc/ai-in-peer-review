Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes approximate inference schemes for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid spectral decompositions. The key idea is to introduce inducing inputs Z and use determinant inequalities to bound the intractable normalizer det(I+L) in both finite and continuous settings. These nonspectral bounds yield (a) a variational lower bound for maximum-likelihood-style training and (b) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) scheme that refines the bounds adaptively by increasing m=|Z|. The approach mirrors sparse GP inducing-point methods (e.g., Titsias, AISTATS’09), but applied to DPP normalizers rather than GP likelihoods. Compared to spectral approaches for DPP inference (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’13; Bornemann, 2010 for numerical Fredholm determinants), this work avoids power iterations/eigendecompositions, thereby improving scalability, especially for continuous domains.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: clever adaptation of inducing-point variational ideas to DPP normalizers; extension to continuous DPPs with operator-level bounds.
- Practicality: bounds are cheap to evaluate (O(n m^2)) and easy to tighten by increasing m; retrospective MH is principled and flexible.
- Breadth: unified treatment of finite and continuous cases; clear links to sparse GPs.
- Empirical promise: indicative speedups and workable inference on real spatial data; retrospective MH matches ideal MH on the toy case.

Weaknesses
- Method description is sometimes rough; key quantities and assumptions (e.g., K vs L, L-ensembles, Ψ, trace-class conditions) are not always introduced early and clearly.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: few datasets, limited baselines, and incomplete runtime/accuracy analyses.
- VI appears brittle on the toy example and the paper relies on MH to rescue identifiability, but guidance on when VI should work/fail is missing.

Quality
- Technical contribution is sound and grounded in known determinant inequalities; the finite/continuous extensions are well-motivated. However, the paper would benefit from more careful derivations in the main text (not only in the supplement) to aid reproducibility.
- Claims about speed/accuracy need stronger quantitative support and broader comparisons.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but notation and background could be improved:
  - Briefly explain marginal kernel K and L-ensembles, and the relationship L = K(I−K)−1, K = L(I+L)−1, and what “marginal kernel” means operationally for DPP intensities/correlations.
  - Define matrices K and L the first time they appear in each setting; define Ψ and state the assumptions for trace and Fredholm determinants clearly.
  - The discussion of advantages of directly learning marginal kernel parameters vs L is inconsistent with the chosen approach (which parameterizes L). Consider trimming this discussion and focusing on why L-ensembles are adopted here.

Originality
- Non-spectral determinant bounds for DPP inference using inducing inputs appear new. The combination of variational and retrospective MH is a novel synthesis for DPPs.

Significance
- If empirically validated, the approach could broaden applicability of DPPs to larger finite sets and continuous domains without specialized spectral tools. The operator-level treatment is particularly useful for practitioners.

Major questions and requests for the authors
1) Complexity and wall-clock comparisons between proposed methods
- Provide per-iteration and end-to-end complexities for VI and retrospective MH as functions of n, m, T (number of realizations), and d. Include fixed costs for optimizing Z (and its frequency) and for increasing m during retrospective MH.
- Report wall-clock times vs held-out log likelihood (or exact/ideal MH where available) for both methods on the toy and real datasets.

2) Comparisons to approximate and exact alternatives
- Add baselines: (a) spectral MH with power iterations (Affandi et al., ICML’13), (b) Nyström-based approximations (Affandi et al., AISTATS’13), (c) exact small-n eigendecomposition where feasible, and (d) learning baselines such as EM (Gillenwater et al., NIPS’14) and fixed-point training (Mariet & Sra, NIPS’15). Report approximation quality (likelihood gap, parameter error) and runtime.

3) When does VI work or fail?
- The toy experiment shows large parameter error for VI and reliance on MH. Please provide guidance on VI failure modes: e.g., weak identifiability of κ when parameterizing L, sensitivity to m, local optima in Z, and mismatch between bound tightness and likelihood curvature. Can you characterize bound tightness as m increases (e.g., via the trace term or a computable gap)? Offer practical criteria to detect when VI is trustworthy (stability across restarts; small upper–lower bound gap; calibration on synthetic data).

4) Inducing inputs equal to sampled points in the toy example
- Evaluate performance when Z is set to the observed realization (finite case: Z=Y; continuous case: project to nearest grid); discuss whether the bounds tighten materially and how this compares to optimized Z.

5) Rule-of-thumb guidance
- Number of inducing points m: propose heuristics such as m proportional to expected cardinality of realizations or to covering numbers of the base measure; increase m until the upper–lower bound gap and the VI objective stabilize.
- Initialization/placement of Z: k-means++ on pooled points; farthest-point or max-min diversity seeding under µ; or sampling from the approximate marginal intensity; then refine via gradient steps.

Minor comments
- Early, define K (marginal kernel governing inclusion probabilities/correlations) and L (L-ensemble kernel used in likelihood with det(LY)/det(I+L)). Provide a 2–3 sentence primer to orient readers unfamiliar with DPP parametrizations.
- Reduce or clarify the discussion that seems to advocate directly learning K’s parameters; since the paper parameterizes L, keep the focus on L-ensembles and briefly note interpretability trade-offs.

Pros
- Novel, elegant nonspectral bounds; applicable to finite and continuous DPPs.
- Practical algorithms (VI and MH) with clear links to sparse GPs.
- Promising empirical speedups; continuous-domain applicability.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation is not yet sufficient: few datasets, limited baselines, missing wall-clock vs accuracy trade-offs.
- Method description and notation could be clearer; background definitions are missing or late.
- Limited guidance on when VI is reliable and how to choose/initialize inducing inputs.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is timely and potentially impactful, but the paper needs clearer exposition and stronger empirical evaluation (comparisons, runtimes, and guidance). If accepted, please (a) improve clarity of the method description and background, and (b) substantially strengthen experiments with the requested comparisons and practical guidance.
