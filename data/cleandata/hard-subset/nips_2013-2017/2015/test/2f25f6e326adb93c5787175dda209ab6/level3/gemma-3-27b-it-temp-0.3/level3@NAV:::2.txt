## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of performing likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), circumventing the typical bottleneck of spectral decomposition. The authors propose novel upper and lower bounds on the DPP likelihood, applicable to both finite and continuous domains, which avoid explicit eigenvalue computations. This is a significant contribution, as it opens the door to more scalable inference methods for DPPs.

The core idea is elegantly simple – leveraging bounds inspired by Gaussian process variational inference and pseudo-inputs to approximate the determinant – and exceptionally well-executed. The theoretical derivations appear sound, and the extension to continuous DPPs demonstrates the generality of the approach. The paper is timely, as DPPs are gaining traction in various machine learning applications where efficient inference is crucial. The choice to focus on parametric inference, directly parameterizing the kernel L rather than K, is a pragmatic one, acknowledged as a trade-off between tractability and interpretability. 

The experimental evaluation is well-constructed, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed bounds in both a synthetic Gaussian setting and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset. The comparison with existing methods, particularly highlighting the failures of variational approximations under certain conditions, is valuable. The results demonstrate that the proposed MCMC method can achieve accurate parameter estimation, while the variational approach provides a computationally efficient alternative. 

However, the paper doesn’t delve deeply into *why* parameterizing L is emphasized, despite acknowledging the interpretability loss. A more thorough discussion of this design choice would be beneficial. Additionally, while the extended paper version is provided as a supplement, a standard supplement document summarizing key proofs and additional details would improve accessibility. 

Future work suggestions, such as directly bounding the determinant in terms of K and assessing the scalability of the pseudo-input method for very large datasets, are pertinent and would further strengthen the contribution. Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant potential impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Strong experimental validation.
*   Potential for broad applicability beyond DPPs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion on the choice of parameterizing L.
*   Supplement format could be improved.



**Overall Score:** Strong Accept. This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.
