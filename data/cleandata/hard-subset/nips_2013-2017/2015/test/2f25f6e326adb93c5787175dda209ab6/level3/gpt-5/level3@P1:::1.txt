This paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decompositions. The authors parametrize the L-kernel directly (common in ML practice), thereby avoiding the constraints tied to the K-kernel’s spectrum, and target the main computational bottleneck: the DPP normalizing constant, which is a large determinant in the finite case and a Fredholm determinant in the continuous case. The core contribution is a pair of nonspectral upper/lower bounds on det(I + L) in the finite and continuous settings, constructed via an inducing-point approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY inspired by sparse GP methods. These bounds are cheap to evaluate (O(n m^2) for m inducing points) and do not require eigenvalue computations, enabling (i) a variational lower bound for fast approximate learning and (ii) retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) that is exact in the limit while avoiding spectral estimates used in prior work.

Relation to prior work: Previous ML approaches to DPP learning either parametrize L and rely on eigendecompositions or partial spectra for normalization (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’14; Gillenwater et al., NIPS’14; Mariet & Sra, NIPS’15), or exploit Nyström-type approximations. Affandi et al. used spectral bounds and retrospective MH based on leading eigenpairs estimated by power iteration, which is memory- and time-intensive for large n and restricted to discrete domains. The present paper replaces spectral bounds with inducing-point bounds reminiscent of Titsias’ variational inducing-variable framework for GPs (AISTATS’09), and extends them to continuous DPPs with Fredholm determinants, connecting to operator inequalities (Seiler & Simon’75) and to quadrature-based Fredholm determinant methods (Bornemann’10). The combination of DPPs with inducing-point bounds and the extension to continuous domains is, to my knowledge, novel.

Quality: The technical development centers on two propositions bounding det(I + L) via det(I + Q) and a trace correction term, both for finite and continuous domains. The construction is mathematically sound and leverages well-established determinant inequalities. The use of these bounds in a variational ELBO and in retrospective MH is standard and correctly executed. Experiments are sensible: (1) a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP where exact spectra and determinants are available confirms that retrospective MH with the new bounds matches “ideal” MH; (2) the diabetic neuropathy data reproduce known class differences in overdispersion and show convergence of estimates as m increases. The authors are transparent about identifiability issues when parametrizing L (e.g., κ) and about the limitations of the variational approach in that toy setting.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The connection to sparse GP inducing-point methods is clearly drawn, aiding intuition. Some presentation issues remain (noted below), but overall the manuscript is readable and informative. Complexity claims and the role of Z are described, and the experimental sections connect the theory to practice.

Originality: Replacing spectral approximations with inducing-point determinant bounds for DPP inference, and proving companion bounds for continuous DPPs, are original and valuable contributions. The link to GP variational methods opens methodological cross-fertilization.

Significance: These bounds make DPP learning accessible in settings where eigendecompositions are impractical or unavailable (large discrete domains; continuous domains without closed-form spectra). The MCMC scheme is more broadly applicable than prior spectral-MH approaches, and the variational objective provides a practical training route with tunable fidelity via m. This is likely to be useful for spatial statistics, summarization, and diversity modeling.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: nonspectral, inducing-point bounds for DPP normalization (finite and continuous) with clear ties to variational GP methods.
- Practicality: O(n m^2) evaluation; no eigen-computations; applicable to continuous domains.
- Soundness: bounds derived from solid determinant inequalities; correct integration into variational and MH frameworks.
- Validation: retrospective MH matches ground truth in a case with known spectra; real-data study replicates known phenomena; convergence of bounds/estimates with m shown.
- Clear discussion of the L vs K parametrization tradeoff and identifiability.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Computational win not fully established: replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization over growing sets of inducing points may not always be faster. Please add wall-clock comparisons against spectral methods (e.g., power iteration or Lanczos) on finite problems as n and target accuracy vary.
- Monotonic refinement guarantees: the retrospective MH relies on refining bounds by increasing m. This is guaranteed if Z is nested; with re-optimized Z, monotonic tightening is not ensured. Recommend (i) using nested Z to guarantee upper/lower bounds tighten monotonically, (ii) providing a proposition showing monotonicity under PSD order (Q increases; tr(L−Q) decreases) when augmenting Z, and (iii) clarifying a fallback if optimization stalls in local minima.
- Variational optimization: the ELBO is nonconvex in Z and θ; provide practical guidance on initializing Z, choosing m, and detecting pathological runs (e.g., large variability across restarts), and consider reporting multiple restarts or a heuristic for annealing Z.
- Theory scope: while the determinant bounds are solid, there is no explicit rate showing how quickly the bounds converge with m for common kernels/measures. Even a qualitative discussion (e.g., in terms of GP posterior variance integrals) would strengthen the claims.
- Experiments: include sensitivity of MH acceptance and runtime to m, and mixing diagnostics; add an ablation comparing fixed Z vs optimized Z.
- Presentation/typos: add axis labels to figures; consider moving the proof sketch of Proposition 1 (or at least key steps and intuition) to the main text; fix minor wording/typos (e.g., “the variational lower is ...”).

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled bounds that remove a key bottleneck in DPP inference, with extensions to continuous domains.
- Solid theoretical basis and practical algorithms (variational + exact MH) with demonstrated correctness.
- Bridges DPPs and variational GP methodology, likely to influence follow-on work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical evidence of superior efficiency vs spectral approaches; lack of comprehensive runtime/accuracy benchmarking.
- No formal monotonic refinement guarantee when Z is re-optimized; potential practical fragility in retrospective MH if local optima impede tightening.
- Variational method can be unstable on some parameters (e.g., κ), with limited guidance on mitigation.

Overall evaluation: A clever and technically sound contribution that broadens the toolbox for DPP inference. With stronger empirical comparisons and clarified refinement guarantees, the paper would make a compelling case. I lean toward acceptance.
