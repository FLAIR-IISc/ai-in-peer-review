- Paper proposes nonspectral upper and lower bounds on DPP likelihoods (finite and continuous) using inducing inputs Z and a low-rank surrogate Q = L_YZ L_Z^{-1} L_ZY to avoid eigenvalue/Fredholm determinant computation.  
- Two inference routes enabled: (i) a variational lower bound optimized jointly over kernel parameters and Z; (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) scheme that tightens acceptance bounds by increasing m = |Z|.  
- Continuous-case extension mirrors finite case and uses Ψ = ∫ L(z_i,x)L(x,z_j) dμ(x) with explicit expressions when Ψ is computable (e.g., Gaussian kernels/measures).  
- Experiments: sanity-check 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP for MCMC, and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset with class-specific variational DPPs and overdispersion comparisons.  
- Relation to prior work: departs from spectral methods (top eigenvalues, Nyström, EM/fixed-point) by avoiding eigen-computations via inducing-point determinant bounds inspired by sparse GP variational bounds and determinant inequalities.  
- Retrospective MH adaptation is incremental relative to prior spectral-bound uses but novel in its nonspectral instantiation and continuous Fredholm extension.  
- Technical quality: core idea is sound and parallels well-understood sparse GP constructions; determinant bounds are plausible given cited inequalities.  
- Correctness caveat: retrospective MH is valid in principle only if bounds can be tightened arbitrarily by increasing m and optimizing Z.  
- Empirical limitations: experiments are limited and do not answer practical tradeoff questions, weakening the paper’s practical guidance.  
- Variational inference failed on the toy example and this failure is acknowledged but not analyzed, reducing confidence in VI’s practical applicability.  
- Clarity issues: exposition is compact but sometimes rough, with missing definitions (e.g., K vs L-ensemble), under-specified algorithmic details, complexity, and continuous-case assumptions for computing Ψ.  
- Missing practical details: optimization of Z, computational complexity and memory scaling, and when VI should be expected to succeed are not adequately described.  
- Originality: inducing-input nonspectral determinant bounds for finite and continuous DPPs and computable Ψ for Gaussian settings appear novel.  
- Significance: potentially important for removing spectral computation bottlenecks and scaling DPP inference, but currently limited by modest empirical validation and lack of guidance on m/Z selection.  
- Major requested revisions: provide detailed complexity/runtime comparisons (variational vs retrospective MH) across m, n, d and memory; clarify cost of optimizing Z.  
- Major requested revisions: add baselines comparing to spectral truncations, EM/fixed-point methods, and exact small-scale MLE where feasible.  
- Major requested revisions: analyze VI failure modes and regimes where VI succeeds or fails (repulsiveness, identifiability, sensitivity to m), and provide diagnostics.  
- Major requested revisions: evaluate inducing-input choices (Z = observed items, current samples, optimized, k-means, grids) and report bound tightness and accuracy.  
- Major requested revisions: offer heuristics/rules of thumb for choosing m and initializing Z, and practical strategies (e.g., increase m until bound gap stabilizes).  
- Major requested revisions: clarify retrospective MH guarantees, conditions for eventual acceptance/rejection as m increases, distribution of m per iteration, and overhead vs spectral MH.  
- Major requested revisions: specify continuous-case assumptions beyond Gaussian–Gaussian for tractable Ψ, discuss numerical stability, quadrature, and scalability in higher dimensions.  
- Minor requests: define K and L-ensemble for readers, reconcile discussion of learning K vs L parametrizations, and condense that narrative.  
- Minor requests: provide pseudo-code for VI and retrospective MH including Z-optimization and stopping rules, and report bound gaps during training/inference as diagnostics.  
- Minor requests: clarify determinant inequality conditions (PSD, trace-class) and summarize continuous-section assumptions and checks.  
- Pros: conceptually simple nonspectral bounds for finite and continuous DPPs, clear connection to sparse GP methodology, O(n m^2) computations, retrospective MH removes eigen-computations, and promising empirical signals.  
- Cons: limited empirical evaluation and baselines, insufficient guidance on m/Z selection and initialization, unexplained VI failure on toy example, and missing methodological details.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject — promising idea but needs stronger experiments, clearer algorithms, and concrete heuristics for m and Z to be suitable for conference acceptance.
