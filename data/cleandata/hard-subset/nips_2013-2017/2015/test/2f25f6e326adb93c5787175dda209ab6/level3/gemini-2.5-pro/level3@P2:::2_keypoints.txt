- Addresses scalable, likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), focusing on the intractable determinant normalizer.  
- Main contribution: novel non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the determinant using pseudo-inputs (inducing variables) for low-rank kernel approximations.  
- Bounds apply to both finite and continuous DPPs and avoid expensive spectrum estimation.  
- Provides two inference integrations: a fast variational approximation and an exact Metropolis-Hastings with retrospective sampling.  
- Theoretical derivation is rigorous, based on a known determinant inequality, and covers finite and continuous cases.  
- Experiments are carefully designed; toy 1D experiment validates MCMC against ground truth and real data (diabetic neuropathy) shows variational convergence with more pseudo-inputs.  
- Authors honestly report limitations: variational method can fail on flat likelihood surfaces, motivating the MCMC alternative.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized; introducing finite case first aids readability and the GP connection is helpful.  
- Minor clarity issue: emphasizes K vs L parametrization problem but proceeds by parametrizing L, which may confuse readers without reframing as a deliberate trade-off.  
- Core idea is highly original—applying inducing-point-style bounds to DPP determinants departs from prior spectral methods.  
- Significant impact: makes DPP inference more scalable and offers a practical speed–accuracy trade-off for practitioners.  
- Method is potentially generalizable to other kernel-based problems involving large determinants.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound, more efficient than spectral methods, and well-validated experimentally.  
- Argument against acceptance (minor): does not resolve the K vs L parametrization issue highlighted in the introduction.  
- Additional suggestions: provide a standard separate supplement, explore bounding determinants in terms of K (not just L), and assess scalability of optimizing pseudo-inputs as m grows.
