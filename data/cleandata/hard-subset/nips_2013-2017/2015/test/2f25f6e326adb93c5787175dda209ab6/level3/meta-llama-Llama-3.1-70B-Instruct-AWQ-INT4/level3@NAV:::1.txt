This paper proposes a novel approach to inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral knowledge. The authors derive bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that can be used for variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The bounds are based on a non-trivial inequality on determinants and do not rely on approximating the spectrum of a large matrix or an operator.

The paper is technically strong and well-written, with a clear and concise presentation of the main ideas and contributions. The authors provide a thorough review of the existing literature on DPPs and their applications, and they clearly explain the limitations of current methods and the need for new approaches.

The proposed bounds are a significant contribution to the field, as they provide a way to perform likelihood-based inference for DPPs without requiring spectral knowledge. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on both synthetic and real-world data.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to draw connections between DPPs and other models, such as Gaussian processes. The authors show that their bounds can be used to perform variational inference and MCMC methods for DPPs, and they provide a clear explanation of how these methods can be implemented.

The paper also has some limitations. One concern is that replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization of increasing dimension may not be a clear computational win. Additionally, increasing the inducing/cardinality may not guarantee convergence of bounds due to coupled, likely non-convex optimizations, and local minima could prevent valid MCMC steps.

In terms of presentation, the paper could benefit from some minor improvements. For example, Figure 1 lacks axis labels, and the main proof (Proposition 1) could be included in the main text rather than the appendix.

Overall, this paper is a significant contribution to the field of machine learning and statistics, and it has the potential to impact a wide range of applications. The authors' approach to inference for DPPs is novel and effective, and their paper provides a clear and concise presentation of their ideas and contributions.
