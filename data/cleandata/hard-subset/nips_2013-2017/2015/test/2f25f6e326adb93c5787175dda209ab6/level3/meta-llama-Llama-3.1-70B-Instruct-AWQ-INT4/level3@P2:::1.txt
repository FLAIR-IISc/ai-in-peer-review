This paper introduces a novel approach to inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral knowledge. DPPs are point process models that encode diversity between points in a realization through a positive definite kernel. However, learning the parameters of the kernel through likelihood-based inference is challenging due to the intractable normalization constant. The authors address this issue by deriving bounds on the likelihood of a DPP, both for finite and continuous domains, that do not rely on approximating the spectrum of a large matrix or an operator.

The paper is well-written and technically strong, with a clear and concise presentation of the main ideas. The authors provide a thorough introduction to DPPs, their properties, and the challenges associated with likelihood-based inference. They then present their main contribution: bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that can be used for variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods.

The bounds proposed in the paper are based on a non-trivial inequality on determinants, and the authors provide a clear and detailed proof of this inequality. The bounds are also shown to be cheap to evaluate, making them a promising approach for inference in DPPs.

One of the strengths of the paper is its connection to inducing-point techniques for scalable Gaussian process inference. The authors draw useful parallels between their approach and these techniques, highlighting the potential for their method to be applied more broadly.

The experimental validation of the proposed method is also thorough and convincing. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both synthetic and real-world data, including a diabetic neuropathy dataset. The results show that the proposed method can provide accurate and efficient inference for DPPs.

However, there are some concerns regarding the computational efficiency of the proposed method. While the authors argue that their approach avoids the need for eigenvalue estimation, which can be computationally expensive, they replace it with nonlinear optimization of increasing dimension. This may not necessarily be a clear computational win, especially for large datasets.

Additionally, the authors' approach requires increasing the inducing/cardinality, which may not guarantee convergence of the bounds due to coupled, likely non-convex optimizations. Local minima could prevent valid MCMC steps, which could affect the accuracy of the inference results.

In terms of presentation, there are some minor issues, such as the lack of axis labels in Figure 1 and the placement of the main proof (Proposition 1) in the appendix rather than the main text. There are also some minor wording and typographical errors.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of DPPs and inference. The authors' approach is novel, well-motivated, and thoroughly validated, and it has the potential to be applied more broadly to other model classes.
