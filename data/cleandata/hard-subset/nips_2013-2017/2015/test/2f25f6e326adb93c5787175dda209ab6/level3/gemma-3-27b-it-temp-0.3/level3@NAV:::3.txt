## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of performing likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without relying on computationally expensive spectral decompositions. The authors propose a novel approach using inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood, leading to both variational inference and approximate MCMC methods. This is a valuable contribution as spectral decomposition is a major bottleneck in applying DPPs to large-scale problems. The work builds upon existing literature in DPPs, Gaussian processes, and variational inference, and clearly articulates the limitations of current methods.

The core idea of bounding the likelihood using inducing inputs is promising and well-motivated. The connection to sparse Gaussian process approximations is clearly drawn, providing intuition for the proposed method. The theoretical development of the bounds, both for finite and continuous domains, appears sound, although the proofs are relegated to supplementary material. The presentation of both variational and MCMC inference schemes leveraging these bounds is a strength, offering practitioners flexibility in their approach. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The method description, particularly regarding the optimization of inducing inputs, feels somewhat rough and lacks sufficient detail. A clearer explanation of how the inducing inputs are updated within the variational and MCMC frameworks would be beneficial. Critically, the empirical evaluation is insufficient. While a toy example and a real dataset are used, the comparison to other approximate and exact inference methods (e.g., those relying on power iteration) is missing. This makes it difficult to assess the practical benefits of the proposed approach in terms of both runtime and approximation quality. The variational inference results on the toy data exhibit large parameter estimation errors, and the reliance on approximate MCMC to “rescue” the variational approach raises concerns about its reliability. The authors need to clarify when variational inference is expected to work well and why it fails in certain scenarios. 

Several specific questions remain unanswered. How do the methods perform when the inducing inputs are initialized to be the actual sampled points? What rule-of-thumb guidance can be provided for selecting the number of inducing points and their initial placement? The introduction of matrices K and L without initial definition hinders readability for those less familiar with the field. Finally, the discussion regarding directly learning the marginal kernel parameters feels somewhat tangential and could be streamlined.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper tackles an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. The proposed method has the potential to significantly improve the scalability of DPP inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The method description lacks clarity, the empirical evaluation is insufficient, and key questions regarding the practical application of the method remain unanswered. 

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors should significantly improve the clarity of the method description, strengthen the empirical evaluation with comprehensive comparisons, and address the outstanding questions raised in this review. If these improvements are made, the paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field.
