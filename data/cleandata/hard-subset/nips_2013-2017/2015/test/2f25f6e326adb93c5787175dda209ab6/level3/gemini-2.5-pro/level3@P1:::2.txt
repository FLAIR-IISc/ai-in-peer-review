This paper addresses the significant challenge of performing likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The primary bottleneck for inference is the computation of the normalizing constant, which involves the determinant of a large matrix `(L + I)` in the finite case, or a Fredholm determinant in the continuous case. Existing methods often rely on spectral approximations, which can be computationally expensive and are typically limited to finite domains.

The main contribution of this work is the introduction of novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds for this determinant. Drawing inspiration from variational methods for sparse Gaussian processes, the authors use a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" to construct a low-rank approximation to the kernel matrix `L`. This approximation is then used to bound the intractable determinant. These bounds are computationally efficient, scaling as O(nm^2) instead of O(n^3), and are applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs. The paper then demonstrates how these bounds can be effectively integrated into two standard inference paradigms: a fast but approximate variational inference (VI) scheme based on maximizing the lower bound, and a more computationally intensive but asymptotically exact MCMC method using retrospective sampling.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-executed piece of work. The theoretical contribution—the bounds themselves—is derived from established matrix inequalities. The claims are well-supported by a thoughtful set of experiments. A particular strength is the honesty and care with which the authors evaluate their methods. For instance, in the toy 1D experiment, they demonstrate a scenario where the VI approach fails to identify a parameter (κ), while the MCMC approach succeeds. This provides valuable insight into the method's limitations and the trade-offs between the two inference schemes. The experiments on the real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset successfully demonstrate the practical utility of the variational approach, showing that the bounds tighten and parameter estimates converge as the number of pseudo-inputs increases.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The decision to introduce the concepts first for the simpler finite case before extending them to the more technical continuous case is effective and makes the core ideas easy to follow. The connection to sparse Gaussian processes is enlightening and helps to ground the work in a familiar context for many machine learning researchers. The notation is consistent and the derivations are clearly explained.

**Originality:** The proposed approach is highly original. While the use of inducing points is well-known in the GP literature, its application to derive non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihoods is novel and creative. This work provides a new and valuable perspective on DPP inference, moving away from the dominant paradigm of spectral approximation. It clearly differentiates itself from prior work (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014) by avoiding the need for iterative eigenvalue solvers. The paper adequately references related work in both the DPP and GP communities.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. DPPs are an increasingly popular tool for modeling diversity, but their practical application has been hampered by the computational cost of inference. By providing a significantly cheaper, non-spectral inference method, this paper lowers the barrier to entry for using DPPs on larger-scale problems. The approach is general, and the core idea of using pseudo-inputs to bound functionals of positive definite kernels may find applications beyond DPPs. The work successfully advances the state of the art in DPP inference in a demonstrable way.

**Weaknesses:** The weaknesses of the paper are minor.
1.  The introduction emphasizes the benefit of parameterizing the `K` kernel for interpretability (e.g., decoupling the expected number of points from the repulsive lengthscale). However, the paper then follows the standard practice of parameterizing the `L` kernel for tractability, leaving the initial motivation partially unaddressed. While this is a pragmatic choice, a brief discussion of the challenges of applying the proposed bounds directly to a model parameterized by `K` would be welcome.
2.  A minor suggestion on presentation: providing a standard, self-contained supplement in addition to an extended version of the paper would be more convenient for reviewers and future readers.

### Arguments for Acceptance
**Pro:**
*   Addresses a well-known and significant bottleneck in inference for DPPs.
*   The core contribution—the non-spectral bounds—is simple, elegant, and novel.
*   The connection established between sparse GPs and DPP inference is insightful and opens up new avenues for research.
*   The proposed inference methods are demonstrated to be effective on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides an honest assessment of the method's strengths and weaknesses.
*   The work is of high significance and is likely to be widely used and built upon by researchers working with DPPs.

**Con:**
*   The paper does not resolve the interpretability issue of parameterizing the `L` kernel versus the `K` kernel, which it raises in its motivation.

### Additional Comments
This is an excellent paper that presents a clever and practical solution to an important problem. For future work, it would be interesting to explore strategies for adapting the number of pseudo-inputs `m` automatically. Furthermore, investigating whether these bounding techniques could be extended to work directly with the `K` kernel would be a very valuable contribution, fully realizing the motivation outlined in the introduction.
