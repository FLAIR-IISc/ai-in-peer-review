- Addresses the important problem of avoiding costly spectral decompositions for DPP likelihood evaluation to enable scalable inference.  
- Proposes novel bounds using inducing inputs to approximate the kernel, applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs.  
- Applies the bounds to both variational inference and MCMC methods, aiming to enable tractable inference without spectral methods.  
- Theoretical justification is provided using determinant inequalities and sparse Gaussian process–inspired techniques.  
- Demonstrated on a toy continuous Gaussian DPP and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset, with comparison to prior work (Affandi et al., 2013).  
- Novelty: using inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood is a meaningful and original contribution.  
- Significance: could substantially broaden DPP applicability if computational and empirical claims hold.  
- Method description is insufficiently clear; connections between the bounds and optimization/MCMC steps are rough and need an explicit algorithm.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks rigorous runtime (wall-clock) and complexity comparisons to existing methods, including power-iteration approaches.  
- Variational inference appears unstable on the toy data with large parameter errors; the paper does not explain causes or failure conditions.  
- Reliance on approximate MCMC to compensate for variational failures raises concerns about overall robustness.  
- No clear guidance on selecting the number or placement of inducing points; their impact on performance is not thoroughly investigated.  
- The paper introduces K and L matrices without adequate definitions for readers unfamiliar with DPP conventions.  
- Inconsistency: the discussion favoring direct learning of K parameters conflicts with the paper’s focus on parameterizing L.  
- Quality: technically sound approach but incomplete experimental rigor and description make the work feel below NIPS standard.  
- Clarity: generally well written, but mathematical notation and method exposition need clearer explanations and algorithmic detail.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a hard problem, offers a theoretically sound and novel approach, and shows preliminary synthetic and real-data results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear method details, insufficient empirical (especially runtime) evaluation, variational instability, and no inducing-point selection guidance.  
- Recommendation: weak reject, contingent on authors addressing clarity, comprehensive runtime/accuracy comparisons (especially to Affandi et al.), and analysis of variational reliability.
