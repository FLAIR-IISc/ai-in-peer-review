Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles a central bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the intractable normalizing constant, which is a determinant for finite domains and a Fredholm determinant for continuous domains. The authors propose simple, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on these determinants that avoid computing eigenvalues/eigenfunctions. The key idea is to build a low-rank approximation Q to L using pseudo-inputs (inducing inputs) Z, and then use determinant inequalities to bound det(I+L) in terms of det(I+Q) and a trace correction, extending analogous ideas from sparse Gaussian processes (GPs) with inducing variables (Titsias, AISTATS 2009). They provide companion bounds for continuous domains that involve only small Gram matrices on Z and kernel integrals Ψ that are tractable for common kernels and base measures (e.g., Gaussian-Gaussian). These bounds enable both a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood and a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme with provably correct accept/reject decisions by refining Z.

Relative to prior work, the approach contrasts with spectral methods for DPP inference that rely on leading eigenpairs (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2013; Gillenwater et al., NIPS; Mariet and Sra, NIPS), Nyström approximations for L, and Fredholm determinant quadrature (Bornemann, 2010). The proposed bounds are eigen-free, work for both finite and continuous DPPs, and leverage the inducing-point machinery from GPs in a novel way to bound DPP likelihoods.

Strengths:
- Novel, clean determinant bounds that are easy to evaluate and broadly applicable. The idea—combine a pseudo-input low-rank surrogate with a determinant inequality—is simple, well executed, and illuminating.
- Generality: the method applies to finite and continuous settings without spectral decompositions and is not restricted to DPPs in principle; any positive definite kernel functional involving det(I+L) may benefit.
- Creative and effective use of pseudo-inputs to bound functionals of PD kernels; the link to sparse GP variational objectives is insightful.
- Solid experimental design illustrating both benefits and limitations. The continuous 1D Gaussian example validates the MCMC against ground truth; the neuropathy dataset shows meaningful conclusions and good convergence as m increases. The paper candidly shows cases where the variational approximation struggles (e.g., unidentifiable κ when parameterizing L), which increases credibility.
- Timely topic with likely community interest, given the surge of DPP usage in ML and spatial statistics.
- Chosen application area is relevant and interesting, and the empirical behavior of pseudo-input locations is informative.

Weaknesses and points for clarification:
- While the bounds are tightenable with larger m and optimized Z, guidance on tightness versus m is limited. Some quantitative analysis (or plots) of bound gaps versus m and Z quality would help practitioners set m.
- For continuous DPPs, computing Ψ requires kernel–measure integrals. Beyond Gaussian-Gaussian, how broadly is Ψ available in closed form? A discussion of numerical quadrature costs and stability would clarify scalability.
- The paper emphasizes parameterizing K versus L in the introduction, but the rest of the work operates on L. If K-parameterization is not addressed later, the emphasis could be toned down or clarified.
- The method requires optimizing Z; details on optimization stability, initialization strategies, and computational overhead (per iteration complexity, conditioning of LZ) would be valuable, especially at large m.
- Baselines: more direct comparisons with spectral truncation and Nyström-based bounds (e.g., Affandi et al.) would strengthen the empirical case for the proposed approach across accuracy and runtime regimes.
- Supplement format: providing a standard supplementary document (proofs, derivations, additional experiments) instead of pointing to an “extended version” would aid reproducibility for reviewers.

Quality:
- Technically sound with clear propositions and proofs (as indicated). The experimental evidence is appropriate and honest, including failure modes of the variational method when parameters are weakly identifiable. The retrospective MH construction is standard and correctly adapted to these bounds.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized. The finite case builds intuition before the continuous case. Suggested improvements: add guidance on selecting m, initialization of Z, and a brief practical recipe for computing Ψ for common kernels; reduce early emphasis on K unless addressed later.

Originality:
- The determinant bounds and their deployment for DPP likelihoods without spectra are original and nontrivial. The import of inducing-point ideas from sparse GPs to bound DPP determinants is creative and likely to inspire follow-on work.

Significance:
- High. The approach removes a key barrier to scalable inference for DPPs and could transfer to other kernel models involving det(I+L). The MCMC scheme is practical; the variational bound is useful for fast approximate training and model selection.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, general, and insightful bounds; broad applicability.
- Eliminates spectral requirements in both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Clear empirical demonstrations, including realistic data.
- Likely to catalyze further work on kernel determinant bounds and non-spectral DPP inference.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited quantitative analysis of bound tightness versus m and Z.
- Sparse comparison to spectral/Nyström baselines on accuracy-speed tradeoffs.
- Some practical details (Ψ computation breadth, Z optimization guidance) could be expanded.

Suggestions and future work:
- Analyze and visualize bound gaps as a function of m and Z selection; study automatic selection or adaptive growth of Z.
- Explore direct determinant bounds parameterized in terms of K (rather than L), to connect back to interpretable modeling; quantify when such bounds are competitive.
- Assess scalability of the pseudo-input approach on larger n and in higher-dimensional continuous spaces, including numerical stability of LZ and Ψ.
- Compare with quadrature-based Fredholm determinant methods and hybrid approaches that mix a few leading eigenpairs with pseudo-input bounds.
- Provide a standard supplement with full proofs, additional experiments, and implementation details; consider releasing code.

Minor comments:
- Clarify why K-parameterization is foregrounded if the paper ultimately uses L throughout; optionally move that discussion to the conclusion.
- Recommend providing a standard supplementary PDF rather than only an extended version.
