Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles a core obstacle in likelihood-based learning for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the intractable normalization, which is a large matrix determinant in the finite case and a Fredholm determinant in the continuous case. Prior work (e.g., Affandi et al. [7]) sidestepped this by estimating the leading spectrum of L to form incremental upper/lower bounds, enabling provably-correct retrospective MCMC for kernel parameters. This paper proposes an alternative family of bounds that avoid spectrum estimation entirely. The bounds are derived by introducing inducing points Z and a low-rank surrogate Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, yielding cheap-to-evaluate upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) and their continuous analog via LZ and Ψ. These bounds support both variational learning (a tractable lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood) and retrospective MCMC (by refining the bounds as needed), and draw a tight connection to inducing-point methods for scalable Gaussian processes (GPs), especially the Titsias variational construction.

On the empirical side, the authors validate both the variational and MCMC approaches. In a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian setting where the spectrum is known, retrospective MCMC using the proposed bounds matches an “ideal” sampler that uses exact determinants. On a spatial neuropathy dataset, variational learning yields coherent, convergent overdispersion estimates across a range of inducing-point budgets, recovering the class differences reported in [7].

Strengths:
- Technical novelty and elegance: Proposition 1 is a key insight—an analytically simple, nonspectral lower-bound inequality that parallels variational sparse GP bounds. Extending the construction to continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinants is a strong contribution.
- Practical impact: The bounds are inexpensive to evaluate (O(n m^2)) and immediately usable for both variational learning and provably-correct retrospective MCMC without any eigenvalue computations.
- Conceptual connections: The inducing-point view unifies DPP inference with scalable GP methodology, which can spur cross-fertilization (e.g., initialization, optimization schedules, amortization strategies).
- Writing quality: The paper is generally clear, technically careful, and well organized.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Computational tradeoffs: While avoiding power iterations is attractive, replacing them with nonlinear optimization over Z (and θ) of increasing dimension is not necessarily a computational win. The overall wall-clock cost (including repeated re-optimization during retrospective MCMC) versus spectral methods remains unclear; a head-to-head comparison would strengthen claims.
- Convergence of bound refinement: The bounds depend on both m and optimized Z. Because the joint problems in Z, Z′ are coupled and nonconvex, increasing m does not guarantee monotonic tightening in practice; local minima could prevent the bounds from narrowing sufficiently to make MH accept/reject decisions, jeopardizing practicality of retrospective steps.
- Scope of experiments: Results are promising but limited; more systematic studies on finite-domain DPPs (where [7] typically operates), and broader scalability tests would help establish general utility.

Quality:
- Theoretical results are sound (Propositions 1–2 rely on a nontrivial determinant inequality and are carefully instantiated for finite and continuous cases).
- The MCMC construction follows established retrospective sampling logic; the nonspectral bounds integrate cleanly.
- Empirics support correctness and provide an indicative view of cost/accuracy, but more comprehensive runtime and robustness analyses are needed to fully support the claimed efficiency gains.

Clarity:
- Overall clear and well written. The link to sparse GP variational bounds is nicely developed.
- Presentation issues to fix:
  - Figure 1 lacks axis labels.
  - The main technical contribution (Proposition 1) is central; the proof should appear in the main text with intuition, relegating only technical details to the appendix.
  - Minor typo/wording: e.g., “the variational lower is …” should read “variational lower bound”.
  - Consider adding pseudocode for the retrospective MH procedure with bound refinement and optimization steps.

Originality:
- New nonspectral determinant bounds tailored to DPP likelihoods, and their deployment for both finite and continuous settings, is original.
- The GP-inducing connection is insightful and broadens the methodological framing.

Significance:
- DPPs are widely used in ML and statistics; reducing the reliance on spectral decompositions can materially broaden applicability (especially continuous domains).
- The variational route provides a scalable, general tool; the retrospective MCMC route keeps a path to exactness.
- The GP connection may catalyze further advances and adoption.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide head-to-head comparisons with [7] (and other spectral approaches) on both finite and continuous tasks: wall-clock time, memory, accuracy, and acceptance rates for MCMC.
- Analyze the refinement behavior: distributions of m required per MH iteration; sensitivity to Z initialization; compare optimizing Z vs. fixed Z (e.g., k-means/Nyström/DPP-initialized) to quantify the value and cost of optimization.
- Discuss and, if possible, give conditions under which the bounds converge as m→∞, and whether monotonicity can be enforced (e.g., via nested Z and warm starts) to stabilize retrospective decisions.
- Report ablations on optimizer choice, step budgets, and stochastic/mini-batch variants.
- Consider an additional discrete-domain application (e.g., document summarization or subset selection) to demonstrate broad utility.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, clean theoretical contribution (Proposition 1), extended to continuous DPPs.
- Eliminates spectral reliance; broadly useful in practice.
- Enables both variational and MCMC inference.
- Clear connection to influential GP methodology; likely to impact multiple communities.
- Well written.

Cons for acceptance:
- Unclear computational advantage in practice due to nonconvex optimization over growing Z.
- No guarantees that increasing m with local optimization tightens bounds enough for robust retrospective MH.
- Limited experimental breadth and runtime comparisons to spectral baselines.

Recommendation: Weak accept. A clever, technically solid approach to bounding the DPP partition function with promising empirical behavior and appealing links to GP inference. With stronger empirical comparisons and discussion of optimization robustness, this could be a clear accept.
