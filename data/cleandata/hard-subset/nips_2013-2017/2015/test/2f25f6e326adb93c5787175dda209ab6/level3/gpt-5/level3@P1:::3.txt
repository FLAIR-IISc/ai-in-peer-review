This paper proposes nonspectral upper and lower bounds on the DPP likelihood (finite and continuous domains) by introducing inducing inputs Z and a low-rank surrogate Q = L_YZ L_Z^{-1} L_ZY. The bounds avoid computing eigenvalues or Fredholm determinants, enabling two inference routes: (i) a variational lower bound that is cheap to evaluate and optimize jointly over kernel parameters and Z; and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that tightens acceptance bounds by increasing m = |Z| until a decision is possible. The continuous case mirrors the finite case with an explicit expression involving L_Z and Ψ = ∫ L(z_i, x)L(x, z_j) dμ(x). Experiments include: a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP (where the spectrum and det(I + L) are known) to sanity check the MCMC, and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset where class-specific DPPs are trained variationally and overdispersion measures are compared.

Relation to prior work: Prior ML work on DPP inference typically relies on (partial) spectral knowledge: learning via top eigenvalues or Nyström approximations (Affandi et al.), EM and fixed-point updates (Gillenwater et al.; Mariet & Sra), or direct L-ensembles with spectral truncations. The paper’s innovation is to avoid eigen-computations entirely by bounding determinants through inducing-point constructions inspired by sparse GP variational bounds (Titsias) and determinant inequalities (Seiler–Simon). The retrospective MH idea has been used with spectral bounds before; the contribution is to adapt it to nonspectral bounds and to extend to continuous Fredholm determinants with computable Ψ for Gaussian kernels/measures.

Quality: The core idea is technically sound and well motivated; the determinant bounds are plausible given the cited inequality, and the construction closely parallels well-understood sparse GP bounds. The retrospective MH scheme is correct in principle if the bounds can be tightened arbitrarily (by increasing m and optimizing Z). However, the empirical evidence is limited, and some important practical/tradeoff questions are unanswered (see below). The variational method’s failure on the toy example is acknowledged but not analyzed; this weakens the practical guidance.

Clarity: The exposition of the bounds is compact but sometimes rough. Important definitions are missing for non-experts (e.g., the distinction between “marginal kernel” K and “L-ensemble,” and why learning L is more convenient). Algorithmic details for optimization of Z, computational complexity, and the continuous-case assumptions needed to compute Ψ efficiently are under-specified. The toy experiment raises identifiability issues without explaining when VI should be expected to succeed.

Originality: Using inducing-input, nonspectral determinant bounds for DPPs (finite and continuous) appears novel, as is the extension to continuous Fredholm determinants with computable Ψ for Gaussian settings. The adaptation of retrospective MH with these bounds is incremental but useful.

Significance: Potentially significant because it removes a major bottleneck (spectral computations) and could broaden DPP inference to larger finite sets and continuous domains. However, the significance is currently limited by a modest empirical section lacking strong baselines, scaling studies, and guidance on choosing m and Z.

Major questions and requests for revision:
- Complexity and runtime: Provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity and wall-clock time between variational and retrospective MH across m and n (and dimension d), including memory. How does the cost of optimizing Z scale?
- Baselines: Compare approximation quality and runtime to (i) spectral bounds/truncations (e.g., Affandi et al.), (ii) EM/fixed-point methods (Gillenwater; Mariet & Sra), and (iii) exact small-scale MLE where feasible (finite sets, small n).
- When does VI work? Analyze failure modes. For which parameter regimes (e.g., weakly vs strongly repulsive, identifiable vs confounded parameters like κ) does VI succeed? Provide diagnostics (e.g., variability across runs, bound gaps, sensitivity to m).
- Choice of inducing inputs: Evaluate performance when Z is set to the observed items (finite case) or to the current sample points (toy continuous case), versus optimized Z, and mixed strategies (e.g., k-means, grids). Report bound tightness and accuracy.
- Heuristics for m and Z: Offer rules of thumb for selecting m (e.g., increase until bound gap stabilizes) and practical initialization/placement (e.g., k-means on pooled Y, low-discrepancy grids for continuous Gaussian base measures, reuse Z across iterations).
- Retrospective MH guarantees: Clarify conditions under which increasing m ensures eventual acceptance/rejection and discuss the empirical distribution of m required per iteration. Quantify overhead vs standard MH with spectral bounds.
- Continuous case: Specify assumptions ensuring Ψ is tractable beyond Gaussian–Gaussian; discuss numerical stability and quadrature for Ψ, and scalability in higher dimensions.

Minor comments and suggestions:
- Define K (marginal kernel with eigenvalues in [0,1]) and L-ensembles, and briefly relate them so uninformed readers can follow the modeling choices.
- The discussion of advantages of directly learning parameters of K is somewhat at odds with the chosen L-parametrization. Consider condensing this discussion and focusing on practical implications (interpretability vs tractability).
- Provide pseudo-code for VI and retrospective MH, including Z-optimization steps and stopping rules for tightening bounds.
- Report bound gaps (upper–lower) during training/inference as a convergence/quality diagnostic.
- Clarify the determinant inequality and any conditions (PSD, trace-class) required; summarize assumptions in the continuous section and how they are checked.

Pros:
- Conceptually simple, nonspectral bounds applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Clear connection to sparse GP methodology; practical O(n m^2) computations.
- Retrospective MH adaptation removes need for eigen-computations.
- Promising empirical signals (converging bounds vs m, class separation on real data).

Cons:
- Limited and uneven empirical evaluation; no strong baselines or scaling studies.
- Insufficient guidance on choosing/initializing inducing inputs and m.
- VI failure on toy example not analyzed; practical scope unclear.
- Methodological description lacks key details (assumptions, complexity, algorithms).

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper contains a promising and potentially impactful idea, but the current version lacks the empirical validation, comparative analysis, and practical guidance needed for NIPS. With stronger experiments, clearer algorithms, and concrete heuristics for m and Z, this work could make a solid contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
