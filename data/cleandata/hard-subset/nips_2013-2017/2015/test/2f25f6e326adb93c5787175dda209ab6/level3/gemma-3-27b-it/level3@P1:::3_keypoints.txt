- Paper tackles the computational bottleneck in DPP inference by avoiding spectral decomposition via novel likelihood bounds using inducing inputs.  
- Authors parametrize the L-kernel directly (instead of K) to enable practical inference despite reduced interpretability.  
- Proposed methods enable both variational inference and MCMC, increasing applicability across scenarios.  
- The inducing-inputs idea is inspired by variational Gaussian processes and provides a tractable approximation to the DPP likelihood.  
- Theoretical contributions include bounds on the DPP likelihood with proofs provided in the supplement.  
- Empirical validation shows feasibility on a synthetic dataset and a diabetic neuropathy real-world dataset.  
- Addresses a significant practical challenge, making DPPs more scalable for large datasets.  
- Novel bounds that remove the need for eigendecomposition are a valuable methodological contribution.  
- Offering both variational and MCMC inference paradigms is a useful design choice.  
- Method description lacks clarity, especially the retrospective MCMC and the link between bounds and MCMC acceptance.  
- Empirical evaluation is insufficient—missing comparisons of computational complexity and wall-clock time versus existing methods.  
- Limited comparisons to other approximate and exact inference techniques impede assessment of relative benefits.  
- Variational inference appears unstable in experiments, with large parameter estimation errors and unclear failure modes.  
- The approach is sensitive to inducing point choice, but the paper gives little guidance on placement, number, or initialization strategies.  
- Discussion about learning marginal kernel parameters is inconsistent and could be streamlined.  
- Some basic definitions (K and L matrices, marginal kernel, L-ensembles) are missing, reducing accessibility.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, offers novel bounds, and is a practical alternative to spectral methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: rough method description, weak empirical validation, and unstable variational inference raise applicability concerns.  
- Overall: promising approach but requires clearer exposition, stronger empirical comparisons, and analysis of variational limitations.  
- Recommendation: conditional acceptance with major revisions focusing on method clarity, comprehensive benchmarks (including runtime), and guidance/analysis for inducing point selection.
