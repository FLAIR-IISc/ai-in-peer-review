- Proposes novel approximate inference methods for DPPs that avoid spectral decomposition using inducing inputs, with variational and approximate MCMC approaches and reported empirical speedups.
- Builds on prior DPP inference work but specifically targets the computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition in likelihood evaluation.
- Technically sound: derives non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood for both finite and continuous domains.
- Variational approach runs in O(n m^2) time; MCMC method yields exact sampling with retrospective acceptance.
- Empirical results (Gaussian toy and diabetic neuropathy data) show computational benefits but the evaluation is insufficiently strong.
- Major weakness: lacks detailed empirical comparisons and complexity analysis, including wall-clock time comparisons between variational inference and MCMC.
- Major weakness: missing comparisons to other approximate and exact methods in both approximation quality and runtime.
- Major weakness: unclear when and why variational inference fails (large parameter estimation errors on toy data) and why the method relies on approximate MCMC.
- Needs analysis of method behavior when inducing inputs are chosen as actual sampled points and practical guidance on selecting number and placement of inducing points.
- Minor issue: matrices K and L are not defined; brief explanations of marginal kernel and L-ensembles would help uninformed readers.
- Minor issue: inconsistent discussion about the advantages of learning marginal kernel parameters that conflicts with the paper’s approach.
- Overall: promising core idea but insufficient empirical evaluation and rough method description prevent acceptance; recommend strengthening comparisons, clarifying applicability, and adding implementation guidelines.
