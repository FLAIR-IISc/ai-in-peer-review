## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of performing likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), circumventing the computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood, avoiding eigenvalue estimation and enabling both Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and variational inference. This is a significant contribution, as prior work [7] relied on incremental spectral approximations, limiting scalability and applicability. The connection drawn to inducing-point techniques for Gaussian Processes (GPs) is insightful, highlighting a broader class of models benefiting from similar bounding strategies. 

The paper is technically strong and exceptionally well-written. Proposition 1, establishing a key lower bound on the likelihood, is a particularly clever and valuable insight. Extending this to continuous DPPs further broadens the applicability of the method. The variational inference capability is a major strength, offering a computationally efficient alternative to MCMC. The authors clearly articulate the trade-offs between the two approaches. 

However, the claim that replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization of increasing dimension is a “computational win” warrants further scrutiny. While avoiding explicit eigenvalue decomposition is appealing, the optimization involved in tightening the bounds (particularly for MCMC) could become expensive, especially in high dimensions. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge a potential issue with convergence: increasing the inducing cardinality doesn’t *guarantee* convergence of the bounds due to potentially non-convex optimization. Local minima could hinder valid MCMC steps, a point that needs more detailed discussion.

Several presentation issues detract slightly from the overall quality. Figure 1 lacks axis labels, hindering interpretation. More importantly, the proof of Proposition 1, a central result, should be included in the main text for accessibility. Minor wording issues ("the variational lower is...") should also be addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to bounding the DPP partition function.
*   Potential for faster inference compared to spectral methods.
*   Strong connections to inducing-point techniques and Gaussian Processes.
*   Clear and well-written presentation (aside from minor issues).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Uncertainty regarding the computational cost of bound tightening via optimization.
*   Potential convergence issues with increasing inducing cardinality.
*   Presentation issues (missing proof, figure labels).



**Overall:** This is a clever and promising approach to a difficult problem. Despite the concerns regarding computational cost and convergence, the potential benefits of avoiding spectral decomposition and the strong theoretical foundation warrant acceptance, contingent on addressing the presentation issues and providing a more thorough discussion of the optimization challenges. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
