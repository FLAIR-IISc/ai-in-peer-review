

# Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in DPP parameter learning: the intractable normalization constant requiring spectral decomposition of the kernel. The authors present a novel approach to bounding the DPP likelihood without spectral knowledge, drawing inspiration from inducing-point techniques in Gaussian processes. Previous work by Affandi et al. [7] used spectral bounds requiring power iterations for eigenvalue estimation, which becomes computationally prohibitive as the number of required eigenvalues increases. The authors' key insight is Proposition 1, which provides cheap-to-evaluate bounds on the DPP normalization constant that avoid spectral decomposition entirely, enabling both variational inference and exact MCMC.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it derives non-spectral bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, with Proposition 1 representing a technically elegant and novel contribution that avoids the need for eigenvalue estimation. The connections to inducing-point methods in Gaussian process inference are particularly insightful, demonstrating how DPPs can benefit from scalable GP techniques. The variational inference and MCMC methods built upon these bounds are well-motivated and effectively leverage the authors' novel bounds.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous proofs (though the main proof of Proposition 1 should be in the main text rather than the appendix), clear exposition of the key ideas, and compelling experimental validation. The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets (diabetic neuropathy) convincingly show that the proposed bounds converge to the true likelihood as the number of inducing points increases, with the variational method providing reasonable parameter estimates that match MCMC results.

The paper's strengths are substantial: the novel bounds avoid the computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition, the connections to GP inference techniques are valuable, and the variational inference capability significantly extends the applicability of DPPs to larger datasets. The technical contribution is substantial, with Proposition 1 providing a clean mathematical insight that could benefit other areas requiring determinant bounds.

However, there are some concerns. The computational advantage of replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization of inducing points is not clearly established. The paper claims "considerably lower" computational cost when m << n, but the paper doesn't provide a detailed complexity analysis comparing the proposed method to [7]. The concern about potential non-convergence due to coupled non-convex optimizations (when increasing the number of inducing points) is valid and should be addressed more thoroughly.

Presentation issues include Figure 1 lacking axis labels, the main proof of Proposition 1 being relegated to the appendix (where it should be in the main text), and minor wording issues like "the variational lower is" instead of "the variational lower bound is."

The paper's significance is high. DPPs have become increasingly popular for modeling repulsiveness in diverse applications, but their computational limitations have hindered broader adoption. This work provides a practical path to more efficient DPP inference, potentially enabling applications to larger datasets. The connections to GP inference techniques also broaden the paper's impact beyond DPPs.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel and technically sound contribution to DPP inference
- Proposition 1 provides a key mathematical insight with broad applicability
- Strong connections to Gaussian process inference techniques
- Demonstrated effectiveness on both synthetic and real datasets
- Addresses a significant computational bottleneck in DPP learning

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Computational advantage over [7] is not clearly demonstrated
- Potential convergence concerns with non-convex optimization not fully addressed
- Presentation issues that could confuse readers
- Limited discussion of when the proposed bounds are particularly advantageous

Overall, this is a technically strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to DPP inference. The key insight in Proposition 1 is novel and well-motivated, and the connections to Gaussian process inference techniques are particularly compelling. While the computational advantage claim could be better supported, and presentation issues should be fixed, the paper represents a significant step forward in practical DPP inference. The authors have provided a clever solution to a well-known problem that should enable more widespread use of DPPs in machine learning applications. I recommend acceptance.
