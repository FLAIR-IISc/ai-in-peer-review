- Proposes nonspectral approximate inference for DPPs using inducing inputs to avoid expensive kernel spectral decomposition, enabling variational inference and approximate MCMC.  
- Novel and relevant: addresses a key computational bottleneck in DPP inference with a creative inducing-inputs approach.  
- Dual inference approaches: offers both variational inference and approximate MCMC for different accuracy/computation trade-offs.  
- Empirical validation: experiments on synthetic data and a diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrate applicability and convergence of overdispersion measures.  
- Potential broader impact: methods could inspire further scalable inference work for DPPs and related models.  
- Methodological clarity is insufficient: descriptions (e.g., matrices K and L, marginal kernels, L-ensembles) lack detail and hinder reproducibility.  
- Empirical evaluation is incomplete: missing runtime comparisons and evaluation when inducing inputs equal sampled points.  
- No comparison with existing approximate or exact inference methods, making relative advantages unclear.  
- Variational inference shows large parameter estimation errors on toy data and the paper lacks guidance on choosing/placing inducing points.  
- Presentation needs improvement: unnecessary sections (e.g., learning marginal kernel parameters) and absent computational complexity analysis.  
- Recommendation: conditional acceptance pending clearer method descriptions, more thorough experiments (including runtimes and inducing-point tests), comparisons to prior methods, practical inducing-point guidelines, and a revised, better-organized write-up.  
- Pros summary: novel, computationally efficient approach; dual inference methods; demonstrated real-world applicability.  
- Cons summary: lacks methodological clarity, thorough empirical evaluation, comparisons to existing methods, and shows variational parameter estimation issues.
