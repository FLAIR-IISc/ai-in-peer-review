This paper presents a novel approach to inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral knowledge. DPPs are point process models that naturally encode diversity between points in a realization through a positive definite kernel K. However, learning the parameters of kernel K through likelihood-based inference is challenging due to the intractable normalizing constant. The authors propose bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that do not rely on approximating the spectrum of a large matrix or an operator, making them cheap to evaluate.

The paper starts by introducing DPPs and their desirable properties, such as exact sampling and analyticity of the moments. However, the likelihood of a DPP involves evaluating a kernel L related to K through an often intractable spectral decomposition. The authors follow the common approach in machine learning of directly parametrizing the kernel L, which favors tractability over interpretability of the inferred parameters.

The main contribution of the paper is the derivation of bounds on the likelihood of a DPP, both for finite and continuous domains. These bounds are inspired by sparse approximations to Gaussian processes using inducing variables. The authors show how to use these bounds to infer the parameters of a DPP using variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods.

The paper is well-written and technically strong, with a clear presentation of the main ideas and results. The connections to inducing-point techniques for scalable Gaussian process inference are particularly appealing and extend the paper's impact. However, there are some concerns regarding the computational efficiency of the proposed method, as replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization of increasing dimension may not be a clear computational win.

Additionally, increasing the inducing/cardinality may not guarantee convergence of bounds due to coupled, likely non-convex optimizations, and local minima could prevent valid MCMC steps. These concerns should be addressed in future work to fully establish the effectiveness of the proposed method.

In terms of presentation, there are some minor issues, such as the lack of axis labels in Figure 1 and the placement of the main proof (Proposition 1) in the appendix rather than the main text. However, these issues do not detract from the overall quality of the paper.

Overall, this paper presents a clever approach to bounding the DPP partition function that could enable faster inference and links to other model classes. The variational-inference capability and GP connections are particularly appealing and extend the paper's impact.
