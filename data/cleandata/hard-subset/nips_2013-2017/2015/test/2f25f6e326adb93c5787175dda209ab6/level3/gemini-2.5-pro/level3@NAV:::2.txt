This paper addresses the challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), where the normalizing constant involves an intractable determinant term, `det(L+I)`. While prior work, such as Affandi et al. (2014), has relied on partial spectral approximations that can be computationally demanding, this paper introduces novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant for both finite and continuous DPPs. The core idea, elegantly drawn from the literature on sparse Gaussian Processes (Titsias, 2009), is the creative use of a set of "pseudo-inputs" to construct these computationally efficient bounds. The authors then demonstrate how these bounds enable both fast, approximate variational inference and more expensive, but exact, MCMC sampling via retrospective sampling.

The paper has several significant strengths.
*   **Quality and Originality:** The main contribution is simple, enlightening, and well-executed. The connection between sparse GPs and DPP inference provides a new and valuable perspective on the problem. The proposed non-spectral bounds are a novel and significant contribution that moves away from computationally intensive spectral methods.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a timely problem of high interest to the machine learning community. The generality of bounding kernel matrix determinants using pseudo-inputs suggests potential applications beyond DPPs.
*   **Clarity and Evaluation:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The experimental evaluation is particularly strong; it not only validates the method on synthetic and real-world data but also commendably investigates a failure case for the variational approximation, demonstrating a nuanced understanding of the method's properties and highlighting the utility of the MCMC alternative.

The weaknesses of the paper are minor and easily addressable.
*   The introduction emphasizes the challenge of parameterizing the interpretable K kernel, but the paper follows convention and parameterizes L. The motivation for this framing could be clarified, as the core contribution does not directly resolve this issue.
*   For accessibility, it would be preferable to provide a standard, self-contained supplementary document rather than only an extended version of the paper.

For future work, it would be interesting to consider if this framework could be extended to bound the determinant in terms of the K kernel directly. Overall, this is a high-quality and impactful paper that advances the state-of-the-art in DPP inference. It is well-motivated, technically sound, and likely to be influential.
