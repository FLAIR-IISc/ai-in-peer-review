This paper addresses the problem of scalable, likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). Learning the parameters of a DPP kernel is computationally challenging due to the intractable normalizing constant in the likelihood, which involves the determinant of a large matrix (or a Fredholm determinant in the continuous case). Previous work, such as Affandi et al. (2014), has tackled this by approximating the spectrum of the kernel matrix, which can be computationally intensive.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel set of non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant term. Inspired by the use of inducing variables in sparse Gaussian processes (Titsias, 2009), the authors introduce a set of "pseudo-inputs" to construct a low-rank approximation of the kernel matrix, which in turn provides cheap-to-evaluate bounds on the likelihood. These bounds are applicable to DPPs on both finite and continuous domains. The authors then demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into standard inference frameworks: a fast, approximate method using variational inference, and a more computationally expensive but exact method using Metropolis-Hastings with retrospective sampling.

**Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed bounds are derived rigorously from a known determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975) and are correctly applied to the DPP likelihood function. The work is presented as a complete contribution, covering the theoretical derivation for both finite and continuous cases and providing practical algorithms for learning.

The claims are well-supported by a carefully designed set of experiments. The toy 1D Gaussian-Gaussian experiment is particularly effective, as it allows for comparison against a ground truth likelihood, validating the correctness of the MCMC sampler. Importantly, the authors are honest about the limitations of their methods, showing a scenario where the variational approach fails to identify model parameters due to a flat likelihood surface, demonstrating the value of the MCMC alternative in such cases. The real-world application to the diabetic neuropathy dataset is relevant and convincingly shows that the variational method's parameter estimates converge as the number of pseudo-inputs increases, providing a practical way to assess the quality of the approximation.

**Clarity**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The decision to first introduce the concepts in the simpler finite setting (Section 2) before extending them to the more technical continuous case (Section 4) is excellent for readability. The connection to sparse GPs is enlightening and helps ground the method in a familiar context for many NIPS readers.

One minor point of confusion arises from the introduction's framing of the K vs. L kernel parametrization problem. The paper emphasizes the challenge and interpretability loss of parametrizing L instead of the more natural kernel K. However, the proposed method then proceeds entirely by parametrizing L, effectively sidestepping the issue. While this is a reasonable choice to focus the paper's scope on the computational bottleneck, the initial emphasis might lead readers to expect a solution to the K-L relationship problem, which is not provided. A slight reframing to present this as a deliberate trade-off would improve clarity.

**Originality**
The core idea of using non-spectral, pseudo-input-based bounds for DPP inference is highly original. It represents a significant departure from previous methods that relied on approximating the kernel's spectrum. While the concept of inducing points is borrowed from the Gaussian process literature, its application to bounding determinants for DPPs is novel and creative. The paper clearly articulates how this approach avoids the need for iterative methods like power iteration, which was a key component of prior work. The related work is adequately cited and distinguished.

**Significance**
The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to have a strong impact. DPPs are a powerful tool for modeling diversity, but their practical application has been hampered by the computational cost of inference. This work provides a new, scalable set of tools that makes DPPs more accessible for larger-scale problems. The dual offering of a fast variational method and an exact MCMC sampler is particularly valuable, allowing practitioners to choose the right trade-off between speed and accuracy for their application.

Furthermore, the central idea of using optimizable pseudo-inputs to bound functionals of positive definite kernels is a general one. It has the potential to find applications in other machine learning models where large determinants or matrix operations are a bottleneck, extending the paper's impact beyond the DPP community.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel, elegant, and technically sound method for scalable inference in DPPs, a timely and important problem.
*   **Pro:** The approach is a clear improvement over previous spectral-based methods, offering a more efficient alternative.
*   **Pro:** The experimental validation is thorough and thoughtfully constructed, demonstrating both the strengths of the method and its potential failure modes.
*   **Pro:** The core idea is generalizable and may inspire further research into scalable kernel methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **(Minor):** The paper does not resolve the K vs. L parametrization issue that it highlights in the introduction, which might be slightly confusing for the reader.

**Additional Comments:**
This is a very strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. The core idea is simple, well-executed, and impactful.

A few minor suggestions for the authors:
*   For the supplementary material, providing a separate, standard supplement in addition to an extended version of the paper would be more convenient for reviewers and readers.
*   For future work, it would be interesting to investigate whether these pseudo-input techniques could be used to bound the determinant directly in terms of the more interpretable K kernel, which would address the major challenge outlined in the introduction. Assessing the scalability of the pseudo-input optimization itself as the number of inputs `m` grows would also be a valuable addition.
