This paper proposes novel, cheap-to-evaluate, nonspectral bounds on the determinants arising in the likelihoods of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), both finite and continuous. The authors draw inspiration from sparse approximations to Gaussian processes using inducing variables to derive these bounds, which do not rely on approximating the spectrum of a large matrix or an operator.

The main contribution of the paper is to provide bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that do not depend on the spectral decomposition of the operator L. The authors derive two propositions, one for the finite case and one for the continuous case, which provide lower and upper bounds on the likelihood of a DPP. These bounds are cheap to evaluate and can be used for variational inference and moderately expensive exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference methods for DPPs.

The paper is technically strong, and the authors provide a clear and well-written explanation of their methods and results. The use of inducing variables to derive the bounds is a clever approach, and the connections to Gaussian processes and variational inference are well-established. The experimental results, including a toy Gaussian continuous experiment and a real-world dataset of spatial patterns of nerve fibers in diabetic patients, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce their results. The writing is clear, and the use of notation is consistent throughout the paper. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the related work and the limitations of their approach.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a novel solution to the problem of learning the parameters of a DPP without requiring spectral knowledge. The authors' approach is more efficient and scalable than previous methods, which makes it more suitable for large-scale applications.

However, there are some concerns regarding the computational complexity of the proposed methods. Replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization of increasing dimension may not be a clear computational win, especially for large datasets. Additionally, increasing the inducing/cardinality may not guarantee convergence of bounds due to coupled, likely non-convex optimizations, which could lead to local minima and prevent valid MCMC steps.

Furthermore, there are some minor presentation issues, such as the lack of axis labels in Figure 1 and the placement of the main proof (Proposition 1) in the appendix rather than the main text. Additionally, there is a minor typo in the text ("the variational lower is ...").

Overall, this paper provides a significant contribution to the field of DPPs and machine learning. The proposed methods are novel, efficient, and scalable, making them suitable for large-scale applications. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of their methods and results.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel solution to the problem of learning the parameters of a DPP without requiring spectral knowledge.
* The proposed methods are efficient and scalable, making them suitable for large-scale applications.
* The paper is technically strong, and the authors provide a clear and well-written explanation of their methods and results.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The computational complexity of the proposed methods may be a concern, especially for large datasets.
* The placement of the main proof (Proposition 1) in the appendix rather than the main text may make it harder for readers to follow.
