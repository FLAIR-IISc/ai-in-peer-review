- Problem: DPP parameter inference is hard because the normalizing constant det(L+I) is intractable.
- Main contribution: novel non-spectral upper and lower bounds on det(L+I) for finite and continuous DPPs using m inducing (pseudo-)inputs.
- Method: bounds arise from a low-rank kernel approximation built from inducing points, connecting to sparse/variational GP methods.
- Inference 1: variational method that maximizes a lower bound on the log-likelihood jointly over kernel parameters and inducing-point locations.
- Inference 2: retrospective MCMC that adaptively tightens bounds by increasing inducing points until MH decisions are unambiguous.
- Empirical validation: tested on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP and a real 2D spatial nerve-fiber dataset.
- Strength — originality/significance: non-spectral bounds and GP-inducing-point connection are novel and important.
- Strength — technical quality: propositions are derived from a non-trivial determinant inequality and algorithms are well-founded.
- Strength — applicability: variational approach offers a much faster (approximate) route, broadening DPP use to larger problems.
- Strength — clarity: paper is well-written, well-structured, and covers finite and continuous cases comprehensively.
- Weakness — computational trade-offs: avoids eigencomputations but requires non-linear optimization of inducing locations; unclear runtime gains and no direct empirical runtime comparison with spectral methods.
- Weakness — convergence/MCMC validity: inducing-location optimization is non-convex; poor local minima could prevent tightening bounds and stall the sampler; practical implications under-discussed.
- Weakness — presentation: key proof (Proposition 1) relegated to supplement, Figure 1 lacks axis labels, and the poor variational performance on the 1D experiment is not analyzed.
- Pro-accept: technically sound, elegant, and conceptually advances DPP inference with practical variational methods.
- Con-accept: practical computational benefits not conclusively shown and robustness concerns due to non-convex optimization within MCMC.
- Conclusion: overall excellent and novel work; strengths outweigh weaknesses; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
