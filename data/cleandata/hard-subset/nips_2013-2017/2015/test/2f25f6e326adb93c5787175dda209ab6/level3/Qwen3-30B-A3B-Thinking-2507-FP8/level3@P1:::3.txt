

This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) by deriving nonspectral bounds on the intractable normalizing constant. The authors build on sparse Gaussian process techniques [11] to propose a framework using inducing inputs (pseudo-inputs) Z to bound the likelihood without spectral decomposition. This enables both variational inference (via a lower bound) and retrospective MCMC (via upper/lower bounds), avoiding the O(n³) eigendecomposition required in prior work [7]. The approach is validated on finite and continuous DPPs, with experiments on a synthetic Gaussian example and a diabetic neuropathy dataset.

**Strengths:**  
The core contribution—nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods—is novel and directly addresses a key limitation in the literature. By avoiding spectral decomposition, the method significantly reduces computational overhead for large-scale applications (e.g., news diversity modeling [3] or spatial forest analysis [4]). The connection to sparse Gaussian processes is well-motivated and strengthens theoretical grounding. The continuous-domain extension (Section 4) is a valuable contribution, as most prior work focuses on finite sets. Empirical results demonstrate convergence of inference with increasing inducing points (m), and the retrospective MCMC approach successfully recovers parameters in the toy example (Figure 1), validating the method’s correctness. The paper also acknowledges the trade-off between interpretability and tractability when parametrizing L instead of K, which is a thoughtful consideration.

**Weaknesses:**  
The method description lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. Key ambiguities include: (1) how to initialize and optimize Z (e.g., "optimizing over Z" in Section 3.1 is underspecified); (2) the absence of a rule-of-thumb for selecting m (e.g., "m ≈ 20" in experiments is arbitrary without justification); and (3) no discussion of why Z should be placed away from data points (Figure 1a shows Z as evenly spaced, but this is unexplained). The empirical evaluation is insufficiently comparative:  
- No wall-clock time comparisons between variational inference (VI) and retrospective MCMC, or against [7]’s spectral bounds.  
- VI fails to recover parameters in the toy example (Figure 1b–d show 100% errors for α, λ), yet the paper does not clarify when VI is expected to succeed (e.g., only for well-conditioned problems) or why it fails here.  
- The diabetic neuropathy experiment (Section 5.2) shows convergence of γd with m, but omits comparisons to baseline methods (e.g., [7], [8]) in terms of approximation quality (e.g., log-likelihood values) or runtime.  
- Crucially, the paper does not test whether using the *actual data points* as Z (e.g., m = |Y|) would yield tighter bounds or improve VI accuracy—this is a critical gap for assessing practical utility.  

The discussion of marginal kernel parametrization (Section 1) is inconsistent with the paper’s approach (which directly uses L), creating confusion. Definitions of K and L (e.g., "K is the marginal kernel") are missing early on, which hinders clarity for readers unfamiliar with DPPs. While the paper cites related work (e.g., [7]’s spectral bounds), it does not explicitly contrast the computational complexity of the proposed bounds (O(nm²)) with alternatives, making it hard to gauge scalability gains.

**Quality & Significance:**  
The theoretical bounds (Propositions 1–2) are sound, but the empirical validation does not sufficiently demonstrate *why* these bounds are practically superior. The significance is limited by the lack of comparisons to existing methods, as the paper fails to show: (1) whether the bounds are tighter than [7]’s spectral-based bounds for the same m, or (2) if the computational savings justify the approximation error. For instance, in the diabetic dataset, the 24-minute runtime for m=1200 (Section 5.2) is not contextualized against [7]’s eigendecomposition cost. Without this, the significance of the method as a scalable solution remains unproven.

**Clarity & Originality:**  
The paper is generally well-organized, but clarity suffers from inconsistent terminology (e.g., "kernel L" vs. "marginal kernel K") and insufficient explanation of Z’s role. Originality is strong in the bound derivation (extending sparse GP techniques to DPPs), though the MCMC adaptation via retrospective sampling [17] is not novel. The key novelty lies in *avoiding spectral decomposition*—a significant practical advance over [7].

**Recommendation:**  
**Reject.** The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution but falls short in empirical validation and methodological clarity. The missing comparisons (to [7], [8]) and unaddressed questions (e.g., VI failure modes, Z selection rules) undermine the claim of practical utility. Strengthening the empirical section with runtime comparisons, parameter recovery benchmarks, and sensitivity analyses (e.g., on m and Z initialization) would be essential for acceptance. As written, the work lacks sufficient evidence that the proposed method offers a meaningful improvement over existing approaches. The theoretical contribution is sound but not sufficiently contextualized within the broader inference landscape.
