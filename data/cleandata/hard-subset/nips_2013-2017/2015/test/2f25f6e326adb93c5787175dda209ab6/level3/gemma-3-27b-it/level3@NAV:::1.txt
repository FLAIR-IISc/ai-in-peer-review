## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a key challenge in applying Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the computational burden of spectral decomposition when performing likelihood-based inference. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood, circumventing the need for eigenvalue estimation – a significant bottleneck in prior work [7]. This is achieved through inspiration from inducing point methods in Gaussian Processes (GPs) [11], establishing a compelling connection between the two models.

The core contribution, Proposition 1, provides a lower bound on the DPP likelihood that doesn’t rely on spectral decomposition, opening the door to both variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The extension to continuous DPPs in Section 4 further broadens the applicability of the work. The paper is technically strong and clearly written, with a good overview of related work in DPPs and GPs. The authors appropriately acknowledge the trade-off between interpretability when parameterizing the kernel L versus K. The connection to inducing points is a particularly appealing aspect, demonstrating a potentially fruitful synergy between DPP and GP methodologies.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the authors claim computational advantages, replacing power iterations with non-linear optimization of increasing dimension (for tightening bounds in MCMC) may not always be a practical win. The concern that increasing the number of inducing points/cardinality might lead to local minima preventing valid MCMC steps is valid and requires further investigation. Moreover, the presentation could be improved: Figure 1 lacks axis labels, and the crucial proof of Proposition 1 should be included in the main text rather than relegated to the appendix. Minor wording issues (“the variational lower is…”), though not critical, detract slightly from the overall clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to bounding DPP partition functions.
*   Potential for faster inference compared to spectral methods.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and clear exposition.
*   Connections to scalable GP inference via inducing points.
*   Demonstrated applicability on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Uncertain practical computational gains over existing methods.
*   Potential for convergence issues with increasing model complexity.
*   Presentation issues detract slightly from clarity.

**Overall:** This is a clever and promising contribution to the field. The proposed bounds offer a potentially significant improvement in the tractability of DPP inference. While concerns remain regarding computational efficiency and convergence, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the presentation issues and provide a more thorough discussion of the computational trade-offs.
