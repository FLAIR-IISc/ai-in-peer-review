The paper "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge" presents a novel approach to bounding the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral knowledge. The main idea is to derive upper and lower bounds on the determinant of a matrix using a set of pseudo-inputs, which are then applied to bound the likelihood of DPPs.

The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to DPPs, highlighting their desirable properties and the challenges associated with likelihood-based inference. They also provide a thorough review of previous work on DPPs, including the limitations of existing approaches.

The proposed method is based on a non-trivial inequality on determinants, which is used to derive bounds on the determinant of a matrix. The authors then apply these bounds to the likelihood of DPPs, both for finite and continuous domains. The use of pseudo-inputs to bound functionals of positive definite kernels is a creative and insightful approach.

The experimental evaluation is well-constructed and illustrates key behaviors of the proposed method. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a toy Gaussian continuous experiment and a real-world dataset of spatial patterns of nerve fibers in diabetic patients. The results show that the proposed method can provide accurate and efficient inference for DPPs.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and results. The use of figures and tables to illustrate the results is also effective.

The approach is general and has potential applications beyond determinantal point processes. The authors suggest that their method could be used to bound the determinant directly in terms of K, which could be an interesting direction for future work.
