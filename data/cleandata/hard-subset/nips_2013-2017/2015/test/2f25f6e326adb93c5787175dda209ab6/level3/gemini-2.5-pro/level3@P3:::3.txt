### **Summary**

This paper addresses the significant computational challenge of likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), which typically requires an expensive spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors propose a novel approach to approximate the intractable determinant term in the DPP likelihood using bounds derived from inducing inputs, drawing a clear and useful parallel to sparse approximations in Gaussian processes. This framework avoids spectral decomposition entirely. Based on these bounds, the paper develops two inference algorithms: a fast but approximate variational inference (VI) method and a more computationally intensive but asymptotically exact MCMC method using retrospective sampling. The methods are applicable to DPPs on both finite and continuous domains. Empirical results on a toy problem and a real-world spatial statistics dataset demonstrate the feasibility of the approach and potential for computational speedups.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—a non-spectral, inducing-point-based approximation for the DPP likelihood—is novel and significant. It provides a new path for scaling DPP inference that is distinct from previous spectral approximation methods (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013). This could make DPPs accessible to a wider range of large-scale problems. The extension of these ideas from the finite to the continuous case is also a valuable contribution.

2.  **Technical Approach:** The connection drawn to sparse Gaussian processes (Titsias, 2009) is elegant and provides strong intuition for the method. Leveraging inducing inputs is a well-established and powerful technique for scaling kernel methods, and its application to DPPs is a natural and clever step.

3.  **Potential Impact:** By providing both a fast, approximate VI method and a more robust MCMC method within the same framework, the paper offers a flexible toolkit for practitioners. If proven to be robust, these methods could become a standard approach for learning DPP parameters.

### **Weaknesses**

While the core idea is promising, the paper in its current form is undermined by a rough methodological description and an insufficient empirical evaluation that fails to adequately characterize the proposed methods' performance and limitations.

**Major Issues:**

1.  **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The experimental section is missing several crucial comparisons that are necessary to assess the contribution.
    *   **VI vs. MCMC:** The paper presents two methods but never directly compares them. A comparison of their computational complexity (in big-O notation) and empirical wall-clock time on the same task is essential for a practitioner to choose between them.
    *   **Comparison to Alternatives:** The proposed methods are not benchmarked against any competing approaches. Comparisons are needed against other approximate methods (e.g., Nyström-based approximations) and, where feasible, exact inference. This comparison should evaluate both approximation quality (e.g., parameter error, final log-likelihood) and runtime. Without this, it is impossible to judge the trade-offs offered by the proposed techniques.

2.  **Failure of Variational Inference:** The VI method is a key contribution, yet the paper reports that it "yields poorly consistent results" on the toy example, with parameter errors up to 100%. The authors then pivot to MCMC without a clear explanation for this failure. This is a major concern. The authors should clarify the conditions under which the VI approach is expected to succeed or fail. Is the variational lower bound too loose? Is the joint optimization over kernel parameters and inducing inputs unstable? A deeper analysis of the VI method's limitations is critical.

3.  **Guidance on Inducing Inputs:** The performance of the method depends critically on the number (`m`) and placement of the inducing inputs (`Z`). However, the paper provides little practical guidance on these choices.
    *   The authors should provide a rule-of-thumb for selecting `m` and for initializing the locations of `Z`. The suggestion to increase `m` until results stabilize is reasonable but could be supported by a more formal analysis or empirical study.
    *   For the toy example, what happens if the inducing inputs are chosen to be the actual sampled points from a realization? This would be an informative sanity check on the tightness of the bounds and the behavior of the algorithms.

**Minor Issues:**

1.  **Clarity of Definitions:** The paper assumes significant familiarity with DPP literature. Key concepts like the marginal kernel `K` and the L-ensemble (defined by kernel `L`) are used without a clear, introductory definition. A brief paragraph explaining the relationship `K = L(I+L)^-1` and the practical reasons for parameterizing `L` directly would make the paper much more accessible to a broader NIPS audience.

2.  **Inconsistent Discussion:** The introduction and discussion briefly mention the advantages of learning the parameters of the more interpretable marginal kernel `K`. However, the paper's entire technical contribution focuses on parameterizing `L`. This creates a slight disconnect. This discussion could be reduced or rephrased to better align with the paper's actual approach and avoid confusing the reader.

### **Recommendation**

This paper introduces a promising and original idea for scalable DPP inference. However, the current manuscript feels incomplete due to the weaknesses in the experimental evaluation and lack of clarity in the exposition. The failure of the VI method on a simple toy problem is particularly concerning and requires a much deeper investigation.

I recommend that the paper be accepted on the condition that the authors perform a substantial revision to address these points. Specifically, the authors should:
1.  Significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation by providing the missing comparisons (VI vs. MCMC, proposed methods vs. alternatives).
2.  Provide a thorough analysis of the VI method's failure on the toy problem and clarify its expected operating regime.
3.  Offer concrete, practical guidance for selecting and placing inducing inputs.
4.  Improve the clarity of the presentation by defining key terms and ensuring the discussion is consistent with the method.

Without these revisions, the paper's claims are not sufficiently supported, and the practical utility of the proposed methods remains unclear.
