Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood by introducing inducing inputs Z and a low-rank surrogate Q, yielding a tractable variational lower bound and an approximate retrospective-MH scheme for both finite and continuous DPPs. This avoids eigenvalue computations central to prior spectral approaches (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’14) and connects to inducing-point variational GP methods (Titsias, AISTATS’09). The extension to continuous DPPs via operator bounds and Fredholm determinants relates to Bornemann (2010) while differing in using data-adaptive inducing inputs rather than quadrature.

Strengths:
- Conceptually neat, broadly applicable likelihood bounds that sidestep spectral decompositions and apply to continuous domains.
- Clear derivation leveraging determinant inequalities; retrospective MH built carefully from bracketing log-likelihoods.
- Practical promise: O(n m^2) variational complexity, and empirical evidence that bound gaps shrink with m.

Weaknesses:
- Method description is rough in places; key objects (K vs L, L-ensembles) are used before definition.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks principled baselines and head-to-head comparisons.
- Variational inference appears unstable on the toy example; paper relies on approximate MCMC without clarifying regimes where VI is appropriate.

Quality: Theoretical contributions are plausible and interesting, but experimental support is insufficient to substantiate claims about accuracy/speed. Clarity: Mixed—main ideas are understandable, but definitions and algorithmic details (e.g., Z optimization, complexity per step) need tightening. Originality: Solid; nonspectral, inducing-point bounds for DPP likelihoods are novel. Significance: Potentially impactful for large-scale and continuous DPP inference, contingent on stronger evidence.

Major questions and requests:
- Provide a direct complexity and wall-clock comparison between the proposed VI and retrospective MH, including cost/memory vs n, m, d, and number of Z-optimizations per MH step.
- Compare against exact (small n) and approximate alternatives: spectral MH (Affandi et al.), Nyström/Krylov power methods, determinant approximations, and recent fixed-point/EM methods, reporting approximation quality and runtime.
- Clarify when VI is expected to work or fail. Why does VI mis-estimate parameters on the toy data? Is tightness of the bound sensitive to Z placement or to κ/σ scales?
- Toy study: how do methods perform if inducing inputs are set to the observed points (and to k-means over them)?
- Provide rules of thumb for (a) choosing m (e.g., stop when bound gap or tr(L−Q) below tolerance), and (b) initializing/placing Z (e.g., k-means on pooled data, samples from estimated marginal intensity, grid for Gaussian base measures).

Minor comments:
- Define K (marginal kernel) and L-ensembles early; explicitly state K = L(I+L)−1 and the role of det(LY)/det(I+L).
- The discussion advocating direct learning of K’s parameters seems inconsistent with your choice to parametrize L; consider trimming or reconciling this.

Recommendation: Borderline accept. If accepted, require improved clarity (definitions, algorithmic details) and a significantly strengthened empirical section with comprehensive baselines and runtime analyses.
