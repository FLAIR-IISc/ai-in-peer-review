

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in DPP inference: the intractable normalizing constant requiring spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors propose a novel approach using inducing inputs to derive bounds on the likelihood that avoid spectral decomposition, enabling both variational inference and approximate MCMC for DPPs. This work builds upon recent advances in sparse Gaussian processes [11] and extends them to DPPs, offering a promising direction for scalable DPP inference.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of non-spectral bounds on the likelihood (Propositions 1 and 2), which enable efficient inference without spectral decomposition. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be used for variational inference (Section 3.1) and retrospective MCMC (Section 3.2), with empirical validation on toy and real datasets (Section 5). The approach is conceptually clean and aligns with the sparse GP literature, making it accessible to the ML community.

**Strengths:** The paper identifies a genuine computational bottleneck in DPP inference and offers a theoretically sound solution. The bounds are derived from nontrivial determinant inequalities [14], and the application to DPPs is well-motivated. The experimental results on the diabetic neuropathy dataset show the method's practical utility, and the MCMC results on the Gaussian-Gaussian toy example demonstrate correctness.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising approach, the paper has significant weaknesses that need addressing for acceptance:

1. **Method description is rough:** The paper introduces matrices K and L without defining them (K is the marginal kernel, L is the kernel used in the likelihood), which is confusing for readers unfamiliar with DPPs. A brief explanation of the relationship between K and L would greatly improve clarity.

2. **Empirical evaluation is insufficient:** The paper lacks critical comparisons that would validate the method's advantages. The authors need to:
   - Compare computational complexity and wall-clock times between variational inference and approximate MCMC
   - Compare against other approximate methods (e.g., [7, 8, 9]) and exact methods (where feasible) in terms of approximation quality and runtime
   - Investigate the toy example when inducing inputs are chosen to be the actual sampled points (this would test whether the method can identify the optimal inducing set)

3. **Variational inference performance is concerning:** The variational approach shows large parameter estimation errors on the toy data (κ varies over several orders of magnitude across runs). The paper fails to clarify when variational inference is expected to work or fail. This is particularly problematic as the authors rely on approximate MCMC as a fallback.

4. **Lack of practical guidance:** The authors provide no rule-of-thumb for selecting (a) the number of inducing points or (b) their initialization/placement. The experimental results show inducing points form "neat grids" (Figures 1a, 4), but the paper doesn't explain how to achieve this or whether it's generalizable.

5. **Inconsistent discussion of kernel learning:** The paper's discussion of advantages of directly learning marginal kernel parameters is inconsistent with the approach (which parametrizes L instead of K). This section could be significantly reduced as it creates confusion about the paper's core contribution.

**Recommendation:** The paper has merit but requires significant revisions. If accepted, the authors should substantially improve the method description (including definitions of K and L), strengthen the empirical evaluation with the comparisons requested above, and provide practical guidance for implementing their approach. The current empirical evaluation lacks sufficient evidence to demonstrate the method's advantages over existing approaches.

The paper's significance lies in addressing a real computational bottleneck in DPP inference, but its current form fails to convincingly demonstrate this contribution. With the proposed revisions, this work could make a valuable contribution to the literature on probabilistic models for diverse sets. As it stands, the insufficient empirical evaluation and unclear method description prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance.
