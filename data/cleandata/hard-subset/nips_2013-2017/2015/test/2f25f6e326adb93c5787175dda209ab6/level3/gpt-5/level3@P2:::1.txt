Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces nonspectral upper and lower bounds on the DPP likelihood that avoid computing eigenvalues of the L-operator. For finite ground sets, the authors construct a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ−1 LZY using m “inducing inputs” Z (not constrained to be a subset of the ground set), and show
det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I).
The bounds are extended to continuous domains, yielding analogous inequalities involving the Fredholm determinant det(I+L) and an integral matrix Ψ. These bounds enable (i) a tractable variational lower bound on the log-likelihood by replacing log det(L+I) with its lower bound, and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) scheme that tightens acceptance bounds by increasing m and re-optimizing Z.

The approach is inspired by sparse GP techniques with inducing variables [Titsias, AISTATS’09], but targets the DPP normalizer rather than a GP marginal. In contrast to prior DPP inference that relies on partial spectra [Affandi et al., ICML’13], these bounds avoid power iterations or large-scale eigendecompositions and apply to continuous domains. Related DPP learning methods (EM, fixed-point) either assume spectral access, work on finite sets, or optimize surrogates. The paper also connects to numerical Fredholm determinant work [Bornemann, 2010] and Nystrom approximations [Affandi et al., AISTATS’13].

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: clean determinant inequalities specialized to DPP normalizers, covering both finite and continuous cases, with simple-to-evaluate expressions once kernel evaluations are available.
- Practicality: supports two complementary inference modes—variational (cheap) and retrospective MH (exact, within numerical tolerance)—without spectral information, including on continuous domains.
- Connections to sparse GPs: the inducing-input construction provides intuition and a toolkit (e.g., optimizing Z, O(n m^2) scaling) that the community knows how to use.
- Experiments: show correctness on a 1D continuous toy where the exact Fredholm determinant is available; demonstrate usefulness on a real spatial dataset (neuropathy) with coherent trends in overdispersion across classes and convergence of bounds with m.
- Writing: generally clear, with a useful discussion of L vs K parameterizations and their identifiability trade-offs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Computational trade-offs not fully settled: replacing power iterations with iterative, nonconvex optimization over Z of increasing dimension may not be a consistent win, and the paper lacks head-to-head runtime/accuracy comparisons against [Affandi et al., 2013] on large finite ground sets.
- Tightening guarantees: while bounds empirically converge with m, there is no theorem (in the main text) guaranteeing convergence or monotonicity as m increases and Z is optimized; retrospective MH depends critically on being able to tighten acceptance bounds reliably.
- Sensitivity to Z-optimization: local minima could stall tightening steps in MH; more diagnostics and ablations (initialization schemes, optimization choices, effect on acceptance) would increase confidence.
- Practical memory/compute: the finite-case method still needs O(n m^2) time and access to L; discussion of memory footprint (e.g., streaming kernel evaluations vs storing L) would clarify scalability.
- Presentation: Figure axes are missing in places; the main proof (Proposition 1) is central enough to merit at least a proof sketch in the main text; a few minor typos (e.g., “the variational lower is ...”).

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The bounds rest on solid determinant inequalities (Seiler–Simon). The finite and continuous derivations are careful and appear correct. The retrospective MH construction mirrors the spectral approach but with nonspectral bounds; however, theoretical guarantees about tightening with m and optimized Z are deferred or informal.
- Clarity: Generally well-organized and readable, with helpful pointers to GP analogies. A proof sketch for Proposition 1, clearer assumptions for the continuous case (trace-class conditions and computability of Ψ), and cleaned-up figures would improve accessibility.
- Originality: High. Applying inducing-point-style determinant bounds to DPP normalizers and extending them to continuous-space DPPs is a novel and interesting contribution beyond prior spectral and Nyström-based approaches.
- Significance: Potentially high. If shown competitive, these bounds could materially broaden practical DPP inference to settings where spectra are costly or unavailable, especially in continuous domains and for Bayesian inference.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, general nonspectral bounds with both upper and lower inequalities.
- Works for continuous DPPs; few existing scalable methods do.
- Enables both variational learning and exact MH via retrospective acceptance.
- Strong conceptual link to sparse GP literature that may catalyze further advances.
- Empirical results are promising and highlight identifiability issues clearly.

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of theoretical tightening guarantees with increasing m and optimized Z, which underpins retrospective MH reliability.
- Limited experimental comparison to spectral methods on large finite problems; unclear net computational advantage in practice.
- Potential fragility due to nonconvex Z-optimization; limited analysis/diagnostics.
- Some presentation and reproducibility details need refinement.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide conditions under which the bounds tighten to the true determinant as m→∞ and, if possible, monotonicity in m for optimally chosen Z (or a practical heuristic with guarantees).
- Add comparative experiments against [Affandi et al., 2013] on large finite ground sets, reporting wall-clock time, memory, and accuracy, plus MH acceptance behavior as a function of m.
- Include ablations on Z: initialization (e.g., k-means, samples from base measure), gradient vs black-box optimization, and sensitivity to local minima; report how often MH needed to increase m and by how much.
- Move a proof sketch of Proposition 1 into the main paper; explicitly state continuous-case assumptions (trace-class, Mercer-type conditions, computability of Ψ).
- Improve figures (axis labels, units), fix minor typos, and clarify complexity and memory requirements.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a clever and potentially impactful alternative to spectral approaches for DPP inference, with solid theory and practical promise, but would benefit from stronger guarantees and broader empirical comparisons to cement its practical advantages.

Confidence: 3/5 (familiar with DPPs and sparse GP methods; did not verify all operator-theoretic details).
