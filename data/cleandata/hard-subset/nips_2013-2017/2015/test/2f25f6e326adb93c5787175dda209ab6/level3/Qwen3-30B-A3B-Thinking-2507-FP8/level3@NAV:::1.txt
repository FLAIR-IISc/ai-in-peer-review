

This paper addresses a core challenge in DPP inference: the intractable normalization constant in the likelihood, which typically requires spectral decomposition. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds that avoid eigenvalue estimation by leveraging inducing-point techniques (drawing direct parallels to sparse Gaussian processes), enabling efficient variational inference and retrospective MCMC without spectral knowledge. This extends prior work [7], which relied on computationally expensive eigenvalue approximations, by providing bounds that are cheap to evaluate and directly applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs. The key technical contribution is Proposition 1, which establishes a tight lower-bound inequality through determinant inequalities—a significant insight that underpins the entire approach.

The paper is technically strong, well-structured, and clearly written. The variational inference and MCMC frameworks are elegantly derived, and the connections to GP inference are particularly compelling, broadening the paper's impact. Experiments on synthetic and real-world diabetic neuropathy data validate the method’s effectiveness, showing convergence of bounds and meaningful parameter estimation. The clarity of the exposition and the practical demonstration of the approach are major strengths.

However, two concerns warrant attention. First, the computational advantage of replacing power iterations with nonlinear optimization over increasing inducing sets is not clearly established—optimizing over a growing set of pseudo-inputs may not yield a net speedup, especially for large-scale problems. Second, the non-convex nature of the optimization (both for inducing points and kernel parameters) risks local minima, potentially invalidating MCMC steps even with increased inducing sets, and the paper does not adequately address convergence guarantees. Additionally, presentation issues include unlabeled axes in Figure 1, the main proof (Proposition 1) being relegated to the appendix (it should be in the main text), and minor wording errors ("the variational lower is...").

Despite these concerns, the paper’s core contribution is novel, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful bottleneck in DPP inference. The GP connections and practical applicability make it highly significant for the field. With minor revisions to address the presentation issues and computational claims, this work would be a strong fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
