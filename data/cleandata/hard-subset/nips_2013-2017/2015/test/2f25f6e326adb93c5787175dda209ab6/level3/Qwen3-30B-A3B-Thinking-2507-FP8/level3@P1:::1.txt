

This paper addresses a core challenge in learning determinantal point processes (DPPs): the intractable normalization constant in the likelihood, which requires spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix for exact evaluation. Prior work [7] circumvented this by approximating the spectrum via power iterations to enable retrospective MCMC, but this remains computationally intensive for large datasets. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood using inducing points, inspired by sparse Gaussian process approximations [11]. The key contribution is Proposition 1, which provides tight lower and upper bounds on the critical determinant term without spectral knowledge, enabling efficient variational inference and MCMC. The bounds are derived via a nontrivial determinant inequality [14] and extended to continuous domains in Section 4, with experimental validation on synthetic and real-world diabetic neuropathy data.

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. Proposition 1’s inequality is a significant theoretical advance, offering a computationally cheaper alternative to spectral methods. The connection to Gaussian process inducing points (Section 3.1) is particularly insightful, bridging two active areas of machine learning. Experiments validate the approach: variational inference on the diabetic dataset (Section 5.2) shows convergence of overdispersion parameters as inducing points increase, and retrospective MCMC (Section 5.1) matches ideal inference despite parametrizing L directly (which obscures interpretability). The writing is precise, with clear exposition of the inference frameworks. The significance is strong: by avoiding spectral decomposition, the method enables scalable DPP learning for large datasets where [7]’s power iteration approach becomes prohibitive.

Several weaknesses warrant consideration. First, the computational advantage over [7] is not clearly established. While the bounds avoid spectral decomposition, the optimization of inducing points (Z) and kernel parameters (θ) requires iterative nonlinear optimization that scales with |Z|, potentially offsetting gains. The paper notes that MCMC iterations may require increasing |Z|, but does not quantify whether the total cost remains lower than power iteration for comparable accuracy. Second, the convergence of bounds under coupled optimization (Z and θ) is not rigorously addressed. The paper acknowledges local minima as a risk (Section 3.2), but the experiments do not demonstrate robustness to poor initialization or quantify how often the bounds converge to tight values. Third, presentation issues diminish clarity: Proposition 1’s proof is relegated to the appendix (it should be in the main text), Figure 1 lacks axis labels, and minor errors persist (e.g., "the variational lower is" in Section 3.1).

The originality is solid: while [7] relies on spectral approximation, this work replaces it with a GP-inspired deterministic bound, avoiding eigenvalue estimation entirely. This aligns with NIPS’ emphasis on novel technical pathways. The significance extends beyond DPPs, as the bound technique could inspire similar approaches for other models with intractable partition functions. The diabetic neuropathy application (Section 5.2) demonstrates practical utility, showing clear separation between disease stages via inferred kernel parameters—a meaningful contribution to spatial statistics.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper that advances DPP inference with a clever, well-motivated approach. The trade-offs between computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees are thoughtfully discussed, though the claimed computational advantage requires further empirical validation. The minor presentation flaws do not undermine the core contribution, and the work is clearly positioned within the DPP and GP literature. For NIPS, this represents a valuable extension of inference methods for a widely used model class, with clear applicability to large-scale applications. While the computational benefits over [7] are not conclusively proven, the method’s flexibility and strong experimental results make it a compelling contribution. The paper is suitable for acceptance, as it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a useful tool for practitioners.
