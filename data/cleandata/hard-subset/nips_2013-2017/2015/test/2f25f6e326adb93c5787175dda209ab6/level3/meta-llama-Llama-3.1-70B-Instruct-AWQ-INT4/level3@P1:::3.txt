This paper proposes novel, cheap-to-evaluate, nonspectral bounds on the determinants arising in the likelihoods of determinantal point processes (DPPs), both finite and continuous. The authors derive bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that do not rely on approximating the spectrum of a large matrix or an operator, making them useful for variational inference and moderately expensive exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference methods for DPPs.

The paper begins by introducing DPPs and their desirable properties, such as exact sampling or analyticity of the moments. However, learning the parameters of the kernel through likelihood-based inference is not straightforward due to two main issues: the kernel that appears in the likelihood is not the original kernel, but another kernel related to it through an often intractable spectral decomposition, and the likelihood has an intractable normalizing constant.

To address these issues, the authors propose a method that directly parametrizes the kernel L instead of the original kernel K, which makes the numerator of the likelihood cheap to evaluate, but favors tractability over interpretability of the inferred parameters. They then derive bounds on the likelihood of a DPP that do not depend on the spectral decomposition of the operator L, using inducing inputs to approximate the likelihood.

The authors demonstrate the use of these bounds for expensive-but-exact MCMC and cheap-but-approximate variational inference. They show that the bounds have some degree of freedom, the pseudo-inputs, which can be optimized to tighten the bounds. This optimization step is crucial for likelihood-based inference of parametric DPP models.

The paper also presents experimental results on a toy Gaussian continuous experiment and a real dataset of spatial patterns of nerve fibers in diabetic patients. The results show that the proposed method can accurately infer the parameters of a DPP and demonstrate its use for expensive-but-exact MCMC and cheap-but-approximate variational inference.

Overall, the paper has merit, but the method description is rough, and the empirical evaluation is insufficient. The authors need to provide more details on the computational complexity and wall-clock time of the proposed methods, as well as comparisons to other approximate and exact alternatives in terms of approximation quality and runtime.

The paper also raises several questions, including when variational inference is expected to work or fail and why, and how the methods perform when inducing inputs are chosen to be the actual sampled points. Additionally, the authors should provide rule-of-thumb guidance for selecting the number of inducing points and initialization/placement of inducing points.

In terms of clarity, the paper could benefit from a brief explanation of marginal kernel and L-ensembles for uninformed readers. The discussion of advantages of directly learning marginal kernel parameters is also inconsistent with the paper's approach and could be reduced.

If accepted, the paper should improve the clarity of the method description, strengthen the empirical evaluation and write-up, and address the questions and concerns raised above.
