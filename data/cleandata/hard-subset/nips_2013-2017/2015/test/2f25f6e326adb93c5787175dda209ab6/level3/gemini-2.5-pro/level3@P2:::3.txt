This paper proposes a new approach for parametric inference in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) that avoids the computationally expensive spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix, which is a major bottleneck in existing methods. The core contribution is the derivation of novel upper and lower bounds on the DPP log-likelihood using a set of "inducing inputs," a technique inspired by sparse Gaussian Processes. These non-spectral bounds are then leveraged to develop two inference procedures: a fast but approximate variational inference (VI) method and a more computationally intensive but exact-in-the-limit MCMC method using retrospective sampling. The framework is presented for both finite and continuous DPPs. This work is related to previous efforts in learning DPP parameters, such as Affandi et al. (2014) [7], but critically, it bypasses the need for even partial spectral information (e.g., top eigenvalues), offering a potentially more scalable alternative.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of using inducing inputs to derive non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood is novel and significant. If effective, it could substantially broaden the applicability of DPPs to larger-scale problems where full or partial eigendecompositions are prohibitive.
2.  **General Framework:** The paper presents a flexible framework that is applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs and provides methodologies for both approximate (VI) and asymptotically exact (MCMC) Bayesian inference.
3.  **Technical Contribution:** The derivation of the likelihood bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 is a solid technical contribution that connects ideas from the sparse GP literature to the DPP setting.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is promising, the paper in its current form is weakened by a lack of clarity in its presentation and an insufficient empirical evaluation that leaves several critical questions unanswered.

1.  **Clarity:** The paper could be made more accessible to a broader audience. For instance, the distinction and roles of the marginal kernel K and the L-ensemble kernel L are central to understanding DPPs, yet they are used without a clear, self-contained definition. While the paper follows the common practice of parametrizing L, the discussion about the interpretability advantages of K feels somewhat disconnected from the actual methods presented and could be streamlined.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are preliminary and do not provide enough evidence to assess the practical utility of the proposed methods.
    *   **Lack of Baselines:** The paper does not compare its proposed methods against relevant alternatives. A thorough evaluation would require comparing both the VI and MCMC approaches to other methods like the spectral-bound MCMC of [7] and Nyström-based approximations [13]. Comparisons should be made on metrics of both approximation quality (e.g., parameter accuracy, final likelihood) and computational cost (e.g., wall-clock time, complexity analysis).
    *   **Internal Comparison:** There is no direct comparison between the proposed VI and MCMC methods in terms of their trade-offs. What is the difference in computational complexity and runtime between the two on the same problem?
    *   **Variational Inference Performance:** The VI method's failure on the simple 1D toy problem is a major concern. The paper notes that it "yields poorly consistent results" but offers no investigation into why this is the case or under what conditions this method might be expected to succeed. Without this analysis, the contribution of the VI method is unclear.

3.  **Lack of Practical Guidance:** The performance of the proposed methods hinges on the choice of inducing inputs, yet the paper offers little practical guidance on this crucial aspect.
    *   How should a practitioner select the number of inducing points, *m*? The paper suggests increasing *m* until results stabilize, but a more principled or heuristic approach would be valuable.
    *   How should the inducing points be initialized and placed? The experiments show that they are optimized, but guidance on initialization would be helpful.
    *   A simple but important experiment is missing: what is the performance if the inducing inputs are chosen to be the observed data points from a realization? This would connect the method more directly to Nyström-style approximations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and elegant approach to bypass the main computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
*   The idea of non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   The framework is general and provides avenues for both fast/approximate and slower/exact inference.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical evaluation is too weak to validate the practical benefits of the proposed methods.
*   The lack of comparison to established baselines makes it impossible to judge the method's performance in the context of existing work.
*   The demonstrated failure of the variational inference method on a simple problem without clear explanation undermines confidence in its reliability.
*   The paper lacks practical guidance on how to use the method effectively (e.g., choosing the number and placement of inducing points).

In summary, the paper introduces a very promising theoretical idea for scalable DPP inference. However, the current manuscript feels like a preliminary report rather than a complete piece of work. The weaknesses in the experimental validation and the lack of practical guidance significantly detract from the impact of the contribution. If the authors were to substantially strengthen the empirical section with thorough comparisons and analysis as suggested above, the paper would be a much stronger candidate for acceptance.
