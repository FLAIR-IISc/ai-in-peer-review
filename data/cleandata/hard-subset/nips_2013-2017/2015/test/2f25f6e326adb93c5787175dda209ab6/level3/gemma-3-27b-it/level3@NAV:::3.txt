## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of performing likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without relying on computationally expensive spectral decompositions. The authors propose using inducing inputs to bound the DPP likelihood, enabling both variational inference and approximate Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). This is a valuable contribution as spectral decomposition is a significant bottleneck for large-scale DPP applications. The approach builds on existing work in sparse Gaussian processes, extending it to the DPP setting.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the difficulties of standard DPP inference and the advantages of avoiding spectral methods. The proposed bounds and resulting inference methods are logically presented. However, the technical description feels somewhat rough around the edges. For example, the introduction of matrices *K* and *L* in Section 2.1 occurs without prior definition, requiring the reader to infer their meaning from context (marginal kernel and L-ensembles). The connection between the proposed variational inference and the accuracy of the approximation is not thoroughly discussed; the observation in the toy example that variational inference exhibits large parameter estimation error is concerning, and the reliance on approximate MCMC to "correct" this is not fully justified. A clearer explanation of *when* the variational approach is expected to succeed or fail, and *why* this occurs, is crucial. 

The empirical evaluation is insufficient. While the toy example demonstrates the MCMC method's ability to recover ground truth parameters, it doesn’t explore the sensitivity to inducing point placement. Specifically, how do the methods perform when the inducing inputs are chosen to be the actual sampled points? Furthermore, there's a lack of comparison to other approximate and exact DPP inference methods in terms of both approximation quality and runtime. The diabetic neuropathy dataset provides a more realistic example, but a quantitative comparison of runtime with existing methods (like the spectral methods in [7]) is missing. 

Regarding practical considerations, the paper lacks guidance for selecting the number of inducing points and their initialization/placement. Providing some rule-of-thumb advice would significantly enhance the paper's usability. Finally, the discussion of advantages from directly learning marginal kernel parameters (early in Section 2) feels somewhat contradictory to the paper’s approach and could be streamlined.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in DPP inference, proposing a potentially impactful method to bypass spectral decomposition.
**Arguments against Acceptance:** The method description lacks clarity, the empirical evaluation is weak, and key questions regarding the method’s limitations and practical considerations remain unanswered.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors need to significantly improve the clarity of the method description, strengthen the empirical evaluation with comprehensive comparisons, and address the open questions raised in this review. If these issues are addressed, the paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field.
