This paper proposes novel, cheap-to-evaluate, nonspectral bounds on the determinants arising in the likelihoods of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), both finite and continuous. The authors demonstrate how to use these bounds to infer the parameters of a DPP, and apply them to expensive-but-exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and cheap-but-approximate variational inference.

The main idea of the paper is to avoid the spectral decomposition of the operator L, which is often intractable in practice, by using inducing inputs to bound the likelihood. The authors draw inspiration from sparse approximations to Gaussian processes using inducing variables and extend these bounds to DPPs over continuous domains. The proposed bounds are cheap to evaluate and do not rely on approximating the spectrum of a large matrix or an operator.

The paper is well-motivated, and the proposed approach is a significant contribution to the field of DPPs. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to DPPs, their properties, and the challenges of likelihood-based inference. The technical sections are well-organized, and the proofs of the propositions are provided in the supplementary material.

However, the method description could be improved. The authors assume a high level of familiarity with DPPs, Gaussian processes, and operator theory, which might make the paper difficult to follow for readers without a strong background in these areas. Additionally, the empirical evaluation is limited, and the authors do not provide a thorough comparison of their approach with other approximate and exact alternatives.

The paper presents both variational inference and approximate MCMC, but the variational inference shows large parameter estimation error on toy data and relies on approximate MCMC. It is unclear when variational inference is expected to work or fail and why. The authors should provide more guidance on the strengths and limitations of their approach.

The empirical evaluation is insufficient, and the authors do not provide a thorough comparison of their approach with other approximate and exact alternatives. The authors should provide a more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons of computational complexity and wall-clock time between the two proposed methods.

Furthermore, the authors do not provide rule-of-thumb guidance for selecting the number of inducing points and their initialization/placement. This is an important aspect of the proposed approach, and the authors should provide more guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to DPPs. However, the method description could be improved, and the empirical evaluation is limited. The paper has merit, but the authors should address the above-mentioned concerns to strengthen their contribution.

In terms of significance, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of DPPs. The proposed approach is novel and has the potential to impact the field of machine learning and statistics. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several datasets, including a real-world dataset of spatial patterns of nerve fibers in diabetic patients.

In terms of originality, the paper is original and presents a new approach to likelihood-based inference for DPPs. The authors draw inspiration from sparse approximations to Gaussian processes, but their approach is novel and has not been previously proposed.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but the method description could be improved. The authors assume a high level of familiarity with DPPs, Gaussian processes, and operator theory, which might make the paper difficult to follow for readers without a strong background in these areas.

Overall, the paper has merit, but the authors should address the above-mentioned concerns to strengthen their contribution. With improvements to the method description, empirical evaluation, and guidance on parameter selection, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact on the field of DPPs.
