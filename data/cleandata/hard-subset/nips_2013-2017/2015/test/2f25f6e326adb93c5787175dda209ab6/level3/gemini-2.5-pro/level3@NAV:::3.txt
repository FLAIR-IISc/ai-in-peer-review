This paper addresses the computationally challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). Standard methods are often intractable for large datasets as they require an expensive spectral decomposition to compute the determinant `det(L+I)`. The authors propose a novel approach, inspired by sparse approximations in Gaussian processes, that uses inducing inputs to derive cheap-to-evaluate upper and lower bounds on this determinant. These non-spectral bounds are then used to develop two inference schemes: a fast approximate method based on variational inference (VI) and a more exact approximate MCMC algorithm.

The core contribution—bypassing spectral decomposition for DPP inference via inducing point bounds—is original and significant. It offers a potentially powerful and scalable alternative to existing methods, especially for continuous DPPs where spectral methods are limited. The connection drawn to sparse GPs is elegant and provides a clear theoretical foundation.

However, the paper in its current form is undermined by an underdeveloped methodological description and an insufficient empirical evaluation. The primary weaknesses are:

**Major Issues:**
1.  **Empirical Baselines:** The experimental section lacks critical comparisons. The two proposed methods (VI and MCMC) are not compared against each other in terms of computational complexity or wall-clock time. More importantly, there are no comparisons to existing approximate (e.g., Nyström, spectral bounds from [7]) or exact DPP inference methods. Without these benchmarks, it is impossible to assess the trade-offs in approximation quality and runtime that the proposed methods offer.

2.  **Variational Inference Performance:** The authors report that their VI method yields "poorly consistent results" on the toy example, with large parameter estimation errors. This is a significant concern. The paper must provide a deeper analysis of why the VI approach fails in this case and clarify the conditions under which it is expected to succeed. As presented, it is unclear when a practitioner should trust the VI results.

3.  **Guidance on Inducing Inputs:** The performance of the method hinges on the number and placement of inducing inputs. The paper provides no practical guidance or rule-of-thumb for setting these crucial hyperparameters. The authors should include an analysis of the method's sensitivity to the number of inducing points and explore strategies for their initialization and placement. For instance, how do the methods perform on the toy example if the inducing inputs are initialized to the observed data points?

**Minor Issues:**
*   **Clarity:** The exposition would benefit from brief definitions of the marginal kernel K and L-ensembles for readers not deeply familiar with DPP literature.
*   **Narrative:** The introduction discusses the interpretability advantages of parameterizing the kernel K, but the paper's methods, like prior work, parameterize L. This discussion could be streamlined to avoid inconsistency.

**Recommendation:**
While the central idea has substantial merit, the paper is not yet ready for acceptance. I recommend major revisions. The authors should substantially strengthen the empirical evaluation with the suggested comparisons and provide a more rigorous analysis of their proposed methods, particularly the failure modes of the variational approach.
