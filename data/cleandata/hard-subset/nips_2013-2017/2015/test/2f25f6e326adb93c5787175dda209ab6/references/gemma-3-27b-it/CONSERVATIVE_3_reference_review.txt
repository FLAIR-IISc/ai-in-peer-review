This paper addresses the challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition, a major computational bottleneck. The authors propose bounds on the DPP likelihood inspired by variational inference in Gaussian Processes, extending these bounds to both finite and continuous domains. They demonstrate the applicability of these bounds within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, providing a potential pathway to scalable DPP inference.

The core idea of bounding the likelihood without spectral decomposition is appealing and tackles a significant practical limitation of DPP modeling. The connection to variational inference for GPs is a reasonable and logical starting point. The extension to continuous DPPs, while mathematically more involved, demonstrates a commendable effort towards generality. The experimental validation, including a synthetic example and a real-world dataset, provides some initial evidence of the method's functionality.

However, despite the appealing premise, several significant weaknesses raise concerns. First, the paper heavily relies on existing work in DPPs and GPs without presenting substantial novelty in the bounding technique itself. The proposition extending GP bounds to DPPs feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The key inequality used (Theorem 1 from [14]) is cited but not explained or justified in detail within the context of DPPs – why *this* inequality is particularly well-suited, or how it differs in application, is unclear. 

Second, the experimental section, while present, is not convincing. The synthetic experiment, although showing agreement with known results when MCMC is employed, highlights a fundamental issue: the parameters of the kernel *L* are poorly identifiable, even with the proposed methods. The authors acknowledge this, but the implications are downplayed. The variational inference results on the diabetic neuropathy dataset are presented without a clear comparison to existing methods (e.g., those utilizing spectral approximations when feasible). Simply showing convergence of the bounds and overdispersion measures isn't sufficient; a quantitative comparison of performance (e.g., likelihood values, predictive accuracy) is necessary. The claim that the method "provides similar estimates" to [7] lacks concrete evidence. Furthermore, the discussion of the optimized pseudo-inputs feels exploratory rather than analytical; why do they arrange in grids, and what does this tell us about the approximation quality?

Third, the presentation lacks clarity in several places. The notation is dense, and the transition between finite and continuous DPPs feels abrupt. The motivation for directly parametrizing *L* instead of *K* is reiterated but not sufficiently justified. While acknowledging the interpretability issues, the paper doesn’t fully explore alternative parameterizations or their potential benefits. The connection between the retrospective sampling and the bounds is also not presented clearly.

Arguments for acceptance:
* Addresses a relevant and practical problem in DPP inference.
* Presents a logically sound approach based on existing variational inference techniques.
* Demonstrates applicability to both finite and continuous DPPs.

Arguments against acceptance:
* Limited novelty in the core bounding technique.
* Weak experimental validation with insufficient comparison to baselines.
* Poor parameter identifiability highlighted by the synthetic experiment.
* Lack of clarity in presentation and insufficient justification of key design choices.
* The connection to existing theoretical results is stated, but not explained in detail.

Overall, this paper presents a reasonable, but ultimately incremental, contribution. The proposed bounds offer a potential workaround for spectral decomposition, but the lack of compelling experimental results and the challenges with parameter identifiability raise significant concerns. The paper, as it stands, does not demonstrate a substantial advance over existing methods and falls short of the high standards expected at NIPS. I recommend rejection. A major revision addressing the experimental weaknesses, providing a more thorough theoretical justification, and clarifying the presentation would be necessary for reconsideration.
