This paper addresses the challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition. The core idea is to derive bounds on the DPP likelihood that circumvent the computational bottleneck of eigendecomposition, utilizing inspiration from variational inference in Gaussian Processes. The authors present both theoretical results (Propositions 1 & 2) for finite and continuous DPPs, along with demonstrations of their applicability in variational inference and MCMC settings.

The paper is meticulously written, and the authors demonstrate a strong understanding of DPPs and related inference techniques. The connection drawn to variational GP inference is insightful, and the extension to continuous domains is a notable contribution. The inclusion of a toy example with known spectral properties allows for validation of the MCMC sampler, a commendable practice. The experimental section, encompassing both a synthetic dataset and a real-world application (diabetic neuropathy), reinforces the practicality of the proposed methods.

However, despite the technical soundness and novelty of the approach, I find several aspects of the paper concerning, leading me to recommend rejection. My concerns primarily stem from a perceived overemphasis on minor methodological details at the expense of broader justification and practical limitations. 

Here's a detailed breakdown of strengths and weaknesses:

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core contribution—non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihoods—is genuinely novel and addresses a significant computational hurdle in DPP inference.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations appear correct (though a more detailed proof verification would be ideal), and the connection to existing GP variational inference techniques is well-motivated.
*   **Clarity (Mostly):** The paper is generally well-written, and the authors attempt to explain the concepts clearly. Section 4, however, feels rushed and could benefit from more detailed explanations of the assumptions and notation.
*   **Validation:** The inclusion of a synthetic experiment with a known ground truth for MCMC validation is a strong point. The real-world application also provides some evidence of practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Interpretability (Repeatedly Highlighted, Yet Unaddressed):** The authors repeatedly acknowledge the loss of interpretability when parametrizing L instead of K. While recognizing this is important, there's no substantial discussion on mitigating this issue or exploring avenues for recovering some interpretability. This feels like a fundamental trade-off that deserves more attention.
*   **Computational Cost – Bound Tightness:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a significant improvement, the bounds themselves rely on optimization over inducing inputs (Z). The authors state "the denominator in the likelihood (15) small," hinting at a cost-benefit balance but this is not quantified. The experiments show that the number of inducing variables (m) needs to be relatively high (up to 1200) to achieve convergence. The reported runtime for m=1200 (24 minutes) seems manageable, but it's unclear how this scales with problem size and dimensionality. Is this *truly* cheaper than spectral methods for moderate n?
*   **Overly Detailed Methodological Focus:** The paper dwells extensively on methodological details, such as the precise optimization algorithm used (CMA-ES) and the adaptive Metropolis algorithm. While thoroughness is admirable, this level of detail feels excessive for a NIPS paper. More space should have been devoted to discussing the theoretical implications of the bounds and their limitations.
*   **Limited Discussion of Prior Choice:** The choice of prior distributions, particularly in the MCMC experiment, is described briefly. The impact of prior selection on the results isn’t adequately discussed. A sensitivity analysis with respect to the prior would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Section 4 – Lack of Justification & Concrete Results:** Section 4, dealing with continuous DPPs, feels underdeveloped. The assumptions required for the results to hold are stated but not convincingly justified. Furthermore, there are no experimental results for the continuous case, leaving the reader wondering about the practical applicability of Proposition 2.
*   **Minor Formatting & Typographical Issues:** Numerous minor typos and formatting inconsistencies are present throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of subscripts, occasional grammatical errors). While not critical, these detract from the overall polish of the manuscript.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel theoretical contribution addressing a practical bottleneck in DPP inference.
*   Sound mathematical foundation and connection to established techniques.
*   Demonstrated applicability on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Con:**

*   Loss of parameter interpretability without adequate discussion or mitigation strategies.
*   Potential computational cost of optimizing inducing inputs.
*   Overemphasis on methodological details at the expense of broader theoretical discussion.
*   Underdeveloped section on continuous DPPs with no experimental validation.
*   Numerous minor formatting and typographical errors.



**Overall:**

While the paper presents a technically sound and novel approach, the concerns outlined above, particularly the lack of a thorough discussion of the trade-offs and practical limitations, coupled with the excessive focus on implementation details, lead me to believe that it does not meet the high standards for acceptance at NIPS. The paper feels like a “proof of concept” rather than a fully developed contribution. I believe significant revisions, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, would be required before this paper could be considered for publication.
