Summary
The paper proposes nonspectral bounds for the DPP likelihood normalizer det(I+L) that avoid eigen-decompositions. For finite domains, the authors introduce an inducing-point approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY and derive bounds on 1/det(I+L) based on determinant inequalities (Seiler–Simon). They plug these into (i) a variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood by replacing log det(I+L) with a tractable surrogate, and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler that refines the bounds by increasing the number of pseudo-inputs m until an accept/reject decision is possible. They extend the bounds to continuous domains using Fredholm determinants, again yielding computable upper/lower bounds provided certain integrals Ψ are available (e.g., Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure). Experiments include a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian toy where spectral quantities are known (validating the MH approach) and a diabetic neuropathy dataset (2D continuous DPP) where the variational approach suggests class-separated overdispersion parameters and convergence as m grows.

Relation to prior work
The approach is clearly inspired by sparse GP variational methods with inducing points (e.g., Titsias AISTATS’09). For DPPs, prior likelihood-based methods often use spectral information (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’13; Bornemann for Fredholm determinants), EM/fixed-point methods (Gillenwater et al.; Mariet & Sra), and Nyström approximations. The main novelty here is bounding det(I+L) without any spectrum using inducing points, and adapting retrospective MH to these bounds.

Strengths
- Addresses a real bottleneck in DPP inference: dependence on spectra that are expensive (finite) or unavailable (continuous).
- The bounds are simple to compute, conceptually appealing, and applicable to both finite and continuous settings.
- The retrospective MH adaptation is reasonable; the toy experiment with known ground truth is a sanity check.
- The variational scheme scales with m and shows monotonic tightening of upper/lower bounds empirically as m increases.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited empirical validation and lack of strong baselines:
  - No finite-domain experiments, despite substantial motivation from discrete DPP applications where eigen-decompositions are expensive.
  - No comparisons against spectral-bound MH (Affandi et al.), EM/fixed-point methods, Nyström approximations, or numerical Fredholm determinant methods (Bornemann) where applicable.
  - No held-out log-likelihoods or predictive assessments; metrics are mostly parameter point estimates and visual convergence of bounds.
  - Scalability claims are not substantiated with larger n or higher dimensions, nor with thorough runtime–accuracy tradeoffs vs baselines.
- Tightness and convergence of bounds:
  - The paper asserts that bounds improve with m and hints at GP posterior variance arguments, but provides no formal result (rates or conditions) ensuring that the upper and lower bounds converge to the true determinant/log-likelihood as m→∞. This is crucial if these bounds are to replace spectral methods.
  - No quantitative assessment of the gap as a function of m or kernel properties (e.g., smoothness, effective rank).
- Optimization over inducing points Z:
  - The method hinges on optimizing Z, yet there is little guidance on initialization, gradients, or regularization, and no analysis of nonconvexity or sensitivity. Reliance on CMA-ES for core optimization raises stability and reproducibility concerns.
  - For MH, optimizing Z at each proposal can be computationally heavy; the paper does not report the overhead or provide guarantees that the refinement will terminate quickly in challenging settings.
- Assumptions for the continuous case:
  - The required conditions (trace-class L, computable Ψ) can be restrictive. Beyond the Gaussian–Gaussian case, it is unclear how broadly applicable the continuous bounds are. More examples and practical guidance are needed.
- Clarity and rigor:
  - Some notation and assumptions are underspecified (e.g., L PSD vs PD; when (finite) determinants vs Fredholm determinants are well-defined).
  - The main proofs are deferred to the supplement; at least a sketch and a precise statement of conditions for Prop. 1–2 in the main text would improve self-containment.
  - References have missing years and some formatting/numbering issues.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a formal statement (and proof sketch) that, under explicit conditions on L and µ, the bounds in Prop. 1–2 tighten to equality as m→∞? Any rate depending on kernel eigenvalue decay?
- How are Z initialized and optimized in practice? Do you use gradients w.r.t. Z and θ? If so, please provide expressions and complexity; if not, why not?
- What is the computational overhead of the retrospective MH refinement per iteration (distribution of m used, wall-clock) on the toy and a higher-dimensional example?
- Can you report quantitative comparisons (runtime vs error in log-likelihood/normalizer) against spectral-bound MH (Affandi et al.) on at least moderate finite problems, and against Bornemann’s quadrature for continuous problems where applicable?
- Do the finite-domain bounds yield competitive performance on standard ML DPP tasks (e.g., subset selection) at scale?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add finite-domain experiments with large n, comparing against spectral MCMC/EM/fixed-point/Nyström baselines, reporting held-out likelihood and runtime.
- Provide theoretical convergence guarantees for the bounds as m increases, or at least explicit conditions and empirical gap curves.
- Include gradient-based optimization details for Z and θ, with ablations comparing CMA-ES vs gradient methods and sensitivity to initialization.
- For the continuous case, broaden the set of (L, µ) where Ψ is tractable, or propose practical approximations with error control.
- Improve clarity of assumptions, notation, and provide a proof sketch in the main text. Release code to support adoption.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current paper lacks rigorous validation against established baselines, formal convergence/error guarantees for the bounds, and finite-domain demonstrations that are central to ML DPP applications. With stronger theory and substantially expanded experiments, this work could become a solid contribution.

Confidence
High.
