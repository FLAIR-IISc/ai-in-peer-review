This paper tackles a significant practical bottleneck in applying Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): avoiding spectral decomposition. The core idea – bounding the DPP likelihood without explicit eigenvalue computation, inspired by variational Gaussian process techniques – is genuinely novel and potentially impactful. While the technical details aren’t groundbreakingly complex, the application to DPPs is, to my knowledge, new, and the extension to continuous domains is a nice addition. The authors clearly articulate the problem and the motivation, and connect their work to existing literature in both DPPs and Gaussian processes.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The central contribution—nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods—is a significant step toward making DPP inference more tractable, especially for large-scale applications. This directly addresses a major limitation of existing methods.
*   **Impact:** The potential impact is high. Removing the spectral decomposition requirement opens up DPP modeling to problems previously inaccessible due to computational constraints.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly explains *why* this problem is important, grounding the work in practical applications.
*   **Connection to Variational Inference & MCMC:**  Demonstrating how these bounds can be used within standard inference paradigms (VI and MCMC) is a practical and valuable contribution.
*   **Continuous Domain Extension:** Extending the bounds to continuous DPPs strengthens the generality and usefulness of the proposed approach.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while relatively simple, demonstrate the method's viability. The results on the diabetic neuropathy dataset are particularly encouraging, showing convergence with increasing inducing points.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Depth:** The theoretical justification for the bounds, while present, feels somewhat light. A deeper exploration of the conditions under which these bounds are tight, or how the approximation error scales with the number of inducing points, would be beneficial.
*   **Parameter Identifiability:** The toy example highlights a potential issue with direct parametrization of the kernel *L*:  difficulties in uniquely identifying parameters like κ. This is acknowledged by the authors, but could be discussed in greater detail.
*   **Limited Experimentation:** The experiments are limited in scope. More extensive evaluations on a wider range of datasets and DPP kernel choices would strengthen the claims. While the diabetic neuropathy example is good, a comparison against existing DPP inference methods (even approximate ones) would be valuable.
*   **Computational Complexity Analysis:** While the authors mention improved computational cost, a more detailed analysis of the complexity of the proposed methods compared to existing approaches (e.g., power iteration) would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is genuinely novel and tackles a significant bottleneck in DPP inference.
*   The proposed bounds provide a practical pathway to applying DPPs to larger and more complex problems.
*   The experimental results, while preliminary, are promising.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem, solution, and potential impact.
*   The extension to continuous DPPs increases the generality of the approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification for the bounds could be more rigorous.
*   The experimental validation is limited and lacks comparison to other methods.
*   Parameter identifiability issues with direct kernel L parametrization are acknowledged but not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP modeling. The novelty of the approach, combined with its potential for practical impact, outweighs the minor weaknesses. While further theoretical analysis and more extensive experimentation would be beneficial, the paper, as it stands, represents a significant step forward. I believe this work is suitable for NIPS, and I recommend **acceptance**.  I particularly value the boldness of the approach; the authors directly address a fundamental limitation of DPP inference and propose a pragmatic, workable solution. The willingness to circumvent spectral decomposition, even at the cost of interpretability, is a refreshing and potentially paradigm-shifting move. I would encourage the authors to address the theoretical and experimental limitations in future work.
