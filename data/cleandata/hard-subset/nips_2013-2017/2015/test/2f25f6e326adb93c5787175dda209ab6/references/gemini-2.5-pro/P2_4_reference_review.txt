### **Summary**

This paper addresses a significant bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the computation of the determinant term `det(L + I)`, which typically requires an expensive `O(n^3)` spectral decomposition for finite DPPs or is intractable for continuous DPPs. The authors propose novel upper and lower bounds on this term that, crucially, do not require any spectral information. The proposed bounds are derived using a set of "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points", drawing a strong and elegant analogy to variational sparse approximations for Gaussian Processes (GPs). The paper demonstrates how these bounds can be used to enable two standard inference schemes: a fast, approximate variational inference (VI) method that maximizes the lower bound, and a more expensive but asymptotically exact MCMC method using retrospective sampling. A key strength of the work is the extension of these ideas from the finite case to DPPs over continuous domains, which significantly broadens the applicability of the method. The effectiveness of the proposed techniques is demonstrated on both a synthetic 1D problem and a real-world 2D spatial statistics dataset.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed bounds are derived from a non-trivial matrix inequality (Seiler & Simon, 2005), and their application to the DPP likelihood is sound. The extension to the continuous case, involving Fredholm determinants and trace-class operators, is handled carefully and adds significant depth to the contribution. The experimental evaluation is well-designed. In the toy experiment, the authors honestly report the limitations of the VI approach (inconsistent results) and demonstrate that their MCMC sampler correctly recovers the posterior of identifiable parameters, matching an ideal sampler that has access to the true likelihood. The real-world experiment on the neuropathy dataset provides a compelling case for the method's utility, showing that parameter estimates converge as the number of inducing points increases, and that the upper and lower bounds on the log-likelihood approach each other, providing a clear diagnostic for the approximation quality.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The structure—introducing the problem, defining the bounds for the simpler finite case, demonstrating their use in inference, and then generalizing to the continuous case—is very effective. The core idea is made highly accessible by repeatedly drawing the parallel to sparse GPs and inducing variables, a concept familiar to much of the NIPS audience. The notation is consistent and the mathematical developments are easy to follow. The figures, particularly those showing the convergence of the bounds and parameter estimates in the real-world experiment, are very effective at illustrating the method's behavior.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While using inducing points for low-rank approximations is a well-established technique in the GP literature (e.g., Titsias, 2009), its application to derive *non-spectral* bounds for the DPP likelihood is novel and insightful. Previous work on scalable DPP inference, such as Affandi et al. (2014) [7], still relied on partial spectral knowledge (e.g., power iteration to find top eigenvalues), which is computationally demanding and primarily restricted to the finite case. This paper's approach completely bypasses the need for eigendecomposition, representing a significant conceptual and practical advance. The unification of the finite and continuous cases under the same inducing-point framework is also a novel contribution.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact. DPPs are a powerful tool for modeling diversity and repulsion, but their practical application has been hampered by computational scaling issues. By providing a principled, scalable, and general-purpose inference framework that works for both finite and continuous DPPs, this work lowers the barrier to entry for practitioners and researchers. The connection to sparse GPs is also significant, as it opens the door to transferring the vast body of research on scalable GPs (e.g., stochastic variational inference, GPU implementation) to the DPP setting. The proposed methods enable the application of DPPs to larger datasets and more complex problems than were previously feasible with likelihood-based approaches.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Comparison with Spectral Methods:** The main weakness of the experimental section is the lack of a direct comparison with the most relevant baseline, Affandi et al. [7]. While the authors correctly state the limitations of that work (requires power iterations, limited to finite domains), a quantitative comparison on a finite problem would be very valuable. For instance, a plot of estimation accuracy versus wall-clock time for both methods would clearly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed non-spectral approach.

*   **Optimization of Inducing Inputs:** The paper mentions that the inducing inputs `Z` can be optimized, for example, using gradient-based methods. For completeness and reproducibility, it would be helpful to provide the gradients of the variational lower bound `F(θ, Z)` with respect to the locations of the inducing inputs `Z`. This is a non-trivial detail that would strengthen the paper.

*   **The L vs. K Parameterization Issue:** The authors correctly identify that parameterizing the L-kernel for tractability comes at the cost of interpretability compared to the K-kernel. Their MCMC experiment provides a clear illustration of this, showing the scaling parameter κ is non-identifiable. While this is a known issue in the literature and not a flaw of the proposed inference method itself, the paper could benefit from a slightly more extended discussion on the practical implications. For example, does the non-identifiability of one parameter affect the posterior uncertainty of the others? The results suggest not, but a brief comment would be welcome.

*   **Choice of `m`:** The paper suggests a practical heuristic for choosing the number of inducing points `m`: increase it until the results stabilize. This is reasonable, but could the authors comment on whether the gap between the upper and lower bounds `(b+ - b-)` could be used as a more formal guide, perhaps in an adaptive scheme for the MCMC algorithm?

### **Final Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling. It solves a long-standing computational problem for an important class of models (DPPs) with an elegant, well-grounded, and generalizable solution. The work is technically strong, clearly presented, and highly significant. Despite the minor weakness of not including a direct experimental comparison to the state-of-the-art spectral method, the conceptual advance is so clear that the paper's value is undisputed. It bridges the gap between DPPs and the mature field of scalable GPs, which is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up work.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
