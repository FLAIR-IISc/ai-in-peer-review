- Proposes bounds on the DPP likelihood that avoid costly spectral decomposition, inspired by variational GP methods.
- Applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs and usable within variational inference and MCMC frameworks.
- Demonstrates effectiveness on synthetic data and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset, with MCMC details well-explained.
- Provides solid theoretical grounding for the bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) using determinant inequalities.
- Clearly connects the approach to variational inference literature in Gaussian Processes, aiding intuition for readers familiar with GPs.
- Authors acknowledge interpretability issues when directly parameterizing the L kernel.
- Core inequality and proofs are complex and relegated to supplementary material, lacking a more intuitive explanation.
- Direct parameterization of L leads to identifiability issues (e.g., κ in the Gaussian experiment), which may limit practical usefulness.
- Optimization of pseudo-inputs (Z) introduces a new computational cost that replaces spectral cost but is not deeply analyzed for tradeoffs.
- Use of variational inference with bounds is relatively standard, making that part of the contribution feel incremental.
- Paper is technically sound, derivations appear correct, and experiments support claims.
- Writing and organization are clear, though the determinant inequality needs more accessible explanation.
- The core idea of avoiding spectral decomposition via bounds is novel and significant for scaling DPPs.
- Removing the spectral decomposition bottleneck could broaden DPP applicability and impact the field.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses major practical limitation, solid theory, practical implementation, and promising experiments.
- Arguments against acceptance: parameter identifiability concerns, added computational cost from pseudo-input optimization, and incremental novelty in the variational component.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — contribution is significant despite noted weaknesses.
