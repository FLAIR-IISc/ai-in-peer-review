## NIPS Review: Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors propose and analyze bounds on the DPP likelihood that *do not* require this decomposition, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques used in Gaussian Processes. They demonstrate the application of these bounds in both variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) settings, extending the results to continuous DPPs. The work provides a practical approach for inference with DPPs, particularly in high-dimensional settings where spectral decomposition is prohibitive.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the established theory of DPPs (Macchi, Hough et al.) and their applications in machine learning (Kulesza & Taskar, Affandi et al.). It acknowledges the common practice of parametrizing the *L* kernel instead of the more interpretable *K* kernel for tractability (Affandi et al., Kulesza & Taskar). The bounds presented are motivated by existing techniques for approximating GPs (Titsias), and the use of retrospective sampling within MCMC builds upon work in spectral DPP inference (Affandi et al.).  The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature, clearly highlighting the gap it addresses – efficient inference *without* spectral decomposition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a significant practical limitation:** The need for spectral decomposition is a major hurdle in applying DPPs to large datasets. This paper offers a viable solution.
*   **Sound theoretical foundation:** The derivation of bounds leverages existing mathematical results (determinant inequalities) and provides a rigorous basis for the proposed methods.
*   **Versatility:** The bounds are applicable to both discrete and continuous DPPs, extending the usefulness of the approach.
*   **Connections to existing inference techniques:** Integrating the bounds with variational inference and MCMC provides practical algorithms for parameter estimation.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The 1D Gaussian example successfully recovers the ground truth parameters (except for the scale parameter, which the authors acknowledge), and the diabetic neuropathy dataset shows promising results with converging estimates as the number of inducing points increases.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and algorithmic details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Interpretability of *L* parameters:**  While the paper acknowledges the trade-off between tractability and interpretability when parametrizing *L* directly, it doesn’t fully explore methods to improve the interpretability of the resulting parameters. The 1D Gaussian experiment confirms this issue, where the scale parameter is unidentifiable.
*   **Computational Cost of Pseudo-input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, optimizing the pseudo-inputs *Z* adds a computational cost. The paper doesn’t fully analyze the scaling of this optimization with dataset size or the number of parameters. The experiments suggest this isn't prohibitive, but a more detailed analysis would be valuable.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are somewhat limited. The 1D Gaussian example is useful for validation but lacks the complexity of real-world datasets. While the diabetic neuropathy dataset is a good starting point, more diverse applications could strengthen the paper.
* **Lack of comparison to other approximate inference methods:** The paper would benefit from comparing the performance of their proposed methods to other state-of-the-art approximate inference techniques for DPPs, like those based on stochastic variational inference or other sampling techniques.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and provides a well-supported solution to a significant problem. The proofs appear rigorous (though a closer inspection in the supplementary material would be ideal).  It’s a complete piece of work given its scope.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written, although some sections (particularly those involving operator theory in Section 4) could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
*   **Originality:** The application of determinant bounds for DPP inference is novel and represents a significant contribution. While the underlying mathematical tools are not entirely new, their adaptation to DPPs is original.
*   **Significance:** This work has the potential to make DPPs more accessible for large-scale applications. By removing the spectral decomposition bottleneck, it opens up new possibilities for using DPPs in diverse fields.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*   Solves a critical computational problem for DPPs.
*   Theoretically sound and well-motivated.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness through experiments.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of parameter interpretability with *L* kernel parametrization.
*   Computational cost of pseudo-input optimization is not fully analyzed.
*   Experiments could be more diverse and comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a **strong paper with significant merit**. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. The paper addresses a crucial limitation of DPPs and provides a practical solution with a solid theoretical foundation. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. I recommend the authors address the parameter interpretability issue, provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of pseudo-input optimization, and consider expanding the experimental evaluation.
