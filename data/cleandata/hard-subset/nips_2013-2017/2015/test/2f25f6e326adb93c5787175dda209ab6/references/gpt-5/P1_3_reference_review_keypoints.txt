- Proposes nonspectral, inducing-point-based upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) for finite and continuous DPPs, avoiding eigenvalue decompositions.
- For finite DPPs: constructs Q from inducing inputs Z and shows det(Q+I) e^{−tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I).
- For continuous DPPs: gives analogous Fredholm-determinant bounds using LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x).
- Enables a variational lower bound on DPP log-likelihood computable in O(n m^2) with m inducing points.
- Introduces a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that tightens bounds by increasing m and optimizing Z until accept/reject is decided.
- Approach adapts sparse GP variational ideas (Titsias 2009) to DPP determinants, explicitly avoiding spectral computations.
- Builds on determinant inequalities (Seiler & Simon, 1975) to produce DPP-specific likelihood bounds.
- Continuous-case methods rely on standard operator theory (Fredholm determinants and traces) and require Ψ to be computable.
- Conceptually clean and broadly applicable bounds that extend to continuous domains without eigenvalues.
- Provides practical inference: scalable VI objective and a sound, tighten-able retrospective MH.
- Strong theoretical grounding with clear construction of Q and operator-theoretic framing; proofs deferred but rely on established results.
- Empirical validation includes a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian example with known determinant and a real 2D neuropathy dataset.
- Real-data results show meaningful overdispersion estimates, convergence of bounds with m, and reasonable runtimes.
- Limited experimental scope: lacks large-scale finite-domain benchmarks and direct runtime/accuracy comparisons to spectral methods.
- Tightness properties of bounds are not quantified (no rates, monotonicity proofs w.r.t. m, or nontrivial convergence conditions).
- MCMC section relies on optimizing Z during acceptance tests without formal justification or citations to exact-approximate MH frameworks.
- Continuous-case practicality depends on closed-form Ψ; guidance for general kernels/measures (quadrature, randomized features) is sparse.
- Parameter identifiability issues when parameterizing L (e.g., κ) are noted but no remedies are proposed.
- Continuous-setting assumptions are reasonable but need clearer sufficient conditions and practical advice.
- Clarity is generally good; continuous section assumes operator familiarity and notation/equation referencing could be tightened.
- Originality: novel application of determinant inequalities to produce nonspectral likelihood bounds and continuous-domain extension.
- Significance: removing spectral decompositions is impactful for large discrete and continuous DPP learning; immediate utility for finite domains.
- Suggested improvements: prove/quantify monotonicity/tightness, formalize retrospective MH via recent literature, add large-scale benchmarks and spectral comparisons.
- Also suggest practical guidance for computing Ψ (quadrature, random features), sensitivity analyses for Z, and methods to address identifiability (reparameterizations/priors).
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept leaning accept — novel, well-founded, and practically promising but needs stronger empirical evaluation and theoretical tightness results.
