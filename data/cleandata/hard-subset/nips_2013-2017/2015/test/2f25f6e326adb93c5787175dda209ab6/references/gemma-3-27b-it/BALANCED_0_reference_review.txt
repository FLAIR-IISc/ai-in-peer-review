This paper addresses the problem of likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), focusing on avoiding the computationally expensive spectral decomposition of the operator L. The authors propose bounds on the DPP likelihood that do not rely on this decomposition, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques for Gaussian processes. They present both theoretical results for finite and continuous DPPs and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments.

The paper is well-motivated. DPPs are increasingly used in various applications, and the computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition is a significant obstacle to scaling these methods. The connection to variational inference, particularly the use of inducing variables, is a natural and promising approach. The extension to continuous DPPs is a valuable addition, broadening the applicability of the proposed method. The experiments, including a synthetic example and a real-world dataset, provide empirical validation of the method’s effectiveness. The inclusion of both variational and MCMC inference schemes offers flexibility for different application scenarios.

Strengths:
* **Addresses a significant bottleneck:** The paper tackles a key computational challenge in DPP inference.
* **Novel bounds:** The derived bounds on the likelihood are a significant contribution, providing a practical alternative to spectral decomposition.
* **Theoretical soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, with clear connections to existing literature on Gaussian processes and operator theory.
* **Comprehensive approach:** The paper covers both finite and continuous DPPs and explores both variational and MCMC inference.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the complex concepts accessible.

Weaknesses:
* **Parameter interpretability:** The authors acknowledge, and the experiments demonstrate, the limited interpretability of parameters when directly parametrizing L. This is a significant drawback, particularly in applications where understanding the underlying repulsive forces is important. While acknowledged, more discussion on potential strategies for addressing this limitation would be beneficial.
* **MCMC convergence:** The description of the MCMC method, particularly the “retrospective sampling” step, feels somewhat concise. More detailed explanation and perhaps some discussion of convergence diagnostics would strengthen the presentation.
* **Computational cost of bound tightening:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, the iterative refinement of the bounds in the MCMC section (increasing 'm') introduces its own computational cost. A more detailed analysis comparing this cost to the spectral decomposition, perhaps with specific examples, would be valuable.
* **Limited comparison to existing methods:** While the authors compare against an “ideal” MCMC method in the synthetic experiment, there is limited comparison to other existing approximation techniques for DPP inference. A more thorough comparison would better establish the relative advantages of the proposed method.
* **Figure clarity:** Some figures (e.g., Figure 1) could benefit from clearer labeling and annotations.

Arguments for acceptance:
* The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in DPP inference.
* The proposed bounds and inference methods are novel and theoretically sound.
* The experimental results demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

Arguments against acceptance:
* The lack of parameter interpretability is a significant limitation.
* The MCMC section could benefit from more detail and convergence analysis.
* The computational cost of bound tightening needs further investigation.
* The comparison to existing methods is limited.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of determinantal point processes. The proposed bounds offer a promising alternative to spectral decomposition, enabling more scalable and efficient inference. While the lack of parameter interpretability and some minor weaknesses in the presentation are concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh these drawbacks. 

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:
1. Expand the discussion of parameter interpretability and potential solutions.
2. Provide more detail on the MCMC implementation and convergence diagnostics.
3. Analyze the computational cost of bound tightening in more detail.
4. Include a more comprehensive comparison to existing approximation techniques for DPP inference.
5. Improve the clarity of the figures.
