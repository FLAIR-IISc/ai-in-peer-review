### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a major computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the evaluation of the term `det(L + I)`, which typically requires an expensive spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix L. The authors propose a novel approach to derive computationally cheap, non-spectral bounds on this determinant. The core idea is inspired by variational methods for sparse Gaussian processes (GPs), introducing a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" `Z`. These points are used to construct a low-rank approximation `Q` of the kernel matrix `L`, leading to both an upper and a lower bound on the likelihood term. This framework is developed for DPPs on both finite sets and continuous domains. The paper then demonstrates how these bounds can be effectively integrated into two standard inference paradigms: 1) a variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes the lower bound on the log-likelihood with respect to both kernel and variational parameters, and 2) a Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm that uses the bounds within a retrospective sampling scheme to perform Metropolis-Hastings without computing the exact likelihood. The utility of the proposed methods is demonstrated on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP (where the ground truth is known) and a real-world 2D spatial point process dataset of nerve fibers in diabetic patients.

### Strengths

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant and original contribution to the DPP literature. By introducing non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds, it provides a powerful new tool for scalable DPP inference. This moves beyond prior work (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014) that still relied on partial spectral information. The proposed method has the potential to make DPPs practical for a much wider range of large-scale problems.

2.  **Elegant Connection to Sparse GPs:** The work draws a clear and well-motivated connection between DPP inference and the widely successful literature on sparse approximations for Gaussian processes (e.g., Titsias, 2009). This cross-pollination of ideas is elegant and demonstrates a deep understanding of related probabilistic models. It provides a principled foundation for the proposed approximation.

3.  **Technical Soundness and Generality:** The paper is technically strong. The derivations of the bounds in Proposition 1 (finite case) and Proposition 2 (continuous case) are well-founded. The extension to continuous DPPs and Fredholm determinants is handled carefully, showing the generality of the approach beyond the simpler finite setting.

4.  **Comprehensive Application:** The authors don't just present the bounds; they thoroughly demonstrate their utility by developing both a VI and an MCMC inference algorithm. This is a major strength, as it provides two distinct recipes for practitioners, catering to different needs (speed vs. accuracy).

5.  **Well-Conducted Experiments:** The experimental validation is thoughtfully designed. The 1D toy experiment is crucial for validating the MCMC sampler against an "ideal" sampler where the true likelihood is computable. The real-world diabetic neuropathy experiment effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the VI approach, showing that the model can learn meaningful differences between classes and that the parameter estimates converge as the number of inducing points increases.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a very strong paper, there are a few areas where the analysis could be deepened to make it even more impactful.

1.  **Analysis of VI vs. MCMC Performance:** In the toy experiment (Section 5.1), the paper states that VI yields "poorly consistent results," whereas the MCMC approach works well. The discussion attributes this to identifiability issues with the parameter `kappa`. While this is a plausible explanation, a more in-depth analysis would be beneficial. Is the failure due to the looseness of the variational bound in this particular parameter regime, or is it an optimization problem where the joint optimization over `θ` and `Z` is prone to poor local optima? Visualizing the variational lower bound surface versus the true log-likelihood surface could provide valuable insight. This would help readers understand the potential failure modes of the VI approach and when to prefer the more robust (but expensive) MCMC alternative.

2.  **Direct Comparison with Spectral Baselines:** The primary motivation for this work is to provide an alternative to methods requiring spectral knowledge. However, the paper lacks a direct empirical comparison to such a baseline, for instance, the spectral-bound MCMC method of Affandi et al. (2014). Including a comparison on a moderately-sized problem (e.g., the diabetic neuropathy dataset) focusing on the trade-off between runtime, number of inducing points (`m`), and the quality of the likelihood approximation or posterior estimates would have made the advantages of the proposed non-spectral approach more concrete.

3.  **Optimization of Inducing Inputs:** The quality of the bounds depends critically on the placement of the inducing inputs `Z`. The paper mentions using CMA-ES or gradient-based methods, but provides limited detail on the practicalities of this optimization. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to initialization, the computational cost of optimizing `Z`, and strategies for setting the number of inducing points `m` (beyond the suggestion to increase it until results stabilize) would enhance the paper's practical value and reproducibility. The observation that optimized points form neat grids is fascinating and warrants a bit more discussion.

4.  **The L vs. K Parametrization Issue:** The paper correctly identifies and follows the standard practice of parametrizing the `L` kernel for tractability, at the cost of interpretability. While this is not a flaw of the proposed method, it would be interesting if the authors could briefly speculate in the discussion whether their bounding technique could offer a new angle on tackling inference for the more interpretable `K` kernel. This could point towards exciting avenues for future work.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, significant, and technically sound contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling. The proposed non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihoods, inspired by sparse GPs, are a powerful tool that significantly advances the state of the art in scalable DPP inference. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and supported by solid experimental evidence. The weaknesses are minor and represent opportunities for further strengthening the work rather than fundamental flaws. I strongly recommend acceptance.
