- Clear problem statement and motivation: identifies spectral decomposition as the key bottleneck in DPP inference and connects solution to sparse GP inducing-variable ideas, noting the trade-off of parametrizing L vs K.  
- Sound theoretical contribution: provides rigorous likelihood bounds (Propositions 1–2) using determinant inequalities and extends theory to continuous DPPs.  
- Practical validation: convincing experiments on synthetic and real data (notably diabetic neuropathy); shows MCMC recovers parameters where variational inference fails.  
- Limited comparison with prior work: lacks quantified computational-complexity comparison (e.g., O(nm²) vs O(n³)) and no table contrasting methods.  
- Insufficient analysis of pseudo-input selection: no sensitivity analysis on placement, initialization, or impact on bound tightness.  
- Underdeveloped continuous case: Section 4 is brief and doesn’t clarify the role of the q-Pochhammer symbol or connect clearly to the finite case.  
- Limited discussion of limitations: omits behavior under strong repulsiveness or ill-conditioned L and practical trade-offs between m and bound tightness.  
- Overall assessment: solid, technically sound contribution that removes a major computational bottleneck; weaknesses are addressable and not fatal.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Suggestion 1: add a table comparing computational complexity across DPP inference methods.  
- Suggestion 2: include a sensitivity experiment on pseudo-input initialization and placement.  
- Suggestion 3: expand Section 4 to better relate the continuous extension to the finite case and explain its implications.  
- Suggestion 4: discuss and empirically illustrate the trade-off between number of pseudo-inputs (m) and bound tightness.
