Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds on the DPP normalizing constant det(I+L) for both finite and continuous DPPs. For finite domains, given inducing inputs Z and the low-rank surrogate Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, they prove det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I). For continuous DPPs (with base measure µ and trace-class L), they derive analogous bounds for the Fredholm determinant involving LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x). These bounds enable (i) a variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood by replacing −log det(I+L) with −log det(Q+I)+tr(L−Q), and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that adaptively tightens acceptance probability bounds by increasing m and optimizing Z, avoiding eigen decompositions entirely.

The approach is explicitly inspired by variational sparse GP methods (Titsias, 2009), but applied to the determinant term of DPP likelihoods, and it contrasts with prior DPP inference relying on partial spectra (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’14), which can be expensive or unavailable in continuous settings. The paper also relates to quadrature-based Fredholm determinant approximations (Bornemann, 2010), positioning its contribution as a non-spectral, kernel-based alternative with tunable accuracy via m and Z.

Quality
- Theory: The finite-domain determinant bounds follow from Seiler–Simon determinant inequalities and the low-rank projector Q, with a clean complexity profile O(n m^2) once kernel evaluations are available. The variational lower bound for the log-likelihood is immediate from the lower bound on 1/det(I+L). For continuous DPPs, the operator assumptions (trace-class, self-adjoint) are reasonable and the bound reduces to computable quantities for Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures. While detailed proofs are deferred to the supplement, the statements are plausible and consistent with known determinant inequalities and GP analogies. A discussion of tightness as m→∞ and when Z is optimized is provided informally; a more formal characterization of convergence rates would strengthen the work.
- Algorithms: Using the bounds in a retrospective MH scheme is standard and correct provided the bounds are valid for any Z and can be refined arbitrarily; the paper mirrors the logic of [Affandi et al., 2014] but replaces spectral bounds with inducing-point bounds. One subtlety is that Z is optimized per θ and θ′; because Z only tightens bounds and does not change the target, this does not bias MH, but a brief discussion of measurability/adaptivity (as in retrospective sampling literature) would help reassure readers.
- Experiments: The 1D Gaussian–Gaussian example is a good sanity check: it validates retrospective MH against an “ideal” method where the Fredholm determinant is computable, and it illustrates identifiability (κ weakly informed) and the advantage of MCMC over the variational objective in that case. The diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrates practical utility of the variational method: estimated overdispersion measures converge as m increases and separate classes meaningfully. However, the empirical section is limited: no comparison in runtime/accuracy to spectral methods on finite problems, no higher-dimensional continuous examples, no sensitivity to Z initialization/optimization strategy, and limited discussion of mixing diagnostics and wall-clock costs for MH. Overall, the methods appear sound; more comprehensive evaluation would increase confidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a helpful finite-to-continuous progression and concrete algorithmic guidance. There are minor notational slips (equation references and indexing) and some heavy reliance on the supplement for proofs. The role of Z (not necessarily a subset of Y) and its connection to Nyström vs inducing variables could be clarified. Practical guidance for selecting/initializing Z and choosing m would help practitioners.

Originality
The application of inducing-point determinant bounds to DPP inference—both finite and continuous—and their use in a retrospective MH framework without spectral knowledge appears novel within the DPP literature. The core mathematical tools (determinant inequalities, inducing-point approximations) are known, but their adaptation to DPP normalization and the continuous-case Fredholm setting is original and useful.

Significance
Removing spectral requirements broadens the applicability of likelihood-based DPP inference, particularly in continuous domains and large finite settings where eigendecompositions are impractical. The approach is simple, modular, and likely to be adopted by practitioners already familiar with sparse GP methods. While the empirical validation is not exhaustive, the contribution advances the state of the art conceptually and practically.

Pros
- Simple, general bounds that avoid spectra and work for both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Clear variational objective and a practical retrospective MH recipe.
- Solid connections to sparse GP literature; computationally attractive (O(n m^2)).
- Experiments validate correctness and show practical separation on real data.

Cons
- Limited theoretical analysis of tightness/convergence with m and optimized Z.
- Experimental evaluation is modest; lacks head-to-head comparisons with spectral methods and broader datasets.
- Practical guidance on choosing/optimizing Z and m is mostly heuristic.
- Minor clarity/notation issues; reliance on supplement for proofs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to spectral-bound methods (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014) on finite problems, reporting accuracy vs runtime.
- Provide guidance or heuristics for initializing/optimizing Z and choosing m (e.g., active set, k-DPP preconditioning, sensitivity analysis).
- Include mixing diagnostics and wall-clock measurements for MH; discuss the effect of re-optimizing Z on computational cost.
- Expand continuous-domain experiments to higher dimensions or different base measures; report how Ψ is computed when closed form is unavailable.
- Clarify theoretical conditions ensuring the retrospective MH remains valid under adaptive Z and m.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a neat and practical contribution—non-spectral likelihood bounds enabling both variational and MH inference for DPPs—supported by correct theory and illustrative experiments. With stronger empirical comparisons and minor clarifications, it would be a solid addition to NeurIPS. Confidence: medium.
