- Summary: Proposes bounds on DPP likelihoods that avoid spectral decomposition, applicable to finite and continuous DPPs, and usable in variational inference and MCMC; validated on synthetic and real nerve-fiber data.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on DPP and GP variational literature, positions against spectral methods and Fredholm-determinant approximations, and connects to variational GP techniques.  
- Strength: Removes need for spectral decomposition, enabling larger/more complex DPP applications.  
- Strength: Theoretical foundation is sound, with rigorous determinant-inequality-based bounds and extension to continuous DPPs.  
- Strength: Provides practical methods for parameter estimation within both variational and MCMC frameworks.  
- Strength: Empirical validation shows effectiveness and retrospective MCMC consistency with spectral methods; real-world diabetic neuropathy example included.  
- Strength: Clear, well-organized presentation, with accessible exposition starting from the finite case.  
- Weakness: Parameter identifiability issues when parametrizing L; only repulsive lengthscale inferred reliably, suggesting need for reparameterization or priors.  
- Weakness: Optimization of pseudo-inputs introduces computational cost and raises scalability questions not fully addressed.  
- Weakness: Limited experimental scope; more diverse datasets and comparisons (e.g., Nyström) would strengthen claims.  
- Weakness: Sparse discussion of connections to quadrature-based bounds and potential integration with those techniques.  
- Weakness: Tone sometimes overly polite/hesitant in critiquing existing literature and trade-offs.  
- Quality: Technically sound with rigorous analysis and well-defined methods.  
- Clarity: Generally very clear and well-organized with consistent notation.  
- Originality: Novel bounds for DPP inference without spectral knowledge and extension to continuous domains.  
- Significance: Important advance that broadens DPP applicability by removing a key computational bottleneck.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses major limitation, theoretically sound and practical, empirically validated, and well-presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Practical issues from identifiability, pseudo-input optimization cost, and limited experiments.  
- Overall assessment: Recommend acceptance; contribution is significant though revisions addressing weaknesses would improve the paper.
