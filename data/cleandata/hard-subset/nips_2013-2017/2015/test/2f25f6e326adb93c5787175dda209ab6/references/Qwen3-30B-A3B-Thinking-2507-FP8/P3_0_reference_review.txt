

# Peer Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator. The authors propose novel bounds on the likelihood that avoid spectral knowledge, making inference feasible for larger datasets. The approach is inspired by sparse Gaussian process approximations using inducing variables, and is extended to both finite and continuous DPPs.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation:** The paper effectively identifies the key computational challenge in DPP inference (spectral decomposition) and explains why it's problematic for large datasets. The connection to Gaussian process literature is well-made, providing a familiar framework for the proposed solution. The authors correctly note that while parametrizing L directly instead of K is computationally advantageous, it sacrifices interpretability - an important trade-off that many prior works [3, 7] overlook.

2. **Sound theoretical contribution:** The bounds derived in Propositions 1 and 2 are mathematically rigorous, building on known determinant inequalities. The proofs are properly referenced to supplementary material, and the theoretical foundation is solid. The extension to continuous DPPs (Section 4) is a valuable contribution that broadens the applicability of the method.

3. **Practical validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real data are convincing. The diabetic neuropathy dataset application (Section 5.2) is particularly compelling, showing clear separation between classes as in prior work [7, 22]. The authors demonstrate that their MCMC method (Section 3.2) can successfully recover parameters where variational inference fails, providing important guidance for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited comparison with prior work:** While the paper acknowledges [7] as using spectral bounds, it doesn't provide a clear comparison of computational complexity. The paper states that [7] requires "applying power iteration methods" but doesn't quantify the computational advantage of the proposed method (O(nm²) vs O(n³) for exact spectral decomposition when m << n). A table comparing computational complexity across methods would significantly strengthen the paper.

2. **Insufficient analysis of pseudo-input selection:** The paper shows that pseudo-inputs tend to spread evenly across the domain (Figures 1a and 4), but doesn't analyze how sensitive the bounds are to their placement. It's unclear whether the method would fail with poor initial placements of pseudo-inputs, or how the optimization of pseudo-inputs affects the tightness of the bounds. A sensitivity analysis would be valuable.

3. **Underdeveloped continuous case:** Section 4 on continuous DPPs feels like an afterthought. The extension to continuous domains is important but lacks the depth of Section 2. The paper states that "the Fredholm determinant det(I + L) in this special case is a q-Pochhammer symbol" but doesn't explain why this matters for their method. The connection to the finite case could be made clearer.

4. **Limited discussion of limitations:** While the paper acknowledges identifiability issues with parametrizing L instead of K, it doesn't discuss other limitations. For instance, how does the method behave when the DPP has strong repulsiveness (i.e., when the kernel L is ill-conditioned)? The paper claims the bounds "can be arbitrarily refined by increasing the cardinality of Z," but doesn't discuss the practical trade-off between computational cost and bound tightness.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution that addresses a real computational challenge in DPP inference. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and validated on relevant data. The main weakness is the limited comparison with prior work and the underdeveloped continuous case. However, these are not fatal flaws.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP inference, and the proposed bounds are both theoretically sound and practically useful. The method has clear potential for application in various domains where DPPs are used for modeling repulsive point patterns.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**

1. Include a table comparing computational complexity of different DPP inference methods, highlighting the advantage of the proposed approach.

2. Add a short analysis of how sensitive the bounds are to the initial placement of pseudo-inputs, perhaps with a small experiment showing how bound quality changes with different initializations.

3. Expand Section 4 to provide a clearer connection to the finite case and explain why the continuous case extension is non-trivial.

4. Discuss the practical trade-off between the number of pseudo-inputs (m) and the tightness of the bounds, perhaps with a small experiment showing how the bound tightness changes with m.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these points. As it stands, it's a valuable contribution that should be accepted for NeurIPS. The method is both theoretically interesting and practically applicable, advancing the state of the art in DPP inference by removing a major computational bottleneck.
