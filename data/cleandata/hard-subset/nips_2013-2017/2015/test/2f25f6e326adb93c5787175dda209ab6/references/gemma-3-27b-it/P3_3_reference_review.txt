## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a core computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood that circumvent this requirement, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques used in Gaussian Processes. They demonstrate the utility of these bounds within both Variational Inference (VI) and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, extending the results to continuous DPPs. The paper provides both theoretical justification and experimental validation on synthetic and real-world data, showcasing the feasibility of their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant bottleneck:** The avoidance of spectral decomposition is a crucial contribution, as it directly tackles a major obstacle to applying DPPs to large-scale problems and continuous domains. Existing methods often rely on approximations or spectral truncation, limiting their applicability.
* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The bounds presented are rigorously justified, building upon existing results in Gaussian process literature and leveraging determinant inequalities. The extension to continuous DPPs is well-motivated and presented.
* **Practical relevance:** The integration of the bounds into VI and MCMC algorithms demonstrates the practical utility of the work, providing viable inference methods for DPPs without spectral information.
* **Comprehensive experimentation:** The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation, including a synthetic example with a known spectral solution for verification and a real-world application to diabetic neuropathy data, mirroring previous work for comparison.  The experiments showcase the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the MCMC approach.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivations are clearly stated, and the mathematical derivations are accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parametrization limitations:**  The authors acknowledge, and the experiments confirm (particularly the synthetic data results), that directly parametrizing the kernel *L* rather than *K* leads to identifiability issues and a decoupling of parameters from intuitive properties like expected number of points and repulsiveness. This is a fundamental limitation of the chosen approach, although acknowledged.
* **Computational cost of pseudo-input optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, the optimization of pseudo-inputs (inducing variables) within the VI and MCMC frameworks adds a computational cost.  The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of this cost and its impact on scalability beyond the presented experiments. How does this cost compare to approximate spectral methods for moderately sized problems?
* **Limited novelty in variational approach:** The variational inference component largely adapts existing techniques from sparse Gaussian processes to DPPs. The primary novelty lies in the bounds themselves, enabling the application of these established VI methods.  This is not a critical flaw, but diminishes the novelty of that specific section.
* **Minor clarity issues:** The connection between Proposition 1 and its continuous counterpart in Section 4 could be elaborated on further. While the authors state the analogy, a more explicit explanation of how the trace-class operator and Fredholm determinant relate to the finite case would be helpful.



**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.2 (MCMC):** The retrospective sampling trick is well-explained.  However, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of iteratively increasing *m* (the number of inducing points) until acceptance is achieved would be valuable. What strategies can be used to balance the computational burden of this process?
* **Section 4 (Continuous DPPs):** The assumptions required for the validity of Proposition 2 (trace-class operator, Mercer’s theorem) are briefly mentioned. A slightly more detailed discussion of these assumptions and their practical implications would be useful.
* **Section 5.1 (Synthetic Experiment):** The observation that the posterior on *κ* is not well-identified is insightful. Perhaps a brief discussion of strategies to address this issue, such as informative priors or alternative parameterizations, would be beneficial.
* **Section 5.2 (Diabetic Neuropathy):**  The convergence of the overdispersion measures as *m* increases is reassuring.  A comparison of the computational time required for different values of *m* would help assess the practical scalability of the VI approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* This paper addresses a core computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
* The proposed bounds are theoretically sound and practically applicable.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the methods on both synthetic and real-world data.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The limitation of directly parameterizing kernel *L* results in identifiability issues.
* The added computational cost of pseudo-input optimization needs further investigation.
* The variational inference component relies heavily on existing techniques.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a **strong contribution** to the field of DPPs. While the parametrization issue is a significant limitation, it is clearly acknowledged by the authors. The primary contribution – the development of non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihoods – is novel and addresses a major practical challenge.  The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and provides a valuable set of tools for researchers working with DPPs. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with minor revisions**. I recommend accepting this paper with a request for the authors to address the points raised in the detailed comments, particularly regarding the computational cost of pseudo-input optimization and a more detailed discussion of the assumptions required for the continuous DPP results.
