Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decompositions. For finite DPPs, it proposes nonspectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) by introducing inducing inputs Z and a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, yielding det(Q+I) as an upper bound and det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) as a lower bound. This is inspired by variational sparse Gaussian process (GP) ideas (notably Titsias, 2009), but applied to DPP likelihoods. The bounds are extended to continuous DPPs by providing analogous Fredholm determinant bounds involving LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x). The bounds are then used for: (i) a variational lower bound for approximate MLE with joint optimization over θ and Z; and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) sampler where acceptance decisions are resolved by refining Z until the upper–lower bound interval excludes log u.

Relative to prior DPP inference based on spectral information (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2014), this work removes the need to compute eigenvalues and thus applies to cases where eigendecompositions are unavailable or expensive, including continuous domains. It also connects DPP inference to sparse GP methodology, a familiar toolkit for the NeurIPS community. The continuous case links to Fredholm determinants and references numerical work by Bornemann (2010).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods with inducing inputs is, to my knowledge, new, and addresses a core practical bottleneck.
- Theoretical soundness: The bounds leverage a known determinant inequality (Seiler–Simon) and the construction mirrors well-understood sparse GP approximations. The continuous extension is carefully framed under trace-class assumptions and relates to Fredholm determinants.
- Practicality: Complexity O(n m^2) and memory O(n m) are attractive when m ≪ n, and do not require storing n×n matrices or computing eigenpairs. The retrospective MH scheme ensures asymptotic exactness despite approximate likelihood bounds.
- Empirical validation: 
  - A 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where the spectrum and det(I+L) are known shows the MH sampler matches an “ideal” MH baseline, validating correctness.
  - A real neuropathy dataset reproduces class separation in overdispersion parameters, with convergence of upper/lower bounds and reasonable runtimes, demonstrating usefulness.

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical scope is limited. The finite discrete setting (common in ML applications like diversity in retrieval) is not empirically evaluated; comparisons against spectral methods (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014) in runtime/accuracy are missing.
- Tightness and convergence: While the bounds are monotone in m and appear to tighten empirically, there is no quantitative analysis of the gap’s decay with m or guidance on selecting/optimizing Z beyond heuristic CMA-ES or gradient descent.
- Sensitivity to Z: The paper notes optimization over Z is important, but does not provide gradients or practical initialization strategies beyond showing resulting grids. Analysis of failure modes (local optima, sensitivity to m and Z) would strengthen the variational section.
- Continuous case practicality: Computing Ψ requires tractable integrals; examples beyond Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure are not discussed. Guidance on numerical quadrature, stability, and cost when Ψ is not analytic is limited.
- Bayesian inference: While retrospective MH is compelling, mixing, acceptance rates, and diagnostics are only lightly discussed. Adaptive MH is used, but more details would be helpful (e.g., average m required, bound widths).
- Parameter identifiability: The instability for κ when parametrizing L is important and is noted, but the paper stops short of proposing a remedy (e.g., priors/constraints, reparametrizations, or moving back to K where interpretable).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The core claims are theoretically grounded and the use of Seiler–Simon determinant inequality is appropriate. The finite and continuous bounds are clearly stated; proofs are said to be in the supplement. The inference schemes (variational and retrospective MH) are standard and correctly adapted. Experiments validate feasibility and correctness on a toy problem with known ground truth and demonstrate practical utility on a real dataset. However, additional comparisons and broader stress tests would improve confidence.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear motivation and connections to GPs and prior DPP work. Some minor notation/typo issues (equation references and footnote markers) are present but do not impede understanding. More explicit derivations (e.g., gradients w.r.t. Z and θ, complexity details for computing tr(L−Q)) and practical guidance on Z selection would further aid reproducibility.
- Originality: The nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods using inducing inputs and their extension to continuous DPPs appear novel. The adaptation of variational sparse GP ideas to DPPs is a creative contribution. The retrospective MH with nonspectral bounds is a natural but useful extension of [Affandi et al., 2014].
- Significance: Removing the dependence on eigen-decompositions broadens the applicability of DPPs, especially on continuous domains or large discrete sets. The approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners who currently face spectral bottlenecks. While some empirical comparisons are missing, the contribution addresses a well-known pain point and could materially influence DPP inference practice.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a finite discrete experiment at scale and compare against spectral-based methods (both accuracy and runtime), including sensitivity to m.
- Provide theoretical or empirical guidance on the rate at which the bounds tighten with m and on strategies for choosing and initializing Z; include gradient expressions to enable deterministic optimization rather than CMA-ES.
- For the continuous case, discuss computation of Ψ beyond the Gaussian–Gaussian setting, including quadrature schemes and errors.
- Report MH diagnostics: acceptance rates, the distribution of m used per iteration, autocorrelations, and effective sample sizes.
- Discuss remedies for κ identifiability (e.g., informative priors, reparametrizations, or hybrid K/L parameterizations).

Pros
- Novel nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods; removes eigenvalue dependence.
- Applicable to continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinant bounds.
- Sound methodology, clear connection to sparse GP literature.
- Retrospective MH provides asymptotically exact Bayesian inference without spectra.
- Empirical demonstrations validate correctness and practical utility.

Cons
- Limited experimental scope; no large-scale finite experiments or head-to-head baselines.
- No quantitative analysis of bound tightness or Z selection strategy.
- Practical computation of Ψ in general continuous settings underexplored.
- Variational instability for certain parameters not fully addressed.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper makes a clear, novel, and practically relevant contribution by enabling DPP inference without spectral knowledge, with solid theory and promising experiments. Expanding empirical comparisons and providing more guidance on Z and bound tightness would further strengthen the paper.
