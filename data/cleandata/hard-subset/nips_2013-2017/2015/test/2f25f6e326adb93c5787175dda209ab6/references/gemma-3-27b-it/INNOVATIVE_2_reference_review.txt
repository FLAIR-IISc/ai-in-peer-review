This paper tackles a significant practical bottleneck in applying Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): avoiding the spectral decomposition of the kernel. The authors correctly identify that likelihood-based inference is hampered by this computational cost, especially for large datasets or continuous domains. Their core contribution – bounding the DPP likelihood without spectral knowledge – is a clever and potentially impactful idea, drawing inspiration from variational Gaussian process approximations. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, presenting the mathematical derivations and experimental validation in a logical manner.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of bypassing spectral decomposition for DPP likelihood estimation is genuinely novel. While spectral methods are standard, this circumvention opens up DPP modeling to much larger scales and more complex kernels.
* **Technical Soundness:** The propositions appear well-motivated and the derivations (as far as can be assessed without meticulously verifying all steps – the supplementary material is crucial here) seem reasonable. The connection to variational inference and MCMC is appropriately made.
* **Practical Relevance:** The proposed bounds directly address a major impediment to using DPPs in real-world applications, as highlighted by the experimental validations.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is easy to follow, even for readers less familiar with DPPs, and carefully explains the motivations and limitations.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, including a synthetic Gaussian example *and* a real-world dataset, provide initial evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The analysis of the retrospective MCMC is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Guarantees:** While the bounds are presented, the conditions under which they are tight (or sufficiently accurate for reliable inference) aren't fully explored. More discussion about the tightness of the bounds and how it depends on the choice of inducing points *Z* would be valuable.
* **Parameter Identifiability:** The experiments reveal a lack of identifiability for some parameters (e.g., κ in the Gaussian example). The authors acknowledge this, but a deeper discussion of the implications and potential solutions (e.g., stronger priors, reparameterization) would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Cost of *Z* Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is the primary goal, the optimization of the inducing points *Z* introduces a new computational cost. A more detailed analysis of this cost, and its dependence on the problem size, would be beneficial.
* **Continuous Domain – Limited Discussion:** The extension to continuous DPPs feels somewhat rushed. While the proposition is stated, the practical implications and challenges of applying this in high-dimensional continuous spaces aren’t thoroughly addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a truly novel approach to a significant problem in DPP inference.
* The proposed bounds offer a viable path towards scaling DPPs to larger datasets and more complex models.
* The experimental validation, though preliminary, is encouraging.
* The clarity of the writing and organization makes the paper accessible.
* The method is generally applicable and isn't constrained to specific kernel choices.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical guarantees regarding the tightness of the bounds are limited.
* Parameter identifiability issues raise concerns about the reliability of the inferred parameters.
* The computational cost of optimizing *Z* needs further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a highly promising contribution. The novelty of avoiding spectral decomposition is compelling, and the proposed bounds offer a practical solution to a long-standing problem. While some theoretical aspects and the optimization of *Z* could be explored in more detail, these are not fatal flaws. The paper introduces a significant new direction in DPP inference and has the potential to impact a wide range of applications.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I am willing to overlook the minor weaknesses in light of the paper's originality and potential impact. I encourage the authors to address the theoretical concerns and computational cost analysis in future work.
