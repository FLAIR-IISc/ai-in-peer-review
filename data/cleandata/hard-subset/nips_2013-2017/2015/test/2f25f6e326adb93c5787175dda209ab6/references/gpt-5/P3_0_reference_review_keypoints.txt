- Proposal: nonspectral, computable upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) using inducing inputs Z and low-rank operator Q, applicable to finite and continuous domains.
- Key derivation: finite-case bounds det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I) and analogous Fredholm determinant bounds using Ψ in the continuous case.
- Uses: enables a tractable variational lower bound for DPP likelihoods (optimize kernel parameters and Z) and a retrospective MH sampler that avoids eigen-decompositions by refining bounds in m.
- Empirical: synthetic 1D experiment validates the MH approach; diabetic neuropathy data shows variational estimates converge with increasing m.
- Strength — novelty: brings inducing-point sparse GP ideas to DPPs and extends nonspectral bounds to Fredholm determinants for continuous domains.
- Strength — correctness/intuitiveness: finite-domain bounds align with Q ≼ L and known determinant monotonicity; exponential correction ties to GP posterior variance.
- Strength — practicality: avoids storing/decomposing n×n matrices; main cost O(n m^2) with computable kernel integrals for common kernels/measures.
- Strength — algorithms: clear variational objective and a principled retrospective MH scheme.
- Weakness — empirical evaluation: limited experiments and no quantitative comparisons to spectral methods (e.g., Affandi et al. ICML’13) or other learning algorithms; scalability/accuracy trade-offs not characterized.
- Weakness — theory gap: no guarantees on tightness, monotonicity, or convergence of bounds as m grows and Z is optimized.
- Weakness — variational evaluation inconclusive: toy example shows poor performance/identifiability for κ without attempted remedies or optimization details.
- Weakness — continuous-case assumptions: operator-theoretic conditions (trace-class, determinant extension, invertibility of LZ) are under-specified and informal.
- Weakness — clarity/reproducibility: notation/equation issues, embedded asides, and insufficient detail on Z construction, initialization, and gradient expressions.
- Quality: finite-domain derivation is sound and variational objective natural; continuous extension plausible but key details deferred; experiments suggestive rather than conclusive.
- Originality/significance: novel and potentially impactful approach to avoid spectral decompositions in DPP inference, especially for continuous domains, but needs stronger empirical and theoretical support.
- Suggested improvements: provide theoretical properties w.r.t. m, practical Z initialization/guidance, head-to-head comparisons, clarified continuous assumptions with proof sketch, remedies for identifiability and gradient formulas, and cleanup of notation.
- Pros for acceptance: novel technique, solid finite-dimensional derivation, meaningful continuous extension, and practical algorithms.
- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical validation, missing theoretical guarantees on bounds, and clarity/rigor gaps in the continuous case.
- Recommendation: Borderline accept.
