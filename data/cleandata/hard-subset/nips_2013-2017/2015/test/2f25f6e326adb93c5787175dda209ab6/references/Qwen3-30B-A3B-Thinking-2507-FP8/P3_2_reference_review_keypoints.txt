- Summary: Proposes nonspectral variational bounds for DPP likelihoods to avoid costly spectral decomposition, enabling inference for large/continuous domains and enabling variational inference and MCMC.

- Strength — Technical contribution: Novel theoretical results (Propositions 1 and 2) extending variational GP techniques to derive nonspectral DPP likelihood bounds with solid proofs.

- Strength — Practical impact: Reduces computational complexity from O(n^3) to O(n m^2) (m ≪ n), making DPP inference feasible at larger scales.

- Strength — Experimental validation: Convincing synthetic and real-data experiments show convergence and effectiveness of the variational/MCMC approaches.

- Strength — Clarity: Well-written, well-structured paper with clear connections to GP literature and thorough supplementary proofs.

- Weakness — Parametrization trade-off: Parametrizing L instead of K harms interpretability and leads to poor identifiability (e.g., κ), and the paper under-justifies this trade-off.

- Weakness — Limited comparisons: Comparison is primarily to [7]; lacks broader empirical comparison with other DPP inference methods like [8, 9].

- Weakness — Theoretical limitations: Bounds rely on assumptions (e.g., trace-class L) whose practical applicability and limitations are not fully discussed.

- Weakness — Optimization cost: Retrospective MCMC requires optimizing pseudo-inputs Z per iteration; scalability and complexity of this overhead are not analyzed.

- Significance: Meaningful advance removing a key computational bottleneck, but practical adoption is tempered by parametrization/interpretability concerns.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions — address parametrization interpretability, justify computational vs interpretability trade-off, and add comparisons to alternative methods.

- Final assessment/scores: Quality 9/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 8/10.
