This paper addresses the significant computational challenge of likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The key bottleneck is the evaluation of a determinant term, `det(L + I)` for finite DPPs or the Fredholm determinant `det(I + L)` for continuous DPPs, which typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix or operator L. This is often intractable for large or continuous domains. The main contribution is a set of novel, non-spectral bounds on this determinant term. Drawing inspiration from sparse approximations in Gaussian processes (GPs), the authors introduce a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" `Z` to construct a low-rank approximation `Q` to `L`, which in turn yields both an upper and a lower bound on the inverse of the determinant. These bounds are computationally efficient, scaling as `O(nm^2)` for the finite case, avoiding the `O(n^3)` cost of direct methods or the iterative cost of spectral methods. The paper demonstrates how these bounds can be effectively integrated into two standard inference paradigms: a fast but approximate variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes the lower bound, and a more computationally intensive but asymptotically exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method using retrospective sampling. The framework is developed for both finite and continuous DPPs, and its efficacy is demonstrated on a synthetic 1D problem with a known ground truth and a real-world 2D spatial statistics problem.

This work relates to a line of research aimed at making DPPs practical for machine learning, such as Affandi et al. (2013) [7], who also proposed an MCMC scheme but relied on bounds derived from partial spectral decompositions. The novelty here is the avoidance of any eigenvalue computation, instead leveraging techniques from the GP literature (Titsias, 2009) [11], providing a new and effective approach to the problem.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of using non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds for the DPP likelihood is novel and significant. It provides a principled and computationally attractive alternative to existing spectral methods. The connection drawn between sparse GPs and DPP inference is insightful and opens up new avenues for cross-pollination between these fields. The work substantially advances the practicality of parametric inference for DPPs.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The bounds are derived rigorously from a known matrix inequality [14]. The extension from the finite matrix case to the infinite-dimensional operator case for continuous DPPs is handled carefully and presented clearly, making the method broadly applicable.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. It begins with the simpler finite case to establish the main ideas before moving to the more technical continuous setting. This pedagogical choice makes the contributions accessible. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the relationship to prior work is well-contextualized.
4.  **Thoroughness:** The authors demonstrate the versatility of their proposed bounds by developing both a VI and an MCMC inference algorithm. This is a major strength, as it provides practitioners with a choice between a fast/approximate method and a slower/exact one. The experimental validation is also thorough: the synthetic experiment cleverly uses a case where the exact likelihood is computable, providing a valuable sanity check for the MCMC sampler. The real-world diabetic neuropathy experiment successfully demonstrates the method's utility and scalability on a practical problem.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **VI Stability and Guidance:** The paper commendably reports that the VI approach "yields poorly consistent results" on the synthetic toy problem, attributing this to parameter non-identifiability. While this honesty is appreciated, it raises questions about the general robustness of the VI method. The paper could be strengthened by a deeper analysis of why VI fails in this case (e.g., local optima in the joint optimization of `θ` and `Z`) and by providing guidance on when practitioners should trust the VI approach versus resorting to the more expensive MCMC.
2.  **Lack of Direct Competitive Benchmarking:** The primary motivation is to provide an alternative to spectral-based inference methods like Affandi et al. [7]. However, the experiments do not include a direct comparison against such methods. A comparison of computational cost (e.g., wall-clock time) versus statistical efficiency (e.g., effective sample size for MCMC, or accuracy of the VI posterior approximation) against a spectral-bound baseline would have made the claimed advantages of the non-spectral approach more concrete.
3.  **Optimization of Pseudo-Inputs:** The performance of the bounds relies on optimizing the locations of the pseudo-inputs `Z`. The paper shows that these points settle into interesting, structured configurations but provides limited discussion on the practicalities of this optimization. For instance, how sensitive is the quality of the final bound to the initialization of `Z`? Is the joint optimization landscape well-behaved? More details on this aspect would increase the paper's practical value.

**Constructive Feedback and Questions**

*   The observation that the optimized pseudo-inputs form a regular grid is fascinating. It suggests they are trying to capture the "typical set" of the DPP prior. This seems related to the concept of quadrature in numerical integration. Could the authors comment on the connection between their method and quadrature-based approximations for Fredholm determinants, as mentioned in the discussion [23]?
*   Regarding the VI failure on the 1D toy problem: Could this be because the simple VI lower bound (Eq. 6) is not tight enough to constrain the posterior appropriately for this specific problem, leading to a flat optimization landscape for `κ`? It would be interesting to see if the upper bound provides any additional information here.
*   The choice of `m` (the number of pseudo-inputs) seems crucial. The experiments use an ad-hoc approach of increasing `m` until the results stabilize. While practical, could the authors suggest a more principled heuristic? For example, could the gap between the upper and lower bounds `(b+ - b-)` be used as a diagnostic to guide the selection of `m`?

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong and valuable contribution to the literature on DPPs. The proposed non-spectral bounds are elegant, well-motivated, and open the door to more efficient and scalable inference. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to opportunities for deeper analysis and more extensive experimental comparisons, which do not detract from the core quality of the work. The paper is a clear candidate for acceptance.
