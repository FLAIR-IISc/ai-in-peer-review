This paper addresses the problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition of the kernel, a significant computational bottleneck for large datasets or continuous domains. The core contribution is a set of bounds on the DPP likelihood, inspired by variational inference techniques for Gaussian Processes, which avoid explicit eigenvalue computations. The authors demonstrate the applicability of these bounds within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, extending the results to both finite and continuous DPPs.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations of the bounds appear rigorous, relying on established inequalities regarding determinants. The extension from finite to continuous DPPs is well-motivated and presented. The connection to variational inference for GPs is clearly articulated. The experimental validation provides supporting evidence for the proposed methods, although the experiments are relatively limited in scope. The discussion of the limitations of directly parameterizing the kernel L, rather than K, is valuable and honest. The inclusion of detailed supplementary material is appreciated. However, the authors rely heavily on citing existing inequalities (e.g., [14, Theorem 1]) without explicitly stating the conditions required for their application, which slightly reduces transparency.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and summarizes the contributions. The mathematical notation is standard and consistent. Section 2 provides a clear review of DPPs for readers unfamiliar with the topic. The logical flow of the derivations is mostly clear, although some steps could benefit from further elaboration. The discussion in Section 6 provides a clear outlook of future works. The presentation of the experimental setup and results is adequate. However, the notation used in section 4 regarding continuous DPPs might be hard to follow for readers without a strong background in operator theory.

**Originality:** The proposed bounds and their application to inference are novel. While the idea of approximating determinants is not new, the specific bounds derived in this paper, tailored to DPPs and inspired by GP variational inference, represent a valuable contribution. The extension to continuous domains further enhances the originality. The paper appropriately references related work in DPPs, Gaussian processes, and determinant approximation techniques. The authors correctly position their work in relation to existing approaches, such as the spectral bounds proposed in [7].

**Significance:** The work addresses a practical limitation of DPP inference, making it feasible for larger datasets and more complex scenarios. The proposed methods have the potential to be used by researchers and practitioners working with DPPs in various applications, such as diversity maximization, recommendation systems, and spatial statistics. The MCMC approach with retrospective sampling is a particularly valuable contribution, offering a more accurate inference method when variational approximations are insufficient. The analysis of the trade-offs between parameter interpretability and computational tractability is also significant.  While the experiments are not extensive, they demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on both a synthetic dataset and a real-world problem.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound bounds on the DPP likelihood.
*   Avoidance of computationally expensive spectral decomposition.
*   Applicability to both finite and continuous DPPs.
*   Clear connection to existing techniques in Gaussian process literature.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness through experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experimental validation.
*   Reliance on cited inequalities without explicitly stating all conditions.
*   The continuous DPP section could be inaccessible to some readers.
*   The parameter identifiability issues (specifically with κ) are acknowledged but not fully addressed.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs. The proposed bounds and inference methods address a significant practical challenge and have the potential to facilitate the application of DPPs to a wider range of problems. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh its limitations, and I recommend acceptance.
