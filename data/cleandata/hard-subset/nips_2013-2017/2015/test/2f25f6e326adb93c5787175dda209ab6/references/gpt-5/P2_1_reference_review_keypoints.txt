- Problem: removes the need for eigendecompositions in DPP likelihoods by deriving non-spectral upper/lower bounds using inducing inputs Z and approximation Q.
- Method: bounds of form det(Q+I)^{-1} and det(Q+I)^{-1} exp(tr(L−Q)) are analogous to inducing-point sparse GP methods and avoid eigenpairs.
- Continuous extension: extends bounds to continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinants and computable Ψ integrals for Gaussian–Gaussian settings.
- Use cases: enables a variational lower bound for joint optimization of kernel parameters and Z, and a retrospective MH sampler with tighten-able acceptance bounds via m and Z.
- Empirics (synthetic): retrospective MH closely matches an ideal MH when true spectrum is known.
- Empirics (real data): variational method recovers coherent parameter trends and class-separated overdispersion on a diabetic neuropathy dataset.
- Strength: addresses a clear, relevant bottleneck, especially for continuous and large finite domains.
- Strength: principled, simple, and extensible bounds grounded in a determinant inequality with natural GP connections.
- Strength: dual applicability to both approximate (VI) and exact (MH) inference with explicit compute–tightness tradeoff.
- Strength: novel, practical operator-theoretic extension for continuous DPPs with computable Ψ in at least one important case.
- Weakness: no formal guarantees on monotone tightening, convergence rates, or conditions for F(θ,Z) to reach true log-likelihood as m→∞.
- Weakness: limited empirical breadth—no quantitative comparisons to spectral methods, no scaling study in n, and no ablations on Z choices.
- Weakness: variational optimization can struggle (e.g., parameter non-identifiability for κ), leaving robustness and failure diagnostics unclear.
- Weakness: unclear how broadly Ψ is tractable beyond Gaussian–Gaussian and lacking discussion of trace-class conditions and numerical stability.
- Weakness: computational costs of optimizing Z per MH proposal are not profiled; scalability to high-dimensional settings is untested.
- Weakness: presentation issues—typos, equation numbering mismatches, incomplete references, and missing proof sketches.
- Quality: technically sound construction based on known determinant inequality; empirical evidence supports correctness but lacks formal tightness proofs and broader validation.
- Clarity: generally well-written with helpful GP connections, but would benefit from notation polish, reference fixes, and a brief proof sketch.
- Originality: novel non-spectral determinant bounds for DPPs and insightful link to inducing-point approximations.
- Significance: high potential impact by enabling DPP inference without eigendecompositions, especially for continuous domains.
- Suggested improvements: provide monotonicity/tightness conditions, add finite-domain baselines and scaling experiments, and ablate/compare Z selection methods.
- Suggested improvements: discuss Ψ computability beyond Gaussian–Gaussian, numerical stability, practical Z initialization/optimization, diagnostics for VI failure, and MH stopping criteria.
- Suggested improvements: fix presentation issues and include a main-text proof sketch.
- Pros for acceptance: tackles an important bottleneck with a clean, general method that supports VI and MH and extends to continuous DPPs with supporting experiments.
- Cons against acceptance: lacks theoretical tightness guarantees, has limited empirical evaluation and scalability analysis, and the variational method’s robustness is unclear.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — solid and likely useful despite theoretical and empirical gaps.
- Reviewer confidence: high on technical correctness; moderate on empirical generality.
