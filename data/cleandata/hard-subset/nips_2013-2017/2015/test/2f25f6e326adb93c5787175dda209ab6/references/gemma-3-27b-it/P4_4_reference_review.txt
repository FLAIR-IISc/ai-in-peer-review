This paper addresses the problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition of the kernel, a common bottleneck in existing methods. The authors propose bounds on the DPP likelihood, inspired by variational inference techniques for Gaussian Processes, applicable to both finite and continuous domains. They demonstrate how these bounds can be incorporated into variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms. The paper is well-structured, and the technical development is generally sound.

**Summary of Contributions:** The core contribution lies in providing determinant bounds that circumvent the need for eigendecomposition when evaluating the DPP likelihood. This is particularly valuable for large-scale problems and continuous domains where spectral decomposition is computationally expensive or analytically intractable. The authors extend existing bounds from Gaussian processes to the DPP setting and demonstrate their applicability through both theoretical analysis and experiments.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the bounds builds upon established results in Gaussian process approximation and determinant inequalities, demonstrating a solid understanding of the underlying mathematical principles. The connection to existing work on sparse GPs is clearly articulated.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Addressing the spectral decomposition bottleneck significantly expands the applicability of DPPs to larger and more complex datasets. The proposed approach offers a viable alternative to existing methods.
*   **Generality:** The results are presented for both finite and continuous DPPs, increasing their broad applicability.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while relatively simple, demonstrate the feasibility and correctness of the proposed methods. The agreement with existing spectral methods in the 1D case is a positive sign, and the diabetic neuropathy dataset shows promise.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts and mathematical derivations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of existing techniques to address the DPP inference problem is valuable, the individual components (bounds from GP approximation, MCMC, variational inference) are not novel in themselves. The novelty primarily lies in adapting and applying these techniques specifically to DPPs in a non-spectral manner.
*   **Computational Cost of Pseudo-input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, the optimization of the pseudo-inputs (inducing variables) in the variational inference and MCMC algorithms introduces a new computational cost. The paper does not provide a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs between the cost of spectral decomposition and pseudo-input optimization, and the scalability of this optimization needs further investigation.
*   **Identifiability Issues:** The experiment on the toy Gaussian continuous case highlights potential identifiability problems with directly parametrizing the kernel L, as demonstrated by the difficulty in accurately estimating the scaling parameter κ. This limitation should be more explicitly discussed.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments, particularly the diabetic neuropathy dataset analysis, are somewhat limited in scope. More extensive evaluation on diverse datasets and comparison with state-of-the-art DPP inference methods would strengthen the paper.
*   **Lack of Detailed Complexity Analysis:** A detailed analysis of the time and space complexity of the proposed methods compared to existing spectral methods is missing.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant practical challenge in DPP inference – the spectral decomposition bottleneck.
*   The proposed bounds are technically sound and provide a viable alternative to spectral methods.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the feasibility and correctness of the approach.
*   The generality of the results to both finite and continuous domains is a strength.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty is incremental, relying on adapting existing techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new ideas.
*   The computational cost of pseudo-input optimization is not fully addressed.
*   The experiments are limited in scope and lack a comprehensive comparison with existing methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs by providing a practical approach to likelihood-based inference without spectral decomposition. While the novelty is incremental, the technical soundness and potential impact are significant.  The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the existing literature and present a clear and well-organized paper.  However, a more thorough analysis of the computational trade-offs and more extensive experimental validation would strengthen the paper considerably.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding the computational cost of pseudo-input optimization, expand the experimental evaluation, and provide a more detailed discussion of the identifiability issues associated with directly parametrizing the kernel L. Adding a complexity analysis comparing the proposed method to existing spectral methods would also be beneficial.
