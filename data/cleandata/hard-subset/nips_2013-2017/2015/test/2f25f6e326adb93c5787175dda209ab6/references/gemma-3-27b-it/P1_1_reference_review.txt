## NIPS Review: Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix *L*. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood that circumvent this requirement, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques used in Gaussian Processes. These bounds are derived for both finite and continuous DPPs. They demonstrate the practical application of these bounds within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, validating their approach with both a synthetic Gaussian example and a real-world dataset of nerve fiber patterns.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the established theory of DPPs (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]), recognizing their utility in modeling repulsive interactions in diverse fields. It acknowledges prior work addressing the computational challenges of DPP inference, specifically referencing the spectral bounds used in [7]. Crucially, this work distinguishes itself by *completely* eliminating the need for spectral decomposition, offering a potentially significant advantage over existing methods. The connection to variational inference in Gaussian Processes ([11]) is well-articulated and provides a strong foundation for their approach. The use of inducing inputs is also a well-established technique for scaling Gaussian process methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical bottleneck:** The avoidance of spectral decomposition is a substantial contribution. This drastically expands the applicability of DPPs to large-scale problems and continuous domains where spectral methods are intractable.
* **Solid theoretical foundation:** The derivation of the bounds on the likelihood, inspired by Gaussian process approximations, appears rigorous and is supported by relevant literature ([14]).  The extension to continuous domains is a notable achievement.
* **Practical Demonstrations:** The experiments, including both a controlled synthetic scenario and a real-world dataset, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The agreement between the retrospective MCMC and "ideal" likelihood-based methods in the synthetic case is particularly convincing.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The authors do a good job of motivating the problem and explaining their approach.
* **Connection to Existing Techniques:** Leveraging concepts from Gaussian Process literature makes the approach more accessible and understandable to a broader audience.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper openly acknowledges limitations regarding parameter interpretability when directly parametrizing *L* and discusses future directions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Identifiability:** The experiments highlight that directly parametrizing *L* can lead to issues with parameter identifiability (as seen with the κ parameter in the synthetic example). While acknowledged, this limitation deserves further discussion – it could significantly impact the practical utility of the method. More investigation into alternative parametrizations or regularization techniques could be valuable.
* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-input Optimization:**  While avoiding spectral decomposition is a major win, the optimization of the pseudo-inputs (Z) within the variational inference framework introduces a new computational cost.  The authors mention that this optimization can be computationally intensive. A more detailed analysis of the scaling of this cost with problem size would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are well-executed, they primarily focus on Gaussian kernels and Gaussian base measures. Investigating the performance with different kernel choices and data distributions would strengthen the generalizability of the results.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** In Section 4, the notation and explanation of the continuous DPP case could be more accessible to readers unfamiliar with operator theory. Some equations could benefit from further clarification.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical results appear valid, and the experimental results support the claims. The work is complete in its presentation of the core idea and its validation, although some avenues for improvement (discussed above) exist.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, and the methodology is explained thoroughly.
* **Originality:** The approach of using non-spectral bounds to perform inference on DPPs is novel and represents a significant contribution. While building upon existing techniques, the combination and application are original.
* **Significance:** This work has the potential to significantly broaden the applicability of DPPs to larger and more complex problems. Eliminating the need for spectral decomposition makes DPPs a more practical tool for a wider range of applications. The results are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in machine learning, statistics, and related fields.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Solves a significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
*  Provides a theoretically sound and well-supported approach.
* Demonstrates practical effectiveness with convincing experiments.
*  Clearly written and well-organized.

**Con:**

*  Parameter identifiability issues when directly parametrizing *L*.
*  Computational cost associated with pseudo-input optimization.
* Limited experimental evaluation in terms of kernel and data distribution choices.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **Acceptance** with minor revisions.  The paper addresses a significant problem in a novel and effective way. While some limitations exist, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I suggest the authors address the parameter identifiability concerns, provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of pseudo-input optimization, and consider expanding the experimental evaluation. Addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
