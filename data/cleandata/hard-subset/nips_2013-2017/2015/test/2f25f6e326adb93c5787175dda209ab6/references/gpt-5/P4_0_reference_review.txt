Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) when the spectrum of the kernel operator is unavailable or expensive to compute. The standard likelihood for DPPs with kernel K is typically expressed via an L-ensemble with kernel L = K(I − K)−1, requiring det(L + I) and often eigen-decompositions. Prior approaches either parametrize K (more interpretable but constrained) or L (more tractable, widely used in ML). A key bottleneck is evaluating det(L + I) without eigenvalues, especially for large finite domains or continuous spaces.

The main contribution is a pair of non-spectral, inducing-point-style determinant bounds applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs. For finite domains, introducing m pseudo-inputs Z and Q = LYZ L−1
Z LZY yields bounds det(Q + I) exp(−tr(L − Q)) ≤ det(L + I) ≤ det(Q + I), akin to the variational bounds used in sparse GPs (Titsias, AISTATS 2009) with σ2 = 1. For continuous DPPs (trace-class L on L2(µ)), the paper derives operator-analytic bounds on the Fredholm determinant det(I + L) via finite-dimensional quantities involving LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(zi, x)L(x, zj) dµ(x), relying on determinant inequalities (Seiler and Simon, 1975). These provide computable upper and lower bounds that avoid spectral information.

The bounds enable: (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood that can be maximized over kernel parameters and inducing locations, with O(n m2) time per evaluation for finite domains; and (ii) a Metropolis–Hastings sampler using retrospective acceptance by tightening bounds in situ via increasing m and optimizing Z, paralleling the spectral-bound MH approach of Affandi et al. (ICML 2014) but without eigen-computations. The paper relates to existing DPP learning methods (e.g., EM and fixed-point algorithms for finite DPPs; variational/Nyström approximations; spectral bounds) and extends ideas from sparse GP inference to the DPP normalization term.

Experiments include: (a) a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian continuous DPP where eigen-structure and det(I + L) are known; the proposed retrospective MH matches “ideal” MH, while variational optimization shows parameter identifiability issues (notably for intensity κ); (b) a real 2D diabetic neuropathy dataset where separate Gaussian–Gaussian DPPs are fitted to two classes using the variational bound; overdispersion measures γd = σd/ρd distinguish classes, and upper/lower bounds converge as m increases.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The finite-domain bounds mirror well-established GP determinant inequalities and appear correctly adapted to det(L + I). The continuous-domain result assumes trace-class L and computable Ψ; the use of Fredholm determinants and determinant inequalities is standard. The retrospective MH construction is standard and correctly adapted to bound-based accept/reject. The paper is careful to state assumptions (trace-class, integral identities) and to discuss when Ψ is tractable (e.g., Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures).
- Completeness: Proofs are deferred to the supplement. The inference schemes are described sufficiently to reproduce with standard optimization/sampling tools, though some implementation details are sparse (see clarity).
- Empirical validation: The toy continuous example validates MH correctness against exact likelihood and highlights identifiability; the real dataset demonstrates practical usefulness and convergence of bounds with m. However, broader benchmarking and comparisons are limited (see weaknesses).

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The motivation for parametrizing L, the computational bottlenecks, and the analogy to sparse GPs are well explained.
- Some equation numbering inconsistencies and minor typographical issues make cross-referencing difficult in places. Clarifying the cost of computing tr(L − Q) and gradient expressions would help. The continuous setting would benefit from a concise list of kernel/measure pairs for which Ψ is closed-form, and guidance when numerical quadrature is needed.

Originality
- Using inducing-point determinant bounds to upper/lower bound the DPP normalizer without spectral information appears novel for DPPs, especially the extension to continuous domains via operator inequalities. Retrospective MH with bounds is known, but applying it with non-spectral bounds for DPPs is a meaningful adaptation. The approach differs from prior spectral truncation/bounds (Affandi et al.) and Nyström-based approximations.

Significance
- The method removes a major practical barrier (eigendecompositions) for both finite and continuous DPPs, enabling scalable approximate variational training and exact-in-the-limit MH. This is potentially impactful for applications where spectral methods are infeasible.
- The current empirical evidence is promising but limited. Demonstrations on larger finite domains and direct runtime/accuracy comparisons to spectral methods and Nyström baselines would strengthen the case for broad adoption.

Strengths
- General, simple, and implementable bounds that avoid any spectral computation.
- Applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs; leverages standard kernels/measures where Ψ is tractable.
- Two complementary inference paths: fast variational lower bound and rigorous retrospective MH.
- Empirical evidence of correctness (agreement with exact MH) and practical utility (real dataset, convergence with m).

Weaknesses
- Limited experimental scope: few datasets; no large-scale finite benchmarks; no direct comparisons to spectral-bound MH (Affandi et al.) or other learning algorithms.
- Tightness and convergence of bounds as m increases are discussed but not analyzed theoretically (e.g., monotonicity, rates, dependence on Z selection).
- Sensitivity to inducing point optimization is not systematically studied; initialization strategies and gradient derivations are not fully detailed.
- For continuous DPPs, tractability is restricted to cases with computable Ψ; broader applicability via numerical quadrature and associated error control is not evaluated.
- Parameter identifiability issues (e.g., κ) suggest reparameterizations or priors; guidance is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add controlled comparisons to spectral methods and Nyström approximations on finite datasets, reporting accuracy vs runtime and memory.
- Provide theoretical statements (or empirical studies) on bound tightening with m and on strategies for selecting/optimizing Z.
- Include gradient expressions for F(θ, Z) and discuss optimization (initialization, step sizes, convergence).
- Discuss and evaluate numerical computation of Ψ for non-Gaussian settings; provide error/complexity trade-offs.
- Address identifiability by reparameterization or informative priors; discuss practical implications when parametrizing L.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and broadly applicable non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihoods (finite and continuous).
- Sound methodology enabling both scalable variational training and exact-in-the-limit MCMC.
- Clear relation to and extension of established sparse GP techniques to DPPs.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks direct comparisons demonstrating practical superiority or parity to prior spectral approaches.
- Some clarity/implementation details are missing; theoretical guarantees about bound behavior with m are not provided.
- Applicability in continuous settings hinges on computability of Ψ; generality beyond Gaussian–Gaussian not demonstrated.
