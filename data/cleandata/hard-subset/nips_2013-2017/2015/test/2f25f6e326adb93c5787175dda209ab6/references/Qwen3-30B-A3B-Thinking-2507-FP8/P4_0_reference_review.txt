

# Review

This paper addresses a significant challenge in inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the computational bottleneck of requiring spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L for likelihood evaluation. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that eliminate this requirement, enabling more practical inference methods for both finite and continuous DPPs.

The paper builds upon prior work on DPP inference, particularly the spectral-based approach in Affandi et al. (ICML 2013), and draws inspiration from sparse Gaussian process approximations (Titsias, AISTATS 2009). While previous work [7] used spectral bounds requiring eigenvalue computation, this paper provides bounds that avoid spectral knowledge entirely. The authors derive these bounds for both finite sets (Section 2) and continuous domains (Section 4), connecting them to variational inference and MCMC methods (Section 3).

The technical contributions are strong and well-motivated. Proposition 1 establishes nonspectral bounds on the finite case likelihood using a determinant inequality [14], with a clear proof sketch in the supplementary material. Proposition 2 extends this to continuous DPPs using Fredholm determinants, with a similarly sound derivation. The paper demonstrates how these bounds can be used in variational inference (Section 3.1) and retrospective MCMC (Section 3.2), providing a clear connection to existing Gaussian process inference techniques.

The experimental validation is thorough and relevant. In Section 5.1, the authors demonstrate the method on a synthetic Gaussian-Gaussian DPP where the spectral decomposition is known, showing that retrospective MCMC using their bounds converges to the same results as ideal likelihood-based methods. In Section 5.2, they apply the variational approach to a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset, showing that the method can distinguish between different stages of diabetic neuropathy with increasing accuracy as the number of inducing variables increases. The paper also provides interesting visualizations of the optimized pseudo-inputs, which appear to align with the underlying marginal distribution.

The paper's strengths include:
- Clear technical contribution that solves a well-identified problem
- Strong theoretical foundation with well-supported propositions
- Good connection to related work in Gaussian processes
- Comprehensive experiments validating the method on both synthetic and real data
- Practical demonstration of the method's utility in a meaningful application

The main weaknesses are:
- Limited discussion of the trade-off between computational efficiency and interpretability when parametrizing L instead of K (as noted in Section 1)
- No direct comparison with alternative inference methods for DPPs (e.g., [8, 9]) to establish relative performance
- Limited exploration of the theoretical limits of the bounds (e.g., tightness as a function of m)
- The paper could better elaborate on the practical scalability implications of the O(nm²) complexity for very large datasets

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by removing a significant computational barrier in DPP inference. The bounds are novel, technically sound, and have been validated in meaningful experiments. The connection to variational inference for Gaussian processes is well-motivated and makes the paper accessible to a broad audience. The extension to continuous DPPs is particularly valuable for applications like spatial modeling [4].

The paper addresses a practical bottleneck in DPP inference that has limited the applicability of these models in larger-scale problems. By eliminating the need for spectral decomposition, the authors enable more efficient and practical inference, which should broaden the use of DPPs in real-world applications. The method is particularly relevant for the growing number of applications using DPPs for modeling repulsive phenomena, as noted in the introduction.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling. It meets all four NIPS criteria:
- Quality: High - technically sound with well-supported claims
- Clarity: High - well-structured and accessible to the intended audience
- Originality: High - novel bounds without spectral knowledge
- Significance: High - addresses a practical bottleneck in DPP inference

The main limitation is the lack of comparative experiments against alternative inference methods, but this does not undermine the core contribution. The paper's focus on a specific technical problem is appropriate for NIPS, and it advances the state of the art in DPP inference in a demonstrable way.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that should be accepted to the NIPS conference. The paper presents a technically sound solution to a well-identified problem with clear practical implications. The theoretical results are novel and well-supported, and the experimental validation demonstrates the method's utility in both synthetic and real-world settings.
