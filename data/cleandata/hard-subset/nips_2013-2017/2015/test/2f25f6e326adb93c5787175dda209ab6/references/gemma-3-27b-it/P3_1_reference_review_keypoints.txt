- Addresses the computational bottleneck of requiring spectral decomposition for parametric inference in DPPs by proposing likelihood bounds that avoid eigendecomposition.  
- Introduces novel bounds on the DPP likelihood inspired by variational inference techniques for Gaussian processes.  
- Demonstrates how these bounds integrate into variational inference and MCMC frameworks for both finite and continuous DPPs.  
- Provides a theoretical foundation for the bounds, including extensions to continuous DPPs under standard kernel/operator assumptions.  
- Validates methods empirically on synthetic and real-world datasets, showing feasibility and effectiveness.  
- Builds on and connects to prior work in DPPs, variational sparse GP methods (Titsias 2009), and retrospective sampling in MCMC.  
- Offers a practical alternative to spectral-based inference methods, potentially enabling DPPs on larger and more complex datasets.  
- Clear and well-structured presentation, with separate treatment of finite and continuous cases improving readability.  
- Discusses identifiability issues when parametrizing the L kernel directly.  
- Theoretical assumptions for continuous DPPs (trace-class operator, Mercer’s theorem) are used but their limitations are not fully explored.  
- Optimization of pseudo-inputs (Z) introduces new computational costs that are not fully analyzed or quantified.  
- Performance likely sensitive to pseudo-input initialization, but no strategies or sensitivity analysis are provided.  
- Empirical evaluation is somewhat limited; more extensive comparisons to other DPP inference methods are needed.  
- Reproducibility is hampered by the lack of released code for experiments.  
- Overall assessment: strong, theoretically sound contribution that addresses an important practical challenge in DPP inference.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solves a key bottleneck, theoretically novel, well-presented, empirically promising, and broadens DPP applicability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: requires deeper analysis of pseudo-input computational trade-offs, more comprehensive empirical validation, and investigation of initialization sensitivity.  
- Reviewer rating: Strong Accept (7/10) with suggested improvements in computational analysis, experiments, and code release.
