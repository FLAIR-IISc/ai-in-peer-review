- Summary: Proposes non-spectral likelihood bounds for DPPs using a determinant inequality and pseudo-inputs Z, enabling inference without expensive eigendecomposition for finite and continuous DPPs.

- Practical contribution: Removes a major computational bottleneck in DPP inference, making it feasible for larger-scale and continuous domains.

- Theoretical soundness: Bounds are derived from a nontrivial determinant inequality and connected to sparse Gaussian process approximations.

- Methodology: Bounds depend only on pseudo-inputs Z and are integrated into variational inference and MCMC frameworks.

- Experiments: Thorough validation on synthetic data (where true likelihood is available) and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset, demonstrating viability and advantages over prior methods.

- Presentation: Well-organized, clearly written, and effectively distinguishes finite and continuous cases; GP connections are helpful.

- Limitations acknowledged: Authors discuss interpretability issues of parametrizing L instead of K and trade-offs with prior work.

- Weakness — pseudo-input selection: Limited analysis of how choice or optimization of Z affects performance and bound quality.

- Weakness — comparison to prior methods: Lacks direct comparisons of predictive performance (e.g., classification accuracy) against spectral-based approaches on real data.

- Weakness — computational complexity: Mentions O(n m^2) runtime but does not thoroughly analyze scaling with m or practical comparisons to spectral methods.

- Weakness — related work connection: Could better justify why the chosen determinant inequality is especially suitable for DPPs versus general matrix problems.

- Overall assessment: High-quality, original work that substantially advances DPP inference by removing the need for spectral decomposition.

- Recommendation: Accept — main weakness (pseudo-input selection) is minor relative to the significant contribution.

- Pros for acceptance: Solves a key computational bottleneck, theoretically sound bounds, comprehensive experiments, clear presentation, honest limitation discussion.

- Cons for acceptance: Limited analysis of pseudo-input selection, missing predictive-performance comparisons with prior methods, and limited discussion of computational-scaling details.
