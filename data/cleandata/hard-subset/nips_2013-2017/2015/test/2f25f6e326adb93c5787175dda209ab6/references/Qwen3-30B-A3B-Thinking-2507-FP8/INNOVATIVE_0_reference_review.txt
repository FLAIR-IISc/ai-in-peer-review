

# Review

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), where the evaluation of the likelihood requires spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix, which is often intractable for large-scale or continuous domains. The authors propose a novel approach that bypasses spectral knowledge entirely, providing bounds on the likelihood that can be efficiently computed without eigendecomposition. This is a bold and refreshing approach to a well-known problem in the DPP literature.

The paper makes two key contributions: (1) nonspectral bounds for finite-domain DPPs, inspired by sparse approximations in Gaussian processes, and (2) extension of these bounds to continuous-domain DPPs. These bounds enable both variational inference and MCMC methods that avoid the expensive spectral computations required in previous approaches [7]. The authors demonstrate their method on both synthetic and real-world datasets, including a diabetic neuropathy dataset, showing that their approach yields consistent results while significantly reducing computational complexity.

The novelty of this work is its fundamental shift from spectral to non-spectral inference for DPPs. While prior work [7] attempted to address the spectral bottleneck by using partial eigendecompositions, this paper completely removes the need for spectral knowledge. This is a significant conceptual advance that opens up new possibilities for DPP inference, particularly for large-scale applications where spectral decomposition is prohibitive. The authors' insight to draw inspiration from variational Gaussian processes [11] and extend it to DPPs represents a creative synthesis of ideas from different domains.

The significance of this work is substantial. DPPs are increasingly used in machine learning for modeling diversity (e.g., in recommendation systems, summarization, and spatial statistics), but their inference has been hampered by computational limitations. By removing the spectral bottleneck, this work makes likelihood-based inference for DPPs feasible in many more settings than previously possible. The experimental results demonstrate that their approach achieves comparable accuracy to spectral-based methods while being significantly more efficient, with the variational method scaling well to larger datasets.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a careful progression from finite to continuous domains. The technical contributions are sound, with the bounds properly derived and the experimental validation thorough. The authors correctly acknowledge the trade-offs in their approach (e.g., the identifiability issues with directly parametrizing L rather than K), showing appropriate scholarly rigor.

Strengths:
- Novel conceptual approach that avoids spectral decomposition entirely
- Significant computational efficiency gains for DPP inference
- Clear connection to Gaussian process literature, making the approach accessible
- Comprehensive experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets
- Demonstrates the practical applicability to real-world problems

Weaknesses:
- The paper doesn't fully address how the choice of inducing points affects the tightness of bounds beyond the optimization procedure
- While the theoretical bounds are sound, the practical convergence rate of the retrospective MCMC method could be more thoroughly analyzed
- The comparison with state-of-the-art DPP inference methods beyond [7] is somewhat limited

However, these weaknesses are minor in the context of the paper's significant contribution. The authors have made a bold conceptual leap that fundamentally changes how we approach DPP inference. While the methodology isn't perfect (as no method is), the novelty and significance of the approach far outweigh the minor limitations. The paper advances the field in a demonstrable way, providing a new direction for DPP inference that will likely be built upon by future work.

In the context of NIPS, this paper aligns well with the conference's emphasis on novel, impactful ideas. It represents a significant step forward in making DPPs more practical for real-world applications, addressing a well-known limitation that has constrained their adoption. The authors' decision to focus on the conceptual novelty rather than methodological perfection is exactly what NIPS should value in a high-quality submission.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper introduces a significant new direction in DPP inference that will be of interest to many researchers in machine learning, statistics, and spatial modeling. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and represents a meaningful advancement over previous approaches. The authors have made a compelling case for why their method is both novel and valuable, and their experimental results support this claim. This paper deserves to be in the NIPS proceedings.
