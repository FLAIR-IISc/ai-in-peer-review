Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a core bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): computing det(I+L) (finite DPPs) or the Fredholm determinant det(I+L) (continuous DPPs), which typically requires spectral decompositions that are costly or analytically unavailable. The authors propose nonspectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) that only require kernel evaluations and a set of m inducing points Z, inspired by variational sparse Gaussian processes (GPs) (cf. Titsias, 2009). For the finite case, they use the low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY and derive bounds on 1/det(I+L) using a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975). They extend these to the continuous case via operator analogues, involving the Gram matrix LZ and Ψ = ∫ LZx LxZ dµ. They then (i) build a variational lower bound to the DPP log-likelihood that can be optimized jointly over kernel parameters and inducing points, and (ii) design a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that refines acceptance bounds by increasing m and re-optimizing Z until an exact accept/reject decision is implied.

This connects to DPP learning literature that relies on spectral computation (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014; Gillenwater et al., 2014; Mariet & Sra, 2015) and Nyström approximations (Affandi et al., 2013). The key novelty is to remove the need for any eigenvalue computation, both in discrete and continuous settings, leveraging GP-style inducing structures. The paper also relates to Bornemann (2010) for numerical Fredholm determinants.

Strengths
- Novelty: Derives general, nonspectral determinant bounds applicable to both finite and continuous L-ensembles. The continuous case, in particular, is rarely addressed in ML DPP inference without spectral knowledge.
- Technical soundness: The bounds rest on a nontrivial determinant inequality (Seiler–Simon). The construction mirrors principled GP variational bounds and Schur complements; Q ≤ L ensures monotonicity properties intuitively expected for the upper bound.
- Practicality: Complexity scales as O(n m^2) with m×m linear algebra, avoiding n×n eigendecompositions; det(I+Q) can be reduced to an m×m determinant via the matrix determinant lemma.
- Algorithmic impact: Provides both a variational surrogate and a way to implement exact MH via retrospective acceptance using bounding intervals that can be tightened by increasing m.
- Experiments: Demonstrate (i) an MCMC sanity check on a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP where det(I+L) is known, showing posterior recovery for identifiable parameters, and (ii) a real 2D spatial dataset (diabetic neuropathy) where the variational approach converges as m increases and replicates known class differences.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical guarantees on tightness/termination: The paper asserts that bounds can be “arbitrarily refined” by increasing m and optimizing Z, but does not provide a proof of convergence of the lower/upper bounds to the true determinant, nor rates or monotonicity with m. This is crucial for the retrospective MH scheme to be provably exact (termination with probability 1).
- Limited empirical scope: Experiments are relatively small-scale. There is no evaluation on large discrete DPPs (e.g., subset selection) where spectral methods struggle, nor wall-clock/accuracy comparisons with spectral truncation [Affandi et al., 2014] or Nyström-based methods. The continuous example is 1D; the real dataset is modest.
- Parameterization issues: The instability/weak identifiability of κ in the L-parameterization is observed but not systematically addressed (e.g., reparameterizations, priors, or constraints that decouple intensity and repulsion).
- Clarity/notation: Several notational glitches and equation cross-references (e.g., 1/det(·) placement, equation numbering mismatches, footnote formatting) hinder readability. The derivations for continuous-case quantities (e.g., computability of Ψ for common kernels and measures) are deferred; at least one concrete closed form would help.
- Optimization details: Gradients w.r.t. Z and θ are not fully developed, and reliance on black-box optimizers (CMA-ES) may be suboptimal; convergence behavior, initialization, and conditioning of LZ are not discussed.

Quality
- Methodologically sound and reasonably justified; proofs are deferred but based on established inequalities. However, lack of guarantees on bound convergence and MH termination is a gap for a fully rigorous “exact” sampler claim.

Clarity
- High-level ideas are clear, but the paper would benefit from polished notation, consistent equation numbering, and a brief algorithmic summary (pseudocode) for both variational optimization and retrospective MH. Provide explicit formulas for det(I+Q) via the determinant lemma and complexity breakdowns.

Originality
- High: First nonspectral determinant bounds tailored for DPP likelihoods in both finite and continuous domains, with a principled inducing-point construction.

Significance
- Potentially significant: If adopted, this could broaden DPP inference to cases where spectra are unavailable/expensive, especially in continuous spaces. Impact is tempered by limited empirical evidence and missing theoretical convergence guarantees.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, novel bounds eliminating spectral computations.
- Bridges GP variational techniques with DPP inference.
- Applicable to continuous DPPs; practically useful.
- Promising initial empirical validation.

Cons for acceptance
- No formal guarantees that bounds converge as m→∞ or that retrospective MH terminates almost surely.
- Limited experiments; no large-scale or comparative benchmarks.
- Some clarity/notation issues; optimization details thin.

Questions for authors
1. Can you provide conditions under which the bounds are guaranteed to tighten to equality as m→∞ (e.g., dense Z spanning the relevant RKHS subspace), and monotonicity properties in m?
2. Does L − Q remain PSD for arbitrary Z not in Y? Please state assumptions ensuring Schur complement PSD and hence the upper bound.
3. For the retrospective MH, can you prove termination with probability 1 under your refinement strategy?
4. Can you give closed forms for Ψ for Gaussian L with Gaussian base measure in d dimensions and discuss numerical stability of LZ?
5. How sensitive is optimization to initialization of Z and θ? Do gradient-based methods with reparametrization outperform CMA-ES?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a theorem (or cite) establishing convergence/tightness of the bounds as m increases and Z becomes dense; discuss practical Z selection (e.g., optimizing ELBO with gradients, K-means, sobol grids).
- Provide pseudocode and complexity per iteration for both methods; include determinant-lemma trick explicitly.
- Expand experiments: (i) a large discrete DPP with comparison to spectral truncation and Nyström; (ii) higher-dimensional continuous examples; (iii) ablation on m vs. accuracy/time; (iv) sensitivity to Z initialization.
- Address κ identifiability by reparameterization or priors; report posterior correlations.
- Polish notation and fix equation references.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful direction—nonspectral, inducing-point bounds enabling variational and exact MCMC inference for DPPs, including continuous cases. While empirical validation and theoretical convergence guarantees need strengthening, the core idea is solid and relevant to the NeurIPS community.
