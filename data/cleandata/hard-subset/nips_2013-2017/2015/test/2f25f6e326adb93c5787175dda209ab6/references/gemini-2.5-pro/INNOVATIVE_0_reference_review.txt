This paper addresses a significant bottleneck in the application of determinantal point processes (DPPs): the computational cost and analytical intractability of the determinant term in the likelihood, `det(L+I)`. This term typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix (or operator) L, which is `O(n^3)` for finite DPPs and often impossible for continuous DPPs. The central contribution of this work is the introduction of novel, non-spectral bounds on this determinant.

The core idea is to leverage a low-rank approximation `Q` of the kernel matrix `L`, constructed using a small set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" `Z`, in a manner directly inspired by the sparse Gaussian process literature (e.g., Titsias, 2009). This approximation yields both an upper and a lower bound on `det(L+I)` that can be computed efficiently in `O(nm^2)` time, avoiding any eigendecomposition. The authors develop these bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, demonstrating the generality of their approach. They then show how these bounds can be plugged into standard inference frameworks: 1) a variational inference (VI) scheme, where the lower bound on the log-likelihood is maximized jointly with respect to the kernel parameters and the locations of the pseudo-inputs, and 2) a Metropolis-Hastings (MH) MCMC algorithm that uses the bounds for "retrospective sampling" to make acceptance decisions without computing the true likelihood. The approach is validated on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP, where the ground truth is known, and on a real-world spatial point process dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution—proposing and deriving non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds for the DPP likelihood—is a fantastic idea. It tackles a fundamental, long-standing problem in a novel and elegant way. By avoiding spectral decomposition entirely, this work has the potential to significantly broaden the applicability of parametric DPPs to much larger-scale problems and more complex kernel choices.

2.  **Excellent Conceptual Connection:** The paper forges a clear and powerful connection between the DPP literature and the well-established sparse Gaussian Process literature. This is not just an analogy; the mathematical machinery is directly and cleverly adapted. This cross-pollination is highly valuable and could inspire further work transferring techniques (e.g., stochastic VI, alternative sparse approximations) between these two fields.

3.  **Generality of the Approach:** The extension from the finite case to the more technically demanding continuous case (requiring operator theory) is a major strength. It demonstrates that the core idea is not a mere matrix trick but a fundamental principle of approximating the kernel operator. This makes the contribution far more impactful.

4.  **Practicality and Completeness:** The paper doesn't just present bounds; it provides two concrete and useful inference algorithms (VI and MCMC). The experiments, while not exhaustive, effectively demonstrate that both approaches are viable. The MCMC validation against a known ground truth is convincing, and the VI results on the real-world data show practical utility and convergence as the number of inducing points increases. The authors are also commendably honest about the limitations they observed, such as the identifiability issues with VI in the toy experiment.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality of Components:** While the synthesis is highly novel, the core technical components are adapted from prior work. The inducing point framework is standard in sparse GPs, and retrospective sampling for MCMC is a known technique (and has even been applied to DPPs with *spectral* bounds, as in Affandi et al., 2013). This is a minor point, as the true novelty lies in recognizing the connection and successfully applying these tools to derive and use *non-spectral* bounds for DPPs.

2.  **The `L` vs. `K` Parametrization:** The paper adopts the common practice of parametrizing the `L` kernel directly for tractability, while acknowledging that this harms the interpretability of parameters compared to parametrizing the `K` kernel. This is not a flaw of the proposed method itself, but a limitation of the overall modeling paradigm it operates within. The proposed method does not resolve this underlying issue.

3.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are well-chosen for validation but could be expanded. A comparison on a larger finite DPP problem (e.g., from NLP or vision) against the Nyström approximation (Affandi et al., 2013) or the spectral-bound MCMC (Affandi et al., 2013) would have more clearly demonstrated the practical trade-offs in speed and accuracy against existing approximation methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a highly novel and significant idea for DPP inference, addressing the primary computational bottleneck in a conceptually elegant way.
*   The approach is bold, general (covering both finite and continuous cases), and establishes a valuable new link between DPPs and sparse GPs.
*   It delivers practical algorithms that are shown to be effective.
*   The work is likely to be highly influential and to inspire follow-up research on scalable inference for DPPs and related models.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The core technical tools (inducing points, retrospective sampling) are not new in and of themselves.
*   The experimental comparison to other approximate DPP inference methods is limited.
*   The VI method showed some instability, suggesting it may require careful handling in practice.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The central idea of using inducing points to form non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood is brilliant. It represents a significant conceptual leap forward for inference in these models. While the methodological components draw inspiration from other fields, their synthesis and application here are highly original and impactful. The weaknesses identified are minor in comparison to the strength and novelty of the core contribution. This is precisely the kind of bold, conceptually-driven work that opens up new research directions and should be highlighted at NIPS.
