This paper addresses the key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the calculation of the term `det(L + I)`, which typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix or operator L. The authors propose a novel and elegant solution by adapting techniques from the sparse Gaussian process literature, specifically the use of inducing points. They derive non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant for both finite and continuous DPPs. These bounds, which depend on a set of `m` pseudo-inputs, can be computed efficiently (e.g., O(nm^2) in the finite case). The paper then demonstrates how these bounds can be effectively integrated into two standard inference frameworks: a fast but approximate variational inference (VI) scheme using the lower bound as an objective, and an asymptotically exact Metropolis-Hastings (MH) MCMC algorithm using retrospective sampling. The experiments on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP and a real-world 2D dataset of nerve fibers demonstrate the viability and properties of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Originality and Novelty:** The main strength of this paper is its highly novel approach. The idea of drawing a connection between the determinantal term in the DPP likelihood and the marginal likelihood bounds in sparse Gaussian processes is a significant conceptual leap. Previous work on scalable DPP inference (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014) has largely focused on approximating the spectrum of L. This paper introduces a fundamentally different and, in many ways, more flexible approach by approximating the kernel matrix itself via a low-rank representation based on inducing variables. This is a bold and creative contribution that opens up new avenues for research in this area.

2.  **Significant Practical Impact:** The proposed methods dramatically improve the scalability of parametric DPP inference. By avoiding any eigenvalue computations, the authors provide a toolkit that is significantly faster and more memory-efficient than previous state-of-the-art methods, especially when the number of required eigenvalues in spectral methods would be large. The dual offering of a fast VI method for quick exploration and a more principled MCMC method for robust inference is a major practical advantage. This work makes fitting parametric DPPs to large datasets a much more feasible proposition.

3.  **Theoretical Soundness and Generality:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for its claims. The bounds are derived from a non-trivial determinant inequality and are cleanly presented. A particularly impressive aspect is the extension of the framework from finite DPPs to the more complex case of continuous DPPs on R^d. This requires non-trivial operator theory and demonstrates the generality and power of the underlying idea.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, explains the proposed bounds, and walks the reader through their application in both VI and MCMC. The separation of the finite and continuous cases makes the paper accessible even to readers not deeply familiar with operator theory. The connection to sparse GPs is made explicit and serves as a useful intuition pump.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limitations of the Variational Approach:** The authors are commendably transparent about the VI method's failure to identify all parameters in the 1D synthetic experiment. While this is presented as a motivation for the MCMC approach, it does raise questions about the general reliability of the VI method for hyperparameter learning in DPPs. It would have been beneficial to include a more in-depth discussion on why this identifiability issue arises (e.g., is it a weakness of the bound, or a more fundamental issue with VI for this model class?) and under what conditions the VI approach can be trusted.

2.  **Limited Experimental Scope for MCMC:** While the MCMC method is shown to work perfectly on the synthetic example where a ground truth is available, it is not applied to the more challenging real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset. Applying the MCMC sampler to this dataset would have provided a valuable comparison against the VI results and strengthened the experimental section by showcasing the "gold standard" method on a real problem.

3.  **Optimization of Pseudo-Inputs:** The performance of the bounds critically depends on the locations of the pseudo-inputs Z. The paper states that these are optimized jointly with the kernel parameters but provides little detail on the practical aspects of this optimization (e.g., sensitivity to initialization, convergence properties, or potential for getting stuck in local optima). While the figures showing the neatly arranged final locations of Z are fascinating, the dynamics of how they get there remain underexplored.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core idea of using inducing-point methods for DPP inference is highly novel and represents a significant departure from previous work.
*   The proposed framework provides a powerful and practical solution to a major bottleneck in a widely used model class.
*   The work is theoretically sound, technically competent, and extends elegantly to the challenging continuous domain setting.
*   The paper is clear, well-motivated, and will likely inspire follow-up work connecting probabilistic models via variational techniques.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental validation, while convincing in demonstrating the core concepts, could have been more comprehensive (e.g., applying MCMC to the real data, testing on a wider range of problems).
*   The demonstrated limitations of the VI approach might limit its practical utility for hyperparameter estimation without further investigation.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The central contribution—bridging the gap between sparse GPs and DPP inference to create a new class of non-spectral inference methods—is a bold, original, and significant advance. The paper presents a complete piece of work, providing both fast/approximate and exact/slower inference schemes built on a solid theoretical foundation. While the experimental section could be strengthened, the existing results are sufficient to demonstrate the great promise of the approach. The weaknesses are minor compared to the novelty and potential impact of the core ideas. This is exactly the kind of creative, high-impact work that should be highlighted at NeurIPS.
