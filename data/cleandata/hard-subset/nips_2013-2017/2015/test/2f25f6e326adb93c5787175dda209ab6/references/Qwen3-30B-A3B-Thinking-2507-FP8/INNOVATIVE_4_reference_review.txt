

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a fundamental computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L. While previous work (e.g., Affandi et al. [7]) has proposed spectral-based approximations, the authors introduce a novel approach that completely bypasses spectral knowledge by deriving non-spectral bounds on the likelihood. The key insight is to use "pseudo-inputs" (similar to inducing variables in sparse Gaussian processes) to create tight bounds on the determinant terms required for likelihood evaluation. These bounds are derived for both finite and continuous DPPs, and the authors demonstrate how they can be incorporated into variational inference and MCMC frameworks.

The main novelty lies in the derivation of bounds that do not require any spectral information, which is a significant departure from the standard approach. The authors build upon the determinant inequality from [14] to establish Propositions 1 and 2, which provide computationally efficient lower and upper bounds on the critical determinant terms. This is particularly valuable for large-scale applications where spectral decomposition is prohibitively expensive. The paper connects this work to sparse Gaussian processes [11], showing how the same intuition can be applied to DPPs, which is a compelling theoretical contribution.

The experimental validation is appropriate and demonstrates the practical utility of the approach. The toy Gaussian continuous experiment shows that retrospective MCMC with the proposed bounds can recover parameters (when the parameters are identifiable), while the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrates the method's scalability and effectiveness on real-world data. The variational approach shows convergence of overdispersion measures as the number of pseudo-inputs increases, suggesting a practical way to tune the approximation quality. The authors correctly acknowledge that parametrizing L instead of K (as they do) sacrifices interpretability, which is an important limitation to note.

The paper makes a strong case for the novelty of the approach by carefully contrasting with prior work. While [7] uses spectral bounds that require eigenvalue computations, this work eliminates that need entirely. The connection to sparse Gaussian processes is well-motivated and shows how the same principle (using inducing variables) can be applied to DPPs, creating a meaningful bridge between two important classes of probabilistic models.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with proofs provided in the supplementary material. The experimental design is appropriate for the claims made, and the authors are honest about limitations (e.g., the interpretability issue with L parametrization). The writing is clear and well-organized, with the finite case introduced before the continuous case, which aids comprehension.

The main weakness is that the variational inference approach struggled with parameter identifiability in the toy example, requiring a switch to MCMC. However, this limitation is acknowledged, and the paper presents MCMC as a complementary approach. The paper doesn't directly compare with other non-spectral methods (if any exist), but this is understandable given the novelty of the approach.

The significance of this work is substantial. By removing the spectral decomposition bottleneck, the authors make likelihood-based inference for DPPs more tractable for larger datasets and continuous domains. This could enable broader application of DPPs in fields like spatial statistics, recommendation systems, and natural language processing, where DPPs have shown promise but have been limited by computational constraints. The approach is general enough to be applied to other kernel-based models that require spectral decomposition.

While the paper acknowledges the interpretability trade-off with parametrizing L instead of K, it doesn't propose a solution to this issue. However, this is a limitation of the problem itself rather than the paper's approach, and the authors are transparent about it.

In summary, this paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to a well-known computational challenge in DPP inference. The bounds are theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and demonstrated to work on both synthetic and real data. While the paper has minor limitations (like the parameter identifiability issue in the toy example), these don't undermine the core contribution. The novelty of the approach—completely bypassing spectral knowledge for DPP inference—far outweighs these minor concerns.

I strongly recommend acceptance, as this paper presents a significant new direction in DPP inference that could have broad impact across multiple application areas. The novelty and potential impact make this a strong candidate for NeurIPS, even if the methodological details have some minor limitations.
