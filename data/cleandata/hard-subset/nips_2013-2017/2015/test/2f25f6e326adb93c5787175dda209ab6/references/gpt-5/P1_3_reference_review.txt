Summary
The paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decompositions of the L-operator. The core contribution is a pair of nonspectral, inducing-point-based bounds on det(I+L) for both finite and continuous DPPs. For the finite case, with inducing inputs Z and Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, the authors show det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I). For continuous DPPs (trace-class L on L2(µ)), they give analogous bounds in terms of LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x), bounding the Fredholm determinant det(I+L). These bounds enable:
- a variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood that is computable in O(n m^2) with m inducing points, and
- a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) scheme that refines bounds by increasing m and optimizing Z until an accept–reject decision is made.

The approach is inspired by sparse GP variational methods (Titsias 2009) but adapted to DPP determinants, and it explicitly avoids any eigenvalue computations. Experiments include a Gaussian–Gaussian 1D continuous DPP where the exact Fredholm determinant is known (to validate the MCMC scheme) and a real 2D diabetic neuropathy dataset, where variational learning yields coherent overdispersion estimates and upper/lower bounds that converge with m.

Relation to prior work
- Previous DPP learning work often parameterizes L directly to avoid K’s spectrum constraints; spectral likelihood evaluation or truncations were used in Affandi et al. (2014). The present work removes spectral requirements.
- The construction of Q and the variational lower bound closely mirror sparse GP inducing-point methods (Titsias 2009), but the determinant inequalities (Seiler & Simon, 1975) produce DPP-specific likelihood bounds.
- For continuous DPPs, the use of Fredholm determinants and operator traces is standard; the paper contributes computable nonspectral bounds requiring Ψ to be available (e.g., Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean, broadly applicable bounds that do not need eigenvalues and extend to continuous domains.
- Practical inference schemes: a scalable variational objective and a sound retrospective MH using tightenable bounds.
- Solid theoretical underpinnings: determinant inequalities, clear construction of Q, and operator-theoretic framing for the continuous case.
- Empirical validation of the retrospective MH against an exact baseline in a case where det(I+L) is known.
- Real-data application showing meaningful class separation via overdispersion parameters, convergence of bounds with m, and reasonable runtimes.

Weaknesses
- Limited experimental scope: no large-scale finite-domain benchmark, and no head-to-head comparisons vs spectral methods (e.g., [7]) in runtime/accuracy.
- Tightness properties of bounds are not quantified (no rates, monotonicity proofs w.r.t. m, or conditions ensuring convergence besides the trivial m=n case).
- The MCMC section relies on optimizing Z during acceptance tests; while valid with retrospective tests, a more formal justification (or citation to modern “confidence sampling”/exact-approximate MH frameworks) would increase confidence.
- For the continuous case, the practicality hinges on availability of closed-form Ψ; guidance for general kernels/measures (e.g., quadrature, randomized features) is sparse.
- Parameter identifiability when parameterizing L is a practical issue (as seen with κ), but the paper does not propose remedies (reparameterizations, priors, constraints).

Quality
- The main claims (bounds and their use in VI/MH) are technically sound and well-supported. Proofs are deferred but rely on established determinant inequalities.
- The continuous setting assumptions (trace-class, tr(L)=∫ L(x,x) dµ) are reasonable but could use clearer sufficient conditions and practical guidance.
- Experimental validation is correct but could be stronger with larger and more diverse benchmarks and ablations (choice of m, Z optimization strategies).

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. The finite case is accessible; the continuous section is concise but assumes operator familiarity (acceptable for NIPS).
- Some notation and equation referencing could be tightened; suggest ensuring consistent numbering and clarifying the role of Z optimization in MH.
- Complexity statements (e.g., O(n m^2)) and memory requirements are helpful; consider adding a brief derivation and numerical stability notes for LZ inversion.

Originality
- New application of determinant inequalities to produce nonspectral likelihood bounds for DPPs, including a continuous-domain extension. The approach is a novel and useful cross-pollination from sparse GP ideas to DPPs.

Significance
- Avoiding spectral decompositions in DPP learning is impactful for both discrete large-n and continuous settings. The methods are likely to be adopted, especially the variational bound when Ψ is tractable.
- Broader applicability depends on the ease of computing Ψ; still, the finite-domain result is immediately useful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical properties of the bounds: monotonicity in m, tightness as m→n (finite case) or under which conditions for continuous case; ideally, a convergence rate or a gap characterization tied to the posterior variance interpretation.
- Formalize the retrospective MH with optimized Z using recent “confidence sampler”/exact-approximate MH references, and report wall-clock comparisons vs spectral MH where feasible.
- Add large-scale finite-domain experiments (e.g., subset selection/summarization) and report predictive/held-out likelihoods.
- Discuss practical computation of Ψ beyond Gaussian–Gaussian (quadrature, low-rank/random feature approximations), and sensitivity to Z initialization/optimization; provide gradients for Z.
- Address identifiability: suggest reparameterizations or priors that decouple intensity and repulsion, or discuss when K-parameterization can be partially recovered.

Pros
- Elegant, general nonspectral bounds; works for continuous domains.
- Enables scalable VI and exact retrospective MH without eigenvalues.
- Clear connections to sparse GPs; practically motivated and empirically validated.

Cons
- Limited empirical breadth and lack of direct comparisons to spectral baselines.
- No quantitative tightness guarantees; practical computation of Ψ not fully addressed.
- Identifiability issues not mitigated.

Recommendation
Borderline accept leaning accept. The paper presents a novel, theoretically grounded approach that removes a key bottleneck in DPP inference, with practical algorithms and promising results. Strengthening empirical evaluation and adding theoretical tightness properties would make it a clear accept.
