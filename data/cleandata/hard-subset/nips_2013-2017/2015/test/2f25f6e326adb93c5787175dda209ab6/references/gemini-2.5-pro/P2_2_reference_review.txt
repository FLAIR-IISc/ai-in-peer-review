### Summary
This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the calculation of the determinant of the kernel matrix (or the Fredholm determinant of the kernel operator in the continuous case), which typically requires a costly spectral decomposition. The authors' main contribution is a set of novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant term. Drawing inspiration from sparse Gaussian Process (GP) methods, these bounds are constructed using a set of "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points". The paper shows how these bounds can be effectively integrated into two standard inference paradigms: 1) a variational inference (VI) framework that maximizes the lower bound on the log-likelihood, and 2) a Metropolis-Hastings (MH) MCMC sampler that uses both bounds to perform exact-in-the-limit retrospective sampling. A significant strength of the work is its unified treatment of both finite and continuous DPPs. The proposed methods are validated on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP, where ground truth is known, and on a real-world spatial statistics problem involving nerve fiber patterns.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The theoretical contribution, Proposition 1 and its continuous counterpart Proposition 2, is grounded in a non-trivial matrix inequality from [14], lending it solid mathematical footing. The generalization from the finite matrix case to the continuous operator case is handled rigorously and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying theory. The experimental evaluation is well-designed and honest. The authors commendably report the failure of their VI approach on the toy problem, correctly identifying it as a parameter identifiability issue, and then successfully use the more robust MCMC method. This transparency strengthens the credibility of their findings. The experiments on the real-world dataset effectively demonstrate the practical utility and controllable nature of the approximation, showing convergence of both the likelihood bounds and the inferred parameters as the number of pseudo-inputs increases.

*   **Weaknesses:** The paper states that the pseudo-inputs Z can be optimized, for example with gradient-based methods or CMA-ES. However, the objective function F(θ,Z) is likely non-convex with respect to Z, and the difficulty and stability of this optimization are not discussed in detail. A more thorough analysis of the optimization landscape for Z would be beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   **Strengths:** The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, precisely defining the problem and positioning the contribution. The structure, which first introduces the simpler finite case and then extends to the more complex continuous case, is logical and effective for the reader. The analogy to sparse GPs is used effectively to build intuition. The distinction between parametrizing the K kernel versus the L kernel, and the conscious choice to focus on the latter, is explained well. The figures, particularly the visualizations of the optimized pseudo-inputs, provide excellent intuition about how the method works.

*   **Weaknesses:** No significant weaknesses in clarity.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and valuable contribution to the DPP literature.
*   **Strengths:** While the inference frameworks (VI and retrospective MH) and the high-level concept of inducing points are borrowed from other areas of machine learning, their application to DPPs via the proposed non-spectral bounds is entirely new. Previous work on scalable DPP inference, such as [7], still relied on computing a subset of the eigenvalues. This paper's "spectral-free" approach represents a significant conceptual and practical advance, making DPP inference feasible in scenarios where even partial eigendecomposition is intractable (e.g., with non-trivial continuous kernels). The unified treatment of finite and continuous cases is also a novel aspect not commonly found in DPP inference papers.

*   **Weaknesses:** The strong parallel to sparse GP methods ([11]) means the core conceptual machinery is not entirely de novo. However, the authors are transparent about this inspiration, and the specific technical challenge of bounding the determinant (rather than the GP marginal likelihood) requires a distinct and non-trivial derivation.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant and likely to be impactful.
*   **Strengths:** The paper tackles a fundamental, long-standing problem that has limited the widespread application of parametric DPP models. By removing the need for spectral decomposition, this work dramatically expands the range of kernels and the scale of problems to which DPPs can be applied. Researchers and practitioners interested in modeling repulsion and diversity now have a more general and scalable toolkit. The methods are applicable to both discrete item selection problems and continuous spatial statistics, broadening their potential impact. The provided techniques could become a standard approach for inference in parametric DPPs.

*   **Weaknesses:** The work inherits the known interpretability issue of parametrizing the L kernel directly, as the authors acknowledge and their own experiment with the `κ` parameter confirms. While this was an explicit scoping choice, it means that a key practical challenge for users of DPPs remains unaddressed. This does not diminish the paper's core contribution but does temper its immediate practical impact for users who need interpretable parameters like repulsion length-scale.

### Justification for Rating

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Solves a Major Bottleneck:** The paper provides an elegant and effective solution to the spectral decomposition problem, a critical barrier to scaling DPP inference.
2.  **Novel and Sound Contribution:** The proposed non-spectral bounds are new, mathematically sound, and form the basis for practical inference algorithms.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The methods are developed for both finite and continuous domains, making them widely applicable.
4.  **High-Quality Presentation and Evaluation:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and supported by convincing and honest experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Familiar High-Level Concept:** The core idea of using inducing points to form a low-rank approximation is very familiar from the GP literature.
2.  **Unresolved Interpretability:** The work focuses on the L-parametrization, leaving the more interpretable K-parametrization problem unsolved.

Overall, the strengths overwhelmingly outweigh the weaknesses. The paper makes a fundamental and highly significant contribution to a popular and important class of models. The technical novelty of the bounds and the practical utility of the resulting algorithms are clear. This is a complete and high-quality piece of work that will likely be influential in the field.
