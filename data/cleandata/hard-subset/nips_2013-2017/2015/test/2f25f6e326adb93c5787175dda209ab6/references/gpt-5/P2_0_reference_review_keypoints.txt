- Tackles the bottleneck of evaluating det(I+L) for DPPs by avoiding spectral decompositions.
- Proposes nonspectral upper and lower determinant bounds using inducing inputs Z and low-rank approximation Q.
- Extends the construction to continuous DPPs via Ψ = ∫ L(z_i,x)L(x,z_j)dµ(x), enabling Fredholm-determinant bounds.
- Approach is inspired by variational sparse GP methods (Titsias 2009) and a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975).
- Uses bounds to (i) build a variational lower bound for DPP log-likelihood and (ii) implement retrospective MH without eigenvalues by tightening bounds (increase m, optimize Z).
- Contrasts with prior DPP work (Affandi et al., Nyström) by removing spectral dependence and extending to continuous kernels with computable Ψ.
- Empirical validation: 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP shows retrospective MH matches gold standard and weak identifiability of κ under L-parameterization.
- Empirical validation: 2D diabetic neuropathy dataset shows VI-estimated overdispersion γ_d stabilizes and bounds tighten as m increases.
- Theory: finite-domain bounds are plausible and supported by operator theory; continuous-domain treatment framed under trace-class assumptions.
- Algorithms: variational objective is practical to optimize with complexity O(n m^2) when m ≪ n; retrospective MH bracket-and-refine strategy is clear.
- Empirics are convincing for correctness but limited in scope and scale, lacking large finite-domain benchmarks and runtime comparisons.
- Paper is generally clear and well organized, with explicit connections to sparse GP literature.
- Clarity improvements needed: more algorithmic detail for optimizing Z (gradients, initialization, LZ conditioning/stability).
- Clarify when Ψ is tractable, provide examples beyond Gaussian–Gaussian, and discuss numerical quadrature costs.
- Add pseudocode for retrospective MH with stopping criteria and m/Z refinement strategy; fix minor typos and references.
- Originality: novel nonspectral inducing-point determinant bounds applied to DPPs and continuous Fredholm treatment within DPP literature.
- Significance: removing spectral dependence broadens applicability to continuous DPPs and large n, but practical superiority not yet established on large-scale finite tasks.
- Strengths: addresses important bottleneck, extends to continuous DPPs, enables VI and provably correct MH, practical complexity, and insightful identifiability discussion.
- Weaknesses: limited empirical scope, unclear practicality in higher dimensions, no quantitative bound-gap vs m analysis, and missing algorithmic/implementation details.
- Key questions for authors: provide gradients for Z/θ and conditioning strategies; explain Z initialization/updates in MH and cost; how to compute Ψ for non-Gaussian kernels; report scaling for large n (≳10^4).
- Pros for acceptance: novel nonspectral bounds, solid theoretical framing, practical VI/MH algorithms, clear potential impact for continuous DPPs.
- Cons for acceptance: modest empirical evaluation, missing practical/theoretical details on tightness and implementation, and identifiability concerns.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — meaningful advance with limited but promising empirical support, deserving of acceptance with improvements.
