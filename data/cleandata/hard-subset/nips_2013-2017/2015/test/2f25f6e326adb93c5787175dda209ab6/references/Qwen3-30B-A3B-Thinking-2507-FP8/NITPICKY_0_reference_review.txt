

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper proposes bounds on the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid spectral decomposition, with applications to variational inference and MCMC. The authors extend variational inference techniques from Gaussian processes to DPPs, providing bounds for both finite and continuous cases. The paper is well-organized and addresses a computational bottleneck in DPP inference.

**Strengths:** The problem of spectral decomposition in DPP likelihood evaluation is well-motivated, and the paper provides a clear technical contribution by avoiding spectral decomposition. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrates practical applicability. The connection to Gaussian process variational inference is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising approach, the paper contains several significant technical issues that undermine its contribution:

1. **Inconsistent terminology and notation:** The paper uses inconsistent notation for matrices and operators. In Section 4.2, Proposition 2, the term "tr(L−1Z Ψ)" is problematic as L_Z is a matrix while L is an operator. The paper fails to clarify the dimensionality or relationship between these entities. This is a fundamental mathematical inconsistency.

2. **Incorrect claims about spectral properties:** The paper claims "the spectral decomposition of the operator L may not be analytically tractable" (Section 1), but then in Section 5.1, they use a Gaussian kernel for which the spectral decomposition is well-known (citing [20]). This undermines their main motivation. They also incorrectly state that "the Fredholm determinant det(I + L) in this special case is a q-Pochhammer symbol" when it is actually related to theta functions.

3. **Theoretical gaps:** The proof of Proposition 1 relies on "a nontrivial inequality on determinants [14, Theorem 1]" but fails to state the actual inequality, making it impossible to verify their claims. The paper also incorrectly states that DPPs exist "if and only if the eigenvalues of K are in [0, 1]" (Section 2.1) when they must be in [0, 1] for the DPP to be a valid probability measure.

4. **Experimental flaws:** In Section 5.1, the paper claims "κ varies over several orders of magnitude from one run to the other" but provides no statistical evidence (e.g., standard deviations or confidence intervals) to support this claim. The statement "relative errors for α and go up to 100%" contains a typo ("go" should be "σ") and lacks precision.

5. **Inadequate related work:** The paper cites [7] as using spectral bounds but doesn't properly contrast with their approach. More importantly, it fails to acknowledge that [8, 9] already discuss non-spectral approaches for DPPs, which weakens the novelty claim.

6. **Formatting errors:** Several mathematical expressions contain typos (e.g., "exp ( −‖x− y‖ 2 σ2K )" should be "exp(-‖x-y‖²/σ_K²)"). The paper references "Equation (15)" in Section 4.1 but this equation number does not exist in the paper.

**Critical flaw:** The paper claims to provide "nonspectral bounds" but the continuous case relies on Fredholm determinants, which are spectral in nature. This fundamental contradiction between the title and the actual approach undermines the paper's core contribution.

**Overall assessment:** While the paper addresses a valid computational problem in DPP inference, the technical flaws are substantial. The incorrect mathematical claims, inconsistent notation, and failure to properly distinguish between spectral and nonspectral methods significantly weaken the contribution. The experimental section lacks sufficient statistical rigor to support the claims.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's technical inconsistencies and contradictions outweigh its potential significance. The authors would need to substantially revise the theoretical foundation, correct the mathematical errors, and properly contextualize their work relative to existing literature before it could be considered for publication. The current version contains too many fundamental errors to be accepted as a scientific contribution at NIPS.
