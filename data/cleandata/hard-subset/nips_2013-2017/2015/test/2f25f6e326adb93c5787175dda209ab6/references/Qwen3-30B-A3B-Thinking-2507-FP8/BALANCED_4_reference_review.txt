

**Review**

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood that do not require spectral decomposition of the kernel operator, which is a major limitation in previous approaches [7]. The work builds on connections to variational sparse Gaussian processes [11] and extends them to DPPs, both in the finite and continuous domains. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be used in variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods, providing practical implementations for both settings.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper identifies a clear, important problem: the computational intractability of spectral decomposition in DPP likelihood evaluation, which has been a barrier to practical applications in large-scale settings and continuous domains.
2. The proposed bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) are technically sound, well-motivated, and derived using established determinant inequalities. The proofs are provided in the supplementary material.
3. The paper demonstrates practical utility through comprehensive experiments on both synthetic (Gaussian continuous DPP) and real-world (diabetic neuropathy) datasets, showing that the proposed methods can be used for both variational inference and MCMC.
4. The connection to related work is well-established, and the paper clearly articulates how it differs from [7] (which requires spectral knowledge) and relates to Gaussian process inference.
5. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the technical content accessible to the NIPS audience. The separation of finite and continuous cases is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other non-spectral inference methods for DPPs. While the authors reference [7] as using spectral bounds, they don't discuss how their approach compares to other methods that might avoid spectral decomposition (e.g., [8, 9]).
2. The limitation of parametrizing L instead of K (as noted in Section 1) is acknowledged but not adequately addressed. The authors mention this is a trade-off between tractability and interpretability, but don't provide a clear path to resolving this issue or discuss when it might be problematic in practice.
3. The experiments on the diabetic neuropathy dataset are somewhat routine and don't fully demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method over previous approaches. The paper shows convergence of the variational bound with increasing inducing inputs but lacks comparison with prior methods' performance.
4. The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the bound optimization step, which is crucial for understanding the practical benefits of the proposed approach.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Include a more thorough comparison with other non-spectral inference methods for DPPs in the experiments. This would strengthen the claim of novelty and practical utility.
2. Expand the discussion on the trade-offs between parametrizing L vs. K. Provide specific examples where parametrizing L leads to problematic inferences and how the proposed method could be adapted to address this.
3. In the experiments, compare the inferred parameters with those obtained from previous methods (e.g., [7]) to demonstrate the quality of the proposed approach.
4. Add a section analyzing the computational complexity of the bound optimization step, including comparisons to spectral-based methods.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a well-written and technically sound paper that addresses a meaningful problem in DPP inference. The proposed bounds are novel, well-motivated, and demonstrated to be useful in practice. The paper makes a solid contribution to the literature on DPPs and has clear potential for practical applications. While the current version has some weaknesses in the experimental validation and comparison with related work, these could be addressed with minor revisions.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper identifies a clear, important problem in DPP inference with significant practical implications.
- The proposed bounds are novel, theoretically grounded, and validated through comprehensive experiments.
- The connection to related work is well-established, and the differences clearly articulated.
- The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of point processes with clear potential for impact.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with other non-spectral inference methods.
- The discussion of the L vs. K parametrization trade-off could be expanded.
- The experimental validation could be strengthened with additional comparisons to prior methods.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs, and I believe it would be of significant interest to the NIPS community. The weaknesses identified are addressable with minor revisions and do not undermine the paper's core contribution.
