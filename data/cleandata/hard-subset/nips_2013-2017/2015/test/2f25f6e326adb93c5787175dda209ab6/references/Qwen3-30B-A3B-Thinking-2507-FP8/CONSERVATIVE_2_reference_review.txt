

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper proposes bounds on DPP likelihoods that avoid spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L, claiming to enable efficient inference for DPPs. The authors derive bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, applying them to variational inference and MCMC.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of bounds on det(L+I) and det(I+L) that don't require spectral decomposition, using a technique inspired by sparse GP approximations. However, the novelty claim is overstated. The authors cite Affandi et al. (2013) as having previously proposed spectral-based bounds for DPP inference, yet fail to clearly articulate how their "nonspectral" approach differs substantially from existing methods. The bounds themselves are derived from a known determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1972), and the approach represents a straightforward extension of variational methods used in Gaussian processes (Titsias, 2009), rather than a novel contribution.

The experimental validation is insufficient for a NIPS paper. In the toy Gaussian experiment, the authors acknowledge that variational inference "yields poorly consistent results" and that MCMC is preferable - undermining their own proposed method. The MCMC results are compared to an "ideal" MH sampler that already knows the spectral decomposition (which their method claims to avoid), creating a circular validation scenario. For the diabetic neuropathy dataset, the paper shows results that converge as inducing points increase, but fails to compare against Affandi et al.'s spectral-based approach, making it impossible to assess if the method offers any advantage.

A fundamental issue with the paper is the authors' choice to parametrize L directly rather than K, which they acknowledge "favors tractability over interpretability" and leads to "identifiability issues" (as evidenced by the poor variational results). They claim their method avoids spectral knowledge, but the experimental results show that the MCMC approach requires increasing the number of pseudo-inputs until the acceptance decision can be made - effectively performing spectral approximations through the inducing variables. This contradicts their claim of avoiding spectral decomposition.

The paper's main strength is its technical correctness in deriving the bounds. However, this does not constitute a significant methodological advance. The authors make a strong claim of "novel, cheap-to-evaluate, nonspectral bounds" but fail to provide concrete computational comparisons against spectral-based methods. The paper also doesn't address the fundamental parametrization issue that makes L less interpretable than K, which has been a known limitation in DPP literature.

The paper's significance is limited. It appears to be a straightforward application of existing variational techniques to DPPs without demonstrating substantial improvements over prior work. The experimental results on the diabetic neuropathy dataset are very similar to those reported in Affandi et al. (2013), suggesting no meaningful advancement. The paper also doesn't address how their method solves the identifiability problems they acknowledge in Section 2.

The paper would be better suited for a specialized journal on point processes rather than NIPS, as it fails to demonstrate a significant advance over established methods. While technically sound in its derivations, the paper lacks the novelty, thorough validation, and clear advantage over existing approaches required for NIPS acceptance.

Strengths:
- Correct mathematical derivations
- Clear connection to Gaussian process literature
- Well-structured presentation

Weaknesses:
- Overstated novelty claim (fails to differentiate from Affandi et al.)
- Insufficient experimental validation (no comparison to spectral-based methods)
- Circular validation in toy experiment
- Doesn't address fundamental parametrization issues
- No clear computational advantage demonstrated

The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of significant methodological advancement. Without thorough comparison against existing spectral-based methods and convincing demonstration of superior performance, this work does not meet NIPS standards for novelty and significance. The authors should substantially revise the paper to better articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon existing methods, and provide proper comparative experiments.
