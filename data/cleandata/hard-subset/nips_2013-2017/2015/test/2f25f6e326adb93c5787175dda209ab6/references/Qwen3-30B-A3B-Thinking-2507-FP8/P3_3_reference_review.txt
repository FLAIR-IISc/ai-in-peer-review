

# Peer Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L to evaluate the likelihood. While previous work (e.g., [7]) has proposed spectral-based bounds, this paper introduces novel nonspectral bounds that avoid spectral decomposition entirely, drawing inspiration from sparse Gaussian process approximations [11]. The authors derive tight bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs using "pseudo-inputs" (inducing points), which can be optimized to tighten the bounds. This work builds on the growing literature on DPP inference (e.g., [3, 4, 6, 7]) while addressing a key computational limitation that has hindered practical application.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper's core contribution—nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods—represents a meaningful theoretical advance. Propositions 1 and 2 provide rigorously derived bounds that avoid spectral decomposition, which is particularly valuable for continuous DPPs where analytical spectral decomposition is typically intractable.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets (diabetic neuropathy data), showing that inference converges as the number of pseudo-inputs increases. This addresses a genuine pain point in DPP applications.

3. **Clear Connection to Existing Work**: The paper effectively connects to sparse Gaussian processes [11], making the approach accessible to the machine learning community. The extension from finite to continuous domains is well-motivated and theoretically sound.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments are well-designed, with careful attention to parameter identifiability issues (e.g., the κ parameter in the Gaussian-Gaussian example). The convergence of results as m increases provides strong evidence for the method's validity.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Justification for Parametrizing L**: The paper adopts the approach of parametrizing L directly (rather than K) for computational convenience, acknowledging interpretability issues. However, it fails to provide a strong justification for this choice. Why not use the more interpretable K parametrization with their proposed bounds? This undermines the paper's central claim of "without spectral knowledge" when the parametrization choice itself introduces interpretability trade-offs.

2. **Limited Comparison with Alternatives**: While the paper references [7], it doesn't directly compare its bounds to spectral-based bounds in terms of computational efficiency or accuracy. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the significance claim—does their method outperform spectral-based approaches in practice?

3. **Superficial Computational Analysis**: The paper states the complexity is O(nm²) compared to O(n³) for direct computation, but doesn't provide concrete runtime comparisons or scaling analysis. Without empirical evidence of computational gains, the practical advantage remains theoretical.

4. **Sensitivity to Pseudo-Inputs**: The paper emphasizes optimizing the pseudo-inputs Z, but doesn't analyze how sensitive the bounds are to the initial placement of Z or the optimization procedure. This is critical for the method's reliability.

5. **Missing Connection to Related Work**: While the paper cites [23] on Fredholm determinants, it doesn't explicitly discuss how their bounds relate to existing quadrature-based methods for Fredholm determinants, which would better position their contribution.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper with a clear, well-motivated contribution to DPP inference. The theoretical bounds are novel and valuable for practitioners working with DPPs, particularly in continuous domains where spectral decomposition is intractable. The experimental validation is thorough and demonstrates the method's practical viability.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) A clearer justification for parametrizing L directly
2) More direct comparison with spectral-based methods
3) Empirical evidence of computational gains
4) Analysis of sensitivity to pseudo-input initialization

The significance claim is somewhat overstated—while the method is valuable, it's primarily a computational improvement rather than a fundamental advance in DPP modeling. The paper makes a good case for scalability but doesn't fully demonstrate how this approach outperforms existing methods in practice.

## Recommendation

**Accept** (with minor revisions)

The paper addresses a genuine computational bottleneck in DPP inference and provides a theoretically sound, practical solution. While not revolutionary, it represents a meaningful contribution to the field that advances the practical application of DPPs. The weaknesses identified can be addressed with relatively minor revisions to the manuscript, particularly in the discussion of parametrization choice and comparison with related work.

The paper would benefit from:
1) More explicit comparison with spectral-based methods [7]
2) A clearer justification for parametrizing L rather than K
3) Empirical runtime comparisons showing computational advantages
4) Discussion of sensitivity to pseudo-input initialization

These revisions would better position the paper's contribution within the existing literature and strengthen its significance claim. As is, the paper makes a valuable technical contribution that would be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on probabilistic models and point processes.
