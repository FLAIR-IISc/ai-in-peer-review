Summary
The paper tackles a central bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decompositions of the L-operator (or large matrix), which is often analytically unavailable (continuous domains) or computationally prohibitive (large finite domains). The authors derive nonspectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) by introducing inducing inputs Z and using the approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY. For finite domains, they prove determinant bounds that lead to a computable lower bound on the log-likelihood and a matched upper bound, reminiscent of variational sparse GP bounds (Titsias 2009) but adapted to DPP likelihoods. They extend the construction to continuous DPPs, replacing matrix determinants by Fredholm determinants and showing analogous bounds involving Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x). They then instantiate two inference strategies: (i) a variational approach that maximizes the lower bound jointly over kernel parameters and Z; (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that uses the bounds to make accept/reject decisions without spectral information by adaptively increasing m and optimizing Z until the decision is determined. Experiments on a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP with known spectrum validate the MH procedure against an “ideal” MH and illustrate identifiability issues for certain parameters under variational training. A real dataset of spatial nerve fiber patterns is used to show convergence of bound gaps and stable estimates of overdispersion measures as m increases, separating two clinical classes.

Relation to prior work
The work is closely related to: (a) learning DPP parameters via spectral bounds [Affandi et al., ICML], which still require partial eigendecompositions; (b) Nyström approximations for discrete DPPs; (c) variational sparse GP methods using inducing variables [Titsias]; and (d) numerical Fredholm determinant approximations [Bornemann]. The novelty here is to derive determinant/Fredholm determinant bounds that do not require any spectral information, import the inducing-variable paradigm into DPP likelihoods (with Z not constrained to be a subset of Y), and to show how these bounds enable both variational and exact (in the limit) MCMC via retrospective acceptance.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clean, nonspectral route to DPP likelihoods for both finite and continuous domains via inducing inputs, extending sparse GP ideas to DPP determinants/Fredholm determinants.
- Breadth: unified treatment for discrete and continuous cases; applicability beyond cases with known spectra.
- Practicality: O(n m^2) computation for bounds; no large-scale eigendecomposition; Z not tied to Y (beyond Nyström).
- Inference toolkit: both a variational objective and a retrospective MH that can be made arbitrarily accurate by increasing m and optimizing Z.
- Empirical indications: (i) MH results match the ideal spectral MH in the 1D toy; (ii) consistent separation of clinical classes on the neuropathy dataset; (iii) bound gaps tighten with m and pseudo-inputs self-organize sensibly.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical scope: limited comparisons to existing spectral methods (accuracy/speed tradeoffs) and to recent non-eigendecomposition baselines; no large-scale discrete experiment where spectral methods are infeasible.
- Theoretical tightness: while intuitively the bounds should converge as m→∞ (as in inducing-point GP variance collapse), the paper stops short of a formal statement/conditions/rates. The continuous case especially would benefit from a theorem under standard kernel/measure assumptions.
- Assumptions and computability: the continuous bound requires tr(L)=∫ L(x,x)dµ(x) and closed-form Ψ. Guidance is needed for cases where Ψ is not analytic (e.g., Monte Carlo approximations, their variance/impact on the bounds).
- Variational stability: the toy example shows strong sensitivity for κ and non-identifiability; the paper acknowledges this but does not discuss regularization, priors, or reparameterizations to mitigate it.
- Algorithmic details: the retrospective MH’s termination guarantees and the strategy to adapt m and optimize Z could be specified more precisely (e.g., how often to re-optimize Z, warm starts, and computational budgets).
- Clarity/organization: some equation references are inconsistent; footnote-like asides break flow; a small editorial pass would improve readability.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide formal conditions under which the upper and lower bounds match as m→∞ (finite and continuous cases)? Are there known rates from GP quadrature/inducing literature that transfer here?
- For the continuous setting, how do you handle Ψ when the integral is not analytically tractable? Would Monte Carlo approximations preserve the inequality direction or only produce stochastic approximations?
- How sensitive are results to the initialization/optimization of Z? Could you provide heuristics (e.g., placing Z according to intensity estimates) that reduce optimization cost?
- Can you report wall-clock and memory comparisons against [Affandi et al.] on a moderate discrete problem, and illustrate scenarios where spectral methods stall but your bounds still operate?
- Does your approach extend to k-DPPs (fixed cardinality) and L-ensembles conditioned on counts?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a finite-domain experiment with n in the tens of thousands comparing runtime/accuracy against partial eigensolvers and Nyström for likelihood evaluation or MH acceptance.
- Provide a short theorem (or pointer to one) establishing convergence of the bounds with m, including assumptions on L and µ.
- Discuss practical computation of tr(L) and Ψ in the continuous case, including Monte Carlo estimators and their effect on inference.
- Offer guidance on choosing m adaptively for VI (e.g., increase until bound gap stabilizes) and practical schedules for Z re-optimization during MH.
- Minor edits to fix equation numbering and streamline the exposition.

Broader impact and significance
Removing the need for spectral decompositions substantially broadens the applicability of DPP models to continuous domains and large discrete sets. The inducing-inputs perspective is elegant and likely to inspire follow-up work (e.g., automated quadrature connections, structured Z, and extensions to other repulsive processes).

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, novel conceptual contribution with broad applicability.
- Solid theoretical backbone (determinant inequalities) and extension to continuous Fredholm determinants.
- Practical algorithms for both VI and MH; validation against ideal MH.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical breadth and lack of direct runtime/accuracy comparisons to strong baselines.
- Missing formal convergence guarantees of the bounds as m grows.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new direction for DPP inference that is likely to influence future work, and the minor empirical/theoretical gaps are addressable.
