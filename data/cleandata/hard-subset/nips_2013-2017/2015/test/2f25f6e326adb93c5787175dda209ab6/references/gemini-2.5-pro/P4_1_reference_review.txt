**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of scalable parametric inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The main computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for DPPs is the evaluation of the term `det(L + I)`, where L is the kernel matrix (in the finite case) or a kernel operator (in the continuous case). This term typically requires a full spectral decomposition, which is computationally prohibitive for large datasets. Previous work, such as Affandi et al. (2014), has tackled this by using bounds based on a partial spectral decomposition (i.e., computing the top `m` eigenvalues), which can still be slow and is primarily suited for finite domains.

The main contribution of this paper is a new set of non-spectral upper and lower bounds on `1/det(L + I)`. The authors draw inspiration from sparse approximations in Gaussian processes (GPs), using a set of `m` "inducing" or "pseudo-input" points `Z`. These points define a low-rank approximation `Q` of the kernel matrix `L`, and the resulting bounds on the determinant can be computed efficiently in O(nm^2) time, avoiding any eigenvalue computations. The paper first derives these bounds for DPPs over finite sets and then extends the result to DPPs over continuous domains, which involves Fredholm determinants of integral operators.

Based on these novel bounds, the authors propose two inference algorithms:
1.  A variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes the derived lower bound on the log-likelihood with respect to both the kernel parameters and the locations of the inducing points.
2.  A Metropolis-Hastings (MH) MCMC algorithm that uses both the upper and lower bounds to perform exact-to-the-model inference via retrospective sampling, where the bounds are tightened by increasing the number of inducing points only when necessary to make an acceptance decision.

The proposed methods are validated on a 1D synthetic continuous DPP, where the exact likelihood is computable, and on a real-world 2D spatial point pattern dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The core theoretical contributions (Propositions 1 and 2) are derived by applying a known determinantal inequality from Seiler & Simon (1975). The extension from the finite matrix case to the continuous operator case is non-trivial and well-executed. The proposed algorithms are direct and appropriate applications of the derived bounds. The experimental evaluation is a significant strength. The use of a synthetic "Gaussian-Gaussian" model where the true likelihood is known allows for a rigorous validation of the MCMC sampler, demonstrating that it correctly recovers the posterior. The authors are also commendably honest about the limitations of the VI approach in this setting, correctly identifying a parameter non-identifiability issue. The real-world data experiment demonstrates the practical utility of the variational method, showing that results converge as the number of inducing points increases and that the method can be used to extract meaningful scientific insights that are consistent with prior work.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. It provides a concise introduction to DPPs and clearly articulates the central problem of the computational bottleneck in likelihood evaluation. The structure, which moves from the finite case to inference methods and then to the continuous case, is logical and easy to follow. The connection to sparse GPs is explicitly stated and serves as a helpful conceptual anchor for readers familiar with that literature. The authors are also clear about the trade-offs involved in parametrizing the `L` kernel directly (tractability) versus the `K` kernel (interpretability).

**Originality:** The primary contribution—using inducing-point-based bounds for the DPP likelihood determinant—is novel. While the technique of using inducing points is well-established in the GP community (e.g., Titsias, 2009), its application, theoretical justification, and extension to DPPs are original and insightful. This work provides a new, non-spectral path to scalable DPP inference, which is a significant departure from previous approximation strategies based on partial eigendecompositions. The extension of this framework to continuous DPPs is also a novel contribution.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be impactful. By providing a method that avoids costly spectral decompositions, this work has the potential to make DPPs practical for much larger-scale problems than were previously feasible. The proposed method's complexity of O(nm^2) compares favorably to the O(n^3) of exact methods or the O(mn^2) of power-iteration-based approximations when `m << n`. The principled extension to continuous domains is also important, as it provides a more natural framework for spatial statistics problems than methods that rely on discretization. The provision of both a fast, approximate VI method and an asymptotically exact MCMC method makes the contribution broadly useful to both practitioners and researchers.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  While the application is novel, the core technical machinery (inducing point approximations) is borrowed from the GP literature. This slightly tempers the fundamental originality of the work, but the adaptation to the DPP context is non-trivial and valuable in its own right.
2.  The VI approach, while fast, was shown to perform poorly on the toy example due to parameter identifiability. While the authors are transparent about this, it does suggest that the variational lower bound may not always be sufficient for robust parameter estimation, and users should be cautious. The MCMC approach appears more robust, albeit more computationally expensive.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and efficient non-spectral approximation for the DPP likelihood, addressing a key bottleneck in the field.
*   The method is technically sound, well-motivated by connections to sparse GPs, and rigorously extended from finite to continuous domains.
*   Provides two practical inference schemes (fast/approximate VI and exact/slower MCMC) that broaden the applicability of DPPs.
*   Experimental validation is strong, including a comparison against a known ground truth and a successful application to a real-world problem.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The core approximation technique is an adaptation of an existing method from another field (Gaussian processes).
*   The variational approach showed limitations in one of the experiments, which might raise concerns about its general robustness.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its minor weaknesses. It makes a clear, novel, and important contribution to the literature on determinantal point processes. The proposed methods are likely to be widely adopted and built upon by other researchers.
