## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood that circumvent this requirement, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques used in Gaussian Processes. These bounds are presented for both finite and continuous DPPs, enabling the application of methods like variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) without the computational cost of eigendecomposition. The paper validates these methods with both a synthetic experiment and a real-world dataset (diabetic neuropathy).

**Relation to Prior Work:**

DPPs have gained traction in machine learning for modeling diversity and repulsiveness, as highlighted by references [3, 4, 6, 7].  The core problem addressed - the computational cost of likelihood evaluation - is recognized in prior work [7], which attempts to mitigate this cost with power iteration methods.  However, the authors rightly point out the limitations of those approaches (finite domains, storage requirements, slow convergence). This paper builds upon existing variational inference techniques [11] for GPs and extends them to the DPP setting, offering a potentially more scalable alternative.  The connection to Nyström approximation [13] is also appropriately addressed, clarifying the novelty of their approach using "pseudo-inputs" not constrained to the observed data.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Significant Bottleneck:** The paper tackles a well-recognized and crucial computational challenge in DPP inference, directly impacting the applicability of these models to larger datasets.
* **Novelty of Approach:** Bypassing spectral decomposition through novel determinant bounds is a significant contribution. Leveraging results from Gaussian process literature offers an elegant and potentially powerful solution.
* **Generality:** The derivation of bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs broadens the scope and applicability of the proposed methods.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:**  The use of established inequalities on determinants [14] provides a strong theoretical basis for the proposed bounds.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of both a synthetic experiment (allowing comparison to "ideal" methods) and a real-world application demonstrates the practical viability of the approach. The convergence analysis and visualizations (Figures 3 & 4) are particularly helpful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas.  The authors acknowledge limitations and trade-offs (e.g., interpretability of L parameters).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-Input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, the optimization of pseudo-inputs (Z) within the variational framework adds a computational cost. The authors acknowledge this but a more detailed discussion of the computational trade-offs – comparing the cost of pseudo-input optimization vs. spectral decomposition for different problem sizes – would be beneficial.
* **Identifiability Issues:**  The experiment in Section 5.1 highlights the difficulty in identifying all parameters when directly parametrizing the kernel L. The authors note this but it's a critical limitation. Further investigation into reparameterizations or priors that address this issue would strengthen the work.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:**  The experiments primarily demonstrate the method's functionality. A more thorough comparison to existing DPP inference techniques (e.g., [7] directly) on the real-world dataset, quantifying the speed-up and accuracy trade-offs, would be highly valuable.
* **Assumptions for Continuous Case:** The derivation of bounds for continuous DPPs relies on specific assumptions regarding the kernel L and base measure µ.  The authors mention these assumptions, but a discussion of their impact on the applicability of the method to different kernel choices would be useful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP inference. The proposed bounds offer a promising alternative to computationally expensive spectral decomposition methods, expanding the applicability of DPPs to larger and more complex datasets. While some limitations exist (primarily related to parameter identifiability and computational trade-offs), the theoretical soundness, empirical validation, and clarity of presentation make this a worthwhile contribution to NIPS.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
* Presents novel and theoretically sound determinant bounds.
* Demonstrates practical viability through both synthetic and real-world experiments.
* Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Parameter identifiability issues when directly parametrizing the kernel L.
* Limited comparative analysis against existing DPP inference methods.
* Computational cost of pseudo-input optimization needs more detailed analysis.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1. Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational trade-offs between pseudo-input optimization and spectral decomposition.
2. Expand the discussion on parameter identifiability and explore potential solutions (e.g., alternative parameterizations or priors).
3. Include a more comprehensive comparative analysis against existing DPP inference techniques on the real-world dataset.
