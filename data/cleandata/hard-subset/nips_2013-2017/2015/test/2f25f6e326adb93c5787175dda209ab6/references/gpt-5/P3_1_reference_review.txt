Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decompositions. For finite ground sets, the authors introduce non-spectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) based on an inducing-point approximation Q = LYZ LZ−1 LZY, inspired by variational sparse GP methods (Titsias, 2009). The bounds follow from Seiler–Simon determinant inequalities and the PSD residual L−Q. They extend the construction to continuous DPPs by bounding the Fredholm determinant det(I+L) using LZ and a computable matrix Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x). They then show how to use these bounds for (i) variational inference by replacing the log det(I+L) term with a tractable surrogate that yields a computable lower bound on the log likelihood, and (ii) retrospective Metropolis–Hastings by adaptively tightening acceptance bounds via increasing m and re-optimizing inducing inputs.

Related work: Prior DPP learning approaches either (a) parametrize K and face spectrum constraints, or (b) parametrize L (L-ensembles) but rely on spectral truncations or power iterations to approximate det(I+L) (Affandi et al., 2014). The proposed approach trades spectral computations for inducing-point constructions familiar in GP literature and extends to the continuous case where spectra are rarely available. The work also relates to Nyström approximations for DPPs (Affandi et al., 2013), fixed-point/EM learning for discrete DPPs (Gillenwater et al., Mariet & Sra), and numerical Fredholm determinant methods (Bornemann, 2010).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean, spectrum-free set of determinant bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs derived from inducing-point constructions. The extension to continuous DPPs with computable Ψ for Gaussian–Gaussian settings is particularly useful.
- Practical relevance: Enables variational and MCMC inference when eigen-solvers are infeasible, especially for continuous domains.
- Methodological simplicity: The bounds are easy to compute, depend only on kernel evaluations and traces, and avoid storing the full n×n matrix when kernel evaluations can be computed on the fly.
- Clear link to sparse GP literature: The analogy to Titsias’ variational objective is insightful and will make the method accessible to practitioners familiar with inducing-point GP methods.
- Empirical sanity checks: A toy 1D Gaussian–Gaussian example with known spectrum validates the retrospective MH, and a real spatial dataset demonstrates that the variational method can recover stable overdispersion measures with increasing m.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Tightness and convergence of bounds: While the lower bound det(I+Q) ≤ det(I+L) and the upper bound det(I+L) ≤ det(I+Q) e^{tr(L−Q)} are plausible and spectrum-free, the upper bound can be very loose. There is no quantitative analysis of the bound gap as a function of m, nor monotonicity guarantees as m increases or Z is optimized. The paper hints at GP posterior variance intuition but provides no theorem ensuring convergence of acceptance bounds in MH as m→∞.
- Missing comparisons: There is no empirical comparison to spectral methods (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014) on problems where spectra are tractable, nor to alternative non-spectral log-det approximations (e.g., stochastic trace/Lanczos-based log-det estimators). This limits the assessment of trade-offs in accuracy and runtime.
- Limited experimental scope: No finite-ground-set experiments are presented, despite the finite case being the main entry point and the place where large-n issues are pronounced. The continuous experiments are limited (1D toy; a small real dataset with 7 samples per class), and the variational method is shown to be unstable for one parameter (κ) without a deeper diagnosis beyond identifiability.
- Algorithmic details: 
  - Optimization over Z is central but under-specified. The paper suggests CMA-ES or gradient methods, but derivative expressions of the bound w.r.t. Z and θ are not provided; practical guidance on initialization and regularization is missing.
  - For MCMC, re-optimizing Z and Z′ at each proposal can be heavy; complexity and caching strategies are not discussed.
  - Complexity statements (O(n m^2)) are provided for one pass, but end-to-end complexity (with repeated re-optimization and increasing m) and memory footprints for large n are not analyzed.
- Assumptions and operator details: In the continuous case, conditions under which L−Q is PSD and the determinant inequality applies are deferred to the supplement. The main text would benefit from a brief statement ensuring the Schur complement/operator projection that guarantees PSD residuals when Z∉Y.
- Clarity and presentation: There are several typos and reference mismatches (e.g., equation numbering (5)/(7); notational slips like K(yi,yi); footnote text blended into the main body). These detract from readability.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Provide a theorem (or cite one) that formalizes convergence of the bounds with m→∞ under mild conditions, ideally with monotonicity of the lower/upper bounds as m increases and Z is augmented.
- Derive gradients of the variational objective w.r.t. θ and Z, and discuss practical optimization (initialization, step sizes, constraints) to make the approach reproducible.
- Include a finite-ground-set experiment with large n comparing:
  - your bounds-based variational/MH methods,
  - spectral truncation/power iteration methods,
  - and stochastic log-det estimators (Chebyshev/Lanczos with Hutchinson trace).
  Report runtime, memory, and accuracy on likelihood and predictive performance.
- For the continuous case, give explicit Ψ for the Gaussian–Gaussian setup and discuss other kernels/measures, including numerical quadrature strategies and their costs.
- Analyze the effect of m on MCMC efficiency: acceptance rate, average m used per accepted move, and total runtime compared to “ideal” MH in the 1D toy example.
- Discuss identifiability and the impact of parametrizing L: e.g., which parameters are estimable from a single realization vs multiple realizations, and whether priors or reparametrizations can mitigate issues for κ.
- Clarify memory requirements: if n is large, storing LYZ is O(nm); suggest mini-batching over Yt or structure exploitation (e.g., inducing grids/Toeplitz for stationary kernels).
- Related work to consider: stochastic Lanczos quadrature for log det; pivoted Cholesky bounds; scalable kernel log det approximations as alternative non-spectral baselines.

Minor issues
- Fix equation references and typos; ensure consistent notation (indices i/j, xi/xj).
- Some footnotes appear in-line and break flow; move them or integrate smoothly.
- Explain briefly the determinant inequality used (Seiler–Simon) in the main text to aid readers.

Evaluation
- Quality: Good core idea and sound inequalities, but theoretical guarantees on tightness/convergence and empirical validation breadth are limited.
- Clarity: Generally understandable but marred by typos and missing derivations.
- Originality: High; to my knowledge, spectrum-free inducing-point bounds for DPP likelihoods (finite and continuous) are new.
- Significance: Potentially significant for DPP inference in continuous domains and large discrete problems; strengthened by broader experiments and comparisons.

Pros
- Novel, simple, and practical spectrum-free bounds.
- Applies to both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Enables variational and retrospective MH without eigen-solvers.
- Empirical evidence that the approach can work in practice.

Cons
- Lack of theoretical convergence analysis of bounds with m.
- Limited experiments and no comparison to strong baselines.
- Optimization details for Z/θ omitted; clarity issues.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can (at minimum) clarify derivations, fix presentation issues, and add a small but meaningful comparison to a spectral or stochastic log-det baseline; otherwise, it is a strong workshop/revision candidate.
