## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses the computationally challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), specifically circumventing the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator. The authors propose bounds on the DPP likelihood inspired by variational inference in Gaussian processes, extending these bounds to both finite and continuous domains. They demonstrate application within variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, with experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world data.

The core idea—leveraging bounds to avoid explicit spectral decomposition—is indeed a valuable contribution. Likelihood-based inference for DPPs is often hampered by the computational cost of eigendecomposition, particularly for large datasets or continuous domains, and this work offers a potential solution. The connection to variational GP inference is clearly articulated. The extension to continuous DPPs, while briefly presented, is a significant step towards broader applicability.

However, despite the novelty of the approach and potential significance, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies that collectively detract from its overall impact. My assessment is heavily influenced by a stringent focus on methodological rigor and detail.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed approach to non-spectral inference for DPPs is a genuine contribution.
*   **Potential Significance:** Circumventing spectral decomposition unlocks the potential for scaling DPP inference to larger and more complex datasets.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper effectively articulates the computational bottlenecks of standard DPP inference.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The links to variational GP methods are well-established and provide a strong foundation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Mathematical Imprecision:** Proposition 1 lacks sufficient justification for the stated inequality. The reference to [14, Theorem 1] feels insufficient without outlining *which* aspect of that theorem is applied. The connection between the bound and the inherent properties of DPPs is not sufficiently detailed.
*   **Notational Inconsistency:** The use of ‘L’ to denote both the kernel *and* the operator is confusing, particularly when discussing parametrizations. Using distinct notation (e.g., K for the kernel and ℒ for the operator) would improve clarity significantly.
*   **Lack of Discussion on Bound Tightness:** The paper doesn’t adequately discuss the tightness of the proposed bounds. How does the bound's quality affect the accuracy of the resulting inference? A theoretical or empirical analysis of this is critical. The statement that the bounds can be "arbitrarily refined" by increasing *m* feels hand-wavy without quantifying the cost/benefit trade-off.
*   **Experimental Setup Details:** The experimental section lacks crucial details. For example, the choice of hyperparameters for the CMA-ES optimizer in Section 5.1 is not mentioned, hindering reproducibility. The diabetic neuropathy experiment lacks a comparison to existing DPP inference methods (e.g., [7]) – simply demonstrating convergence doesn't validate the *accuracy* of the method.
*   **Formatting Errors:** Numerous minor formatting issues exist throughout the paper, including inconsistent spacing and punctuation. These detract from the professionalism of the presentation. For instance, Equation (3) is not properly formatted, and uses inconsistent spacing. The reference list has formatting inconsistencies as well.
*   **Section 4's Brevity:** The extension to continuous DPPs (Section 4) feels rushed and lacks sufficient detail. The assumptions required for the treatment of the Fredholm determinant are glossed over, and the practical implications are not explored.
* **Redundancy and verbosity:** The introduction is overly verbose and contains repetitive statements.

**Specific Concerns:**

*   In Section 2.2, the phrase "drawing inspiration from" feels weak. A more detailed explanation of *how* the GP bounds are extended to DPPs is needed.
*   The discussion of identifiability in Section 5.1 is insightful, but the authors do not offer any strategies to address this issue.
*   The retrospective sampling technique in Section 3.2 is described as a "Bernoulli trick" without sufficient context.
*   In section 6, the connection to quadrature-based bounds feels tangential and lacks concrete explanation.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a computationally challenging problem.
*   Potential for significant impact on the scalability of DPP inference.
*   Sound theoretical foundation (though the presentation could be improved).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies detract from the overall quality.
*   Lack of detailed experimental validation and comparison to existing methods.
*   Insufficient discussion of the tightness of the proposed bounds.
*   Mathematical imprecision and notational confusion.
*   Formatting and presentation issues.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea is promising and addresses an important problem, the paper's numerous flaws prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The lack of mathematical rigor, combined with the minor formatting issues and insufficient experimental details, significantly diminish the contribution. I believe a major revision, addressing the points raised above, is necessary for this paper to be considered for NIPS. The paper has the *potential* to be a valuable contribution, but currently falls short of the standards expected for NIPS publication.

**Recommendation: Reject** (with encouragement to revise and resubmit).
