- Proposes novel nonspectral bounds on DPP likelihoods that avoid spectral decomposition, enabling efficient variational inference and MCMC.
- Provides theoretical bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs and draws connections to sparse Gaussian process inducing-variable methods.
- Develops practical inference methods leveraging these bounds and demonstrates how they remove the spectral bottleneck.
- Theoretical results (Propositions 1 and 2) are rigorously derived with detailed proofs.
- Experimental validation is comprehensive: synthetic Gaussian continuous experiment with ground truth and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset.
- Methods are shown to be accurate and efficient: MCMC converges to correct posterior and variational estimates improve with more inducing variables.
- Novelty: significantly different from prior spectral approaches and nontrivially extends inference from finite to continuous DPPs.
- Significance: addresses a key scalability limitation of DPPs and could broaden their practical applications (recommendation systems, image search, spatial stats).
- Clarity: paper is well-written, organized, and reproduci ble with consistent notation and clear GP connection.
- Weakness: limited empirical comparison to alternative approximate DPP inference methods (e.g., Nyström, fixed-point algorithms).
- Weakness: identifiability concerns when parametrizing L (κ not identifiable) are acknowledged but no practical solution is offered.
- Weakness: lacks sensitivity analysis on the choice/optimization of inducing points Z and robustness of the bounds.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, and practically useful contribution; reviewer recommends acceptance despite noted limitations.
