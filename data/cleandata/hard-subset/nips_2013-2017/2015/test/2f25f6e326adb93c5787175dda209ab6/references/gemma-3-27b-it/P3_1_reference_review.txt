## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix, which becomes intractable for large datasets or continuous domains. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood that circumvent this requirement, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques for Gaussian Processes. They demonstrate the applicability of these bounds within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, both for finite and continuous DPPs. The paper provides a theoretical foundation for these bounds and validates them through experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the growing body of work on DPPs for modeling diverse data (Kulesza & Taskar, 2012; Lavancier et al., 2016). It directly tackles the limitations of existing likelihood-based inference methods that rely on spectral decomposition (e.g., Affandi et al., 2015), offering a practical alternative. The use of bounds inspired by variational Gaussian process inference (Titsias, 2009) is a clever connection, and the retrospective sampling approach within MCMC is borrowed from prior work (e.g., Haario et al., 2001).  The work acknowledges and builds on the existing literature of approximating determinants, referencing relevant works.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Significant Practical Issue:** The avoidance of spectral decomposition is a major contribution. It unlocks the potential for applying DPPs to larger, more complex datasets where eigendecomposition is computationally prohibitive.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical justification for the proposed bounds, grounding them in established results on determinants. The extension to continuous DPPs is a valuable addition.
* **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly articulate the problem, their approach, and the key results.  The decision to present the finite and continuous cases separately improves readability.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while relatively limited, demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the proposed methods.  The comparison to existing approaches (in the 1D Gaussian case) and the application to a real-world dataset are encouraging.
* **Connection to Variational Inference and MCMC:** Showing how the bounds integrate into established inference frameworks increases the paper’s practicality and impact.
* **Identifiability Discussion**: The authors rightfully point out the difficulties in interpreting parameters when directly parametrizing the L kernel.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-Input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, the optimization of pseudo-inputs (Z) within variational inference and the retrospective sampling in MCMC introduces a new computational cost. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the trade-offs involved (e.g., how the optimal number of pseudo-inputs scales with problem size).
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments, particularly the real-world application, could be more extensive. A more comprehensive comparison to alternative DPP inference methods (e.g., variational approximations that *do* rely on spectral decomposition for smaller datasets) would strengthen the claims.
* **Sensitivity to Pseudo-Input Initialization:** The performance of the proposed methods likely depends on the initialization of the pseudo-inputs.  The paper doesn’t discuss this sensitivity or strategies for robust initialization.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:**  The continuous DPP extension relies on assumptions about the kernel and base measure (trace-class operator, Mercer’s theorem).  The limitations of these assumptions are not fully discussed.
* **Lack of Code Release**:  Reproducibility would be greatly enhanced by releasing the code used in the experiments.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important challenge in DPP inference. The proposed bounds offer a compelling alternative to spectral decomposition, opening up new possibilities for applying DPPs to large-scale problems. While the computational cost of pseudo-input optimization and the limited empirical evaluation are weaknesses, the theoretical soundness and clarity of the presentation make this a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a key practical bottleneck in DPP inference.
* Provides a theoretically sound and novel solution.
* Clearly presented and well-motivated.
* Demonstrates feasibility through experiments.
* Has the potential to broaden the applicability of DPPs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Computational cost of pseudo-input optimization needs further analysis.
*  Empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*  Sensitivity to pseudo-input initialization is not addressed.



**Rating:**  **Strong Accept (7/10)**.  I believe this paper makes a significant contribution that warrants acceptance to NIPS. Addressing the weaknesses identified above (particularly a more detailed analysis of the computational trade-offs and more extensive empirical validation) would further strengthen the paper.  Releasing the code would be highly beneficial.
