Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) when the eigendecomposition of the L-operator is unavailable or impractical. The main technical contribution is a pair of non-spectral bounds on det(I+L) for both finite and continuous DPPs, built via an inducing-point approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY in the spirit of variational sparse GPs (Titsias, AISTATS’09). For the finite case, the authors show det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I); for continuous DPPs, an analogous bound involves the Fredholm determinant and a computable matrix Ψ of L–L convolutions under the base measure. These bounds avoid eigenvalues and can be evaluated in O(n m^2) given m inducing points.

The bounds are used in two inference schemes: (1) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood (summing over realizations and plugging the lower bound on log det(I+L)), optimized over kernel parameters and inducing inputs; and (2) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler that uses upper/lower bounds on the acceptance probability and tightens them by growing m and re-optimizing Z until a decision is possible. The continuous case is worked out for Gaussian kernels/base measures where Ψ is analytic. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP with known spectrum (to validate MCMC) and a real 2D neuropathy dataset where the variational approach recovers separation between classes via overdispersion metrics, with runtimes reported.

Relative to prior DPP learning: (i) Affandi et al. (ICML’13) used spectral bounds (requiring leading eigenpairs) within retrospective MH; (ii) Gillenwater et al. (NIPS’14) and Mariet & Sra (NIPS’15) propose EM/fixed-point methods for discrete L-ensembles; (iii) Nyström approximations have been used (Affandi et al., AISTATS’13) but still typically rely on spectral components. This paper’s novelty is to remove spectral dependence entirely using determinant inequalities (Seiler & Simon, PNAS’75) and inducing points.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and novel non-spectral determinant bounds that are simple to compute and broadly applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Clear link to variational sparse GP methodology; the construction (Q and the trace correction) is familiar, giving intuition and potential for leveraging optimization heuristics from GPs.
- Retrospective MH adaptation is natural and correct when bounds can be tightened; avoids expensive power iterations and storage of large matrices of eigenvectors.
- Continuous-case treatment with Fredholm determinants and explicit Ψ for Gaussian/Gaussian is useful and bridges operator-theoretic and practical ML viewpoints.
- Experiments validate correctness (agreement with “ideal” MH on the 1D case) and show practical utility on the neuropathy dataset, with convergence of bounds as m grows.
- Pseudo-inputs appear to adapt meaningfully to the marginal support, hinting at an interpretable mechanism and potentially efficient placement strategies.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope and comparisons: no large-scale discrete DPP experiments despite the finite-setting motivation, and no direct comparison to spectral-bound MH (Affandi et al., 2013), EM/fixed-point learners, or Nyström baselines in terms of runtime/accuracy.
- Tightness and convergence of bounds: while intuition is given (as m increases, bounds tighten), there is no formal guarantee or diagnostic of termination rates for retrospective MH; practical guidance on m vs. n and Z optimization is limited.
- Identifiability issues are surfaced (e.g., κ) but not addressed methodologically (e.g., reparametrization, priors, or constraints that disentangle intensity vs. repulsion).
- For the continuous case, Ψ must be computable; outside Gaussian/Gaussian, the practicality is unclear. Numerical strategies (quadrature, random features) are not explored.
- Some presentation/notation issues: equation numbering and references are occasionally inconsistent; a few typographical errors; proofs are deferred entirely to the supplement.
- Optimization details for Z, gradients, and stability (log-determinant computations, conditioning, jitter) are not fully discussed.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The core inequalities appear sound (built on Seiler–Simon). The methodology is well-motivated and correctly adapted for variational and MH regimes. However, theoretical analysis of bound monotonicity/tightness and MH termination is informal. Empirical evaluation is convincing for correctness but limited in breadth and baselines.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and structured, with intuitive connections to sparse GPs. Minor issues with equation references/typos and some missing implementation details.
- Originality: High. To my knowledge, this is the first non-spectral determinant-bounding approach for DPP inference via inducing points, and the continuous extension is novel.
- Significance: Potentially impactful, as eigendecompositions are a key bottleneck in DPP learning. Demonstrations are promising, but broader empirical validation and comparisons are needed to establish practical dominance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add large-scale discrete DPP experiments (e.g., n ≥ 10^4) with comparisons to spectral-bound MH, EM/fixed-point, and Nyström approximations. Report wall-clock time, memory, and accuracy (log-likelihood, parameter error).
- Provide theory or at least empirical diagnostics for bound tightness vs. m and Z optimization, and conditions for almost-sure termination of retrospective MH; consider an adaptive m schedule with stopping rules based on bound gaps.
- Detail gradient computations for θ and Z; discuss numerical stability (Cholesky-based logdet, regularization of LZ, conditioning strategies).
- Explore Z initialization/placement strategies (k-means, k-DPP, farthest-point) and ablations; quantify sensitivity across runs.
- For the continuous case, discuss practical computation of Ψ beyond Gaussian/Gaussian: quadrature (Bornemann, 2010), random Fourier features, or kernel-specific identities; analyze resulting errors.
- Address identifiability: reparametrize to separate intensity and repulsion or impose informative priors; discuss learning K vs. L and possible connections without spectrum.
- Improve presentation: fix equation numbering, clarify assumptions for tr(L) = ∫ L(x,x) dµ, and expand the supplement with tighter bounds and proofs. Consider releasing code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, general, and simple non-spectral bounds enabling both variational and exact (MH) inference.
- Bridges finite and continuous DPPs with a unified technique; practically useful in settings where spectra are inaccessible.
- Promising empirical results and good conceptual clarity.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient empirical breadth and lack of strong baselines/comparisons, especially for the finite case which is a primary motivation.
- Limited theoretical guarantees on bound tightening and MH termination; practical guidance is sparse.
- Some clarity/notation issues and missing implementation details.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper proposes an elegant and potentially impactful idea, but the experimental validation and comparative analysis are not yet at the level expected for NeurIPS. With stronger empirical evaluation (especially on large discrete problems and against spectral/EM baselines), clearer guidance on optimization and tightness, and minor presentation fixes, this work would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
