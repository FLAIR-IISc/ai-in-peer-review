## NIPS Paper Review: Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator. The authors propose non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood, inspired by variational inference techniques for Gaussian Processes, applicable to both finite and continuous domains. They demonstrate how these bounds can be used within Variational Inference (VI) and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, avoiding the costly eigendecomposition. The paper includes experimental validation on a toy 1D Gaussian DPP and a real-world dataset of nerve fiber patterns.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the growing body of work leveraging DPPs for modeling diverse data (Kulesza & Taskar, 2012; Lavancier et al., 2016).  It acknowledges the common practice of parameterizing the kernel *L* directly to avoid spectral computation (Affandi et al., 2013; Gillenwater et al., 2015) and directly addresses the limitations of existing bounds (Affandi et al., 2017) that still require power iteration. The use of inducing variables for approximation echoes work in sparse Gaussian Processes (Titsias, 2009).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant practical problem:** The spectral decomposition is a major hurdle for scaling DPP inference to large datasets, and this work offers a viable alternative.
* **Sound theoretical foundation:** The derivation of the bounds, leveraging existing results on determinant inequalities, appears solid.  The extension to continuous domains is a valuable contribution.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The authors clearly motivate the problem and explain their approach.
* **Comprehensive inference frameworks:**  Demonstrating the applicability of the bounds within both VI and MCMC frameworks provides flexibility for users.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments, including a synthetic example with ground truth and a real-world dataset, provide evidence of the method's effectiveness. The convergence analysis in the real dataset is particularly convincing.
* **Connection to existing methods:** The paper clearly draws parallels to sparse Gaussian process methods, and makes useful comparisons to previous approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Interpretability concerns remain:** As the authors acknowledge, parameterizing *L* directly sacrifices interpretability. While this is a known trade-off, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of its implications. The results showing limited identification of κ in the toy example emphasize this point.
* **Computational cost of bound tightening:** While avoiding eigendecomposition is a major win, the optimization of the inducing inputs *Z* within the MCMC framework (retrospective sampling) could still be computationally expensive, especially for high-dimensional data.  A more detailed analysis of the scaling of this optimization would be beneficial.
* **Limited comparison to alternative approximations:** While the authors compare to Affandi et al. (2017), a more thorough comparison to other DPP approximation methods (e.g., those based on low-rank approximations of the kernel) would strengthen the paper.
* **Mathematical notation could be improved:** While generally clear, the notation in Section 4 (continuous DPPs) is dense and could benefit from more explanation.  Referring to key definitions and equations would improve readability.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and presents a novel contribution to DPP inference. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the experimental results are supportive.
* **Clarity (7/10):** The paper is generally well-written, but Section 4 could be improved with more detailed explanations and a more accessible notation.
* **Originality (8/10):**  The proposed non-spectral bounds represent a novel approach to DPP inference, overcoming a significant limitation of existing methods. The extension to continuous domains adds further originality.
* **Significance (7/10):**  The ability to perform likelihood-based inference for DPPs without spectral decomposition is a valuable contribution with the potential to broaden the applicability of DPPs to larger and more complex datasets.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and practical challenge in DPP inference.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound solution.
* Demonstrates effectiveness through both synthetic and real-world experiments.
* Clearly written and well-motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The loss of interpretability associated with parameterizing *L* directly remains a concern.
* The computational cost of bound tightening within MCMC warrants further investigation.
* A more comprehensive comparison to alternative approximation methods would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a relevant and important problem in the field of DPPs. The proposed solution is novel, theoretically justified, and empirically validated. While some areas could be improved (particularly regarding interpretability and computational cost analysis), the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  The authors should consider addressing the comments regarding interpretability, computational cost, and comparison to alternative methods.
