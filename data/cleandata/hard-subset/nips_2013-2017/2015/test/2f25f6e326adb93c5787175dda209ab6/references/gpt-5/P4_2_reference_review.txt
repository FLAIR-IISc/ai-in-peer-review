Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) when the spectral decomposition of the L-operator is unavailable or impractical. For DPPs on finite sets, the likelihood involves det(L+I) in the normalization and det(LY) for observed subsets; for continuous DPPs, the Fredholm determinant det(I+L) appears. Prior work typically parameterizes L to ensure validity and exploits spectral decompositions or Nyström approximations; e.g., Affandi et al. (ICML 2013) use spectral bounds and power iterations to approximate leading eigenvalues, and Affandi et al. (AISTATS 2013) use Nyström for finite domains. The authors propose non-spectral bounds for det(L+I) derived via an inducing-point approximation Q = LYZ LZ−1 LZY and a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975). The bounds yield
det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I)
for finite domains (Proposition 1), and analogous operator bounds for continuous DPPs (Proposition 2) involving LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(zi, x)L(x, zj)dµ(x). These bounds enable:
- a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood F(θ, Z) computable in O(n m^2) time, and
- a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme using lower/upper bounds on the acceptance ratio that can be tightened by increasing m and re-optimizing Z.

Experiments include: (i) a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where the spectrum and det(I+L) are known, showing retrospective MH matches an “ideal” MH and highlighting identifiability issues when parameterizing L; and (ii) a 2D real dataset (diabetic neuropathy), where variational training with different numbers of inducing points yields consistent overdispersion measures separating two clinical classes, and bound tightness improves with m. The approach is conceptually linked to variational sparse GPs (Titsias, AISTATS 2009): the construction of Q and the trace correction mirror GP bounds on log determinants and predictive variances.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The finite and continuous bounds are grounded in a known determinant inequality and standard operator assumptions (trace-class L; Fredholm determinant well-defined). The finite-domain complexity claims are plausible: computing tr(L−Q) reduces to O(n m^2) using matrix identities and avoids O(n^3) eigendecompositions. The continuous setting carefully states conditions (e.g., tr(L) = ∫ L(x, x)dµ(x)) and identifies cases where Ψ is computable in closed form (Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure).
- Algorithms: The variational bound is straightforward to optimize over θ and Z; the MH variant uses standard retrospective sampling to resolve accept/reject decisions. Both are consistent with the proposed bounds. However, convergence/tightness guarantees as m→∞ are only asserted informally; a more rigorous statement or conditions would strengthen the contribution.
- Empirical support: The synthetic continuous example is appropriate for validating correctness against ground truth. The real-data experiment demonstrates practical usability and bound convergence with m. Nonetheless, experimental breadth is limited: there is no comparison to spectral methods [Affandi et al., ICML 2013], to Nyström-based finite-domain approximations [Affandi et al., AISTATS 2013], or to other DPP learning approaches (e.g., EM or fixed-point methods [Gillenwater et al.; Mariet & Sra]). No finite-domain experiment is provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear motivation and consistent notation across finite and continuous settings. The connection to sparse GPs is well explained. Some aspects could be clearer:
- Provide explicit algorithmic descriptions (pseudocode) for the variational optimization of Z and for retrospective MH.
- Detail how gradients w.r.t. Z and θ are computed (or justify using derivative-free optimizers), and how Z is initialized.
- In the continuous case, discuss when Ψ is tractable and how to handle cases without closed forms (e.g., numerical quadrature or further bounds).
- A few editorial issues (missing years in references, minor typos) should be corrected.

Originality
The main idea—non-spectral, inducing-point-based determinant bounds for DPP normalization constants—and their use in both variational inference and retrospective MH appear novel. Extending these ideas to continuous DPPs with operator-theoretic bounds on Fredholm determinants is also original and nontrivial. The approach is a principled transfer of variational GP techniques to DPPs but addresses a different mathematical object (normalization determinant rather than marginal likelihood of GPs).

Significance
Removing reliance on spectral decompositions substantially broadens the applicability of DPP inference, particularly on large discrete domains and continuous spaces where eigenanalysis is infeasible. The method is computationally attractive (O(n m^2)) and flexible via optimization of inducing inputs. The potential impact is high, but the empirical validation is modest. Stronger evidence (scalability on large n, comparisons to spectral baselines, discrete-domain tasks) would better establish practical significance.

Pros
- Conceptually clean, general nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods in finite and continuous settings.
- Enables both fast variational training and exact posterior inference via retrospective MH.
- Clear connection to and extension of sparse GP methodology.
- Demonstrated bound-tightening with m and practical runtimes; correctness validated on a case with known ground truth.

Cons
- Limited empirical evaluation: no comparisons to leading spectral or Nyström baselines; no large-scale discrete experiments.
- No formal tightness/convergence analysis of bounds as m increases beyond informal arguments.
- Applicability in continuous settings hinges on tractable Ψ; guidance for general kernels/measures is limited.
- Optimization of inducing locations is nonconvex; practical strategies and robustness are not deeply analyzed.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide theoretical conditions under which the bounds converge to the true determinant as m→∞, and rates if available?
- For continuous DPPs, can Ψ be approximated efficiently via quadrature or random features, and how would this affect bounds (introducing additional approximation error)?
- Please add comparisons to spectral MH [Affandi et al., ICML 2013] and to Nyström-based methods on at least one finite-domain dataset, reporting accuracy, runtime, and memory.
- Include a finite-domain experiment (e.g., summarization or recommendation) to demonstrate generality.
- Provide implementation details: initialization of Z, gradient expressions, and sensitivity to m and optimizer choice.

Overall assessment
A technically sound and original contribution that removes a major bottleneck in DPP inference by avoiding spectral decompositions. The paper is clear and the ideas are likely to be useful. Strengthening the empirical section with baseline comparisons and at least one finite-domain experiment would materially improve the case for acceptance.
