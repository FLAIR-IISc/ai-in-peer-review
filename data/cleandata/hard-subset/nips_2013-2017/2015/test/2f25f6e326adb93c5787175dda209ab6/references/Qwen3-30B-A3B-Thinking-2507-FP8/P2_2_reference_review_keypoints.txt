- Summary: Proposes non-spectral likelihood bounds for DPP inference using pseudo-inputs, enabling efficient variational inference and MCMC without spectral decomposition, extended to continuous domains and validated empirically.

- Strength — Technical novelty: Derives theoretically sound, non-spectral bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) that avoid expensive spectral decomposition, addressing a major bottleneck in DPP inference.

- Strength — Connection to GP literature: Builds on variational inducing-variable ideas from Gaussian processes, making the approach accessible and well-motivated.

- Strength — Clarity and organization: Exceptionally well-written, clearly develops finite-to-continuous extensions and presents experiments coherently.

- Strength — Significance: Broadens practical applicability of likelihood-based DPP inference to larger and continuous problems, with convincing toy and real-data results.

- Strength — Practical treatment: Shows application to variational inference and MCMC and discusses identifiability when parametrizing L directly.

- Weakness — Comparisons: Lacks quantitative comparisons with other approximate DPP inference methods (e.g., fixed-point algorithms, EM) beyond spectral-based work.

- Weakness — Scalability analysis: Insufficient discussion and concrete analysis of how the number of pseudo-inputs m affects scalability for large datasets.

- Weakness — Identifiability depth: Notes identifiability issues when parametrizing L but doesn't deeply explain causes or mitigation strategies.

- Weakness — Positioning in literature: Could better situate and compare the approach within the broader DPP inference literature and recent related methods.

- Overall assessment: Strong, technically sound contribution with novel theoretical and practical impact, despite some empirical and discussion gaps.

- Recommendation: Accept for NIPS — quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria are met.
