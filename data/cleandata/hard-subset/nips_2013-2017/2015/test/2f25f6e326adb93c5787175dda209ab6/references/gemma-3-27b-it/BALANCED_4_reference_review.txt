This paper addresses the computationally challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), specifically avoiding the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel. The authors provide bounds on the DPP likelihood and demonstrate their application in variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods, extending existing techniques from Gaussian Processes to the DPP setting. They also generalize their results to continuous DPPs.

The core idea – leveraging bounds on determinants to circumvent eigendecomposition – is a significant contribution. The connection to variational inference for GPs is well-motivated, and the extension to DPPs is a natural and valuable step. The work is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem definition to experimental validation. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work and position their contributions within the existing literature. The inclusion of both theoretical results and empirical demonstrations strengthens the paper. The continuous DPP extension, while potentially more complex for some readers (as acknowledged by the authors), expands the applicability of the proposed methods. 

However, several aspects could be improved. Firstly, while the motivation for avoiding spectral decomposition is clear, the practical impact of this avoidance isn’t always fully conveyed. The paper often states improvements in computational cost, but concrete comparisons (e.g., runtime on datasets of varying sizes) against methods *requiring* eigendecomposition would be highly valuable.  Secondly, the discussion of the limitations of directly parameterizing the kernel L (vs. K) feels somewhat repeated and could be more concise. While the trade-offs are mentioned, a deeper exploration of potential strategies for mitigating the interpretability issues would be welcome. Thirdly, the experimental section, while thorough, leans heavily on reproducing results from [7]. While validation against existing work is important, the authors should more strongly emphasize the unique contributions of *their* method in the experimental setup. For example, a comparative analysis of the MCMC performance with and without the proposed bounds (even on a smaller dataset) would be insightful. Fourthly, the connection between the choice of pseudo-inputs and the quality of the bounds isn't fully explored.  A more detailed analysis of how the pseudo-inputs affect the tightness of the bounds, and potentially strategies for more intelligent pseudo-input selection, would strengthen the theoretical foundations. Finally, the notation, while generally clear, could be made more consistent, particularly with regard to kernel definitions (K vs. L) and the use of subscripts.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations appear correct and are supported by relevant citations. The extension of GP variational bounds to DPPs is logically sound.
*   **Originality:** Avoiding spectral decomposition for DPP inference is a novel and practically relevant contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the concepts are explained clearly, even for those not deeply familiar with DPPs.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental bottleneck in DPP inference, potentially enabling applications to larger and more complex datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Demonstration of Speedup:**  The paper lacks direct runtime comparisons with methods requiring spectral decomposition.
*   **Repetitive Discussion of L vs K:** The trade-offs of parameterizing L are reiterated without proposing solutions.
*   **Reliance on Existing Results in Experiments:** Validation largely focuses on reproducing known results, rather than demonstrating unique advantages.
*   **Insufficient Exploration of Pseudo-Input Influence:** The role and optimization of pseudo-inputs deserve more detailed attention.
*   **Minor Notational Inconsistencies:**  Some notational elements could be streamlined for clarity.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant practical limitation of DPP inference.
*   The proposed bounds are mathematically sound and well-motivated.
*   The methods are clearly explained and implemented.
*   The work is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working with DPPs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical validation, while thorough, doesn't fully demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed method.
*   The discussion of limitations and potential extensions could be more in-depth.
*   The exploration of pseudo-input selection is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs. The proposed bounds offer a practical solution to a significant computational challenge. While the empirical validation could be strengthened, and some aspects of the discussion could be more nuanced, the technical soundness and originality of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly focusing on providing more concrete runtime comparisons and expanding the discussion of pseudo-input optimization.
