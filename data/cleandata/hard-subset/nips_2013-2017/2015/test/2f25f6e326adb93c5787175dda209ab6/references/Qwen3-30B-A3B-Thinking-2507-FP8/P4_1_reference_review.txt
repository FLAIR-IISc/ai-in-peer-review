

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental computational challenge in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The authors identify that existing methods for DPP inference require spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L, which is computationally prohibitive for large domains. They propose non-spectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid this requirement, drawing inspiration from sparse approximations used in Gaussian processes. The paper provides theoretical bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs (Propositions 1 and 2), demonstrates their application in variational inference and MCMC frameworks, and validates the approach on synthetic and real-world datasets. This work builds on prior NIPS contributions like Affandi et al. (2013) and Gillenwater et al. (2012), which focused on spectral-based methods for DPP inference, but addresses a key computational limitation that has hindered wider application of likelihood-based methods.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous mathematical derivations for the proposed bounds (Propositions 1 and 2), supported by references to relevant determinant inequalities. The theoretical framework is well-grounded in existing literature on Gaussian processes and operator theory.

2. **Novelty**: The non-spectral approach represents a significant departure from prior work that relied on spectral decomposition (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013). The extension to continuous domains is particularly valuable, as previous work primarily focused on finite settings.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The authors provide thorough experimental validation across both synthetic (Gaussian continuous experiment) and real-world (diabetic neuropathy dataset) scenarios. The results demonstrate convergence of the variational approach with increasing number of pseudo-inputs, and the MCMC approach successfully recovers true parameters in the synthetic case.

4. **Practical Impact**: The proposed methods significantly reduce computational complexity for DPP inference. The variational approach achieves O(nm²) complexity (vs. O(n³) for spectral methods), making likelihood-based inference feasible for larger datasets.

5. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized, with clear section headings and logical progression from theoretical development to experimental validation. The connection to Gaussian process literature (Section 3.1) helps readers unfamiliar with DPPs understand the approach.

## Weaknesses
1. **Parameter Identifiability**: The paper acknowledges that parametrizing L directly rather than K leads to identifiability issues (e.g., κ in the continuous case), but does not provide a comprehensive discussion of how this limitation affects the practical utility of the method.

2. **Pseudo-input Selection**: While the paper optimizes over pseudo-inputs Z to tighten bounds, it doesn't thoroughly explore how different initializations or strategies for selecting Z affect convergence and inference quality. The observation that pseudo-inputs appear to "evenly spread" across the marginal distribution (Figure 1a) suggests an interesting property that merits deeper investigation.

3. **Comparison with Alternatives**: The paper contrasts its approach with spectral-based methods (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013), but doesn't compare with other approximate inference techniques for DPPs, such as those based on Monte Carlo methods without spectral decomposition.

4. **Continuous Domain Theoretical Justification**: While the continuous case is well-motivated, the theoretical justification for the Fredholm determinant bounds (Proposition 2) could be strengthened with more detailed discussion of the assumptions required for the bounds to hold.

## Quality Assessment
The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical derivations are correct and properly referenced, and the experimental results validate the proposed approach. The authors are transparent about limitations (e.g., parameter identifiability issues when parametrizing L), which demonstrates scientific honesty. The work represents a complete piece of research rather than a position paper.

## Clarity Assessment
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The connection to Gaussian process literature helps bridge understanding for readers familiar with that domain. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The supplementary material provides necessary proof details, though some sections (e.g., Section 4) may require familiarity with operator theory for full comprehension.

## Originality Assessment
The non-spectral approach to DPP inference represents a clear departure from prior NIPS work. The paper properly contextualizes its contribution relative to related work (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013; Gillenwater et al., 2012), and demonstrates that their method addresses a significant computational bottleneck that has limited the application of likelihood-based DPP inference. The extension to continuous domains further differentiates this work from previous contributions.

## Significance Assessment
The proposed method has significant practical implications for DPP applications, particularly for large-scale or continuous domains where spectral decomposition is infeasible. The ability to perform likelihood-based inference without spectral decomposition opens new possibilities for DPP applications in diverse domains (e.g., spatial statistics, recommendation systems). The experimental validation on real-world data (diabetic neuropathy dataset) demonstrates clear utility beyond synthetic examples.

## Recommendation
The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by providing a novel, computationally efficient approach to DPP inference. The theoretical development is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the method addresses a genuine limitation in existing DPP inference techniques. While there are some limitations regarding parameter identifiability and pseudo-input selection, these are acknowledged by the authors and do not undermine the core contribution.

Pro: Novel non-spectral bounds for DPP inference; significant computational efficiency gains; thorough experimental validation; clear theoretical foundation.

Con: Limited discussion of parameter identifiability issues; some aspects of pseudo-input selection could be explored more deeply; comparison with alternative inference methods is limited.

Overall, this paper represents a significant advance in DPP inference methodology that would be of interest to the NIPS community. It meets all four criteria for quality (technical soundness), clarity (well-organized and well-written), originality (novel non-spectral approach), and significance (practical utility for large-scale applications). The work advances the state of the art in DPP inference in a demonstrable way.
