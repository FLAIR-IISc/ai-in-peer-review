- Tackles likelihood-based inference for DPPs without spectral decompositions by deriving nonspectral upper/lower bounds on det(I+L) for finite and continuous domains.
- Proposes an inducing-point low-rank surrogate Q and proves bounds det(Q+I)^{-1} ≤ det(L+I)^{-1} ≤ e^{-tr(L−Q)} det(Q+I)^{-1}.
- Derives a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood analogous to Titsias sparse GP and a retrospective MH scheme that tightens bounds by increasing the number of inducing points m.
- Extends results to continuous DPPs by bounding Fredholm determinants using determinants of LZ and a computable Ψ under trace-class and mild measure assumptions.
- Experiments: 1D Gaussian-Gaussian toy validates retrospective MH and exposes κ non-identifiability; 2D neuropathy dataset shows overdispersion estimates stabilize with increasing m and separate classes.
- Novelty: new transposition of inducing-point variational ideas from GPs to DPP determinants and nonspectral determinant bounds for finite and continuous settings.
- Relation to prior work: departs from spectral-dependent discrete DPP learning (e.g., Affandi et al., EM/fixed-point) enabling continuous-domain inference.
- Theory relies on Seiler–Simon determinant inequalities and is plausible, but full proofs are assumed in the supplement.
- Major theoretical gap: no formal guarantee of monotone tightening or convergence of bounds as m increases, important for VI bias and retrospective MH correctness.
- Key assumptions (trace-class L, tr(L)=∫L(x,x)dµ(x)) are not universally valid and conditions for closed-form Ψ are only partially discussed.
- Numerical stability concerns (ill-conditioning of LZ and need for jitter/preconditioning) are not addressed.
- Algorithmic claim: variational objective is computationally attractive (O(n m^2) plus |Yt|-scale determinants) but cost terms like tr(L−Q) need explicit inclusion.
- Retrospective MH is a natural nonspectral extension but lacks formal correctness guarantees under finite-m sequences.
- Empirics limited: no direct comparisons to spectral or Nyström baselines, no quantitative reporting of bound gaps vs m or monotonicity, and limited large-scale scalability evidence.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized, but propositions need clearer inequality directions, assumptions, and intuition from Seiler–Simon; optimization of Z needs practical guidance.
- Practical guidance missing: gradient formulas for Z/θ, initialization/regularization strategies, and handling Ψ via quadrature/MC with error control are not provided.
- Suggested improvements: add theoretical/empirical evidence of bound monotonicity/convergence, compare against spectral methods on discrete problems, and implement stabilization for LZ.
- Significance: potentially high—avoiding spectra broadens applicability to continuous DPPs and large discrete problems if bounds tighten reliably with moderate m.
- Pros: conceptually elegant nonspectral bounds, practical variational and MH algorithms, clear linkage to sparse GP methodology, and initial empirical validation.
- Cons: lack of formal convergence/monotonicity guarantees, limited empirical baselines/scalability tests, underexplored practicalities (conditioning, gradients, Ψ computation), and unresolved identifiability issues (κ).
- Recommendation: Weak accept contingent on correct supplemental proofs; stronger theoretical guarantees and empirical validation would increase impact.
