

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary
This paper proposes "nonspectral bounds" for likelihood-based inference of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) that avoid spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L. The authors derive bounds on the likelihood determinant for both finite and continuous DPPs, and demonstrate their use in variational inference and MCMC. The work builds on existing approaches for GP inference but extends them to DPPs.

## Strengths
The paper identifies a genuine computational bottleneck in DPP inference (the need for spectral decomposition) and proposes a theoretically sound approach to address it. The derivation of bounds for continuous DPPs is novel, and the experimental validation on both synthetic and real data demonstrates practical utility. The connection to variational inference for GPs is well-maintained, and the paper clearly articulates the trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational cost.

## Weaknesses

### Fundamental Conceptual Confusion
The paper suffers from a fundamental confusion between the kernel K and L that undermines its entire theoretical foundation. In standard DPP theory (e.g., [5]), the likelihood is expressed in terms of kernel K as P(Y = Y1) = det(KY1)/det(I+K), not det(LY1)/det(L+I) as claimed in equation (2). The paper correctly notes that L = (I-K)^{-1}K, but then uses L in the likelihood formula without proper transformation. This is a critical error that would mislead readers about the correct formulation of DPP likelihoods.

### Technical Errors
1. In equation (3), the squared exponential kernel is written as "K(x,y) = aK exp ( −‖x− y‖ 2 σ2K )" - the exponent is missing a division symbol and should be "‖x− y‖^2 / σ^2K".

2. In section 4.1, the paper states "tr(L) = ∫ X L(x,x)dµ(x)" but then references "(14)", which is not a properly numbered equation in the text.

3. The paper claims "the Fredholm determinant det(I + L) in this special case is a q-Pochhammer symbol" (section 5.1), which is incorrect. The Fredholm determinant of a Gaussian kernel is related to theta functions, not q-Pochhammer symbols.

### Inconsistent Terminology and Presentation
1. The paper claims to "avoid spectral knowledge" but the bounds still require computing the matrix L, which is the same as standard approaches. The key innovation (avoiding spectral decomposition) is not clearly demonstrated.

2. The paper states "the computational bottleneck in the evaluation of (2) is computing det(L + I)" but this is incorrect - the bottleneck is computing the determinant of the full matrix L+I, which is O(n^3), not the matrix L itself.

3. In the abstract, "determinantal point processes" is misspelled as "determinantal point processes".

### Experimental Limitations
1. The variational inference results show poor consistency (κ varying over orders of magnitude), but the paper doesn't adequately address this as a limitation of the model or parameterization, rather than the method itself.

2. The paper doesn't compare its approach to Nyström approximation methods for DPPs, which are standard for avoiding spectral decomposition.

3. The claim that "the spectral decomposition of L is known" for the Gaussian kernel (section 5.1) is misleading - this is true only for very specific cases, not generally applicable to DPPs.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper identifies an important computational challenge in DPP inference, it suffers from fundamental theoretical errors that undermine its core contribution. The confusion between kernels K and L is particularly troubling, as it suggests a misunderstanding of basic DPP theory. The technical errors and inconsistencies in presentation further diminish the paper's credibility.

The paper's claims about "nonspectral bounds" are not well-supported by the mathematics presented. The bounds still require computing the full matrix L, which is the same computational burden as the standard approach. The paper fails to demonstrate how these bounds actually avoid spectral decomposition, which was the stated goal.

The experimental section has limitations in its comparison to prior work and doesn't adequately address the poor identifiability of parameters observed in the variational inference results.

## Recommendation
Reject

The paper's fundamental theoretical confusion between kernels K and L, combined with multiple technical errors and inconsistent presentation, makes it difficult to assess the validity of the proposed method. While the problem addressed is important, the paper does not provide a sound solution to the stated problem. The errors are substantial enough to undermine the entire contribution, and the paper would need major revision to address these issues before being suitable for publication.
