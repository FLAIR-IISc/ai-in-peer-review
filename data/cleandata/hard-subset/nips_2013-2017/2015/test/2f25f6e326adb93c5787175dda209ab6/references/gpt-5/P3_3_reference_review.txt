Summary
The paper tackles likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) when the spectrum of the L-operator is unavailable or impractical to compute. The central idea is to derive nonspectral upper and lower bounds on det(I + L) that can be evaluated without eigendecompositions, both in finite and continuous settings. In the finite case, the authors introduce an inducing-point low-rank surrogate Q = LYZ LZ−1 LZY (with Z arbitrary in Rd) and prove bounds of the form det(Q + I)−1 ≤ det(L + I)−1 ≤ e−tr(L−Q) det(Q + I)−1. This leads to: (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood that is reminiscent of the Titsias variational free energy for sparse GPs, and (ii) a retrospective MH scheme that accepts/rejects proposals using progressively tightened bounds by increasing the number of inducing points. They extend these results to continuous DPPs by bounding the Fredholm determinant det(I + L) via determinants of LZ and a computable Ψ = ∫ L(zi, x)L(x, zj)dµ(x), under trace-class assumptions on L and mild conditions on µ. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian toy setting (with known spectrum) and a real 2D diabetic neuropathy dataset. The toy shows variational difficulties (non-identifiability of κ) but validates the retrospective MH against an “ideal” MH; the real-data experiment shows that overdispersion measures estimated by the variational method stabilize with increasing m and separate classes, consistent with prior work.

Relation to prior work
- Discrete DPP parameter learning has relied on spectral decompositions or approximations: Affandi et al. (ICML’14) use spectral bounds with power iterations; EM/fixed-point methods (Gillenwater et al.; Mariet & Sra) target discrete L-ensembles. This paper departs by not using spectra at all, enabling continuous-domain inference.
- The inducing-point construction and bound are directly inspired by sparse GP variational methods (Titsias, AISTATS’09), but adapted to DPP determinants. The extension to Fredholm determinants relates to numerical quadrature methods (Bornemann’10), which the authors cite as future connections.

Quality
- Theory: The propositions hinge on Seiler–Simon determinant inequalities. Assuming the supplementary material contains full proofs, the finite- and continuous-domain bounds are plausible and technically interesting. However:
  - Tightness and convergence: The paper asserts that bounds can be “arbitrarily refined” by increasing m and optimizing Z, but does not provide a formal guarantee (e.g., monotone tightening of upper/lower bounds with m, or conditions ensuring convergence to the true determinant). This is important for the correctness of retrospective MH and for understanding VI bias.
  - Conditions: Proposition 2 relies on trace-class L and the identity tr(L) = ∫ L(x, x)dµ(x), which do not hold universally; the assumptions under which Ψ is available in closed form are only partially discussed (Gaussian-Gaussian). Guidance for non-closed-form Ψ (e.g., quadrature/MC with error control) is missing.
  - Numerical stability: Inverting LZ can be ill-conditioned; strategies (jitter, preconditioning) are not discussed.
- Algorithms: The variational objective F(θ, Z) is well-motivated and computationally attractive (O(n m^2), plus |Yt|-scale determinants for the numerator). The retrospective MH scheme is a natural extension of [Affandi et al.] replacing spectral by nonspectral bounds.
- Empirics: The toy validates the MH with ground truth and highlights κ non-identifiability under L-parameterization. The neuropathy study shows convergence of overdispersion metrics with increasing m and a class separation consistent with prior findings. However:
  - No direct comparison to spectral methods where feasible (e.g., discrete synthetic datasets) to quantify tightness/efficiency trade-offs.
  - No quantitative evaluation of bound gaps vs m, monotonicity behavior, or diagnostic of how often m needs to increase in MH.
  - Limited scalability evidence (e.g., large n discrete problems); runtimes are reported only for the 2D continuous case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The finite vs continuous developments are separated cleanly. A few concerns:
- Inequality directions and assumptions in Propositions 1–2 should be stated with care; including key steps or intuition from the Seiler–Simon inequality would help.
- Complexity statements should explicitly include the cost of computing tr(L − Q) and LZY/LYZ, and costs for multiple realizations.
- Practical guidance on optimizing Z (gradients vs black-box, initialization, regularization) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
High. The transposition of inducing-point variational ideas from GPs to DPP likelihood determinants and the derivation of nonspectral determinant bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs appear novel. Using these bounds in a retrospective MH scheme to avoid spectral computations is a neat generalization of prior spectral approaches.

Significance
Potentially significant. Avoiding spectral decompositions meaningfully broadens applicability to continuous DPPs and to discrete problems where eigendecompositions are prohibitive. If the bounds tighten reliably with moderate m, this could become a practical tool. Current empirical evidence is promising but somewhat limited; stronger quantitative comparisons and theoretical tightening guarantees would substantially increase impact.

Pros
- Conceptually elegant, nonspectral determinant bounds applicable in finite and continuous settings.
- Practical inference algorithms (variational and MH) with clear computational benefits.
- Solid linkage to sparse GP methodology; new angle for continuous DPPs.
- Empirical validation includes an exact-spectrum toy and a real dataset; observations about identifiability are honest and useful.

Cons
- No formal guarantee of monotone/tight convergence of bounds with m or of exactness of retrospective MH under finite m sequences.
- Limited empirical comparisons to spectral or Nyström baselines; lack of large-scale discrete experiments.
- Practical aspects underexplored: conditioning of LZ, gradient computation for Z, handling Ψ when not closed form.
- The known identifiability issues with L-parameterization (e.g., κ) are acknowledged but not mitigated (e.g., using k-DPPs or reparameterizations).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical results (or at least empirical evidence) on monotonicity/tightness of bounds with increasing m and on conditions ensuring convergence as m→∞ and Z optimized.
- Add comparisons against spectral methods on discrete problems where power iterations are feasible; report accuracy vs time and bound gaps.
- Discuss and implement stabilization (jitter) for LZ, and provide gradient expressions w.r.t. Z and θ for common kernels.
- For continuous settings without closed-form Ψ, describe quadrature/MC approximations and how their error affects the bounds and MH correctness.
- Consider addressing κ non-identifiability via priors/constraints or by moving to fixed-cardinality (k-DPP) models.

Recommendation: Weak accept, assuming proofs in the supplement are correct. The theoretical idea is novel and promising, and the continuous-domain applicability is particularly compelling, but the paper would benefit from tighter theoretical guarantees and stronger empirical validation.

Confidence: High on context and methodology; medium on the unverified proofs.
