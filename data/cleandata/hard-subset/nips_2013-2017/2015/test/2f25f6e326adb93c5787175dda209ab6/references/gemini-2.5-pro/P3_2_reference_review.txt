### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based parametric inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the evaluation of the term `det(L + I)`. This term, present in the DPP likelihood, typically requires an O(n³) spectral decomposition for finite DPPs, or is analytically intractable for continuous DPPs. The authors propose novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant. The key idea is inspired by variational sparse approximations for Gaussian Processes (GPs), utilizing a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" to construct a low-rank approximation `Q` to the kernel matrix `L`. These bounds can be tightened by increasing `m` and optimizing the locations of the pseudo-inputs. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference paradigms: (1) a fast, approximate variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes the evidence lower bound, and (2) an asymptotically exact Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method using retrospective sampling. The proposed methods are developed for both finite and continuous DPPs and are validated on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating their effectiveness in parameter inference while avoiding costly spectral computations.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and crucial problem in the practical application of DPPs. Avoiding spectral decomposition is a major step towards making DPPs a more scalable and widely usable tool for modeling repulsion. The proposed methods significantly lower the computational barrier for parametric inference, moving from O(n³) per likelihood evaluation to O(nm²) for VI, which is a substantial improvement when `m << n`.

2.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Approach:** While the use of inducing points is a familiar concept in the GP literature (e.g., Titsias, 2009), its application to bound the determinant in the DPP likelihood is novel and elegant. The connection is non-trivial and relies on a specific determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975). The extension of this idea from the finite matrix case to the continuous operator case (bounding the Fredholm determinant) is a strong technical contribution, broadening the applicability of the method.

3.  **Technical Quality and Completeness:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the bounds is clearly laid out (with proofs in the supplementary material). The authors provide two distinct inference frameworks (VI and MCMC), which addresses the trade-off between computational cost and accuracy. This makes the contribution more complete and useful for a wider range of applications. The experimental evaluation is well-designed, using a synthetic problem with a known ground truth to validate the MCMC sampler and a real-world problem to demonstrate the practical utility and convergence properties of the VI approach.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, and the structure is logical. Introducing the concepts first for the simpler finite case before moving to the more technical continuous case is an excellent pedagogical choice that makes the paper accessible. The connection to sparse GPs is explicitly stated, which helps orient readers familiar with that field.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Optimization of Variational Parameters:** The paper states that the variational lower bound `F(θ, Z)` is maximized jointly with respect to kernel parameters `θ` and pseudo-inputs `Z`, suggesting an "EM-like scheme" or black-box optimizers. This joint optimization can be challenging and prone to local optima. The experiments show that the pseudo-inputs move significantly to form neat grids, which suggests a complex optimization landscape. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of this optimization procedure. For instance:
    *   How are the pseudo-inputs `Z` initialized? Randomly from the data? On a grid?
    *   What were the practical challenges encountered during optimization? Is the procedure sensitive to initialization?
    *   A brief discussion on the properties of the objective `F(θ, Z)` would be valuable.

2.  **Guidance on Choosing `m`:** The number of inducing points, `m`, controls the trade-off between approximation accuracy and computational cost. The experiments show that increasing `m` tightens the bounds and leads to more stable parameter estimates. However, the paper only offers the heuristic of increasing `m` until results stabilize. While practical, it would be beneficial to discuss if more principled methods for choosing `m` could be adapted from the sparse GP literature, or if the gap between the upper and lower bounds itself could be used to guide the choice of `m` during inference.

3.  **L-Kernel vs. K-Kernel Parametrization:** The authors acknowledge the known issue of parametrizing the L-kernel for tractability at the cost of interpretability. While this is not the core focus of the paper, the experiments highlight this problem vividly: the MCMC experiment shows that the scale parameter `κ` is completely unidentifiable from the likelihood. This is an important negative result. The paper could frame this more explicitly as a general caution for practitioners using the L-parametrization, reinforcing that even with exact inference machinery like MCMC, some parameters may not be learnable. This is not a flaw of the proposed method, but a broader issue the paper could comment on more strongly.

4.  **Comparison to Spectral Methods:** The paper convincingly argues against spectral methods on computational grounds. However, for the MCMC experiment, it would have been insightful to include a timing comparison against the spectral-bound approach of Affandi et al. (2014). While the authors' method is expected to be faster when many eigenvalues are needed, a quantitative comparison on the toy problem would have concretely demonstrated the practical advantage.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant and practical contribution to the literature on determinantal point processes. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method for scalable parametric inference. The work is clearly presented and thoroughly evaluated. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and relate more to providing additional practical guidance and discussion rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a key bottleneck in DPP inference, making them more practical.
*   Novel and clever transfer of ideas from the sparse GP literature.
*   Technically solid, covering both finite and continuous DPPs.
*   Provides both fast (VI) and exact (MCMC) inference schemes.
*   Well-written with convincing experimental validation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The core concepts (inducing points, retrospective sampling) are not new, though their application here is.
*   Could provide more practical detail on the optimization of the variational parameters and the choice of `m`.

The arguments for acceptance far outweigh the arguments against. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and the proposed methods are likely to be widely used by researchers and practitioners working with DPPs. I strongly recommend acceptance.
