Summary
This paper proposes nonspectral upper and lower bounds on the log normalizer in DPP likelihoods that avoid eigendecompositions. For finite ground sets, the authors construct a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ L−1Z LZY using inducing points Z (not necessarily a subset of Y) and derive bounds of the form det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I), leveraging a determinant inequality (Seiler–Simon). They use these bounds to (i) form a variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood, jointly optimizing kernel parameters and inducing inputs, and (ii) implement a retrospective MH sampler by tightening bounds (increasing m and optimizing Z) until an accept/reject decision is unambiguous. They extend the bounds to continuous DPPs via operator-theoretic analogs, requiring trace-class assumptions and giving closed-form Ψ terms for Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian synthetic example (where spectral quantities are known) and a real 2D diabetic neuropathy dataset; they show that (a) variational optimization may be unstable for some parameters, while retrospective MH matches “ideal” MH on the synthetic task; and (b) on the real data, variational estimates of overdispersion γd stabilize as m increases and separate two clinical classes.

Relation to prior work
The spectral MH of Affandi et al. (ICML’13) relies on leading eigenvalues; this paper replaces spectral quantities with inducing-point-based bounds similar in spirit to variational sparse GP bounds (Titsias AISTATS’09). The continuous Fredholm determinant bound parallels quadrature-based work (Bornemann 2010) but is derived via determinant inequalities. The connection to variational GP machinery is apt and timely for DPPs where spectra are unavailable.

Strengths
- A simple, general recipe to avoid eigendecompositions for both finite and continuous DPPs, with clear computational advantages when m ≪ n.
- The finite-domain bound recovers the familiar exp(−trace residual) correction known from variational GP theory; the continuous analog is conceptually clean and practically computable for Gaussian–Gaussian models.
- Retrospective MH adaptation is sensible and directly replaces spectral bounds in [7].
- The diabetic neuropathy results are consistent with prior findings and show apparent convergence of the bounds as m increases.
- The paper candidly discusses identifiability issues arising from parametrizing L (vs K).

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- The paper relies critically on the claim that the bounds tighten to the exact likelihood as m→∞ and Z is optimized. This is asserted informally (via a GP posterior-variance analogy) but not proven. For the MH scheme, an almost-sure termination guarantee (monotone tightening leading to decision in finite steps with probability 1) is essential; no theorem or conditions are provided.
- Experimental validation is limited. The synthetic study uses a single small dataset and primarily shows that variational can be unstable while MH matches an oracle. There is no quantitative analysis of bound tightness vs m, acceptance rate/ESS/time, or comparison to spectral MH [7].
- For the real dataset, results focus on overdispersion measures and qualitative separation. There is no quantitative comparison against spectral baselines, held-out likelihoods, or predictive performance. No ablations on Z initialization/optimization or sensitivity to m beyond plots.
- For the finite case—arguably the most common DPP use in ML (subset selection, recommendation)—there are no experiments on large n (e.g., n ≥ 10^4), no comparison against Nyström-based or stochastic logdet approximations (e.g., Hutchinson + Lanczos/Chebyshev), and no wall-clock/accuracy benchmarks.
- Optimization details are thin. Gradients w.r.t. θ and Z are not derived; CMA-ES is used in places despite the apparent availability of analytic gradients, raising practicality/scalability concerns.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but with several notation/numbering inconsistencies (e.g., equation references (5)/(7)/(8)/(13)), typos, and occasional imprecision (e.g., using yi in a 2×2 det). Proofs are deferred entirely to the supplement. Guidance on choosing/initializing Z and m is minimal.

Originality
- The core inequality is known; the novelty lies in adapting inducing-point bounds to DPP normalizers and extending to continuous operators. This is a reasonable but incremental contribution. The retrospective MH adaptation mirrors [7] but swaps spectral with inducing-point bounds.

Significance
- If robust and practically tight, nonspectral bounds could broaden DPP inference to settings where spectra are unavailable. However, given the limited and mostly qualitative empirical evidence, and missing comparisons to strong baselines, the current submission does not yet demonstrate a clear advance in state of the art or reliability at scale.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you provide a theorem stating conditions under which the upper/lower bounds converge to the true log normalizer as m→∞ and Z becomes dense (finite and continuous cases)? Any rates?
2) Do you have an almost-sure termination guarantee for retrospective MH under your tightening scheme?
3) Why not use gradients for Z and θ? Please provide derivatives and show that gradient-based optimization improves robustness and speed over CMA-ES.
4) How does your method compare, in accuracy/time, to (a) spectral MH [7], and (b) stochastic logdet estimators (Hutchinson + Lanczos/Chebyshev) on moderate n where exact logdet is feasible for ground truth?
5) For finite DPPs with large n, can you report scaling experiments (n up to, say, 50k–100k), time vs error to exact logdet (when feasible), and sensitivity to m?
6) In the continuous case beyond Gaussian–Gaussian, how often is Ψ computable? Can you discuss numerical quadrature approximations and their impact?

Pros
- Clean, general nonspectral bounds; theoretically motivated.
- Applicable to finite and continuous DPPs.
- Practical MH trick that mirrors prior spectral methods.
- Consistent real-data findings; convergence of bounds with m.

Cons
- No formal tightening/termination guarantees; MH correctness remains heuristic.
- Sparse and mostly qualitative experiments; weak comparisons to baselines.
- No finite-domain large-scale validation.
- Missing gradient-based implementation details; reliance on black-box optimization.
- Presentation issues (equation/notation inconsistencies).

Recommendation: Weak reject
The idea is promising and potentially useful, but the paper lacks rigorous guarantees on tightening/termination and sufficiently thorough empirical validation against established baselines, especially in the finite, large-scale regime. Strengthening theory and experiments could make this a solid contribution.

Confidence: 0.7
