

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that eliminate the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L, which is typically required for DPP inference. The paper makes two primary contributions: (1) nonspectral bounds for finite DPPs inspired by variational inference for Gaussian processes, and (2) extension of these bounds to continuous DPPs. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be used within variational inference and MCMC frameworks, providing a practical solution to a problem that has limited the scalability of DPP models in applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a mathematically rigorous approach. The bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 are derived from nontrivial determinant inequalities and properly referenced. The connection to Gaussian process variational inference is well-motivated and well-executed, making the approach conceptually clean.

2. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with a clear progression from problem statement to theoretical development to experimental validation. The authors effectively explain the technical challenges of DPP inference and clearly articulate how their approach overcomes these challenges. The experimental section is particularly strong, with appropriate synthetic and real-world datasets.

3. **Originality:** The paper's core contribution—nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods—is novel and addresses a clear gap in the literature. While previous work [7] has used spectral bounds, this paper provides the first method that completely avoids spectral decomposition, which is a significant advancement for large-scale and continuous DPP inference.

4. **Significance:** DPPs have become increasingly popular for modeling repulsive phenomena in machine learning applications. This work directly addresses a major computational barrier that has limited their application to larger datasets. The experimental results on the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrate practical utility, showing that the method can successfully distinguish between different classes of spatial patterns.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The paper compares their method with the ideal likelihood (in the toy experiment) but does not provide a thorough comparison with the spectral-based method from [7], which is the closest prior work. A quantitative comparison of computational efficiency and inference quality would strengthen the claims about the method's advantages.

2. **Identifiability Issues:** The paper correctly identifies that parametrizing L directly rather than K leads to identifiability issues (Section 5.1), but doesn't provide concrete guidance for practitioners on how to address this. The authors should discuss practical strategies for choosing parametrization based on specific application needs.

3. **Computational Complexity:** While the paper demonstrates that their method is more efficient than spectral-based approaches, it doesn't provide a detailed complexity analysis of the proposed bounds. It would be helpful to quantify the computational savings as a function of the number of pseudo-inputs m.

4. **Theoretical Limitations:** The paper states that "the upper and lower bounds in (4) match when m goes to infinity," but doesn't provide a formal convergence rate or discuss the conditions under which this convergence occurs. This is important for understanding the theoretical guarantees of the method.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the DPP inference literature. The proposed nonspectral bounds are theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrate practical utility through comprehensive experiments. The work advances the state of the art in DPP inference by removing a major computational bottleneck that has limited the scalability of these models.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above. Specifically, the authors should:
1. Add a detailed comparison with the spectral-based method [7] in terms of both computational efficiency and inference quality
2. Provide more concrete discussion of the identifiability issues and practical guidance for parametrization
3. Include a formal analysis of the convergence rate of the bounds as m increases
4. Provide a more detailed complexity analysis of the proposed method

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem with clear practical relevance. With the suggested revisions, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
