### Summary

This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), which stems from the `det(L+I)` term requiring an expensive spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix L. The authors propose a novel approach using non-spectral bounds on this determinant. The core idea is inspired by variational sparse approximations for Gaussian Processes (GPs), utilizing a set of "inducing points" or "pseudo-inputs" `Z` to form a low-rank approximation `Q` of the matrix `L`. This approximation leads to a tractable lower and upper bound on the determinant. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference paradigms: variational inference (VI) by maximizing the lower bound, and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) using a retrospective sampling scheme that leverages both bounds. The framework is developed for both DPPs on finite sets and extended to continuous domains. The method is evaluated on a synthetic 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP and a real-world spatial point process dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a well-known and important limitation of DPPs. The O(n³) complexity of the likelihood calculation severely restricts the application of DPPs to large-scale problems. Providing a method that circumvents the need for spectral decomposition is a valuable goal.

2.  **Principled Connection to Established Methods:** The approach of using inducing points to derive bounds is elegantly borrowed from the well-established literature on sparse Gaussian Processes (e.g., Titsias, 2009). This provides a solid and familiar theoretical foundation for the proposed approximation, which is a significant strength compared to more ad-hoc approximation schemes.

3.  **Versatility of Application:** The paper demonstrates the utility of the derived bounds for both approximate (VI) and asymptotically exact (MCMC) inference. This flexibility allows practitioners to choose the appropriate trade-off between computational cost and accuracy for their specific application.

4.  **Extension to Continuous DPPs:** The formal extension of the bounds to the continuous operator setting is a non-trivial and valuable contribution, broadening the applicability of the proposed inference techniques beyond the finite case.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Technique:** While the application to DPPs is new, the underlying technical machinery is a direct adaptation of sparse variational GP methods. The key determinant inequality used for the proof is from prior work, and the concept of using inducing points to form a low-rank approximation is the cornerstone of sparse GP literature. The contribution is thus more of an application of an existing, powerful idea to a new domain rather than the development of a fundamentally new inference technique.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation Against Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous and lacks comparison to the most relevant established methods.
    *   The primary competitor for this work is the MCMC method of Affandi et al. (2014), which also performs inference for DPPs by bounding the likelihood, albeit using partial spectral information (computing the first `m` eigenvalues). A direct comparison in terms of runtime, accuracy, and the required number of inducing points (`m` in this paper) versus the number of eigenvalues (`m` in Affandi et al.) is crucial for evaluating the practical benefits of the proposed non-spectral approach. This comparison is absent.
    *   The paper does not compare against other approximation schemes, such as the Nyström approximation (Affandi et al., 2013), which is another standard technique for handling large kernel matrices in DPPs.

3.  **Inconsistent and Poorly Explained VI Performance:** The authors report that their VI method yields "poorly consistent results" on the simple 1D synthetic experiment, showing high variance in parameter estimates across runs. However, they then proceed to use this same VI method as the sole inference technique for the more complex, real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset. This is a major methodological inconsistency. If the VI approach is unreliable in a controlled setting where ground truth is known, its results on the real-world dataset are difficult to trust. The paper offers no investigation into why the VI method fails or under what conditions it might be expected to work, which undermines its contribution as a practical inference tool.

4.  **Lack of Rigor in Optimization of Inducing Points:** The performance of the bounds critically depends on the placement of the inducing points `Z`. The paper states that `Z` can be optimized jointly with the kernel parameters using "an EM-like scheme" or "black-box optimizers." This treatment is somewhat superficial. A more thorough analysis of the optimization landscape, sensitivity to initialization, and practical guidance on how to perform this optimization effectively would be necessary to make the method robust and reproducible. The observation that optimized points form neat grids is interesting but anecdotal without further analysis.

### Conclusion

The paper presents an interesting and well-motivated application of ideas from the sparse GP literature to the problem of scalable DPP inference. The core concept of using inducing-point bounds to avoid spectral decomposition is sound and elegant. However, the work in its current form falls short of the standards required for publication at NIPS. The primary concerns are the lack of a convincing experimental comparison against established baselines and the paradoxical use of a variational method that is shown to be unreliable in the paper's own synthetic experiments. Without a thorough validation that demonstrates a clear advantage over existing methods (e.g., spectral-based MCMC), the practical significance of the contribution remains unproven.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem in a popular model class (DPPs).
*   The proposed method is built on a principled and well-understood foundation from sparse GPs.
*   The method completely avoids expensive eigendecompositions, which is a conceptual advantage.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The core technical idea is not novel but rather an application of an existing technique.
*   The experimental evaluation is critically flawed due to the absence of comparisons to key established baselines for DPP inference.
*   The variational inference approach, presented as a main contribution, is shown to perform poorly on a simple task, casting serious doubt on the results obtained with it in the real-world experiment.
*   The paper lacks a rigorous discussion of the practical aspects of optimizing the inducing points.
