Summary
The paper proposes nonspectral upper and lower bounds on the normalizing determinant det(I+L) in DPP likelihoods, applicable to both finite domains (matrices) and continuous domains (Fredholm determinants). The bounds are built from an inducing-point (pseudo-input) Nyström-type approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, inspired by the variational sparse GP bound of Titsias (2009). The finite-domain bound (Proposition 1) yields det(Q+I)·exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I). A continuous analogue (Proposition 2) bounds 1/det(I+L) using determinants of LZ and LZ+Ψ, with Ψ involving integrals of L against the base measure. The authors plug these bounds into (i) a variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood, optimized over both kernel parameters and inducing points, and (ii) a retrospective MH scheme that refines bounds adaptively by increasing m and re-optimizing Z. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where the spectrum is known and a diabetic neuropathy dataset, demonstrating convergence of the bounds and qualitative separation of classes.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on sparse GP variational ideas (Titsias 2009) and leverages a determinant inequality (Seiler–Simon 1975). It contrasts with spectral methods for DPPs (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2014; AISTATS 2013) that require eigencomponents. The continuous-domain treatment connects to classical operator theory and Fredholm determinant numerics (Bornemann 2010). The idea of retrospective MH is standard and was used for DPPs with spectral bounds (Affandi et al., 2014); here it is adapted to nonspectral bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptually neat: repurposes well-known determinant/variational tools to avoid eigendecompositions in DPP inference.
- Potentially practical: O(n m^2) dependence (for fixed m) and O(n m) memory vs O(n^3)/O(n^2).
- Provides both finite and continuous versions; the latter is relatively rare in ML DPP work.
- The MCMC experiment on the Gaussian–Gaussian case validates correctness against an “ideal” spectral baseline.
- Variational experiments show bound tightening with m and qualitatively reproduce prior findings on the neuropathy dataset.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical rigor and completeness
- Proofs of the central propositions are deferred; no sketch is provided. More critically, there is no theoretical result on the behavior of the bounds as m increases: monotonicity, tightness when Z grows, or convergence conditions (finite: Z=Y yields equality, but this is neither stated nor proved; continuous: no rigorous convergence statement). The remark in Section 4.1 equating tightness to “posterior variance going to zero” is heuristic and not a proof.
- The retrospective MH procedure assumes that the bracket on log α can be tightened arbitrarily by increasing m and re-optimizing Z. This needs a formal guarantee of convergence of the bounds (or a stopping rule with probabilistic error control); otherwise termination is not guaranteed.
- Numerical stability is not addressed. LZ may be ill-conditioned; no jitter/regularization strategy is discussed for LZ^{-1} and log det computations.
- Computational complexity is understated: the variational objective includes Σ_t log det(L_{Y_t}), which costs O(|Y_t|^3) per t. This is not reflected in the reported O(n m^2) statement. Gradients w.r.t. Z, θ are not derived or referenced.

Methodological detail and experimental evaluation
- For the toy continuous experiment, only qualitative agreement is shown. No ESS, acceptance rates, wall-clock times, or comparison to [7]’s spectral MH are reported, although this setting uniquely allows such a comparison.
- The neuropathy experiment lacks quantitative metrics (e.g., held-out likelihood, predictive performance), ablations (effect of Z optimization vs random Z), and baselines beyond qualitative agreement with prior literature. The class separation plots lack statistical tests or confidence intervals.
- The MH procedure involves re-optimizing Z per proposal; the overhead and scaling are not reported. This could negate the claimed computational advantages.
- Sensitivity of results to m, initialization of Z, and kernel hyperparameters is not systematically studied; the pseudo-input “grid” behavior is observed but not analyzed.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos and inconsistencies impede readability:
  - Notation: xi ⊂ Rd (should be xi ∈ Rd); “K(yi,yi)” should be K(xj,xj); repeated overloading of Y for both ground set and random set; inconsistent use of subscripts/superscripts (e.g., LYZ, LZY).
  - Equation numbering is inconsistent throughout: references to (7), (8), (13) do not match; “using (7)” where (2)/(5) was intended; footnote markers appear inline (e.g., “until1 log u”).
  - Missing bounds in summations (e.g., “∑ d=1” without upper limit), missing years and incomplete references; misspellings (“Proccessing,” “systems”).
  - Several statements are imprecise or misleading (e.g., “Q… do not rely on a Nyström-type approximation” – this is a Nyström/inducing point approximation; the difference is only that Z need not be a subset of Y).
- The paper would benefit from a clear statement of assumptions for Propositions 1–2 (PSD, invertibility of LZ, trace-class conditions), a brief proof sketch, and a discussion of the tightness regime (e.g., Z=Y).

Originality and significance
- The finite-domain bound is essentially the Titsias-style variational determinant bound repurposed for DPP normalization, which is incremental but useful. The continuous extension and its application to Fredholm determinants are more original within the DPP literature.
- Practical impact is plausible, but current evidence is limited; stronger comparisons to spectral methods and more rigorous guarantees would significantly improve significance.

Pros
- Avoids spectral decompositions; applicable to continuous domains.
- Simple, general recipe leveraging inducing points.
- Bridges sparse GP variational techniques and DPP inference.

Cons
- Insufficient theoretical guarantees (tightness/monotonicity/convergence).
- Understated computational costs; unclear scalability for MH with Z-optimization.
- Sparse empirical evaluation; no strong quantitative baselines.
- Many presentation/notation/reference issues.

Detailed suggestions
- Add formal statements on tightness: finite case (Z=Y exactness), monotonicity w.r.t. nested Z; continuous case convergence under Mercer-type assumptions.
- Provide stability strategies (jitter on LZ, numerically stable Cholesky, log-det tricks).
- Include runtime/ESS comparisons to spectral MH where feasible; report acceptance rates and the distribution of m increments.
- Derive and include gradients for Z and θ, or clearly state the optimizer used and its settings.
- Clarify computational complexity, including the numerator costs.
- Fix notation and equation numbering; clean up references (complete bibliographic details, correct spellings).

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The core idea is promising and relevant, but the paper in its current form has too many presentation issues and lacks key theoretical and empirical substantiation. With stronger guarantees, clearer exposition, and more thorough experiments, this work could be impactful.
