

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a fundamental computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the operator L in the likelihood evaluation. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid the expensive spectral decomposition, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques in Gaussian processes. They derive these bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs and demonstrate their use in variational inference and MCMC methods.

The paper makes a significant contribution by recognizing that the spectral decomposition requirement is a major barrier to applying likelihood-based methods to DPPs, particularly for large or continuous domains. Previous work [7, 8, 9] has grappled with this issue, but the authors provide a fresh perspective by focusing on bounds that don't require spectral knowledge at all. The key innovation is the introduction of "pseudo-inputs" (or "inducing inputs") that allow the authors to construct tight bounds on the likelihood without spectral decomposition. This approach is conceptually elegant and builds a clear connection to sparse Gaussian process methods [11].

The paper's strengths are numerous. First, it addresses a genuine pain point in DPP research with a novel, practical solution. The authors successfully extend their finite-domain bounds to continuous domains, which is a non-trivial technical contribution. Their experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets (diabetic neuropathy data) demonstrates the practical utility of their approach. The retrospective MCMC method they propose is particularly clever and well-motivated. The paper also clearly acknowledges limitations, such as the trade-off between interpretability when using L instead of K, and the fact that their method doesn't solve the identifiability issues with certain parameters (as shown in the toy experiment).

The paper's main weaknesses are somewhat minor but worth noting. The variational inference approach shows poor consistency in the toy experiment (κ varies over several orders of magnitude), which suggests that the method might require careful tuning or additional constraints for certain parameters. The paper doesn't provide a thorough computational complexity analysis showing how much faster their method is compared to spectral-based approaches. Additionally, while they acknowledge the interpretability issues with using L instead of K, they don't propose a solution to this problem, which is a significant drawback for many applications where kernel interpretability matters.

The significance of this work is substantial. DPPs are increasingly used in applications requiring repulsive point processes (diversity modeling in search engines, spatial ecology, etc.), but their computational complexity has limited their adoption. By removing the spectral decomposition requirement, this work makes likelihood-based inference for DPPs feasible for larger datasets and continuous domains, potentially opening up new application areas. The authors' approach is general enough to be applicable to many existing DPP frameworks, and they provide clear implementation details for both variational inference and MCMC.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem statement to theoretical contributions to experimental validation. The supplementary material appears to contain the necessary proofs, which is good practice.

In terms of novelty, this paper stands out from previous work. While previous papers [7, 8, 9] have attempted to approximate the spectral decomposition, this paper completely avoids it. The connection to Gaussian process literature is well-motivated and makes the contribution more accessible to the broader machine learning community. The paper's approach is genuinely new and represents a significant shift in how DPP inference might be performed.

I find this paper to be highly significant and novel. While the authors acknowledge some limitations, the core contribution—avoiding spectral decomposition entirely in DPP inference—is bold, elegant, and potentially transformative for the field. The paper successfully demonstrates that this approach works in practice, with experiments showing convergence to the correct posterior in the toy example and meaningful results on real data.

The minor weaknesses (variational inference inconsistency, lack of computational complexity analysis) don't detract from the paper's overall contribution, especially given the paper's focus on conceptual novelty rather than methodological perfection. The authors have provided a fundamentally new direction for DPP inference that is likely to be adopted and built upon by the community.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It offers a genuinely novel approach to a long-standing computational problem in DPP inference, with clear practical implications for a growing class of applications. The paper's contribution represents a meaningful step forward in making DPPs more accessible to practitioners and researchers working with larger or continuous datasets.
