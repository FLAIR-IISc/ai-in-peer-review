**Summary:**
This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the evaluation of the determinant term `det(L + I)`, which typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix L. The authors propose a novel approach that bypasses this requirement by deriving cheap-to-compute, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the likelihood. The core idea is inspired by sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes (GPs), using a small set of `m` "inducing points" to construct a low-rank approximation `Q` of the full `n x n` kernel matrix `L`. These bounds are first derived for DPPs over finite sets and then extended to the more general case of DPPs on continuous domains. The paper demonstrates how these bounds can be incorporated into two standard inference paradigms: a fast, approximate variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes the lower bound, and an asymptotically exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method using retrospective sampling. The methods are validated on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP, where the true likelihood is known, and on a real-world 2D spatial point process dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed bounds are derived from a non-trivial determinant inequality [14] and appear sound. The theoretical contribution is solid, providing a complete framework for both finite and continuous DPPs. The experimental evaluation is well-designed. The synthetic experiment correctly uses a case where the ground truth is known to validate the MCMC sampler, demonstrating its correctness. The real-world experiment demonstrates the practical utility and scalability of the VI approach. The authors are commendably honest about the limitations, such as the poor performance of VI on the toy problem due to identifiability issues and the general problem of parametrizing the `L` kernel instead of the more interpretable `K` kernel.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a clear motivation for the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The decision to present the finite case first (Section 2) before the more technical continuous case (Section 4) is excellent for readability and allows readers to grasp the core concepts without needing a background in operator theory. The connection to sparse GPs is made explicit and helps to ground the method in a familiar context for the machine learning audience. However, clarity could be improved by providing more intuition behind the continuous bounds (Proposition 2) and their relationship to the finite case in the main text, rather than deferring this entirely to the supplementary material. A sentence or two connecting the matrix `Ψ` to its discrete analogue `L_ZY` would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The main contribution is highly original. While inducing point methods are standard in GPs, their application to derive non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihoods is novel and insightful. Previous work on scalable DPP inference, such as Affandi et al. [7], still relied on partial spectral information (e.g., computing a subset of eigenvalues via power iteration). This paper's approach is fundamentally different as it is entirely "non-spectral," replacing the need for any eigenvalue computation with matrix operations involving the small set of inducing points. The extension of this idea from the discrete to the continuous setting, providing bounds on the Fredholm determinant, is also a significant and novel contribution.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant. It addresses a fundamental computational barrier that has limited the application of parametric DPPs to relatively small or specialized cases where spectral decompositions are feasible. By providing a general-purpose, scalable inference framework, this work makes DPPs much more practical for large-scale machine learning problems. The variational approach, in particular, offers a compelling trade-off between speed and accuracy, scalable to problems with many data points (`n`) so long as the number of inducing points (`m`) is kept manageable. Researchers and practitioners interested in modeling diversity and repulsion in large datasets (e.g., recommendation systems, summarization, spatial statistics) are likely to use and build upon these ideas. This work demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art in DPP inference.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Significant Contribution:** The paper introduces a new, non-spectral approach to DPP inference that overcomes a major computational bottleneck, significantly increasing the applicability of these models.
2.  **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The proposed bounds are well-grounded in matrix theory and are rigorously extended to the continuous case.
3.  **Strong and Honest Evaluation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate both the correctness (MCMC on synthetic data) and practical utility (VI on real data) of the proposed methods. The authors are transparent about the method's limitations.
4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains a technically complex topic, making it accessible to a broad NIPS audience.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Missing Direct Comparison:** The paper frequently contrasts its approach with the spectral-bound method of Affandi et al. [7], but it does not provide a direct empirical comparison. A comparison of runtime, memory usage, and accuracy against that work on the same dataset (e.g., the diabetic neuropathy data) would have made the experimental section much stronger.
2.  **Limited Exploration of VI Failure:** The VI method is shown to perform poorly on the toy example, which is attributed to identifiability issues. While the honesty is appreciated, a deeper investigation into why the variational approach fails here (e.g., is it the optimizer, the looseness of the bound, or the objective landscape?) would strengthen the paper's conclusions about when to use VI versus the more expensive MCMC.

**Overall Recommendation:**
The arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh those for rejection. This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, significant, and well-executed contribution to an important area of machine learning. It solves a long-standing problem in a clever way, and the proposed methods are likely to be widely adopted. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the experimental section, which could be strengthened but is already sufficient to validate the paper's core claims. I strongly recommend acceptance.
