## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix *L*. The authors propose and analyze bounds on the DPP likelihood that *do not* require this decomposition, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques used in Gaussian Processes. They demonstrate the applicability of these bounds in both variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) settings, extending their results to continuous DPPs. The core idea is to approximate the likelihood using a set of "pseudo-inputs" and leverage determinant inequalities to establish bounds.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the existing literature on DPPs (references [1-6]) and parametric inference techniques [7]. It acknowledges the trade-off between parameter interpretability (using kernel *K*) and computational tractability (using kernel *L*), aligning with the trend towards tractability seen in previous work [3,7]. Importantly, it distinguishes itself from prior work like [7] by avoiding the need for power iteration methods, making it potentially applicable to larger datasets and continuous domains.  The connection to sparse GP approximations [11] is clearly articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Significant Bottleneck:** The elimination of the spectral decomposition requirement is a crucial contribution, enabling DPP inference in scenarios where it was previously intractable.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The bounds presented in Propositions 1 and 2 are rigorously derived (proofs in the supplementary material), building upon established inequalities.
* **Versatility:** The proposed bounds are applicable to both discrete and continuous DPPs, broadening their impact.
* **Practical Demonstration:** The experiments, including the Gaussian 1D example and the diabetic neuropathy dataset, demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the proposed approach. The validation using MCMC with retrospective sampling and variational inference is valuable.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Interpretability:** As acknowledged by the authors, directly parametrizing *L* leads to a loss of interpretability. While the paper addresses computational issues, the resulting parameters may be harder to relate to the underlying phenomenon being modeled.
* **Dependence on Pseudo-Inputs:** The performance of the bounds, and therefore the inference, relies on the quality and number of the chosen pseudo-inputs *Z*. The paper provides some intuition for their placement (e.g., resembling samples from the marginal), but a more systematic exploration of pseudo-input selection strategies would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost of Optimization over Z:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a win, optimizing over the pseudo-inputs *Z* introduces its own computational cost. The paper doesn't offer a detailed analysis of this cost relative to power iteration methods.  
* **Limited Novelty in Bounds Themselves:** The core determinant inequality (Theorem 1 in [14], cited) is not new. The novelty lies in *applying* this inequality to the specific problem of DPP inference and deriving practical inference algorithms.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the experimental results provide supporting evidence.  The use of established techniques (variational inference, MCMC) lends credibility.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally very clear and well-organized. The mathematical notation is standard and the explanations are concise.
* **Originality:** The originality lies in the *application* of existing determinant inequalities to DPP inference and the development of practical algorithms based on these bounds. While the core inequalities aren't novel, the adaptation to DPPs and the resulting inference methods are a significant contribution.
* **Significance:** The work has the potential to be significant.  Making DPP inference tractable for larger datasets and continuous domains opens up new applications in areas like diversity modeling, recommendation systems, and spatial statistics.  The insights into parameter identifiability (demonstrated in the Gaussian experiment) are also valuable.




**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Addresses a major computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
* Provides a theoretically sound and practically demonstrated solution.
* Applicable to both discrete and continuous DPPs.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Against:**

* Loss of parameter interpretability.
* Dependence on the selection of pseudo-inputs.
* Optimization over pseudo-inputs introduces additional computational cost.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**.  While there are minor weaknesses, the contribution is significant and the technical execution is excellent. The paper addresses a real-world problem in DPP inference and provides a practical and theoretically justified solution. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach (e.g., parameter interpretability) and provide a clear roadmap for future work. The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.  The benefits of avoiding spectral decomposition outweigh the drawbacks, particularly for applications involving large datasets or continuous domains.
