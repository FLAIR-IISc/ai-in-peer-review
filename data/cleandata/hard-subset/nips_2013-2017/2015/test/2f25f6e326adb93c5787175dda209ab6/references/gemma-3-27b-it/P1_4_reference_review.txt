## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors propose and analyze bounds on the DPP likelihood that *do not* rely on this decomposition, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques used in Gaussian Processes. They present these bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, and demonstrate their applicability within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks. The authors validate their approach through experiments on a synthetic dataset and a real-world application involving nerve fiber patterns in diabetic neuropathy.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on the growing body of work applying DPPs in machine learning, particularly for modeling diversity (e.g., [3, 7]). It acknowledges existing work on spectral methods for DPP inference [7] and highlights their limitations in terms of scalability and applicability to continuous domains. The connection to variational inference in Gaussian Processes [11] is clearly articulated, serving as a foundation for the proposed bounds. It also correctly positions itself within the broader literature on approximating Fredholm determinants [23].

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant practical limitation:** The avoidance of spectral decomposition is a crucial contribution, unlocking the potential to apply DPPs to larger and more complex datasets.
* **Sound theoretical foundation:** The proposed bounds are rigorously justified with references to existing determinant inequalities.  The extension to continuous DPPs is a notable addition.
* **Practical applicability:** The demonstration of the bounds within both variational inference and MCMC frameworks provides concrete methods for parameter estimation.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments, while relatively simple, provide evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach, especially the retrospective MCMC sampler's consistency with ideal spectral methods. The diabetic neuropathy example demonstrates applicability to a real-world problem.
* **Clear writing & organization:** The paper is generally well-written and structured, with clear explanations of key concepts and motivations. The decision to present the finite case first makes the exposition more accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Identifiability:**  The experiments, particularly the synthetic one, reveal challenges in identifying the kernel parameters when directly parametrizing L. The authors acknowledge this but could further discuss the implications. The observation that only the repulsive "lengthscale" is accurately inferred suggests a potential re-parameterization or prior structure to address this issue.
* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a major win, the optimization of pseudo-inputs (Z) within the variational framework introduces its own computational cost.  A more detailed discussion of the scalability of this optimization, and its dependence on the dataset size and desired accuracy, would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  The experiments, while demonstrative, are somewhat limited in scope. Expanding the experimental evaluation to more diverse datasets and comparing the performance of the proposed methods to other approximate inference techniques (e.g., those based on Nyström approximation) would strengthen the paper.
* **Connection to Existing Work on Quadrature:** The authors mention the potential connection to quadrature-based bounds [23] but do not elaborate. Expanding on this connection, and potentially incorporating those techniques, could further improve the bounds and efficiency.
* **Overly Polite Tone:** While politeness is appreciated, the paper sometimes seems overly hesitant in criticizing the existing literature (e.g., the widespread adoption of parametrizing L instead of K). A more direct assessment of the trade-offs would be helpful.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported. The analysis of the bounds is rigorous, and the proposed methods appear to be complete and well-defined. 

**Clarity:** The paper is generally very clear and well-organized. The explanations of the mathematical concepts are accessible, and the notation is consistent.

**Originality:** The proposed bounds and their application to DPP inference are novel and represent a significant contribution to the field. While building on existing ideas, the combination of techniques and the extension to continuous domains are original.

**Significance:** The ability to perform DPP inference without spectral decomposition is a significant advance with the potential to broaden the applicability of DPPs in various machine learning tasks. The work addresses a practical bottleneck and opens up new avenues for research.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a key limitation of DPP inference.
* Provides a theoretically sound and practically applicable solution.
* Empirically validated and demonstrates promising results.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Parameter identifiability issues may limit the practical usefulness of the approach.
* Optimization of pseudo-inputs adds computational cost.
* Limited scope of experimental evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While there are some areas for improvement, the contribution is significant and the paper is of high quality. The avoidance of spectral decomposition is a crucial advance for DPP inference, and the proposed methods offer a practical and scalable alternative to existing approaches.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above in a revision would further strengthen the paper.  The authors have clearly identified a real problem and provided a valuable solution.
