## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper addresses a significant practical bottleneck in working with Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) – the computational cost of spectral decomposition. The authors propose a theoretically sound and practically viable approach using bounds on the likelihood that avoids this decomposition. The paper is well-written, clearly explains the motivation and methodology, and provides empirical validation. While the presented work builds heavily on existing techniques (variational inference, MCMC, GP approximations), the adaptation to DPPs and the theoretical justification are novel and valuable. I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the NIPS community and recommend acceptance.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the issue of expensive likelihood evaluation in parametric DPP inference. Standard likelihood-based methods require computing determinants involving the kernel matrix *L*, which necessitates spectral decomposition – a computational burden, especially for large datasets or continuous domains.  The authors circumvent this by deriving bounds on the DPP likelihood that *do not* rely on the eigendecomposition of *L*. They draw inspiration from variational inference techniques for Gaussian Processes (GPs) and extend them to the DPP setting.  The work is positioned well within the existing literature, accurately referencing key papers in DPP theory ([1, 2, 3, 5]) and their applications in machine learning [3, 4, 6, 7]. The connection to variational sparse GPs [11] is explicitly drawn, highlighting the source of inspiration. They also acknowledge related work on approximating the kernel [4] and previous attempts at spectral bounds [7].

**2. Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Core Practical Problem:** The computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition is a major limitation in applying DPPs to real-world problems, particularly with large datasets. This work provides a viable workaround.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The bounds are derived using established mathematical results on determinants [14] and are rigorously presented. The extension to continuous DPPs is a significant contribution.
* **Clear and Accessible Writing:**  The paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors clearly explain complex concepts and make the paper accessible to a broad audience with a reasonable understanding of probabilistic modeling.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, including the synthetic Gaussian DPP and the diabetic neuropathy dataset, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The comparison to existing methods (even where an exact solution is available) is valuable. The convergence analysis with varying *m* (number of pseudo-inputs) is convincing.
* **Connections to Existing Techniques:** The skillful adaptation of techniques from the GP literature to the DPP setting demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying principles.
* **Identifiability Discussion:** The authors correctly point out the difficulties in identifying the kernel parameters when directly parametrizing *L*, and relate it to their experimental findings.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Novelty:** While the adaptation to DPPs and the derived bounds are novel, the core techniques (variational inference, MCMC, inducing variables) are well-established. The primary contribution lies in the specific application and theoretical justification within the DPP framework.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** As acknowledged in the discussion, the performance of the methods (particularly variational inference) can be sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters and the initialization of the pseudo-inputs. More extensive analysis of this sensitivity would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-Input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, the optimization of the pseudo-inputs *Z* adds a computational overhead.  A more detailed analysis of the trade-off between the cost of pseudo-input optimization and the savings from avoiding spectral decomposition would be useful.  
* **Limited Exploration of Kernel Choices:**  The experiments primarily focus on squared exponential kernels.  Exploring the performance of the methods with different kernel choices (e.g., Matern kernels) could provide further insights.

**4.  Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Proposition 1 & 2:**  While the proofs are mentioned to be in the supplementary material, a brief intuition behind the inequalities in the main text would enhance understanding.
* **Section 3.2 (MCMC):** The “Bernoulli trick” (retrospective sampling) is briefly described. A more detailed explanation, or a pointer to a more comprehensive resource, would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with this technique.
* **Section 5.1 (Toy Experiment):** It would be useful to visualize the learned kernels (e.g., plot the estimated squared exponential kernel) in addition to the marginal histograms of the parameters.
* **Section 5.2 (Diabetic Neuropathy Dataset):** The authors mention that the estimated overdispersion measures converge with increasing *m*. Could they quantify this convergence (e.g., by reporting the change in the overdispersion measures for successive increases in *m*)?




**5. Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  Solves a significant practical problem.
*  Theoretically sound and well-justified.
*  Clear and well-written.
*  Strong empirical validation.
*  Significant contribution to DPP methodology.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Incremental novelty – builds upon established techniques.
*  Potential sensitivity to hyperparameters and pseudo-input optimization.



**6. Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept.**

Despite the incremental nature of the novelty, the work addresses a crucial practical challenge in DPP inference and provides a theoretically grounded and empirically validated solution. The clear writing and thorough experimental evaluation make this a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I believe the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses.
