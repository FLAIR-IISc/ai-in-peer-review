Summary
This paper proposes non-spectral, inducing-point-based upper and lower bounds on the normalizing determinant in DPP likelihoods, both for finite domains (det(I+L) with L an n×n matrix) and for continuous DPPs (Fredholm determinant det(I+L) of a trace-class operator). The bounds avoid any eigendecomposition and instead use a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY built from pseudo-inputs Z, inspired by variational sparse Gaussian processes. The authors show how to plug these bounds into (i) a variational lower bound for approximate maximum likelihood and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler that adaptively refines Z until the accept/reject decision becomes unambiguous. They extend the construction to continuous domains via operator inequalities, yielding computable bounds when Ψ = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x) is tractable (e.g., Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure). Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian toy case (where spectral ground truth is available) and a real 2D neuropathy dataset; the retrospective MH matches the ideal sampler in 1D, and the variational approach shows convergence of overdispersion measures as m increases.

Relation to prior work: Previous DPP learning methods either (i) require eigendecompositions or leading eigenpairs (e.g., Affandi et al. 2014) or (ii) operate on finite L-ensembles with cubic costs per evaluation (e.g., EM/fixed-point methods). This paper’s key contribution is to avoid spectral information entirely and to cover continuous DPPs, leveraging determinant inequalities akin to the inducing-variable variational bound in sparse GPs (Titsias 2009), but in a DPP likelihood setting.

Strengths
- Originality/novelty: A clear, conceptually simple, and bold idea—non-spectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods—extended cohesively from finite to continuous domains. To my knowledge, this specific construction and its use for both variational and retrospective MH inference in DPPs is new.
- Practical significance: Avoiding eigen-computation is impactful in both large finite problems and in continuous settings where spectra are unavailable. The O(n m^2) cost with m ≪ n is attractive, and the retrospective MH enabled by two-sided bounds is elegant and practically useful.
- Theoretical soundness: The bounds are derived from a nontrivial determinant inequality (Seiler–Simon), and the continuous case is handled carefully under trace-class assumptions with a computable Ψ. The link to sparse GP inducing methods is well-motivated.
- Empirical indications: The 1D toy validates the MH scheme against exact ground truth and diagnoses identifiability issues that can foil variational training; the real dataset shows coherent convergence of overdispersion metrics and tight bounds as m increases, with reasonable runtimes.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Experimental scope: Limited to a 1D synthetic and a small 2D real dataset. Missing are larger finite-domain experiments (e.g., n in the tens/hundreds of thousands) directly comparing against spectral-based baselines (power iteration/eigendecomposition or the spectral bounds of Affandi et al.) in terms of accuracy, runtime, and memory.
- Tightness and guidance: No theoretical characterization of bound tightness (beyond asymptotic m→∞). Practitioners would benefit from empirical plots of gap vs m and heuristics for choosing/placing Z (e.g., DPP-like design, k-means on data, or optimizing from informative initializations). The observed “griddy” pseudo-input layouts are intriguing—can this be systematized?
- Inner optimization burden: For retrospective MH, acceptance decisions depend on adaptively increasing m and re-optimizing Z, which could be costly or introduce mixing issues. Some profiling of per-iteration costs, number of refinements, and acceptance rates would strengthen the case.
- Continuous-case assumptions: The paper lists conditions under which Ψ is computable; however, in higher dimensions and for non-Gaussian kernels/µ, computing Ψ may be nontrivial. A discussion of numerical quadrature or Monte Carlo approximations (and their effect on the bounds/variance) would be useful.
- Clarity/minor issues: Some equation numbering/notation inconsistencies; it would help to summarize computational complexity for all terms (including det(LYt)) and to give practical advice on Z initialization, m schedules, and stopping criteria for variational optimization.

Quality
- The theory appears sound and connects to established inequalities. The algorithms are well-motivated. Empirical validation is modest but consistent with claims; more comprehensive comparisons would improve confidence.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized, with helpful connections to sparse GP literature. A bit more precision on assumptions in the continuous case and on computational details would further improve readability.

Originality
- High. The non-spectral determinant bounds and continuous extension constitute a new direction for DPP inference.

Significance
- Potentially high. If broadly adopted, this can enable DPP learning in settings previously hampered by spectral costs or intractability, especially in continuous domains.

Pros
- Novel non-spectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods.
- Unified treatment of finite and continuous cases.
- Practical variational and retrospective MH inference schemes.
- Promising complexity and empirical behavior; removes need for eigendecompositions.

Cons
- Limited experiments and lack of head-to-head comparisons with spectral methods at scale.
- No quantitative analysis of bound tightness vs m or of Z design strategies.
- Continuous-case practicality beyond Gaussian–Gaussian not fully explored.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide runtime/accuracy comparisons to spectral power-iteration methods on larger finite problems?
- Any heuristics for initializing/placing Z that consistently yield tight bounds and fast convergence?
- How does the bound gap scale with m in practice?
- For continuous high-dimensional settings, how do you compute Ψ, and what is the impact of numerical approximation on the bounds and inference?

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept). The paper introduces a genuinely novel and useful idea—nonspectral determinant bounds enabling DPP inference in settings where spectral knowledge is unavailable. Despite limited empirical breadth, the conceptual contribution and the continuous extension are significant and likely to influence subsequent work. I encourage acceptance provided the authors strengthen experimental comparisons and clarify assumptions and practical guidance.
