

# Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The authors identify that standard DPP inference requires spectral decomposition of the operator L (related to the kernel K), which is computationally prohibitive for large datasets or continuous domains. Their key contribution is deriving non-spectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid this spectral decomposition, using "pseudo-inputs" (inducing variables) inspired by variational inference for Gaussian processes. These bounds enable efficient variational inference and MCMC methods for DPPs. The paper extends these results to continuous domains, providing theoretical bounds and validating them with both synthetic and real-world experiments.

## Strengths

**Technical Quality and Novelty:** The paper makes a significant contribution by providing theoretically sound bounds that avoid spectral decomposition, a major limitation in current DPP inference methods. The bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) are derived from established determinant inequalities, and their extension to continuous domains is particularly valuable. This addresses a concrete problem in the DPP literature (e.g., as noted in [7]), where spectral-based methods become computationally intractable. The authors clearly differentiate their work from prior art by highlighting that their method doesn't require spectral knowledge, unlike [7].

**Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The authors effectively build from the finite case to the continuous case, with clear explanations of the mathematical concepts. The connection to variational Gaussian processes (Section 3.1) is well-articulated, making the approach accessible to the machine learning community. The experimental section (Section 5) is well-designed, with both synthetic and real-world validation.

**Significance:** DPPs have become increasingly important in machine learning for modeling repulsiveness (e.g., [3, 4, 6, 7]). The computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference has limited their applicability to large-scale problems. By providing a method that avoids spectral decomposition, this paper significantly expands the practical scope of DPPs. The experiments demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both a toy Gaussian example and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset, showing meaningful results.

**Comprehensive Treatment:** The authors don't just present theory—they show how to apply the bounds to practical inference paradigms (variational inference and MCMC). The discussion of identifiability issues when parametrizing L directly (Section 5.1) demonstrates careful consideration of limitations.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Comparison with Alternative Methods:** While the paper contrasts with [7] (spectral-based methods), it doesn't compare with other approximate inference methods for DPPs (e.g., [8, 9, 10]). A quantitative comparison of computational efficiency versus accuracy would strengthen the case for their approach.

**Limited Discussion of Scalability:** The paper doesn't thoroughly address how the number of pseudo-inputs m affects scalability. While they mention that "m n" is beneficial, they don't provide concrete scaling analysis for large datasets, which would be crucial for practitioners.

**Identifiability Issue Explanation:** The paper notes that parametrizing L directly leads to identifiability issues (Section 5.1), but doesn't deeply explore why this occurs or how it might be mitigated. A more detailed explanation would help readers understand this limitation better.

**Connection to Broader DPP Literature:** While the related work is adequate, the paper could better position itself within the broader DPP inference literature. For instance, it would be valuable to discuss how their approach compares to recent work on fixed-point algorithms [9] or expectation-maximization [8].

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the DPP inference literature. The theoretical bounds are novel, well-justified, and extend to continuous domains—a significant advancement. The experiments validate the approach effectively, and the paper is clearly written with appropriate depth.

The main limitations are the lack of comparison with other approximate inference methods and insufficient discussion of scalability implications. However, these do not undermine the core contribution, which is a valuable alternative to spectral-based inference for DPPs.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** for NIPS. This paper addresses a real problem in DPP inference with a theoretically sound solution that has practical implications. The work advances the state of the art in DPP modeling by making likelihood-based inference more feasible for larger and continuous domains. While not all limitations are fully addressed, the paper is complete and well-executed, with clear significance for the machine learning community.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria:
- **Quality:** Technically sound, well-supported by theory and experiments
- **Clarity:** Exceptionally well-written and organized
- **Originality:** Novel non-spectral bounds for DPP inference
- **Significance:** Addresses a real bottleneck in DPP applications with practical impact

The work is a valuable contribution that will be useful to researchers and practitioners working with DPPs in machine learning.
