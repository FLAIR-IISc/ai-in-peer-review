Summary
The paper addresses a central pain point in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): evaluating the normalizing constant det(I+L) typically requires spectral decompositions that are analytically unavailable or computationally prohibitive. The authors propose nonspectral, inducing-point-based bounds on the DPP likelihood that avoid eigenvalue computations:

- Finite setting: With inducing inputs Z and Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, they prove determinant bounds (via Seiler–Simon inequalities) that sandwich 1/det(I+L) between det(Q+I)^{-1} and det(Q+I)^{-1} exp(tr(L−Q)). This yields a computable lower bound on the log-likelihood usable for variational inference, and paired lower/upper bounds enabling retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) without eigenvalue computations.

- Continuous setting: They extend the approach to trace-class operators with Fredholm determinants, obtaining analogous bounds involving LZ and Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x). For Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures, Ψ is analytic.

- Inference: They detail a variational objective F(θ,Z) = sum_t log det(LYt) − T log det(Q+I) + T tr(L−Q), optimized jointly over kernel parameters and inducing inputs, and an MH scheme that adaptively tightens acceptance bounds by increasing m and optimizing Z.

- Experiments: (i) 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where exact spectrum is known: the retrospective MH matches an “ideal” MH using the exact Fredholm determinant; variational inference struggles to identify κ (expected non-identifiability), but α and lengthscale are recovered. (ii) Diabetic neuropathy spatial data: variational training with increasing m shows convergence of upper/lower likelihood bounds and stable overdispersion measures γd = σd/ρd that separate clinical classes; inducing points arrange into sensible grids.

Relation to prior work
- The construction closely parallels variational sparse GP bounds with inducing variables (Titsias, 2009), repurposed here to bound DPP normalizers. This connection is clearly acknowledged.
- It contrasts with prior DPP inference relying on spectral truncation (Affandi et al., ICML 2014), avoiding power iterations and extending to continuous domains.
- It complements Nyström-style approximations for finite DPPs (Affandi et al., AISTATS 2013) by using free inducing inputs not restricted to the observed set.
- The continuous bounds relate to numerical Fredholm determinant work (Bornemann, 2010), which is cited for future connections.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean, general way to avoid eigendecompositions in both finite and continuous DPPs, with rigorous upper/lower bounds derived from a classic determinant inequality.
- Practicality: Computation in O(n m^2) via matrix determinant lemma reduces cost significantly when m ≪ n; applicability to continuous DPPs where spectra are unknown.
- Inference toolkit: Both a variational lower bound and a bounding-based MH scheme are provided; the latter is exact in the limit via retrospective sampling.
- Experiments: The continuous 1D example validates correctness against exact likelihood; the real data study shows stable estimates as m increases and practical runtimes.

Weaknesses and questions
- Tightness and convergence: While intuition is provided, the paper lacks formal results on the monotonicity/tightness of the bounds as m increases and Z is optimized (beyond the GP variance analogy). A theorem or conditions ensuring convergence would strengthen the contribution.
- Optimization details: The bounds depend critically on optimizing Z. Practical guidance on initializing Z, using gradients vs. black-box search, and regularizing LZ (e.g., jitter) would be helpful, especially for high m and ill-conditioned kernels.
- Complexity accounting: The stated O(n m^2) ignores the O(|Yt|^3) cost of det(LYt) per realization; clarifying this assumption and its impact when realizations are large would improve transparency.
- Generality in the continuous case: Computing Ψ analytically is feasible for Gaussian–Gaussian pairs; for other kernels/base measures, quadrature or approximation is needed. Discussion or experiments beyond the Gaussian–Gaussian case would broaden applicability.
- Comparisons: The paper does not empirically compare against spectral MH (e.g., Affandi et al. 2014) or stochastic log-det approximations (e.g., Lanczos/SLQ + Hutchinson estimators) that do not require full spectra but provide approximations rather than bounds. Such comparisons would better position the method.
- Identifiability: The κ non-identifiability is observed; more systematic discussion (e.g., invariances of L-ensembles and base measure scaling) and practical remedies (priors, reparameterization) would be valuable.

Quality
- The theory appears sound, built on a well-established determinant inequality, and extends carefully to trace-class operators. Algorithmic use in VI and MH is well motivated. Experimental validation is limited but convincing for correctness and illustrates tradeoffs (VI vs. MH, m sensitivity).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. Some notation could be streamlined (clearly defining all submatrices early; emphasizing the matrix determinant lemma use for O(n m^2)). Minor typos and numbering inconsistencies appear. More explicit pseudocode for retrospective MH would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The idea to adapt variational GP determinant bounds to DPP normalizers is novel in this context, especially the continuous-domain extension and use for retrospective MH with nonspectral bounds.

Significance
- Likely to be useful to researchers/practitioners working with DPPs on large finite sets or continuous domains where spectra are unavailable. Provides a practical alternative to spectral methods and may spur further work on nonspectral DPP inference and Fredholm determinant bounds.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a theorem (or counterexamples) on bound behavior as m→∞ and Z optimized; at least monotonicity in m under mild conditions.
- Discuss and implement gradient-based optimization of Z and θ; add jitter to LZ and comment on numerical stability.
- Include a runtime/accuracy comparison to spectral MH (finite case) and to stochastic log-det approximations (e.g., Lanczos/SLQ) where appropriate.
- Broaden continuous experiments beyond Gaussian–Gaussian if possible, or explain how to compute Ψ via quadrature in general.
- Clarify computational costs, including det(LYt), and provide guidance on choosing m adaptively.
- Release code or detailed pseudocode.

Pros
- Eliminates spectral computations; applies to continuous DPPs.
- Rigorous, simple bounds; practical algorithms (VI and MH).
- Correctness validated; real-data utility demonstrated.

Cons
- Limited empirical comparisons and breadth.
- No formal tightness guarantees; strong dependence on Z optimization.
- Practical details (stability, initialization) under-discussed.

Recommendation
- Leaning toward acceptance. The paper is original, technically solid, and addresses a relevant bottleneck with broadly applicable methods. Strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying theoretical/convergence aspects would further improve it.
