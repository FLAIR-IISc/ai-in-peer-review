- Proposes novel determinant-based bounds that remove the need for spectral decomposition in parametric DPP inference, applicable to both finite and continuous cases.  
- Leverages variational inference techniques from Gaussian processes and introduces pseudo-inputs not constrained to observed data.  
- Addresses a major computational bottleneck in DPP likelihood evaluation, enabling scalable inference methods like variational inference and MCMC.  
- Builds on and clarifies connections to prior work (e.g., power iteration, Nyström) while highlighting limitations of earlier approaches.  
- Provides a sound theoretical foundation using established determinant inequalities.  
- Empirically validated on a synthetic experiment and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset, with useful convergence analyses and visualizations.  
- Well-written and clearly organized presentation with acknowledgment of limitations and trade-offs.  
- Main weakness: optimizing pseudo-inputs introduces additional computational cost that needs more detailed comparison to spectral decomposition.  
- Identifiability issues when directly parametrizing kernel L limit parameter recovery and require further investigation or alternative parameterizations/priors.  
- Limited experimental comparison to existing DPP inference methods (e.g., direct comparison to [7]) on real data to quantify speed/accuracy trade-offs.  
- Continuous-case derivations depend on specific assumptions about kernel L and base measure µ; implications for different kernels need discussion.  
- Overall assessment: strong, theoretically sound contribution that broadens DPP applicability, recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: detail computational trade-offs between pseudo-input optimization and spectral methods, expand on identifiability solutions, and add more comprehensive comparative experiments.
