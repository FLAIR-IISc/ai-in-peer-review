

# Peer Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). Standard DPP inference requires spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L, which becomes prohibitively expensive for large finite domains or continuous spaces. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood that avoid this spectral decomposition entirely. Their approach, inspired by sparse Gaussian process methods, uses "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing inputs" to construct bounds that can be optimized to tighten the likelihood approximation. The paper provides theoretical bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, and demonstrates their application to variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo inference. This work builds on prior DPP inference methods [7] but eliminates their spectral decomposition requirement, making likelihood-based inference feasible for larger datasets and continuous domains.

## Strengths

1. **Significant practical contribution**: The paper solves a real computational bottleneck that has hindered practical applications of DPPs. The avoidance of spectral decomposition is a major practical advantage over prior work.

2. **Solid theoretical foundation**: The bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) are derived from a nontrivial inequality on determinants [14], with proofs referenced to supplementary material. The extension to continuous DPPs is well-executed and properly justified.

3. **Strong experimental validation**: The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets (diabetic neuropathy) demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. The results show convergence as the number of pseudo-inputs increases, and the method provides meaningful insights into the data.

4. **Clear writing and organization**: The paper is well-structured with a logical progression from problem statement to theoretical development to experimental validation. Mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, making it accessible to readers familiar with DPPs and Gaussian processes.

5. **Honest discussion of limitations**: The authors acknowledge the interpretability issue of parametrizing L instead of K, which is crucial for readers to understand the trade-offs between computational efficiency and model interpretability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with prior work**: While the paper references [7], it lacks a direct comparison of runtime and accuracy with this method on the same datasets. A thorough comparison would better demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.

2. **Insufficient discussion of interpretability**: The paper acknowledges that parametrizing L instead of K makes parameters less interpretable but doesn't explore potential solutions or alternatives. This is a significant limitation for practical applications where interpretability matters.

3. **Limited theoretical generality**: The bounds are derived for specific cases (e.g., the Fredholm determinant in continuous case), but the paper doesn't sufficiently discuss their applicability to general DPPs. A more detailed analysis of the scope of the bounds would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

4. **Incomplete computational trade-off analysis**: While the paper shows convergence as m increases, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational trade-off between m and inference time, which would be valuable for practitioners.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is solid, and the experimental results validate the theoretical claims. The paper is a complete piece of work that addresses a well-defined problem.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the paper provides sufficient background for readers unfamiliar with DPPs. The main contributions are clearly articulated.

**Originality**: The key contribution—nonspectral bounds for DPP inference—is novel. While the paper builds on sparse Gaussian process methods, applying these to DPPs represents a significant new direction. The extension to continuous DPPs is particularly valuable and differentiates this work from prior research.

**Significance**: The results are important for practical applications of DPPs, which model repulsiveness/diversity. The proposed methods make likelihood-based inference feasible for larger datasets and continuous domains, potentially enabling broader application of DPPs in machine learning. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a demonstrably better way than previous research.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP inference by providing a practical solution to a key computational bottleneck. The theoretical contribution is sound, and the experimental results validate the approach. While there are some limitations, they do not detract from the overall value of the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- Solves a significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference
- Novel theoretical contribution that avoids spectral decomposition
- Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- Limited direct comparison with prior work (e.g., [7])
- Does not address the interpretability issue of parametrizing L instead of K
- Limited discussion of the computational trade-off between m and accuracy

## Specific suggestions for improvement (if accepted)

1. Add a direct comparison with [7] on runtime and accuracy for the same datasets to better demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method.

2. Discuss potential approaches to address the interpretability issue (e.g., approximating K from L) in more detail.

3. Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational trade-off between the number of pseudo-inputs m and inference time/accuracy.

4. Include a discussion of the limitations of the bounds for general DPPs beyond the specific cases considered in the paper.

Overall, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and should be accepted at NIPS. It addresses an important practical problem with a novel theoretical approach, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. The minor weaknesses identified do not undermine the core contribution of the paper.
