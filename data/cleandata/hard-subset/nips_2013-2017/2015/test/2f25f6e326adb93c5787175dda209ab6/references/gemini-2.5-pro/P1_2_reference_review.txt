**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the calculation of the determinant term `det(L + I)`, which typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix L (or operator in the continuous case). The authors propose novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant. The core idea is inspired by variational sparse approximations for Gaussian Processes (GPs), using a small set of `m` "inducing" or "pseudo-inputs" to form a low-rank approximation to the kernel matrix. By leveraging a matrix determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975), they derive bounds that can be computed in O(nm^2) time for the finite case, a significant improvement over the O(n^3) cost of direct eigendecomposition.

The paper demonstrates how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference paradigms:
1.  **Variational Inference (VI):** The lower bound on the likelihood serves as a tractable objective function (an ELBO-equivalent) that can be maximized jointly with respect to the DPP kernel parameters and the locations of the inducing inputs.
2.  **Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC):** The bounds are used within a Metropolis-Hastings sampler using a "retrospective sampling" trick. This allows for exact Bayesian inference by tightening the bounds (e.g., by increasing `m`) only as needed to make an accept/reject decision, avoiding the full likelihood computation at every step.

The authors extend their framework from DPPs on finite sets to those on continuous domains, providing analogous bounds for the Fredholm determinant. The approach is validated experimentally on a synthetic 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP (where the true likelihood is known, allowing for a direct comparison) and a real-world 2D dataset of nerve fibers, replicating and extending previous work (Affandi et al., 2014).

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Contribution:** The paper tackles a well-known and important problem in the practical application of DPPs. Bypassing the need for any spectral information is a strong and practical contribution that makes DPP inference more scalable and broadly applicable, especially for continuous domains where spectral decompositions are rarely known analytically.

2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Approach:** The connection drawn to sparse GPs is insightful and elegant. It successfully ports a powerful and well-understood idea (inducing variables) from the GP literature to the DPP setting. This not only provides a computationally efficient solution but also a framework (jointly optimizing pseudo-input locations) that is known to be effective.

3.  **Technical Soundness and Completeness:** The work is technically solid, grounding the proposed bounds in established matrix analysis results. The authors provide a complete inference pipeline, showing how the bounds can be used for both fast/approximate (VI) and exact/slower (MCMC) inference. The extension to the continuous case is non-trivial and substantially broadens the impact of the work.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It logically progresses from the finite case to the continuous case, clearly explains the limitations of prior work, and motivates its own contributions effectively. The distinction between parametrizing kernel K (interpretable) versus L (tractable) is clearly articulated.

5.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed. The 1D synthetic experiment is crucial as it allows for a direct comparison against an "ideal" MCMC sampler with access to the true likelihood, convincingly demonstrating the correctness of their retrospective MH sampler. The real-world diabetic neuropathy experiment demonstrates the practical utility of the VI approach on a real problem, and the analysis of parameter convergence as `m` increases provides a useful practical diagnostic.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Discussion on Practical Aspects of Optimization:** The paper proposes jointly optimizing the variational lower bound with respect to kernel parameters `θ` and pseudo-inputs `Z`. This is a non-convex optimization problem, and it would be beneficial to include a discussion on the challenges, such as local optima. How sensitive are the final results to the initialization of `Z`? The figures show neatly structured optimized `Z`, but is this always the case?

2.  **Comparison with Spectral-Based MCMC:** The primary MCMC baseline is Affandi et al. (2014), which uses partial spectral information. While the proposed method is more general (e.g., applicable to continuous domains where power iteration is not an option), a direct empirical comparison of runtime and efficiency (e.g., effective samples per second) on a shared finite problem would have strengthened the paper's claims of superiority in that setting.

3.  **VI Failure on the Toy Problem:** The authors honestly report that VI performed poorly on the 1D toy problem due to parameter identifiability issues, while MCMC succeeded. This is an interesting and important finding. However, the paper could benefit from a deeper analysis of why this failure occurred. Is it an inherent limitation of the variational approximation for this model, or could it be mitigated with different VI techniques or priors? This seems like a critical point for practitioners deciding between the two proposed methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and practical limitation of DPP models.
*   The proposed non-spectral bounds are novel in the context of DPPs and significantly advance the state of the art for inference.
*   The method is general, covering both finite and continuous DPPs.
*   The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and provides a complete solution with both VI and MCMC algorithms.
*   The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the method's correctness and practical utility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The core conceptual idea of using inducing points to create a low-rank approximation for a kernel method is heavily borrowed from the sparse GP literature. While the application and technical details are novel for DPPs, the high-level concept is not.
*   The experimental comparison to the most relevant MCMC baseline is absent.
*   Practical guidance on setting `m` and navigating the joint optimization landscape is somewhat limited.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is a very strong paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the machine learning literature on probabilistic point processes. The proposed method for non-spectral inference in DPPs is both technically elegant and practically valuable. The strengths—in particular, the novelty of the bounds, the clarity of the presentation, and the thoroughness of the empirical validation—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper successfully bridges ideas from the GP community to solve a long-standing problem for DPPs, which is likely to inspire follow-up work and be used by other researchers. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Detailed Comments on NIPS Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by derivations (reportedly in the supplement) and solid experimental results. It is a complete piece of work, presenting both the theory and its application in two different inference frameworks. The authors are commendably honest about the limitations, such as the VI failure in one experiment.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. It is a model of good academic writing. An expert could likely reproduce the results based on the descriptions provided.
*   **Originality:** While the high-level concept of inducing points is not new, its application to derive non-spectral determinantal bounds for DPPs is highly original. The work clearly delineates itself from previous spectral-based approximation methods (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014) and Nyström methods. Related work is well-cited.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. Making DPP inference more scalable and applicable to settings without analytical spectral decompositions will lower the barrier to entry for practitioners and researchers. The provision of both a fast approximate method (VI) and an exact MCMC method makes the contribution broadly useful. This work is very likely to be built upon in the future.
