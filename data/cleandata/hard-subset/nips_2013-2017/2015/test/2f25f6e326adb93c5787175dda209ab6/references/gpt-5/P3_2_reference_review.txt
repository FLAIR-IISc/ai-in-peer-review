Summary
The paper tackles a central bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): computing det(I+L) and, in continuous domains, the Fredholm determinant det(I+L), both of which typically require spectral decompositions that are costly or unavailable. The authors propose nonspectral, inducing-point-based determinant bounds inspired by variational sparse Gaussian processes (Titsias 2009). For finite domains, they define Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY with arbitrary pseudo-inputs Z (not constrained to be a subset of observed items) and establish two-sided bounds on 1/det(I+L): det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I). For continuous domains, with suitable trace-class assumptions, they analogously bound 1/det(I+L) via det LZ / det(LZ+Ψ) and an exponential trace correction, where Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x). They then (i) plug the lower bound into a variational lower bound for the DPP log-likelihood and (ii) use the two-sided bounds for retrospective Metropolis–Hastings without spectral quantities. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian example (where the spectrum is known) to validate MCMC, and a diabetic neuropathy spatial dataset demonstrating coherent estimates of overdispersion as the number of inducing points increases.

Quality
- Theory: The finite-domain bounds are plausible and well-motivated by the GP variational literature. The upper bound follows from monotonicity of determinant under PSD ordering (L≽Q), and the lower bound matches Seiler–Simon-type determinant inequalities with a trace correction. The extension to continuous operators is nontrivial and, if the technical assumptions (trace-class, tr(L)=∫L(x,x)dµ) hold, appears sound. However, the paper defers all proofs to the supplement; a brief proof sketch in the main text would help assess subtle operator-theoretic steps.
- Algorithms: The variational objective F(θ,Z) is clear, with complexity O(n m^2) for the denominator term (plus per-sample det(LYt), which can be O(|Yt|^3)). The retrospective MH construction using lower/upper bounds on log α is standard and adapted cleanly to these bounds.
- Experiments: The 1D Gaussian-Gaussian study is appropriate for validating the sampler against “ideal” spectral MH and reveals identifiability issues (κ), which the authors diagnose. The neuropathy dataset results show convergence of overdispersion measures with increasing m and are consistent with prior work. However, empirical evaluation remains limited: no large finite-domain experiments (e.g., text or recommendation settings) and no quantitative comparison to spectral MCMC or power-iteration-based methods on cases where those are feasible.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The connection to sparse GP inducing-point methods is explicit and helpful. Some notational slips and equation number mismatches (e.g., references to (7)) should be fixed.
- Important practical aspects are underexplained: gradients w.r.t. Z and θ, handling ill-conditioning of LZ (regularization), initialization of Z, and computational/memory costs for the per-iteration optimization of Z (especially inside retrospective MH).
- For the continuous case, the requirement that Ψ be computable is stated; it would help to tabulate kernel/measure pairs admitting closed forms and to discuss numerical quadrature alternatives.

Originality
- The main novelty is to replace spectral approximations/bounds used in prior DPP inference (e.g., Affandi et al. 2014) with inducing-point determinant bounds that require no eigenpairs and extend naturally to continuous domains via operator inequalities. To my knowledge, determinant bounds of this specific form (with a trace correction) have not been applied to DPP likelihoods before, and the continuous Fredholm determinant bounds using inducing points appear novel.
- The work draws on established ideas from sparse GP variational bounds and determinant inequalities; the contribution is a careful adaptation to DPP likelihoods and integration into VI and MH.

Significance
- Practically, eliminating spectral computations is valuable for both large finite domains and continuous spaces, where spectra are unavailable. The proposed bounds enable both scalable approximate VI and exact (in the limit) retrospective MH. This lowers a significant barrier for DPP modeling in spatial statistics and ML applications with continuous features.
- The impact would be stronger with broader empirical validation, complexity/accuracy trade-off characterization (as a function of m and tr(L−Q)), and guidance on choosing/optimizing Z. Nevertheless, the methodological contribution is likely to be adopted and extended.

Strengths
- Elegant, nonspectral, two-sided determinant bounds with a clear connection to inducing-point methods.
- Extension to continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinant bounds under reasonable operator assumptions.
- Integration into both variational and retrospective MCMC inference.
- Validation against exact spectral MH on a case with known spectrum.
- Empirical demonstration on a real spatial dataset showing convergence with m and practical runtimes.

Weaknesses and questions
- Tightness and convergence: No theoretical guarantees on how fast the gap (controlled by tr(L−Q)) closes with m or optimized Z; no adaptive selection strategy beyond heuristic optimization. Can the authors provide monotonicity guarantees in m and a stopping rule based on the bound gap?
- Optimization of Z: How are gradients computed for Z in practice? Any issues with nonconvexity and local optima? How sensitive are results to initialization? Are there greedy/active-set schemes (as in sparse GP selection) that work well?
- Numerical stability: LZ can be ill-conditioned; do you add jitter or regularize? How does this affect the bounds?
- Scope of continuous case: For which kernels/measures is Ψ available in closed form? Are quadrature approximations to Ψ compatible with preserving the bounds (i.e., maintaining PSD and determinant inequalities)?
- Computational cost in MH: Retrospective MH requires iteratively increasing m and re-optimizing Z per accept decision. What is the typical overhead relative to spectral MH when the latter is feasible?
- Comparisons: No head-to-head timing/accuracy comparisons with spectral bound methods (e.g., Affandi et al.) on moderate n finite problems; no discrete-domain experiments.
- Clarity issues: Minor typos and equation references; the reliance on det(LYt) computation may be nontrivial for large subsets—please comment on scalability in T and |Yt|.

Pros/cons for acceptance
- Pros: Novel nonspectral bounds; solid methodological contribution; continuous-domain extension; practical algorithms; promising empirical indications; relevance to ML and spatial statistics.
- Cons: Limited empirical breadth and comparisons; missing theoretical guidance on Z selection and convergence; some practical details deferred; proofs in supplement.

Recommendation
- Weak accept. The paper addresses a real and important obstacle in DPP inference with a principled, broadly applicable approach. With improved clarity on optimization/stability and expanded empirical comparisons, it could be a strong contribution.

Confidence
- High: familiar with DPP inference, operator inequalities, and sparse GP methods.
