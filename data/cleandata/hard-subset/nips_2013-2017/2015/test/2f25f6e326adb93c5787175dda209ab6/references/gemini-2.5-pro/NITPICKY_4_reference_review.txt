### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the computationally prohibitive task of parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), which typically requires the spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix `L`. The authors' main contribution is the introduction of novel, non-spectral bounds for the term `det(L + I)` that appears in the DPP likelihood. Drawing inspiration from sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes (GPs), these bounds are formulated using a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" `Z`. The paper derives these bounds for DPPs over both finite sets and continuous domains. Subsequently, it demonstrates how these bounds can be integrated into standard inference frameworks: 1) a variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes a lower bound on the log-likelihood, and 2) a Metropolis-Hastings (MH) MCMC algorithm that uses the bounds for "retrospective sampling" to make acceptance decisions without computing the exact likelihood. The methods are evaluated on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP, where the MCMC sampler's correctness is verified against a known ground truth, and on a real-world spatial point pattern dataset of diabetic neuropathy, where the VI approach is shown to recover meaningful parameter differences between patient classes.

### Strengths
1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The core idea of adapting the inducing variable framework from sparse GPs to derive non-spectral bounds for the DPP likelihood is both novel and elegant. It establishes a compelling connection between two important classes of kernel-based probabilistic models and provides a new avenue for scalable DPP inference.
2.  **Significance:** The proposed method tackles a fundamental bottleneck in DPP modeling. By avoiding expensive spectral decompositions (`O(n^3)` for the finite case), it makes DPPs more scalable. More importantly, it enables likelihood-based inference in continuous domains where the spectral decomposition of the kernel operator is often analytically intractable, significantly broadening the applicability of DPPs.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-structured, logically progressing from the simpler finite case (Section 2) to the more technical continuous case (Section 4), which allows readers to grasp the main concepts before delving into operator theory.

### Weaknesses
Despite the promising core contribution, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from minor formatting issues to significant omissions of methodological detail, which collectively undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Lack of Careful Preparation:** The paper exhibits a concerning lack of polish, suggesting a rushed preparation.
    *   **Referencing Errors:** There are multiple, glaring errors in equation referencing. The text in Section 3.2 refers to bounds in "(13)", but no such equation exists; the relevant equations are (8). Similarly, equations (9), (10), and (11) are incorrectly referred to in the text as (14), (15), and (16), respectively.
    *   **Typos and Notational Imprecision:** Several typos detract from clarity. In Section 2.1, the expansion of `det K_{xi,xj}` incorrectly contains `K(yi,yi)` instead of `K(xj,xj)` and uses `K(xi,xj) 2` instead of `K(xi,xj)^2`. In Equation (10), the division symbol is missing, incorrectly showing multiplication by `det(I + L)`. The notation `P(A ⊂ Y)` in Equation (1) is unconventional for a probability mass function; `P(Y = A)` where `Y` is the random set would be standard. Unconventional commas are placed after variable definitions (e.g., `F(θ,Z) ,`) and equation numbers.

2.  **Omission of Critical Methodological Details:** The paper is frequently vague on key practical aspects of the implementation, posing a significant barrier to reproducibility.
    *   **Optimization of Inducing Inputs (Z):** The procedure for optimizing the locations of the inducing inputs `Z` is glossed over. Section 3.1 mentions "an EM-like scheme," "gradient-based methods," and "black-box optimizers" without providing any concrete details. For gradient-based methods, are the gradients of `F(θ,Z)` with respect to `Z` analytically available? If so, their derivation should be included. This optimization is central to the performance of the bounds, and its treatment here is insufficient.
    *   **Details for the Continuous Case:** Section 4 states that the matrix `Ψ` in Proposition 2 is computable for the "popular Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure" but fails to provide the resulting closed-form expression or a citation where it can be found. This integral is a non-trivial component of the method for continuous DPPs, and its omission is a critical gap.
    *   **Choice of `m`:** The number of inducing points, `m`, is a crucial hyperparameter, but the paper offers little practical guidance on how to set it. The suggestion to "increase it until inference results stop varying" is a reasonable but ad-hoc heuristic. For the MCMC method, the protocol for choosing the initial `m` and the computational cost of re-optimizing `Z` when `m` is increased are not discussed.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Analysis:**
    *   **Dismissal of VI Failure:** In the toy experiment (Section 5.1), the VI approach is reported to yield "poorly consistent results" which are then "not shown." Simply stating that MCMC is preferable due to identifiability issues is an inadequate analysis. Why does the variational approximation fail? Is the lower bound too loose for this problem? Hiding these negative results prevents a deeper understanding of the method's limitations.
    *   **Lack of Direct Comparison:** The primary claim is a computational advantage over spectral methods like those in Affandi et al. (2014) [7]. However, no direct wall-clock time comparisons are provided. A compelling experiment would have been to benchmark both methods on a problem of moderate size where the spectral approach is slow but feasible, thereby quantifying the speed-up.
    *   **Unresolved Interpretability:** The paper acknowledges the known issue that parametrizing the `L` kernel instead of the more intuitive `K` kernel complicates parameter interpretation. The experiments confirm this (e.g., non-identifiability of `κ`), but the work offers no new solution or insight, merely accepting a major limitation of the prevailing approach.

### Recommendation
**Reject.**

While the central idea of this paper is strong and potentially impactful, the execution presented in this manuscript is not up to the standards of NIPS. The high number of typographical and referencing errors demonstrates a lack of care that pervades the entire paper. More importantly, the omission of key methodological details regarding the optimization of inducing inputs and the computation of crucial terms (like the `Ψ` matrix) severely hinders the paper's clarity and makes the work difficult to reproduce. The experimental evaluation is also incomplete, failing to properly analyze the limitations of the VI approach or provide direct computational comparisons to justify its claimed advantages.

The paper contains the kernel of a valuable contribution, but it requires a major revision to address the numerous flaws in its presentation, methodological description, and experimental analysis before it can be considered for publication.
