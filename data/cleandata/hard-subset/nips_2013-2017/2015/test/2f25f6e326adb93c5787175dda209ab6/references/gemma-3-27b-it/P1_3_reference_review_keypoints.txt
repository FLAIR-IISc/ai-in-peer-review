- Problem addressed: removes the need for costly spectral decomposition in parametric inference for DPPs by deriving likelihood bounds that avoid eigendecomposition.  
- Method: proposes determinant-based bounds inspired by variational GP techniques, applicable in variational inference and MCMC, and extends to continuous DPPs.  
- Theoretical foundation: derivations rely on determinant inequalities and appear rigorous with supporting proofs.  
- Practical impact: enables scalable DPP inference in high-dimensional/large datasets where spectral methods are prohibitive.  
- Empirical validation: 1D Gaussian recovers ground-truth parameters except scale; diabetic neuropathy experiment shows parameter convergence with more inducing points.  
- Relation to prior work: builds on DPP theory and GP variational approximations, and leverages retrospective sampling ideas from spectral DPP inference.  
- Strength — versatility: bounds apply to both discrete and continuous DPPs and integrate with VI and MCMC.  
- Strength — clarity: paper is well-written and well-organized, making methods and algorithms accessible.  
- Strength — originality: novel application of determinant bounds to DPP inference.  
- Weakness — interpretability: parametrizing the L kernel reduces interpretability and leads to unidentifiable scale in examples.  
- Weakness — computational cost: optimizing pseudo-inputs Z introduces extra cost; scaling with data size/parameters is not fully analyzed.  
- Weakness — limited experiments: experimental scope is narrow; more diverse/real-world datasets would strengthen claims.  
- Weakness — comparisons missing: lacks benchmarking against other approximate DPP inference methods (e.g., stochastic variational or alternative samplers).  
- Clarity caveat: some operator-theory sections (Section 4) need more intuitive explanation.  
- Significance: work likely broadens practical applicability of DPPs for large-scale problems.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions — address interpretability of L, analyze pseudo-input optimization cost, and expand experimental comparisons.
