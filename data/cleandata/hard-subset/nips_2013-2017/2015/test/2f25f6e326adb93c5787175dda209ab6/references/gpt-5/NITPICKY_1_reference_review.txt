Summary
The paper proposes non-spectral, inducing-point-based upper and lower bounds for the normalizing determinant in DPP likelihoods, both for finite ground sets and for continuous (Fredholm) cases. For the finite case, with Q = LYZ LZ−1 LZY, the authors show det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I). Analogous bounds are derived for trace-class operators in continuous domains using a matrix Ψ with entries Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x). These bounds are then used (i) to construct a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood (by replacing −log det(I+L)), and (ii) within a retrospective MH procedure that refines bounds adaptively by increasing the number of inducing points. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP where the spectrum is known, and a real 2D spatial dataset (diabetic neuropathy), demonstrating monotone tightening of bounds with m and qualitative parameter recovery.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, eigen-decomposition-free bounds that mirror the Titsias-style variational GP determinant bound; likely broadly applicable when L’s spectrum is unavailable or expensive.
- Extension to continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinants and trace-class operators is valuable and underrepresented in ML literature.
- The retrospective MH construction is natural given two-sided bounds and is potentially exact while avoiding power iterations.
- Empirical evidence (albeit limited) indicates the bounds tighten with m and can underpin both VI and MH workflows; the 2D dataset shows coherent convergence of overdispersion metrics.
- Computational scalability in n through O(n m^2) operations for the denominator is attractive.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/theoretical
- Convergence of bounds with m: The paper asserts (Section 4) that upper/lower bounds coincide as m→∞ under “fairly weak assumptions,” invoking GP posterior variance intuition. There is no theorem, proof sketch, or precise conditions (e.g., density of Z in X, Mercer conditions, kernels on non-compact domains, role of µ) guaranteeing convergence of the determinant bounds. This is central; at least a rigorous statement with assumptions is needed.
- Invertibility of LZ: Both Propositions require LZ to be invertible, yet no discussion of conditions, regularization (jitter), or stability is provided. This is a practical and theoretical gap.
- Retrospective MH correctness: The algorithm adaptively optimizes Z and increases m within an acceptance test. While the “Bernoulli factory”/retrospective idea is sound in principle, the paper provides no proof that the stopping rule and the adaptive tightening strategy preserve detailed balance in the presence of (i) adaptive proposals (AM) and (ii) optimizing Z conditional on θ, θ′. A self-contained theorem or explicit citation establishing exactness under these adaptations is needed.
- Continuous case assumptions: The paper assumes L is trace-class and states tr(L)=∫ L(x,x)dµ(x), citing general operator results; however, the precise conditions on L and µ ensuring the bounds (and the matrix Ψ construction) apply are not clearly stated. The claim “Ψ is computable” for Gaussian kernels/measures is not substantiated with closed forms, complexity, or numerical stability details.
- Complexity discussion is partial: While O(n m^2) is given for the denominator term, the numerator det(LYt) still costs O(|Yt|^3) per realization; practicality for large |Yt| (e.g., large k) is not discussed. Gradients for joint optimization in θ and Z are mentioned but not derived.

Clarity and presentation (numerous issues)
- Equation numbering and references are inconsistent: e.g., log-likelihood is (5) but later referred to as (7); MH acceptance bound is (8)/(13) with mismatches; “maximize (8)” while F was (6).
- Notational/typographical errors: e.g., detK{xi,xj} expression has K(yi,yi) instead of K(xj,xj); missing spaces/slashes “1det(L+I)”; superscripts and squares typeset inconsistently; missing limits in summations (Eq. 12 “∑ d=1” without upper bound); stray footnote markers (1,2,3,4) misplaced in main text; “Proccessing” typos; journal names inconsistent.
- In Section 5.2, θ excludes the base mean µ though µ appears in the model; how µ is treated (fixed/estimated) is not specified. Earlier, 5.1 uses (κ, α, ·) but notation is inconsistent and some parameters are unnamed.
- The relationship to prior DPP approximations is not deeply discussed. How do these bounds compare theoretically and empirically to Nyström approximations for DPPs (Affandi et al. AISTATS’13) or to spectral truncation bounds (Affandi et al. ICML’14)? The “not constrained to Z⊂Y” point is minor; a more substantive comparison is needed.
- Proofs are deferred to the supplement with only a brief mention of Seiler–Simon; a succinct sketch in the main text would aid comprehension.

Experimental evaluation
- Limited scope and baselines: No finite, large-n experiment contrasting to power iteration/spectral MH as in [7]; no timing or accuracy comparisons; no ablation on Z optimization vs random Z; no ESS/time for MH; no sensitivity to m, initialization, or kernel misspecification. The 2D dataset results are qualitative; no predictive assessment or likelihood on held-out.
- MCMC diagnostics missing: No ESS, autocorrelation, or convergence checks beyond agreement with “ideal MH” in the toy case. The impact of optimizing Z during MH on cost/accuracy is not quantified.
- Variational optimization: The paper alternates between CMA-ES (derivative-free) and “gradient-based optimization” without providing gradients or implementation details. The failure of VI in 1D (κ unidentifiable) is informative but suggests the need for a more principled treatment of identifiability and priors.

Relation to prior work and novelty
- The finite-case bound is a fairly direct transposition of the variational GP determinant inequality (Titsias 2009) to the DPP normalizer; the main novelty is its use for DPPs (finite and continuous) and in retrospective MH. This is interesting but somewhat incremental; stronger theoretical development and empirical comparisons would help justify significance.

Pros
- Simple, general bounds; practical removal of spectral computations.
- Extension to continuous DPPs is valuable and technically nontrivial.
- Two algorithms (VI and exact MH with bounds) derived from the same tool.
- Promising preliminary results; bounds tighten with m.

Cons
- Numerous clarity/typographical issues and inconsistent notation.
- Missing theoretical guarantees (bound convergence; MH exactness under adaptation; conditions for LZ).
- Insufficient empirical evaluation and baselines; limited scalability demonstrations.
- Incomplete discussion of continuous-case computability and numerical stability.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The core idea is appealing and potentially useful, but the current submission has too many presentation issues, lacks key theoretical guarantees, and provides limited empirical evidence relative to prior work. With substantial revisions—cleaned-up exposition, rigorous convergence guarantees, correctness proof for the adaptive retrospective MH, stronger and broader experiments with comparisons—the paper could become a strong contribution.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a theorem (with assumptions) on the convergence of the bounds as m→∞ (finite and continuous cases).
- State and handle conditions for invertibility of LZ; recommend and analyze regularization.
- Formalize the retrospective MH test with adaptive m and optimized Z; prove exactness (or cite a precise result) and provide pseudocode and complexity analysis.
- For the continuous case, give explicit formulas and complexity for Ψ for Gaussian kernels/measures; discuss numerical stability.
- Expand experiments: large finite ground sets with timing/accuracy vs spectral baselines; sensitivity to m and Z; MH diagnostics; predictive evaluation.
- Thoroughly fix notation, equation numbering, typos, and complete the bibliography (years, venues, correct titles). Clarify treatment of µ in 2D experiments and unify parameterization across sections.
