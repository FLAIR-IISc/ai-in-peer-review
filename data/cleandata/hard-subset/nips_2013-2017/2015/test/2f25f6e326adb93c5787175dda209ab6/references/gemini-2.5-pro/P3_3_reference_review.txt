### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the evaluation of the term `det(L + I)` in the DPP likelihood, which typically requires a full or partial spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix/operator L. The authors propose a novel approach that avoids spectral methods entirely. Drawing inspiration from variational methods for sparse Gaussian processes (GPs), they introduce a set of inducing points (termed "pseudo-inputs") to construct computationally tractable lower and upper bounds on this determinant. These bounds are derived for both DPPs on finite sets and DPPs on continuous domains. The authors then demonstrate how these non-spectral bounds can be integrated into two standard inference paradigms: 1) a variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes the lower bound on the log-likelihood, and 2) a Metropolis-Hastings (MH) algorithm that uses retrospective sampling to make acceptance decisions based on the bounds without computing the exact likelihood ratio. The efficacy of the proposed methods is demonstrated on a 1D synthetic dataset where the exact likelihood is known, validating the MCMC approach, and on a real-world spatial point process dataset of nerve fibers, showcasing the practical utility of the VI approach.

### Strengths and Contributions

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The computational cost of the determinant in the DPP likelihood is a major obstacle to their widespread application, especially in large-scale or continuous settings. This paper provides a practical and elegant solution that completely bypasses the need for expensive and sometimes intractable spectral decompositions. This is a significant contribution to the DPP literature.

2.  **Novel Connection and Methodology:** The core idea of using inducing points to build bounds on the determinant is cleverly adapted from the sparse GP literature [11]. While the mathematical inequality itself is from prior work [14], its application to the DPP likelihood is novel and insightful. This connection opens up new avenues for cross-pollination of ideas between the GP and DPP communities. The extension of this framework from the finite to the continuous case (Section 4) is also a valuable theoretical contribution, making the method broadly applicable.

3.  **Flexible Inference Framework:** The paper demonstrates the versatility of the proposed bounds by incorporating them into both an approximate (VI) and an asymptotically exact (MCMC) inference framework. This provides practitioners with a choice between speed and accuracy, depending on their needs. The use of retrospective sampling in the MCMC context is particularly elegant, allowing for exact Bayesian inference without ever computing the true likelihood.

4.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing.
    *   The 1D Gaussian-Gaussian toy problem is an excellent choice for a sanity check, as the known true likelihood allows for a direct comparison with an "ideal" MH sampler. This experiment convincingly demonstrates the correctness of the retrospective MCMC sampler. It also honestly highlights the inherent parameter identifiability issues with the L-parametrization, which is an important insight.
    *   The diabetic neuropathy dataset experiment shows that the method scales to real-world problems. The analysis of how parameter estimates and likelihood bounds converge as the number of inducing points (`m`) increases is particularly useful, as it provides a practical heuristic for tuning this crucial hyperparameter. The observation that optimized inducing points form structured grids is also an interesting empirical finding.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity on the Connection to Sparse GPs:** The paper states it draws "inspiration" from sparse GPs. The connection is, in fact, much deeper and more direct. The lower bound on the log-likelihood in Eq. (6) is mathematically equivalent to the variational lower bound (ELBO) for a sparse GP model (specifically, the Titsias 2009 framework) if one considers a GP with a likelihood that involves `det(L+I)`. The authors should make this connection more explicit. This is not a weakness of the method itself—rather, acknowledging the direct link would strengthen the paper by placing it more precisely in the literature and clarifying the origin of the bounds.

2.  **Optimization of Inducing Inputs (Z):** The paper proposes jointly optimizing the kernel parameters `θ` and the inducing inputs `Z`. However, it provides limited detail on the practicalities of this optimization. This joint optimization can be challenging, with potential for local minima. A brief discussion on initialization strategies for `Z` (e.g., k-means clustering on the data, or a random subset), the stability of the optimization, and its sensitivity to the number of inducing points `m` would be beneficial. The observation that the optimized points form regular grids is fascinating; does this suggest a principled way to initialize or constrain them?

3.  **The L vs. K Parametrization Issue:** The authors are upfront about the decision to parametrize the `L` kernel for tractability, at the cost of interpretability compared to parametrizing the `K` kernel. The 1D experiment powerfully illustrates this downside, showing that the `κ` parameter is practically unidentifiable from the likelihood alone. While the paper's contribution is on *inference* given the L-parametrization, this remains a significant practical limitation for modeling. It would be valuable for the discussion to briefly speculate on whether their bounding technique could, in the future, be adapted to help with inference for the more interpretable K-parametrization, which would be a major breakthrough.

4.  **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper mentions an `O(nm^2)` complexity for the VI bound evaluation but could provide a more detailed comparison. For instance, how does the wall-clock time of the proposed VI method compare against the spectral method from [7] for a fixed level of accuracy? The spectral method's cost depends on the number of eigenvalues needed, which can vary. A comparative experiment or a more detailed theoretical discussion of the trade-offs would strengthen the claims of computational efficiency.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality, well-written, and impactful paper. It tackles a significant, well-known problem in the field of DPPs with a novel and effective method. The theoretical contributions are sound, and the experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to suggestions for improving clarity and adding further discussion, rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology. The work is original, technically sound, and highly significant, as it is likely to become a standard technique for performing inference in DPP models, thereby making them accessible to a wider range of applications. I strongly recommend acceptance.
