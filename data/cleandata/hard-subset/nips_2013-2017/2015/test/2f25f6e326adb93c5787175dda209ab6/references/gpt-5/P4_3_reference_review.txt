Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decompositions. For finite ground sets, it introduces nonspectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) using a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY built from “inducing inputs” Z, inspired by sparse GP techniques (e.g., Titsias 2009). The bounds rely on determinant inequalities (Seiler–Simon) and the Schur complement property that L−Q is PSD for Gram matrices from positive definite kernels, even when Z is not a subset of Y. These bounds enable (i) a variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood that can be optimized jointly over kernel parameters and inducing inputs, and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) scheme that adaptively tightens acceptance bounds by increasing |Z|.

The paper further extends the approach to continuous DPPs by bounding the Fredholm determinant det(I+L) via finite-dimensional quantities involving LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x). This yields computable bounds for Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures.

Empirically, the authors demonstrate: (a) on a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP with a known spectrum, their retrospective MH recovers informative parameters (lengthscale, base measure spread) while highlighting non-identifiability of an overall intensity parameter κ under the L-ensemble parametrization; (b) on a 2D diabetic neuropathy dataset, variational training with increasing m shows convergence of both bounds and of overdispersion measures that separate two clinical classes, in line with prior findings (Affandi et al., Lavancier et al.).

The work connects to: DPP learning via eigenspectra (Affandi et al., ICML 2013/2014), DPP MLE/EM and fixed-point methods (Gillenwater et al., Mariet & Sra, NIPS), statistical DPPs in continuous spaces (Lavancier et al., JRSSB), and numerical Fredholm determinants (Bornemann). The novelty lies in providing determinant bounds that do not require spectral information and are applicable in continuous settings.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Elegant, simple nonspectral bounds for det(I+L) and their continuous analogue, grounded in known matrix/operator inequalities. This directly addresses a key computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
- Practicality: Bounds reduce determinant computations to m×m systems via Sylvester/MDL identities, yielding O(n m^2) scaling for the denominator term and avoiding storage of full eigenspectra.
- Generality: Works for both finite and continuous domains; the continuous results are rarely available in ML-oriented DPP work and are useful when Fredholm determinants are intractable.
- Inference machinery: Clear integration into variational training and retrospective MH; the latter is a principled way to make exact decisions with adaptive bounds.
- Empirical sanity checks: The 1D synthetic experiment leverages a case with known spectrum to validate the MH sampler; the real-data study shows convergence with m and interpretable effects on overdispersion.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Theoretical tightness/convergence: While it is plausible that bounds tighten as |Z| increases and Z is optimized (by analogy to sparse GPs), the paper does not provide a formal statement or rate on bound convergence (finite or operator case). A theorem (even under standard kernel conditions) would strengthen the contribution.
- MCMC correctness details: Retrospective MH with adaptive m and Z optimization is standard, but a brief argument ensuring that the adaptive refinement does not bias acceptance (i.e., bounds always valid and refinement independent of u beyond the stopping rule) would improve rigor.
- Empirical scope: No finite-discrete large-n experiments, despite the method being motivated by such settings (e.g., summarization, recommendation). A comparison to spectral methods (e.g., power iteration-based bounds) on a discrete benchmark would better quantify gains and accuracy tradeoffs.
- Comparisons and metrics: Beyond the toy continuous setting (where ground truth is available), the real-data study lacks comparisons to prior spectral inference methods or to alternative parametrizations (K vs L), and does not report approximate held-out likelihood or classification performance using the learned models.
- Identifiability and parametrization: The non-identifiability of κ under L-ensemble is demonstrated, but guidance on priors or reparametrizations to mitigate this in practice would be useful.
- Clarity/minor issues: Some equation numbering and footnotes are awkward; assumptions in the continuous section (trace-class, tr(L)=∫ L(x,x)dµ) could be stated more cleanly with a short lemma or reference for common kernels/measures; gradients for Ψ and computational details beyond Gaussian–Gaussian could be briefly discussed.

Quality
- The bounds appear technically sound, following known determinant inequalities and PSD properties of Schur complements. The construction Q ≤ L in Loewner order is standard for Gram matrices/operator analogues.
- The inference procedures are reasonable; variational bound and retrospective MH are correctly set up.
- Missing formal convergence analyses and limited empirical comparisons modestly weaken the overall evaluation.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable, with helpful motivation and connections to sparse GP inference. The continuous section could benefit from slightly more self-contained assumptions and computational guidance. Proofs in the supplement are acceptable, but an outline sketch in the main text would aid intuition.

Originality
- Provides new, nonspectral determinant bounds tailored to DPP likelihoods and extends them to continuous DPPs. While the variational perspective is inspired by sparse GPs, the application to DPP determinants and to Fredholm determinants is novel within the DPP literature.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for practitioners: removes spectral bottlenecks, offers scalable approximate inference and an exact MH option. The continuous-domain extension is particularly valuable.
- Stronger empirical evidence on standard discrete benchmarks and comparisons to eigen-based methods would increase practical significance.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you provide a formal result on the monotone tightening or convergence of the bounds as m→∞ under standard kernel assumptions (finite and continuous cases)?
- For retrospective MH, please include a brief correctness argument (or reference) for adaptive refinement of Z and m.
- Report a discrete large-n experiment comparing runtime/accuracy against spectral methods (e.g., Affandi et al.).
- Discuss strategies to address κ non-identifiability (e.g., priors, fixing scale, or reparametrizations relating L and K without full spectra).
- Clarify computation and differentiation of Ψ for non-Gaussian settings (e.g., quadrature, random features).
- Fix minor notation/numbering issues and give a short proof sketch of Proposition 1 in the main text.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, simple, and broadly applicable nonspectral bounds for DPP inference.
- Applicable to continuous DPPs; fills a gap in ML literature.
- Integrates into both variational and exact MH inference.
- Empirical results consistent with theory; practical runtimes.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of formal convergence/tightness guarantees.
- Limited empirical validation on discrete large-scale tasks and lack of direct comparisons to eigen-based baselines.
- Some clarity gaps in the continuous section and MCMC correctness details.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: Medium
