- Avoids costly spectral decomposition by proposing novel bounds on DPP likelihoods, enabling inference without spectral knowledge.  
- Bounds are inspired by GP variational techniques and are theoretically justified using determinant inequalities.  
- Bounds are integrated into both Variational Inference (VI) and MCMC frameworks, demonstrating practical applicability.  
- Results are extended to continuous DPPs, using trace-class operator assumptions and Fredholm determinants.  
- Comprehensive experiments include a synthetic example with known spectrum and a diabetic neuropathy real-data case, showing method effectiveness (especially MCMC).  
- Paper is generally well-written, with clear motivations and accessible derivations.  
- Direct parametrization of kernel L (rather than K) causes identifiability issues and decouples parameters from interpretable properties (e.g., expected count, repulsiveness).  
- Optimizing pseudo-inputs introduces additional computational cost; the paper lacks detailed analysis of this cost and its scalability compared to spectral approximations.  
- The VI component largely adapts existing sparse GP methods; the main novelty is the likelihood bounds rather than the VI machinery.  
- Connection between finite-case Proposition 1 and continuous-case Proposition 2 could be explained more explicitly (trace-class operator and Fredholm determinant link).  
- MCMC retrospective sampling is well-explained but needs discussion of the cost of iteratively increasing m and strategies to mitigate it.  
- Continuous DPP assumptions (Mercer’s theorem, trace-class) are briefly mentioned and would benefit from more practical discussion.  
- Synthetic experiment reveals poor identification of κ; authors could discuss remedies like informative priors or alternative parameterizations.  
- Diabetic neuropathy experiment shows overdispersion convergence as m increases but lacks runtime comparisons across m to assess practical scalability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a core bottleneck in DPP inference, provides sound theoretical bounds, and shows effective empirical performance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: identifiability from L parametrization, added pseudo-input optimization cost, and limited novelty in the VI section.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions, asking for clarification on computational costs and continuous-DPP assumptions.
