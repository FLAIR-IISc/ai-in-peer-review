

# Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L when evaluating the likelihood. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid this computational burden, drawing inspiration from sparse approximations in Gaussian processes. They derive bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, demonstrating their utility in variational inference and MCMC frameworks. The paper makes a clear connection to prior work on sparse Gaussian processes while addressing a specific gap in DPP inference literature.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach to a well-defined problem. The bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 are derived from a nontrivial determinant inequality [14], providing a theoretically grounded solution. The authors carefully address the practical challenges of DPP inference, acknowledging the trade-off between interpretability (parametrizing K) and computational tractability (parametrizing L). The experimental validation on both a synthetic Gaussian continuous dataset and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrates the practical utility of their approach.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, particularly regarding the parametrization of L instead of K, which complicates interpretation. The supplementary material contains the necessary proofs, which is standard for conference papers. The experiments are well-designed to validate the core claims, though a more comprehensive computational efficiency comparison with [7] would strengthen the evaluation.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The authors effectively build from the problem statement to the solution, with a logical progression through the finite case before extending to continuous domains. The connection to Gaussian process literature is well-explained, helping readers unfamiliar with DPPs understand the approach. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout, and the main contributions are clearly articulated early in the paper.

The experimental section is particularly clear, with well-chosen examples that demonstrate the method's utility without overwhelming the reader. The supplementary material appears to contain sufficient detail for reproducibility. The only minor clarity issue is that the connection between the pseudo-inputs' placement (Figure 1a) and the underlying process's marginal distribution could be more explicitly explained.

## Originality
This work makes a clear contribution to the DPP inference literature by providing nonspectral bounds, which is a significant departure from existing approaches that require spectral decomposition [7]. While the approach borrows from sparse Gaussian process methods [11], the adaptation to DPPs is novel and addresses a specific problem in DPP literature. The paper properly situates itself against prior work, citing [7] as the most relevant method that uses spectral bounds, and clearly articulating how their approach differs.

The authors' insight that the bounds can be tightened by optimizing over pseudo-inputs is particularly novel. This optimization step is crucial for making likelihood-based inference feasible and is a key contribution that distinguishes their work from other approximate inference methods.

## Significance
The paper addresses a meaningful bottleneck in DPP applications, which are increasingly used for modeling diversity in machine learning (e.g., [3, 6, 7]). Efficient inference is essential for scaling DPPs to larger datasets, and this work provides a practical approach to doing so without spectral knowledge. The experimental results demonstrate that their method works well on both synthetic and real-world data, showing clear separation between classes in the diabetic neuropathy dataset.

The significance is further enhanced by the connection to broader point process literature and the potential for extension to other problems. The authors appropriately note future directions, including exploring connections with quadrature-based bounds for Fredholm determinants and addressing the L vs. K parametrization issue.

## Strengths
- Solves a well-defined, practical problem in DPP inference
- Provides theoretically grounded bounds that avoid spectral decomposition
- Demonstrates the approach through both variational inference and MCMC
- Validated on both synthetic and real-world datasets
- Clear connections to related work in Gaussian processes
- Well-organized and clearly written

## Weaknesses
- Limited computational efficiency comparison with existing spectral-based methods
- Could more thoroughly explore the relationship between pseudo-input selection and inference quality
- Superficial treatment of the interpretability trade-off between L and K parametrization
- The "retrospective sampling" MCMC approach is not compared directly with other MCMC methods

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong contribution to the field that addresses a significant practical limitation in DPP inference. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the literature. While the computational efficiency comparison with existing methods could be strengthened, the core contribution of providing nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihood evaluation is valuable and timely.

The paper meets the quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria for NIPS. It presents a novel solution to a well-defined problem with practical applications, and the results are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The work advances the state of the art in DPP inference by removing a key computational bottleneck that has limited the applicability of likelihood-based methods.

I recommend acceptance. The paper provides a valuable tool for practitioners working with DPPs, and the approach could potentially be extended to other point process models. The authors have made a significant contribution to the literature that will be useful for both researchers and practitioners.
