### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the calculation of the normalization constant, `det(L + I)`, which typically requires an expensive spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix or operator `L`. The authors propose a novel approach that bypasses this requirement by deriving non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant. The proposed bounds are inspired by variational methods for sparse Gaussian Processes (GPs), utilizing a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" `Z`. A key aspect of the method is that these pseudo-inputs are variational parameters that can be optimized to tighten the bounds.

The authors derive these bounds first for DPPs over finite sets and then generalize them to the more complex case of DPPs over continuous domains. They demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference frameworks:
1.  **Variational Inference (VI):** The derived lower bound on the log-likelihood is maximized jointly with respect to the model parameters `θ` and the pseudo-input locations `Z`, providing an efficient, albeit approximate, inference scheme.
2.  **Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC):** The bounds are used within a Metropolis-Hastings algorithm using a "retrospective sampling" technique. This allows for exact MCMC sampling from the true posterior by adaptively tightening the bounds (e.g., by increasing `m` and re-optimizing `Z`) only until an accept/reject decision can be made, avoiding the full likelihood calculation.

The paper validates these methods on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP, where the exact likelihood is known, and on a real-world spatial point process dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—the non-spectral, optimizable bounds on the DPP likelihood—is highly original and significant. It offers a new and principled way to perform inference for DPPs, moving away from methods that rely on partial or full eigendecompositions [7]. This approach has the potential to broaden the applicability of DPPs to settings where spectral methods are intractable, particularly for continuous domains. The connection drawn to sparse GPs is insightful and provides a strong theoretical foundation.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The bounds are derived rigorously from a known determinant inequality [14], and their application to both VI and MCMC is well-formulated. The extension from the finite to the continuous case is non-trivial and handled carefully, making the contribution more general and impactful.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors made an excellent choice by first presenting the concepts in the simpler setting of finite DPPs before moving to the continuous case. This structure makes the paper accessible to a broad audience. The motivation is clear, and the relationship to prior work is well-articulated.

4.  **Comprehensive Inference Framework:** By showing how the bounds can be used for both fast/approximate VI and exact/slower MCMC, the paper provides a flexible toolkit for practitioners. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the utility of both approaches. The 1D toy experiment, which validates the MCMC sampler against a ground-truth likelihood, is particularly strong evidence of the method's correctness. The real-world experiment demonstrates practical utility and shows the sensible behavior of the bounds converging as the number of pseudo-inputs increases.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The paper demonstrates that the proposed methods work well, but it lacks a direct quantitative comparison to the most relevant baseline, the spectral-bound MCMC method of Affandi et al. [7]. While the paper argues conceptually that avoiding eigendecompositions is advantageous, an empirical comparison of runtime versus accuracy (e.g., quality of posterior approximation or effective sample size per second) would have made the practical benefits more concrete. It is not immediately clear how the cost of optimizing pseudo-inputs at each MCMC step compares to computing a small number of eigenvalues.

2.  **Cost of Optimizing Pseudo-inputs:** The performance of the method relies on optimizing the locations of the pseudo-inputs `Z`. This optimization is a non-convex problem that adds computational overhead, especially within the MCMC framework where it is performed at each iteration. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical cost of this step and strategies to manage it. For example, is a full optimization required at every MCMC step, or would a few gradient steps suffice? This optimization cost seems to be the primary trade-off against the cost of spectral methods.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The VI method has a complexity of `O(nm^2)`, which is a significant improvement over `O(n^3)`. However, the linear dependence on `n` (the size of the ground set) may still be a limitation for extremely large-scale discrete DPPs. A brief discussion of the practical scalability limits of the approach would be welcome.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Empirical Baseline Comparison:** I strongly encourage the authors to add an experiment comparing their MCMC approach to the spectral-bound MCMC from [7] on a moderately large finite DPP problem. This would provide a direct measure of the computational trade-offs and help practitioners choose between the methods.

*   **Details on Pseudo-input Optimization:** To address the weakness concerning the optimization cost, it would be helpful to provide more details in the appendix. Specifically, what is the complexity of one optimization step for `Z`? What was the typical number of iterations required for convergence in the experiments? Discussing practical strategies, such as using the previously accepted `Z` as a warm-start for the next MCMC iteration, would be valuable.

*   **Insight from Optimized Pseudo-inputs:** The observation that the optimized pseudo-inputs form a regular grid resembling an "ideal" DPP sample is fascinating (Figures 1a and 4). This insight could be discussed further. Does this suggest a principled way to initialize `Z` (e.g., using k-means or another clustering method on the data) to speed up convergence?

*   **Minor Clarification:** The paper mentions the interpretability issue of parameterizing the `L` kernel directly, which is a known problem in the literature. While solving this is outside the scope of the paper, it might be worth briefly mentioning in the discussion that this remains an open challenge for tractable DPP inference methods, including the one proposed here.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong and novel contribution to the literature on DPPs. The proposed non-spectral bounds provide a new, flexible, and powerful foundation for likelihood-based inference. The work is technically solid, clearly presented, and well-motivated. While the experimental comparison could be strengthened, the existing results are convincing and demonstrate the method's effectiveness. This work is likely to be of high interest to researchers and practitioners working with DPPs and related models. I recommend acceptance.
