- Addresses the major computational bottleneck in DPP likelihood inference by avoiding spectral decomposition.  
- Proposes non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood applicable to finite and continuous domains.  
- Derives bounds using determinant inequalities and variational-inference-inspired techniques.  
- Uses inducing-variable approximations analogous to sparse Gaussian processes (Titsias, 2009).  
- Demonstrates applicability within both Variational Inference (VI) and MCMC frameworks.  
- Empirically validated on a 1D Gaussian DPP toy example and a real nerve-fiber dataset.  
- Builds on prior DPP work and acknowledges existing parameterizations that avoid spectral computation.  
- Improves over prior bounds (Affandi et al., 2017) by removing the need for power iteration.  
- Strength: directly tackles a significant practical scaling problem for DPPs.  
- Strength: theoretically sound derivation and valuable extension to continuous domains.  
- Strength: clear presentation and logical structure overall.  
- Strength: provides flexible inference options (VI and MCMC) and convincing empirical convergence analysis.  
- Weakness: parameterizing L directly reduces interpretability; limited identification of some parameters (e.g., κ).  
- Weakness: optimizing inducing inputs Z (bound tightening) within MCMC may be computationally expensive, especially in high dimensions.  
- Weakness: limited empirical comparison to other DPP approximation methods (e.g., low-rank kernel approximations).  
- Weakness: dense notation in the continuous DPP section (Section 4) reduces readability.  
- Review scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 7/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 7/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically justified, practically important, and empirically supported.  
- Arguments against acceptance: interpretability loss, potential computational cost, and need for broader comparisons.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to address interpretability, computational scaling, and comparisons.
