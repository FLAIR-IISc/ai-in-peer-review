This paper tackles a critical and long-standing bottleneck in the practical application of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the prohibitive computational cost of likelihood-based inference. The likelihood for a DPP involves a determinant term, `det(L+I)`, which typically requires a full O(n^3) spectral decomposition for finite domains or is analytically intractable for continuous domains. Previous attempts to address this, such as the work of Affandi et al. (2014), still relied on partial spectral information (e.g., power iteration to find top eigenvalues), limiting their scalability and applicability.

The core contribution of this paper is a completely novel, non-spectral approach to bound this determinant. Drawing a clever and insightful connection to sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes (GPs), the authors introduce a set of "inducing points" to construct a low-rank approximation `Q` of the kernel matrix `L`. This approximation leads to computationally cheap upper and lower bounds on the determinant, reducing the complexity from O(n^3) to a much more manageable O(nm^2), where `m` is the number of inducing points. A major strength of the work is the elegant extension of this framework from finite sets to DPPs on continuous domains, providing analogous bounds for the Fredholm determinant. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be seamlessly integrated into standard inference machinery, namely Variational Inference (by maximizing the lower bound) and MCMC (using retrospective sampling).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Creativity:** The central idea of using an inducing point framework, borrowed from the GP literature, to create non-spectral bounds for the DPP likelihood is a significant conceptual breakthrough. It reframes the problem of DPP inference from one of spectral analysis to one of low-rank approximation. This is a bold and highly original direction that moves the field beyond the limitations of previous eigenvalue-based methods.

2.  **Major Practical Significance:** This work has the potential to make DPPs a far more practical and widely-used tool. By removing the dependence on spectral decomposition, the proposed methods dramatically improve scalability. The extension to continuous domains, where spectral methods are often impossible, is particularly impactful and opens the door for new applications of DPPs in areas dealing with spatial or functional data. Researchers and practitioners will likely adopt and build upon this framework.

3.  **Theoretical Soundness and Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. It builds intuition by first presenting the finite case before tackling the more theoretically demanding continuous case. The theoretical claims are well-supported, rooting the bounds in established matrix inequalities.

4.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the method's value. The 1D toy experiment is an excellent sanity check, showing that the MCMC sampler recovers the correct posterior distribution when compared to an "ideal" sampler that has access to the true determinant. The real-world diabetic neuropathy experiment demonstrates the method's utility on a challenging continuous problem, replicating previous scientific findings and showing how the bounds and parameter estimates converge gracefully as the number of inducing points increases. The authors are also commendably honest about the limitations they encounter, such as the parameter identifiability issues in the toy example, which they correctly attribute to the model parametrization rather than their inference technique.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Guidance on Inducing Point Selection:** The performance of the method hinges on the number (`m`) and locations (`Z`) of the inducing points. The paper treats these as variational parameters to be optimized, which is the correct approach. However, practical guidance on initialization strategies or heuristics for choosing `m` is limited, beyond the sensible suggestion to increase it until results stabilize. While this is a common challenge in all inducing point methods and not a fundamental flaw, a more detailed discussion would have been beneficial.

2.  **Does Not Address the L vs. K Parametrization Issue:** The authors correctly identify that parametrizing the L-kernel directly for tractability harms the interpretability of the model's parameters (e.g., decoupling repulsion from the expected number of points). While their work focuses on making inference with the L-kernel tractable, it does not offer a new solution to this more fundamental modeling issue. This is not a flaw of the proposed method itself, but an acknowledged limitation of the paradigm within which it operates.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and creative solution to a major bottleneck in a popular modeling family.
*   The work is of high significance, drastically improving the scalability and applicability of DPPs.
*   The theoretical framework is sound and elegantly unifies inference for finite and continuous domains.
*   Experimental validation is thorough and convincing.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The core technical machinery (inducing points) is adapted from another field (GPs), though its application here is non-trivial and insightful.
*   Practical guidance on hyperparameter choices (e.g., number of inducing points) could be more extensive.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a significant and bold new direction for inference in determinantal point processes. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to the practicalities of implementation and the limitations of the broader field, rather than the core contribution. The paper's novelty and impact far outweigh these concerns. It is a complete and high-quality piece of work that will undoubtedly influence future research in this area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
