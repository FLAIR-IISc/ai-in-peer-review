- Proposes nonspectral, inducing-point-based two-sided bounds on determinants in DPP likelihoods for finite matrices and continuous integral operators.
- Introduces inducing inputs Z and a low-rank surrogate Q that yields determinant bounds via a Seiler & Simon inequality.
- Extends bounds to continuous DPPs using Fredholm determinants under trace-class assumptions and computable cross-covariance Ψ.
- Main advantage: avoids eigendecompositions, valuable when spectra are unavailable or expensive to compute.
- Develops two inference schemes using the bounds: a variational lower bound optimized over θ and Z, and a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) sampler using progressively tighter bounds.
- Methodology parallels sparse GP variational ideas (Titsias) and Nyström-like structure but allows Z beyond observed items.
- Experiments: synthetic 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP (closed-form baseline) showing retrospective MH matches ideal posterior; variational method unstable due to identifiability.
- Real-data experiment on 2D diabetic neuropathy shows bound convergence, class-separating overdispersion measures, reasonable runtimes, and structured inducing inputs.
- Situates work relative to prior spectral/Nyström DPP approaches and connects continuous-case treatment to numerical Fredholm determinant literature.
- Technical soundness: finite/infinite determinant bounds rely on known inequality and mirror GP variational constructions; operator assumptions reasonable for Gaussian setups.
- Empirical support is promising but limited: good synthetic validation and one real dataset, but missing head-to-head comparisons and large-scale discrete experiments.
- Authors acknowledge limitations: identifiability of L parametrization and need for computable Ψ in the continuous case.
- Clarity: generally well organized with helpful connections to sparse GPs, but equation numbering and some dense sentences need fixes.
- Requested clarifications: systematic computational complexities, explicit gradients for θ and Z (or supplement pointer), and Z initialization strategy.
- Unclear whether bounds tighten monotonically with m for nested Z; this should be clarified.
- Novelty: two-sided nonspectral determinant bounds for DPPs and extension to Fredholm determinants are new and useful despite inspiration from sparse GP methods.
- Significance: addresses a key bottleneck in DPP learning (dependence on spectra) and enables fitting DPPs where spectral methods are impractical.
- Strengths: simple general-purpose bounds, continuous extension, practical VI and retrospective MH algorithms, synthetic validation and real-data demonstration, clear GP connections.
- Weaknesses: limited empirical scope (no large-scale discrete tests or spectral baselines), no theoretical tightness characterization or monotonicity guarantees, and sensitivity/practical guidance for Z missing.
- Continuous-case practicality depends on closed-form Ψ; more guidance needed for non-Gaussian settings (quadrature, random features).
- Parameterization/identifiability of L is an issue; regularization or alternative parametrizations could help.
- Reproducibility concern: code availability not mentioned.
- Questions for authors include requests for empirical comparisons to spectral methods, monotonicity of bounds with m, sensitivity to Z initialization, Ψ approximation strategies for non-Gaussian cases, and gradient details/auto-diff use.
- Minor fixes: correct equation numbering, report complexities and memory costs, and add a large finite-domain experiment (n in tens of thousands).
- Acceptance arguments: important bottleneck addressed, solid theory bridging finite and continuous cases, practical algorithms and validations, likely uptake by community.
- Rejection arguments: limited empirical comparisons and scale, lack of theoretical tightness analysis, and insufficient practical guidance for Z selection and non-Gaussian continuous settings.
