## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decomposition, a computationally expensive operation, especially for large datasets or continuous domains. The authors propose bounds on the DPP likelihood inspired by variational inference for Gaussian Processes, extending these bounds to both finite and continuous settings. They demonstrate the utility of these bounds within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, presenting experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The core idea – leveraging bounds to circumvent spectral decomposition – is a significant contribution. The connection to variational GP inference is insightful and provides a principled foundation for the proposed bounds. The extension to continuous DPPs, while concise, broadens the applicability of the method. The experimental validation, including the comparison with existing MCMC approaches on a 1D Gaussian example and application to a nerve fiber dataset, provides evidence of the method’s practical utility.

However, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet cumulative, flaws that raise concerns regarding rigor and thoroughness. 

Firstly, the writing frequently lacks precision. Phrases like “some work investigating approximations” (Section 1) are too vague and should be specific. Numerous instances of “e.g.” are followed by only one example, rendering the abbreviation inappropriate. The introduction contains several instances of repeated information (e.g., the challenges of spectral decomposition).

Secondly, the mathematical presentation is occasionally sloppy. The equation numbering is inconsistent. Proposition 1 is stated without defining what Q represents *before* the equation, making it difficult to follow. The statement "det(Q + I)e −tr(L−Q) ≤ 1det(L + I) ≤ 1 det(Q + I)" uses "e" where it appears "1" is intended (a typo). Similarly, the derivation of bounds (4) and (11) relies on a cited inequality [14] but lacks sufficient justification for its application within the DPP context. A reader unfamiliar with [14] would be left wondering why it is applicable here. The definition of the continuous DPP in Section 4, while referencing existing work, feels abrupt and lacks sufficient motivation for its chosen formulation. 

Thirdly, the experimental section is underwhelming. While results are presented, the analysis lacks depth. The choice of hyper-parameters for the variational inference and MCMC algorithms isn’t clearly explained. The statement "Retrospective MH and the ideal MH agree" in Section 5.1 is insufficiently supported. The convergence of the MCMC chains isn’t visually assessed (trace plots are missing). The discussion of the optimized pseudo-inputs (Figure 1a) lacks insightful interpretation beyond observing their grid-like arrangement. The convergence analysis in Section 5.2, while mentioning convergence to similar values with increasing *m*, doesn't quantify this convergence with error bars or a similar metric.

Fourthly, the discussion section is rather generic. The suggested future work (“connections with the quadrature-based bounds”) feels superficial. The lack of exploration of potential limitations of the proposed approach, especially regarding the choice of pseudo-inputs *Z*, is a notable omission.

Finally, there are several minor formatting issues: inconsistent use of boldface, incorrect citation formatting, and a couple of instances where equations are not properly aligned.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant practical bottleneck in DPP inference.
*   Provides novel bounds with theoretical grounding.
*   Demonstrates empirical performance on relevant datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor technical inaccuracies and imprecisions.
*   Lack of thorough experimental analysis and justification of choices.
*   Insufficient clarity in writing and mathematical presentation.
*   Generic discussion section with limited insights.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the approach, the numerous flaws, while individually minor, collectively undermine the rigor and polish expected of a NIPS publication. The paper feels incomplete and could benefit significantly from a thorough revision addressing the points raised above. While the core idea is valuable, the current presentation falls short of NIPS standards.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**.  I believe a major revision addressing the points outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration. The paper’s potential is clear, but the current iteration lacks the precision and thoroughness required for acceptance.
