- Addresses the computational bottleneck of needing spectral decomposition for DPP likelihoods by proposing nonspectral bounds.  
- Introduces tight nonspectral bounds for finite and continuous DPPs using pseudo-inputs (inducing points) inspired by sparse Gaussian processes.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical results (Propositions 1 and 2) that avoid spectral decomposition, important for continuous DPPs.  
- Demonstrates practical relevance with experiments on synthetic and real diabetic neuropathy data showing inference converges as number of pseudo-inputs increases.  
- Effectively connects the approach to sparse Gaussian processes and motivates the extension from finite to continuous domains.  
- Experimental design carefully addresses parameter identifiability and shows convergence behavior as m increases.  
- Chooses to parametrize L directly for computational convenience but provides weak justification and raises interpretability concerns.  
- Lacks direct empirical comparison to spectral-based bounds (e.g., [7]) in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency.  
- Computational complexity claim (O(nm²) vs O(n³)) is not supported by concrete runtime or scaling experiments.  
- Does not analyze sensitivity of results to pseudo-input initialization or optimization procedure.  
- Fails to explicitly relate the proposed bounds to existing quadrature-based or Fredholm-determinant methods.  
- Overall: technically sound and practically valuable for continuous DPPs, but significance is mainly computational rather than fundamental.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: justify parametrizing L vs K, include direct comparisons with spectral methods, add empirical runtime/scale evidence, and analyze sensitivity to pseudo-input initialization.
