This paper proposes a new method for parametric inference in determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoids the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix (or operator), which is a major computational bottleneck in existing approaches. The central contribution is the derivation of novel, computationally efficient, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the determinant term that appears in the DPP likelihood. The authors cleverly adapt the concept of "inducing points" from the sparse Gaussian process literature to construct these bounds. The method is presented for both DPPs on finite sets and DPPs on continuous domains, highlighting its generality. These bounds are then integrated into two standard inference frameworks: a variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes the evidence lower bound, and a Metropolis-Hastings (MH) MCMC algorithm that uses the "retrospective sampling" trick to make acceptance decisions without computing the exact likelihood. The experimental section validates the approach on a synthetic 1D problem where the true likelihood is known, and on a real-world 2D spatial point process dataset of nerve fibers in diabetic patients.

### Strengths

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a well-known and important limitation of DPPs: the high computational cost of the likelihood function, which scales cubically with the number of items in the ground set for finite DPPs. By providing bounds that avoid any spectral computation, this work offers a path towards more scalable inference. The connection drawn to inducing variable methods in Gaussian processes is insightful and elegant, providing a principled approximation framework that is new in the context of DPP inference. This is a clear and valuable contribution to the field.
*   **Technical Quality and Generality:** The work is technically solid. It provides a unified framework that covers both the discrete/finite case and the more complex continuous case, which involves operators on function spaces. The derivation of the bounds for the continuous case on the Fredholm determinant is a non-trivial extension and broadens the applicability of the method significantly.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. It begins with the simpler finite case to build intuition before moving to the more technical continuous case, which makes the material highly accessible. The relationship to and distinction from prior work, particularly Affandi et al. [7], is clearly articulated: that work also uses bounds for MCMC but requires partial eigendecompositions (via power iteration), whereas the proposed method is entirely spectral-free.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the utility of the proposed methods. The 1D Gaussian-Gaussian toy problem is an excellent choice, as the exact likelihood is computable, allowing for a direct and convincing validation of the MCMC sampler's correctness. The real-world diabetic neuropathy experiment shows that the method can be applied to practical problems, reproducing the scientific findings of previous studies and demonstrating that the variational inference approach converges to stable parameter estimates as the number of inducing points (`m`) increases.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are several areas where it could be improved.

1.  **Analysis of the Variational Method's Failure:** The authors honestly report that the VI approach "yields poorly consistent results" on the toy problem, which motivates their use of MCMC. This is a significant finding that deserves more investigation. Why did it fail? Was it due to the looseness of the variational bound for this problem, leading to a poor approximation of the true posterior? Or was it an optimization issue, with the joint optimization over kernel parameters `θ` and inducing inputs `Z` getting stuck in poor local optima? A deeper analysis here would be very valuable, as it would provide guidance on when the cheaper VI approach is likely to be sufficient versus when the more robust MCMC method is necessary.
2.  **Details on Optimization of Inducing Inputs:** The paper proposes to jointly optimize the variational lower bound with respect to both kernel parameters `θ` and the inducing inputs `Z`. This joint optimization can be challenging in practice. The manuscript would be strengthened by providing more details on the optimization strategy. For example: How were the inducing inputs initialized in the experiments? What were the practical challenges encountered during optimization? A discussion of the optimization landscape and potential strategies for avoiding poor local optima would be beneficial for practitioners seeking to apply this method.
3.  **Direct Comparison with Spectral-Bound Methods:** The experiments convincingly show the internal consistency of the proposed method (i.e., convergence as `m` increases). However, a direct quantitative comparison to the most relevant baseline, the spectral-bound MCMC from Affandi et al. [7], is missing. Such a comparison on the diabetic neuropathy dataset, for instance, could highlight the trade-offs in terms of computational time versus statistical efficiency (e.g., number of effective samples per second). This would make the practical advantages of the proposed non-spectral approach much more concrete.
4.  **Guidance on Selecting the Number of Inducing Inputs `m`:** The paper suggests a practical heuristic for choosing `m`: increase it until the inference results stabilize. While reasonable, it would be helpful to discuss more principled approaches. For example, during optimization, the gap between the upper and lower bounds (`tr(L-Q)` in the finite case) provides a direct measure of the approximation quality. Could this gap be monitored to provide a diagnostic for choosing `m` or even to adapt `m` during the optimization/sampling process?

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a key computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
*   Novel and elegant technical contribution, connecting DPPs to sparse GPs.
*   Technically sound, providing a general framework for both finite and continuous DPPs.
*   Clearly written and well-motivated.
*   Strong experimental validation, including a case with a known ground truth.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Lack of a direct experimental comparison with the main competing method (spectral-bound MCMC).
*   The reported failure of the variational approach on one of the experiments is not sufficiently analyzed.
*   Practical details regarding the challenging optimization of inducing inputs are sparse.

### Conclusion

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, significant, and well-executed contribution to the literature on learning with determinantal point processes. The proposed spectral-free bounds are a substantial step forward for making DPPs more scalable and practical. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to missing experimental comparisons and deeper analysis that would further strengthen the paper, rather than fundamental flaws in the method itself. The paper is a clear fit for NIPS and I recommend its acceptance. Addressing the suggested points during the rebuttal/camera-ready process would make this an even more impactful contribution.
