Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a key bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the dependence on spectral decompositions of the L-operator, which are analytically unavailable in continuous domains and computationally prohibitive for large finite sets. The authors propose non-spectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) built from a low-rank inducing-point approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, inspired by sparse GP variational methods (Titsias 2009), and underpinned by determinant inequalities (Seiler & Simon 1975). They provide (i) finite-domain bounds: det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I), and (ii) continuous-domain bounds using Fredholm determinants: det LZ / det(LZ+Ψ) multiplied by an exponential correction term as a lower bound, where Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x). They then (a) derive a variational lower bound for joint optimization over kernel parameters and inducing points, and (b) adapt retrospective Metropolis–Hastings to decide accept/reject using bounds that can be tightened by increasing m and optimizing Z.

Related work: The approach contrasts with spectral truncation methods (Affandi et al., ICML’13), EM and fixed-point methods for finite DPPs (Gillenwater et al., NIPS’12; Mariet & Sra, NIPS’15), and Nyström approximations (Affandi et al., AISTATS’13). The paper’s main novelty is to avoid any eigen-computation, and to extend to continuous DPPs with closed-form Ψ for Gaussian kernels and Gaussian base measures. They also relate to Bornemann’s quadrature for Fredholm determinants (2010).

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: clean, non-spectral determinant bounds applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs; elegant reuse of inducing-point machinery from sparse GPs.
- Practicality: computational complexity O(n m^2) and memory O(n m) for finite cases, substantially cheaper than O(n^3) eigendecompositions; does not store the full n×n Gram in continuous settings.
- Generality: bounds hold for any choice of inducing locations Z (not restricted to Nyström subsets), enabling optimization of Z to tighten bounds.
- Inference versatility: both a variational lower bound and a principled retrospective MH scheme; the latter is unbiased if bounds can be tightened arbitrarily.
- Continuous case: explicit construction with Fredholm determinants and closed-form Ψ for Gaussian/Gaussian, which is a widely used modeling choice.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality/technical soundness
- Convergence/tightness guarantees: The retrospective MH requires that the acceptance probability can be decided almost surely by increasing m. The paper stops short of a formal theorem stating conditions under which the upper/lower bounds converge to the true det(I+L) (or to the true log-likelihood) as m→∞. The GP posterior-variance heuristic is suggestive but not a proof; a precise statement (e.g., in terms of the trace norm of L−Q) would strengthen correctness claims.
- Invertibility/conditioning of LZ: The method assumes LZ is invertible. In practice, LZ can be ill-conditioned; a discussion of regularization (jitter), its effect on bounds, and stability would be useful.
- Scope of Ψ: In the continuous case, tractability hinges on computing Ψ. Beyond Gaussian kernels and Gaussian base measures, when is Ψ available in closed form or efficiently approximable (quadrature/MC)? Guidance on error control if Ψ is approximated would help.
- Complexity accounting: While O(n m^2) is attractive, practical large-n deployments need memory/time breakdowns (e.g., batch computations, kernel caching) and comparisons to power iterations when many eigenvalues are needed.

Clarity
- Some notation/equation inconsistencies and dangling references (e.g., equation numbering around (9)/(14); footnotes; minor typos). Tighten presentation, especially where K vs L are contrasted, and ensure all assumptions are clearly stated before propositions.
- The link to determinant inequality [14] is central; a proof sketch in the main text (with the precise inequality used) would aid readability.

Originality
- The application of Seiler–Simon determinant inequalities with inducing-point approximations to bound DPP normalizers appears novel, especially in the continuous setting. The conceptual bridge to sparse GPs is well-motivated.

Significance
- Potential to broaden applicability of DPPs where spectral decompositions are infeasible. However, empirical validation is limited:
  - No large-scale finite-domain experiments comparing against spectral methods on accuracy vs runtime.
  - The synthetic 1D Gaussian/Gaussian case is a sanity check, but strongly specialized.
  - The neuropathy dataset is small; while overdispersion separation is shown, comparisons to [7] (accuracy, runtime) and to alternative training (EM/fixed-point) are missing.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and potentially impactful idea; avoids reliance on spectra.
- Extends beyond discrete to continuous DPPs with explicit formulas.
- Provides two inference routes (variational and MCMC) and practical guidance.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of formal tightening/convergence guarantees, which are critical for the retrospective MH correctness.
- Limited empirical evaluation and absence of head-to-head comparisons and scaling studies.
- Clarity issues and some missing practical details (conditioning, gradients for Z/θ, applicability of Ψ beyond Gaussian/Gaussian).

Detailed suggestions for improvement
- Provide a theorem giving sufficient conditions for limm→∞ bounds → true det(I+L), with rates in terms of ||L−Q|| (trace/Frobenius/operator norms), and discuss how optimizing Z impacts these rates.
- Discuss and implement regularization for LZ, and quantify the impact on bounds; include numerical stability tips.
- Add finite-domain experiments with large n (e.g., n≥10^4), varying m, reporting wall-clock, memory, and bound gaps; compare to spectral MH with power iterations (when feasible) and to fixed-point/EM methods.
- In the continuous case, include at least one example where Ψ is not closed-form, showing quadrature/MC approximations and sensitivity.
- For the neuropathy case, report held-out log-likelihoods or predictive performance, and compare against [7] under similar budgets.
- Clarify optimization: provide gradients for θ and Z when kernels are differentiable; report convergence behavior and sensitivity to initialization of Z. Discuss the observed “grid-like” Z patterns and whether they can be initialized via sampling from marginal intensities.
- Clean up notation and references; ensure all assumptions (trace-class, (9), etc.) are stated precisely; move key proof elements into the main text.

Decision
Borderline reject. The paper presents a neat and potentially influential idea with solid intuition and preliminary evidence, but falls short of NeurIPS standards in terms of theoretical guarantees for bound tightening (critical for unbiased MH) and comprehensive empirical validation. With strengthened theory and more thorough experiments (including comparisons and scaling), this work would be a strong candidate.
