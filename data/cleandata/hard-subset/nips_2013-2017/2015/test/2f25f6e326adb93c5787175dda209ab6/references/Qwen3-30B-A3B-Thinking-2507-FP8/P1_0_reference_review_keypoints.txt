- Summary: Proposes nonspectral bounds on DPP likelihood using inducing points to enable likelihood-based inference without spectral decomposition, validated on synthetic and real datasets including continuous domains.

- Strength — Technical Quality: Provides rigorous, nontrivial theoretical derivations and proofs (Propositions 1 and 2) grounded in operator theory.

- Strength — Practical Significance: Addresses a major computational bottleneck in DPP inference, making likelihood-based methods feasible for larger and continuous problems.

- Strength — Experimental Validation: Thorough experiments compare to exact methods on toy data and demonstrate utility on a diabetic neuropathy dataset.

- Strength — Clarity and Organization: Well-structured exposition with consistent notation and accessible explanations for readers outside operator theory.

- Weakness — Limited Discussion of Limitations: Insufficient guidance on when parametrizing L instead of K is appropriate and how identifiability issues affect performance.

- Weakness — Computational Complexity: Claims O(nm²) complexity but lacks detailed runtime comparisons to spectral or other alternative approaches.

- Weakness — Broader Literature Connection: Could better situate the work within recent scalable inference methods (e.g., scalable GP literature) and other approximate inference advances.

- Originality and Significance: Novel nonspectral likelihood bounds, notably extending to continuous DPPs, substantially broadening applicability where spectral decomposition is intractable.

- Argument for Acceptance — Novelty: First practical nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihood, a significant departure from prior spectral approaches.

- Argument for Acceptance — Practical Impact: Enables DPP inference on datasets and domains previously infeasible due to spectral requirements.

- Argument for Acceptance — Theoretical Soundness: Strong mathematical backing and proofs supporting the proposed bounds.

- Argument for Acceptance — Empirical Support: Solid experimental evidence on synthetic and real-world tasks showing effectiveness.

- Argument for Acceptance — Presentation: Clear writing and organization suitable for NIPS audience.

- Argument Against Acceptance — L parametrization concerns: Need for more concrete analysis of drawbacks when modeling via L instead of K.

- Argument Against Acceptance — Insufficient runtime analysis: Lacks empirical comparisons of computational performance with alternatives.

- Argument Against Acceptance — Accessibility: Some technical depth may challenge readers unfamiliar with operator theory.

- Overall Assessment: High-quality, significant contribution with minor weaknesses in discussion and runtime analysis; recommended for acceptance as it meaningfully advances DPP inference.
