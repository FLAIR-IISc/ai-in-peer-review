This paper addresses the problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition of the kernel, a major computational bottleneck. The authors provide bounds on the DPP likelihood, inspired by variational inference techniques for Gaussian Processes, applicable to both finite and continuous domains. They demonstrate the utility of these bounds within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, and validate their approach through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant practical challenge:** Avoiding spectral decomposition is crucial for scaling DPP inference to larger datasets and more complex kernels. The proposed bounds directly tackle this limitation.
* **Sound theoretical foundation:** The derivation of the bounds builds upon established results in GP approximation and determinant inequalities, providing a solid theoretical basis.
* **Generality:** The results are extended to both discrete and continuous DPPs, increasing the applicability of the work.
* **Clear exposition:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear progression of ideas. The connection to related work is well-established.
* **Experimental validation:** The authors provide experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach, including a comparison with existing methods and an analysis of parameter estimation.
* **Connections to existing methods:** The work appropriately builds upon existing techniques such as variational inference and MCMC, demonstrating a good understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational cost of pseudo-input optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a key contribution, the optimization of the pseudo-inputs (Z) within the variational inference framework adds a computational cost. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t provide a detailed analysis of its scalability. The experimental results suggest it is manageable but could become a bottleneck for very high-dimensional problems.
* **Interpretability of L parametrization:** The paper repeatedly notes the trade-off between tractability and interpretability when directly parametrizing the kernel L. While acknowledging this limitation is good, a deeper discussion of how to mitigate this interpretability issue could strengthen the work.
* **Limited novelty in variational inference:** The application of variational inference with bounds is a relatively standard technique. The core novelty lies in the derivation of the bounds themselves, but the inference procedure itself isn’t particularly groundbreaking.
* **Empirical comparisons:** While the experiments demonstrate that the proposed method works, a more direct comparison with state-of-the-art DPP inference methods that *do* rely on spectral decomposition (perhaps on smaller datasets where that is feasible) would provide stronger evidence of its advantages.
* **Lack of error bounds/guarantees:** The paper presents bounds on the likelihood but doesn't provide any guarantees on the tightness of these bounds or the accuracy of the resulting inference.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The work addresses a crucial bottleneck in DPP inference, making it practical for larger-scale problems.
* The theoretical derivation is sound and well-motivated.
* The experimental results provide compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational cost associated with pseudo-input optimization could limit the scalability of the approach.
* The interpretability issues associated with directly parametrizing the L kernel remain a concern.
* The variational inference framework itself isn’t particularly novel.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of DPP modeling. The proposed bounds significantly reduce the computational complexity of likelihood-based inference, making DPPs more accessible for a wider range of applications. While the computational cost of optimizing the pseudo-inputs and the interpretability trade-offs are valid concerns, the authors acknowledge these limitations and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of their approach. The paper meets the criteria for NIPS acceptance, particularly in terms of quality, clarity, and significance. 

**Recommendation:** Accept.
