This paper addresses the problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition of the kernel, a computationally expensive operation. The authors provide bounds on the DPP likelihood, inspired by variational inference techniques for Gaussian Processes, extending them to both finite and continuous domains. They demonstrate the use of these bounds within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, and validate their approach through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the bounds on the likelihood appears mathematically sound, drawing inspiration from established results in Gaussian process literature. The extension to continuous DPPs is a valuable contribution.
*   **Novelty:**  The proposed approach of avoiding spectral decomposition for DPP inference is novel and addresses a significant computational bottleneck in existing methods. The use of inducing inputs to tighten the bounds is a clever idea.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the technical details are presented in a relatively accessible manner. The connection to Gaussian process variational inference is well-explained.
*   **Significance:** The development of methods for scalable DPP inference has the potential to broaden the applicability of DPPs to larger and more complex datasets. The presented experiments demonstrate the practical feasibility of the approach.
*   **Completeness:** The paper covers both the finite and continuous cases, making it a more complete contribution. The inclusion of experimental validation further strengthens the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Identifiability:**  The experimental results highlight an issue with parameter identifiability, particularly concerning the scale parameter κ in the continuous DPP example. While acknowledged, a deeper discussion of this limitation and potential remedies would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the paper focuses on avoiding spectral decomposition, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed methods (especially the optimization of inducing inputs) compared to alternative approaches would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Connection to Related Work:** Although the related work section is adequate, a more thorough discussion of existing approaches for approximating DPP likelihoods, beyond the cited work in [7], could be valuable. Specifically, exploring links to other quadrature-based methods or alternative approximation techniques would enhance the context.
*   **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, the application to a limited number of datasets might not fully represent the range of scenarios where DPPs are used. Expanding the experimental evaluation to more diverse datasets could enhance the robustness of the findings.
*   **Mathematical Rigor in Continuous Case:** The continuous case relies on assumptions about the kernel and measure (Mercer’s theorem or similar). Explicitly stating these assumptions and their implications for the applicability of the results would increase clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant computational challenge in DPP inference.
*   Proposes a novel and technically sound approach based on established variational inference techniques.
*   Demonstrates practical feasibility through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Offers a valuable contribution to the broader field of probabilistic modeling.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Parameter identifiability issues may limit the practical usefulness of the approach.
*   A more thorough discussion of computational complexity and comparison to existing methods is needed.
*   The experimental evaluation could be expanded to more diverse datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in the field of DPPs. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and demonstrates promising results. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding parameter identifiability and a more detailed complexity analysis.
