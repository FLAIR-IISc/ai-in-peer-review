Summary
This paper tackles a core bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the need for eigendecompositions of the L-operator (finite) or its spectrum (continuous), which is often analytically unavailable or computationally prohibitive. The authors derive non-spectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) by introducing inducing inputs Z and an approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, yielding bounds of the form det(Q+I)^{-1} and det(Q+I)^{-1} exp(tr(L−Q)). These are analogous in spirit to variational inducing-point methods for sparse Gaussian processes, and do not require eigenpairs. The bounds extend to continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinants and trace-class assumptions, producing computable expressions involving LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x). The authors show how to use the bounds for (i) a variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood that can be optimized jointly over kernel parameters and inducing inputs, and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler with acceptance bounds that are tightened by increasing m and optimizing Z. Experiments include a synthetic 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where an “ideal” MH is available, and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset where the variational method recovers class-separated overdispersion measures.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant problem: removes a major practical obstacle to DPP inference by avoiding spectral decompositions; particularly important for continuous domains and large finite domains.
- Principled bounds: finite- and continuous-domain results are grounded in a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon), with a natural connection to inducing-point GP methods. The construction is simple, general, and extensible.
- Dual use: supports both approximate (variational) and exact (retrospective MH) inference paradigms, with an explicit mechanism (m, Z) to trade compute for tightness.
- Continuous DPPs: the operator-theoretic extension and computable Ψ for Gaussian–Gaussian settings are novel and practically useful.
- Empirical validation: (a) on a synthetic case where the true determinant and spectrum are known, the retrospective MH closely matches an ideal MH; (b) on a real dataset, the variational approach yields coherent parameter trends as m increases and reproduces known class differences.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical tightness/convergence: While it is intuitive that the bounds tighten as m→∞ and Z are optimized, the paper does not provide formal guarantees (e.g., monotone tightening, rates, or conditions ensuring convergence of F(θ,Z) to the true log-likelihood). The brief remark linking tightness to GP posterior variance decay is suggestive but not a proof.
- Limited empirical breadth and baselines: There is no quantitative comparison to spectral methods (e.g., Affandi et al.) on finite domains, no scaling study in n for the finite case, and no ablations on Z (e.g., fixed grids vs. optimized vs. Nyström with Z⊂Y). For the real dataset, performance is demonstrated via overdispersion separation, but predictive metrics or likelihood comparisons are absent.
- Variational practicality: In the synthetic example, variational optimization struggles (strong parameter non-identifiability for κ). While this is an honest observation, it leaves open when the variational route is reliable and how to detect failure beyond multiple restarts.
- Assumptions and computability in the continuous case: The Ψ integral is tractable for Gaussian–Gaussian, but it’s unclear how broadly this extends. More discussion on conditions guaranteeing (i) trace-class L, (ii) computability or approximation of Ψ in common kernels/measures, and (iii) numerical stability would strengthen applicability claims.
- Computational costs: Retrospective MH requires optimizing Z at each proposal to refine bounds; the cost and scaling of this inner optimization are not profiled. The largest m in the synthetic example is modest; scalability to high-dimensional continuous domains or large n in finite settings remains to be shown.
- Presentation: Some typos, equation numbering mismatches, and incomplete references (missing years) detract from clarity. A brief proof sketch in the main text (beyond citing [14]) would help readers assess the determinant bounds.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The finite and continuous bounds rest on a known determinant inequality and standard operator assumptions; the constructions are plausible and consistent with GP inducing-point ideas. Empirical evidence supports correctness. Missing are formal tightness/convergence guarantees and broader empirical validation.
- Completeness: The methods are fully specified for use in VI and MH; experiments are illustrative but not exhaustive.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, with helpful connections to GP literature. However, readability would benefit from polishing notation, fixing references, providing a brief proof sketch, and clarifying when Ψ is computable.

Originality
- New nonspectral determinant bounds tailored to DPP likelihoods and their extension to continuous domains appear original. The link to variational inducing-point approximations is insightful. Prior work on spectral bounds is clearly differentiated.

Significance
- High potential impact: enabling DPP inference without eigendecompositions broadens applicability, especially in continuous settings. If adopted, these bounds could become a standard tool, analogous to sparse GP methods.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a monotonicity/tightness result or at least conditions ensuring convergence of bounds as m increases and Z densifies.
- Add finite-domain experiments with larger n and comparisons to spectral-bounds baselines, including time/accuracy trade-offs.
- Include ablations on Z (selection vs. optimization) and sensitivity to m; plot bound gaps vs. m.
- Discuss computability/approximation of Ψ beyond Gaussian–Gaussian, and numeric stability.
- Clarify practical guidance: initialization of Z, optimization strategies, diagnostics for VI failure, and stopping criteria for retrospective MH refinement.
- Improve presentation: fix references, equation numbering, and include a proof sketch.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, widely acknowledged bottleneck.
- Clean, general methodology with both VI and MH instantiations.
- Extends to continuous DPPs with concrete computable cases.
- Empirical evidence supports correctness; real-data application is meaningful.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees on tightness/convergence.
- Empirical evaluation lacks breadth, baselines, and scalability analysis.
- Variational method’s robustness is unclear; synthetic example highlights instability.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept
This is a solid, meaningful contribution that is likely to be useful to the community, despite some theoretical and empirical gaps.

Reviewer confidence: High on technical correctness of the approach; moderate on empirical generality.
