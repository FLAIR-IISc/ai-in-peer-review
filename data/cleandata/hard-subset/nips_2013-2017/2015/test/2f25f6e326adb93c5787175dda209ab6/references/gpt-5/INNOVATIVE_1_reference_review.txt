Summary
This paper tackles a central bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): dependence on spectral decompositions of the L operator (matrix in the finite case; compact operator and Fredholm determinant in the continuous case). The main idea is to derive nonspectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) using an inducing-point construction inspired by sparse GP approximations (Titsias, 2009) and a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975). For finite domains, the bounds yield a tractable variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood and enable retrospective Metropolis–Hastings with adaptively tightened acceptance intervals. The approach is extended to continuous DPPs via bounds on the Fredholm determinant in terms of LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x), making it applicable when Ψ is computable (e.g., Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure). Experiments include a 1D synthetic setting where the spectrum is known (validating MCMC) and a real spatial dataset (diabetic neuropathy), showing convergence of the variational bounds and coherent separation of classes via overdispersion metrics.

Relation to prior work
- Affandi et al. (ICML’14) proposed spectral bounds requiring leading eigenpairs; this paper avoids any eigendecomposition and works in continuous spaces.
- Nyström approximations for DPPs (AISTATS’13) and k-DPP learning (Gillenwater et al., Mariet & Sra) target different parts of the pipeline; here the focus is bounding det(I+L) for general L-ensembles.
- The inducing-point idea is borrowed from sparse GP VI (Titsias, 2009), but its application to DPP likelihood determinants and to Fredholm determinants appears novel.
- Numerical Fredholm determinant evaluation (Bornemann, 2010) is discussed as complementary.

Strengths
- Novelty and boldness: a clean, general, nonspectral route to DPP inference, including continuous domains, is a meaningful departure from eigen-based methods.
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: the same inducing-point construction yields bounds usable for both VI and MCMC, with clear computational trade-offs (O(n m^2)).
- Practical significance: removes the major barrier to continuous DPP inference; retrospective MH using bound refinement is elegant and broadly useful.
- Empirical signals: in the 1D synthetic case, retrospective MH matches “ideal” MH; on the neuropathy data, variational estimates stabilize with increasing m and recover known class differences.
- Optimizing inducing inputs Z is a valuable degree of freedom; the observed spatial structure of Z is insightful.

Weaknesses
- Theory:
  - No formal guarantee on tightness/rates of convergence of the bounds as m→∞ or under what conditions optimizing Z ensures monotone tightening.
  - The continuous-case assumptions are stated but practical guidance is limited; clarity on when Ψ is tractable beyond the Gaussian–Gaussian case would help.
- Empirics:
  - Limited breadth. Missing comparisons on large finite problems (big n) where spectral methods are commonly used; no end-to-end timing/accuracy curves vs Affandi et al. or Nyström baselines.
  - No ablation on Z initialization, gradient-based optimization details, or numerical stability (e.g., conditioning of LZ and need for jitter).
  - The variational approach appears brittle in the synthetic example; more discussion on why (identifiability of κ, optimization pathologies) and mitigation strategies would be valuable.
- Scope and usability:
  - Computing Ψ can be nontrivial; guidelines or recipes for common kernels/measures (e.g., periodic SE, Matérn, mixture base measures) are missing.
  - Little discussion on stochastic/mini-batch strategies across multiple realizations, which would be important for scalability.

Quality
The core claims (determinant bounds; applicability to VI and MH; extension to continuous DPPs) are supported by sound references and appear technically correct; proofs are in the supplement. Some important practical details are deferred (stability, gradients, complexity constants), and theoretical guarantees on bound tightness are qualitative rather than quantitative.

Clarity
Generally clear, with good motivation and context. There are typos and some notation slips; Section 4 could better signpost assumptions and computability of Ψ. Algorithmic details for optimizing Z and θ could be elaborated (initialization, regularization, adding jitter to LZ). Figures are informative.

Originality
High. The nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods (finite and continuous) via inducing points and their deployment for both VI and retrospective MH represent a novel and promising direction beyond eigen-based methods.

Significance
Potentially high. If broadly applicable, this removes a key barrier for DPP inference in continuous spaces and large finite problems. Even if some pieces (e.g., Ψ) are restricted, the approach should stimulate follow-on work, including alternative kernels/measures, tighter bounds, and scalable training.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide any monotonicity/tightness guarantees as m increases and Z is optimized? Any empirical plots of bound gap vs m?
- How do you initialize and regularize Z and LZ (e.g., jitter) in practice? Are gradients through Z used, and do they help vs black-box optimizers?
- Beyond Gaussian–Gaussian, what other kernel/measure pairs yield closed-form or tractable Ψ? Any guidance on numerical quadrature?
- Can you include a large finite-n experiment comparing runtime/accuracy vs spectral-bound methods and Nyström approximations?
- Is the approach directly extensible to k-DPPs (fixed-size) and to multiple independent realizations with stochastic optimization?

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a significant, novel idea that removes reliance on spectral decompositions for DPP inference, including continuous domains.
- Provides practical algorithms (VI and MH) with clear computational benefits.
- Conceptually neat connection to sparse GPs; likely to inspire follow-up research.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees on bound tightness are limited.
- Empirical validation is not yet comprehensive and lacks strong baselines on large finite problems.
- Practical scope currently hinges on availability of Ψ.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some incompleteness in theory and evaluation, the paper advances an important new direction for DPP inference with broad potential impact. The novelty and conceptual clarity outweigh the current experimental limitations.
