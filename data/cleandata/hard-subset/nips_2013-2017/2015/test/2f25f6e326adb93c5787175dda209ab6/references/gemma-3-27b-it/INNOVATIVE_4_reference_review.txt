This paper tackles a significant practical bottleneck in applying Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): avoiding spectral decomposition of the kernel. The core idea – bounding the likelihood without eigenvalue computation, drawing inspiration from variational Gaussian process literature – is both clever and potentially impactful. The authors successfully extend these bounds to both finite and continuous DPPs, and demonstrate their application within both variational inference and MCMC frameworks. The connection to sparse GP approximations is well-articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** This is a genuinely novel approach. Likelihood-based inference for DPPs is notoriously difficult due to spectral decomposition. Bypassing this requirement opens up applications to much larger datasets and more complex kernels.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundations, particularly Proposition 1 and 2, appear solid, relying on established determinant inequalities. The extensions to continuous DPPs are handled carefully.
* **Broad Applicability:** The bounds are generally applicable to any kernel, offering flexibility in model specification.
* **Demonstrated Implementation:** The authors provide experimental validation, showcasing the effectiveness of their approach in both a synthetic Gaussian case and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset. The retrospective MCMC implementation, while complex, is a particularly strong result.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the complex mathematics accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Interpretability Issues (Acknowledged):** The authors themselves acknowledge the loss of interpretability when directly parameterizing L rather than K. While tractable, the parameters become less intuitive.
* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-Input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, the MCMC and variational approaches require optimizing over the pseudo-inputs Z. This adds a computational cost, although the authors demonstrate this is manageable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental validation, while encouraging, could be more extensive. Specifically, comparing performance (e.g., log-likelihood, predictive accuracy) against existing DPP inference methods (even those limited by spectral decomposition) would strengthen the claims.
* **Convergence in Variational Inference:** The results in Section 5.1 suggest convergence issues with the variational inference, necessitating MCMC for reliable results. A deeper investigation into why the variational approach struggles would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a critical limitation of DPP inference, opening up new avenues for application.
* The proposed bounds are theoretically sound and generally applicable.
* The experimental results demonstrate the feasibility and potential of the approach.
* The novelty of bypassing spectral decomposition significantly outweighs the minor methodological shortcomings.
* The authors acknowledge and discuss the limitations of their work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The loss of parameter interpretability is a drawback.
* The computational cost of pseudo-input optimization is a concern, especially for very large datasets.
* The experimental validation is somewhat limited.
* The variational method’s convergence issues raise concerns about its practical utility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs. While not without minor flaws, the novelty and potential impact of the work are substantial. The authors have successfully navigated a challenging theoretical problem and demonstrated a practical solution. I believe the benefits of bypassing spectral decomposition far outweigh the drawbacks. I lean towards acceptance. The paper represents a significant step forward in making DPPs more accessible for real-world applications, and I anticipate it will stimulate further research in this area.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I would encourage the authors to address the comments regarding interpretability and convergence in a future version, and to expand the experimental validation with more comprehensive comparisons to existing methods.
