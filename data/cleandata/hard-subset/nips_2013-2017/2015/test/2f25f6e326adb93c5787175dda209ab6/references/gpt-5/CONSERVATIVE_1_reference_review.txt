Summary
This paper proposes nonspectral upper/lower bounds on the normalizing determinant det(I+L) that appears in DPP likelihoods (finite and continuous cases), thereby avoiding eigendecompositions. The construction uses an inducing-point approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY (analogous to sparse GP Nyström/variational approximations) and a determinant inequality (Seiler–Simon). For finite DPPs the authors derive det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I); an analogous bound is given for Fredholm determinants in the continuous setting via det(LZ)/det(LZ+Ψ) with an exponential correction term. They then (i) maximize a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood that replaces log det(I+L) with the bound (jointly over kernel parameters and inducing inputs), and (ii) use the bounds in a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme, increasing m and re-optimizing inducing inputs until the acceptance decision can be made. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where the spectrum is known (showing that VI struggles to identify κ while MH recovers α and length-scale) and a spatial neuropathy dataset, where they fit separate DPPs for two classes and show convergence of estimated overdispersion measures as m increases.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical bottleneck in DPP inference: dependence on spectral decompositions (costly in finite domains, intractable in many continuous cases).
- The determinant bounds are simple to evaluate, require only kernel evaluations and m×m linear algebra, and connect nicely to established sparse GP methodology.
- The retrospective MH adaptation is conceptually sound if bounds can be tightened arbitrarily; avoids computing any eigenvalues.
- The continuous-case treatment and formulas for Ψ provide a route to working with Fredholm determinants under certain kernel/base-measure pairs.
- The neuropathy experiment demonstrates that a VI approach can produce stable overdispersion estimates as m increases, with reasonable runtimes.

Weaknesses
- Lack of rigorous convergence guarantees. The paper asserts that bounds can be “arbitrarily refined” by increasing m and optimizing Z, and that upper/lower bounds match as m→∞ “under fairly weak assumptions,” but there is no theorem establishing:
  - convergence of bounds to the true determinant for finite and continuous cases for general kernels/measures,
  - monotonicity/tightening properties in m,
  - almost-sure termination of the retrospective MH acceptance test.
- Limited empirical validation and baselines. The experiments are small-scale and mostly qualitative:
  - No comparisons in runtime/accuracy to spectral methods (e.g., power iterations) on finite domains or to Bornemann-style quadrature for Fredholm determinants.
  - No held-out likelihoods, predictive assessments, or downstream performance comparisons.
  - No large finite-domain DPPs (e.g., document or image summarization) where spectral costs are prohibitive, which is the key purported use case.
- Practical scope of the continuous bound is narrow. Computing Ψ requires closed-form integrals; examples focus on Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure. It is unclear how broadly applicable this is in real applications without bespoke derivations or heavy quadrature.
- Optimization concerns:
  - The VI objective depends on optimized pseudo-inputs; no discussion of nonconvexity, initialization sensitivity, or gradient computation for Z in the continuous case.
  - The toy example shows VI instability/identifiability issues without proposing remedies (priors, constraints, reparametrizations).
- Complexity analysis is incomplete. Claims of O(n m^2) omit scaling with number/size of realizations and the cost of re-optimizing Z repeatedly within MH. Memory and wall-clock comparisons to spectral alternatives are not provided.
- Presentation issues: some references lack years; a few statements are hand-wavy (e.g., convergence of bounds). The notation around likelihood equations and equation numbering is occasionally inconsistent.

Quality
The core technical claims (bounds via Seiler–Simon and Nyström-like Q) are plausible and align with known matrix inequalities and GP approximations. However, the absence of formal convergence results and proofs of properties needed for exactness of the MH scheme undermines the theoretical completeness. Empirically, the experiments are not sufficient to demonstrate superiority or robustness across settings where spectral methods are infeasible. Overall soundness is moderate but incomplete.

Clarity
Generally readable and well-structured, with helpful connections to GP literature. However, the paper should tighten notation (finite vs. continuous cases), provide more detail on computing Ψ and gradients, and add missing references/years. Claims about convergence should be either proven or clearly labeled as conjectures/empirical observations.

Originality
The idea to bound DPP determinants via inducing-point approximations and Seiler–Simon is, to my knowledge, novel in the DPP context. The extension to continuous Fredholm determinants is also interesting. That said, the components (Nyström/variational GP ideas, determinant inequalities, retrospective MH) are established; the contribution is primarily a combination/adaptation to DPP inference.

Significance
If robust and broadly applicable, nonspectral DPP inference would be valuable, especially in continuous domains and large finite sets. However, the current work falls short of demonstrating practical impact: limited datasets, no strong comparisons, and no guarantees for the MH acceptance mechanism. As it stands, the significance is promising but unproven.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a theorem with conditions under which the upper and lower bounds converge to the true det(I+L) (finite and continuous cases), and quantify the rate in m and Z coverage?
- Do the bounds tighten monotonically with m (for optimized Z)? Any guarantee that retrospective MH terminates almost surely?
- How general is the ability to compute Ψ in the continuous case? Can you cover non-Gaussian base measures or kernels via quadrature with error control?
- Please include quantitative comparisons to spectral methods (finite DPPs) and to quadrature-based Fredholm determinant evaluation (Bornemann 2010) in the continuous case, with wall-clock and accuracy.
- Can you report held-out log-likelihoods or predictive scores to assess model quality?
- What is the sensitivity to Z initialization and the choice of m? Any adaptive scheme to place/augment inducing points?

Pros
- Avoids eigencomputations; simple kernel-based bounds.
- Bridges sparse GP techniques with DPP inference.
- Potentially enables continuous DPP inference where spectra are unknown.

Cons
- No formal guarantees that are crucial for correctness of the MH scheme.
- Empirical evaluation is thin and lacks strong baselines.
- Applicability of continuous-case bounds seems narrow.
- Optimization over inducing inputs may be fragile; no systematic study.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and has potential, but the paper needs stronger theoretical guarantees (especially for retrospective MH), broader and more convincing empirical validation against established baselines, and clearer discussion of scope and limitations before it can be recommended for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to strengthen the theory, add quantitative comparisons, and demonstrate scalability and robustness on larger, representative datasets.
