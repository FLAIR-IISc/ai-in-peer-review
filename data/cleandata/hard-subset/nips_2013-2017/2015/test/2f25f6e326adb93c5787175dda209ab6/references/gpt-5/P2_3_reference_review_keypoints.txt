- Proposes non-spectral inducing-point bounds on the DPP normalizer det(I+L) for finite and continuous DPPs, avoiding eigendecompositions.  
- Derives bounds det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I) for finite domains using low-rank surrogate Q.  
- Extends analogous Fredholm-determinant bounds to continuous DPPs with trace-class operators and kernel-based Ψ quantities.  
- Enables a variational lower bound on DPP log-likelihood by replacing −log det(I+L) with −log det(Q+I)+tr(L−Q).  
- Introduces a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that tightens acceptance-probability bounds by increasing m and optimizing inducing points Z.  
- Approach is explicitly inspired by sparse GP variational methods (Titsias, 2009) and contrasts with prior spectral DPP methods (Affandi et al., 2014).  
- Theoretical derivations are plausible and based on known determinant inequalities (Seiler–Simon); proofs are deferred to the supplement.  
- Computational complexity is attractive at O(n m^2) once kernel evaluations are available.  
- Continuous-case operator assumptions (trace-class, self-adjoint) are reasonable; Gaussian–Gaussian example makes bounds computable.  
- Lacks formal characterization of tightness and convergence rates as m→∞ when Z is optimized.  
- Retrospective MH scheme logic is standard and correct, but adaptive optimization of Z raises measurability/adaptivity concerns that merit discussion.  
- 1D Gaussian–Gaussian experiment validates MH and highlights identifiability issues; diabetic neuropathy case shows practical utility of the variational method.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited: no runtime/accuracy comparison to spectral methods, no higher-dimensional continuous examples, limited sensitivity analysis for Z and m.  
- Clarity is generally good with a sensible finite-to-continuous progression, but has minor notational slips and heavy reliance on the supplement.  
- The role of Z (not necessarily a subset of Y) and its relation to Nyström vs inducing variables should be clarified; practical heuristics for choosing Z and m are lacking.  
- Applying inducing-point determinant bounds to DPPs and the continuous Fredholm setting is a novel and useful adaptation of known tools.  
- Removing spectral requirements broadens applicability to continuous domains and large finite problems where eigendecompositions are impractical.  
- Pros: simple, general non-spectral bounds; clear variational objective; practical retrospective MH; strong GP literature connections; favorable computational profile.  
- Cons: limited theoretical analysis of bound tightness; modest empirical evaluation; heuristic guidance for Z and m; minor clarity/notation issues and reliance on supplement.  
- Suggestions: add spectral method comparisons, provide heuristics for Z/m selection, include MH mixing and wall-clock diagnostics, expand continuous experiments, and clarify MH validity under adaptive Z/m.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — neat, practical, and original contribution with correct theory and illustrative experiments, but needs stronger empirical comparisons and minor clarifications.
