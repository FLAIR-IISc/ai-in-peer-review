

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper proposes bounds on the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs) that do not require spectral decomposition, addressing a key computational bottleneck in DPP inference. The authors derive bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, which can be used in variational inference and MCMC methods. The approach is inspired by sparse approximations for Gaussian processes and appears to offer a practical alternative to spectral-based methods.

## Strengths
The paper identifies a genuine computational bottleneck in DPP inference that has been largely overlooked in the literature. The proposed bounds are theoretically sound and extend prior work on sparse approximations for Gaussian processes to DPPs. The experiments demonstrate the practical utility of the method on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing convergence of the bounds as the number of inducing points increases. The connection to variational inference and MCMC is well-motivated and well-executed.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Errors and Inconsistencies
1. **Critical definition error**: The paper defines a DPP as P(A ⊂ Y) = det(K_A) (Equation 1), which is incorrect. For a DPP, the probability of observing a specific subset A is P(Y = A) = det(K_A), not P(A ⊂ Y). This is a fundamental error that undermines the entire likelihood formulation.

2. **Inequality direction error**: In Proposition 1, the stated inequality det(Q + I)e^{-tr(L-Q)} ≤ 1/det(L + I) is likely reversed. Typically, for such bounds, we would expect 1/det(L + I) ≤ 1/det(Q + I) and possibly det(Q + I) ≤ det(L + I), but the left-hand side inequality appears incorrect as written.

3. **Missing reference**: The paper cites [14] for "a nontrivial inequality on determinants" but fails to provide the specific inequality or reference, making it impossible to verify the proof.

4. **Incomplete notation**: The paper states "the posterior marginals of α and are centered around the values" but omits the symbol for the second parameter, creating confusion about which parameter is being discussed.

5. **Incorrect kernel definition**: In Section 4.1, the paper states "L defines a self-adjoint operator on L2(µ) through L(f) = ∫ L(x,y)f(y)dµ(y)" which is incorrect. The correct definition should be (L(f))(x) = ∫ L(x,y)f(y)dµ(y).

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
1. **Missing figures**: The paper refers to "Figures 3(a) and 3(b)" in Section 5.2 but does not include these figures, which is a major issue for evaluating the experimental results.

2. **Incomplete references**: The reference for "An Introduction to the Theory of Point Processes" is missing the publication year, and [20] is cited as "We follow the parametrization of [20] for ease of reference" without providing the actual reference.

3. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for the kernel (sometimes L, sometimes K) without always clarifying the relationship between them, which creates confusion.

4. **Missing equation numbers**: The paper refers to "bounds in (13)" in Section 3.2 but does not have an equation labeled (13) in the text.

### Experimental Issues
1. **Lack of quantitative evidence**: The paper claims "κ varies over several orders of magnitude from one run to the other" in Section 5.1 but provides no numerical evidence or statistics to support this claim.

2. **Unverified claims**: The paper states "the Fredholm determinant det(I + L) in this special case is a q-Pochhammer symbol" without providing the exact relationship or reference, which is critical for reproducibility.

3. **Incomplete experimental details**: The paper mentions "SymPy library in Python" but fails to specify the version or exact implementation details, making it difficult to reproduce the experiments.

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses a genuine computational challenge in DPP inference and proposes a theoretically sound approach. However, the technical errors in the definition of DPPs, the inequality direction in Proposition 1, and the incomplete presentation (missing figures, inconsistent notation, and incomplete references) significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The experimental section contains unsubstantiated claims without supporting evidence.

While the core idea is promising and the paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature, the technical errors are too severe to overlook. The definition error alone is sufficient to question the validity of the entire likelihood formulation. The paper would need substantial revision to correct these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The technical errors in the core mathematical formulation (particularly the definition of the DPP) are too severe to be overlooked, and the paper's credibility is significantly damaged by these issues. While the authors have identified a genuine problem in the field, the paper's execution contains critical flaws that would mislead readers if published as-is. The authors should be encouraged to correct these fundamental errors before resubmitting.

The paper's main contributions are potentially valuable, but the technical errors make it impossible to assess the validity of the proposed bounds and their implications. The field would benefit from this work, but it requires complete revision to address the fundamental mathematical errors before it can be considered for publication.
