- Main contribution: nonspectral, inducing-point-based bounds on DPP likelihoods that avoid eigendecompositions, for both finite and continuous domains.  
- Finite-case result: computable bounds det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I), enabling O(n m^2) variational training and retrospective MH.  
- Continuous-case result: operator-level bounds using Ψ = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x) that give analogous nonspectral Fredholm-determinant bounds.  
- Method builds on a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975) and parallels sparse GP variational constructions (Titsias, 2009).  
- Allows variational inference by maximizing a lower bound over θ and inducing locations Z, and supports adaptive retrospective MH by refining m.  
- Experiments: 1D Gaussian–Gaussian validation showing MH matches spectral MH; 2D real-data (diabetic neuropathy) where variational method recovers discriminative overdispersion measures.  
- Strength — novelty: first nonspectral inducing-point bounds for DPP normalizers applicable to continuous DPPs.  
- Strength — practicality: avoids eigen-solves, O(n m^2) cost, compatible with VI and MH.  
- Strength — continuous theory: explicit computable terms for common kernels/base measures, filling a gap where spectral decompositions are rare.  
- Strength — empirical validation: MH agreement in 1D and sensible real-data results with bounds tightening as m increases.  
- Weakness — limited experiments: no large-scale finite-domain benchmarks or direct runtime/accuracy comparisons to spectral/truncated methods.  
- Weakness — theoretical gaps: no theorem on monotonicity in m or conditions/rates for asymptotic exactness; only heuristic GP-variance analogy.  
- Weakness — optimization details sparse: gradients w.r.t. Z and θ not derived; reliance on CMA-ES may hinder scalability/reproducibility versus gradient-based AD.  
- Weakness — identifiability: κ parameter weakly identified; lacks systematic analysis or recommended parameterizations/priors.  
- Weakness — continuous integrals: Ψ and ∫L(x,x)dµ tractable for Gaussian–Gaussian but guidance for general µ/kernels and numerical approximation effects is limited.  
- Weakness — clarity/edits: occasional typos, missing years in refs, and assumptions (e.g., invertibility/jitter for LZ) could be stated more crisply.  
- Quality: theoretical development appears sound and grounded in established inequalities, but key proofs are relegated to the supplement.  
- MCMC validation: convincing in the 1D case; real-data study is promising but broader benchmarks needed.  
- Clarity suggestions: add computational complexity subsection, explicit MH algorithm box with adaptive m, and derivative expressions for F(θ,Z).  
- Originality: high — novel nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods and extension to continuous domains.  
- Significance: potentially high — could enable DPP learning in continuous and large discrete settings, but impact depends on larger-scale comparisons.  
- Actionable questions: prove monotonicity/convergence in m or provide conditions/rates; provide gradients and stability tips (jitter); discuss numerical approximation of Ψ and its effect on bounds/MH.  
- Experimental requests: include large finite-domain experiments (n in tens/hundreds of thousands) comparing to spectral baselines and report sensitivity to Z initialization.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel, general, practical approach with solid theory and compatible inference methods.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited experimental breadth, missing theoretical guarantees on convergence/tightness, and limited optimization/implementation details.  
- Recommendation: weak accept.  
- Reviewer confidence: moderate.
