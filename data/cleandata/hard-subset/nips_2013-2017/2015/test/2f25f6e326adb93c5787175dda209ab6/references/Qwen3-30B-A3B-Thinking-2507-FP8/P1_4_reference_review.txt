

# Peer Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the requirement for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid this expensive computation, enabling more efficient inference methods. The main contributions include:
1. Nonspectral bounds for finite DPPs (Proposition 1) and continuous DPPs (Proposition 2)
2. Application of these bounds to variational inference and MCMC inference
3. Experimental validation on synthetic and real datasets

The work builds upon the connection between DPPs and Gaussian processes, drawing inspiration from sparse GP approximations with inducing variables. It directly addresses a limitation noted in prior work (e.g., [7]), where spectral bounds required expensive power iterations. The paper makes a clear distinction between parametrizing the kernel K (more interpretable) versus L (computationally favorable), acknowledging the interpretability trade-off.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs of Propositions 1 and 2 rely on established determinant inequalities, and the supplementary material provides the necessary details. The experimental validation is comprehensive, including both synthetic data (where ground truth is available) and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the interpretability issues from parametrizing L instead of K, and demonstrates how variational inference can be unreliable while MCMC provides more consistent results.

However, the paper could strengthen its evaluation by providing more detailed computational comparisons with spectral-based methods. While it mentions O(nm²) time complexity for variational inference, a concrete benchmark against spectral methods would better demonstrate the computational advantage.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The connections to Gaussian process literature are clearly explained, making the approach accessible to machine learning researchers. The notation is consistent throughout, and the flow from finite to continuous DPPs is logical. The experimental section effectively demonstrates the method's functionality through clear visualizations (Figures 1-4). The supplementary material is appropriately referenced for proofs, avoiding unnecessary clutter in the main text.

## Originality
This is a strong contribution to the DPP literature. While previous work (e.g., [7]) addressed similar inference challenges, it relied on spectral information, whereas this paper provides the first nonspectral bounds. The extension from finite to continuous DPPs is non-trivial and represents a meaningful contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work in Sections 1 and 2.1, and the references adequately cover related work.

## Significance
The paper addresses a significant practical bottleneck in DPP applications, which have gained traction in diverse fields from news aggregation [3] to spatial statistics [4]. The proposed approach makes likelihood-based inference feasible for larger datasets, potentially enabling new applications. The real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset validation demonstrates practical relevance. While the paper acknowledges the interpretability trade-off from parametrizing L instead of K, the method provides a viable path for practitioners to apply DPPs to larger-scale problems.

## Strengths
- Novel nonspectral bounds that avoid computationally expensive spectral decompositions
- Comprehensive theoretical development with clear connections to GP literature
- Effective experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets
- Practical implementation details for variational inference and MCMC
- Clear acknowledgment of limitations and trade-offs

## Weaknesses
- Limited comparison to alternative inference methods beyond spectral-based approaches
- Insufficient analysis of how the choice of inducing points Z affects bound tightness
- The interpretability issue with parametrizing L instead of K is acknowledged but not resolved
- Computational complexity claims could be strengthened with more concrete benchmarks

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Solves a well-known computational bottleneck in DPP inference
- Provides theoretically sound, practical bounds that work for both finite and continuous cases
- Demonstrates clear utility through real-world application
- Well-written with strong connections to established literature
- Offers a viable path for practitioners to apply DPPs to larger-scale problems

**Con:**
- Does not fully address the interpretability trade-off from parametrizing L instead of K
- Limited comparison to alternative inference methods beyond spectral-based approaches
- Insufficient analysis of how inducing points selection affects bound quality

## Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the DPP inference literature. The proposed nonspectral bounds address a real and important computational challenge, and the paper provides both theoretical foundations and practical implementation details. The experimental validation is convincing, and the connections to Gaussian process literature make the approach accessible to the NIPS community.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a novel, theoretically grounded method with clear practical implications. While there are minor weaknesses in the comparison to alternative methods and the interpretability trade-off, these do not undermine the main contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP inference and provides a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners working with repulsive point processes.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the computational complexity claims and provide more detailed discussion of the inducing points selection strategy. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling.
