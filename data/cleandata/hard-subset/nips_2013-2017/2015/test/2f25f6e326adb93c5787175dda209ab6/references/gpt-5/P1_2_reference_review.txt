Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a central bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): computing det(I+L), which requires spectral decompositions that are often unavailable or prohibitively expensive for large finite domains or continuous settings. The authors propose nonspectral, inducing-point-based bounds on det(I+L) that avoid eigen computations. For finite domains, they define a low-rank surrogate Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY using m pseudo-inputs Z, and prove determinant bounds of the form det(Q+I) exp(-tr(L−Q)) ≤ det(L+I) ≤ det(Q+I). For continuous DPPs (with Fredholm determinant det(I+L)), they derive analogous bounds involving LZ and Ψ, where Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x). The approach is closely inspired by inducing-point variational approximations in Gaussian processes (e.g., Titsias 2009) and relies on a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975).

They instantiate these bounds in two inference paradigms: (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood that can be optimized jointly over kernel parameters and Z in O(n m^2) per evaluation for finite domains; and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) sampler that uses bracketing bounds on log acceptance probabilities, refining Z (size and location) on-the-fly until a decision is possible, avoiding eigen computations as in Affandi et al. (ICML 2014) but without computing leading eigenpairs. They extend the construction to continuous DPPs by providing computable bounds when Ψ can be evaluated (e.g., Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures).

Experiments include: (1) a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where the spectrum is known, showing that the retrospective MH with nonspectral bounds matches an “ideal” MH; and (2) a 2D real dataset (diabetic neuropathy), where variational training with increasing m yields coherent overdispersion measures that separate classes, and the bounds tighten with m.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Nonspectral, inducing-point determinant bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs is new and practically impactful, especially for continuous DPPs where spectral decompositions are rarely available.
- Clear link to sparse GP literature: The construction mirrors Titsias-style variational bounds, providing intuition (Q as Nyström-like surrogate) and computational efficiency O(n m^2).
- Two inference modalities: Both variational and MH with retrospective acceptance are well-motivated and broadly useful; the latter is exact (up to randomized acceptance) given sufficiently tight bounds.
- Continuous case: The extension using operator-theoretic tools and Fredholm determinants is nontrivial and broadens applicability; Gaussian–Gaussian case permits analytic Ψ and validation against exact quantities.
- Practical insights: Pseudo-inputs self-organize into informative configurations; variational results stabilize with m; complexity scales without storing the full L.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Theoretical guarantees on tightness: Aside from intuitive arguments, there is no formal convergence or rate guarantee that bounds tighten as m increases and Z becomes dense, neither in finite nor continuous settings. Given the centrality of Z-optimization, even mild conditions (e.g., monotone tightening with nested Z, or convergence under standard kernel assumptions) would strengthen the contribution.
- Scope of experiments: No large-scale discrete experiment (e.g., summarization or recommender datasets where DPPs are popular) and no runtime/accuracy comparison to spectral-based methods (Affandi et al., power iterations), to fixed-point MLE (Mariet & Sra), or to non-likelihood approaches (e.g., minimum contrast in spatial statistics). This makes it hard to quantify practical gains.
- Variational instability: On the toy example, variational inference is inconsistent across runs due to parameter non-identifiability (κ vs α, σ). While the analysis is honest and the MH remedy is appropriate, the paper would benefit from guidance on when VI is expected to work (e.g., identifiable parameterizations, priors, or reparameterizations) and whether alternative parameterizations (e.g., of K) alleviate issues.
- Computational requirements in continuous case: The bounds hinge on computing Ψ; beyond Gaussian–Gaussian, when Ψ is intractable, are quadrature or Monte Carlo acceptable without degrading correctness of the MH bounds? A brief treatment (accuracy vs. tightness) would be useful.
- Missing related work: There is a broader literature on scalable log-determinant approximations without eigenpairs (stochastic trace estimation with Lanczos/Chebyshev). While those address log det rather than det directly, they are relevant baselines for finite DPPs. A short discussion would better position the contribution.
- Details deferred: Gradients w.r.t. Z and θ (for differentiable kernels), numerical stability (invertibility of LZ), and initialization strategies for Z are only sketched. A brief algorithmic section or appendix pointers would aid reproducibility.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the finite and continuous bounds are plausible and supported by appropriate references. The inference constructions are correct. Proofs are in the supplement. Some claims about asymptotic tightness are informal.
- Empirical validation is convincing for correctness (toy) and indicative for usefulness (neuropathy), but lacks comparative baselines and large-scale tests.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized; motivation and derivations are clear. Some notation and assumptions (e.g., conditions for trace identities and Fredholm determinants) are concise; adding a short assumption box would help. Minor typos and notational slips exist but do not impede understanding.

Originality
- The inducing-point-based nonspectral determinant bounds for DPPs—and especially their continuous extension—represent a novel combination of ideas (Seiler–Simon inequality + GP variational machinery) not previously applied to DPP likelihoods.

Significance
- Potentially high: enabling DPP inference when spectra are unavailable is important in practice. The continuous case is particularly impactful for spatial statistics and machine learning applications. Wider adoption would be aided by stronger empirical comparisons and guidance on choosing m and Z.

Specific suggestions
- Provide theoretical statements on bound convergence as m→∞ under standard kernel assumptions (even qualitative monotonicity would help).
- Include at least one large discrete experiment with n in the tens of thousands, comparing to spectral methods and fixed-point MLE, reporting wall-clock and accuracy.
- Discuss/compare to log-det approximation methods (Hutchinson/Lanczos), clarifying advantages and limits.
- Add implementation details: gradient expressions w.r.t. Z, handling of LZ invertibility, and initialization heuristics (e.g., k-means or sampling from estimated intensity).
- Clarify feasibility of Ψ computation beyond Gaussian–Gaussian; discuss quadrature and its impact on MH correctness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, general nonspectral bounds applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Practical inference methods with demonstrated correctness and clear computational advantages.
- Bridges DPP and GP variational literatures; likely to spur follow-up work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical scope and lack of comparative baselines hinder assessment of practical superiority.
- Missing theoretical guarantees on bound tightness and guidance on Z selection.
- Variational instability in simple cases raises concerns about robustness without additional prescriptions.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution with clear potential impact. Strengthening theoretical guarantees and empirical comparisons would make it an excellent paper. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the clarity and experimental comparison points as much as page limits allow.
