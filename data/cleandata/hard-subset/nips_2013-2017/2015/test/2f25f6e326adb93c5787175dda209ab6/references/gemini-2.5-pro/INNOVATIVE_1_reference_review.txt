### Summary
This paper addresses the key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the calculation of the partition function, which involves the determinant of a matrix (or an operator in the continuous case) of the form `det(L + I)`. This calculation typically requires a full or partial spectral decomposition, which is computationally prohibitive for large-scale problems and often analytically intractable for continuous DPPs.

The authors propose a novel and elegant solution by introducing non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant term. The core idea is cleverly adapted from the literature on sparse/variational Gaussian Processes, using a set of `m` "inducing points" (or pseudo-inputs) to form a low-rank approximation of the kernel matrix `L`. These bounds can be computed much more efficiently (in O(nm^2) time for the finite case) than the exact determinant. The paper shows how these bounds can be seamlessly integrated into two standard inference frameworks: an approximate variational inference scheme by maximizing the lower bound on the log-likelihood, and an asymptotically exact MCMC scheme using retrospective sampling. A significant strength of the work is its unified treatment, deriving analogous bounds for both finite DPPs and DPPs on continuous domains. The efficacy of the approach is demonstrated on a synthetic 1D continuous problem and a real-world 2D spatial statistics dataset.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the bounds relies on a non-trivial but established determinant inequality, and its application to the DPP likelihood appears correct. The proposed VI and MCMC algorithms are standard applications of these bounds. The authors are honest about the limitations of their work, for example by noting the poor performance of VI on their toy example and acknowledging the known interpretability issues with parametrizing the L-kernel directly. The experiments, while not exhaustive, successfully demonstrate the viability of the approach and validate the correctness of the MCMC sampler against a ground-truth case.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and clearly positioning their contribution relative to prior work (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014). Presenting the simpler finite case before tackling the more technical continuous case is an effective pedagogical choice that makes the paper accessible. The connection to sparse GPs, which is the source of the main idea, is explicitly stated and well-explained.

**Originality:** The originality of this work is its primary strength. While DPPs and GPs are both kernel methods, the idea of using inducing point approximations from the GP literature to tackle the DPP partition function is a significant and non-obvious conceptual leap. To my knowledge, this is the first work to propose a non-spectral, inducing-point-based approximation for the DPP likelihood. It represents a genuinely new direction for scalable DPP inference, moving away from the dominant paradigm of spectral approximations (e.g., Nyström methods or power iteration). This is a bold and creative contribution.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. The proposed method effectively replaces an O(n^3) eigendecomposition with computations dominated by O(nm^2) operations, making likelihood-based inference for DPPs tractable for much larger `n`. The extension to continuous domains is particularly powerful, as it provides a practical computational tool for a setting where the exact likelihood (the Fredholm determinant) is almost always intractable. Researchers and practitioners who have been hesitant to use DPPs due to their computational cost may find this work enables new applications. This work significantly advances the state-of-the-art in scalable inference for DPPs.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The experiments demonstrate that the method works, but they lack a direct quantitative comparison against the most relevant baseline, namely the spectral MCMC method of Affandi et al. (2014). A comparison of runtime, number of likelihood evaluations, and posterior quality against that work would have more concretely established the practical advantages of the proposed non-spectral approach.

2.  **Robustness of Variational Inference:** The variational inference approach is reported to yield "poorly consistent results" on the toy problem, forcing a switch to the more robust MCMC method. While VI works well on the real-world dataset, this inconsistency raises questions about the difficulty of the joint optimization landscape of the variational lower bound and its general applicability. The paper would be strengthened by a deeper analysis of this behavior.

3.  **Guidance on Inducing Points:** The number and placement of inducing points `Z` are crucial hyper-parameters. The paper suggests increasing the number `m` until the results stabilize, which is a reasonable heuristic. However, more practical guidance or a more principled method for setting `m` and initializing the locations of `Z` would be beneficial for future users of this method.

### Arguments for acceptance:
*   **High Novelty:** The core contribution—connecting sparse GP methods to DPP inference to create non-spectral likelihood bounds—is highly original and opens up a new research avenue.
*   **Significant Impact:** The work provides a practical and much more scalable method for inference in DPPs, addressing a major long-standing challenge in the field.
*   **Elegant and General:** The proposed framework is elegant and applies to both finite and continuous DPPs, with the latter being a particularly important contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and easy to follow.

### Arguments against acceptance:
*   **Incomplete Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more direct comparisons to state-of-the-art baselines.
*   **VI Instability:** The reported instability of the variational method on one of the tasks is a minor concern regarding its general robustness.

### Recommendation
I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The central idea is highly novel, significant, and represents a bold step forward for scalable inference in DPP models. It is a beautiful example of cross-pollination of ideas between different areas of machine learning. While the experimental validation could be more thorough, the core conceptual contribution is outstanding and more than sufficient for a NIPS paper. The weaknesses are minor and can be readily addressed in future work that will surely build upon this excellent foundation. This is exactly the kind of creative and impactful paper that the NIPS community values.
