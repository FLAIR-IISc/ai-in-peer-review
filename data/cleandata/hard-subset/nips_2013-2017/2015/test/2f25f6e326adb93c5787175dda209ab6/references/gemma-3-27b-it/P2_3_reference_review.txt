## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors propose novel, non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood, inspired by variational inference techniques for Gaussian Processes. These bounds allow for parameter estimation (via variational inference and MCMC) without explicitly computing eigenvalues/eigenvectors, which is crucial for scaling to larger datasets and continuous domains. The paper presents both theoretical results and empirical validation on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant practical limitation:** The spectral decomposition requirement is a major hurdle for applying DPPs to large-scale problems. This work directly tackles this issue with a clever and theoretically grounded approach.
* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The bounds are derived using existing results on determinants and are presented with clear mathematical rigor. The connection to variational inference for GPs provides intuition and justification.
* **Versatility:**  The proposed bounds are applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs, increasing the breadth of potential applications.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments, including a comparison to exact methods on a toy problem and application to a real-world dataset (diabetic neuropathy), demonstrate the practicality and effectiveness of the proposed techniques.  The experiments show convergence to previously known results and highlight the limitations of the parametrization chosen.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the core ideas accessible even to readers less familiar with DPPs.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parametrization limitations:** While the authors acknowledge it, the choice to directly parametrize the kernel *L* instead of *K* leads to interpretability issues (as demonstrated in the Gaussian example where κ is not well-identified). This is a fundamental trade-off made for tractability, but should be prominently discussed.
* **Computational cost of pseudo-input optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a win, the iterative optimization over the pseudo-inputs *Z* in the MCMC and variational inference adds a computational overhead. The authors acknowledge this but a more detailed analysis of the scalability of this optimization would be valuable.
* **Limited novelty of core techniques:** The use of bounds derived from GP variational inference is a clever adaptation, but the underlying variational inference and MCMC techniques themselves are not new. The primary novelty lies in applying these techniques to the DPP context and deriving the specific bounds.  The paper does a reasonable job of framing this as a key contribution within the DPP literature.
* **Missing comparison to alternative approximations:**  The paper focuses on the proposed method, but doesn’t compare it to other approximation strategies for DPP likelihood evaluation, such as Nyström methods or other low-rank approximations.  A comparison would provide valuable context.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The authors carefully acknowledge limitations and potential issues.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are clear.
* **Originality:**  The approach of using non-spectral bounds for DPP inference is novel, particularly within the context of DPPs. The combination of existing techniques (variational inference, MCMC, determinant inequalities) is effectively applied to a new problem.
* **Significance:** The results are important because they address a key bottleneck in applying DPPs to real-world problems. This work has the potential to make DPPs more practical and scalable for a wider range of applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant practical problem in DPP inference.
* Provides a theoretically grounded and well-motivated solution.
* Demonstrates empirical effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world data.
* Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The chosen parametrization limits interpretability.
* The optimization of pseudo-inputs introduces computational cost.
* Limited novelty in the core inference techniques.
* Missing comparison to alternative approximation methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS.  The work addresses a significant challenge in the DPP literature and proposes a practical and theoretically sound solution. While the parametrization choice and computational cost of pseudo-input optimization are valid concerns, they are acknowledged by the authors and do not detract significantly from the overall contribution. The clear writing and thorough experimental evaluation further strengthen the paper’s impact. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is likely to be of interest to a broad audience working on DPPs and related areas.
