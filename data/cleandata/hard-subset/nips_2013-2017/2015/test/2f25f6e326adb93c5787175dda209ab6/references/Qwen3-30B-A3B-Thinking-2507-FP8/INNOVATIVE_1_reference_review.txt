

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper introduces a novel approach to likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) by providing nonspectral bounds that eliminate the need for spectral decomposition. The authors address a significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference: the requirement for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L in the likelihood evaluation (2). While previous work (e.g., [7]) has attempted to address this by using spectral bounds, the authors propose a fundamentally different approach that completely avoids spectral knowledge.

The core contribution is Propositions 1 and 2, which provide tight bounds on the likelihood determinant that depend only on a set of "pseudo-inputs" (inducing points) rather than spectral properties. This is particularly significant because it enables likelihood-based inference for DPPs without requiring expensive eigendecompositions, which have been a major computational barrier. The authors extend these bounds to both finite and continuous domains, demonstrating how they can be incorporated into variational inference (Section 3.1) and retrospective MCMC (Section 3.2).

The paper's novelty is substantial. While there has been considerable work on DPPs (e.g., [3, 4, 7]), this is the first to provide a spectral-free approach to likelihood evaluation. The connection to variational sparse Gaussian processes [11] is clever and well-motivated, but the authors successfully extend this to DPPs without spectral knowledge—a bold and novel direction. The paper's key insight is that the bounds can be tightened by optimizing over the pseudo-inputs, which is crucial for making the bounds useful in practice.

The theoretical contributions are sound, with the proofs relying on nontrivial determinant inequalities [14]. The experimental validation on both a synthetic Gaussian-Gaussian DPP (Section 5.1) and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset (Section 5.2) demonstrates the approach's practical utility. The results show that the retrospective MCMC method converges to the same posterior as the ideal MH method, and the variational approach provides consistent estimates as the number of pseudo-inputs increases. The paper's discussion of how the optimized pseudo-inputs align with the marginal distribution of the DPP is particularly insightful.

The paper's significance lies in making likelihood-based inference for DPPs more accessible. DPPs have gained popularity in applications requiring repulsive point processes (e.g., news summarization [3], search engine diversity [7], forest modeling [4]), but computational barriers have limited their adoption. This work removes a major barrier by enabling inference without spectral decomposition, potentially opening up new applications.

**Strengths**:
- Novel approach to a well-known computational bottleneck in DPP inference
- Clear theoretical contributions with rigorous bounds for both finite and continuous cases
- Practical implementation that works on real-world datasets
- Insightful connection to variational sparse Gaussian processes
- Demonstrates the approach's effectiveness through experiments
- The paper's main idea is conceptually simple yet powerful

**Weaknesses**:
- The paper doesn't fully address why parametrizing L directly (instead of K) is problematic beyond noting it "favors tractability over interpretability" (Section 2.1). The authors acknowledge this as a limitation but don't explore how their method might help with the interpretability issue.
- The experimental section is somewhat limited, with only two datasets and no comparison to alternative methods beyond [7].
- The connection to the broader DPP literature could be strengthened, particularly regarding how this method compares to non-likelihood-based approaches (e.g., [8, 9]).

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and good motivation. The technical details are presented accessibly, and the supplementary material provides the necessary proofs. The paper avoids overclaiming, acknowledging limitations such as the identifiability issues with parametrizing L directly.

This paper represents a significant step forward in DPP inference by introducing a novel, spectral-free approach. While the methodology is not without limitations, the core idea is bold and addresses a real barrier to DPP adoption. The authors' approach is conceptually simple yet powerful, and it enables new applications of DPPs that were previously computationally intractable.

In my view, the paper's novelty and potential impact outweigh the minor experimental limitations. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this work compelling. It introduces a new direction in DPP inference that is both theoretically sound and practically useful. The paper's main contribution—nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods—is sufficiently novel to merit acceptance at NIPS.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- Novel spectral-free approach to DPP inference
- Solves a significant computational bottleneck
- Clear theoretical contributions with practical implementation
- Demonstrated effectiveness on synthetic and real data
- Potential to enable new applications of DPPs

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- Limited experimental comparison to alternative methods
- Some ambiguity about the interpretability issues with L parametrization
- Only two experimental datasets

Overall, this is a strong, novel contribution that advances the state of the art in DPP inference. The paper's main idea is bold and impactful, and it provides a clear path forward for likelihood-based inference of DPPs without spectral knowledge.
