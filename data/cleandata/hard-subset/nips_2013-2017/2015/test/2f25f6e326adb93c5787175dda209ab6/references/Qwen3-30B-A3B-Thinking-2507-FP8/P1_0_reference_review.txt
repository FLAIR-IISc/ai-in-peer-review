

# Peer Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the requirement for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L, which is computationally expensive or analytically intractable for large datasets or continuous domains. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood that bypass the need for spectral knowledge, inspired by variational inference techniques for Gaussian processes. The key contribution is a set of bounds that depend on "pseudo-inputs" (or inducing points) and can be efficiently computed without spectral decomposition. The paper demonstrates these bounds in both finite and continuous settings, applying them to variational inference and MCMC methods. The authors validate their approach on synthetic and real-world diabetic neuropathy datasets, showing that their method enables practical inference where previous approaches fail.

This work relates directly to recent NIPS papers on DPPs, particularly [7] (Affandi et al., ICML 2013) and [3] (Kulesza & Taskar, NIPS 2011), which have primarily relied on spectral decomposition for inference. The authors correctly identify that spectral decomposition is a major computational bottleneck in practical applications of DPPs.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Quality**: The paper presents a theoretically sound contribution with rigorous proofs of the key bounds (Propositions 1 and 2). The derivation of the bounds is nontrivial and properly references the necessary mathematical results. The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the mathematical foundations of DPPs and operator theory.

2. **Practical Significance**: The authors correctly identify a major computational bottleneck in DPP inference that has limited their applicability to larger datasets and continuous domains. Their approach provides a practical solution that enables likelihood-based inference without spectral decomposition, which is a significant step toward broader adoption of DPPs in machine learning applications.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The paper includes thorough experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets. The toy experiment demonstrates the correctness of the method by comparing with exact likelihood-based methods, while the diabetic neuropathy dataset shows practical utility on a real-world problem. The authors carefully analyze the behavior of their method across different approximation settings.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-structured, with clear progression from problem statement to theoretical contribution to experimental validation. The notation is consistent, and the key ideas are explained clearly, even for readers less familiar with operator theory.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: While the paper acknowledges the identifiability issues with parametrizing L directly rather than K (as noted in Section 1), it could provide more concrete guidance on when this parametrization choice is appropriate. The authors note that "the number of points and the repulsiveness of the points in Y do not decouple as nicely" when using L, but they don't quantify this impact on model performance.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The paper states that the variational approach runs in O(nm²) time, but doesn't thoroughly compare this to alternative approaches (e.g., the spectral bounds approach in [7]) in terms of actual runtime performance. The computational advantage would be clearer with more explicit runtime comparisons.

3. **Connection to Broader Literature**: While the paper cites relevant DPP literature, it could better connect to recent advances in approximate inference for other probabilistic models. The relationship to recent work on scalable Gaussian processes (e.g., [11]) is noted, but the paper could more explicitly position its contribution within the broader landscape of scalable probabilistic inference.

## Originality and Significance

The paper makes a novel contribution by providing bounds on DPP likelihood that do not require spectral decomposition, which is a significant departure from existing approaches. This is particularly valuable for continuous domains where spectral decomposition is often analytically intractable. The extension to continuous DPPs (Section 4) is a substantial contribution that broadens the applicability of the method.

The significance of this work is high, as it directly addresses a major practical limitation in DPP inference that has hindered their use in real-world applications. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method enables inference on datasets where previous approaches would fail due to computational constraints, making DPPs more accessible to practitioners.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Novel contribution: Provides the first nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihood, bypassing a major computational bottleneck.
- Practical impact: Enables likelihood-based inference for DPPs on larger datasets and continuous domains.
- Theoretical soundness: Rigorous proofs of key bounds supported by mathematical literature.
- Experimental validation: Thorough evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- Clear writing: Well-organized and accessible to the NIPS audience.

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Limited discussion of practical limitations when using L instead of K for parametrization.
- Insufficient computational complexity analysis compared to alternative approaches.
- Some technical depth required for readers unfamiliar with operator theory (though this is expected for the topic).

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental practical limitation in DPP inference. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling. While there are minor weaknesses in the discussion of limitations and computational complexity, these do not detract from the paper's overall strength and significance.

The work is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental challenge in probabilistic modeling with practical implications. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP inference by providing a method that is both theoretically sound and practically applicable to larger datasets and continuous domains.

I recommend acceptance of this paper as a strong contribution to NIPS. The authors have made a clear and valuable contribution that will be of interest to the broader machine learning community working with point processes and probabilistic models. The method provides a practical solution to a significant bottleneck in DPP inference that has limited their applicability in real-world scenarios.
