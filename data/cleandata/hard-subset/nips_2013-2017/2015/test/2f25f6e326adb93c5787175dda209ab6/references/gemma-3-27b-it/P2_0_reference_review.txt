## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors propose bounds on the DPP likelihood that circumvent this requirement, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques in Gaussian Processes. They demonstrate the applicability of these bounds to both finite and continuous DPPs, showcasing their use within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks.  Experimental validation on both a synthetic dataset and a real-world application (diabetic neuropathy) demonstrates the efficacy of their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant practical problem:** The spectral decomposition is a major limiting factor for applying DPPs to large-scale problems. This work offers a concrete and potentially widely applicable solution.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The bounds derived in Propositions 1 and 2 are theoretically grounded, leveraging established results from determinant inequalities.
* **Broad Applicability:** The method applies to both finite and continuous DPPs, broadening its potential impact.
* **Practical Demonstrations:**  The experiments, particularly the diabetic neuropathy example, show the method works in a real-world scenario and are comparable to results reported using spectral methods. The retrospective MCMC implementation is particularly well-explained.
* **Clear Connection to Gaussian Process literature:**  The authors draw clear parallels to variational inference in GPs, providing intuition and facilitating understanding for researchers familiar with that field.
* **Acknowledges Limitations:** The authors openly acknowledge the interpretability issues associated with directly parameterizing the L kernel.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Proofs:** While the authors state the proofs are in the supplementary material, the core inequality (Theorem 1 in the cited paper [14]) appears complex and might require significant effort to fully grasp without specific expertise. A more intuitive explanation of the core inequality would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Identifiability:** The experiments highlight that directly parametrizing the L kernel can lead to identifiability issues (e.g., κ in the Gaussian experiment). While acknowledged, this remains a significant drawback and potentially limits the practical usefulness of the method. Further investigation of strategies to address this (e.g., alternative parameterizations, informative priors) would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-Input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a win, the optimization of pseudo-inputs (Z) within the MCMC and variational inference frameworks introduces a new computational cost. The paper doesn’t deeply analyze the tradeoff between this cost and the savings from avoiding spectral decomposition – a more comprehensive discussion of computational complexity would be helpful.
* **Limited Novelty in Variational Inference:** The use of variational inference with bounds is a relatively standard technique, and the specific application to DPPs feels incremental rather than transformative. The real contribution lies in the derived bounds themselves.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations appear correct, and the experiments support the claims. The work is complete in the sense that it provides a practical methodology, but it feels like a stepping stone for more sophisticated future work.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations are clear, and the connections between different concepts are well-established. However, as noted above, a more intuitive explanation of the determinant inequality would be beneficial.
* **Originality:** The core idea of using bounds to avoid spectral decomposition is novel and represents a significant contribution. While the surrounding methods (variational inference, MCMC) are not new, their application in this context is original.
* **Significance:** The results are important.  Removing the spectral decomposition bottleneck could significantly broaden the applicability of DPPs. The work is likely to be used by other researchers and practitioners in the field.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a major practical limitation of DPPs.
* Provides a sound theoretical foundation and practical implementation.
* Demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real datasets.
* Has the potential to significantly impact the adoption of DPPs in various applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Parameter identifiability issues may limit practical use.
* Optimization of pseudo-inputs introduces a new computational cost.
* The contribution of the variational inference part is incremental.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**.  The authors address a crucial practical challenge in the application of DPPs, providing a theoretically sound and experimentally validated solution. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding parameter identifiability and computational cost, the significance of the contribution outweighs these concerns. The paper is well-written, clear, and makes a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
