Summary
The paper proposes nonspectral, inducing-point-based bounds for the normalizing determinants that appear in the likelihoods of determinantal point processes (DPPs), both for finite domains (matrices) and continuous domains (integral operators). The central idea is to introduce a set of “pseudo-/inducing inputs” Z and define a low-rank surrogate Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY (in the finite case) that yields two-sided bounds on 1/det(I+L) via a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975). Analogous operator bounds are derived for continuous DPPs using Fredholm determinants, under trace-class assumptions and with computable cross-covariance terms Ψ. These bounds avoid eigendecompositions, which is valuable when spectra are unavailable or large/numerically expensive.

The authors plug the bounds into two inference schemes: (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood (summing over realizations) that can be optimized jointly in kernel parameters θ and inducing inputs Z; and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler that uses increasingly tight bounds to make accept/reject decisions without ever evaluating eigendecompositions. The approach is conceptually akin to variational sparse GP methods (Titsias, 2009), with Nystrom-like structure but with Z not restricted to the observed items.

Experiments include: (a) a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where spectra and Fredholm determinants are available in closed form, allowing an “ideal” MH baseline. The proposed retrospective MH matches the ideal posterior on identifiable parameters; the variational method shows instability, highlighting identifiability issues when parametrizing L. (b) A real 2D point pattern dataset (diabetic neuropathy), where variational training with increasing m shows convergence of upper/lower bounds and of overdispersion measures that separate classes, with reasonable runtimes, and inducing inputs forming structured grids.

Relation to prior work: The paper directly addresses a key bottleneck highlighted in spectral approaches to DPP learning (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’14; Nyström approximations, AISTATS’13), by avoiding eigen computations entirely. The methodology borrows from and extends sparse GP variational bounds to the DPP setting and from matrices to operators. The continuous-case treatment connects to numerical Fredholm determinant work (Bornemann, 2010).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The finite- and infinite-dimensional determinant bounds hinge on a known inequality; the construction of Q mirrors rigorous GP variational constructions. The resulting variational lower bound and retrospective MH are standard and correctly adapted. Assumptions for the operator case (trace-class, computable Ψ) are stated and reasonable for Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures.
- Empirical support: The synthetic continuous experiment is well-chosen to validate correctness against ground truth. The real dataset demonstrates practical optimization and convergence behavior. However, comparisons to spectral baselines (e.g., power iteration-based MH or EM/fixed-point learning) are missing, and there is no large-scale finite (discrete) experiment where the approach should particularly shine.
- Limitations acknowledged: The authors discuss identifiability when parametrizing L and stress the importance of Z-optimization; they also note the need for computable Ψ in the continuous case.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized, with good background and motivation. The connection to sparse GP ideas is helpful. Some equation numbering appears inconsistent in the provided draft (e.g., references to (7)/(13)), and a few sentences are dense. It would help to:
  - State computational complexities more systematically (per evaluation and per iteration; dependence on T, n, m).
  - Explicitly provide gradients for θ and Z (or direct readers to the supplement) and discuss initialization of Z.
  - Clarify when bounds tighten as m increases and whether monotonicity holds for nested Z.

Originality
- Novelty lies in providing two-sided, nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods using inducing inputs, extending finite to continuous settings, and operationalizing them for VI and retrospective MH. While inspired by sparse GP methodology, the application to DPP determinants and the continuous Fredholm determinant bounds are, to my knowledge, new and useful. Related work is cited appropriately.

Significance
- The work addresses a real blocker in DPP parameter learning: dependence on spectra. The proposed bounds are simple, flexible, and broadly applicable in both discrete and continuous domains. This can enable practitioners to fit DPPs where spectral methods are impractical, and gives a complementary tool to Nyström/spectral approximation pipelines. The ability to run exact MH (in the limit) without spectra is particularly compelling. Wider significance would be strengthened by more extensive empirical evidence (scaling to large n, comparisons to existing methods, classification or predictive tasks).

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, general-purpose bounds that avoid eigendecomposition.
- Extension to continuous DPPs with Fredholm determinants, under clear assumptions.
- Practical inference algorithms: VI and retrospective MH using the same bounds.
- Synthetic validation against exact spectral computations; real-data demonstration; encouraging runtimes.
- Clear connection to and reuse of ideas from variational sparse GPs.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirics are limited:
  - No discrete/finite large-scale experiment where spectral costs are prohibitive.
  - No direct comparisons to spectral MH (Affandi et al., 2014), EM/fixed-point methods (Gillenwater et al.; Mariet & Sra), or to Nyström DPP approximations on the same tasks.
  - No analysis of mixing/acceptance rates and computational budgets for retrospective MH (vs. spectral).
- Tightness: No theoretical characterization of bound tightness vs m or Z placement; no monotonicity guarantees with nested Z; practical heuristics for Z initialization and optimization could be elaborated.
- Continuous case practicality relies on closed-form Ψ; guidance for non-Gaussian settings (e.g., quadrature, randomized features) would broaden applicability.
- Parameterization: L-parametrization’s interpretability/identifiability issues are observed but not mitigated; consider regularization or partially decoupled parametrizations to improve identifiability.
- Reproducibility: Code availability is not mentioned.

Questions for authors
1. Can you provide empirical comparisons to spectral MH/EM on at least one dataset, including wall-clock time and accuracy?
2. Do the bounds become tighter monotonically as m increases for nested Z? If not, are there practical heuristics to ensure monotonic tightening?
3. How sensitive are results to Z initialization? Did you try k-means or sampling from estimated marginals?
4. For continuous non-Gaussian settings, can Ψ be approximated efficiently (e.g., quadrature, random features)? Any guidance on error control?
5. Can you report gradient expressions and whether end-to-end auto-diff was used?

Minor comments
- Fix equation numbering and a few typographical glitches.
- State complexities for computing Q and the bounds, and memory costs.
- Consider adding a finite-domain experiment with n in the tens of thousands.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important bottleneck in DPP learning with a general, elegant idea.
- Solid theory bridging finite and continuous settings.
- Practical algorithms (VI and MH) and validation on synthetic and real data.
- Likely to be adopted and built upon by the DPP/point process community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical scope and lack of head-to-head comparisons with strong baselines.
- No theoretical analysis of bound tightness or convergence with m.
- Practical guidance (Z selection, non-Gaussian continuous cases) could be strengthened.

Overall: This is a technically sound and useful contribution with clear novelty and potential impact. I recommend acceptance provided the authors add at least one substantive empirical comparison to spectral methods and clarify computational aspects and Z strategies.
