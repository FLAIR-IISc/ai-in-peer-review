### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the significant computational challenge of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The DPP likelihood contains a determinant term, `det(L + I)`, which in the finite case requires an `O(n^3)` matrix eigendecomposition and is often analytically intractable in the continuous case. Existing scalable methods, such as Affandi et al. (2014), mitigate this cost by using bounds based on a partial spectral decomposition, which still requires iterative methods like power iteration and is primarily suited for finite domains.

The main contribution of this work is a novel set of non-spectral bounds for this determinant term. Drawing inspiration from sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes (e.g., Titsias, 2009), the authors introduce a set of `m` "inducing points" or "pseudo-inputs" `Z` to construct a low-rank approximation `Q` of the kernel matrix `L`. This leads to both an upper and a lower bound on `det(L + I)` that can be computed efficiently in `O(nm^2)` time for the finite case. The authors then provide a principled extension of these bounds to DPPs over continuous domains, a setting where spectral methods are particularly challenging.

These bounds are then leveraged to enable two standard inference paradigms. First, the lower bound on the log-likelihood is used as an objective for variational inference (VI), where kernel parameters and the locations of the inducing points `Z` are optimized jointly. Second, the upper and lower bounds are employed within a Metropolis-Hastings (MH) algorithm using a retrospective sampling scheme, allowing for asymptotically exact MCMC-based inference without computing the true likelihood. The efficacy of these methods is demonstrated through experiments on both a synthetic 1D continuous DPP (where the ground truth is known) and a real-world 2D spatial point process dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The proposed bounds are derived from a known matrix inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975), lending them a solid theoretical foundation. The application of these bounds to both VI and MCMC is sound and follows established statistical methodology. The experimental evaluation is well-designed and convincing. The 1D Gaussian-Gaussian toy experiment is particularly strong, as it allows for direct comparison against an "ideal" MH sampler with access to the exact likelihood, thereby validating the correctness of the proposed retrospective MCMC sampler. The authors are also commendably transparent about the limitations of their approach and the broader challenges in the field, such as the parameter identifiability issues that arise from parametrizing the `L` kernel directly. Their analysis showing that MCMC is more robust than VI in this scenario is insightful.
*   **Weaknesses:** The optimization of the inducing point locations `Z` is a crucial component of the method's success, yet the properties of this optimization landscape are not discussed in detail. Since this is likely a non-convex problem, a brief discussion of potential issues like local minima would have strengthened the paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   **Strengths:** The organization is excellent. The authors wisely introduce the core ideas in the simpler finite setting (Section 2) before moving to the inference frameworks (Section 3) and the more technical continuous case (Section 4). This structure makes the paper highly accessible. The explicit connection drawn to the sparse Gaussian Process literature is very effective, as it allows readers familiar with that area to quickly grasp the core mechanic of the proposed method. The prose is precise and the mathematical notation is consistent.
*   **Weaknesses:** This is a minor point, but some of the footnotes contain substantive comments (e.g., Footnote 1 on the convergence of the bounds) that might be better integrated into the main text for improved flow.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and original approach to a known problem.
*   **Strengths:** The central idea of using non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds for the DPP likelihood determinant is new. This marks a significant conceptual departure from prior work (Affandi et al., 2014) that relied on partial spectral knowledge. While the use of inducing points is borrowed from the GP literature and retrospective sampling is a known technique, their synthesis and application to derive these specific bounds for DPP inference is a non-trivial and original contribution. The extension of the method to continuous DPPs is a particularly important and novel aspect, significantly broadening the applicability of scalable DPP inference.
*   **Weaknesses:** The inference frameworks themselves (VI and retrospective MCMC) are standard. The novelty lies entirely in the bounds that are being plugged into them, a fact the authors are clear about.

**Significance:** The paper is highly significant and likely to have a strong impact on the field.
*   **Strengths:** This work provides a practical and powerful tool for researchers and practitioners using DPPs. By removing the dependency on spectral decompositions, it makes likelihood-based inference for DPPs more general, scalable, and easier to implement, especially for continuous domains where eigenfunctions are rarely known. The method addresses a key bottleneck that has limited the wider application of parametric DPP models. The successful re-analysis of the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrates the method's utility on real-world scientific problems. The approach is likely to be widely adopted and built upon.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** Proposes a new class of non-spectral bounds for the DPP likelihood, moving beyond the limitations of previous spectral-based approximations.
2.  **Significance:** Addresses a major computational bottleneck in DPP inference, making parametric models more practical and scalable, especially for continuous domains.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The method is theoretically well-founded and empirically validated through rigorous experiments, including a comparison against a known ground truth.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making a technical contribution highly accessible.
5.  **Generality:** The framework is developed for both finite and continuous DPPs, a key advantage over much of the prior work on scalable inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The optimization of the variational parameters (the inducing points `Z`) is a non-convex problem, and the paper does not provide a characterization of this optimization problem, relying instead on general-purpose optimizers.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance are compelling. This paper presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution that advances the state of the art in inference for determinantal point processes.
