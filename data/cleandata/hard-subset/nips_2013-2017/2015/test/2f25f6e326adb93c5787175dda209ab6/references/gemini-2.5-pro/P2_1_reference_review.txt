**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the calculation of the normalizing constant, which involves the determinant of a matrix (or the Fredholm determinant of an operator) of the form `det(L + I)`. This calculation typically requires an expensive spectral decomposition (O(n³) for an n-point finite set) or is analytically intractable in continuous domains. The authors propose a novel approach that bypasses spectral methods entirely. Drawing inspiration from sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes (GPs), they introduce a set of "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" to derive cheap-to-compute upper and lower bounds on the log-likelihood. These non-spectral bounds are applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs. The paper then demonstrates how these bounds can be effectively integrated into two standard inference frameworks: a fast but approximate variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes the lower bound, and an asymptotically exact Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method using retrospective sampling to make accept/reject decisions. The utility of the methods is demonstrated on a synthetic 1D continuous problem and a real-world spatial statistics dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The theoretical contribution is sound, basing its core bounds on a non-trivial determinantal inequality from Seiler & Simon (2009). The adaptation of this inequality to both finite and continuous DPP likelihoods is rigorous and well-explained.
    *   The proposed inference schemes are direct and well-justified applications of the derived bounds. Providing both a VI and an MCMC approach is a significant strength, offering a trade-off between computational cost and statistical accuracy that will be valuable to practitioners.
    *   The experimental evaluation is well-designed. The 1D toy experiment is particularly effective because it has a known ground truth, allowing for a clear validation of the MCMC sampler's correctness. The authors are commendably transparent about the failure of the VI method in this setting, correctly identifying it as a symptom of parameter non-identifiability, which strengthens the paper's overall credibility. The diabetic neuropathy experiment demonstrates the practical utility and scalability of the VI approach on a real-world problem, showing convergence of the estimated parameters and likelihood bounds as the number of inducing points increases.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The proofs of the main propositions are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is standard practice, it makes a full technical verification of the core claims more difficult from the main text alone.
    *   The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of the computational complexity of optimizing the inducing point locations `Z`. While the complexity of evaluating the bound is given (O(nm²)), the cost of finding a good set of `Z` via gradient-based or black-box methods is a key part of the practical algorithm and is not fully analyzed.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The motivation for the work is established clearly in the introduction. The problem is well-defined, and the limitations of existing methods (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014) are explained concisely.
    *   The structure is logical, building from the simpler finite case to the more complex continuous case. This makes the paper accessible to readers who may not be experts in operator theory.
    *   The analogy to inducing variables in sparse GPs is used effectively throughout, providing helpful intuition for readers familiar with that literature.
    *   Figures are clear and effectively support the experimental results. For instance, the visualization of the optimized inducing points in Figures 1 and 4 provides compelling intuition about what the variational optimization is learning.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The transition from the bound for the finite case (Proposition 1) to the continuous case (Proposition 2) could be made more intuitive in the main text. While the supplement likely contains the details, a brief explanation of how the matrix `Q` approximation translates into the operator-theoretic objects in Proposition 2 would improve readability.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The primary idea of using non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds for the DPP likelihood is novel. While the use of inducing points is a cornerstone of the sparse GP literature (Titsias, 2009), its application to this specific problem in DPP inference is new and insightful. It represents a significant conceptual shift away from the spectral approximation methods previously used.
    *   This work clearly differentiates itself from prior work. The main point of comparison, Affandi et al. (2014), relies on partial eigendecomposition. This paper's method avoids eigendecomposition entirely, offering a fundamentally different and often more efficient approach.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   This work stands to make likelihood-based inference for parametric DPPs significantly more practical and scalable. The proposed methods lower the computational barrier for applying DPPs to large finite datasets and to continuous domains with general-purpose kernels.
    *   The dual VI/MCMC framework makes the contribution broadly useful. Researchers seeking fast, scalable point estimates can use the VI approach, while those requiring full posterior inference can use the MCMC method.
    *   The results on the diabetic neuropathy dataset show that the method can provide meaningful scientific insights, successfully distinguishing between patient classes based on the inferred repulsive properties of their nerve fiber distributions. This demonstrates the potential for real-world application.
    *   The ideas presented here are likely to be built upon by future research, for instance, in developing more sophisticated variational approximations or exploring connections to other areas where Fredholm determinants appear.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and powerful method for tackling a fundamental and long-standing challenge in DPP inference. It significantly advances the state-of-the-art in scalable DPP modeling.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The approach is well-grounded in theory and is rigorously presented.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and easy to follow.
4.  **Practical Utility:** It provides practical algorithms (both approximate VI and exact MCMC) that are validated on both synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating clear real-world applicability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

I find no compelling arguments against accepting this paper. The minor weaknesses identified (e.g., lack of complexity analysis for optimizing `Z`) are easily addressable and do not detract from the core contribution.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on probabilistic modeling. It solves a key computational problem in DPP inference with an elegant and effective method. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and experimentally validated. It will be of great interest to researchers and practitioners in machine learning and statistics who use point processes. I strongly recommend acceptance.
