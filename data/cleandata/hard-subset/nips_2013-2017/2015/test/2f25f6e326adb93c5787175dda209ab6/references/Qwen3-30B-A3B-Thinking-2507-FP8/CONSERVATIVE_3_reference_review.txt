

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper proposes bounds on the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid spectral decomposition, enabling likelihood-based inference without requiring eigendecomposition of the kernel operator L. The authors derive non-spectral bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, demonstrating their use in variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods. The work builds on connections to variational Gaussian processes [11] and extends them to DPPs.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with proper mathematical development.
- The theoretical bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) are derived from established determinant inequalities and are technically sound.
- The connection to Gaussian process literature is well-motivated and appropriately cited.
- The experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets provides some empirical support for the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's main contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The bounds rely on existing variational techniques from Gaussian processes, and the paper fails to demonstrate significant advantages over established methods for DPP inference.
- The authors acknowledge but do not adequately address the fundamental interpretability issue of parametrizing L directly instead of K (Section 2). As noted in the paper, "this step favours tractability over interpretability," yet the paper does not explore how to recover interpretability or address this limitation. This is a critical flaw in a paper claiming to advance DPP inference.
- The experimental evaluation lacks rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art DPP inference methods. For instance, the paper does not compare against the spectral bounds approach in [7], which the authors cite as the current standard. Without such comparison, it's impossible to determine if the proposed method offers meaningful improvements.
- The computational benefits are overstated. While the authors claim their method avoids spectral decomposition, the approach still requires O(nm²) time where m is the number of pseudo-inputs. When m is large (as in the experiments, where m reaches 80-1200), this can be comparable or worse than spectral methods.
- The variational inference results (Section 5.1) show poor consistency across runs, with κ varying over orders of magnitude. The authors attribute this to parameter identifiability but fail to demonstrate that their method resolves this issue better than alternatives.
- The paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the bounds are tighter than existing spectral-based approaches. The continuous DPP experiments (Section 5.1) rely on a special case where the spectral decomposition is known, which undermines the generality of the claimed contribution.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its mathematical development but lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its claims. The authors do not properly evaluate their method against established baselines, making it difficult to assess the practical value of their contribution. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The connection to Gaussian process literature is well-explained, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.

**Originality:** The paper's contribution is incremental rather than novel. While the bounds are derived for DPPs, the approach is directly inspired by existing variational techniques for Gaussian processes. The authors do not sufficiently distinguish their work from prior art, particularly [7] which uses spectral bounds.

**Significance:** The proposed method does not address the core challenges of DPP inference. The paper fails to demonstrate that the bounds provide meaningful improvements in accuracy or efficiency over existing methods. The parametrization issue (using L instead of K) remains unresolved, which is a fundamental limitation for practical applications of DPPs.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Well-written with clear mathematical development
- Theoretical bounds are sound and derived from established inequalities
- Provides a useful extension to continuous DPPs

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Lacks rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art DPP inference methods
- Fails to address the fundamental interpretability issue of parametrizing L instead of K
- Overstates computational benefits without proper empirical validation
- The contribution is incremental rather than transformative for the field

**Recommendation:** Reject.

The paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution that fails to demonstrate significant advantages over existing methods. The authors do not properly address the interpretability limitations of their approach, and the experimental validation lacks sufficient comparison with established baselines. While the theoretical bounds are correct, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that this approach provides meaningful improvements for practical DPP inference. For a paper to be accepted at NIPS, it should either present a fundamentally new approach with clear advantages or demonstrate substantial improvements over existing methods through rigorous experiments. This paper falls short on both counts.
