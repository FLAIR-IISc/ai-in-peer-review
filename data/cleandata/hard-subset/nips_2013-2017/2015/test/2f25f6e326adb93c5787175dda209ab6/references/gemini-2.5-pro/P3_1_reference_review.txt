### Summary

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the calculation of the normalization constant, `det(L + I)`, which typically requires an expensive spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix (or operator) L. The authors' main contribution is a set of novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant for both finite and continuous DPPs.

The proposed bounds are inspired by variational methods for sparse Gaussian Processes (GPs), utilizing a small set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" `Z`. These bounds can be computed efficiently, scaling as O(nm^2) instead of O(n^3) in the finite case, where `n` is the total number of items and `m << n`. The authors then demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference paradigms:
1.  **Variational Inference (VI):** The lower bound on the likelihood is used as a tractable objective function (an ELBO), which can be maximized jointly with respect to the kernel parameters `θ` and the locations of the pseudo-inputs `Z`.
2.  **Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC):** The upper and lower bounds are used within a Metropolis-Hastings framework using retrospective sampling. This allows for asymptotically exact inference by adaptively tightening the bounds (by increasing `m`) only when necessary to make an accept/reject decision.

The methodology is first developed for DPPs on finite sets and then elegantly extended to the more general case of DPPs on continuous domains using operator-theoretic concepts. The approach is validated on a synthetic 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP, where the MCMC sampler's results match an "ideal" sampler with access to the true likelihood, and on a real-world spatial point process dataset of diabetic neuropathy, where the VI method effectively distinguishes between patient classes.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and highly practical problem. The requirement for spectral decomposition has significantly limited the scalability and applicability of parametric DPP models. Providing a "spectral-free" inference framework is a major practical advance that could make DPPs accessible to a much wider range of large-scale problems.

2.  **Methodological Soundness and Elegance:** The core idea of borrowing the inducing point methodology from the sparse GP literature [11] is both clever and well-motivated. The mathematical derivation, based on the determinant inequality from Seiler & Simon [14], is sound. The extension to the continuous case in Section 4 is non-trivial and demonstrates a strong command of the underlying theory, connecting matrix determinants to Fredholm determinants in a principled way.

3.  **Completeness:** The paper provides a comprehensive solution by presenting two distinct inference algorithms. The VI approach offers a fast, scalable, but approximate solution, while the MCMC approach provides a more computationally intensive but asymptotically exact alternative. This duality is valuable, as different applications will have different trade-offs between speed and accuracy.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The decision to present the finite case first (Section 2) before moving to the more abstract continuous case (Section 4) is excellent for readability. The connection to sparse GPs is clearly stated, which helps readers familiar with that literature to quickly grasp the core concept.

5.  **Convincing Experiments:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the utility of the proposed methods. The toy experiment provides crucial validation for the MCMC sampler by comparing it against a ground-truth case. The diabetic neuropathy experiment shows that the VI approach is practical for real-world problems, and the analysis of how the parameter estimates and likelihood bounds converge as `m` increases (Figures 3a-d) provides both insight and practical guidance for users.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Novelty in Context:** While the application to DPPs is novel, the core components of the methodology have appeared elsewhere. The use of inducing points to form low-rank approximations is the foundation of sparse GPs [11]. The retrospective sampling technique for MCMC with bounds has also been used for DPP inference before, notably in [7], albeit with *spectral* bounds. The main novelty here is the specific non-spectral bound itself and its successful integration into these frameworks. This is a strong contribution, but it's more of a novel and powerful combination of existing concepts than a fundamentally new paradigm.

2.  **Optimization of Pseudo-Inputs:** The paper states that for VI, one maximizes the lower bound `F(θ, Z)` jointly with respect to `θ` and `Z`. While gradient-based methods are mentioned, the manuscript would be strengthened by providing more detail on this optimization. For instance, are the gradients with respect to the locations `Z` straightforward to compute for standard kernels? The experiments show that the final locations of `Z` form neat grids, which is interesting, but the dynamics of the optimization process that leads there remain somewhat opaque.

3.  **L-Kernel Parametrization Issue:** The authors correctly acknowledge the interpretability issues that arise from parametrizing the L-kernel directly instead of the more natural K-kernel. Their experiments confirm this, showing that the `κ` parameter (related to the expected number of points) is not well-identified by the data. While solving this L-K relationship is outside the scope of *this* paper, it remains a significant limitation of the overall approach. The work solves the computational problem but does not address this modeling problem.

4.  **Lack of Direct Competitive Baselines:** In the VI experiment, the method is primarily evaluated by its own internal consistency as `m` varies. A direct comparison against other scalable DPP inference methods (e.g., Nyström approximations [13] or the spectral-bound MCMC from [7]) in terms of a speed-vs-accuracy trade-off would have provided a clearer picture of where the proposed method stands in the landscape of existing techniques.

### Final Assessment

**Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, the claims are well-supported by both theoretical derivations and solid experimental results. It is a complete piece of work.

**Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making a potentially complex topic accessible.

**Originality:** Good. The paper presents a novel and highly useful method for DPP inference by cleverly combining ideas from sparse GPs and determinantal inequalities. The primary contribution is the specific non-spectral bound and its application, which is a new and valuable addition to the DPP literature.

**Significance:** High. This work has the potential to be very influential. By removing the spectral decomposition bottleneck, it makes parametric DPPs a viable tool for a much larger class of problems, which will likely encourage their wider adoption in the machine learning community.

**Recommendation: Accept.**

This is a strong paper that presents a clear, elegant, and highly practical solution to a well-known problem. The strengths in terms of significance, technical soundness, and clarity far outweigh the minor weaknesses regarding the positioning of its novelty and the lack of some experimental baselines. This work is a valuable contribution to the NIPS community and is likely to be widely cited and built upon.
