### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the calculation of the determinant of a large matrix or a Fredholm determinant of an operator, which typically requires a costly spectral decomposition. The authors propose novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds for this determinant term. The core idea is inspired by sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes (GPs), utilizing a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing variables" to form a low-rank approximation of the DPP kernel matrix/operator `L`. These bounds are computationally efficient, scaling in `O(nm^2)` for the finite case, making inference feasible for large `n`.

The paper demonstrates how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference frameworks:
1.  **Variational Inference (VI):** The lower bound on the likelihood is used as an evidence lower bound (ELBO) which can be maximized jointly with respect to the kernel parameters and the locations of the pseudo-inputs.
2.  **Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC):** The upper and lower bounds are used within a Metropolis-Hastings algorithm to bound the acceptance ratio. This enables an asymptotically exact sampling procedure via retrospective sampling, where the bounds are tightened (by increasing `m`) only when necessary to make an accept/reject decision.

The proposed methods are developed for DPPs on both finite sets and continuous domains, and their effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic 1D problem with a known ground truth and a real-world 2D spatial statistics dataset of nerve fibers.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Practical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is that it provides a practical and scalable solution to a well-known and prohibitive problem in DPP inference. By removing the need for any spectral calculations, it makes likelihood-based DPP modeling accessible for much larger datasets than was previously feasible with methods requiring even partial eigendecompositions (e.g., [7]).

2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Approach:** The connection drawn to sparse approximations in GPs is insightful and powerful. It leverages a mature and successful idea (inducing variables) from a related field and creatively applies it to the DPP likelihood, which is a non-obvious but very effective transfer of knowledge.

3.  **Theoretical Soundness and Generality:** The bounds are grounded in a non-trivial determinant inequality ([14]), lending them theoretical credibility. The authors further demonstrate the generality of their approach by extending it from the finite matrix case to the continuous operator case (Section 4), which is technically more demanding and broadens the applicability of the work.

4.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental validation is strong. The 1D toy experiment is well-designed, as the known true likelihood allows for a direct comparison that validates their MCMC sampler. The honest reporting of the VI method's failure on this problem due to identifiability issues is commendable. The real-world diabetic neuropathy experiment successfully demonstrates the method's utility on a practical problem, showing convergence of parameter estimates as the approximation quality (`m`) improves and reproducing the scientific conclusions of prior work ([7, 22]).

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The decision to introduce the simpler finite case first before tackling the more abstract continuous case is excellent for readability. The notation is clear, and the overall structure is logical.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Inference Schemes:** While the bounds themselves are novel in this context, the methods used to deploy them (VI via an ELBO, and MCMC via retrospective sampling) are standard applications of such bounds. The VI framework is a direct analogue to sparse GP regression, and retrospective sampling has been previously used for DPP inference in [7]. The main novelty lies squarely in the bounds themselves, not in the surrounding inference machinery. This is a minor weakness, as the contribution is still substantial.

2.  **The L-parametrization Problem:** The paper follows the common practice of parametrizing the `L` kernel for tractability, which the authors correctly acknowledge comes at the cost of interpretability compared to parametrizing the `K` kernel. The 1D experiment provides a stark illustration of this issue, where the scale parameter `κ` is non-identifiable. While this is a known general problem for this class of DPP models rather than a flaw of the proposed inference method, the paper doesn't offer new insights into resolving it.

3.  **Practical Guidance for Pseudo-inputs:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion on the practicalities of choosing the number (`m`) and initial placement of the pseudo-inputs `Z`. The experiments show that results converge as `m` increases and that optimization finds good locations for `Z`, but guidance on how to set `m` a priori or heuristics for initialization would make the method even more accessible to practitioners.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are derived from established mathematical results and the experimental results robustly support the claims of efficiency and correctness. The work is complete and self-contained. The authors are careful to discuss the limitations of their approach, particularly the L vs. K parametrization issue.

**Clarity:** The paper is written with excellent clarity. The motivation is clear, the methods are explained logically, and the connections to prior work in both DPPs and GPs are explicitly made. The structure makes the paper accessible even to readers who might not be experts in operator theory (by allowing them to skip Section 4).

**Originality:** The core contribution—the specific non-spectral bounds for the DPP likelihood based on inducing variables—is highly original and constitutes a novel bridge between two important areas of machine learning. While it builds on ideas from sparse GPs ([11]) and retrospective MCMC ([7]), the synthesis and application to remove the spectral dependency is a new and valuable contribution.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant. This work has the potential to become a standard technique for inference in parametric DPP models. It demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art by providing a method that is more scalable and broadly applicable than previous spectral-based approaches. Researchers and practitioners interested in modeling diversity and repulsion will likely use and build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Solves a major, well-known computational bottleneck in a popular class of models.
*   The proposed method is elegant, well-founded, and demonstrably effective.
*   The contribution is significant, making DPPs practical for larger-scale problems.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and supported by strong experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The inference frameworks (VI, MCMC) built around the novel bounds are themselves standard applications. (This is a very minor point).
*   The work does not address the underlying interpretability issue of L-parametrization, though it acknowledges it.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept. This is a solid, well-executed piece of work that makes a significant and practical contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling.
