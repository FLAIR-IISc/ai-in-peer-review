## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors propose novel, non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood, inspired by variational inference techniques for Gaussian Processes. They demonstrate how these bounds can be used within both variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, for both finite and continuous DPPs. The core contribution is a practical method to bypass the computationally expensive eigendecomposition, enabling inference for larger DPPs and potentially for kernels where analytical decomposition is intractable.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical practical limitation:** The spectral decomposition requirement is a major obstacle to applying DPPs to large-scale problems.  This paper directly tackles this issue.
* **Solid theoretical foundation:** The bounds are rigorously derived, building upon established results in Gaussian process approximation and determinant inequalities.  The connection to existing variational GP literature provides a strong basis.
* **Generality:** The approach extends to both finite and continuous DPPs, increasing its applicability.
* **Demonstrated effectiveness:** The experimental results, particularly the retrospective MCMC example, showcase the practical utility of the method.  The convergence of variational inference results with increasing inducing points is also encouraging.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is well-structured and generally easy to follow, even for those not deeply familiar with DPPs.  The authors acknowledge potential limitations and future directions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Identifiability:** The toy Gaussian experiment clearly highlights a weakness: difficulties in identifying the kernel parameters, particularly the scale parameter (κ). The authors acknowledge this, but it’s a substantial limitation. It suggests that the proposed method may be better suited for scenarios where some parameters are known or fixed.
* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-Input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a win, the optimization over the pseudo-inputs (Z) in both variational inference and MCMC adds a computational cost. The paper doesn’t fully explore the trade-off between avoiding spectral decomposition and the cost of pseudo-input optimization.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to existing methods is somewhat limited. While the authors demonstrate that their MCMC approach agrees with an exact method in a simple case, a more thorough comparison to alternatives (e.g., approximate spectral decomposition methods) would be beneficial.
* **Practical guidance is limited:** While the paper showcases the feasibility of the method, it provides limited practical guidance on how to choose the number of pseudo-inputs (m) or other hyperparameters. The suggestion to increase m until results stabilize is a reasonable starting point, but a more systematic exploration would be helpful.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations appear correct (although a thorough verification would require deeper expertise), and the experimental results support the claims. The work is complete in the sense that it provides a fully described method and demonstrates its application. The authors are honest about limitations (e.g., parameter identifiability).
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The notation is mostly clear, and the authors provide sufficient background information.
* **Originality:** The approach of using non-spectral bounds inspired by GP variational inference to tackle DPP inference is novel. While the underlying techniques (GP variational inference, determinant inequalities) are known, their application to DPPs in this way is a significant contribution. The extension to continuous DPPs further adds to the originality.
* **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial.  Overcoming the spectral decomposition bottleneck could open up DPPs to a much wider range of applications, particularly in areas like large-scale recommendation systems, document summarization, and spatial modeling.  

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Addresses a major practical limitation in DPP inference.
* Provides a theoretically sound and well-motivated method.
* Demonstrates effectiveness through experiments.
* Is a novel contribution to the field.

**Against:**

*  Parameter identifiability issues.
*  Additional computational cost associated with pseudo-input optimization.
* Limited comparison to existing methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS. While the parameter identifiability issue is a concern, the authors acknowledge it, and the core contribution – bypassing the spectral decomposition bottleneck – is significant. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and represents a valuable advance in the field of DPP modeling. The potential benefits of this work outweigh its limitations, and I expect it to be of interest to a broad audience within NIPS.
