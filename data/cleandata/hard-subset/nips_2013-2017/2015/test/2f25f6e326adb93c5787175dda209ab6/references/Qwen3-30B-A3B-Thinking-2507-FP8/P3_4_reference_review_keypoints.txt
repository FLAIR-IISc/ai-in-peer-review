- Addresses the computational bottleneck of requiring spectral decomposition for DPP likelihoods by proposing nonspectral bounds that enable efficient variational inference and MCMC for finite and continuous DPPs.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical foundation with proofs of key nonspectral bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) derived from determinant inequalities.  
- Extends prior DPP work by handling continuous domains, not just discrete settings.  
- Experimental validation is comprehensive, showing improved computational efficiency and inference quality on synthetic and real datasets (including Gaussian continuous and diabetic neuropathy experiments).  
- Effectively connects and extends sparse Gaussian process variational methods to DPPs, enabling integration into standard inference paradigms without eigendecomposition.  
- Demonstrates practical convergence behavior as the number of inducing points increases (empirical support in Figures 3c-3d).  
- Practical applicability is high: method can be applied to existing DPP implementations without spectral knowledge.  
- Fails to address interpretability issues of parametrizing L instead of K and does not resolve identifiability problems (e.g., poor estimation of parameter κ).  
- Lacks a thorough quantitative comparison (runtime and inference quality) with prior spectral-bound approaches [7].  
- Does not analyze how the choice or number of pseudo-inputs Z affects bound tightness, convergence, or provide guidance on selecting |Z|.  
- Omits discussion of practical scalability limits for very large datasets (e.g., millions of points) and comparisons to other scalable DPP methods [13].  
- Overall assessment: novel, technically sound, and practically useful contribution, but would be strengthened by addressing interpretability, comparisons, and pseudo-input analysis.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, requesting more thorough comparison to spectral methods, expanded L vs K/identifiability discussion, and detailed analysis of pseudo-input selection.
