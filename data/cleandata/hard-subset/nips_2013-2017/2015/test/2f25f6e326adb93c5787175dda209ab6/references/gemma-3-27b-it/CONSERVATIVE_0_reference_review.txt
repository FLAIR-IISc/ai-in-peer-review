This paper addresses the challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decomposition, a common bottleneck in practical applications. The authors propose bounds on the likelihood that circumvent this requirement, drawing inspiration from variational Gaussian Processes. They explore both variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods utilizing these bounds, extending their approach to continuous DPPs.

The primary strength of this work lies in its technical contribution: deriving tractable bounds on the DPP likelihood. The extension of variational inference techniques from GPs to DPPs is a logical step, and the proposal for retrospective MCMC using these bounds is clever. The authors clearly articulate the motivation – avoiding computationally expensive spectral decompositions – and relate their work to existing approaches. The inclusion of both finite and continuous DPP cases broadens the applicability of the method. The experimental validation, including a synthetic example and a real-world dataset (diabetic neuropathy), is a positive aspect, demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed bounds.

However, my concerns regarding this submission are substantial. While the technical work is sound *given* the chosen approach, the core idea – avoiding spectral decomposition by parametrizing L directly – feels like a workaround rather than a fundamental advance. As the authors themselves acknowledge, parametrizing L introduces interpretability issues and can lead to identifiability problems (demonstrated in the Gaussian experiment with κ). The extensive discussion of these limitations within the paper almost reads as a self-critique, and underscores my skepticism. 

The presented bounds, while mathematically correct, don't fundamentally alter the computational complexity in a way that makes DPP inference truly scalable to extremely large datasets. Optimizing over the pseudo-inputs Z, particularly within the MCMC framework, adds a computational cost that may still be prohibitive for very high-dimensional problems. The experiments, while helpful, don't convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods when spectral decomposition is feasible or approximate spectral methods can be applied. The comparison to the "ideal" MH in the Gaussian example validates the MCMC *implementation*, not the core idea of avoiding spectral methods. The results on the real dataset are promising, but the comparison is primarily with the results reported in [7], and a more thorough benchmarking against state-of-the-art DPP inference techniques would be beneficial. 

Furthermore, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in certain sections. The connection between the continuous and discrete cases, and the justification for the assumptions made in the continuous setting, are not entirely clear. The introduction of several concepts (e.g., Fredholm determinants) without sufficient explanation for a reader unfamiliar with operator theory is also a weakness.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel bounds on DPP likelihood that avoid spectral decomposition.
*   Extension of variational inference and MCMC techniques to DPPs with these bounds.
*   Demonstrated applicability to both finite and continuous DPPs.
*   Empirical validation on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core idea of directly parametrizing L introduces interpretability issues and potential identifiability problems.
*   The computational benefits may be limited, especially for large datasets.
*   The paper doesn’t fully demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.
*   Some sections lack clarity and require more detailed explanations.
*   The work feels like a workaround rather than a fundamental breakthrough.



**Overall Assessment:**

I view this paper as technically sound but ultimately incremental. While the proposed bounds are a valid contribution, they address a symptom (spectral decomposition) rather than the underlying challenge of efficient DPP inference. The limitations associated with directly parametrizing L, and the lack of a compelling demonstration of scalability, lead me to recommend **rejection**. The work is not sufficiently novel or significant to warrant inclusion in NIPS at this time. I encourage the authors to explore alternative approaches that address the fundamental computational challenges of DPP inference, rather than relying on workarounds.
