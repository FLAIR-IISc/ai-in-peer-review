This paper addresses the challenging problem of inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition, a significant bottleneck in many applications. The authors provide bounds on the DPP likelihood that circumvent this decomposition, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques used in Gaussian Processes. They present theoretical results for both finite and continuous DPPs and validate their approach with experiments, including a real-world dataset.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation and technical details. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem and highlights the limitations of existing methods. The derivation of the bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 is the core contribution, and the connections drawn to variational inference and MCMC are logical and well-explained. The experimental validation, including the application to a relevant dataset (diabetic neuropathy), strengthens the paper's claims. The authors are careful to discuss the trade-offs of their approach, particularly regarding parameter interpretability and the need for optimizing pseudo-inputs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical derivations appear rigorous, building upon established results from Gaussian process literature and utilizing relevant inequalities on determinants. The extension from finite to continuous DPPs is a notable achievement.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed bounds offer a significant advance over existing methods that rely on spectral decomposition, opening up possibilities for applying DPPs to larger and more complex datasets.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the concepts are explained in a reasonably accessible manner, despite the inherent complexity of the topic.
*   **Significance:** Overcoming the computational barrier of spectral decomposition has the potential to broaden the applicability of DPPs in various fields, including machine learning, spatial statistics, and modeling diversity.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost of Pseudo-Inputs:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, the introduction of pseudo-inputs (Z) and the need to optimize them adds a new computational challenge. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t provide a comprehensive analysis of the scaling behavior of this optimization with respect to dataset size.
*   **Parameter Interpretability:** The authors repeatedly emphasize the loss of interpretability when directly parametrizing the kernel L instead of K. This is a valid concern, and while acknowledged, a deeper discussion of potential strategies to mitigate this issue would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The experimental section provides a good demonstration of the method’s functionality, but a more thorough comparison with existing DPP inference techniques (even if approximate) would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Complexity of Proofs:** While the paper states that proofs are available in the supplementary material, referencing specific theorem/inequality numbers within the main text would aid the reader.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
*   The proposed bounds are a novel and theoretically sound contribution.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the approach.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The introduction of pseudo-inputs adds a new computational cost that requires further investigation.
*   The loss of parameter interpretability is a significant drawback.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP modeling. The proposed bounds offer a practical solution to a longstanding computational challenge, and the theoretical and experimental results are convincing. While the introduction of pseudo-inputs and the loss of parameter interpretability are valid concerns, they are acknowledged by the authors and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, specifically providing a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the pseudo-input optimization and expanding the experimental comparisons.
