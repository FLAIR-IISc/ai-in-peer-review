

# Peer Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix/operator for likelihood evaluation. The authors propose novel, non-spectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid the computationally expensive eigendecomposition required by previous approaches (e.g., [7]). Their key insight is to use a determinant inequality to derive bounds that depend only on a set of "pseudo-inputs" (Z), rather than requiring spectral knowledge. The paper develops these bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, and demonstrates how they can be incorporated into variational inference and MCMC methods. This work builds on the foundational NIPS papers on DPPs [3, 7] and extends the framework to remove a key computational barrier that has limited the application of DPPs to larger or continuous domains.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Contribution**: The paper solves a real and important computational bottleneck in DPP inference. Previous methods [7] require spectral decomposition, which is prohibitively expensive for large datasets or continuous domains. The authors' non-spectral approach removes this barrier, making DPP inference feasible for larger-scale applications.

2. **Theoretical Soundness**: The bounds are derived from a nontrivial determinant inequality [14], and the theoretical development is sound. The connection to sparse Gaussian process approximations [11] is well-motivated and helps situate the work in a broader machine learning context.

3. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The authors provide thorough validation through both synthetic experiments (where the true likelihood is computable) and a real-world application (diabetic neuropathy dataset). The experiments clearly demonstrate the viability of their approach and its advantages over previous methods.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and effectively communicates the technical content. The distinction between finite and continuous cases is well-maintained, and readers familiar with Gaussian processes will find the connections helpful.

5. **Honest Assessment of Limitations**: The authors acknowledge the interpretability issues of parametrizing L instead of K (a common practice in the literature), and they provide a clear discussion of the trade-offs between their approach and previous work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Pseudo-Input Selection**: While the paper optimizes over the pseudo-inputs Z as part of the inference procedure, it doesn't thoroughly analyze how the choice of Z affects performance or how to select it beyond the optimization process. This is particularly important since the quality of the bounds depends on Z.

2. **Comparison with Previous Methods**: The experiments on the real dataset (Section 5.2) show that the approach works, but they don't directly compare the predictive performance (e.g., classification accuracy) of the DPP models with previous methods. The paper states that "the estimated measures converge to coherent values," but doesn't quantify how these values compare to those from spectral-based methods.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper mentions that the variational approach runs in O(nm²) time but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how the computational complexity scales with m (the number of pseudo-inputs) or how it compares to spectral-based methods in practice.

4. **Connection to Related Work**: While the paper references related work on Gaussian processes, it could better explain why the specific determinant inequality they use is particularly suited for DPPs rather than general matrix problems.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling by addressing a key computational limitation in DPP inference. The proposed non-spectral bounds are theoretically sound, practically useful, and well-validated through experiments. The work advances the state of the art in DPP inference by removing a major barrier to applying these models to larger and more complex datasets.

The paper is of high quality, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. It is clearly original, as it solves a problem that previous NIPS papers on DPPs [3, 7] have not addressed. The significance of the work is substantial, as it makes DPPs more applicable to real-world problems where spectral decomposition is infeasible.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper should be accepted for NIPS. The main weakness is the limited discussion of pseudo-input selection, but this is a minor point compared to the substantial contribution. The authors have clearly demonstrated the utility of their approach through both theoretical development and empirical validation. The paper advances the field in a meaningful way by making DPP inference computationally feasible for larger datasets and continuous domains.

## Pros and Cons for Acceptance

**Pros:**
- Solves a real and important computational bottleneck in DPP inference
- Theoretical bounds are sound and well-motivated
- Comprehensive experimental validation on both synthetic and real data
- Clear presentation and well-organized structure
- Honest discussion of limitations and trade-offs

**Cons:**
- Limited analysis of pseudo-input selection and its impact on performance
- Some missing comparisons with previous methods in terms of predictive performance
- Limited discussion of computational complexity scaling

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a significant technical challenge in a well-established area of machine learning with clear practical implications. The authors have made a clear contribution that will be valuable to researchers working with DPPs and similar probabilistic models.
