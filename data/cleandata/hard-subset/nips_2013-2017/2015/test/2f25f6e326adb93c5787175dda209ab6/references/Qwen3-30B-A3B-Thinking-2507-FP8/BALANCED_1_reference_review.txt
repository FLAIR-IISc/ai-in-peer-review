

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood that avoid the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L, which is typically required for likelihood evaluation. The approach draws inspiration from sparse approximations in Gaussian processes, using "pseudo-inputs" to derive bounds that can be efficiently evaluated. The paper extends these bounds to both finite and continuous DPPs, demonstrating their utility in variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods.

## Strengths

The paper makes a strong technical contribution by providing non-spectral bounds that significantly reduce the computational burden of DPP inference. The bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) are theoretically sound and well-motivated, with clear connections to sparse Gaussian process approximations. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be practically applied to both finite and continuous DPPs, filling a notable gap in the literature as previous work (e.g., Affandi et al. 2013) relies on spectral knowledge that is often unavailable.

The experimental validation is thorough and well-designed. The toy continuous experiment (Section 5.1) provides a controlled setting where the authors can verify their method against exact inference, showing that their retrospective MCMC sampler produces results consistent with ideal inference. The real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset experiment (Section 5.2) demonstrates practical applicability, showing that the variational approach produces consistent estimates as the number of pseudo-inputs increases, with clear separation between the two classes of data.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem statement to theoretical development to experimental validation. The connections to sparse Gaussian processes are particularly helpful for readers familiar with that literature. The authors also thoughtfully acknowledge limitations, such as the identifiability issues when parametrizing L directly rather than K.

## Weaknesses

While the paper makes a valuable contribution, it has several notable weaknesses. First, the paper does not adequately address the fundamental issue of why parametrizing L is preferable to K, which is the core of the identifiability problem the authors mention. The paper states that "the machine learning literature almost exclusively adopts the more tractable parametrization of L," but fails to provide a convincing justification for this choice. A more thorough discussion of when parametrizing L is appropriate versus when K would be preferable would strengthen the paper.

Second, the paper does not sufficiently compare their approach with alternative approximation methods for DPP inference. For instance, the Nyström approximation (cited as [13]) is mentioned but not evaluated against the proposed bounds. Given that Nyström-based methods are commonly used for DPPs, a comparative analysis would help position the contribution more clearly.

Third, the variational inference results in Section 5.1 show poor consistency (κ varies over orders of magnitude across runs), but the paper does not deeply analyze this issue or suggest how to mitigate it. The authors note this as a reason to prefer MCMC, but don't provide guidance for practitioners on when to use which method.

Finally, the computational complexity analysis could be more detailed, particularly for the continuous case. The paper states that the bounds can be computed in O(nm²) time for finite DPPs, but doesn't provide a similar analysis for the continuous case, which would be important for practitioners.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to DPP inference with clear practical benefits. The bounds presented are novel, well-motivated, and demonstrated to work effectively in the experimental settings. The work addresses a genuine computational bottleneck in DPP inference that has been a limitation in previous approaches.

However, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by its failure to address the fundamental question of why parametrizing L is preferable to K, which is central to the identifiability issues it discusses. While the authors acknowledge these issues, they don't provide a compelling argument for why this trade-off is acceptable for most applications.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) A more thorough comparison with alternative approximation methods for DPP inference
2) A deeper analysis of the identifiability issues and when parametrizing L is appropriate
3) A more detailed computational complexity analysis for the continuous case
4) A clearer justification for the choice of parametrization

## Recommendation

The paper presents a technically sound contribution with clear practical benefits for DPP inference. While the work is novel and well-executed, it could be strengthened by addressing the identified weaknesses. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP inference by providing a practical method that avoids the need for spectral decomposition, which is a significant computational bottleneck. However, the failure to adequately address the fundamental identifiability issues and to compare with alternative methods slightly limits its significance.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above. The authors should strengthen their justification for parametrizing L, provide a more thorough comparison with alternative approaches, and include a more detailed computational analysis. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
