This paper tackles a significant practical bottleneck in applying Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the computational cost of spectral decomposition. The core idea – to provide bounds on the DPP likelihood that *avoid* spectral decomposition – is genuinely novel and addresses a long-standing limitation. The authors draw inspiration from variational Gaussian processes, extending those bounds to the DPP setting, and demonstrate application to both finite and continuous domains. This is a bold move; most recent work has focused on approximations *to* the kernel to retain interpretability, whereas this paper directly addresses the computational issue with a clever bounding technique.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core contribution – non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihoods – is a significant advance. It opens up the possibility of applying DPPs to larger, more complex datasets where spectral decomposition is intractable.
*   **Generality:** The results are presented for both discrete and continuous DPPs, demonstrating broad applicability.
*   **Connections to Variational Inference & MCMC:** Showing how these bounds integrate into established inference paradigms (VI and MCMC) is crucial for practical use. The retrospective MCMC approach is a smart adaptation.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The toy Gaussian experiment and the diabetic neuropathy dataset provide some empirical support. The observation of pseudo-inputs aligning with marginal distributions is insightful.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the bound is presented and proven (in the supplementary material), the tightness of the bound isn’t thoroughly investigated. How often is the bound loose? What are the conditions under which it becomes particularly inaccurate? This is a critical question for assessing practical usefulness.
*   **Computational Cost of Optimization:** The optimization of pseudo-inputs (Z) within the MCMC scheme seems potentially expensive, even if it avoids the full spectral decomposition. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed analysis of this cost (e.g., scaling with dataset size and number of inducing points) would be valuable.
*   **Parameter Identifiability:** The toy experiment reveals difficulties in identifying kernel parameters, specifically *κ*, when directly parametrizing L. This hints at fundamental issues with this parametrization, but the discussion is somewhat brief.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The diabetic neuropathy dataset validation lacks a strong comparison to existing DPP approaches. It would be more convincing to demonstrate a clear advantage – even a modest one – over existing methods, or to show that the proposed method can handle a dataset size that is intractable for others.
*   **Continuous Case Details:** The discussion of the continuous case (Section 4) is relatively high-level. More details on the computational aspects and practical considerations would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a truly novel idea with the potential to significantly impact the field.
*   The theoretical contribution is sound, and the connection to existing inference methods is well-established.
*   The experimental results, while not exhaustive, provide initial evidence of the method’s effectiveness.
*   The paper clearly identifies limitations and directions for future work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The tightness of the bounds is not rigorously analyzed.
*   The computational cost of the pseudo-input optimization is a potential concern.
*   The parameter identifiability issue needs further investigation.
*   The experimental validation could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution. The novelty and boldness of the approach outweigh the minor flaws. The authors have identified a critical bottleneck in DPP inference and proposed a clever solution. While further work is needed to fully characterize the performance and scalability of the method, the paper's potential impact is substantial. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the concerns regarding bound tightness and computational cost in future revisions. The work is a clear step forward in making DPPs more practical for real-world applications.
