### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the significant computational challenge of likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), namely the evaluation of the term `det(L+I)`, which typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix or operator `L`. The authors propose a novel approach that circumvents this requirement by deriving non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant. The core idea is inspired by inducing point methods in sparse Gaussian processes, where a low-rank matrix `Q` constructed from a small set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" is used to approximate `L`. The paper presents these bounds for DPPs on both finite sets and continuous domains. Subsequently, it demonstrates how these bounds can be integrated into standard inference frameworks: first, by using the lower bound as a variational objective for approximate maximum likelihood, and second, by enabling an exact-but-expensive Metropolis-Hastings MCMC sampler via retrospective sampling. The methods are evaluated on a synthetic 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP and a real-world spatial statistics dataset of diabetic neuropathy. The work positions itself as an alternative to previous methods like Affandi et al. (2014) [7], which rely on partial spectral knowledge.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Core Idea:** The central contribution—adapting the inducing variable paradigm from Gaussian processes to derive non-spectral bounds for the DPP likelihood—is both novel and elegant. It provides a new and principled way to tackle a well-known bottleneck in DPP inference.

2.  **Generality:** The proposed framework is quite general. It not only applies to the common case of DPPs on finite sets but is also thoughtfully extended to DPPs on continuous domains. This extension is particularly valuable, as spectral methods are often analytically intractable or computationally infeasible in continuous settings.

3.  **Practical Inference Schemes:** The paper successfully demonstrates how the derived bounds can be operationalized for two distinct and widely used inference paradigms (variational inference and MCMC). This provides practitioners with a flexible trade-off between computational cost and inferential accuracy.

4.  **Potential for Broader Impact:** By removing the dependency on spectral decomposition, this work could significantly broaden the applicability of DPP models to larger-scale problems and more complex domains where eigensolvers are prohibitive.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising core contribution, the manuscript is undermined by a significant number of flaws in its presentation, methodological clarity, and experimental rigor. These issues, while individually minor in some cases, collectively detract from the quality of the paper and raise concerns about its readiness for publication.

1.  **Poor Manuscript Preparation and Lack of Polish:** The paper suffers from a startling number of typographical and referencing errors, which severely impacts its readability and credibility.
    *   **Incorrect Equation Referencing:** There are multiple instances of incorrect equation numbering throughout the text. For example, Section 3.1 refers to maximizing "(8)" when the relevant equation is (6). Section 3.2 refers to "(7)" for the log-likelihood defined in (5), and later to "(13)" for the bounds in (8). This pattern continues in Section 4 with references to (14), (15), and (16) for equations (9), (10), and (11), respectively. This is a critical flaw that confuses the reader.
    *   **Typographical Errors:** A crucial formula in Section 2.1 contains a significant error: `detK{xi,xj} = K(xi,xi)K(yi,yi)−K(xi,xj)^2`. The term `K(yi,yi)` should clearly be `K(xj,xj)`.
    *   **Inconsistent Formatting:** The formatting of the bibliography is sloppy, with inconsistent styling and extraneous trailing commas in several entries (e.g., [1], [2], [11]). In-text citation style is also inconsistent (e.g., `[11],` vs. `[12]`).
    *   **Confusing Notation and Numbering:** The text mentions "Figure 5.1" when referring to what is labeled "Figure 1". The notation for the probability of a subset in Equation (1), `P(A ⊂ Y)`, is imprecise and inconsistent with the likelihood expression in Equation (2), `P(Y = Y1)`.

2.  **Insufficient Methodological Detail:** Crucial details required for reproducibility and for assessing the practical utility of the method are either glossed over or omitted entirely.
    *   The optimization of the pseudo-input locations `Z` is a key component of the method's performance. However, the paper provides no detail on how this optimization is performed, especially within the MCMC loop where the bounds are iteratively tightened. What algorithm is used? Is it guaranteed to find a good solution? What is its computational cost? Without this information, the true cost of the MCMC sampler cannot be compared to competitors like [7].
    *   The number of inducing points, `m`, is a critical hyperparameter. The paper's only guidance is an ad-hoc procedure of increasing `m` until the results stabilize. This lacks rigor and provides little practical advice for new users.

3.  **Incomplete Experimental Analysis:**
    *   The toy experiment in Section 5.1 correctly identifies a non-identifiability issue with the parameter `κ`. However, the analysis stops there. The paper simply concludes that "almost nothing is learnt about κ" and attributes it to the L-parametrization. This is a missed opportunity to discuss potential remedies (e.g., more informative priors, re-parametrization) or to more deeply analyze the limitations of the model.
    *   A direct, quantitative comparison of the computational cost and performance against the most relevant baseline, the spectral MCMC method of [7], is conspicuously absent. Claims of avoiding "prohibitively slow" power iterations need to be substantiated with empirical timing results that account for the cost of the proposed method's own sub-routines (i.e., optimizing `Z`).
    *   The paper notes the interesting emergent grid-like structure of the optimized inducing points but offers no analysis or intuition as to why this occurs. This observation could have led to deeper insights into the nature of the approximation.

### Recommendation

**Reject.**

While the paper introduces a clever and potentially significant idea for DPP inference, the current manuscript falls well short of the standards expected for NIPS. The high density of careless errors, from incorrect equation references to typos in core formulas, suggests a lack of diligence in the paper's preparation. Furthermore, the omission of key methodological details and the lack of a rigorous comparative experimental evaluation make it impossible to fully assess the practical advantages of the proposed method. The core contribution is strong, and I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise and polish their work for a future submission. However, in its current state, the paper is not ready for publication.
