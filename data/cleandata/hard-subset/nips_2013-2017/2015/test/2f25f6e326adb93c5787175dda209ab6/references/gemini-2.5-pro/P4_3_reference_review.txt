### Summary

This paper addresses the significant computational challenge of likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The primary bottleneck is the calculation of a determinant term, `det(L + I)`, which appears in the likelihood of the L-ensemble formulation of a DPP. Traditionally, this requires an expensive spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix `L` (for finite DPPs) or is analytically intractable (for continuous DPPs).

The authors' main contribution is a novel set of computationally efficient, non-spectral bounds on this determinant. Inspired by sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes using inducing variables [11], they introduce a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" to construct a low-rank approximation `Q` to the kernel matrix `L`. Using a known determinantal inequality [14], they derive both an upper and a lower bound on `det(L + I)` that depend only on kernel evaluations involving these pseudo-inputs. A key strength of this work is the extension of these bounds from the finite setting to the more general case of DPPs on continuous domains.

The paper then demonstrates how these bounds can be effectively integrated into two standard inference paradigms. First, the lower bound on the likelihood serves as a variational objective function, enabling fast, approximate inference by jointly optimizing over the model parameters and the locations of the pseudo-inputs. Second, the upper and lower bounds are used within a Metropolis-Hastings MCMC algorithm via retrospective sampling, a technique previously used with spectral bounds [7], to enable asymptotically exact Bayesian inference. The proposed methods are validated on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP, where the true likelihood is known, and on a real-world spatial point process dataset of nerve fibers in diabetic patients.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):** The paper is technically sound. The core theoretical contributions, Propositions 1 and 2, are derived by applying a known matrix inequality from Seiler and Simon [14] to the DPP likelihood, which is a valid and clever application. The subsequent use of these bounds in variational inference and MCMC follows established principles. The claims are well-supported by the derivations and the experimental results. The experiments are carefully designed: the toy problem provides a crucial sanity check against a ground truth, while the real-world example demonstrates practical utility.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and clearly stating their contributions. Structuring the paper by first introducing the simpler finite case (Section 2) before tackling the more technical continuous case (Section 4) makes the core ideas highly accessible. The connection to the well-known sparse Gaussian Process literature [11] provides helpful intuition for readers familiar with that area.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While DPP inference has been studied before [7, 8], the proposed approach of using non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds on the likelihood determinant is novel. It represents a significant departure from previous methods that relied on partial or full eigendecompositions [7]. The extension of this framework to continuous DPPs is also a novel and important contribution, as inference in this setting is particularly challenging.

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are significant and likely to be impactful. By circumventing the need for spectral information, the proposed methods have the potential to scale DPP models to much larger datasets than were previously feasible, particularly the variational approach. This could broaden the applicability of DPPs as a tool for modeling diversity and repulsion in various machine learning applications. The provision of both a fast approximate (VI) and a more robust exact (MCMC) inference method using the same underlying bounds is a powerful and practical result.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Practical Details of Pseudo-Input Optimization:** The performance of the bounds is critically dependent on the placement of the pseudo-inputs `Z`. The paper states that `Z` is optimized, but provides limited detail on the strategy and computational cost, especially within the MCMC framework. The paper notes that for MCMC, `Z` is re-optimized when a decision cannot be made. This could be computationally demanding. It would be beneficial to elaborate on: Is `Z` optimized from scratch at each such MCMC step? What optimization method is used? A discussion of the trade-off between the cost of this optimization and the frequency with which it is needed would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Handling of Parameter Identifiability in VI:** The experiments show that the VI approach struggled with the 1D toy problem due to identifiability issues with the scaling parameter `κ`. The authors correctly identify this as a known issue with the L-parametrization. However, given that VI is positioned as the scalable method of choice, this is a notable limitation. The paper could be improved by discussing whether this issue also affected the real-world neuropathy experiment and, more generally, by providing guidance on when the VI approach is likely to be reliable versus when the more expensive MCMC approach is necessary.

3.  **Quantitative Comparison to Baselines:** While the qualitative results on the neuropathy dataset align with previous findings [7], the paper would be stronger with a direct quantitative comparison to existing methods. For example, a comparison of the proposed MCMC method against the spectral-bound MCMC from [7] on a moderately sized problem could clearly demonstrate the trade-offs in terms of runtime, number of likelihood evaluations, and accuracy. This would help practitioners better understand the advantages of the non-spectral approach.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novel and significant contribution:** The paper introduces a new, non-spectral method for DPP likelihood approximation, addressing a key bottleneck in the field.
*   **Broad applicability:** The methods are applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs and support both variational and MCMC inference.
*   **Strong technical quality:** The theoretical derivations are sound and the methodology is well-founded.
*   **Clarity and presentation:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and easy to follow.
*   **Good empirical validation:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the correctness of the MCMC approach and the practical utility of the VI approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Limited experimental comparison:** The paper lacks a direct, quantitative comparison to the most relevant prior work (e.g., [7]), which would help to precisely situate the contribution in terms of performance trade-offs.
*   **Unaddressed limitations of VI:** The reported failure of VI on the toy problem raises questions about its robustness, which are not fully explored.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. The paper presents a novel, elegant, and significant contribution to an important problem in machine learning. The weaknesses identified are minor and could be addressed in a final version.
