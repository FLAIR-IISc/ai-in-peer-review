- Paper addresses the costly spectral decomposition requirement in parametric DPP inference by deriving likelihood bounds that avoid spectral knowledge.
- Proposes and analyzes non-spectral bounds usable for large-scale and continuous DPPs, and integrates them into VI and MCMC frameworks.
- Provides theoretical derivations grounded in determinant inequalities and extends results to continuous DPPs.
- Validates the approach experimentally on synthetic (with ground truth) and real-world datasets, including comparison to spectral bounds [7].
- Leverages sparse-GP-like techniques (pseudo-inputs) to make inference tractable without eigendecomposition.
- Strength: tackles a major practical bottleneck in DPP inference with a theoretically sound solution.
- Strength: experimental results and comparisons demonstrate practical effectiveness.
- Strength: paper is clearly written and well organized, with accessible exposition for non-experts in operator theory.
- Weakness: directly parameterizing L instead of K reduces interpretability of inferred parameters.
- Weakness: optimizing pseudo-inputs Z introduces additional computational cost that may scale poorly with dimensionality; trade-offs not fully explored.
- Weakness: limited comparison to other non-spectral or alternative DPP approximation methods.
- Weakness: continuous-case results depend on kernel/measure assumptions (e.g., Mercer-type conditions) whose practical implications could be discussed more.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, clear, original application of sparse-GP ideas to DPPs, and significant for scalable DPP inference.
- Recommendation: accept — benefits of avoiding spectral decomposition outweigh drawbacks (loss of interpretability and extra pseudo-input optimization cost).
