This paper addresses a significant bottleneck in the application of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the computational cost and analytical intractability of the `det(L+I)` term in the likelihood, which typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel operator `L`. The authors propose a novel and practical solution by deriving non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant. The core idea is inspired by variational methods for sparse Gaussian Processes (GPs), utilizing a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" `Z` to form a low-rank approximation of `L`. These bounds are then integrated into two standard inference paradigms: a fast but approximate variational inference (VI) scheme based on the lower bound, and an asymptotically exact Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) scheme using retrospective sampling. A key strength of the paper is its unified treatment of both finite and continuous DPPs, extending the applicability of the proposed methods. The approach is validated on a synthetic 1D continuous dataset, where the ground truth is known, and a real-world 2D spatial statistics problem involving diabetic neuropathy.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and critical problem in the DPP literature. Likelihood-based inference is fundamental for fitting parametric models, but the spectral bottleneck has limited the application of DPPs to cases with small finite domains or specific kernels where the spectrum is known. By providing a general, non-spectral approach, this work significantly broadens the scope of DPPs that can be practically learned from data.

2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Methodology:** The connection to sparse GPs is both insightful and powerful. Leveraging the well-established inducing point framework from the GP community provides a solid theoretical foundation and an intuitive understanding of the approximation. The idea of jointly optimizing the kernel parameters `θ` and the inducing point locations `Z` to tighten the variational bound is a standard and effective technique in that field, and its application here is a strong contribution.

3.  **Comprehensive Inference Framework:** The paper doesn't just propose bounds; it demonstrates their utility in two complementary inference settings. The VI approach offers a scalable, approximate solution suitable for large datasets, while the retrospective MCMC method provides a path to exact Bayesian inference, albeit at a higher computational cost. This provides practitioners with a valuable trade-off.

4.  **Strong and Honest Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed.
    *   The 1D Gaussian-Gaussian toy problem (Sec 5.1) is an excellent choice for validation because the true likelihood is computable. Showing that the retrospective MCMC sampler recovers the posterior from an "ideal" MH sampler provides convincing evidence of the method's correctness.
    *   The authors' honest discussion of the VI method's inconsistency on this toy problem and the identifiability issue with the `κ` parameter is commendable. It highlights the practical challenges of direct L-kernel parametrization and appropriately motivates the use of the more robust MCMC approach in such cases.
    *   The diabetic neuropathy experiment (Sec 5.2) demonstrates the method's applicability to a real-world problem, successfully reproducing the scientific findings of prior work [7, 22]. The analysis showing the convergence of the bounds and parameter estimates as `m` increases provides a practical guideline for choosing the number of inducing points.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Optimization:** The paper proposes jointly maximizing the lower bound `F(θ,Z)` but provides limited details on the practical aspects of this optimization. The joint optimization landscape can be complex and prone to local optima. More details on the optimizer used (gradient-based vs. black-box), initialization strategies for `Z`, and the stability of the results would strengthen the paper's reproducibility and practical guidance. For instance, in Sec 5.2, were gradients used? How were the inducing points initialized before optimization?

2.  **Originality vs. Adaptation:** While the application to DPPs is novel and significant, the core technical machinery (inducing point bounds derived from a determinant inequality) is a direct adaptation from the GP literature [11]. The paper is transparent about this inspiration, which is good, but the originality lies more in the novel application and extension to the DPP context rather than in the development of a fundamentally new mathematical tool. This is a minor point and does not detract significantly from the paper's value.

3.  **Comparison to Spectral Methods:** The paper argues that its non-spectral approach is superior to the spectral-bound method of [7], which requires computing a partial eigendecomposition. However, a more direct quantitative comparison would be beneficial. For instance, how does the computational time of optimizing the proposed bounds with `m` inducing points compare to the time taken by [7]'s method needing `m_eig` eigenvalues to make an MCMC decision? This would help practitioners better understand the performance trade-offs.

4.  **The L vs. K Parametrization Issue:** The paper correctly identifies the interpretability issue that arises from parametrizing the L-kernel directly instead of the more natural K-kernel. While this is a known problem that this work does not aim to solve, the experiments strongly highlight its practical consequences (e.g., the non-identifiability of `κ`). The discussion could briefly touch on whether these new non-spectral tools might open future avenues for tackling the L-to-K relationship, perhaps by framing it as an optimization problem.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the machine learning literature on probabilistic point processes. It solves a major practical problem with an elegant, well-motivated, and thoroughly validated approach. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to requests for additional detail rather than fundamental flaws.

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   Solves a long-standing computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
    *   The proposed non-spectral bounds are a clear improvement over previous methods requiring partial eigendecompositions.
    *   The methodology is sound, cleverly adapting a successful technique from the GP literature.
    *   The paper provides both fast/approximate and exact/slower inference methods.
    *   Experiments are convincing and demonstrate both correctness and practical utility.

*   **Arguments for Rejection:**
    *   The core mathematical technique is an adaptation of existing work, slightly limiting its fundamental originality.
    *   Some practical details of the optimization procedure could be elaborated further.

The arguments for acceptance far outweigh those against. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and presents results of high significance to researchers and practitioners working with DPPs. I strongly recommend acceptance.
