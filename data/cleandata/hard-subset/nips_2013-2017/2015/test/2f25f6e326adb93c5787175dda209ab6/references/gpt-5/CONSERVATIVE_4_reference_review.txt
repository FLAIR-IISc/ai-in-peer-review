Summary
The paper proposes nonspectral, low-rank bounds on the normalizing determinant det(I+L) that appears in DPP likelihoods, both for finite domains (matrices) and for continuous DPPs (Fredholm determinants). The bounds are constructed by introducing inducing inputs Z and a Nyström-like approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY (with Z not necessarily a subset of Y). For the finite case, the authors derive det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I), enabling a tractable lower bound on the log-likelihood and facilitating (i) a variational objective and (ii) a retrospective MH scheme that tightens bounds by increasing m and re-optimizing Z. They extend the construction to continuous DPPs via an analogous bound involving the matrix Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x). Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP (where the spectrum is known) and a small real dataset of nerve fiber spatial patterns; they show qualitative agreement of retrospective MH with “ideal” MH and that variational training yields class separation using overdispersion measures γd.

Strengths
- Addresses a real bottleneck: computing det(I+L) can be O(n^3) and/or require spectral knowledge (for continuous DPPs). Bounds that avoid eigendecompositions are potentially useful.
- The finite-domain bounds are reminiscent of established sparse GP ideas (Titsias 2009), giving them some credibility; the extension to continuous Fredholm determinants is natural.
- Retrospective MH with sandwich bounds is a sensible adaptation of ideas used in [Affandi et al., ICML’13], now without spectral requirements.
- The continuous Gaussian–Gaussian example is an appropriate sanity check (ground truth spectrum available) to validate the MCMC mechanism.
- The diabetic neuropathy experiment suggests the bounds tighten monotonically with m and that overdispersion parameters stabilize with larger m.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Lack of theoretical guarantees on tightness and convergence: The paper asserts that increasing m and optimizing Z “arbitrarily refines” the bounds, but provides no quantitative rates, no monotone nesting guarantee, and no proof that optimization over Z yields nonincreasing (or nondecreasing) intervals. This is central to the correctness and practical use of retrospective MH.
- Correctness of MH with adaptive, optimized bounds: Retrospective acceptance tests typically require deterministic, nested bounds as computation is increased, or unbiased estimators in pseudo-marginal schemes. Here, Z and Z′ are re-optimized at each proposal and as m grows. Without a proof that the resulting bounds form valid sandwiches that are independent of u and are nested as m increases, detailed balance and exactness of the sampler are unclear.
- Experimental scope is limited:
  - No finite-DPP experiment, despite the finite case being the primary ML use case (e.g., document summarization, subset selection). There is no comparison to spectral-based approaches in speed/accuracy or to direct Cholesky-based det(I+L) for moderate n.
  - The continuous experiments are small-scale (1D synthetic; a tiny real dataset with 7 samples). There is no quantitative evaluation of held-out likelihood, predictive performance, or parameter recovery beyond qualitative plots.
  - No direct comparison to Bornemann-style quadrature for Fredholm determinants [23], which is a strong baseline in the continuous case, or to the spectral-MH method of [7] in terms of wall-clock, acceptance rate, and accuracy.
- Variational method appears fragile: On the toy example, the variational approach is reported to be unreliable (non-identifiable κ, large variability across runs). This undermines the practical value of the variational contribution; no mitigations (e.g., priors, regularization, structured parameterizations) are explored.
- Practical limitations of the continuous case: The Ψ integral must be tractable. This is true for Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures, but many practical kernels/measures won’t admit closed forms; the paper provides no strategies (quadrature, low-rank features) in such cases.
- Claims about “spectral knowledge” are somewhat overstated for finite DPPs: det(I+L) can be computed via Cholesky without eigen-decomposition in O(n^3). The contribution is mainly about cheaper approximations, not avoiding spectra per se.
- Clarity/rigor: Several key proofs are deferred to the supplement. The connection to variational sparse GPs is asserted but not formalized (e.g., no explicit KL interpretation). Some notation and typos reduce clarity; references lack years and precise details.

Quality
- The finite and continuous bounds are plausible (they cite Seiler–Simon), but the paper’s main algorithmic contributions (variational training and retrospective MH with adaptive Z) lack rigorous guarantees. Empirical validation is too limited to compensate.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but important algorithmic details (e.g., ensuring nested bounds for MH, gradient expressions for Z, computational costs per iteration with T replicates) are missing. The supplement seems essential.

Originality
- Moderate. The determinant bounds are close in spirit to sparse GP bounds; extending to DPPs and to Fredholm determinants is incremental. Retrospective MH is known in this DPP context; the novelty is swapping in nonspectral bounds.

Significance
- Potentially useful for continuous DPPs where spectra are unavailable; however, the lack of strong theory and limited empirical evidence reduce immediate impact. For finite DPPs, the absence of large-scale experiments diminishes practical significance.

Questions and requests for the authors
- Can you prove that for fixed θ, the lower and upper bounds form nested intervals as m increases and Z is re-optimized (or at least non-widening), ensuring correct retrospective MH?
- Provide a correctness proof (detailed balance) for the MH scheme with adaptive m and optimized Z and Z′.
- Quantify bound tightness vs m (e.g., relative error in log det) for several kernels/datasets; provide rates or at least empirical scaling curves.
- Add finite-DPP experiments with large n, comparing to [7] and to exact Cholesky where feasible; report wall-clock, memory, acceptance rates, and posterior accuracy.
- Compare against Bornemann’s quadrature in the continuous case (both accuracy and runtime).
- Provide gradients and an efficient optimizer for Z and θ; explore regularization or constrained parameterizations to mitigate non-identifiability in the variational setting.
- Discuss applicability when Ψ is intractable; can numerical quadrature or random features be used with error control?

Pros
- Simple, potentially broadly applicable determinant bounds avoiding eigendecompositions.
- Extension to continuous DPPs is valuable where spectra are rarely available.
- Retrospective MH idea is practical and aligns with prior DPP inference work.

Cons
- Missing theoretical guarantees for the key retrospective MH component with adaptive optimized bounds.
- Limited, mostly qualitative experiments; no finite-domain benchmarks; no comparisons to strong baselines.
- Variational approach appears unreliable on even simple synthetic data.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is promising, but given my emphasis on methodological soundness and rigorous validation, the lack of guarantees for the MH scheme with adaptive Z and the limited empirical evaluation (especially for finite DPPs) make it premature for NeurIPS. Strengthening the theory around bound refinement and MH correctness, and adding comprehensive experiments and baselines, would significantly improve the case for acceptance. Confidence: high.
