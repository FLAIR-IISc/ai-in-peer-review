This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the evaluation of the `det(L + I)` term in the likelihood, which typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix/operator L. The authors propose a novel and elegant solution by drawing a strong analogy to sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes (GPs). They introduce non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant term using a set of "inducing points" or "pseudo-inputs". These bounds are computationally efficient, scaling as O(nm²) instead of O(n³), where `n` is the number of data points and `m` is the (much smaller) number of inducing points. The paper demonstrates how these bounds can be seamlessly integrated into two standard inference frameworks: approximate Variational Inference (VI) by maximizing the lower bound, and asymptotically exact Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) using a retrospective sampling scheme. The proposed methodology is developed for both DPPs on finite sets and, impressively, extended to the more theoretically demanding case of DPPs on continuous domains.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—adapting the inducing point formalism from sparse GPs to derive non-spectral bounds for the DPP likelihood—is highly original and significant. Previous work on scalable DPP inference, such as Affandi et al. (2014) [7], relied on partial spectral decompositions, which can still be slow and are primarily suited for finite domains. This work provides a completely different, non-spectral path to scalability that is applicable to both finite and continuous settings. This is a substantial advance that could make DPPs practical for a much wider range of large-scale problems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the bounds hinges on a non-trivial determinant inequality from Seiler & Simon (2012) [14], and its application to both the matrix and operator-theoretic settings appears correct. The experimental validation is well-conceived. The 1D toy experiment is an excellent sanity check, as the availability of the exact likelihood allows for a direct comparison with an "ideal" MCMC sampler, convincingly demonstrating the correctness of the proposed retrospective sampling approach. The real-world diabetic neuropathy experiment successfully demonstrates the method's practical utility on a spatial point process problem, replicating findings from prior work and showing empirical convergence of the bounds and parameters as `m` increases.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. Presenting the finite case first before tackling the more abstract continuous case is an effective pedagogical choice. The connection to sparse GPs is made explicit and serves as a helpful intuition pump for readers familiar with that literature. The descriptions of how to incorporate the bounds into VI and MCMC frameworks are clear and concise.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **The L vs. K Parametrization Issue:** The paper forthrightly acknowledges the standard practice of parametrizing the L-kernel for tractability rather than the more interpretable K-kernel. However, the first experiment highlights just how severe the consequences of this choice can be. The posterior for the intensity parameter `κ` is shown to be almost identical to the prior, indicating a severe lack of identifiability. While the authors correctly identify this as an issue with the L-parametrization itself, this is a major practical limitation of the overall approach presented. A more thorough discussion of this trade-off is warranted, as it significantly impacts the practical utility of the inferred parameters. The paper's contribution is on *inference*, but if the parameters being inferred are not identifiable or interpretable, the value is diminished.

2.  **Optimization of Inducing Inputs (Z):** The performance of the method relies critically on the quality of the inducing inputs `Z`. The paper states that `Z` is jointly optimized with the kernel parameters `θ`, mentioning methods like CMA-ES or gradient-based optimization. This is a non-trivial joint optimization problem, and the paper provides little detail or analysis on its practical aspects. How are the points in Z initialized? How sensitive is the final result to this initialization? The visualizations of the optimized `Z` (Figures 1a and 4) are intriguing, showing highly structured patterns, but the paper offers no insight into the dynamics of this optimization or potential pitfalls like local minima. A more detailed treatment of this crucial component would strengthen the paper considerably.

3.  **Lack of Direct Comparison to Baselines:** The primary motivation is to overcome the limitations of spectral methods, particularly the approach in Affandi et al. [7]. However, there is no direct empirical comparison between the proposed non-spectral bounds and their spectral counterparts. A comparison on a moderately-sized problem showing the trade-offs between wall-clock time, bound tightness, and final parameter accuracy for the two approaches would have been extremely valuable. Without this, the claimed advantages in computational efficiency remain somewhat abstract.

4.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** In Section 3.2, the text refers to equation (13) twice, but the relevant equation for the bounds on `log(α)` is (8). This appears to be a typo.

**Summary of evaluation:**

This is a strong paper with a novel, elegant, and potentially impactful contribution. The idea of using inducing points for non-spectral DPP inference is an excellent one that bridges two important areas of machine learning. The theoretical development is sound and the experimental validation is convincing. The primary weaknesses relate to a lack of depth in discussing practical challenges (the L vs. K problem, optimization of Z) and the absence of a direct comparison with the most relevant prior work. Despite these points, the strengths of the paper in terms of originality and significance are substantial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a genuinely novel, non-spectral approach to a key bottleneck in DPP inference.
*   The method significantly improves scalability, making DPPs accessible to larger problems.
*   The framework is general, covering both finite and continuous DPPs, and both VI and MCMC inference.
*   The connection drawn between sparse GPs and DPPs is insightful and elegant.
*   The work is technically sound and well-supported by carefully designed experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The paper largely sidesteps the severe parameter interpretability/identifiability issues arising from the L-kernel parametrization, which is a major practical caveat.
*   Lack of empirical comparison against the most relevant spectral-based baseline [7].
*   Insufficient detail on the practical aspects of optimizing the inducing point locations.

Overall, I recommend acceptance. The core contribution is strong enough to outweigh the weaknesses, which could be addressed in a final version of the paper or in future work. This paper will likely inspire follow-up research on scalable DPPs and the connections between DPPs and GPs.
