This paper addresses the challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition, a significant computational bottleneck. The core idea – leveraging bounds inspired by variational Gaussian processes – is a sensible direction, and the extension to continuous DPPs is a welcome addition. However, despite the technical soundness and clear presentation, I remain unconvinced of the *significance* of this work as it stands, primarily due to a lack of compelling evidence that these bounds offer a substantial practical advantage over existing methods, or that they unlock applications previously inaccessible due to computational constraints.

The paper is well-written and logically structured. The authors clearly articulate the motivations and challenges, and the mathematical derivations appear correct (though a more thorough examination of the supplementary material is warranted). The connection to variational inference and MCMC is clearly explained, and the extension to continuous domains feels natural. The inclusion of experimental validation is positive, though somewhat limited in scope.

However, my primary concern lies with the empirical evaluation. The toy example, while demonstrating correctness against a case where the spectral decomposition *is* available, doesn’t showcase a practical benefit. The authors acknowledge the lack of parameter identifiability (κ), a crucial weakness when advocating for parameter estimation. The diabetic neuropathy dataset experiment is more compelling, demonstrating convergence with varying numbers of inducing points. However, the comparison is primarily to *itself* (convergence as m increases) rather than to existing DPP inference techniques like those in Affandi et al. (2017) – even if those techniques require spectral methods.  A direct comparison demonstrating faster runtime or improved accuracy with these bounds, especially for larger datasets, is crucial. The authors state the variational inference is "relatively fast", but provide no quantitative comparison to alternative methods. This leaves me questioning whether the proposed approach truly alleviates the computational burden in a meaningful way.

Regarding originality, the core idea of using bounds to avoid spectral decomposition, while novel in the context of DPPs, is heavily inspired by existing work in Gaussian processes. The paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight what makes this application distinct or significantly more challenging than the GP setting.  The cited work on retrospective sampling for DPPs [7] already attempts to circumvent spectral computation, and a detailed comparison with that approach is missing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and mathematically rigorous.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Addresses a practical challenge in DPP inference.
*   Extension to continuous DPPs broadens applicability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of compelling empirical evidence demonstrating a significant practical advantage over existing methods.
*   Limited comparison to state-of-the-art approaches.
*   Originality is incremental, building heavily on existing GP techniques.
*   Identifiability issues highlighted in the toy example raise concerns about the usefulness of parameter estimation.
*   The significance of the bounds remains unclear without demonstration of enabling previously intractable applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the technical work is solid and the presentation is commendable, the lack of convincing empirical evidence and a clear demonstration of practical significance prevent me from recommending acceptance. The authors should focus on strengthening the experimental evaluation by including a thorough comparison with existing DPP inference methods and showcasing the ability of their approach to tackle larger, more realistic datasets that were previously inaccessible due to computational limitations.  Addressing the parameter identifiability issues would also strengthen the paper. Without these improvements, the contribution remains incremental and its impact on the field is uncertain.
