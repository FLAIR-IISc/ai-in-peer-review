

# Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that eliminate the need for spectral decomposition of the operator L, which is typically required for DPP inference. The approach is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and validated through experiments on both synthetic and real data.

## Summary and Context
The paper focuses on the computational challenge in DPP inference where likelihood evaluation requires spectral decomposition of operator L, making it prohibitive for large-scale applications. Previous work (e.g., [7]) addresses this by using spectral bounds but still requires eigenvalue computations. The authors' key insight is to derive bounds that don't rely on spectral knowledge at all, drawing inspiration from variational inference for Gaussian processes. They extend their approach from finite sets to continuous domains, providing a unified framework for DPP inference.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound. The theoretical contributions (Propositions 1 and 2) are derived from nontrivial determinant inequalities [14], properly referenced, and proofs are provided in the supplementary material. The experimental validation is thorough: the synthetic Gaussian example allows comparison with exact spectral methods, while the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrates practical utility. The authors honestly acknowledge the interpretability trade-off of parametrizing L instead of K (as noted in Section 1), showing appropriate awareness of limitations.

The main weakness is the lack of a detailed runtime comparison with existing methods (like [7]), which would strengthen the significance claim. While the paper mentions that their approach is "considerably lower" than power iteration, a concrete comparison would better demonstrate practical advantages.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly states the problem and contributions, and the authors provide a helpful roadmap for readers (noting that Section 4 can be skipped for those unfamiliar with operator theory). Technical content flows logically from definitions to theory to experiments. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce results, and the writing avoids unnecessary jargon.

## Originality
This work makes a clear, significant contribution to DPP literature. While some bounds for DPPs exist (e.g., [7]), this is the first to provide nonspectral bounds for both finite and continuous domains without requiring eigenvalue computations. The extension from finite to continuous domains is particularly novel and well-executed. The paper properly contextualizes itself within the literature, citing relevant work on DPPs [3, 4, 7] and variational inference [11].

## Significance
The problem addressed is highly significant: efficient DPP inference is crucial for applications like diversity modeling in news [3], search engines [7], and spatial statistics [4]. By removing the spectral decomposition bottleneck, this work enables DPP inference on larger datasets where previous methods would be intractable. The experimental results demonstrate that the approach achieves results comparable to spectral-based methods. The continuous domain extension broadens the applicability to important spatial statistics applications.

The paper could better emphasize practical impact by adding a runtime comparison with [7] in the experiments. The variational inference results in Section 5.1 (described as "poorly consistent") also need more explanation—perhaps by contrasting with the better MCMC results shown in the same section.

## Strengths vs. Weaknesses

*Strengths:*
- Theoretical contribution is sound and novel
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple domains
- Clear writing and organization
- Well-motivated problem statement
- Honest discussion of limitations (parametrizing L instead of K)

*Weaknesses:*
- Lack of detailed runtime comparison with existing methods
- Limited discussion of how to address interpretability issues
- Variational results described as "poorly consistent" without sufficient explanation

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to DPP inference by removing a major computational bottleneck. The theoretical results are well-supported, the experiments are thorough, and the paper is clearly written. The identified weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed in the camera-ready version.

The paper advances the state of the art in DPP inference in a demonstrable way, providing a practical solution to a real computational challenge. It would be valuable to the NIPS community, particularly for practitioners working with large-scale DPP applications. The work also opens interesting future directions, such as connections to quadrature-based bounds for Fredholm determinants [23].

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Add a runtime comparison table showing execution time vs. spectral-based methods [7]
2. Expand the discussion of interpretability trade-offs (Section 1) with potential mitigation strategies
3. Clarify the variational inference results in Section 5.1, perhaps by showing how MCMC performs better in the same context

Overall, this is a strong contribution that meets all NIPS quality criteria and would be a valuable addition to the conference proceedings.
