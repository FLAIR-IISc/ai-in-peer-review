### Review

**Summary:**
This paper addresses the computational challenge of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), which is dominated by the need to compute the determinant of an n x n matrix, `det(L + I)`, requiring an O(n^3) spectral decomposition. The authors propose novel, non-spectral bounds on this determinant for both finite and continuous DPPs. The proposed bounds are inspired by variational methods for sparse Gaussian Processes (GPs), utilizing a set of `m` "inducing points" or "pseudo-inputs" to form a low-rank approximation. The key contribution is a method to approximate the likelihood without any eigenvalue computations. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be incorporated into two standard inference frameworks: a fast but approximate variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes a lower bound on the log-likelihood, and a more computationally intensive but asymptotically exact Metropolis-Hastings (MH) MCMC algorithm using retrospective sampling. The methods are evaluated on a 1D synthetic dataset and a real-world 2D spatial point process dataset of nerve fibers.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novelty of the Approach:** The paper introduces a genuinely new approach to approximating the DPP likelihood. Moving away from spectral approximations (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014) towards a method inspired by inducing point GPs (Titsias, 2009) is an elegant and clever idea. This provides a new perspective on a well-known problem.
2.  **General Applicability:** The proposed bounds are applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs, and the paper provides a clear derivation for both settings. This breadth increases the potential impact of the work.
3.  **Versatility in Inference:** The authors demonstrate how the same set of bounds can be used to drive two different inference paradigms: a fast, approximate VI method and a slower, asymptotically exact MCMC method. This provides practitioners with a trade-off between computational cost and accuracy.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Lack of Comparison with Established Baselines:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the absence of any empirical comparison to existing methods for approximate DPP inference. The most relevant baseline is the MCMC method of Affandi et al. (2014), which also uses bounds (albeit spectral ones) to accelerate inference. Without a direct comparison in terms of computational time, accuracy, or stability, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed non-spectral approach offers any practical advantages over the established state-of-the-art. The paper motivates its contribution by highlighting the drawbacks of power iteration methods used in prior work but provides no experimental evidence to substantiate this claim.
2.  **Unconvincing Validation of the Variational Method:** The paper's own synthetic experiment (Section 5.1) reports that the proposed VI method "yields poorly consistent results," failing to identify model parameters. While the authors attribute this to identifiability issues inherent in the L-kernel parametrization, it severely undermines the credibility of the VI approach. If the method fails on a simple, controlled 1D problem, its reliability on the more complex real-world dataset (Section 5.2) is questionable. The real-data experiment shows that inferred parameters converge as the number of inducing points `m` increases, but without a ground truth or comparison to a more robust method (like the proposed MCMC), it's unclear if they converge to the *correct* values.
3.  **Limited Experimental Scale:** The work is motivated by the need to scale DPPs to large `n`. However, the experiments are conducted on datasets of modest size (the diabetic neuropathy data has ~70-90 points per sample). The performance and scalability of the proposed methods in the large-`n`, small-`m` regime, where they are intended to be most useful, are not demonstrated. The O(nm^2) complexity of the VI method can still be prohibitive if `n` is very large.
4.  **Practical Complexity:** The method introduces a new set of hyperparameters: the number of inducing points `m` and their locations `Z`. The paper proposes to optimize `Z` and increase `m` until the results stabilize. This introduces a significant and potentially complex tuning procedure that is not fully explored. The reliance on general-purpose black-box optimizers like CMA-ES for both kernel parameters and inducing point locations can be fragile and computationally intensive.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

-   **Quality:** The theoretical derivation of the bounds appears sound, leveraging a known determinant inequality. However, the experimental support for the claims is weak. The work feels incomplete due to the missing baseline comparisons and the demonstrated failure of the VI method in the synthetic experiment. A rigorous evaluation should compare against the method of Affandi et al. (2014) on a problem where both are applicable, measuring wall-clock time and posterior quality.
-   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The connection to sparse GPs is explained clearly, which helps readers familiar with that literature to understand the core mechanism. The distinction between the finite and continuous cases is also handled well.
-   **Originality:** The core idea of using non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds for the DPP likelihood is highly original. It is a significant departure from previous work in this specific area.
-   **Significance:** The potential significance is high, as an efficient and reliable method for DPP inference would be of great interest to the community. However, in its current state, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate that its proposed method is a practical advancement over existing techniques. The lack of rigorous validation makes it difficult for a reader to be confident in adopting this approach.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting idea for approximate inference in DPPs. The connection to sparse GPs is insightful, and the development of non-spectral bounds is a clear contribution. However, the work is let down by a weak and unconvincing experimental evaluation. The failure to compare against established, state-of-the-art approximation methods is a critical omission. Furthermore, the authors' own results cast doubt on the reliability of their proposed variational inference scheme. While the MCMC approach seems more promising, its advantages over existing MCMC schemes are not demonstrated. Therefore, while the core idea has merit, the paper in its current form does not meet the high standards of rigor and thorough validation expected at NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Reject
