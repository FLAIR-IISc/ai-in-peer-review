This paper addresses the problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition, a significant computational bottleneck, particularly for large datasets or continuous domains. The authors propose bounds on the DPP likelihood, inspired by variational inference in Gaussian Processes, and demonstrate their application to variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods, extending the results from finite to continuous DPPs.

The core idea – leveraging bounds to avoid explicit spectral decomposition – is conceptually sound and addresses a practical limitation in DPP inference. The derivation of Proposition 1 and Proposition 2, while relying on a somewhat opaque determinant inequality, is a key contribution. The extension to continuous DPPs is a valuable addition, broadening the applicability of the proposed method. The experimental validation, including a synthetic example and a real-world dataset (diabetic neuropathy), is commendable and provides some evidence of the method's effectiveness.

However, despite the potentially significant contribution, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet persistent, issues that collectively detract from its overall quality. The writing, while generally clear, is occasionally verbose and repetitive. For example, the discussion of the trade-offs between parametrizing K vs. L is reiterated multiple times. The notation is inconsistent; for instance, the use of both θK and θL for kernel parameters is confusing. More critically, the experimental section, while showcasing results, lacks sufficient detail. The hyperparameter settings for the variational inference and MCMC experiments are not fully specified, hindering reproducibility. The convergence diagnostics for the MCMC sampler are not reported, raising concerns about the reliability of the posterior samples. In the continuous DPP experiment, the link between the inferred parameters and the biological interpretation of nerve fiber clustering is superficial. 

The claim that the retrospective sampling approach is "simply adapted" from [7] is misleading. While the core idea is similar, the application to non-spectral bounds introduces nuances not present in the original work. Furthermore, the choice of the proposal distribution in the MCMC section (Gaussian with adaptive covariance) is presented without justification. A more rigorous justification for its appropriateness would strengthen the paper. The connection to quadrature-based bounds mentioned in the discussion section feels tacked on and underdeveloped.

A particularly frustrating aspect is the meticulous attention to detail *within* the paper, contrasted with a lack of meticulousness *regarding* the overall presentation. Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies exist (e.g., uneven spacing in equations, inconsistent use of boldface), which, while individually insignificant, contribute to a sense of sloppiness. The phrase “Similarly…” is overused, particularly at the beginning of sections.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant practical challenge in DPP inference.
*   Provides novel and theoretically sound bounds on the likelihood.
*   Extends the method to continuous DPPs.
*   Demonstrates proof-of-concept validation on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The writing is verbose and repetitive.
*   The notation is inconsistent.
*   The experimental section lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility.
*   Convergence diagnostics are missing for the MCMC sampler.
*   Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies detract from the paper's overall polish.
*   The discussion section contains underdeveloped ideas.
*   The claim of "simply adapting" a technique is misleading.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a technically sound idea with the potential for significant impact, the numerous minor flaws and lack of thoroughness in the experimental validation and presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper feels incomplete and requires substantial revision to meet the standards of NIPS. I believe a rejection is warranted at this time, with strong encouragement to address the issues outlined above and resubmit in the future. The core contribution is valuable, but the execution is subpar.
