Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses a central bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the L-ensemble operator to evaluate the normalizer det(I+L). The authors propose nonspectral, inducing-point-based bounds for this determinant in both finite and continuous settings, enabling two inference schemes: (a) variational inference via a lower bound on the log-likelihood and (b) exact Bayesian inference via retrospective Metropolis–Hastings that uses upper/lower bounds to decide accept/reject events without computing eigenvalues.

The finite-domain bound uses a projection Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY (with Z arbitrary pseudo-inputs) to obtain det(Q+I)^{-1} as an upper bound and a trace-corrected quantity det(Q+I)^{-1}·exp(tr(L−Q)) as a lower bound on 1/det(I+L), following determinant inequalities of Seiler & Simon (1975). This mirrors variational sparse GP ideas (Titsias, AISTATS’09) but applied to the DPP normalizer rather than a GP marginal likelihood. The continuous-domain bound uses Fredholm determinants and a computable Ψ = ∫ L(z_i, x)L(x, z_j) dµ(x), yielding analogous nonspectral bounds; Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures make Ψ analytic, connecting to Bornemann (2010) for numerical Fredholm determinants.

The paper relates to prior NeurIPS/ICML work on DPP learning via spectral methods (Affandi et al., ICML’14; Gillenwater et al., NIPS’14; Mariet & Sra, NIPS’15), Nyström approximations (Affandi et al., AISTATS’13), and application-driven DPPs (Kulesza & Taskar, FnT ML). The novelty is dispensing with spectral decompositions entirely and extending to continuous DPPs where spectra are typically unavailable.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, general, and computationally attractive nonspectral bounds derivable from well-known determinant inequalities.
- Works in both finite and continuous domains; the latter is particularly valuable where spectral knowledge is rare.
- Clean connection to sparse GP methodology; computational complexity scales as O(n m^2) for the normalizer term, with m controllable.
- Two inference modes: a variational lower bound and an exact MCMC scheme via retrospective acceptance, with a clear tightening mechanism (increase m and optimize Z).
- Empirical demonstrations: a synthetic 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where exact spectra enable validation of the MCMC, and a real 2D spatial dataset showing convergent estimates and class separation consistent with prior work.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical breadth and comparison. No finite-domain large-n experiment (e.g., item catalogs) and no direct runtime/accuracy comparisons to spectral methods (e.g., power iteration–based truncations) or other recent DPP learners.
- Tightness and convergence properties of the bounds are discussed informally; there is no formal monotonicity/tightness guarantee as m increases or as Z is optimized, nor rates.
- Optimization over pseudo-inputs Z is nonconvex; the paper shows nice behavior but lacks analysis or robust initialization strategies. Variational results can be inconsistent in the synthetic example, underscoring sensitivity.
- Practicality in general continuous settings hinges on the ability to compute Ψ analytically or efficiently; beyond Gaussian–Gaussian, this may be restrictive.
- Some clarity/notation issues (inconsistent equation numbering, small typos) and missing details (e.g., explicit proof that L−Q is PSD, conditions ensuring the determinant inequality applies).

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The propositions are plausible and likely correct under standard PSD ordering (Q ≤ L), but key details (PSD property, assumptions for continuous operators) should be stated explicitly. The MCMC scheme is principled via retrospective sampling. Empirical validations support correctness but are limited in scope and do not quantify trade-offs versus spectral baselines.
- Clarity: Generally well written, with a good high-level narrative and connections to sparse GPs. However, some notation/numbering slips and missing technical conditions reduce reproducibility. More algorithmic specifics (gradients for Z, initialization, computational/memory costs per step) would help practitioners.
- Originality: Applying inducing-point determinant bounds to DPP normalizers, and extending them to Fredholm determinants for continuous DPPs, is novel and neat. The methodological framing (variational + retrospective MH without spectra) is original in the DPP literature.
- Significance: Potentially impactful, especially for continuous DPPs and large discrete problems where spectra are inaccessible. However, the current empirical evidence is not strong enough to fully establish practical superiority or robustness, which tempers immediate significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general idea with potential to broaden DPP inference beyond spectral regimes.
- Solid theoretical grounding via determinant inequalities and operator theory.
- Dual inference pathways with a clear tightening knob (m and Z).
- Positive real-data results consistent with prior findings.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is narrow; lacks comparisons to established spectral methods or recent scalable DPP learners on standard benchmarks.
- Missing formal analysis of bound tightness/monotonicity and optimization behavior for Z.
- Practicality beyond Gaussian–Gaussian continuous models is unclear without Ψ tractability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal statements showing Q ≤ L (PSD order) and the precise conditions under which the determinant inequalities apply, both finite and continuous. Clarify assumptions (trace-class, (9) validity) and give guidance for verifying them in practice.
- Add experiments on a large finite-domain DPP (e.g., document or product diversity) comparing against spectral MH/EM and Nyström-based learners, with wall-clock and accuracy metrics. Include ablations vs m, n, and d.
- Analyze tightness empirically: convergence of bounds as m grows, effect of Z optimization vs random/k-means/blue-noise initializations, and monotonicity under nested Z.
- Provide gradients for Z and θ, and a recommended optimization scheme; consider regularizers or repulsive priors for Z to stabilize training.
- Discuss and demonstrate applicability when Ψ is not analytic (e.g., numerical quadrature, random features).
- Address identifiability and interpretability when parametrizing L (e.g., priors, reparameterizations) and relate to K-parametrized DPPs.

Recommendation: Weak Reject. The idea is promising and, with stronger empirical validation and clearer theory/algorithms, could make a valuable NeurIPS contribution. As it stands, the scope and comparisons are too limited for a strong case, but I encourage resubmission with the above enhancements.
