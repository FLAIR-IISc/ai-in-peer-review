Summary
This paper tackles a central bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): evaluating log det(I+L) (or its Fredholm analogue) typically requires spectral decompositions that are computationally heavy in the finite case and analytically intractable in the continuous case. The authors propose nonspectral, computable upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) based on a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY formed using a set of m “pseudo-inputs” Z (not necessarily a subset of the items). The bounds follow from a determinant inequality (Seiler–Simon), yielding for the finite case:
det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I).
They derive analogous bounds for continuous DPPs in terms of operator traces/Fredholm determinants, with Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dμ(x). They use these bounds (i) to form a variational lower bound on the log likelihood (summing det(LYt) terms for observed subsets and bounding the partition term), and (ii) in a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme where acceptance decisions are made using progressively tightened bounds by increasing m and re-optimizing Z. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where spectral quantities are available for validation, and a 2D neuropathy dataset where the variational method is used to estimate overdispersion parameters across two classes.

Relation to prior work: This paper builds on L-ensemble DPP inference (e.g., Gillenwater et al., Mariet & Sra), spectral-bounds MCMC for DPPs (Affandi et al., 2013), Nyström approximations for DPPs (Affandi et al., 2014), and variational sparse Gaussian processes (Titsias, 2009). It complements numerical techniques for Fredholm determinants (Bornemann, 2010) by giving bounds that can be optimized with inducing inputs and avoid spectral decompositions.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and practicality: A clean, spectral-free bounding strategy for det(I+L) that is applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs, with a natural interpretation via inducing points analogous to sparse GP methods.
- Generality: The construction does not require Z ⊂ Y, avoids storing the full n×n matrix, and extends to continuous domains when Ψ is tractable (e.g., Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures).
- Two inference modes: The bounds are leveraged for both variational (fast, approximate) and retrospective MCMC (exact in the limit), showing versatility.
- Empirical validation: The 1D Gaussian–Gaussian example cross-checks the retrospective MH against an “ideal” MH using exact Fredholm determinants. The neuropathy dataset demonstrates scalable variational training and convergence of overdispersion summaries with m.
- Clarity of the main idea: The analogy to Titsias’ sparse GP bound is well-motivated and helps intuition (Q as the rank-m projector and tr(L−Q) as a correction).

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical guarantees on tightness/convergence are not established. While the paper asserts that bounds can be “arbitrarily refined” by increasing m, formal conditions ensuring that upper and lower bounds converge to the true determinant (finite and continuous cases) are not provided. In the finite case, exactness requires Z=Y (m=n); if Z∉Y, exact recovery may not be possible. In the continuous case, a theorem with conditions on L, μ, and a sequence of Zm would strengthen the claims (e.g., monotone tightening and rates).
- Validity of retrospective MH with adaptively optimized Z: The acceptance decision uses bounds that depend on Z re-optimized per (θ,θ′). While the retrospective logic can be valid if the acceptance probability is computed exactly with a stopping rule depending only on bounds and a single uniform u, the paper does not provide a correctness argument under optimization of auxiliary variables. A short justification (or citation) that such deterministic tightening preserves detailed balance/invariance would be important.
- Computational complexity details are incomplete. For the finite case, evaluating the numerator det(LYt) still costs O(|Yt|^3) per realization; in settings with large selections Yt, this can dominate. The claimed O(n m^2) for the bound omits this cost and assumes efficient computation of tr(L−Q); a derivation of the per-iteration cost (including storage) and when the approach is advantageous over alternatives would help. Numerical stability and conditioning of LZ (need for jitter) are not discussed.
- Scope of Ψ computability in the continuous case: The method hinges on closed-form or efficient numerical evaluation of Ψ. Beyond Gaussian–Gaussian, guidance is limited. Comparing against Bornemann’s quadrature for Fredholm determinants (as mentioned in the discussion) would contextualize when each approach is preferable.
- Missing comparisons to modern log-det approximations. Stochastic Lanczos quadrature and related Hutchinson-based log-det approximations could be competitive spectral-free alternatives for det(I+L) in the finite case; a brief comparison (conceptual or empirical) would increase relevance.
- Empirical breadth: The finite-case setting (e.g., document summarization, image diversity) is not empirically evaluated, nor are comparisons to EM/fixed-point DPP learners or spectral-bounds MCMC on discrete problems. The neuropathy experiment shows parameter convergence but not predictive or likelihood-based comparisons.
- Presentation/clarity: Some equation numbering and cross-references are inconsistent; a few footnotes are misaligned. The statement linking GP posterior variance vanishing to bound tightness is informal and could be replaced by a precise lemma or removed.

Quality
Technically sound derivation of determinant bounds via Seiler–Simon, with reasonable extensions to operators. The inference schemes are standard once the bounds are available. However, theoretical claims about convergence/tightness and MH validity under adaptive Z optimization need either proofs or precise conditions. Experiments are informative but limited in breadth.

Clarity
The main ideas are communicated clearly, but the manuscript would benefit from: consistent equation numbering, a careful complexity discussion, and a more rigorous treatment of convergence and MH correctness. Practical details (e.g., regularization of LZ, gradients for Z and θ) would help reproducibility.

Originality
High in the DPP context: nonspectral determinant bounds with inducing points seem new, and the operator extension is nontrivial. The link to sparse GP variational ideas is elegant and well-executed.

Significance
Potentially impactful: enabling scalable DPP inference without eigendecompositions is valuable for both discrete and continuous applications. With stronger theoretical guarantees and broader experiments, this could become a go-to approach.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, general, and practically useful bounds avoiding spectral decompositions.
- Applies to both finite and continuous DPPs, with clear algorithmic use in VI and MCMC.
- Solid intuition via sparse GP analogy; empirical validation on continuous case.

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of theoretical guarantees on tightness/convergence and MH correctness under adaptive Z.
- Limited experimental scope and comparisons, especially for discrete domains.
- Incomplete complexity/stability discussion; some clarity issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a theorem (with assumptions) on convergence of bounds as m→∞ and, if possible, monotonicity in m or Z-optimization steps.
- Justify retrospective MH correctness when Z is optimized per proposal; clarify stopping rules and invariance.
- Add discrete-domain experiments with comparisons to EM/fixed-point learners and spectral-bounds MCMC; report time/accuracy trade-offs.
- Discuss numerical stability (conditioning of LZ, jitter) and provide gradient expressions or autodiff details.
- Compare conceptually with stochastic log-det approximations; clarify regimes where the proposed method excels.
- Fix equation numbering and tighten the presentation.

Overall: A strong and promising contribution that would benefit from additional theory and broader empirical validation.
