Summary
This paper proposes nonspectral, computable upper and lower bounds on the normalizing determinant det(I+L) that appears in DPP likelihoods, for both finite ground sets and continuous domains. The key idea is to introduce inducing inputs Z and approximate L by the low-rank matrix/operator Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, in the spirit of variational sparse GP methods (Titsias, 2009). Using an inequality on determinants (Seiler & Simon, 1975), the authors derive bounds of the form det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I) in the finite case, and analogous Fredholm determinant bounds in the continuous case involving Ψ = ∫ L(z,·)L(·,z′) dµ. These bounds enable: (i) a tractable variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood that can be maximized jointly over kernel parameters and inducing inputs; and (ii) a retrospective MH sampler that avoids eigenvalue computations by refining bounds adaptively in m. Experiments on a synthetic 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP (where the spectrum is known) validate the MH approach, and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrates that the variational method yields coherent overdispersion estimates as m increases.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and elegance: brings the inducing-point variational GP toolkit to DPPs to derive nonspectral determinant bounds. Extends to continuous domains with Fredholm determinants—an area where ML work has mostly relied on spectra or special cases.
- Correctness and intuition: For finite DPPs, Q ≼ L and the bounds match known determinant monotonicity; the exponential correction via tr(L−Q) is reasonable and connects to GP posterior variance.
- Practicality: Avoids storing or decomposing n×n matrices; main costs are O(n m^2) with m ≪ n, and kernel integrals Ψ for certain continuous models (e.g., Gaussian kernel/base measure) are computable.
- Algorithms: Clear integration into a variational lower bound and a retrospective MH scheme; the latter is a principled adaptation of earlier spectral-MH for DPPs.
- Empirical indications: Synthetic experiment shows MH recovers identifiable parameters without spectra; continuous real-data application illustrates convergence of estimates and bounds with m and reasonable runtimes.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical evaluation and baselines: No quantitative comparison to spectral methods (e.g., Affandi et al. ICML’13) on finite domains, nor to other parametric learning algorithms (EM, fixed-point). Scalability and accuracy trade-offs versus eigen-based approaches are not characterized.
- Dependence on inducing-point optimization without theory: Tightness and monotonicity of bounds as m grows (and Z is optimized) are only observed empirically. No guarantees (e.g., nondecreasing lower bound, nonincreasing upper bound, convergence as m→∞) are provided.
- Variational method evaluation is inconclusive: On the toy 1D example, the variational approach performs poorly (κ not identifiable), but the paper stops at this diagnosis. Alternative parameterizations (e.g., fixing κ, reparameterizing L to isolate scale, or priors/regularization) or reporting gradient-based optimization details could have clarified whether the issue is identifiability or looseness of the bound/optimization.
- Continuous case assumptions are under-specified: The operator-theoretic conditions (trace-class, equality of trace to ∫ L(x,x) dµ, invertibility/conditioning of LZ, extension of determinant inequality to operators) are discussed informally. Precise, checkable assumptions and proof sketches in the main text would strengthen confidence.
- Clarity/notation issues: Some equation numbering inconsistencies, footnote-like sentences embedded in the main text, and minor typos in Section 4 make it harder to follow. The construction and optimization of Z (initialization, constraints, gradient expressions) are not described in sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Quality
- The finite-domain bounds are technically sound and consistent with known matrix inequalities; the variational objective is a natural analogue of Titsias’ bound. The continuous-domain extension seems plausible given the cited operator inequality, but key details are deferred. The MH scheme is standard once bounds are available.
- Experimental support is suggestive rather than conclusive. The MH validation on a case with known spectrum is appropriate; however, the absence of head-to-head comparisons limits assessment of practical gains.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, with an effective motivation and placement in the DPP literature. Sections 2–3 are clear; Section 4 would benefit from tighter assumptions and notation. Minor editorial issues should be addressed.

Originality
- The nonspectral determinant bounds via inducing approximations for DPPs, and their extension to Fredholm determinants, appear novel. The connection to sparse GPs is insightful. The use of retrospective MH with these bounds is a natural, but meaningful, extension.

Significance
- If broadly applicable and robust, this approach removes a central bottleneck in DPP inference, especially for continuous domains or large finite ground sets. The potential impact is high, but stronger empirical evidence and theoretical guarantees would help establish it as a go-to method.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical properties of the bounds as m increases (e.g., monotonicity and convergence under mild conditions), and guidance on choosing/optimizing Z (initialization strategies such as k-means, sampling from µ, or greedily maximizing the lower bound).
- Add comparisons on finite problems against spectral MH/EM/fixed-point methods, reporting runtime, memory, and accuracy versus m and n.
- Clarify continuous-domain assumptions and give a brief proof sketch of Proposition 2 in the main text; discuss numerical stability of LZ inversion and regularization.
- For the variational method, mitigate identifiability issues (e.g., fix κ or reparameterize scale) and provide gradient formulas for θ and Z to enable reproducibility.
- Clean up notation and equation references; move footnotes and asides out of the main text.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and potentially impactful technique to avoid spectral decompositions in DPP inference.
- Solid finite-dimensional derivation grounded in known inequalities; meaningful continuous-domain extension.
- Practical algorithms (variational and MH) that can be adopted by practitioners.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation and lack of comparisons to existing methods.
- Missing theoretical guarantees on bound behavior with m and Z.
- Some clarity and rigor gaps in the continuous case.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents an elegant and promising idea with clear potential for the community, but would benefit from stronger empirical comparisons and more rigorous treatment of the continuous case and inducing-point optimization.
