- Proposes the first nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods that remove the need for spectral decomposition, applicable to finite and continuous domains.  
- Derives mathematically sound bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) using a nontrivial determinant inequality.  
- Adapts pseudo-input (inducing point) ideas from Gaussian processes to DPP inference, avoiding eigenvalue computations.  
- Demonstrates practical effectiveness on synthetic and real data (diabetic neuropathy), showing bounds tighten as pseudo-inputs increase.  
- Validates the approach with both variational inference and MCMC, showing theoretical and empirical benefits.  
- Clearly situates the work within existing DPP and GP literature and contrasts with spectral-based methods like [7].  
- Lacks direct computational comparisons with prior spectral-based methods on similar datasets to quantify efficiency gains.  
- Does not provide theoretical guidance on optimal pseudo-input selection or how Z choice affects bound tightness and cost.  
- Mentions interpretability concerns of parametrizing L vs K but does not explore how the new bounds address interpretability.  
- The continuous DPP extension is mathematically justified but could better motivate why Proposition 2 is the natural analogue of the finite case.  
- Overall quality: technically rigorous with thorough experiments and careful acknowledgment of limitations.  
- Clarity: well-organized and clearly written, with helpful connections to GP literature; continuous DPP section could be more accessible.  
- Originality: novel nonspectral approach to DPP inference and a novel extension to continuous domains.  
- Significance: removes a key computational bottleneck, enabling DPP inference for previously infeasible real-world applications.  
- Recommendation: recommend acceptance—strong theoretical and practical contribution—while noting needs for stronger empirical comparisons and more guidance on pseudo-inputs.
