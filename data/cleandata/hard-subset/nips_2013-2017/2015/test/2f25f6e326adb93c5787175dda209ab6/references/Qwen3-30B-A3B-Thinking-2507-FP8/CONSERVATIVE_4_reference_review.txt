

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper proposes methods for likelihood-based inference of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) that avoid the computationally expensive spectral decomposition of the operator L, which is typically required for likelihood evaluation. The authors derive nonspectral bounds on the likelihood for both finite and continuous DPPs, inspired by similar bounds used in Gaussian process inference. These bounds are then incorporated into variational inference and MCMC algorithms. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a connection to the Gaussian process literature that is helpful for readers familiar with that field.

**Strengths:**
- The paper clearly identifies a real computational bottleneck in DPP inference (the need for spectral decomposition) and proposes a potentially useful approach to address it.
- The mathematical derivations are rigorous and well-supported by references to existing determinant inequalities.
- The connection to variational sparse Gaussian processes is well-explained and provides valuable context.
- The experiments on synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness of this paper is the insufficient evidence supporting the practical advantages of the proposed method. While the paper claims to provide "cheap-to-evaluate, nonspectral bounds," it fails to demonstrate that these bounds are sufficiently tight or computationally advantageous compared to existing approaches. Specifically:

1. The paper does not provide a thorough comparison with the most relevant prior work, [7], which also addresses the spectral decomposition problem in DPP inference. The authors mention [7] but do not systematically compare their bounds to those in [7] in terms of tightness, computational cost, or accuracy. Without such a comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers a meaningful improvement.

2. The experimental validation is limited in scope. The synthetic experiment (Section 5.1) shows that retrospective MCMC with the proposed bounds agrees with "ideal" likelihood-based methods, but this is expected since the spectral decomposition is known in this special case. The real dataset experiment (Section 5.2) shows convergence of overdispersion measures as the number of inducing variables increases, but it doesn't establish whether the proposed method achieves comparable or better results than existing methods with fewer computational resources.

3. The paper lacks analysis of the trade-off between the number of pseudo-inputs (m) and the tightness of the bounds. The authors note that they increase m when acceptance decisions cannot be made, but they don't quantify how much better the proposed method performs compared to alternatives (like the spectral bounds in [7]) for a given computational budget.

4. The paper doesn't address the interpretability issues mentioned in Section 1 when parametrizing L directly rather than K. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't provide evidence that their method overcomes this issue or demonstrate how the proposed bounds affect the interpretability of the inferred parameters.

5. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the proposed bounds are practically useful for large-scale applications. The experiments use small datasets (e.g., 13 points in the synthetic example, 67-90 points in the real dataset), which are not representative of the large-scale applications where spectral decomposition would be most problematic.

**Significance and Originality:**
While the problem addressed is important, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. The bounds themselves are inspired by existing work in Gaussian process literature, and the application to DPPs doesn't introduce fundamentally new mathematical techniques. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this approach is substantially better than existing methods for practical DPP inference. The connection to Gaussian processes is valuable, but the paper doesn't establish that the proposed method offers advantages over the established approaches in that domain.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a technically sound approach to a well-known problem in DPP inference, but it falls short of NeurIPS standards by not providing sufficient evidence to support its claims. The authors have not thoroughly validated their approach against established baselines, and the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed method. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison with [7] (the most relevant prior work), an analysis of the trade-off between computational cost and accuracy, and experiments on larger-scale datasets that better reflect the intended applications.

Without stronger evidence of practical advantages over existing methods, this paper does not advance the state of the art sufficiently to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS. While the problem is important and the approach is technically sound, the lack of thorough validation against established baselines and the absence of compelling evidence demonstrating practical advantages make this paper unsuitable for publication at NeurIPS in its current form.
