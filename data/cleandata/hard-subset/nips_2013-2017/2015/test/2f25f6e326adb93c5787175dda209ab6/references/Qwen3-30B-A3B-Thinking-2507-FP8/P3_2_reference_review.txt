

# Peer Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary and Context
This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference: the requirement for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L, which is typically intractable for large-scale or continuous domains. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid this need, building on variational approaches from Gaussian processes (GP) literature [11]. They derive bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, then demonstrate their use in variational inference and MCMC. The work connects to prior DPP inference methods [7, 8, 9] but offers a more general approach that doesn't require spectral knowledge.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper provides a genuinely novel theoretical contribution with Propositions 1 and 2, establishing nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood. These bounds extend variational GP techniques to DPPs in a nontrivial way, with careful mathematical justification.

2. **Practical Impact**: The proposed bounds significantly reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(nm²) for finite domains (with m ≪ n), making DPP inference feasible for larger datasets. The authors demonstrate this with experiments on both synthetic and real-world diabetic neuropathy data.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The synthetic 1D Gaussian-Gaussian example validates the MCMC approach against "ideal" likelihood-based methods, while the real dataset shows clear separation between classes with increasing m (number of inducing points), demonstrating convergence of the variational approach.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear connections to GP literature, well-structured sections, and careful explanation of the theoretical contributions. The supplementary material provides the necessary proofs.

## Weaknesses and Limitations

1. **Parametrization Trade-off**: The paper acknowledges the interpretability issue of parametrizing L instead of K (Section 1), but doesn't adequately address whether this trade-off is justifiable. The experiments in Section 5.1 show poor identifiability of κ, which is a direct consequence of this parametrization choice. The paper could better justify why parametrizing L is preferable despite these issues.

2. **Limited Comparison**: The paper compares against [7] but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with other DPP inference methods (e.g., [8, 9]) that might use different approximations. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

3. **Theoretical Limitations**: The bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 are derived under specific assumptions (e.g., L must be trace-class for continuous domains). While the authors acknowledge these, they don't thoroughly discuss the practical limitations of these assumptions for real-world applications.

4. **Computational Cost of Optimization**: The retrospective MCMC approach requires optimizing over pseudo-inputs Z at each iteration. The paper mentions this is feasible for the examples, but doesn't provide complexity analysis or discuss how the optimization overhead scales with problem size.

## Significance Assessment

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the DPP inference literature by removing a major computational barrier. The proposed bounds are general and applicable to a wide range of DPP applications. However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the parametrization trade-off, which affects the interpretability of the inferred parameters. While the paper demonstrates the computational benefits, it doesn't fully address whether the resulting models are as useful for practitioners as models parametrized by K.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper presents a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications for DPP inference. The methodology is sound, the experiments are well-executed, and the writing is excellent. The main weakness is the insufficient discussion of the parametrization trade-off, which is a critical issue for practitioners. The authors should address this in the revision by:

1. More thoroughly discussing the interpretability limitations of parametrizing L instead of K
2. Providing a clearer justification for why the computational benefits outweigh these limitations in practical applications
3. Adding a brief comparison with alternative DPP inference approaches

The paper is technically sound, addresses a real problem in the field, and advances the state of the art in DPP inference. The significance is strong for the machine learning community working with repulsive point processes, and the results are likely to be widely adopted by practitioners.

## Final Assessment

*Quality*: 9/10 - Strong theoretical contribution with well-executed experiments
*Clarity*: 9/10 - Exceptionally well-written and organized
*Originality*: 8/10 - Novel application of GP techniques to DPPs, though bounds themselves are inspired by GP work
*Significance*: 8/10 - Addresses a real computational bottleneck in DPP inference, though parametrization trade-offs limit immediate practical adoption

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and deserves publication at NeurIPS, with minor revisions addressing the parametrization discussion.
