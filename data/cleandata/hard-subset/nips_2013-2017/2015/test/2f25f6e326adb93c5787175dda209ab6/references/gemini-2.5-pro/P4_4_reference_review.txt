### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the calculation of the term `det(L + I)`, which typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix L. This is computationally prohibitive for large finite sets (O(n^3)) and often analytically intractable for continuous domains.

The main contribution is the introduction of novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on `det(L + I)`. Drawing inspiration from sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes, the authors use a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" to construct a low-rank approximation to the kernel matrix L. This leads to bounds on the likelihood that can be computed efficiently in O(nm^2) time for the finite case, avoiding any eigenvalue computations. The paper provides these bounds for both DPPs on finite sets (Proposition 1) and on continuous domains (Proposition 2).

The authors then demonstrate how these bounds can be leveraged for parametric inference within two standard frameworks:
1.  **Variational Inference (VI):** The derived lower bound on the log-likelihood serves as a tractable objective function (an ELBO) that can be maximized jointly with respect to the kernel parameters and the locations of the inducing points. This provides a fast, scalable method for approximate inference.
2.  **Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC):** The bounds are integrated into a Metropolis-Hastings (MH) algorithm using a "retrospective sampling" scheme. This allows the algorithm to make an accept/reject decision without computing the true likelihood, by adaptively tightening the bounds (by increasing the number of inducing points) only when necessary. This enables asymptotically exact Bayesian inference.

This work is positioned as a direct improvement over previous likelihood-based inference methods for DPPs, such as Affandi et al. (2013), which also used bounds within an MCMC sampler but required the computation of a partial eigenspectrum, a potentially slow process that is limited to finite domains. By contrast, the proposed method is entirely "non-spectral."

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the bounds relies on a known but non-trivial determinantal inequality from Seiler & Simon (1975), and its application to the DPP likelihood appears correct. The extension from the finite case to the continuous case involving Fredholm determinants is handled carefully and demonstrates a solid theoretical foundation. The experimental validation is well-designed. The 1D synthetic experiment convincingly shows that the proposed MCMC sampler recovers the posterior distribution obtained from an "ideal" MH sampler with access to the true likelihood, validating the correctness of the method. The experiments on the real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrate the practical utility of the variational approach, showing that parameter estimates converge as the number of inducing points `m` increases, and that the upper and lower bounds on the likelihood converge as expected.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides a clear motivation for the problem and a concise background on DPPs. The main contributions are presented logically, starting with the simpler finite case before moving to the more technical continuous case, which allows readers to grasp the core idea without needing expertise in operator theory. The connection to sparse Gaussian Processes is explicitly and effectively used to provide intuition for the method. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly and support the paper's claims.

**Originality (Novelty):**
The core contribution—the development of non-spectral bounds for the DPP likelihood using inducing points—is novel and significant. While the use of inducing points is a well-established technique in the Gaussian Process literature (Titsias, 2009), its adaptation to the determinantal structure of the DPP likelihood is a creative and non-obvious step. This approach is fundamentally different from and represents a conceptual advance over previous methods for DPP inference that rely on partial or full spectral decompositions (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013).

**Significance:**
The results presented are highly significant for the machine learning community working with DPPs. The proposed methods directly address the primary scalability limitation of parametric DPPs. By removing the need for eigendecomposition, this work makes likelihood-based inference feasible for much larger datasets and for continuous DPPs with arbitrary kernels for which spectral properties are unknown. This has the potential to substantially broaden the practical applicability of DPPs in areas like recommender systems, summarization, and spatial statistics. Both the fast variational method and the exact MCMC sampler are valuable contributions to the practitioner's toolkit.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Lack of Direct Competitive Baseline:** The experiments effectively validate the proposed methods but lack a direct quantitative comparison against the most relevant prior work, namely the spectral-bound MCMC sampler of Affandi et al. (2013). While the paper argues conceptually that avoiding power iterations is advantageous, a direct comparison of runtime and sampler efficiency (e.g., effective sample size per second) on a shared task would have made the practical benefits more concrete.

2.  **Details on Inducing Point Optimization in MCMC:** The paper states that for the MCMC method, the inducing points `Z` are re-optimized when the bounds need to be tightened. The cost and specifics of this optimization within the MH loop are not detailed. This could be a non-trivial computational cost that affects the overall sampler efficiency, and more information on this procedure would be beneficial for reproducibility and for assessing the method's practical performance.

3.  **The L vs. K Parametrization Issue:** The paper correctly identifies and adopts the standard practice of parametrizing the `L` kernel for tractability, while acknowledging this harms the interpretability of parameters (e.g., the lengthscale no longer cleanly separates from the expected number of points). While this is a known issue in the field and not a flaw of this specific work, the paper does not offer new insights into resolving it, which remains an important open problem.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Strong Novelty:** The paper introduces a genuinely new and clever technique for DPP inference by adapting inducing point methods to derive non-spectral likelihood bounds.
*   **High Significance:** The method overcomes a major computational barrier, significantly improving the scalability and applicability of parametric DPP models.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical claims are well-supported, and the experimental validation is convincing in demonstrating the correctness and utility of the proposed frameworks.
*   **Excellent Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making a technical contribution accessible and easy to understand.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation, while good, could be strengthened by a direct comparison to the state-of-the-art spectral-based inference method.
*   The practical details concerning the optimization of inducing points within the MCMC algorithm are somewhat sparse.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—particularly its novelty and significance—far outweigh its minor weaknesses. The work represents a substantial and valuable contribution to the literature on probabilistic modeling with DPPs.
