- Addresses the practical bottleneck of spectral decomposition in DPP inference by proposing likelihood bounds that avoid eigendecomposition.
- Derives theoretically sound bounds for DPP likelihoods, extending methods to continuous DPPs with rigorous justification.
- Adapts variational sparse GP techniques and MCMC ideas to the DPP setting, explicitly drawing inspiration from prior GP work.
- Well-written and accessible presentation that clearly explains motivation, methodology, and theoretical results.
- Strong empirical validation: synthetic Gaussian DPP and diabetic neuropathy dataset experiments demonstrating effectiveness.
- Shows convergence behavior with increasing number of pseudo-inputs m and compares to existing methods where possible.
- Correctly discusses identifiability issues when parametrizing L and relates them to experimental findings.
- Novelty is somewhat incremental since core techniques (variational inference, MCMC, inducing variables) are established, with novelty in adaptation to DPPs.
- Method performance can be sensitive to hyperparameters and initialization of pseudo-inputs, needing further sensitivity analysis.
- Optimization of pseudo-inputs introduces computational overhead; trade-off vs. avoided spectral decomposition needs more detailed analysis.
- Experiments focus mainly on squared exponential kernels; broader kernel choices (e.g., Matern) are not explored.
- Proposition proofs are deferred to supplementary material; brief intuition in the main text would improve accessibility.
- The MCMC “Bernoulli trick” is only briefly described and would benefit from a more detailed explanation or reference.
- Suggests additional experimental visualizations (e.g., learned kernel plots) and quantification of convergence in overdispersion with increasing m.
- Overall recommendation: accept—the paper addresses a crucial practical problem with solid theory and experiments, and strengths outweigh weaknesses.
