This paper tackles a key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need to compute the determinant of the matrix `(L + I)`, which typically requires an expensive spectral decomposition. The authors' main contribution is a set of novel, non-spectral bounds (both upper and lower) on this determinant term. Inspired by variational methods for sparse Gaussian Processes (GPs), these bounds are formulated using a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points," `Z`. This provides an elegant and effective approximation framework. The paper demonstrates how these bounds can be directly integrated into two standard inference paradigms: a fast, approximate variational inference (VI) scheme based on maximizing the evidence lower bound, and an asymptotically exact Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm that uses the bounds for retrospective sampling. A significant strength of the work is its generality, as the framework is developed for both DPPs on finite sets and on continuous domains. The methods are validated empirically on a 1D synthetic dataset where the exact likelihood is known, and on a real-world spatial point process dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem that has limited the scalability and applicability of parametric DPP models. The proposed non-spectral bounds are a novel and highly significant contribution that circumvents the need for eigendecomposition. This could substantially broaden the scope of problems where DPPs can be practically applied.

2.  **Elegant Connection to Gaussian Processes:** Drawing an analogy to sparse GPs and using inducing variables to construct the bounds is a powerful and insightful idea. This not only provides a solid theoretical underpinning but also makes the approach intuitive and accessible to the large segment of the machine learning community familiar with GPs.

3.  **Technical Soundness and Generality:** The work is technically solid, with the bounds derived from established matrix inequalities. The extension of the framework from the finite case (matrices) to the continuous case (operators on L2 spaces) is non-trivial and demonstrates the theoretical depth and generality of the proposed approach.

4.  **Practical and Complete Inference Framework:** The paper goes beyond merely presenting the bounds; it provides two concrete and useful algorithms for inference. The inclusion of both a fast VI method for scalable approximate inference and an MCMC method for asymptotically exact Bayesian inference makes the contribution immediately applicable to a wide range of practical scenarios.

5.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically organized. Introducing the concepts first in the simpler finite setting before generalizing to the continuous case is an excellent pedagogical choice that greatly improves readability.

6.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling. The toy 1D Gaussian-Gaussian experiment is an excellent choice for validation, as the known ground truth allows for a direct and convincing verification of the MCMC sampler's correctness. The real-world diabetic neuropathy application demonstrates the practical utility of the VI method on a challenging problem and thoughtfully analyzes the convergence of the model as the number of inducing points `m` increases.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Feedback

1.  **Optimization of Pseudo-inputs:** The paper proposes jointly optimizing the pseudo-inputs `Z` along with the kernel hyperparameters `θ`. This is a non-convex optimization problem, and the sparse GP literature has shown it can be fraught with challenges like poor local minima. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of practical considerations for this optimization. For example, how were the pseudo-inputs initialized in the experiments (e.g., random subset of data, k-means clustering)? A brief discussion of the optimization landscape or potential strategies to mitigate local minima would be very helpful for practitioners.

2.  **Guidance on Selecting `m`:** The number of pseudo-inputs `m` is a critical hyperparameter that balances accuracy and computational cost. The experiments show results for different values of `m` and suggest increasing it until parameter estimates stabilize. While pragmatic, this is a heuristic approach. It would be beneficial to discuss if there are more principled ways to select `m`. For instance, can the gap between the upper and lower bounds on the log-likelihood be used as a diagnostic to determine if `m` is sufficient?

3.  **Direct Computational Comparison:** The method is motivated as being more computationally efficient than spectral-based approaches like the MCMC sampler in [7]. While the asymptotic complexity analysis supports this, the paper lacks a direct empirical comparison of wall-clock time versus accuracy/bound tightness against this key baseline. Including such a comparison, even on a moderately sized finite dataset, would make the practical benefits more concrete and compelling.

4.  **Details for Reproducibility:** In the continuous case, computing the matrix `Ψ` requires evaluating an integral. The paper states this is computable for the Gaussian kernel and Gaussian base measure used in the experiments. To aid reproducibility, it would be very helpful to include the closed-form expression for this integral, perhaps in the supplementary material.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   A highly novel and significant core contribution that removes a major computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
*   An elegant and powerful connection to the well-established field of sparse Gaussian Processes.
*   Provides a complete and practical solution with both fast/approximate (VI) and exact (MCMC) inference algorithms.
*   The approach is technically sound and general, covering both finite and continuous domains.
*   The paper is clearly written and supported by strong, well-chosen experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The discussion on the practical challenges of optimizing the pseudo-inputs is limited.
*   The guidance for selecting the number of pseudo-inputs (`m`) is heuristic.
*   The paper lacks a direct runtime comparison against the primary spectral-based MCMC competitor.

### Decision

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong and impactful contribution to the literature on probabilistic modeling. The proposed method for non-spectral inference in DPPs is novel, technically sound, and practically very useful. The strengths, particularly the significance of the core idea and the quality of the execution, substantially outweigh the minor weaknesses. The latter are primarily suggestions for additions that would further enhance an already strong paper. The work clearly meets the high standards of NeurIPS for quality, originality, and significance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
