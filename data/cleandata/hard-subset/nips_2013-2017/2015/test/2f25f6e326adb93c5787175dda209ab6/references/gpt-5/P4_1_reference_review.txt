Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) when the spectral decomposition of the L-ensemble kernel operator is unavailable or impractical. For finite ground sets, the authors introduce upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) that avoid eigenvalue computations by constructing a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY based on “inducing” points Z and using determinant inequalities (Seiler–Simon). This yields a tractable variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood and enables a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) scheme whose acceptance decision is made by tightening bounds via increasing m = |Z|. They extend these results to continuous DPPs on Rd by framing the likelihood via Janossy densities and Fredholm determinants, and provide analogous operator bounds involving LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x) under trace-class assumptions.

The approach is closely related to variational sparse Gaussian processes (GPs), in particular Titsias (AISTATS’09), where similar determinant and trace corrections yield a tight lower bound on the GP marginal likelihood. For DPPs, prior learning methods either require spectral information (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’13; spectral tail bounds for MH) or optimize finite L-ensemble parameters via EM/fixed-point methods (Gillenwater et al., NIPS’14; Mariet & Sra, NIPS’15) typically in the finite setting. The present work’s main novelty is to remove spectral requirements entirely and to cover continuous DPPs with computable Ψ (e.g., Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures), providing both a variational training objective and an exact MH scheme with retrospective acceptance.

Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where the spectrum is known, allowing validation of the retrospective MH against an “ideal” MH, and a real dataset of epidermal nerve fiber patterns where the variational method with increasing m produces convergent overdispersion estimates that separate clinical classes, consistent with prior analyses.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The determinant bounds (finite and operator forms) are plausible and consistent with known inequalities used in sparse GP variational bounds. Assuming the supplemental proofs are correct, the core claims are sound.
- The finite-case variational lower bound F(θ,Z) = Σt log det(LYt) − T log det(Q+I) + T tr(L−Q) is a direct application of the bound and is computationally attractive. A precise complexity discussion should include the cost of inverting LZ (O(m3)) and forming LZY (O(nm)), so per evaluation is O(n m2 + m3) rather than purely O(n m2).
- The retrospective MH construction is standard given lower/upper bounds and appears correctly adapted. A formal statement of conditions under which the bounds converge to the exact likelihood as m→∞ (monotonicity/tightness) would strengthen the argument; the paper states this informally.
- In the continuous case, the assumptions (trace-class L, integrability, computable Ψ) are clearly articulated. It would be useful to list kernel–measure pairs beyond the Gaussian–Gaussian case where Ψ has a closed form or can be reliably approximated.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The link between the GP variational bound and the DPP bound is clear and helpful.
- A few notational/technical points to clarify:
  - State explicitly that LZ must be invertible; suggest adding jitter if needed.
  - In Section 3.1, specify the cost of computing tr(Q) and note the dependence on m3 from inverting LZ.
  - For continuous bounds, clearly define the operator order and the exact determinant inequality invoked (with citation) to guide readers not versed in operator theory.
  - Minor typos around K(yi,yi) vs K(xi,xi) and equation numbering.

Originality
- The nonspectral determinant bounds tailored to DPP likelihoods and their use for both variational inference and retrospective MH appear novel. The extension to continuous DPPs with explicit Fredholm determinant bounds is, to my knowledge, new in the ML literature and fills a gap relative to spectral methods that are often infeasible in continuous settings.

Significance
- Eliminating spectral requirements is important for scalability (large finite sets) and for continuous domains where eigenpairs are analytically unavailable. The proposed methods can broaden the applicability of DPPs in practice.
- The variational objective is simple to implement and efficient; the retrospective MH provides a principled exact alternative.
- Empirical evidence is promising but somewhat limited. Including larger finite-domain experiments and direct comparisons to spectral methods (e.g., [7]) would more convincingly demonstrate advantages in runtime/memory and statistical accuracy.

Pros
- Conceptually simple, general, and practical nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods.
- Covers both finite and continuous DPPs; operator-theoretic extension is valuable.
- Two complementary inference schemes (variational and retrospective MH).
- Solid linkage to well-understood sparse GP methodology.

Cons
- Limited empirical scope; no large-scale finite benchmark or head-to-head comparison with spectral approaches.
- No quantitative analysis of bound tightness versus m, nor monotonicity/tightening guarantees.
- Variational optimization over Z is nonconvex; no guidance on initialization/selection or sensitivity analysis beyond anecdotal observations.
- Continuous-case applicability hinges on computable Ψ; broader guidance/examples would help.
- Identifiability issues (e.g., κ) highlighted but not addressed within the modeling framework.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical properties of the bounds as m increases: monotonicity of the lower bound, convergence conditions, and possibly data-dependent error bounds.
- Detail computational complexity per iteration, including m3 terms and the cost of determinants of LYt (O(|Yt|3)).
- Discuss strategies for choosing/initializing Z (e.g., k-means, grid, or leverage-score heuristics) and report sensitivity.
- Add experiments on large finite DPPs comparing to spectral MH/EM methods in [7, 8, 9], including runtime, memory, and estimation accuracy.
- For continuous DPPs, list additional kernel–measure pairs with closed-form Ψ or propose numerical approximations (and their impact).
- Address identifiability by considering k-DPPs or priors/constraints on κ, or discuss reparametrizations (e.g., separating intensity from repulsion).

Overall assessment
The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to DPP inference without spectral knowledge, with clear potential impact. Strengthening the empirical validation and adding theoretical guarantees on bound behavior would substantially improve the contribution. I view it as a meaningful step toward scalable DPP inference in both finite and continuous settings.
