This paper tackles the challenging problem of inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition, a major computational bottleneck in many applications. The authors provide bounds on the DPP likelihood that circumvent this need, extending techniques from variational Gaussian processes to the DPP setting and generalizing to continuous domains. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a significant contribution towards making DPP inference more practical.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Core Problem:** The computational cost of spectral decomposition is a major limitation in applying DPPs to large datasets. This work directly addresses this issue, providing a valuable contribution to the field.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the bounds on the likelihood, inspired by variational inference for GPs, appears rigorous and well-justified. The extension to continuous domains is a notable achievement.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient background information on DPPs and clearly explain the motivation and methodology. The separation of the finite and continuous cases aids clarity.
* **Practical Methodology:**  The proposed bounds are integrated into both variational inference and MCMC frameworks, demonstrating their practicality. The discussion of retrospective sampling within the MCMC framework is particularly insightful.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, including a toy example and a real-world dataset, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The comparison with existing methods (where applicable) and the analysis of the pseudo-inputs further support the claims.
* **Connections to Related Work:** The paper appropriately cites relevant work on DPPs, Gaussian processes, and related inference techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Interpretability:** As acknowledged by the authors, directly parametrizing the kernel L rather than K leads to a loss of interpretability of the parameters. While tractability is gained, understanding the meaning of the learned parameters is diminished. This limitation could be more thoroughly discussed, potentially exploring avenues for improving interpretability.
* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-Input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a significant win, the optimization of the pseudo-inputs (Z) within the variational and MCMC frameworks adds a computational cost. The authors mention this, but a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of this optimization step, and its impact on scalability, would be beneficial.
* **Limited Exploration of Prior Sensitivity:** The experiments show results for a specific prior distribution. Investigating the sensitivity of the results to different prior choices could strengthen the analysis.
* **Assumptions for Continuous Case:** The continuous case relies on specific assumptions (e.g. Mercer's Theorem) to ensure the validity of the derivations.  A more detailed discussion of the limitations of these assumptions, and potential challenges in applying the method to other continuous DPP settings, would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant bottleneck in DPP inference.
* The proposed bounds are theoretically sound and provide a practical alternative to spectral decomposition.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and contributes a valuable addition to the literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The loss of parameter interpretability is a notable drawback.
* The computational cost of pseudo-input optimization needs further scrutiny.
* The sensitivity analysis of priors is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of DPPs. The proposed method addresses a critical challenge in applying DPPs to real-world problems, and the experimental results provide compelling evidence of its effectiveness. While there are some weaknesses, they are acknowledged by the authors and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NeurIPS. 

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Discuss Parameter Interpretability Further:** Explore potential strategies for improving the interpretability of the learned parameters when directly parametrizing L.
* **Analyze Pseudo-Input Optimization Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the pseudo-input optimization step and its impact on scalability.
* **Investigate Prior Sensitivity:** Conduct experiments with different prior distributions to assess the sensitivity of the results.
* **Elaborate on Continuous Case Assumptions:** Discuss the limitations of the assumptions required for the continuous case and potential challenges in applying the method to other settings.
* **Consider Ablation Studies:** In the experimental section, consider ablation studies to show the impact of the number of pseudo-inputs on the performance.
