

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the requirement for spectral decomposition of the operator L. The authors propose novel bounds on the likelihood that avoid this spectral knowledge requirement, making inference feasible for large-scale applications. Previous work on DPP inference (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2013; Gillenwater et al., NIPS 2012) has relied on spectral decomposition or approximations thereof, which become computationally prohibitive for large datasets. The paper makes the important observation that while it's more natural to parametrize the kernel K, the likelihood formulation requires the related kernel L, and the spectral decomposition of L is rarely available in practice.

The main contributions are Propositions 1 and 2, which provide nonspectral bounds on the likelihood for finite and continuous DPPs, respectively. The bounds are derived using a determinant inequality [14] and involve "pseudo-inputs" (inducing points) that can be optimized to tighten the bounds. The authors then demonstrate how these bounds can be incorporated into variational inference (Section 3.1) and Markov chain Monte Carlo (Section 3.2) frameworks, extending the approach to continuous domains in Section 4.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the authors effectively distinguish between the finite and continuous cases, noting that readers unfamiliar with operator theory can safely skip Section 4. The connection to Gaussian process literature (specifically variational sparse GP methods [11]) is well-explained, providing helpful context for readers familiar with that area.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical results are sound, with proofs referenced to the supplementary material. The authors carefully address the trade-off between computational tractability and interpretability by parametrizing L directly rather than K, acknowledging this choice's interpretability limitations but justifying it for computational reasons. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets (Section 5) demonstrates the practical utility of their approach. The toy Gaussian experiment shows that their retrospective MCMC method correctly recovers parameters when spectral knowledge is available, while the diabetic neuropathy dataset application shows the method's effectiveness on real-world data.

The novelty of the paper is clear. While prior work has explored DPP inference [7, 8, 9], this is the first to provide bounds that do not require spectral decomposition of L. The extension to continuous domains is particularly valuable, as the spectral decomposition is often analytically intractable in this case. The authors cite relevant prior work and clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous methods.

The significance of the work is substantial. By eliminating the need for spectral decomposition, the authors make DPP inference computationally feasible for large-scale applications where previous methods were impractical. The paper demonstrates this through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, showing that their approach can yield meaningful results on the diabetic neuropathy dataset, which has been used in prior DPP inference work [7, 22]. The authors also acknowledge limitations, such as the interpretability trade-off from parametrizing L instead of K, and suggest directions for future work.

Strengths:
- Addresses a significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference
- Provides theoretically sound bounds that avoid spectral decomposition
- Extends to both finite and continuous domains
- Demonstrates practical utility on real-world data
- Clear exposition of technical material
- Careful acknowledgment of limitations

Weaknesses:
- The paper could more thoroughly compare with the most recent state-of-the-art methods for DPP inference
- The sensitivity of the approach to the choice of pseudo-inputs Z is not deeply explored
- The connection to non-parametric DPP inference work [8, 9] could be more explicitly articulated
- The interpretability trade-off from parametrizing L instead of K is acknowledged but not fully addressed

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper solves a significant computational problem in DPP inference
- The theoretical results are sound and well-motivated
- The approach is demonstrated to work on real-world data
- The extension to continuous domains is novel and valuable
- The paper is well-written and clear

Arguments against acceptance:
- The parametrization choice (L vs. K) introduces interpretability issues that remain unresolved
- The experimental comparison with state-of-the-art methods is limited
- The paper doesn't fully explore the sensitivity of the approach to the choice of pseudo-inputs

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of point process modeling and inference. While the parametrization trade-off is acknowledged, the computational benefits of the proposed approach are significant and well-demonstrated. The paper meets the quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria for NIPS, and represents a valuable advancement in making DPP inference computationally feasible for larger applications. The work is well-positioned to be useful to researchers working with DPPs in machine learning and statistics.
