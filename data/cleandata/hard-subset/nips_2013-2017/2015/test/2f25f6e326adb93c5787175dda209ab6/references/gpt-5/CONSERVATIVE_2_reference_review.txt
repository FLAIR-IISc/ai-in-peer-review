Summary
The paper proposes nonspectral, inducing-point-based bounds on the normalizing determinant in DPP likelihoods. For finite DPPs, with an inducing set Z, the authors bound 1/det(L+I) between det(Q+I)−1 and det(Q+I)−1 exp(−tr(L−Q)), where Q = LYZ L−1_Z LZY. They leverage the Seiler–Simon determinant inequality and draw an analogy to variational sparse GP bounds. They use the bounds for (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood and (ii) a retrospective MH scheme that refines acceptance bounds by increasing |Z| and re-optimizing Z. They extend the construction to continuous DPPs, yielding bounds on the Fredholm determinant det(I+L) in terms of L_Z and Ψ = ∫ L(z_i, x)L(x, z_j)dµ(x). Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian toy where the spectrum is known (comparisons to “ideal” MH) and a diabetic neuropathy dataset, where they train separate DPPs for two classes and report convergence of overdispersion metrics γ_d as the number of inducing points grows.

Strengths
- Addresses a well-known bottleneck in DPP inference (spectral computations) with a conceptually simple and broadly applicable alternative that is reminiscent of robust GP practice.
- Extends to continuous DPPs and provides computable expressions for Gaussian–Gaussian cases.
- The bounds enable both approximate variational training and exact-in-principle MH via retrospective acceptance tests.
- The toy example demonstrates agreement with an “ideal” MH sampler when spectra are available, lending some credibility.
- Practical complexity O(n m^2) is attractive when m ≪ n; inducing inputs can be optimized.

Weaknesses
- Lack of rigorous guarantees about bound tightness and convergence as m increases. The paper asserts refinability “under fairly weak assumptions” but provides no theorem ensuring the upper/lower bounds converge to the true determinant (finite or continuous) with m→∞ or monotonic shrinkage of the interval.
- Correctness of retrospective MH hinges on bounds that are guaranteed (not approximate) and nested; the procedure relies on optimizing Z numerically at each refinement. Without a proof that the computed bounds always contain the true likelihood (despite numerical optimization error), the MH chain may not be exact.
- Experimental validation is limited. No quantitative comparisons against state-of-the-art spectral methods ([Affandi et al., 2013], Bornemann quadrature for Fredholm determinants) in accuracy, runtime, or scalability. No large discrete DPP experiment (e.g., document summarization) where spectral methods are typical.
- Variational inference is shown to be unreliable on the toy example (κ unidentifiable), but the paper still leans on it in the real-data task; there is no out-of-sample evaluation, no uncertainty quantification, and no predictive or model selection metric.
- Important implementation details are missing: how Z is initialized/optimized (gradients vs black-box), stability and conditioning of L_Z inversion, sensitivity to m and Z, and wall-clock comparisons for MCMC.
- Several presentation issues (equation numbering inconsistencies, missing years in references, reliance on supplement for all proofs) impede assessment.

Quality
- The finite/continuous bounds plausibly follow from Seiler–Simon and inducing constructions, but proofs are deferred to the supplement. More importantly, the paper lacks theoretical results needed for inference: (i) conditions for equality/tightness as m grows, (ii) monotonicity of the bounds with m or under greedy augmentation of Z, and (iii) guarantees that the retrospective MH acceptance test is exact given numerical optimization of Z. Without these, the methodology is not fully justified.
- The empirical section does not thoroughly stress-test the method or benchmark against established baselines. Evidence for practical superiority over spectral methods is absent.

Clarity
- The narrative is mostly clear and well-motivated, with helpful connections to sparse GPs. However, equation numbering is inconsistent, some assumptions are hand-wavy (e.g., the remark linking convergence of bounds to GP posterior variance vanishing), and crucial algorithmic details are missing. References have formatting gaps. The paper would benefit from a precise statement of assumptions (for both finite and continuous cases), and a clear algorithm box for the retrospective MH procedure including guarantees.

Originality
- Using inducing-point determinant bounds for DPP normalization without spectral knowledge appears novel, especially the extension to continuous DPPs. The construction builds on established tools (Seiler–Simon inequality, variational sparse GP ideas) in a new context. However, the finite-domain approximation Q is close in spirit to Nyström; the paper should better differentiate from prior low-rank DPP approximations and clarify what is genuinely new beyond avoiding explicit eigen-computation.

Significance
- If made rigorous and shown to be competitive, nonspectral bounds could broaden DPP applicability, particularly in continuous settings and when spectra are unavailable. As it stands, the empirical evidence is insufficient to establish state-of-the-art performance or reliability, and the lack of theoretical guarantees for the MCMC acceptance test is a red flag for practitioners.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, potentially impactful idea to bypass spectra.
- Extension to continuous DPPs with computable Gaussian–Gaussian case.
- Some empirical indication that the MCMC variant can match ideal MH.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing theoretical guarantees for bound convergence/tightness and exactness of retrospective MH under practical optimization.
- Limited and largely qualitative experiments; no comparisons to established spectral approaches.
- Variational method shown to be unreliable in toy case; real-data results lack rigorous evaluation.
- Algorithmic details and stability considerations are underdeveloped.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theorems establishing convergence/tightness of the bounds as m→∞ and, if possible, monotonicity with respect to m or a constructive Z selection scheme.
- Prove that the retrospective MH sampler remains exact when Z is optimized numerically (or adopt a pseudo-marginal framework with stochastic but unbiased likelihood estimates).
- Include comprehensive comparisons to spectral methods (finite and continuous), ablations on m and Z, wall-clock vs accuracy trade-offs, and larger-scale discrete experiments.
- Clarify implementation details (gradients for Z and θ, conditioning of L_Z, initialization) and report sensitivity analyses.
- Fix presentation issues and ensure proofs of core propositions are accessible.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the current submission lacks crucial theoretical guarantees and thorough empirical validation against established baselines. Confidence: High.
