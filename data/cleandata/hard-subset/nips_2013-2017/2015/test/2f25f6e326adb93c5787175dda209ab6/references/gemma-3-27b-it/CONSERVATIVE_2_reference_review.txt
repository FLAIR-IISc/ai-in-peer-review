This paper addresses the problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition, a significant computational bottleneck for large datasets and continuous domains. The authors provide bounds on the DPP likelihood inspired by variational inference in Gaussian Processes (GPs), extending these bounds to continuous DPPs. They demonstrate the utility of these bounds within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, accompanied by experiments on both synthetic and real-world data.

The core idea—avoiding spectral decomposition through bounding techniques—is reasonable and addresses a clear limitation of existing DPP inference methods. The connection to variational inference for GPs is well-motivated, and the extension to continuous DPPs is a natural progression. The experimental validation, demonstrating the applicability of the proposed methods on a diabetic neuropathy dataset and a synthetic example, is a positive aspect.

However, my primary concern lies with the novelty and significance of the contribution. While the bounds themselves *appear* novel, the underlying principle of using bounding techniques to circumvent computationally expensive steps is hardly groundbreaking. Variational inference, upon which these bounds are heavily inspired, already employs such strategies routinely. The paper doesn't sufficiently articulate *why* these specific bounds are particularly advantageous over other potential bounding approaches. 

Furthermore, the paper leans heavily on established techniques without offering substantial innovation beyond the specific bounds. The application of variational inference and MCMC *using* these bounds feels incremental. The MCMC example showcases the retrospective sampling trick (already present in [7]) and doesn't offer substantial improvements.  The Gaussian-Gaussian synthetic experiment, while useful for validation, reveals a key limitation: the parameter κ (related to the base measure) is poorly identified, highlighting inherent issues with directly parametrizing the kernel L. This isn’t adequately discussed, and the authors acknowledge it only briefly.

The clarity of the presentation is generally good, but the mathematical notation could be improved with more consistent labeling. For instance, the meaning of Ψ in the continuous DPP section isn't immediately clear without referring back to the definitions. The paper could also benefit from a more thorough discussion of computational complexity, specifically comparing the cost of evaluating the bounds with existing methods, beyond simply stating that it avoids O(n^3) time.

Regarding originality, the paper builds upon a well-established foundation of DPP theory and variational inference. The extension to continuous DPPs is a natural extension, but doesn’t represent a radical departure. The related work section is adequate but could be more comprehensive regarding bounding techniques in other areas of probabilistic modeling.

In terms of significance, the presented work offers a practical tool for DPP inference, especially in scenarios where spectral decomposition is intractable. However, the lack of a clear advantage over existing methods, and the inherent identifiability issues with the L parametrization, limit its potential impact.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practical limitation of DPP inference.
*   Provides potentially useful bounds on DPP likelihoods.
*   Demonstrates the applicability of the bounds in standard inference frameworks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty – the core idea of bounding is not new.
*   Limited demonstration of superiority over existing methods.
*   Identifiability issues with the kernel L parametrization are not adequately addressed.
*   Insufficient discussion of computational complexity relative to alternatives.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While technically sound and clearly presented, it lacks the substantial novelty and significance expected of a NIPS publication. The contribution feels more like an engineering application of existing tools rather than a significant scientific advance. I believe that the paper would be more appropriate for a more applied venue. A major revision, focusing on demonstrating a clear advantage over existing methods, addressing the identifiability issues, and providing a more rigorous computational analysis, would be necessary for reconsideration.
