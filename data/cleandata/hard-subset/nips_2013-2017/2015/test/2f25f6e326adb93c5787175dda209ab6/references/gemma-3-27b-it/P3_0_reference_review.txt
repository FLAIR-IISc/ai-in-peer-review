## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of kernel matrices, which becomes intractable for large datasets or continuous domains. The authors circumvent this issue by deriving novel bounds on the DPP likelihood that *do not* rely on eigenvalue calculations. They then demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into both variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods, offering practical alternatives to existing approaches. The work extends prior bounds inspired by Gaussian Process variational inference to the DPP setting and provides analogous results for continuous DPPs. The paper includes both synthetic experiments demonstrating the MCMC method's accuracy and a real-world application to a diabetic neuropathy dataset, showcasing the utility of the proposed variational inference approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Core Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental limitation of DPP inference – the spectral decomposition requirement – making DPPs more applicable to larger and more complex problems. This is a significant contribution, as the computational cost has been a major barrier to wider adoption.
* **Novel Bounds:** The derivation of non-spectral bounds on the DPP likelihood is the central technical contribution.  These bounds, inspired by and extending GP variational inference techniques, provide a concrete path towards tractable inference.
* **Broad Applicability:** The methodology is presented for both discrete and continuous DPPs, broadening its potential impact. The extension to continuous domains is particularly valuable.
* **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors provide experiments on both synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating the efficacy of their proposed methods. The comparison of MCMC results with those achievable using the full likelihood (in the 1D case) is particularly convincing. The diabetic neuropathy application showcases real-world applicability.
* **Connections to Existing Literature:**  The paper builds clearly on prior work in DPPs, Gaussian Processes, and variational inference. The authors make explicit connections, citing relevant literature throughout.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly articulate the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Identifiability Issues:** The synthetic experiment highlights an inherent issue with directly parameterizing the kernel L, rather than K: the parameters are not always identifiable, as seen with the results for κ. While acknowledged, this limitation could be more thoroughly discussed. This suggests that while the method *works*, interpretability of the learned parameters is challenging.
* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-Input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a win, the optimization of pseudo-inputs (Z) within the MCMC and variational schemes adds a computational cost.  The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed analysis of the trade-off between the cost of pseudo-input optimization and the benefit of avoiding spectral decomposition would be valuable. How does this scale with dataset size?
* **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods:** The diabetic neuropathy experiments compare against results from [7], but a more comprehensive comparison to other existing DPP inference methods (even those relying on spectral approximations) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Theoretical Justification of Bounds:** While the proof relies on existing determinant inequalities, a deeper intuition for *why* these bounds are effective and *when* they are tight would be beneficial.  The authors mention the connection to GP quadrature, but further exploration of this connection could provide valuable insights.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs. The authors address a significant practical limitation and provide a novel and effective solution. While there are some limitations regarding parameter identifiability and the cost of pseudo-input optimization, these are acknowledged and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and well-supported by experimental results.  The significance of the work is high, as it opens up new possibilities for applying DPPs to larger and more complex datasets.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Solves a critical bottleneck in DPP inference.
* Provides novel and mathematically sound bounds.
* Demonstrates effectiveness through both synthetic and real-world experiments.
* Clear and well-organized presentation.
* Broad applicability to both discrete and continuous DPPs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Parameter identifiability issues with direct kernel L parameterization.
* Computational cost associated with pseudo-input optimization.
* Limited comparison to other existing inference methods.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  The strengths of this paper outweigh the weaknesses. It represents a significant advance in DPP inference and has the potential to impact a wide range of applications. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
