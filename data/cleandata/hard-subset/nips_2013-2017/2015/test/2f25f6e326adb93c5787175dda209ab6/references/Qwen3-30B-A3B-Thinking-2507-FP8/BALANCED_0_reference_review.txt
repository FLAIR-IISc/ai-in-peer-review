

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a significant computational challenge in determinantal point process (DPP) inference: the requirement for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L in likelihood evaluation. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid spectral decomposition, enabling efficient variational inference and MCMC methods for DPPs over both finite and continuous domains. The work builds on variational inference techniques from Gaussian processes [11] and extends them to DPPs, making a theoretically sound contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper identifies a genuine computational bottleneck in DPP inference that has been acknowledged in prior work (e.g., [7]), where spectral decomposition becomes intractable for large or continuous domains.
2. The theoretical contributions (Propositions 1 and 2) are well-motivated and derived from non-trivial determinant inequalities [14], with clear connections to Gaussian process literature.
3. The authors successfully extend their finite-domain results to continuous domains, which is non-trivial and represents a meaningful contribution beyond previous work.
4. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets (diabetic neuropathy) demonstrates practical utility, showing convergence of results as the number of pseudo-inputs increases.
5. The paper acknowledges important limitations, particularly regarding parameter identifiability when parametrizing L directly rather than K, which aligns with prior observations in the literature [4, 7].

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper lacks a quantitative comparison between the proposed bounds and existing spectral-based methods (e.g., [7]) in terms of bound tightness, computational cost, or inference accuracy. Without this, it's difficult to assess the practical advantages of the proposed approach.
2. The discussion of pseudo-input selection could be strengthened. While the paper shows that optimized pseudo-inputs appear to align with the marginal distribution (Figure 1a), the paper doesn't provide theoretical justification for why this occurs or how to optimally choose them.
3. The connection to Gaussian processes is mentioned but not fully exploited. The authors could better articulate how their bounds relate to or extend existing sparse GP methods.
4. The paper doesn't address how the proposed approach compares to alternative DPP inference methods that avoid spectral decomposition (e.g., [8, 9]), which would help position their contribution more clearly.
5. The experimental section could better demonstrate the computational advantages of the proposed method by comparing runtimes with spectral-based methods.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**
1. Add a table comparing the computational complexity, bound tightness, and inference accuracy of the proposed method versus spectral-based approaches [7] across different problem sizes.
2. Expand the discussion on pseudo-input selection by analyzing how the quality of the bounds depends on the choice of pseudo-inputs, and perhaps providing theoretical guarantees about optimal selection.
3. Include a comparison with non-spectral DPP inference methods (e.g., [8, 9]) in the experiments to better position the contribution.
4. Clarify the relationship between the proposed bounds and existing sparse GP methods, perhaps by deriving a formal connection between the two frameworks.
5. Discuss the implications of the parameter identifiability issue (highlighted in Section 5.1) more thoroughly, particularly how it might impact practical applications.

**Overall assessment:**
This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to DPP inference by avoiding spectral decomposition. The theoretical results are solid, and the experimental validation demonstrates practical utility. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison with existing methods and a deeper analysis of the proposed approach's advantages.

The paper addresses a genuine problem in DPP inference that has practical significance for applications in machine learning (e.g., [3, 6, 7]). While the proposed method is not a complete solution to the DPP inference problem, it provides a valuable alternative for settings where spectral decomposition is intractable. The significance of the work is supported by the growing use of DPPs in machine learning applications.

**Decision recommendation:** Accept (if the authors address the weaknesses noted above in the camera-ready version). The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in DPP inference, and the experimental results validate the approach. The weaknesses are primarily in the presentation and comparison of the method rather than in the technical contribution itself. With the suggested improvements, this paper would be a strong addition to the NeurIPS proceedings.
