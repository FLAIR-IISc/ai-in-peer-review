

# Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the operator L in the likelihood expression. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid this spectral decomposition, enabling more efficient variational inference and MCMC methods. The approach draws inspiration from sparse Gaussian process approximations using inducing variables, extending these ideas to DPPs. The paper makes two main contributions: (1) theoretical bounds for finite and continuous DPPs that don't require spectral knowledge, and (2) practical inference methods that leverage these bounds. This work builds on prior DPP inference approaches like [7] but eliminates the need for spectral decomposition, which is a significant computational advantage.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The theoretical bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) are rigorously derived using nontrivial determinant inequalities, and the authors provide detailed proofs in supplementary material. The experimental validation is comprehensive, including both a synthetic Gaussian continuous experiment (where exact likelihood is available for comparison) and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset. The results demonstrate that the proposed methods are both accurate and efficient, with the MCMC approach converging to the correct posterior distribution and the variational approach providing consistent estimates as the number of inducing variables increases.

**Originality:** The core idea of avoiding spectral knowledge for DPP inference is novel and addresses a well-known limitation in the DPP literature. While [7] used spectral bounds, this paper's nonspectral approach is significantly different and more general, as it doesn't require any spectral information. The extension from finite to continuous DPPs is non-trivial and well-executed. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work and establishes a connection to sparse Gaussian processes that's both relevant and insightful.

**Significance:** DPPs have become increasingly popular in machine learning for modeling diversity, but their computational complexity has limited practical adoption. This paper directly addresses this limitation, making DPP inference more scalable. The experiments demonstrate that the approach works well on both synthetic and real data, with the variational method providing a practical solution for large-scale problems. The paper's framework could enable broader application of DPPs in areas like recommendation systems, image search, and spatial statistics.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, and the connection to Gaussian processes is made clear. The experimental setup is described thoroughly, making the results reproducible. The paper's structure (from problem statement to theory to experiments) follows a logical flow that's easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with operator theory.

## Weaknesses

**Limited comparison with alternatives:** While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach, it doesn't provide a thorough comparison with other approximate inference methods for DPPs. For instance, how does the proposed method compare to the Nyström approximation in [13] or the fixed-point algorithms in [9]? A comparative analysis would strengthen the significance claim.

**Identifiability concerns:** The paper correctly acknowledges that parametrizing L directly (rather than K) leads to identifiability issues (Section 5.1), but doesn't offer a practical solution. The experiments show that κ is not identifiable, but the paper doesn't suggest how to address this limitation in practice. This is a significant practical concern for real-world applications.

**Sensitivity analysis:** The paper optimizes the inducing points Z, but doesn't analyze how sensitive the bounds are to the initial choice of Z or the quality of the optimization. A sensitivity analysis would provide more confidence in the robustness of the approach.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the DPP literature. The theoretical results are novel, well-justified, and practically useful. The experimental validation is thorough and demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed methods. The paper is clearly written and makes a significant step toward making DPP inference more scalable and practical.

The main weaknesses are the limited comparison with alternative inference methods and the unaddressed identifiability issue with L parametrization. However, these do not undermine the core contribution of the paper, which is the development of nonspectral bounds for DPP inference.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a real and important problem in DPP inference with a technically sound and novel solution. The experimental results are convincing, and the writing is clear and well-organized. While the identifiability issue is a limitation, it's acknowledged by the authors and is a separate problem from the core contribution of the paper. The proposed approach opens new avenues for efficient DPP inference and has clear potential for broad impact in machine learning applications.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field, advancing the state of the art in DPP inference by eliminating the computational bottleneck of spectral decomposition. This is exactly the kind of practical theoretical advance that NIPS values. The paper meets all four review criteria well, with particular strength in quality and originality.

**Pro:** Novel nonspectral bounds for DPP inference, rigorous theoretical foundation, comprehensive experimental validation, clear writing.

**Con:** Limited comparison with alternative inference methods, unaddressed identifiability issue with L parametrization.
