

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper proposes non-spectral bounds on determinants arising in the likelihoods of determinantal point processes (DPPs), both finite and continuous. The authors derive bounds that avoid spectral decomposition, which is computationally expensive for large-scale DPPs. These bounds enable variational inference and MCMC methods without requiring eigendecomposition, addressing a key computational bottleneck in DPP parameter estimation. The work builds on connections to Gaussian processes and extends sparse approximation techniques to DPPs.

**Strengths:**
The paper identifies a genuine computational challenge in DPP inference and proposes a theoretically sound approach to address it. The bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 are novel and extend previous work on variational inference for Gaussian processes to DPPs. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrates the practical applicability of the approach. The connection to existing literature on DPPs and Gaussian processes is well-established, with appropriate citations to key works by Kulesza, Taskar, and others.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical contradiction in methodology:** In Section 2.2, the authors claim "our bounds do not rely on a Nyström-type approximation" while simultaneously defining Q = LYZ[LZ]⁻¹LZY, which is precisely the Nyström approximation. This is a fundamental contradiction that undermines the paper's central claim about avoiding Nyström approximations. This is not a minor typographical error but a conceptual inconsistency that requires significant revision.

2. **Inconsistent and ambiguous notation:** The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout, switching between L and K for the kernel matrix without clear justification. In Section 2.1, the paper states "K(yi,yi)" where it should be "K(xj,xj)" (typo in equation). In Section 5.1, the kernel is written as "exp ( − 2‖x− y‖2 )" without clear exponentiation, making it ambiguous whether it's exp(-2‖x-y‖²) or exp(-2‖x-y‖)². The reference to "log " in Section 5.1 (without specifying the variable) is incomplete and unprofessional.

3. **Incorrect technical claims:** The paper states "the Fredholm determinant det(I + L) in this special case is a q-Pochhammer symbol" (Section 5.1), which is factually incorrect. While certain special cases can be expressed using q-Pochhammer symbols, this is not generally true for the Fredholm determinant of a Gaussian kernel. This misstatement suggests a lack of precision in technical claims.

4. **Figure reference error:** The paper refers to "Figure 5.1" in Section 5.1, but the paper only contains Figures 1-4. This is a significant formatting error that indicates poor proofreading.

5. **Incomplete reference formatting:** Several references contain inconsistent formatting (e.g., extra commas, missing publication years), which is unprofessional for a NIPS submission.

6. **Unjustified claims about identifiability:** The paper claims that "κ also mildly influences repulsion" (Section 5.2) without providing evidence or derivation. This statement requires theoretical justification to be credible.

7. **Ambiguous convergence claims:** The paper states that "the estimated overdispersion measures converge to coherent values as m increases" (Section 5.2), but provides no theoretical justification for this convergence or error bounds on the approximation.

**Minor but significant issues:**
- In Section 4.2, Proposition 2 uses "tr(L⁻¹_Z Ψ)" where L_Z is a matrix and Ψ is an operator, creating a notational inconsistency.
- The paper claims "the eigenvalues of K are in [0, 1]" (Section 2.1), but the correct condition is that the eigenvalues must be in [0, 1] for the DPP to exist as a valid probability distribution, not just for the likelihood to be defined.
- The paper states "the Fredholm determinant det(I + L) , ∏∞ i=1(λi+1)" (Section 4.1), but this is only true for trace-class operators, which is not explicitly stated.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper addresses a genuine computational challenge in DPP inference and presents theoretically sound bounds, the cumulative effect of these minor but significant technical inconsistencies, notational errors, and contradictory claims undermines the paper's credibility. The contradiction in Section 2.2 is particularly severe, as it directly contradicts the paper's central contribution. The numerous formatting and notational errors, while individually minor, collectively indicate a lack of careful attention to detail that is unacceptable for a NIPS submission.

The paper's main contribution is interesting and potentially useful, but the technical shortcomings and presentation errors are substantial enough to warrant rejection. A revised version addressing these issues would be appropriate for consideration, but as submitted, the paper does not meet NIPS's standards for technical precision and scholarly rigor. The authors should carefully revise the notation, correct the contradictory claims, and thoroughly proofread the manuscript before resubmission.
