Summary
This paper proposes nonspectral, inducing-point-style bounds for the DPP normalizing determinant det(I+L) that avoid computing eigenvalues, enabling both a variational lower bound for maximum-likelihood-style training and a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler for Bayesian inference. For finite-ground-set DPPs, the authors define a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY (with “pseudo-inputs” Z not constrained to be a subset of Y) and show bounds of the form det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I), inspired by sparse GP theory (Titsias, 2009). They extend these ideas to continuous DPPs via a Fredholm-determinant bound that replaces the Nyström algebra by the integral Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x), yielding upper/lower bounds depending only on LZ and Ψ, again without eigenvalues. They then plug these bounds into: (i) a variational objective summing log det(LYt) minus T log det(Q+I) plus T tr(L−Q), optimized jointly over kernel parameters and inducing points; and (ii) a retrospective MH scheme that refines the bounds (by increasing m and re-optimizing Z) until an accept/reject decision is determined. Experiments on a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP (where spectral quantities are known) validate the MH posterior and show that κ is weakly identifiable, and on a diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrate convergence of overdispersion measures as m increases and qualitative class separation.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: clean, general, nonspectral determinant bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, adapting powerful GP-inducing machinery to DPP likelihoods.
- Finite case bound is plausible and elegant: Q ≤ L in PSD order gives the sandwich; computationally enables O(n m^2 + m^3) evaluation via matrix determinant lemma.
- Continuous case uses only kernel integrals (Ψ), enabling tractable Fredholm determinant bounds for common Gaussian/Gaussian settings.
- Practicality: two inference routes (variational and retrospective MH); retrospective MH is exact in principle if bounds can be arbitrarily tightened.
- Experiments illustrate behavior and provide some sanity checks (agreement with “ideal” spectral MH in 1D; convergence vs m; pseudo-inputs adapt to data).

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and missing guarantees
- Retrospective MH requires that bounds can be tightened arbitrarily (to ensure eventual decision with probability 1 and unbiased acceptance). The paper does not provide a theorem guaranteeing that the upper–lower gap shrinks to 0 as m→∞ (finite case is OK when m=n and Z spans Y, but this is not stated; continuous case is more delicate and needs conditions such as fill distance→0 and trace-class assumptions). The brief remark in Sec. 4 (“this necessarily happens...”) is informal and insufficient; no rates or monotonicity results are given.
- Assumptions for LZ invertibility and the PSD ordering L−Q ≽ 0 are not stated. One needs Z distinct and L strictly PD to ensure LZ ≻ 0; and the Schur complement argument for L−Q ≽ 0 requires the joint Gram matrix over Y∪Z to be PSD (trivial for kernels, but worth stating carefully in both finite and continuous cases).
- Continuous bound (Prop. 2): the operator-theoretic steps are not detailed. Conditions ensuring tr(L) < ∞ and tr(LZ^{-1}Ψ) < ∞ need to be stated; the claim in (9) (“Assume tr(L)=∫ L(x,x) dµ(x)”) is an assumption, not proved; the link to [19] is mentioned but not instantiated. It is unclear under which concrete conditions on L and µ the lower/upper bounds converge and how Ψ is computed beyond the Gaussian/Gaussian case.
- Complexity claims: the variational objective includes ∑t log det(LYt). Its cost is O(|Yt|^3) per t; this may dominate in some regimes. The paper claims O(n m^2) overall without acknowledging this term or discussing typical sizes of |Yt|.
- Gradients: no derivations for ∂F/∂θ and ∂F/∂Z are provided; since Ψ involves integrals in the continuous case, clarity on differentiability and practical computation is necessary for gradient-based optimization.

Clarity and presentation
- Several notational/typographical issues obscure the exposition:
  - Sec. 2.1: det K{xi,xj} expression uses K(yi,yi) instead of K(xj,xj); and the subsequent text suggests “xi and xj are likely to co-occur if det K{xi,xj} is large,” which contradicts DPP repulsion (co-occurrence is less likely when |Kij| is large).
  - Equation numbering is inconsistent: `(θ)` is referred to as (7) though defined earlier (5); (13) is referenced for the retrospective MH bounds but earlier equations bear different numbers; there are footnote markers (1–4) floating mid-text.
  - Several grammatical slips and formatting inconsistencies (“Proccessing,” “systems,” mismatched brackets, missing commas).
- The continuous section includes a dangling paragraph (“Note that this necessarily happens under fairly weak assumptions...”), which reads as commentary rather than a theorem and interrupts flow.
- References are incomplete (missing years/pages in many entries), and some citations are imprecise (e.g., [20] for Gaussian RBF interpolation not obviously tied to the spectral decomposition claim; the Hermite–Gaussian spectral result typically cites classical Mercer/kernel literature).

Empirical evaluation
- Missing comparisons to state-of-the-art DPP learning methods beyond the 1D toy: no quantitative comparison to spectral-bounds MH [Affandi et al., 2013], EM/fixed-point methods [Gillenwater et al., Mariet & Sra], or Nyström-based approximations [Affandi et al., 2014], in terms of runtime, accuracy (held-out log-likelihood), and scalability.
- For the diabetic dataset, only overdispersion measures and bound convergence are shown. There is no predictive evaluation, no uncertainty quantification, no cross-validation, and no numerical comparison to [7] on the same data.
- No ablations on the choice/optimization of Z: how much does optimizing Z matter vs random/k-means? How sensitive are results to m? Are the bounds monotone in m?
- Practicalities for continuous Ψ: beyond Gaussian/Gaussian, do the authors rely on quadrature? Stability/accuracy of Ψ estimates is not discussed.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a theorem (finite and continuous cases) guaranteeing that the upper–lower gap for det(I+L) shrinks to zero as m→∞ under explicit conditions, and preferably a (problem-dependent) rate?
2. In the finite case, do the bounds become exact if Z=Y (m=n)? Please state this and discuss monotonicity in m if any.
3. Please detail the proof conditions for L−Q ≽ 0 and LZ ≻ 0, and the operator inequalities used in Prop. 2.
4. How are gradients w.r.t. θ and Z computed in the continuous case (including Ψ terms)? Are closed forms available for Gaussian/Gaussian?
5. Can you add quantitative comparisons to spectral MH [7] (accuracy/time) and to EM/fixed-point baselines on standard finite DPP benchmarks?

Minor comments and typos (non-exhaustive)
- Sec. 2.1: “K(yi,yi)” → “K(xj,xj)”; clarify the co-occurrence comment to reflect repulsion correctly.
- Several equation references incorrect (e.g., (7), (13)); please renumber consistently.
- Footnotes (1–4) appear misplaced; integrate cleanly.
- “Proccessing,” “systems,” “Nyström” accent consistency; “butexact” → “but-exact”; “repartition” → “distribution” (if that’s intended).
- Consistency: positive definite vs positive semidefinite; specify where strictness is required.
- References: add years and full bibliographic details; ensure the spectral decomposition citation is correct.

Assessment
- Quality: Promising ideas, but important theoretical guarantees for retrospective MH and some operator assumptions are missing; clarity issues and several technical details are underspecified.
- Clarity: Needs significant polishing and correction of notation/typos; more precise statements/assumptions and algorithmic details are required.
- Originality: The nonspectral determinant bounds and extension to continuous DPPs via inducing-point ideas are novel and interesting.
- Significance: Potentially useful for scaling DPP inference without spectra; current empirical section is too limited to assess impact.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The core idea is compelling, but the paper in its present form has too many technical gaps, missing guarantees, unclear assumptions, and insufficient experimental validation. Strengthening the theory (tightening guarantees), cleaning up the presentation, and adding thorough comparisons would make this a strong submission.
