### **Summary**

This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), namely the evaluation of the `det(L + I)` term, which typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix or operator L. The authors propose novel non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant. The core idea is inspired by variational inference in sparse Gaussian Processes, using a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" to construct a low-rank approximation to the kernel matrix. These bounds are derived for both DPPs on finite sets and on continuous domains. The utility of these bounds is demonstrated through two inference paradigms: a fast but approximate variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes the lower bound, and a more computationally intensive but asymptotically exact MCMC (Metropolis-Hastings) algorithm that uses the bounds to make accept/reject decisions via retrospective sampling. The methods are evaluated on a synthetic 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP and a real-world spatial point process dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Key Problem:** The computational cost of the determinant in the DPP likelihood is a well-known and significant barrier to scaling these models. The paper tackles a problem of practical importance to the machine learning community working with DPPs.

2.  **General and Elegant Approach:** The proposed method avoids direct spectral computations (like power iteration), making it applicable in settings where these are difficult, such as for DPPs on continuous domains. The conceptual link to inducing point methods in Gaussian Processes is elegant and provides a unified perspective on approximation for these related kernel-based models.

3.  **Versatility of Inference:** The authors demonstrate that their bounds can be plugged into two very different and widely-used inference frameworks. The VI approach offers a scalable, optimization-based approximation, while the MCMC approach provides a path to (asymptotically) exact Bayesian inference. This flexibility is a notable strength.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It effectively explains the problem, draws the analogy to sparse GPs, and walks the reader through the application of the bounds for both VI and MCMC. The separation of the finite and continuous cases aids readability.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Comparison to Key Baselines:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the absence of an empirical comparison to the most relevant prior work, specifically the spectral bound approach of Affandi et al. [7]. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of that method (e.g., reliance on power iteration, applicability to finite domains), but they do not provide any experiments to demonstrate that their proposed method is superior in terms of runtime, accuracy, or scalability. A direct comparison on the same dataset, measuring wall-clock time and inference quality for a given approximation level (i.e., comparing `m` inducing points vs. `m` eigenvalues), is essential to substantiate the claims of the paper. Without this, the contribution remains a potentially interesting alternative rather than a demonstrated improvement.

2.  **Limited Technical Originality:** The core technical idea of using inducing points to form a low-rank approximation `Q = LYZ [LZ]−1LZY` is directly borrowed from the sparse Gaussian Process literature (e.g., Titsias, 2009). While applying this idea to the DPP likelihood and using the determinant inequality from Seiler & Simon [14] is a novel combination, the fundamental approximation strategy is well-established. This diminishes the originality of the core methodological contribution.

3.  **Inconclusive Experimental Results:** The experiments, while demonstrating that the method "works," raise some concerns.
    *   In the synthetic experiment (Sec 5.1), the VI approach is shown to produce "poorly consistent results," which undermines its credibility as a reliable inference method. While the authors correctly attribute this to identifiability issues with the L-parametrization, this is a significant negative result for one of the two main proposed applications of their bounds.
    *   The real-data experiment (Sec 5.2) uses only the VI method. It shows that the parameter estimates and likelihood bounds converge as `m` increases. This is a useful internal consistency check, but it does not validate the accuracy of the final result against a more exact method (like their own MCMC sampler or the baseline from [7]). The convergence of the bounds simply shows that the approximation becomes tighter, not that the optimization finds the correct parameters.

4.  **Practical Guidance:** The choice of the number of inducing points `m` is a critical hyperparameter. The paper suggests increasing `m` until the results stabilize. While pragmatic, this is a heuristic approach that can be computationally expensive and lacks a more principled guideline or theoretical justification.

### **Detailed Comments**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its derivations. The reliance on established mathematical inequalities and approximation techniques from related fields is solid. However, the quality of the experimental validation is a major concern. The claims of providing a better inference method are not well-supported by the evidence provided, primarily due to the missing baseline comparisons. The authors are commendably honest about the failure of VI on the toy problem, but this point requires deeper analysis rather than just a passing mention.

*   **Significance:** The potential significance is high. A truly efficient, general-purpose, non-spectral inference method for DPPs would be a valuable contribution. However, in its current form, the paper presents a promising idea that has not been rigorously validated against the state of the art. As such, its immediate impact is likely to be limited until its advantages are more clearly demonstrated.

*   **Recommendation:**
    This paper presents an interesting and well-motivated approach for DPP inference by adapting a successful technique from the Gaussian Process literature. The core idea is sound and the presentation is clear. However, the work is let down by a weak experimental section that fails to compare against the most obvious and relevant baseline (Affandi et al., 2014). This omission makes it impossible to judge whether the proposed method offers a tangible advantage in practice. Furthermore, the demonstrated failure of the VI approach in a simple setting raises questions about its robustness. While the connection to sparse GPs is neat, the contribution feels more incremental than transformative without stronger empirical backing. For these reasons, I am hesitant to recommend acceptance in its current state. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a direct and thorough empirical comparison with existing spectral methods.
