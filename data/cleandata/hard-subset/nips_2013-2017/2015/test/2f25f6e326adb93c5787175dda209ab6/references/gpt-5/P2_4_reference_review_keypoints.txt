- Proposes nonspectral determinant bounds for DPP likelihoods using inducing inputs Z and a low-rank approximation Q, enabling a variational lower bound and retrospective MH.

- Extends the approach to continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinant bounds using Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x).

- Empirically validated on a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP (retrospective MH, identifiability issues) and diabetic neuropathy data (variational recovery and bound convergence with m).

- Builds on variational sparse GP ideas and determinant/trace inequalities to avoid spectral decompositions used in prior DPP work.

- Continuous extension uses operator-theoretic tools and parallels known spectral cases without requiring eigenstructure.

- Main technical claims are plausible and consistent with known matrix/operator inequalities; finite-case complexity O(n m^2) is credible.

- Variational objective is coherent and the retrospective MH adaptation is well-motivated and demonstrated on a toy problem.

- Lacks theoretical characterization of the gap or convergence rate of bounds as m increases and no proof of monotonic improvement when optimizing Z.

- Proofs are deferred to the supplement and the paper omits discussion of numerical stability issues (e.g., LZ invertibility, jitter).

- Continuous-case tractability hinges on computing Ψ; paper focuses on Gaussian–Gaussian and gives limited guidance for general measures or kernels.

- Generally clear exposition, but has minor typos/misnumbered equations and would benefit from gradient computation and optimization guidance for Z and θ.

- Core contribution is novel to the DPP literature: nonspectral determinant bounds and an inducing-point Fredholm extension.

- Avoiding eigen-decompositions addresses a significant bottleneck and could broaden applicability, but empirical evidence at scale is limited.

- Pros: principled nonspectral bounds, applicability to finite and continuous DPPs, practical variational and MH algorithms, and empirical bound convergence with interpretable inducing inputs.

- Cons: limited large-scale experiments and head-to-head comparisons, no quantitative bounds on tightness/convergence, sparse practical guidance on Z selection and stability, unclear general Ψ computation.

- Suggestions: provide theoretical monotonicity/convergence results, analyze LZ conditioning strategies, include gradients/optimization details, add large finite-set comparisons (spectral/Nyström), and show non-Gaussian continuous examples or Ψ approximation methods.

- Overall: solid, timely, and novel contribution with promising methods but weakened by limited empirical validation and theoretical quantification; reviewer leans positive.
