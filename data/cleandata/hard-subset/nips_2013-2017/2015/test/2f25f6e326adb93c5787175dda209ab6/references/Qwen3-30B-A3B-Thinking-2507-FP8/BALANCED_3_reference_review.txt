

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the requirement for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L. The authors propose novel bounds on the likelihood that avoid spectral knowledge, enabling variational inference and MCMC methods for both finite and continuous DPPs. The work builds on existing variational bounds for Gaussian processes [11] and extends them to DPPs, providing a practical solution to a well-known challenge in DPP inference.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness and Quality:** The theoretical contributions (Propositions 1 and 2) are well-motivated and rigorously proven. The bounds effectively avoid the spectral decomposition bottleneck, which is a major computational hurdle in DPP inference. The connection to Gaussian process inference provides a natural bridge for readers familiar with that field. The experimental validation is thorough, with both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrating the practical utility of the approach.

2. **Originality:** While bounds for Gaussian processes have been studied, extending them to DPPs without spectral knowledge is novel. Previous work on DPP inference [7] relies on spectral methods, making this a meaningful departure. The paper clearly differentiates its contribution from prior work, particularly in Section 1 and the discussion of parametrizing L directly.

3. **Significance:** DPPs are increasingly used for diversity modeling in machine learning, but their computational limitations have restricted wider adoption. This work directly addresses that barrier, making DPP inference more tractable for large-scale and continuous applications. The results on the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrate practical value for real-world applications.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear progression from finite to continuous domains. The authors appropriately note that Section 4 can be skipped by readers unfamiliar with operator theory. The writing is precise and accessible to machine learning researchers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison with Prior Work:** While the paper mentions [7] as using spectral bounds, it lacks a detailed quantitative comparison of computational efficiency and accuracy between the proposed method and spectral-based approaches. A table comparing runtime and accuracy across different problem sizes would strengthen the claims.

2. **Interpretability Trade-off:** The paper acknowledges that parametrizing L instead of K affects interpretability (Section 1), but this limitation isn't fully explored. It would be valuable to investigate how the authors' approach compares to recent work on approximating K from L [4] in terms of both computational cost and interpretability.

3. **Variational Inference Results:** The variational inference results on the synthetic Gaussian dataset (Section 5.1) show poor consistency for parameter κ (varying over several orders of magnitude), which suggests limitations in the approach. The paper attributes this to identifiability issues but doesn't explore whether the retrospective MH method could be improved or whether there are specific conditions under which the variational approach works better.

4. **Pseudo-input Selection:** The paper optimizes over pseudo-inputs Z to tighten the bounds, but doesn't thoroughly analyze how the choice of Z affects performance. A more detailed study of the sensitivity to initial Z positions or the optimal number of pseudo-inputs would be valuable.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. Add a quantitative comparison table showing runtime and accuracy for the proposed method versus [7] across different problem sizes (n, m).

2. Include a brief discussion on how to choose the number of pseudo-inputs m in practice, perhaps with a sensitivity analysis.

3. Extend the discussion on the L vs. K parametrization trade-off by comparing the interpretability of results when using L versus methods that approximate K.

4. In Section 5.1, provide more insight into why κ is poorly identified (is it truly unidentifiable, or is it a limitation of the variational approach rather than the bounds themselves?).

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP inference by addressing a significant computational bottleneck. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the approach has clear practical significance. While there are some limitations in the comparison with prior work and the discussion of identifiability, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful problem in the DPP literature.

I recommend **acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the constructive suggestions above in the camera-ready version. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP inference and provides a practical tool that could enable broader adoption of DPPs in machine learning applications.
