### Summary

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the calculation of the term `det(L + I)`, which typically requires a full O(n^3) spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix `L`. The authors propose novel, non-spectral bounds on this determinant for both finite and continuous DPPs. The core idea is to leverage a low-rank approximation `Q` of the kernel matrix `L`, constructed using a set of `m` "inducing inputs," a technique inspired by sparse Gaussian Processes (GPs). These bounds, derived from a matrix inequality by Seiler and Simon, can be computed efficiently in O(nm^2) time. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference frameworks: an approximate but fast variational inference (VI) scheme that maximizes the lower bound, and an asymptotically exact Metropolis-Hastings (MH) algorithm using retrospective sampling. The methods are validated on a synthetic 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP and a real-world spatial statistics dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contribution, while relying on a previously known determinant inequality, is its novel application to the DPP likelihood, which is a significant and non-trivial step. The derivation is presented for both the finite case (Proposition 1) and the more complex continuous case (Proposition 2), showing a good command of the underlying operator theory. The claims are well-supported by a combination of theoretical grounding and thoughtful experimental validation.

The experiments are a key strength. The 1D toy experiment is particularly well-chosen because the exact Fredholm determinant is computable, allowing for a direct comparison between the proposed MCMC sampler and an "ideal" MH sampler. This provides strong evidence for the correctness of the method. Furthermore, the authors are honest about the results: they show the MCMC sampler works well but also highlight a parameter identifiability issue and the failure of the simpler VI approach on this problem. This transparency is commendable. The real-world diabetic neuropathy experiment successfully demonstrates the practical utility of the VI approach on a larger problem, showing that parameter estimates converge as the number of inducing points increases, which is exactly the behavior one would hope for.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. It follows a logical progression, starting with the simpler and more common case of DPPs on finite sets before extending the ideas to the more technical continuous setting. This structure makes the core contribution accessible even to readers not deeply familiar with operator theory. The motivation for the work is laid out clearly in the introduction, highlighting the specific computational challenges that the paper aims to solve. The connection to sparse GPs is explicitly drawn, which helps ground the work in a familiar context for many in the NIPS community.

One area for minor improvement would be to provide more detail on the optimization of the inducing inputs `Z`. The paper mentions using CMA-ES and gradient-based methods, but more specifics on the optimization strategy (e.g., initialization, alternating vs. joint optimization of `θ` and `Z`, learning rates) would enhance reproducibility.

### Originality

The work is highly original. While DPPs and sparse GPs are established topics, the connection made between them to derive non-spectral likelihood bounds for DPPs is novel and insightful. Previous work on scalable DPP inference, such as Affandi et al. [7], also used bounds for MCMC, but those were *spectral* bounds, still requiring the computation of a subset of eigenvalues via methods like power iteration. This paper's main contribution is to bypass the need for *any* spectral information, which is a fundamental shift in approach. The extension of this inducing-point idea to continuous DPPs and the Fredholm determinant is also a novel contribution. The paper does a good job of positioning its work relative to prior art, clearly differentiating it from methods that rely on Nyström approximations or partial eigendecompositions.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be impactful. The computational cost of the eigendecomposition of `L` has been a primary barrier to the application of DPPs in large-scale settings. By providing a principled and efficient way to approximate the likelihood without spectral knowledge, this work makes DPPs significantly more accessible for parametric inference. The proposed methods are general-purpose and can be applied to any DPP model where the kernel `L` can be evaluated.

The dual offering of a fast, approximate VI method and a slower, asymptotically exact MCMC method is particularly valuable, as it allows practitioners to choose the right tool for their specific trade-off between computational budget and accuracy. This work could spur further research into scalable DPP models and their application in areas like diversified recommendation, summarization, and spatial modeling. The ideas presented here could become a standard technique for anyone performing likelihood-based inference with DPPs.

A potential weakness in the practical significance is the lack of a direct experimental comparison to the spectral-bound method of Affandi et al. [7]. While the conceptual advantage of avoiding power iteration is clear, a quantitative comparison of runtime and accuracy on the same problem would have made the paper's claims even stronger.

### Pros and Cons for Acceptance

**Pros:**
*   **Novelty:** Proposes a new, non-spectral approach to bound the DPP likelihood, cleverly adapting ideas from sparse GPs.
*   **Significance:** Addresses a fundamental computational bottleneck in DPP inference, making them more scalable.
*   **Generality:** Provides methods for both finite and continuous DPPs, and for both approximate (VI) and exact (MCMC) inference.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is theoretically sound and the experiments are well-designed, providing strong support for the claims.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Cons:**
*   **Missing Comparison:** Lacks a direct experimental comparison with the most relevant baseline (Affandi et al. [7]), which uses spectral bounds.
*   **Reproducibility Details:** Could provide more specific details on the optimization of the inducing point locations to improve reproducibility.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, significant, and well-executed piece of research that advances the state of the art in inference for determinantal point processes. It is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
