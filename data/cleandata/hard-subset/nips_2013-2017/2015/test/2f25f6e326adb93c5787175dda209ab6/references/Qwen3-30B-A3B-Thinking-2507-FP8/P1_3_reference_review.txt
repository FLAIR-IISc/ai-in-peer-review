

# Peer Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in determinantal point process (DPP) inference: the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L in likelihood evaluation, which is computationally prohibitive for large datasets or analytically intractable for continuous domains. The authors propose novel, nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that eliminate this requirement. While previous work [7] used spectral bounds requiring eigenvalue computations, this paper draws inspiration from variational inference for Gaussian processes [11] to develop bounds using "pseudo-inputs" (inducing points) that avoid spectral knowledge. The paper extends these bounds to both finite and continuous DPPs, demonstrating their application in variational inference and MCMC methods. This work builds upon and extends the growing literature on DPPs for machine learning [3, 4, 7], but specifically targets a critical computational bottleneck that has limited practical adoption.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods that work for both finite and continuous domains. The derivation of Propositions 1 and 2 is mathematically sound and leverages a nontrivial determinant inequality from [14].

2. **Practical Relevance**: The authors successfully demonstrate the approach on both synthetic and real-world data (diabetic neuropathy dataset), showing that their methods can handle practical inference problems without spectral knowledge. The experiments clearly illustrate the convergence of bounds as the number of pseudo-inputs increases.

3. **Clear Connection to Existing Work**: The paper effectively bridges DPP inference with Gaussian process literature, making the contributions accessible to a broader ML audience. The comparison with spectral-based methods [7] is well-motivated and highlights the advantages of the proposed approach.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors carefully validate their approach through multiple inference paradigms (variational inference and MCMC) and demonstrate both theoretical and practical benefits.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Previous Methods**: While the paper acknowledges the spectral-based approach of [7], it does not provide direct computational comparisons between their method and [7] on similar datasets. This would strengthen the claim about practical efficiency.

2. **Pseudo-Input Selection**: The paper doesn't fully address how to optimally select the pseudo-inputs (inducing points). While they optimize over Z in the experiments, the paper lacks theoretical guidance on how the choice of Z affects bound tightness or computational efficiency.

3. **Connection to Interpretability**: The paper mentions the interpretability issues of parametrizing L instead of K, but doesn't explore how their bounds might help bridge this gap, which would be valuable for applications where interpretability matters.

4. **Continuous Case Justification**: The extension to continuous DPPs, while mathematically sound, could better justify why the specific form of the bounds (Proposition 2) is the natural continuous extension of the finite case.

## Evaluation on Key Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs for the bounds. The experimental validation is thorough and demonstrates the practical viability of the approach. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, particularly regarding the parametrization of L instead of K. The paper represents a complete, well-executed piece of work rather than a position paper.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem statement to solution to validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The connection to Gaussian process literature is particularly helpful for readers unfamiliar with DPPs. Some minor improvements could be made to the continuous DPP section for better accessibility.

**Originality**: The nonspectral bounds represent a genuine novelty in DPP inference. While the "pseudo-input" approach is inspired by Gaussian processes, its application to DPPs without spectral knowledge is new. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work (e.g., [7]) and properly cites related contributions. The extension to continuous domains is particularly novel.

**Significance**: The work addresses a critical practical barrier to DPP adoption in real-world applications. By eliminating the need for spectral decomposition, the authors enable DPP inference in scenarios where previous methods would be infeasible. The demonstrated success on the diabetic neuropathy dataset shows clear potential for practical impact in applications requiring diversity modeling.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing literature on DPPs by addressing a fundamental computational bottleneck. The nonspectral bounds are theoretically sound, practically useful, and well-validated through experiments. While there are minor areas for improvement (particularly in comparing computational efficiency with previous methods and providing more guidance on pseudo-input selection), these do not detract from the paper's significant contribution.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it presents a novel technical approach to a well-motivated problem in machine learning with clear potential for real-world impact. The work advances the state of the art in DPP inference in a demonstrable way and provides a foundation for future work on scalable DPP modeling.

**Pro Acceptance**: Novel theoretical contribution addressing a practical bottleneck, strong experimental validation, clear connection to existing literature, significant potential for impact.

**Con Acceptance**: Limited direct comparison with previous spectral-based methods on computational complexity, some aspects of pseudo-input selection could be better explained.

Overall, I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to the field of machine learning with practical implications for diverse applications of DPPs.
