Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a key bottleneck in learning determinantal point processes (DPPs): evaluating det(I+L) in the likelihood, which typically requires eigendecompositions that are expensive or analytically unavailable, especially in continuous domains. The core contribution is a pair of nonspectral, inducing-point-based bounds on 1/det(I+L). In the finite case, with inducing inputs Z and low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, the authors derive bounds of the form det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I). They extend this to continuous DPPs via operator-theoretic analogs involving det(LZ), det(LZ+Ψ) and an exponential trace correction, where Ψ = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dμ(x). These bounds avoid any spectral computation.

They then show how to use these bounds for (i) a variational approach that replaces the intractable log-likelihood with a lower bound optimized over kernel parameters and inducing inputs, and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that tightens acceptance bounds by increasing m and optimizing Z until the decision is determined.

This connects to sparse variational GP methods (Titsias, 2009) in spirit—using inducing inputs to control approximation error—but is novel in the DPP context and particularly impactful for continuous DPPs where spectra are rarely known. Compared to Affandi et al. (ICML’14), which used partial spectral information to bound det(I+L), this paper removes the need for spectral computations entirely and applies to both finite and continuous settings.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: A clean, general, nonspectral approach to DPP likelihood bounding for both finite and continuous domains. The extension to Fredholm determinants is especially valuable.
- Conceptual clarity and portability: The inducing-point construction builds a bridge from variational GP ideas to DPPs, likely to inspire follow-up work.
- Algorithmic usefulness: The bounds are computable in O(n m^2), and the retrospective MH idea is well-adapted from earlier spectral-based MH to this nonspectral setting.
- Empirical indications: On a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where exact spectrum is known, retrospective MH matches “ideal” MH; on a real 2D neuropathy dataset, the variational method yields stable estimates of overdispersion as m increases, separating classes consistent with prior work.
- Practical angle: The paper emphasizes how to pick/optimize inducing inputs and shows they concentrate in informative regions.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical breadth: Experiments are limited. There is no head-to-head comparison against spectral bounding methods (when feasible), low-rank Nyström DPPs, or recent fixed-point/EM approaches on standard finite benchmarks (e.g., diversification tasks). The real-data study is valuable but narrow.
- Theoretical tightness and convergence: While the bounds are derived from solid determinant inequalities, the paper does not provide a formal result that the bounds converge to the true determinant (or likelihood) as m → ∞ under stated conditions, nor monotonicity guarantees with respect to m or optimized Z. The statement in Section 4 about posterior variance integrals is suggestive but not a proof.
- Optimization of Z: The paper relies critically on optimizing inducing inputs, both for variational training and retrospective MH. Practicalities (initialization strategies, nonconvexity, convergence guarantees) are not fully discussed, and the computational overhead per MH step could be substantial for complex models.
- Scope limitation for purely discrete DPPs: The method assumes access to a kernel function over a feature space to place Z off-support. For arbitrary finite DPPs specified only by an L matrix without a feature map, this approach is not directly applicable, whereas spectral methods are.
- Clarity and polish: There are several equation-numbering inconsistencies and typos; a careful editorial pass would help. Some assumptions in the continuous case (trace-class, computability of Ψ) could be stated more explicitly with practical guidance (e.g., when Gaussian assumptions do not hold).

Requests for clarification
- Can the authors provide conditions under which the bounds provably converge to the exact determinant as m increases and Z ranges over a dense set? Any monotone behavior with respect to m?
- For retrospective MH, how often does m need to grow in practice on larger problems? What is the amortized cost per accepted move compared to spectral-MH when spectra are partially available?
- For finite problems without a known feature map, can one recover a version of the method (e.g., by learning a feature map or using Nyström-style landmarks) and still retain the theoretical bounds?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a finite-domain benchmark comparing runtime/accuracy to spectral-bounds MH and to low-rank/Nyström DPP training.
- Provide ablations on Z initialization strategies and report sensitivity; consider a heuristic placement rule (e.g., k-means++) as a baseline.
- Strengthen theory with a convergence/tightness result as m → ∞ under standard kernel/measure assumptions, or at least establish conditions for asymptotic exactness.
- Discuss memory and computational complexities more explicitly, including dependence on T (number of realizations) and d.
- Improve exposition (fix numbering/typos) and add a short practical guide for choosing m and monitoring bound tightness.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a genuinely new, broadly applicable idea (nonspectral determinant bounds) that could change how DPPs are trained, especially in continuous domains.
- Conceptually elegant connection to inducing-point methods; likely to influence subsequent work.
- Empirical evidence, while limited, is consistent and demonstrates feasibility.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental evaluation is not comprehensive and lacks strong baselines.
- Missing formal tightness/convergence results and limited guidance on Z optimization complexities.
- Some editorial issues.

Overall rating: 7 (accept)
Rationale: The paper opens a promising direction with a clear, nontrivial idea that removes a long-standing practical bottleneck in DPP inference. Despite modest experimental breadth and some theoretical gaps about asymptotic tightness, the contribution is sufficiently novel and potentially impactful to merit acceptance.
