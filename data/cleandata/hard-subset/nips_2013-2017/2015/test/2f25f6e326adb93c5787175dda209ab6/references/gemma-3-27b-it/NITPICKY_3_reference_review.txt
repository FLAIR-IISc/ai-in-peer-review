This paper addresses the challenging problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decomposition, a significant bottleneck for large-scale applications. The core idea – leveraging bounds inspired by variational Gaussian Processes – is conceptually sound and directly tackles a practical limitation of DPP modeling. However, while the proposed approach holds promise, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and a lack of thoroughness in its experimental validation, ultimately leading me to recommend rejection.

The introduction is adequate, providing sufficient context and motivation. However, it lacks precision in stating the exact limitations of existing spectral methods; quantifying the computational cost (beyond “O(n^3)”) would be beneficial. The transition between parametrizing K versus L, while crucial, feels somewhat abrupt and could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs. 

Section 2 meticulously defines DPPs and their likelihood. The mathematical notation is correct, but the explanation of why parametrizing L is more tractable, despite losing interpretability, feels underdeveloped. The statement that "valid choices of θK are constrained" is vague; providing examples of such constraints would strengthen the argument. The core contribution – Proposition 1 – is presented clearly, but the reference to [14, Theorem 1] feels insufficient without briefly outlining the theorem’s core idea. 

Section 3 details variational inference and MCMC integration of the bounds. The connection to variational sparse GPs is well-articulated. However, the EM-like scheme mentioned lacks specifics. What are the update rules? How is convergence assessed? The description of the retrospective MH sampler, while accurate, is also terse. The discussion of potential optimization algorithms feels cursory.

Section 4 extends the results to continuous DPPs. The derivation and proposition are mathematically sound, but the assumptions required for validity (especially regarding the kernel L and base measure µ) are glossed over. The connection to Mercer’s theorem is mentioned, but a more detailed explanation of the required conditions would be valuable.

The experimental section (Section 5) is the weakest part of the paper. The toy Gaussian experiment demonstrates the MCMC sampler’s correctness *when the true parameters are known*. This is a minimal validation. The observation that κ is not well-identified is acknowledged, but not thoroughly investigated. Why is this the case? What modifications to the approach might address this? The diabetic neuropathy dataset experiment is more compelling, but the analysis is superficial. The claim of “coherent” convergence is not backed up by quantitative metrics. Showing the convergence curves for the parameters would be essential. Furthermore, the discussion of the optimized pseudo-inputs is limited to a qualitative observation about their arrangement – a more detailed analysis of their properties (e.g., their relationship to the data distribution) would be insightful. The statement regarding optimization speed is helpful, but lacks context. What hardware was used? What were the specific optimization settings?

Throughout the paper, minor stylistic issues abound. The frequent use of "e.g." feels excessive. Some sentences are awkwardly phrased. The figure captions are often incomplete or lack sufficient detail. The bibliography contains some formatting inconsistencies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a real and important problem in DPP inference.
*   Proposes a theoretically sound approach based on existing techniques.
*   Demonstrates the feasibility of the method on synthetic and real datasets.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Experimental validation is inadequate and lacks quantitative rigor.
*   The paper is riddled with minor flaws in presentation and completeness.
*   The discussion of limitations is insufficient.
*   The connection between the parameters of L and meaningful quantities related to the underlying point process is underdeveloped.
*   The paper feels incomplete, leaving many details unspecified.



In conclusion, while the core idea of this paper is promising, the lack of thoroughness in its development and evaluation, combined with the numerous minor flaws, lead me to recommend rejection. A significantly revised version, with a more rigorous experimental evaluation and a more detailed discussion of the theoretical aspects, might be reconsidered. The bar for NIPS is high, and this paper, in its current form, does not meet that standard.
