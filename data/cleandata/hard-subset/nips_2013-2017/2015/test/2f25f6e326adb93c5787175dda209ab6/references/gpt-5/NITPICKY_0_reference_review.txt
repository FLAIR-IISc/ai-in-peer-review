Summary
The paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decompositions. For finite domains, it proposes nonspectral lower/upper bounds on det(I+L) derived from a low-rank surrogate Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY built from m inducing inputs Z, inspired by Titsias’ variational sparse GP construction and a determinant inequality of Seiler–Simon. These bounds are then used to (i) form a variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood, optimizing jointly over kernel parameters θ and inducing inputs Z, and (ii) enable retrospective Metropolis–Hastings by tightening acceptance bounds through increasing m and re-optimizing Z. The methodology is extended to continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinants, yielding analogous bounds involving integrals Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x). Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian toy (where spectra are known) and a neuropathy dataset in 2D, demonstrating that the MCMC matches an “ideal” oracle in the toy case and that the variational estimates of dispersion stabilize as m increases on the real dataset.

Strengths
- Clear goal: avoiding spectral decompositions for DPP likelihoods, applicable in both finite and continuous settings.
- Technically motivated: uses determinant inequalities and the Q approximation familiar from sparse GPs; the construction seems broadly applicable.
- Practical utility: retrospective MH using nonspectral, tunable bounds is appealing when eigen-decompositions are infeasible.
- Continuous-domain treatment with Fredholm determinants and a class (Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure) where Ψ is tractable.
- Empirical sanity checks: (i) MCMC agreement with oracle on a case with known spectra; (ii) convergence of variational estimates as m increases; (iii) reasonable computational times reported.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and completeness:
- Missing theoretical guarantees on tightness and convergence of the bounds with m. The finite case trivially achieves equality with m = n and Z=Y (not stated), but in general there is no theorem guaranteeing that the bounds close as m→∞ for arbitrary Z or that optimizing Z yields monotone tightening. In the continuous case, the text states informally that the bounds match “under fairly weak assumptions,” equating it to integrated GP posterior variance going to zero, but provides no formal statement, conditions, or rates. This is critical for the retrospective MH to terminate almost surely and for variational consistency.
- The retrospective MH procedure relies on adaptively increasing m and re-optimizing Z, Z′ until log u exits the acceptance interval. While correctness follows if the final decision matches the oracle’s, there is no proof of almost sure termination or guidance on how m scales, especially in higher dimensions. Moreover, adapting Z and proposal covariance “on-the-fly” without a diminishing adaptation proof could in principle jeopardize ergodicity (Haario et al. assumes diminishing adaptation).
- Proposition 1 relies on Q ≤ L in the Loewner order to apply the determinant inequality. This should be stated and proved (Schur complement of the joint Gram on Y ∪ Z suffices). As written, the paper does not state the required PSD ordering explicitly, nor the conditions on L and Z to ensure L − Q is PSD.
- Complexity claims: computing tr(L − Q) costs O(n m^2 + m^3); the paper states O(n m^2) only. For large m, the m^3 term can be non-negligible. Likewise, the comparison to “power iterations in O(n^2)” is imprecise; Lanczos for the top m eigenvalues is typically O(m n^2) for dense matrices.
- Gradients: the variational section advocates gradient-based optimization over θ and Z but does not provide derivatives of F(θ,Z), discuss their stability, or address nonconvexity. In the toy experiment, CMA-ES is used and the variational method is reported to be unreliable (identifiability), but no diagnostics are provided (e.g., sensitivity to initialization, Hessian conditioning).
- Continuous case: Proposition 2 assumes trace-class and eq. tr(L) = ∫ L(x,x)dµ(x), but references and equation numbering are inconsistent. It would help to state conditions ensuring LZ is nonsingular and the operator inequalities needed for the determinant bounds. Also, the requirement “Ψ computable” is strong; guidance for approximate quadrature and its impact on bounds is missing.

Novelty and relation to prior work:
- The construction is closely analogous to Titsias’ variational GP bound, applied to the DPP normalization determinant; the determinant inequality is known (Seiler–Simon). The contribution is to bring these tools to DPP likelihoods and Fredholm determinants. This is useful, but the novelty is somewhat incremental compared to spectral-bounds-based DPP inference (Affandi et al.) and variational DPP learning (Gillenwater et al., Mariet & Sra). A more careful positioning versus Nyström-based DPP approximations (Affandi et al., 2013/2014) is necessary; the present Q is effectively Nyström-like with free Z, despite the claim “do not rely on a Nyström-type approximation.”

Experimental scope:
- No finite-domain experiments where n is large enough to demonstrate scalability versus spectral methods, nor timing/accuracy comparisons to existing spectral bounds (e.g., Affandi et al.). The continuous experiments are limited (1D synthetic; one 2D application) and lack ablations (e.g., varying n, m, runtime-accuracy curves, acceptance rates, termination behavior of retrospective MH, comparison with Bornemann’s quadrature where applicable).
- The neuropathy experiment reports convergence of γd as m increases, but no uncertainty quantification, and no comparison to spectral baselines used in prior work.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous notational and formatting issues that hinder readability:
  - In Sec. 2.1, K is stated “symmetric positive definite” but DPP requires eigenvalues in [0,1]; semidefinite would be appropriate. The 2×2 determinant uses K(xi,xi)K(yi,yi) with yi instead of xj.
  - Equation numbering is inconsistent throughout: e.g., `(θ) defined in (7)` but earlier it is (5); references to (8)/(13)/(14) do not match; “Figure 5.1” appears.
  - Proposition 1: spacing/typos “1det(L + I)”, missing slashes/spaces. Sec. 3.1 references maximizing “(8)” but F was (6).
  - Sec. 4.1: “Assume (14)” right after displaying (9). Several cross-references are off.
  - Sec. 5.1: parameters are written as (κ, α, ) with a missing symbol; later “resulting in 13 points shown in red in Figure 1(a)” but figures are not included; the base measure parameterization is unusual and unexplained.
  - Reference list is incomplete (many missing years/venues; typos: “Proccessing”, “Neural Information systems”; inconsistent NIPS spelling).
- The claim “bounds can be arbitrarily refined by increasing m” is not substantiated with theory or practical guidance for choosing/initializing Z; the observed “grid-like” Z is interesting but anecdotal.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant bottleneck in DPP inference with an elegant, broadly applicable idea.
- Extends to continuous DPPs with Fredholm determinants and gives implementable formulas for Gaussian–Gaussian cases.
- The retrospective MH adaptation using nonspectral bounds is practically appealing.
- Some empirical evidence of correctness and usefulness.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of theoretical guarantees on bound tightness and MH termination; missing key lemmas (PSD ordering of L − Q) and clarity on assumptions.
- Limited and unbalanced experiments; no convincing demonstration of superiority or scalability on finite-domain tasks; sparse comparisons to prior methods.
- Significant clarity and presentation issues, including pervasive typos, equation/reference mismatches, and incomplete references.
- Novelty is moderate; positioning w.r.t. Nyström-based DPP approximations and existing variational/spectral approaches needs sharpening.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a theorem (with assumptions) that the bounds converge as m→∞, both in finite and continuous settings, and conditions ensuring a.s. termination of the retrospective MH?
- Please state and prove that L − Q is PSD for your construction with arbitrary Z.
- How are gradients of F wrt θ and Z computed in practice? Any stabilization tricks?
- Can you add finite-domain experiments with large n and comparisons to spectral bounds/power methods, including runtime/accuracy?
- What adaptation scheme is used for the AM proposal, and does it satisfy diminishing adaptation?

Minor/comments
- Fix numerous typos and equation numbering; standardize notation (PSD vs PD; indices).
- Complexity should include O(m^3). Clarify costs for continuous Ψ (exact vs quadrature).
- Acknowledge the Nyström connection explicitly.

Overall recommendation
Reject. While the core idea is promising and relevant, the paper in its current form has too many technical, theoretical, and presentation shortcomings. Strengthening the theory (tightness/termination), clarifying assumptions, improving clarity, and adding substantive empirical comparisons would make this a strong submission.
