- Tackles likelihood-based inference for DPPs without spectral decompositions using inducing-point determinant bounds for finite and continuous cases.  
- Introduces non-spectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) via an inducing-point approximation Q, extending to Fredholm determinants with a computable Ψ in continuous settings.  
- Uses bounds for variational inference (tractable surrogate log-likelihood lower bound) and retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (adaptively tightening acceptance via increasing m and re-optimizing inducing inputs).  

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: clean, spectrum-free determinant bounds for finite and continuous DPPs.  
- Practical relevance: enables variational and MCMC inference when eigen-solvers are infeasible, especially on continuous domains.  
- Methodological simplicity: bounds depend only on kernel evaluations and traces and avoid storing full n×n matrices.  
- Clear connection to sparse GP literature (Titsias-style objective) making the method accessible.  
- Empirical sanity checks: toy 1D example validates retrospective MH; real spatial dataset shows variational recovery of overdispersion with increasing m.

Weaknesses / concerns:
- Tightness/convergence: upper bound can be very loose; no quantitative analysis of bound gap vs. m and no monotonicity or convergence theorem as m→∞.  
- Missing empirical comparisons to spectral methods and alternative non-spectral log-det estimators (e.g., stochastic trace/Lanczos), limiting assessment of trade-offs.  
- Limited experiments: no finite-ground-set experiments; continuous experiments are small (1D toy, tiny real dataset), and parameter instability (κ) is not deeply diagnosed.  
- Algorithmic details under-specified: optimization over inducing points Z lacks gradient derivations, initialization/regularization guidance, and complexity/caching analysis for repeated re-optimizations in MCMC.  
- Complexity claims incomplete: O(n m^2) per pass given, but end-to-end complexity with re-optimization and increasing m and memory footprints for large n are not analyzed.  
- Assumptions/operator details deferred: conditions ensuring L−Q PSD and applicability of determinant inequality are in the supplement and not summarized in main text.  
- Presentation issues: typos, reference/numbering errors, notational slips, and misplaced footnotes reduce readability.

Detailed suggestions (key demands):
- Provide a theorem or citation formalizing convergence of bounds as m→∞, ideally with monotonicity when augmenting Z.  
- Derive gradients of the variational objective w.r.t. θ and Z and give practical optimization details (initialization, step sizes, constraints).  
- Add finite-ground-set experiments comparing the bounds-based methods to spectral truncation/power iteration and stochastic log-det estimators, reporting runtime, memory, and accuracy.  
- For continuous case, give explicit Ψ for Gaussian–Gaussian and discuss other kernels/measures and quadrature strategies and costs.  
- Analyze how m affects MCMC efficiency: acceptance rates, average m per accepted move, and runtime versus ideal MH.  
- Discuss identifiability when parametrizing L (which parameters are estimable from single vs multiple realizations) and mitigation via priors/reparametrizations.  
- Clarify memory requirements for large n and suggest strategies (mini-batching, structured inducing grids, Toeplitz exploitation).  
- Compare to additional related work: stochastic Lanczos quadrature, pivoted Cholesky bounds, and other scalable kernel log-det approximations.

Minor fixes:
- Fix equation references, typos, notation (indices, arguments), and footnote placement.  
- Briefly explain the Seiler–Simon determinant inequality in the main text for reader accessibility.

Evaluation summary:
- Quality: solid core idea and sound inequalities but lacking theoretical guarantees on tightness/convergence and broader empirical validation.  
- Clarity: generally understandable but marred by missing derivations and presentation errors.  
- Originality: high — spectrum-free inducing-point bounds for DPP likelihoods appear novel.  
- Significance: potentially important for continuous and large discrete DPP inference if strengthened by theory and comparisons.

Pros (condensed):
- Novel, simple, practical spectrum-free bounds applicable to finite and continuous DPPs; enables variational and retrospective MH.

Cons (condensed):
- No theoretical convergence analysis, limited experiments and baselines, and missing optimization/implementation details.

Recommendation:
- Borderline accept; lean toward acceptance if authors fix derivations/presentation and add at least a small but meaningful comparison to spectral or stochastic log-det baselines.
