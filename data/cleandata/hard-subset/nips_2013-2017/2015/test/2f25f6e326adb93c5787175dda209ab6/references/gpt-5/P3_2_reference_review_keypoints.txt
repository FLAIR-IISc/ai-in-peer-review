- Paper proposes nonspectral, inducing-point-based two-sided determinant bounds for DPPs, covering finite and continuous domains, to avoid costly spectral decompositions.  
- Finite-domain result: with Q defined from pseudo-inputs Z, bounds satisfy det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I).  
- Continuous-domain result: analogous Fredholm determinant bounds using Ψ (Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x)) under trace-class assumptions.  
- Methods: use the lower bound in a variational lower bound for the DPP log-likelihood and the two-sided bounds for retrospective Metropolis–Hastings without eigenpairs.  
- Experiments: 1D Gaussian–Gaussian validates sampler against spectral MH; diabetic neuropathy dataset shows overdispersion estimates converging with increasing m.  
- Theory: finite-domain bounds are well-motivated via GP variational literature; continuous extension is nontrivial but plausible if technical assumptions hold; proofs are deferred to supplement.  
- Algorithms: variational objective is clear with complexity O(n m^2) plus per-sample dets; retrospective MH adaptation is standard and clean.  
- Empirical limitations: lacks large finite-domain benchmarks and quantitative comparisons to spectral or power-iteration-based methods where those are feasible.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well written and organized but has notational slips, equation reference errors, and omits practical details (gradients, regularization, Z initialization, per-iteration optimization cost).  
- Continuous-case practicality: need catalog of kernel/measure pairs with closed-form Ψ and discussion of numerical quadrature alternatives and their effect on bounds.  
- Originality: novel adaptation of inducing-point determinant bounds (with trace correction) to DPP likelihoods and novel continuous Fredholm determinant application.  
- Significance: removing spectral computations is valuable for large/continuous DPPs and enables scalable VI and exact-in-limit retrospective MH; broader validation and guidance on Z would strengthen impact.  
- Strengths: elegant nonspectral two-sided bounds, continuous extension, integration into VI and MH, validation against exact spectral MH, and real-data demonstration.  
- Weaknesses/questions: no theoretical rates for gap/tr(L−Q) vs m or stopping rules; unclear optimization details and sensitivity for Z; numerical stability of LZ and regularization effects; which kernels admit closed-form Ψ and whether quadrature preserves PSD/determinant bounds; MH overhead vs spectral MH; missing head-to-head timing/accuracy comparisons and discrete-domain experiments; scalability issues for computing det(LYt).  
- Pros/cons for acceptance: Pros—novel nonspectral bounds, continuous extension, practical algorithms; Cons—limited empirical breadth, missing theoretical/optimization guidance, practical details and proofs deferred.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept.  
- Confidence: High.
