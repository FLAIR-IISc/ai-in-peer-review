**Summary:**
This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), which stems from the need to compute a determinant term that typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix or operator. The authors propose non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant, applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs. The bounds are constructed using a set of `m` "inducing" or "pseudo" inputs, drawing a direct analogy to sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes. The paper then demonstrates how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference paradigms: a cheap-but-approximate variational inference (VI) scheme based on maximizing the lower bound, and an expensive-but-asymptotically-exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm using a retrospective sampling trick. The methods are evaluated on a 1D synthetic dataset and a 2D real-world spatial point pattern dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

**Strengths:**
1.  The paper tackles a well-known and significant problem in the practical application of DPPs. Avoiding spectral decomposition is a valuable goal that could broaden the applicability of these models.
2.  The proposed bounds are computationally cheaper (`O(nm^2)`) than spectral methods (`O(n^3)`), providing a clear path to scalability, especially when `m << n`.
3.  The connection drawn between sparse GPs and DPP inference is insightful and provides a principled way to approximate the DPP likelihood.
4.  The work considers both discrete and continuous DPPs, and demonstrates the utility of the bounds for both VI and MCMC, showcasing the flexibility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, while containing a promising core idea, is undermined by a significant number of flaws in its execution, ranging from critical clarity issues to unaddressed methodological weaknesses.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript is exceptionally difficult to read due to numerous and consistent errors in referencing and notation.
    *   The equation numbering is completely scrambled. For instance, in Section 3.1, the text refers to maximizing equation (8) when the relevant equation is labeled (6). In Section 3.2, references are made to (7) and (13), but the corresponding equations are (5) and (8). This pattern of incorrect cross-referencing persists throughout Section 4 as well. This is not a minor typo; it is a systematic error that forces the reader to constantly guess the authors' intent and severely disrupts the flow of the paper.
    *   There are careless typographical errors. In Section 2.1, the formula for `det K_{xi,xj}` incorrectly contains `K(yi,yi)` instead of `K(xj,xj)`. In the references, the entry for Titsias [11] is malformed as `[11], , ,`. These suggest a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   In Section 5.1, the text refers to "Figure 5.1" when the figure is labeled "Figure 1".

2.  **Methodological Rigor and Nuances:**
    *   The term "variational inference" is used loosely. The proposed method maximizes a lower bound on the marginal log-likelihood. While inspired by VI for sparse GPs, it does not seem to fit the standard framework of maximizing an Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) derived via Jensen's inequality from a specific choice of variational distribution. This distinction is important and should be clarified. Is it truly VI, or simply maximization of a deterministic lower bound?
    *   The extension to continuous DPPs in Section 4.2 hinges on the ability to compute the matrix `Psi`, whose elements are integrals of the form `∫ L(zi,x)L(x, zj)dµ(x)`. The authors acknowledge this is only feasible for specific kernel-measure pairs, such as the Gaussian-Gaussian case used in the experiments. This is a very strong limitation that significantly curtails the general applicability of the continuous-domain method. The paper understates the severity of this constraint.
    *   The optimization with respect to the inducing inputs `Z` is a complex, non-convex problem. The paper offers no discussion of potential issues like local optima, sensitivity to initialization, or the stability of the optimization process. The presented results, where `Z` forms neat grids, are interesting but may not be representative of the optimization landscape in general.

3.  **Originality:** The contribution, while useful, appears to be an assembly of existing components. The core determinant inequality is from Seiler and Simon [14], the use of inducing variables to form low-rank approximations is the central idea of sparse GPs [11], and the retrospective sampling MCMC technique for DPPs was introduced by Affandi et al. [7]. The main contribution is the specific application of the inequality from [14] to the DPP likelihood, which seems to be a straightforward, if clever, adaptation of the sparse GP framework. The novelty is therefore modest.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The experimental section presents a confusing narrative. In the synthetic experiment, VI is shown to perform poorly due to parameter identifiability issues, while MCMC succeeds. However, in the real-data experiment, only the "failed" VI method is used, with no justification for why MCMC was not applied. This leaves the reader with no clear guidance on which method is appropriate and when. A more thorough comparison of the two proposed inference schemes on both datasets is needed.
    *   A crucial baseline is missing. The primary motivation is to provide an alternative to the spectral methods of [7]. A direct comparison against that work in terms of runtime, accuracy, and the number of required eigenvalues (`m` in their work) versus inducing points (`m` here) is essential to concretely establish the superiority of the proposed non-spectral approach. Without this benchmark, the practical benefits remain asserted rather than proven.

**Decision:**
I recommend **rejection**.

While the paper addresses an important problem and the core idea is appealing, the execution is deeply flawed. The pervasive errors in equation referencing and notation make the paper almost unreadable in its current state and fall far below the standard expected for a NIPS publication. Beyond these presentational issues, the methodological discussion is superficial (e.g., regarding the optimization of `Z` and the limitations of the continuous case), the originality is limited, and the experimental evaluation is incomplete and lacks the necessary comparisons to contextualize the contribution. The paper has potential, but it requires a major revision to correct the extensive formatting errors, clarify the methodology, and provide a more comprehensive and convincing experimental validation.
