

# Review: Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the requirement for spectral decomposition of the operator L in likelihood evaluation. The authors propose novel, nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that eliminate the need for spectral knowledge, making likelihood-based inference feasible for both finite and continuous DPPs. The work extends previous approaches in variational inference for Gaussian processes (e.g., [11]) to DPPs, while also addressing the continuous domain case, which has not been fully explored in prior NIPS work.

Previous work on DPP inference (e.g., [7]) has relied on spectral bounds that require computing eigenvalues of L, which becomes computationally prohibitive for large or continuous domains. The authors' approach differs fundamentally by not requiring spectral decomposition at all, instead using "pseudo-inputs" to create bounds that can be optimized to tighten the likelihood estimates. This represents a significant departure from the spectral approach used in [7] and other recent NIPS papers on DPPs (e.g., [8, 9]).

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical contributions (Propositions 1 and 2) are rigorously proven using determinant inequalities from [14]. The bounds are well-motivated, with clear connections to existing GP variational methods. The extension to continuous domains (Section 4) is technically sound, with appropriate handling of Fredholm determinants.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively frames the problem, and the logical progression from finite to continuous domains is seamless. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The experimental section clearly demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed bounds.

3. **Novelty:** The paper provides a fundamentally new approach to DPP inference by eliminating the spectral requirement. While [7] used spectral bounds, this work removes that dependency entirely. The extension to continuous domains with bounds that don't require Fredholm determinant computation is particularly novel, as prior continuous DPP work (e.g., [4]) has not addressed this computational bottleneck.

4. **Significance:** The proposed methods make likelihood-based inference for DPPs practical for large-scale and continuous applications. The experiments on both synthetic and real-world diabetic neuropathy data demonstrate clear utility, with results converging as the number of pseudo-inputs increases. The approach enables exact MCMC inference (via retrospective sampling) and efficient variational inference, both valuable for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Interpretability Trade-off:** The paper acknowledges that parametrizing L instead of K reduces interpretability (Section 1), but does not propose a solution. This remains a limitation for applications where interpretability is crucial, such as in the forest modeling example cited in the introduction.

2. **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are well-conducted, they only test two datasets. More extensive comparisons with other inference methods (beyond the spectral bounds in [7]) would strengthen the claims about the method's superiority.

3. **Practical Guidance:** The paper lacks clear guidance on selecting the number of pseudo-inputs m for different problem sizes. While experiments show convergence with increasing m, the optimal m for different domain sizes is not systematically analyzed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides a fundamental solution to a well-known computational bottleneck in DPP inference
- Theoretical contributions are sound and well-supported
- Clear extension to continuous domains, which is significant for many applications
- Strong experimental validation with both synthetic and real data
- Demonstrates practical utility through variational inference and MCMC

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The interpretability trade-off between parametrizing L vs. K remains unaddressed
- Limited experimental scope compared to the theoretical contribution
- No comparison with alternative non-spectral inference methods for DPPs

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a strong technical contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling by solving a significant computational problem in DPP inference. The theoretical results are novel, well-proven, and extend the authors' previous work in a meaningful way. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the approach, with results converging as expected with increasing pseudo-inputs. While the interpretability trade-off is a limitation, it's a known issue in the DPP literature (as acknowledged by the authors) and doesn't undermine the core contribution.

The work is clearly within NIPS scope, addressing a statistical learning problem with significant computational and theoretical depth. The paper meets all four criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), provides a novel approach to a known problem (Originality), and offers a practical solution with clear applications (Significance).

The paper is an excellent contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling with DPPs, and I recommend acceptance.
