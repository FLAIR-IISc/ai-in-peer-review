

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary
This paper addresses a technical challenge in DPP inference: the need for spectral decomposition when evaluating the likelihood. The authors propose nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid eigendecomposition by using inducing inputs. The paper develops these bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, and demonstrates their use in variational inference and MCMC. The work builds on existing DPP literature (e.g., [3, 7]) and Gaussian process literature (e.g., [11]).

## Strengths
- The problem addressed is well-motivated and relevant to the DPP community.
- The approach is conceptually clean, extending variational GP methods to DPPs.
- The experiments demonstrate reasonable performance on both synthetic and real data.
- The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Notational inconsistencies (major issue)
The most critical flaw is the confusing and inconsistent use of notation. The paper uses "L" to denote both:
- The operator defined as L = (I-K)^{-1}K (in Section 2.1)
- The kernel (in Section 2.1: "parametrize K instead of K" and "kernel L" in Section 2.2)

This is a severe notational conflict. In Section 2.1, the authors state: "the likelihood (2) with matrix L = ((L(xi,xj))) corresponds to a valid choice of K". But L is defined as (I-K)^{-1}K, not the kernel. The paper should consistently use K for the kernel and L for the operator, but instead conflates them. This confusion permeates the entire paper and will mislead readers.

### 2. Technical errors in key equations
- Equation (2): P(Y = Y1) = det LY1 / det(L + I). This is incorrect. The standard DPP likelihood is det(K_{Y1}) / det(I + K), not with L. The authors have confused the kernel K with the operator L.
- Equation (10): P(...) = det((L(xi,xj))det(I + L) μ'(x1)...μ'(xn) is missing a division symbol between the determinants. It should be det(L_{xi,xj}) / det(I + L) μ'(x1)...μ'(xn).
- Proposition 1: The inequality direction appears suspect. For positive definite matrices, det(Q+I) ≥ det(L+I) when Q is a low-rank approximation of L, but the paper claims det(Q+I)e^{-tr(L-Q)} ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I). The upper bound is correct (since det(Q+I) ≤ det(L+I) when Q ≤ L), but the lower bound requires careful justification. The citation [14, Theorem 1] is insufficient without the exact statement.

### 3. Incomplete theoretical justification
- The paper states "the expected number of items in Y depends on aL and σL now" when using L instead of K. This is not rigorously justified. The expected number is tr(K) = tr(L(I+L)^{-1}), but the authors don't explain how this depends on the parameters.
- The claim that "the spectral decomposition of L is known [20]" for the Gaussian kernel example is vague. The reference [20] is not properly identified in the text, making it impossible to verify.

### 4. Formatting and presentation issues
- Inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "‖x− y‖ 2" should be "‖x− y‖^2").
- In Section 4.1, "the Fredholm determinant det(I+L), ∏∞i=1(λi+1)" should have a space before the comma.
- The reference [1] is cited as a paper but is actually a book (Daley and Vere-Jones, "An Introduction to the Theory of Point Processes").
- The paper uses "DPPs" and "DPP" interchangeably, which is inconsistent.
- Section 5.1 claims "the Fredholm determinant det(I + L) in this special case is a q-Pochhammer symbol" without providing the exact expression or reference.

### 5. Experimental issues
- In Section 5.1, the variational inference results are described as "poorly consistent" with κ varying over several orders of magnitude. However, the authors don't provide sufficient detail on the optimization procedure or hyperparameters to reproduce this result.
- The claim that "posterior and prior roughly coincide" for κ is not quantified (e.g., with posterior standard deviations or credible intervals).

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses an important problem in DPP inference and presents a theoretically interesting approach, the technical errors and notational inconsistencies are severe enough to undermine the paper's credibility. The confusion between kernel K and operator L is a fundamental flaw that permeates the entire paper. The incorrect equation (2) and the missing division in equation (10) are particularly problematic for a technical paper. The paper also lacks sufficient theoretical justification for key claims, and the presentation has numerous formatting issues.

These are not minor issues but rather substantial technical errors that would lead to confusion and misinterpretation by readers. While the core idea of using inducing variables for DPP inference is sound and builds on existing work, the paper's execution is flawed in critical ways.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper contains multiple severe technical errors that make it difficult to trust the results or understand the contributions. The notational inconsistencies and incorrect equations are particularly concerning for a technical machine learning paper. The authors should thoroughly revise the paper to resolve these fundamental issues before considering resubmission.
