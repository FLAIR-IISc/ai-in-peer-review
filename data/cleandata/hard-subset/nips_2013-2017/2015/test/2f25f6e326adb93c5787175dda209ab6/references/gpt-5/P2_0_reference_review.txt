Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a key bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): evaluating det(I+L) (or the Fredholm determinant in continuous settings), which typically requires spectral decompositions that are impractical for large finite problems and often unavailable for continuous kernels. The authors propose nonspectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) by introducing inducing (pseudo-)inputs Z and forming a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY (finite case) or an analogous construction involving Ψ = ∫ L(z_i,x)L(x,z_j)dµ(x) (continuous case). The bounds are inspired by variational sparse GP ideas (e.g., Titsias 2009) and rely on a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975). They show how to use these bounds to (i) build a variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood for approximate ML/VI, and (ii) implement retrospective Metropolis–Hastings without eigenvalues by iteratively tightening the bounds via increasing m and optimizing Z.

Relative to prior DPP work: Affandi et al. (ICML’13) and follow-ups rely on spectral bounds or partial eigendecompositions; Nystrom approximations have been used in finite settings but still tie to spectral surrogates and require storing the full kernel. This paper removes spectral dependence and extends to continuous DPPs with computable Ψ (e.g., Gaussian kernels with Gaussian measures). Empirically, the paper validates on (a) a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where “ideal” likelihoods and spectra are available, showing retrospective MH matches the gold standard and that κ is weakly identifiable under L-parameterization, and (b) a real 2D dataset (diabetic neuropathy), demonstrating that overdispersion measures γ_d estimated by the VI bound stabilize as m increases, and that the bounds tighten.

Quality
- Theory: The finite-domain bounds (det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I)) are plausible and well-motivated by the determinant inequality cited. The continuous-domain analog is carefully framed under trace-class assumptions with an explicit Fredholm determinant form. While proofs are in the supplement, the constructions align with standard operator theory and sparse GP methodology; the assumptions (trace-class, computable Ψ) are reasonable for common kernels/measures.
- Algorithms: The variational bound F(θ,Z) is straightforward to optimize; complexity O(n m^2) is a practical regime when m ≪ n. The retrospective MH scheme is standard and the adaptation is clear: maintain bracketing bounds on the acceptance log-ratio and refine m and Z until the decision is determined.
- Empirics: The experiments convincingly validate correctness of the MH approach on a case with known ground truth and show practical convergence of variational estimates and bound tightness on a real dataset. However, evaluation is limited in scope and scale (no large finite-domain experiments, limited comparisons to spectral baselines and runtime scaling).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized: finite-domain derivations first, then continuous, then applications to VI and MCMC. The connection to sparse GPs is explicit and helpful. A few areas could be improved:
- Provide more algorithmic detail for optimizing Z (gradients, initialization, constraints, stability of LZ inversion).
- Clarify when Ψ is tractable and give examples beyond Gaussian–Gaussian; discuss numerical quadrature for Ψ and its cost.
- Add pseudocode for the retrospective MH with clear stopping criteria and m/Z refinement strategy.
- Fix minor typos and inconsistent equation numbering; add missing years in references.

Originality
The core novelty is adapting inducing-point determinant bounds to DPP likelihoods and extending them to continuous DPPs, enabling both VI and nonspectral MH. To my knowledge, this nonspectral approach (with provable bounds and retrospective sampling) has not been developed for DPPs. The conceptual transfer from sparse GPs is not new per se, but the application, the specific determinant bounds, and the continuous Fredholm determinant treatment are original within the DPP literature.

Significance
Removing spectral dependence is impactful: it broadens applicability to continuous DPPs and large finite problems where eigen-computation is costly or infeasible. The VI and MH frameworks are standard and likely to be adopted if the code is available. That said, the empirical evaluation is not yet sufficient to fully establish practical superiority versus spectral/approximate baselines on large-scale finite tasks commonly seen in ML (e.g., diverse subset selection). The lack of theoretical rates on bound tightness in m (beyond the qualitative statement) also leaves open how to set m in general.

Strengths
- Addresses an important bottleneck for DPP inference with elegant, general bounds that avoid spectral information.
- Extends to continuous DPPs where spectra are rarely accessible, with a practical Ψ for Gaussian–Gaussian models.
- Enables both variational learning and provably correct retrospective MH acceptance without eigenvalues.
- Complexity and implementation details suggest practicality (O(n m^2)), and experiments show bound tightening and stable overdispersion estimates.
- Clear connections to sparse GP methodology and insightful discussion on parameter identifiability under L.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope: no large-scale finite-domain benchmarks; limited runtime and accuracy comparisons against spectral methods; no ablation on Z selection/initialization.
- Practicality in higher dimensions not fully demonstrated; cost of Ψ for non-Gaussian kernels/measures unclear.
- No quantitative analysis of bound gap vs m (e.g., convergence rates or a priori m selection).
- Some algorithmic details (gradients for Z, conditioning of LZ, constraints) and presentation polish are missing.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide gradients with respect to Z and θ, and comment on conditioning/regularization for LZ inversion?
- How do you initialize and update Z in MH? Does re-optimizing Z at each proposal induce bias or excessive cost?
- For non-Gaussian kernels/measures, how do you compute Ψ efficiently? Is numerical quadrature feasible and stable?
- Can you report scaling on a large finite-domain task (n ≳ 10^4) vs spectral baselines?

Pro/con for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods (finite and continuous).
- Solid theoretical framing; practical algorithms for VI and MH.
- Clear potential impact for continuous DPPs and large n.
- Experiments validate correctness and show useful behavior.

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation is modest; lacks large-scale finite benchmarks and strong comparisons.
- Some practical details and theoretical tightness analysis are missing.
- Parameter identifiability issues under L remain a concern for interpretability.

Overall rating: Weak accept
Rationale: The paper presents a meaningful theoretical/algorithmic advance—nonspectral determinant bounds enabling DPP inference in settings where spectral methods are impractical. While the experiments are limited, the contribution is clear, well-motivated, and likely to stimulate follow-up work and practical use, especially for continuous DPPs. Strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying implementation details would further solidify the case. Confidence: moderate.
