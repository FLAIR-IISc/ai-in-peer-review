Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decompositions. For a DPP parameterized via an L-kernel, the likelihood involves det(L + I), which is expensive to compute in the finite case and analytically intractable in the continuous case. The main contribution is to derive nonspectral upper and lower bounds on 1/det(L + I) using an inducing-point, low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY (with Z not necessarily a subset of the ground set), inspired by variational sparse Gaussian processes (Titsias, 2009). The finite-domain bound is:
det(Q + I) e^{-tr(L − Q)} ≤ 1/det(L + I) ≤ 1/det(Q + I).
An analogous bound is provided for continuous DPPs in terms of Fredholm determinants, requiring the computation of LZ and Ψij = ∫ L(zi, x)L(x, zj) dµ(x). These bounds are then used (i) to form a tractable variational lower bound to the log-likelihood by replacing −log det(L + I) with −log det(Q + I) + tr(L − Q), and (ii) to build a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) sampler by bounding the MH acceptance ratio and refining it by increasing m and re-optimizing Z until a decision is possible. Empirically, the paper presents a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian synthetic example (where spectral quantities are available for validation) and a small real dataset of nerve fiber spatial patterns, showing bound convergence with m and reasonable separation of classes via overdispersion measures γd = σd/ρd. The approach complements prior DPP learning methods that rely on spectral truncations (Affandi et al., 2013) or Nyström approximations by avoiding eigen-computation and extending to continuous domains.

Evaluation

Quality
- The determinant bounds are grounded in a known determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975) and mirror the structure of GP variational bounds. The finite-domain bound appears technically sound; the continuous analogue is derived under trace-class assumptions and computable Ψ (e.g., Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure).
- The variational objective is a valid lower bound to the log-likelihood and computable in O(n m^2) (dominant term for computing Q). The retrospective MH adaptation is standard and correctly adapted to use the proposed bounds.
- Limitations: no formal guarantees on the tightness of the bounds as m increases with optimized Z (beyond an informal analogy to GP posterior variance decay); termination of retrospective MH is not proven in finite time. The continuous-case applicability depends on availability/tractability of Ψ. Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and scale.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear, with a clean presentation of the finite case before the continuous extension. Some notation/typo issues (e.g., occasional equation numbering mismatches, Y used for both ground set and random subset) and incomplete references should be corrected. Practical details on optimizing Z and θ (e.g., gradient expressions) are brief.

Originality
- Using inducing-point, nonspectral determinant bounds specifically for DPP likelihoods (finite and continuous) and leveraging them for both variational and retrospective MH inference appears novel. The connection to GP variational methods is explicit and well-motivated. Prior DPP work typically relies on spectral decompositions or Nyström eigenvalue approximations; this avoids eigen-computation entirely and extends to continuous domains.

Significance
- The method addresses a known bottleneck in DPP learning and is potentially impactful for cases where spectra are unavailable or expensive, especially in continuous spaces. However, significance is tempered by the limited empirical validation: no large-scale finite-domain experiments, no direct comparisons to spectral/ Nyström-based baselines in runtime/accuracy, and only small real-data evaluation. Practical guidance on choosing m and optimizing Z is limited.

Pros
- Conceptually simple, general nonspectral bounds; avoids eigenvalue computations.
- Extends naturally to continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinants under reasonable assumptions.
- Works for both variational training and retrospective MH; the latter validated against an “ideal” MH in 1D.
- Ties to established variational GP methodology provide intuition and potential for further extensions.

Cons
- Empirical evidence is limited: small datasets, no large finite-domain benchmarks, no direct comparisons to spectral or Nyström methods.
- No theoretical rates or guarantees on bound tightness with increasing m and optimized Z; termination of retrospective MH not guaranteed.
- Continuous-case practicality hinges on computing Ψ; guidance for non-Gaussian cases is sparse.
- Variational method shows instability for certain parameters (e.g., κ), and the paper offers limited mitigation beyond moving to MCMC.
- Some clarity/notation issues and incomplete references.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide gradient derivations for F(θ, Z) w.r.t. θ and Z, and clarify optimization details (initialization of Z, constraints, regularization).
- Discuss theoretical conditions under which the bounds tighten as m grows and Z becomes dense (and, if possible, provide convergence statements or empirical tightness plots versus m).
- For continuous DPPs, catalogue kernel–measure pairs where Ψ is tractable, and discuss numerical strategies (quadrature, low-rank features) when it is not.
- Add finite-domain experiments at larger n with direct comparisons to spectral-truncation MCMC (Affandi et al., 2013) and Nyström-based methods, reporting wall-clock time, memory, and accuracy as functions of m.
- Provide practical guidance on choosing m and stopping rules for retrospective MH (e.g., schedules, expected refinements).
- Address identifiability and sensitivity to parameterization (e.g., κ) by considering reparameterizations or informative priors; report posterior correlations.

Recommendation: Borderline accept. The paper presents a technically sound and conceptually valuable contribution—nonspectral determinant bounds enabling DPP inference without eigen-computations, including continuous domains. To strengthen impact, broaden the empirical evaluation and add theoretical or empirical guidance on bound tightness and practical deployment.

Confidence: Moderate.
