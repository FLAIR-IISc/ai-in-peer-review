**Summary**

This paper addresses the computational challenge of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), specifically the calculation of the `det(L + I)` term. Current state-of-the-art methods, such as Affandi et al. (2014), rely on spectral bounds that require iterative computation of eigenvalues. The authors propose a novel alternative: non-spectral bounds on this determinant. The core idea is inspired by variational methods for sparse Gaussian Processes (e.g., Titsias, 2009), introducing a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" to form a low-rank approximation of the kernel matrix `L`. This approximation yields both an upper and a lower bound on the determinant, which can be computed in O(nm^2) time for the finite case. The paper demonstrates how these bounds can be incorporated into two standard inference schemes: a variational inference (VI) approach that maximizes the lower bound, and a Metropolis-Hastings MCMC approach that uses the bounds for retrospective sampling to make acceptance decisions. The proposed framework is developed for both finite and continuous DPPs, with experiments on a synthetic 1D continuous problem and a real-world 2D spatial point process dataset.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The theoretical foundation of the paper appears sound. The derivation of the bounds from a known matrix inequality [14] is a clever application of an existing result to a new domain. The extension from the finite to the continuous case is also handled with appropriate rigor.

However, the empirical validation of the proposed methods is unconvincing and raises significant concerns.
1.  In the synthetic experiment (Sec 5.1), the variational inference method is shown to produce "poorly consistent results," which the authors attribute to parameter identifiability issues with the L-kernel parametrization. While this identifiability problem is known, presenting a new inference method that fails on a simple, controlled toy problem is a major weakness. It immediately casts doubt on the general utility and robustness of the proposed VI approach.
2.  The MCMC method performs well on the same toy problem, matching the results of an "ideal" MCMC sampler. While this is positive, it is a very simple 1D case. There is no analysis of how the method's performance or computational cost scales with the dimensionality of the problem or the complexity of the data distribution.
3.  Most troublingly, the real-world experiment (Sec 5.2) exclusively uses the VI method—the same method that was shown to be unreliable in the synthetic experiment. This is a contradictory and questionable experimental choice. Why was the more robust MCMC method not applied here? The results show that parameter estimates converge as the number of inducing points `m` increases, which is an expected and necessary sanity check for the approximation. However, the experiment lacks a crucial component: a comparison to a relevant baseline. The most obvious baseline would be the spectral method from Affandi et al. (2014), which was used on the same dataset. Without this comparison, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers any advantage in terms of accuracy, computational cost, or final inferred parameters. The claim that the method is faster than spectral approaches is asserted but not empirically demonstrated.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The motivation is well-established, and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow for a reader familiar with DPPs and GPs. The distinction between the finite and continuous cases is helpful for accessibility.

**Originality:** The core contribution—the application of non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds to the DPP likelihood—is novel. However, the surrounding inference framework is heavily based on established techniques. The variational approach is a direct analogue of sparse GP methods, and the MCMC retrospective sampling technique is explicitly adapted from Affandi et al. (2014). Therefore, the work can be seen as replacing one approximation component (spectral bounds) with another (inducing point bounds) within existing inference machinery. While this is a valid contribution, it feels more incremental than foundational.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work hinges on whether it provides a demonstrably better way to perform inference for DPPs. A scalable, non-spectral inference method would indeed be a valuable contribution. Unfortunately, the paper fails to provide the evidence needed to make this case. The lack of a direct comparison to established spectral methods [7] is a critical omission. Without evidence of superior speed, accuracy, or scalability, the impact of the proposed method is unclear. Practitioners and researchers are unlikely to adopt this new approach without a compelling reason, which the current experimental results do not provide.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and technically sound method for approximating the DPP likelihood determinant, avoiding the need for eigendecomposition.
*   The connection drawn between sparse GPs and DPP inference is elegant and potentially fruitful for future work.
*   The method is presented for both finite and continuous DPPs, demonstrating its generality.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental validation is critically weak. The main competitor and prior art [7] is not used as a baseline, making it impossible to evaluate the proposed method's practical utility.
*   The proposed VI method is shown to be unreliable on a simple synthetic problem, yet it is the only method used in the more complex real-data experiment. This is a major flaw in the experimental design.
*   The claims of computational advantage are not substantiated with empirical timing comparisons against spectral methods.
*   The contribution feels incremental, as it primarily adapts well-known approximation techniques from other fields (GPs) and plugs them into an existing inference algorithm (retrospective MCMC from [7]).

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound idea, it is not yet a complete piece of work. The lack of rigorous and comparative experimental validation undermines the paper's claims and makes it difficult to assess the true value of the contribution. I would recommend rejecting the paper in its current form but would be open to reconsidering a revised version with a substantially improved and more convincing experimental section.
