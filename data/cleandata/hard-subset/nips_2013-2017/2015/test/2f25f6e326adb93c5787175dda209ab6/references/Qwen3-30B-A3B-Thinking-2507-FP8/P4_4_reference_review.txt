

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses a significant bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the requirement for spectral decomposition of the operator L in the likelihood evaluation. While DPPs have gained traction as modeling tools for diverse applications (e.g., news summarization [3], search engine results [7], spatial tree patterns [4]), the computational cost of spectral decomposition has limited their practical application, particularly for large datasets or continuous domains. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood that do not require spectral knowledge, enabling efficient variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods.

The paper makes two primary contributions. First, for finite sets, the authors derive Proposition 1, which provides nonspectral bounds on the likelihood using pseudo-inputs (inducing points), extending variational sparse Gaussian process techniques to DPPs. Second, they generalize these bounds to continuous domains (Proposition 2), providing computable bounds on the Fredholm determinant required for continuous DPP likelihoods. The authors then demonstrate how these bounds can be incorporated into variational inference and retrospective MCMC methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The connections to Gaussian process literature are well-explained, making the technical contributions accessible to readers familiar with GP methods. The theoretical development is sound, with proper references to determinant inequalities used in the proofs. The experimental section validates the approach on both synthetic and real datasets (diabetic neuropathy spatial patterns), demonstrating convergence of inference results as the number of pseudo-inputs increases. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, particularly the interpretability issues when parametrizing L directly rather than K.

Strengths include the clear identification of a practical bottleneck in DPP inference, the technically sound theoretical development of nonspectral bounds, and the effective extension to continuous domains. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the method's applicability to real-world problems. The paper's focus on computational efficiency without spectral decomposition directly addresses a key limitation in previous work [7], which relied on spectral bounds requiring power iteration methods.

Weaknesses include limited comparison of computational complexity between the proposed method and previous approaches (e.g., [7]), particularly in terms of time complexity. While the paper mentions that the variational approach requires O(nm²) time (where m is the number of pseudo-inputs), it doesn't provide a concrete comparison with the O(n³) complexity of direct spectral decomposition. The paper also doesn't deeply explore how the choice of pseudo-inputs affects the tightness of the bounds, though it does optimize over them. The interpretability issue with parametrizing L instead of K is acknowledged but not fully addressed as a potential limitation for practitioners.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a computationally efficient approach to DPP inference that doesn't rely on spectral decomposition. This addresses a well-known limitation in the literature and enables DPPs to be applied to larger datasets and continuous domains where spectral methods are intractable. The method is both theoretically sound and practically validated, making it likely to be useful for practitioners working with DPPs. The extension to continuous domains is particularly valuable, as it broadens the applicability of the method beyond the finite case.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work: it builds on [7] (which used spectral bounds) but overcomes its computational limitations, and it connects to Gaussian process literature [11] through the variational framework. The authors properly cite relevant prior work on DPPs [3, 4, 5, 6, 7] and Gaussian processes [10, 11].

In terms of significance, this work advances the state of the art by providing a practical inference method for DPPs that removes a major computational bottleneck. The approach could enable more widespread use of DPPs in applications where spectral decomposition is intractable, particularly in large-scale or continuous settings. The experimental results on the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrate the method's utility for real-world problems.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses an important practical limitation in DPP inference. The method provides a valuable alternative to spectral-based approaches, with demonstrated effectiveness on both synthetic and real data. While the computational complexity comparison could be more detailed, the paper's contributions are substantial and well-supported.

Pro: Addresses a significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference that limits practical applications, provides sound theoretical bounds, extends to continuous domains, demonstrates practical utility on real data.

Con: Limited computational complexity comparison with previous methods, interpretability limitations of parametrizing L instead of K not fully explored, limited discussion of how pseudo-input selection affects bound tightness.

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling with DPPs. It provides a practical solution to a well-known problem, with sound theory and convincing empirical validation. The work is technically rigorous, well-written, and addresses a significant gap in the literature. I recommend acceptance.
