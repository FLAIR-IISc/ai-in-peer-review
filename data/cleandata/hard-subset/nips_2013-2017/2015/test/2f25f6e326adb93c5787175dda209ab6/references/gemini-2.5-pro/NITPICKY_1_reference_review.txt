**Summary:**
This paper proposes a novel method for likelihood-based inference in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) that avoids the computationally expensive spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix L. The central contribution is a set of non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the determinant term `det(L + I)` that appears in the DPP likelihood. These bounds are inspired by variational methods for sparse Gaussian Processes, utilizing a set of "pseudo-inputs" or inducing points `Z`. The authors derive these bounds for DPPs on both finite sets and continuous domains. They then demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference paradigms: approximate variational inference (VI) by maximizing the lower bound, and an "exact" MCMC approach using retrospective sampling. The methods are evaluated on a 1D synthetic dataset and a 2D real-world spatial statistics dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

**Strengths:**
1.  The core idea of adapting inducing-point methods from the Gaussian Process literature to derive non-spectral bounds for DPP likelihoods is novel and elegant. It addresses a well-known and significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
2.  The extension of the proposed bounds to the case of continuous DPPs is a valuable contribution, broadening the applicability of the method.
3.  The paper is generally well-structured, motivating the problem clearly and separating the derivations for the finite and continuous cases.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, while containing a promising core idea, is beset by a considerable number of errors, inconsistencies, and points of ambiguity that severely detract from its quality and call into question its readiness for publication.

1.  **Lack of Careful Proofreading:** The manuscript is riddled with typographical and referential errors, suggesting a hasty preparation. This lack of attention to detail undermines the reader's confidence in the technical results.
    *   In Section 2.1, the formula for the determinant of a 2x2 matrix contains a typo: `K(yi,yi)` should clearly be `K(xj,xj)`.
    *   In Section 3.1, the text instructs the reader to "maximize (8)", but it is clearly referring to the objective function `F(θ,Z)` defined in equation (6).
    *   In Section 3.2, the text refers to equation "(13)" three separate times when it should be referring to equation (8). This is a significant copy-editing failure.
    *   In Section 4.1, the crucial likelihood formula in equation (10) is syntactically incorrect, appearing as `det((L(xi,xj))det(I + L)` instead of `det((L(xi,xj))) / det(I + L)`.
    *   In Section 5.1, the caption for Figure 1 incorrectly refers to itself as "Figure 5.1".
    *   The numbering of equations seems to have been corrupted; for instance, the text refers to equations (14), (15), and (16), but the corresponding displayed equations are numbered (9), (10), and (11).
    *   The reference list is inconsistently formatted (e.g., inconsistent use of terminal punctuation, missing author initials in [20], spurious commas in [11]).

2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility Issues:** Several key aspects of the methodology and experiments are insufficiently detailed.
    *   The parameterization of the kernels used in the experiments is ambiguous. In Section 5.1, the squared exponential kernel is given as `exp ( − 2‖x− y‖2 )`, which has no free parameters, yet the authors proceed to infer a parameter vector `θ = (κ, α, )`. The role of the third parameter is never explained, and the relationship between `α` and the kernel form is unclear. This makes the experiment impossible to reproduce. A similar ambiguity exists for the model in Section 5.2.
    *   The optimization of the inducing points `Z` is a critical component of the method but is described superficially. The paper mentions "an EM-like scheme", "gradient-based methods", and "CMA-ES" as possibilities. For the MCMC experiment, it states that `Z` is re-optimized, but provides no details on the cost or method of this optimization. How is this inner-loop optimization performed? Is it guaranteed to find a good local optimum? Its cost is essential for evaluating the overall efficiency of the MCMC scheme.
    *   The initial presentation in Section 2.1 is confusing. The paper defines a DPP via a kernel K in (1) and then immediately pivots to a likelihood based on a different kernel L in (2). While the two are related, the transition is abrupt and could be clarified for readers less familiar with the dual parameterizations of DPPs.

3.  **Methodological and Experimental Shortcomings:**
    *   A major weakness of the proposed VI approach is highlighted by the authors' own experiment in Section 5.1, where it "yields poorly consistent results". While they attribute this to parameter identifiability, it could equally suggest that the variational lower bound `F(θ,Z)` is a poor proxy for the true log-likelihood surface, containing local optima that do not correspond to the true maximum. This failure significantly tempers enthusiasm for the VI method presented.
    *   The paper's main claim is to be an improvement over spectral methods like those in [7]. However, there is no direct empirical comparison. A quantitative comparison of wall-clock time and accuracy (e.g., posterior quality) against the spectral-bound MCMC from [7] on a shared task is a critical missing piece of evaluation.
    *   The qualitative observation that optimized pseudo-inputs `Z` form neat grids is interesting but remains an unsubstantiated anecdote. A deeper analysis or theoretical justification for this phenomenon would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and potentially impactful method for making DPP inference more scalable by avoiding spectral decompositions.
*   The theoretical contribution is sound, based on a known determinant inequality, and its application to DPPs in both finite and continuous settings is a solid piece of work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript is unacceptably sloppy, with numerous errors that suggest it was not carefully reviewed before submission.
*   Key experimental details are missing, preventing reproducibility and a full assessment of the method's practicality.
*   The empirical evaluation is incomplete. It lacks a direct comparison to the most relevant baseline ([7]) and demonstrates that the proposed VI method can fail, which is a significant negative result that is not sufficiently explored.
*   The paper acknowledges the critical interpretability issue of parameterizing the L-kernel instead of the K-kernel but makes no progress towards solving it, framing it merely as a necessary compromise.

**Recommendation:**
While the central idea of this paper is promising, the execution is deeply flawed. The sheer volume of typos, referential errors, and methodological ambiguities makes the paper fall well short of the standard expected for NIPS. The experimental evaluation is also insufficient to fully support the claims of the paper's utility. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors have a strong core concept, but the paper requires a major revision to address the extensive list of flaws in its presentation, clarity, and evaluation before it can be considered for publication.
