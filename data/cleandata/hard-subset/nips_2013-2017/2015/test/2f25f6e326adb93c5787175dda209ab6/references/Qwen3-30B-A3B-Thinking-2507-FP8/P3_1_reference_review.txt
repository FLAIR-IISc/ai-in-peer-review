

# Peer Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary and Context:**
This paper tackles a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the requirement for spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood that avoid this expensive computation, drawing inspiration from variational inference techniques for Gaussian processes. The work extends to both finite and continuous domains, with promising experimental results on synthetic and real datasets. While prior work [7] also uses bounds for DPP inference, this paper claims to eliminate the need for spectral knowledge entirely.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing computationally tractable bounds for DPP likelihoods that avoid spectral decomposition. The bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) are derived using a nontrivial determinant inequality, and their effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.

2. **Practical Impact:** The proposed approach enables efficient variational inference and MCMC for DPPs without spectral knowledge, which is a significant practical improvement over previous methods. The experiments on the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrate clear utility in a real-world application.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured with clear mathematical derivations. The connection to Gaussian process variational inference is well-explained, and the extension to continuous domains is logically presented.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Prior Work:** The paper claims its bounds "do not require any eigendecomposition," but [7] also uses bounds that require only a few eigenvalues (not the full spectral decomposition). The paper fails to provide a clear computational complexity analysis comparing their approach with [7], making it difficult to assess the claimed advantage.

2. **Identifiability Issues:** The paper acknowledges (Section 5.1) that parametrizing L directly leads to identifiability problems (e.g., κ is not well-learned), but doesn't adequately address this fundamental limitation. The authors should discuss how this affects the practical utility of their approach and whether there are principled ways to address it.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks a formal analysis of bound tightness as a function of the number of pseudo-inputs m. How do the bounds behave as m increases? What is the convergence rate? This would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

4. **Interpretability Trade-off:** While the paper mentions the interpretability trade-off of using L instead of K (Section 2.1), it doesn't explore how this affects downstream applications. For example, in the forest application [4], the repulsiveness parameter is more interpretable when parametrizing K. The authors should discuss this practical impact.

**Specific Suggestions:**

1. Add a subsection discussing the identifiability issues with parametrizing L directly, and how the proposed method interacts with this problem.

2. Provide a detailed computational complexity analysis comparing the proposed approach with [7], including empirical runtimes.

3. Add a theoretical analysis of bound tightness as a function of m (number of pseudo-inputs).

4. Discuss the practical implications of the L vs. K parametrization choice for specific applications mentioned in the introduction.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound paper that addresses a real computational bottleneck in DPP inference. The work advances the state of the art by providing a computationally efficient alternative to spectral decomposition. However, the paper's claims about advantages over prior work need stronger justification, and the fundamental limitations of parametrizing L instead of K require more careful discussion.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*Pros:*
- Novel nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods
- Practical implementation for both finite and continuous domains
- Demonstrated utility on real-world dataset
- Clear and well-structured presentation

*Cons:*
- Insufficient comparison with prior work [7]
- Inadequate discussion of identifiability issues with L parametrization
- Lack of theoretical analysis of bound tightness
- Limited exploration of interpretability trade-offs

**Recommendation:**
Weak Accept (with major revisions). The paper presents a valuable technical contribution but requires significant revisions to address the concerns about differentiation from prior work, identifiability issues, and theoretical analysis. With these revisions, this would be a strong contribution to the field.
