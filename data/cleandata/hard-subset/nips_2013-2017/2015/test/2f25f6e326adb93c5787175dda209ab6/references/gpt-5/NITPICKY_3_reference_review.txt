Summary and contributions
The paper proposes nonspectral upper and lower bounds on the determinants that appear in DPP likelihoods to avoid eigendecompositions. For finite ground sets Y, the authors introduce inducing inputs Z and a Nyström-like low-rank matrix Q = LYZ L−1 Z LZY. Using a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975), they state bounds on 1/det(I+L) in terms of det(I+Q) and tr(L−Q), leading to a computable lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood that can be optimized variationally over both kernel parameters θ and inducing inputs Z. They also adapt these bounds to an MH sampler using retrospective acceptance. The approach is then extended to continuous DPPs with base measure μ, yielding analogous bounds involving LZ and Ψ = ∫ L(z_i,x)L(x,z_j)dμ(x). Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian–Gaussian 1D setup (where the spectrum is known) and a diabetic neuropathy dataset in 2D are presented. The synthetic study suggests variational learning may be unreliable due to identifiability of κ, whereas retrospective MH recovers parameters; the real-data study shows estimated overdispersion measures converge with m and separate classes, with runtimes reported.

Relation to prior work
- Prior DPP learning approaches often rely on spectral knowledge or computing eigenpairs (Affandi et al., 2013; Gillenwater et al., 2014; Mariet & Sra, 2015). This paper aims to avoid spectral computations entirely.
- The core idea is inspired by variational sparse GP bounds (Titsias, 2009) and the matrix determinant lemma, adapted to DPP likelihood determinants.
- For continuous DPPs, Bornemann (2010) provides numerical Fredholm determinant evaluation; authors propose an alternative bound-based route.
- Not discussed but relevant: nonspectral log-det approximation/estimation methods (e.g., stochastic trace/Lanczos approaches) that could be competitive baselines.

Strengths
- Addresses a practical bottleneck in DPP inference: avoiding eigendecompositions in both finite and continuous domains.
- Clean, general mechanism using inducing inputs that is conceptually aligned with sparse GPs.
- Extension to continuous DPPs with explicit Ψ is useful, especially under Gaussian kernels and Gaussian base measures where Ψ is tractable.
- Retrospective MH adaptation is a reasonable idea and is validated against an “ideal” MH on a setup where the determinant is known.
- The diabetic neuropathy case study suggests the variational approach can yield coherent overdispersion measures as m grows, with moderate runtimes.

Weaknesses and technical issues
- Potential sign mistake in the variational bound. Proposition 1 states det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I). The log-likelihood is ∑_t log det(L_Yt) − T log det(I+L) = ∑_t log det(L_Yt) + T log(1/det(I+L)). Using the stated lower bound on 1/det(I+L) yields a lower bound F(θ,Z) = ∑_t log det(L_Yt) + T[log det(Q+I) − tr(L−Q)]. However, Eq. (6) in the paper defines F(θ,Z) = ∑_t log det(L_Yt) − T log det(Q+I) + T tr(L−Q) ≤ `(θ), which flips the signs of both terms. As written, Eq. (6) does not follow from Proposition 1. This is a serious correctness/clarity issue that must be fixed.
- Missing assumptions for the bounds: For the finite case, the Schur complement argument requires L_Z ≻ 0 and L − Q ≽ 0. These conditions (and continuity/PSD assumptions) should be stated explicitly.
- Complexity claims: The O(n m^2) claim for computing F hinges on efficient evaluation of log det(I+Q) via the matrix determinant lemma and tr(Q) via trace-cyclic properties. Derivations are not shown; explicit formulas and costs for computing tr(L) and tr(Q) without forming L would improve reproducibility. For continuous DPPs, the cost of Ψ and tr(L) should be detailed.
- Experimental evaluation is limited.
  - No comparison against spectral methods (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013) in runtime/accuracy on finite problems.
  - No ablation on optimizing Z vs. fixed Z vs. Nystrom subsets; no quantitative tightness analysis of the bounds as m increases (e.g., gap sizes).
  - On the synthetic 1D case, results are largely qualitative; show quantitative errors (posterior KLs, acceptance rates vs m, bound gaps) to substantiate claims.
  - On the diabetic dataset, there is no held-out likelihood/predictive task; only overdispersion measures are reported.
- Claims of generality for the continuous case hinge on Ψ being computable; beyond Gaussian–Gaussian, this may not hold. The scope should be explicitly delimited.
- MCMC practicality: Retrospective MH requires repeatedly increasing m and re-optimizing Z for θ and θ′; this could be very costly in higher dimensions. Provide empirical costs and convergence diagnostics.
- Clarity and formatting issues (numerous and substantial):
  - Notational errors: xi ⊂ Rd should be xi ∈ Rd; detK{xi,xj} formula mixes yi and xj and has K(xi,xj) 2 instead of K^2_ij; inconsistent use of subscripts/superscripts.
  - Equation numbering and cross-references are broken throughout: references to (7), (8), (13), (14)–(16) don’t match; Figure 5.1 referenced; footnotes (1–4) leak into the main text.
  - Several typos and grammatical issues (“Proccessing”, “systems”, “repartition” instead of “distribution”, strange bracket in MH acceptance formula).
  - Many references miss years or complete bibliographic data.
- Originality: While useful, the core technique is a fairly direct adaptation of inducing-point GP bounds with known determinant inequalities. The continuous extension is a more interesting addition but still rests on standard operator-theoretic identities.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Clarify and correct the variational bound (Eq. (6)) vis-à-vis Proposition 1; provide a complete derivation. Similarly, clearly state the conditions under which Proposition 1/2 hold (PSD, invertibility, trace-class).
- Provide explicit formulas and algorithms for computing log det(I+Q) and tr(L−Q) with their costs; detail how gradients w.r.t. Z and θ are computed (and whether you used analytic gradients or black-box optimizers in each experiment).
- Quantify bound tightness vs m on both datasets (e.g., relative gap) and report retrospective MH statistics (average m, optimization iterations per decision).
- Compare to spectral baselines where feasible (finite Y or 1D continuous) and to stochastic log-det methods (trace/Lanczos) as additional nonspectral alternatives.
- Discuss when Ψ is tractable beyond Gaussian–Gaussian and how one might proceed otherwise (numerical quadrature?).
- Clean up notation, equation numbering, figures, and references substantially.

Pros
- Practical, spectrum-free scheme for DPP likelihoods in both finite and continuous settings.
- Retrospective MH adaptation is sensible and validated on a case with known ground truth.
- Inducing-input perspective connects DPPs to well-studied sparse GP machinery.

Cons
- Apparent sign error in the central variational bound; missing assumptions.
- Limited and mostly qualitative experiments; no strong baselines or quantitative tightness/runtime evaluations.
- Heavy clarity/formatting issues that hinder verification and reproducibility.
- Generality of continuous case limited by availability of Ψ in closed form.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is promising and potentially useful, but the current submission has a likely technical error in the key bound, lacks important assumptions/derivations, and suffers from significant clarity issues. With corrected theory, stronger experiments, and thorough editing, this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
