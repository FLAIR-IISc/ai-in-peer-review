

# Peer Review: "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary and Context
This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in parametric inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix L when evaluating the likelihood. The authors propose novel nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that avoid eigendecomposition, enabling efficient variational inference and MCMC for both finite and continuous DPPs. The work builds on connections to variational sparse Gaussian processes [11], extending these ideas to DPPs while addressing a critical limitation in existing DPP inference methods [7].

## Strengths

The paper makes a well-motivated contribution to an important problem in DPP inference. The theoretical foundation is sound, with rigorous proofs of the key bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) derived from determinant inequalities. The extension from finite to continuous domains is particularly valuable, as most prior work focuses on discrete settings. The experimental validation is comprehensive, demonstrating clear improvements in both computational efficiency and inference quality across synthetic and real-world datasets (Gaussian continuous experiment and diabetic neuropathy dataset).

The connection to Gaussian process literature is well-executed and helpful for readers familiar with sparse GP methods. The authors effectively demonstrate that their approach can be integrated into standard inference paradigms (variational inference and MCMC) without requiring spectral knowledge, making it immediately applicable to existing DPP implementations. The practical demonstration of the method's convergence with increasing inducing points (Figures 3c-3d) provides strong empirical evidence for the theoretical claims.

## Weaknesses

The paper has several notable weaknesses that require careful consideration. First, the authors acknowledge the interpretability issues of parametrizing L instead of K (Section 1), but fail to adequately address how their method helps overcome this fundamental limitation. The identifiability problem with parameter κ in Section 5.1 (which remains poorly estimated despite the proposed method) underscores this issue, yet the paper doesn't explore whether their bounds could help with the L-to-K transformation.

Second, while the paper references [7] as using spectral bounds, it doesn't provide a thorough comparison of computational complexity or accuracy with this prior work. A quantitative comparison of runtime and inference quality (e.g., log-likelihood estimates) between the spectral approach and their nonspectral bounds would strengthen the claims.

Third, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of how the choice of pseudo-inputs (Z) affects bound tightness and inference quality. While the authors optimize Z as part of the inference procedure, they don't discuss the theoretical convergence of the bounds as |Z| increases or provide guidance on selecting an optimal number of inducing points for different problem sizes.

Fourth, the paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach for large-scale problems. While they claim the method is O(nm²) compared to O(n³) for spectral methods, they don't discuss the practical scalability as n becomes extremely large (e.g., millions of points) or how their method compares to other scalable DPP approaches [13].

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution to DPP inference by providing a theoretically sound alternative to spectral-based methods. The core technical ideas are novel and well-executed, with clear connections to existing literature. The experimental validation is strong and demonstrates practical utility. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the limitations mentioned above, particularly regarding the interpretability issue of parametrizing L instead of K and providing more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods.

The significance of this work is high, as it enables more efficient DPP inference for large-scale applications where spectral decomposition is computationally prohibitive. The method could be broadly applicable to various DPP modeling tasks in machine learning, spatial statistics, and computational biology. While the paper doesn't fundamentally solve the interpretability problem of using L instead of K, it provides a practical solution to an important computational bottleneck.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field that addresses a genuine computational challenge in DPP inference. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more thorough comparison with spectral-based methods [7] in terms of both computational complexity and inference quality
2. Expand the discussion of the L vs. K parametrization trade-offs, particularly addressing the identifiability issues they observed
3. Include a more detailed analysis of how the choice of pseudo-inputs affects bound tightness and inference quality

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in a meaningful way. With these revisions, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS. The work is clearly within the scope of NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with practical implications for diverse applications.

## Final Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions)
