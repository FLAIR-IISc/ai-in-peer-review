Summary
The paper addresses a central bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): computing the normalizing determinant det(I+L), which typically requires eigendecompositions that are costly or unavailable, especially in continuous domains. The main contribution is a pair of nonspectral, inducing-point-based bounds on the DPP likelihood that avoid eigenvalues:

- Finite case: With inducing inputs Z, define Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY. Then
  det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I).
  This yields a computable lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood, enabling variational training in O(n m^2), and supports retrospective MH by bracketing acceptance probabilities.

- Continuous case: For trace-class L on L2(µ), with Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x),
  det LZ / det(LZ+Ψ) · exp(−∫ L(x,x)dµ + tr(LZ^{-1}Ψ)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ det LZ / det(LZ+Ψ).
  This provides analogous nonspectral likelihood bounds for continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinants.

The paper leverages a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975) and parallels sparse GP variational methods (Titsias, 2009). It shows how to use the bounds for (i) variational inference by maximizing the lower bound over θ, Z, and (ii) retrospective Metropolis–Hastings by refining bounds adaptively in m. Experiments include a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian DPP where the spectrum is known (validating the MH sampler against an ideal spectral MH) and a 2D diabetic neuropathy dataset, where variational training recovers overdispersion measures that separate two clinical classes; bounds tighten as m increases.

Relation to prior work
- Prior spectral approaches to DPP learning (Affandi et al., ICML’14; Affandi et al., AISTATS’13) used eigen-information (often truncated) and Nyström approximations; EM/fixed-point methods (Gillenwater et al., NIPS’14; Mariet & Sra, NIPS’15) also rely on finite domains. This paper departs by avoiding spectral information entirely and extending to continuous domains with operator-theoretic bounds.
- The inducing-point construction and trace correction echo the sparse GP variational literature (Titsias, 2009), but the application to DPP normalizers and continuous DPPs is novel.
- For continuous Fredholm determinants, numerical quadrature approaches (Bornemann, 2010) exist but require discretization; the proposed bounds instead use low-rank structure via inducing points. The paper mentions connections as future work.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: first nonspectral, inducing-point bounds for DPP likelihoods applicable to both finite and continuous domains.
- Practicality: O(n m^2) evaluation, no eigen-solves, and natural compatibility with variational inference and retrospective MH.
- Continuous-case theory: operator-level bound with explicit computable terms for Gaussian kernels/base measures; bridges a gap where spectral decompositions are rare.
- Empirical validation: MH agrees with “ideal” spectral MH in a case where ground-truth is known; on real 2D data, the variational method yields consistent overdispersion estimates as m increases, with reasonable runtimes.

Weaknesses
- Experimental scope is limited: no large-scale finite-domain experiments (e.g., typical NeurIPS DPP use-cases like document or subset selection) or head-to-head comparisons with spectral/truncated-eigenvalue methods in terms of runtime/accuracy.
- Tightness and convergence: while bounds empirically tighten with m, there is no theorem on monotonicity w.r.t. m or conditions for asymptotic exactness; the text contains a heuristic GP-variance analogy instead of a formal result.
- Optimization details are light: gradients w.r.t. Z and θ are not derived; CMA-ES is used in places, which may underplay scalability and reproducibility relative to gradient-based methods with AD.
- Identifiability: the κ parameter in L is shown to be weakly identified; a more systematic analysis of identifiability and recommended parameterizations (or priors/constraints) would help practitioners.
- Continuous case relies on integrals (Ψ, ∫ L(x,x)dµ) that are tractable for Gaussian–Gaussian but may be nontrivial more generally; guidance on numerical approximation and its effect on bounds is limited.
- Some clarity issues: occasional typos and missing years in references; assumptions for Proposition 2 could be stated more crisply (e.g., invertibility/conditioning of LZ, need for jitter).

Quality
- Theoretical development appears sound and well-motivated; propositions rest on established determinant inequalities. However, key proofs are in the supplement; adding sketch proofs or intuition in the main text would help.
- The MCMC validation is convincing in the 1D example. The real-data study shows practical usefulness, but broader benchmarks would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized, with a good walkthrough from finite to continuous. The link to sparse GP bounds is helpful. Some notation is dense; a short computational complexity subsection (finite/continuous) and an explicit algorithm box for MH with adaptive m would improve readability.
- Provide explicit derivative expressions for F(θ, Z) to enable gradient-based optimization.

Originality
- High: nonspectral bounds for DPP likelihoods and their extension to continuous domains are new to the best of my knowledge, and clearly differentiated from spectral/Nyström and EM/fixed-point methods.

Significance
- Potentially high: removes a major barrier to DPP learning in continuous domains and large discrete problems. If adopted, could broaden DPP use in ML and spatial statistics. The impact would be clearer with stronger large-scale comparisons and ablations.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you prove monotonicity of the lower bound in m (after optimizing Z), or at least provide conditions where the bounds converge to the exact likelihood? Any rates in terms of fill distance of Z?
- Provide gradients of F(θ, Z) for common kernels and discuss stability (e.g., adding jitter to LZ). Can you exploit matrix determinant/trace lemmas to reduce constants in O(n m^2)?
- For the continuous case, discuss numerical approximation of Ψ and ∫ L(x,x)dµ for non-Gaussian µ/kernels, and its effect on bounds and MH correctness (stochastic bounds?).
- Include a finite-domain experiment with n in the tens/hundreds of thousands comparing to spectral/truncated methods in runtime, memory, and final likelihood/parameter error.
- Discuss practical guidance for choosing/initializing Z (e.g., k-means on observed points, sampling from estimated marginals), and report sensitivity.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, general, and practical approach to a long-standing bottleneck; applicable to continuous DPPs.
- Clean theory grounded in classical determinant inequalities; compatible with both VI and MH.
- Promising empirical results and reasonable runtime.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth and lack of direct comparisons to strong baselines on standard finite-domain tasks.
- Missing theoretical guarantees on bound convergence/tightness and limited optimization details.

Recommendation
- Weak accept. The core idea is novel and valuable; with expanded experiments and some added theoretical/algorithmic detail, this could be a strong contribution.

Confidence
- Moderate.
