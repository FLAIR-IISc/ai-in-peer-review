- Summary: Proposes non-spectral likelihood bounds for DPPs (inspired by variational GP) to avoid costly eigendecomposition, applicable to variational inference and MCMC for finite and continuous DPPs.

- Strength: Directly addresses the spectral decomposition bottleneck limiting large-scale DPP use.

- Strength: Bounds have a solid theoretical foundation, leveraging GP variational methods and determinant inequalities.

- Strength: Method is general, extending to both finite and continuous DPPs.

- Strength: Experiments (including retrospective MCMC) demonstrate practical effectiveness and VI convergence with more inducing points.

- Strength: Paper is well-written, well-organized, and candid about limitations and future work.

- Weakness: Parameter identifiability issues (notably scale κ) evident in toy experiments, limiting applicability when parameters are unknown.

- Weakness: Optimization over pseudo-inputs introduces extra computational cost; trade-off versus eigendecomposition is not fully explored.

- Weakness: Comparisons to existing or approximate spectral methods are limited.

- Weakness: Limited practical guidance on choosing number of pseudo-inputs (m) and other hyperparameters.

- Quality: Technically sound derivations and experiments; method fully described and validated at a basic level.

- Clarity: Notation and exposition are clear and accessible.

- Originality: Novel application of GP variational inference techniques to DPPs, including continuous extensions.

- Significance: Removing the spectral bottleneck could substantially expand practical DPP applications.

- For acceptance: Addresses a major problem, is theoretically motivated, empirically effective, and novel.

- Against acceptance: Concerns about parameter identifiability, added pseudo-input optimization cost, and limited empirical comparisons.

- Overall: Reviewer recommends a strong accept for NIPS, judging the benefits to outweigh the limitations.
