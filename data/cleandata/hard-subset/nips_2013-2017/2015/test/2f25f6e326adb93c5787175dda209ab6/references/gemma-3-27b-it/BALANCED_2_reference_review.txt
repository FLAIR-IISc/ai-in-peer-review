This paper addresses the challenging problem of inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decomposition, a computational bottleneck in many applications. The authors provide bounds on the DPP likelihood that circumvent the need for eigendecomposition, offering a significant practical advantage, particularly for large-scale problems and continuous domains. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and builds upon a strong foundation of existing work in DPPs, Gaussian Processes, and variational inference.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core contribution – deriving nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood – is genuinely novel and addresses a significant practical limitation of existing inference methods. This is especially valuable for continuous DPPs where spectral decomposition is often intractable.
* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the bounds, inspired by variational inference for GPs, appears mathematically sound. The connection to existing results and the clear exposition of the derivations enhance confidence in the technical validity.
* **Generality:** The authors successfully extend their approach from finite sets to continuous domains, demonstrating the broader applicability of their methodology.
* **Practicality:** The proposed methods – variational inference and MCMC using the bounds – are immediately applicable and offer computational advantages over traditional approaches.  The experimental validation supports this claim.
* **Connections to Related Work:** The paper demonstrates a strong awareness of relevant literature, properly citing and contextualizing the work within the broader fields of DPPs, Gaussian Processes, and Bayesian inference.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while relatively simple, effectively demonstrate the feasibility and performance of the proposed methods. The comparisons to existing methods (in the toy example) and the demonstration of convergence in the diabetic neuropathy dataset are convincing.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with all the technical details. The organization is logical, and the explanations are clear and concise.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parametrization Choice:** The paper acknowledges the interpretability drawbacks of directly parametrizing the kernel L instead of K. While this trade-off is justified by tractability, a more in-depth discussion of its implications could be beneficial. Specifically, the lack of decoupling between the expected number of points and repulsiveness with the L parametrization is a significant limitation.
* **Scalability of MCMC:** While the MCMC approach avoids explicit spectral decomposition, the retrospective sampling step, requiring adaptive increases in the number of inducing points (m), could still be computationally expensive in high dimensions or with very large datasets.  A more detailed analysis of the scaling of this step would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments, while well-executed, are somewhat limited in scope.  Further evaluation on more complex datasets and comparisons to state-of-the-art DPP inference methods (beyond those mentioned) would strengthen the claims of practical impact.
* **Discussion of Assumptions:** The continuous DPP section relies on several assumptions regarding the kernel L and base measure µ. A more explicit discussion of the implications of violating these assumptions would improve the clarity and robustness of the results.
* **Connection to Quadrature:** The authors mention a potential connection to quadrature-based bounds in the discussion.  Elaborating on this connection, even briefly, could provide valuable insights and potential avenues for future research.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound solution to a significant practical challenge in DPP inference.
* The proposed methods are generally applicable and offer computational advantages over existing approaches.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature.
* The experimental validation, while limited, provides evidence of the feasibility and performance of the proposed methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The parametrization choice of directly inferring L may limit interpretability and flexibility.
* The scalability of the MCMC approach with the retrospective sampling step needs further analysis.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs. The proposed nonspectral bounds offer a significant advance in the practical applicability of these models, particularly for large-scale problems and continuous domains. While the parametrization choice and the scalability of the MCMC approach are potential concerns, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should expand the discussion of the trade-offs associated with parametrizing L, provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the MCMC approach, and consider including additional experimental results on more challenging datasets.
