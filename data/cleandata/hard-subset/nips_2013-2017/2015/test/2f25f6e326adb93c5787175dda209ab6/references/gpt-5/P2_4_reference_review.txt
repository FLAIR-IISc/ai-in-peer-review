Summary
The paper tackles likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decompositions of the L-operator. For finite ground sets, it introduces nonspectral determinant bounds constructed from inducing inputs Z, using a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY. The bounds det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I) enable both a tractable variational lower bound for maximum-likelihood–style training and a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) scheme that adaptively tightens acceptance bounds by increasing |Z|. The approach is extended to continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinant bounds: det(LZ)/det(LZ+Ψ) exp(−∫ L(x,x)dµ(x) + tr(LZ^{-1}Ψ)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ det(LZ)/det(LZ+Ψ), with Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x). Experiments include (i) a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP where ground-truth eigenstructure exists, validating the retrospective MH procedure and highlighting identifiability issues, and (ii) diabetic neuropathy spatial data, where the variational approach recovers class-dependent overdispersion measures and shows convergence of upper/lower likelihood bounds as m increases.

Relation to prior work
- Prior DPP learning often relies on spectral decompositions (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’14) or EM/fixed-point methods (e.g., Gillenwater et al., NIPS’14; Mariet & Sra, NIPS’15), which remain challenging at scale. This paper provides an alternative that avoids eigencomputations entirely.
- The construction mirrors variational sparse GP ideas (Titsias, AISTATS’09), using inducing inputs and leveraging determinant and trace inequalities (Seiler & Simon, 1975). The extension to continuous DPPs leverages operator-theoretic notions (Fredholm determinants; Bornemann, 2010).
- For continuous kernels and Gaussian base measures, the paper draws parallels to cases where spectral properties are known (Hermite basis), but the proposed method does not require them.

Quality
- The main technical claims are plausible and align with known matrix/operator inequalities and GP variational bounds. The finite-domain bounds reduce to standard low-rank approximations with a trace correction that lower-bounds −log det(I+L). The continuous-domain result is the natural operator analogue.
- The proposed variational objective is coherent: summing log det(LYt) and replacing −log det(I+L) by its lower bound −log det(Q+I) − tr(L−Q). Complexity O(n m^2) (finite case) is credible.
- The retrospective MH adaptation is standard and well-motivated; the paper demonstrates its correctness on a case with known ground truth.
- Limitations: (i) No theoretical characterization of the gap or convergence rate of bounds vs. m, or monotonic improvements as Z is optimized; (ii) proofs are deferred to the supplement; (iii) numerical stability issues (e.g., LZ invertibility, jitter) are not discussed; (iv) for continuous DPPs, tractability hinges on computing Ψ; the paper focuses on Gaussian–Gaussian but offers limited guidance for more general µ, L (e.g., quadrature, random features).

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear, with intelligible motivation, derivations, and connections to GPs. The finite-then-continuous structure aids readability.
- Some minor issues: a few misnumbered equations and small typos; more explicit guidance on computing gradients w.r.t. Z and θ would help reproducibility; a brief proof sketch in the main text would strengthen trust.

Originality
- The core contribution—nonspectral determinant bounds tailored to DPP likelihoods via inducing inputs—is novel to the DPP literature, even if inspired by variational sparse GPs.
- The extension to continuous DPPs and Fredholm determinant bounds that preserve the inducing-point structure appears new and valuable.

Significance
- Avoiding eigen-decompositions addresses a key bottleneck for both finite and continuous DPPs, potentially broadening applicability.
- The experiments show promise but fall short of demonstrating clear state-of-the-art improvements at scale. Additional empirical evidence on larger finite domains (e.g., standard DPP benchmarks in summarization/retrieval) and comparisons to spectral-bound MH (Affandi et al., 2014) would strengthen the impact claims.

Pros
- Conceptually clean, principled nonspectral bounds; leverages well-understood variational machinery.
- Applies to both finite and continuous DPPs; continuous case is rarely handled in ML papers.
- Practical algorithms: a variational lower bound and a retrospective MH scheme.
- Empirical evidence of convergence of bounds with m; neat visualization/behavior of optimized inducing inputs.

Cons
- Limited experimental validation: no large-scale finite-domain benchmarks, no direct head-to-head with spectral methods in runtime/accuracy.
- No theoretical quantification of bound tightness or convergence in m (beyond intuition); no guarantees that optimizing Z monotonically tightens the bound.
- Practical guidance for choosing/optimizing Z and handling numerical stability (e.g., LZ conditioning) is sparse.
- Continuous case depends on computing Ψ; scope beyond Gaussian–Gaussian is unclear.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide theoretical monotonicity or convergence properties as m increases and Z is optimized? Any a priori error bounds?
- How do you ensure LZ is well-conditioned in practice (jitter, priors on Z)? Any sensitivity analyses?
- Could you include gradients for Z and θ and discuss optimization strategies (initialization, constraints) to improve the stability of the variational method?
- Please add experiments on a large finite ground set comparing against spectral-bound MH and Nyström approximations (runtime, memory, accuracy).
- For the continuous setting, can you discuss/generalize Ψ computation (e.g., quadrature, random features) and show at least one non-Gaussian example?

Overall evaluation
This is a solid and timely contribution: technically sound, novel to DPPs, and broadly relevant. The main weakness is limited empirical validation and lack of quantitative analysis of bound tightness. With stronger experiments and some theoretical guidance on the behavior of the bounds/inducing inputs, this work could have substantial impact. As it stands, I lean positive, especially given the generality (finite and continuous) and the clean link to scalable variational methods.
