This paper addresses the problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without requiring spectral decomposition of the kernel, a common bottleneck in many applications. The authors provide bounds on the DPP likelihood, drawing inspiration from variational inference in Gaussian Processes (GPs), applicable to both finite and continuous domains. They demonstrate the utility of these bounds within variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, validating their approach through both a synthetic experiment and a real-world dataset.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important practical limitation:** The computational cost of spectral decomposition is a significant obstacle to applying DPPs, particularly in large-scale settings. This paper directly tackles this issue.
* **Sound theoretical foundation:** The derivation of the bounds is well-motivated, building on existing results from GP literature and leveraging established inequalities on determinants.
* **Generality:** The results are presented for both discrete and continuous DPPs, increasing the applicability of the proposed methods. The extension to continuous DPPs is particularly valuable.
* **Thorough experimental validation:** The authors provide two experiments, one synthetic and one real-world, to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. The synthetic experiment includes a comparison with ideal MCMC, while the real-world experiment demonstrates applicability to a biologically relevant dataset.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is well-structured and the technical details are presented in a clear and understandable manner. The motivation for each step is clearly articulated.
* **Connection to related work:** The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature on DPPs, GPs, and variational inference.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational cost of bound evaluation:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, evaluating the bounds still requires computing and inverting matrices (or their continuous equivalents), potentially becoming a bottleneck for very large datasets. The computational complexity isn’t always clearly stated in relation to the size of the dataset.
* **Parameter identifiability:** The authors acknowledge issues with parameter identifiability when directly parametrizing the kernel L. While they demonstrate that the MCMC approach is consistent with ideal methods in a controlled setting, the issue of non-identifiability remains a potential limitation. Further discussion on strategies to address this (e.g., regularization, prior selection) would be beneficial.
* **Pseudo-input optimization:** The choice of optimizing pseudo-inputs is a good one, but the paper doesn’t delve deeply into the sensitivity of the results to the initialization and optimization procedure for these inputs.  A sensitivity analysis would be valuable.
* **Limited comparison to existing non-spectral methods:** While the authors motivate their work by referencing existing non-spectral approaches (e.g., [7]), a more direct quantitative comparison of computational cost and accuracy would strengthen the paper. How does their approach compare to the power iteration methods mentioned in the introduction in terms of runtime and scalability?
* **Assumptions for continuous case:**  The assumptions required for the continuous case regarding the kernel L and base measure µ are stated but not thoroughly discussed. A clearer explanation of the practical implications of these assumptions would be helpful.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant practical challenge in DPP inference.
* The proposed bounds are theoretically sound and offer a viable alternative to spectral decomposition.
* The experimental validation demonstrates the effectiveness of the methods.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented.
* The paper pushes the boundaries of DPP inference and opens avenues for future research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational cost of evaluating the bounds may still be substantial for very large datasets.
* The parameter identifiability issue needs further investigation.
* The paper lacks a direct, quantitative comparison to other non-spectral DPP inference methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs. The proposed bounds offer a practical solution to a long-standing computational challenge. While some limitations remain, the authors have presented a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and experimentally validated approach. I believe that the paper is suitable for acceptance, pending minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I recommend that the authors:

1.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the bound evaluation.
2.  Discuss strategies for addressing the parameter identifiability issue.
3.  Include a more direct comparison to other non-spectral DPP inference methods.
4.  Elaborate on the practical implications of the assumptions required for the continuous case.
