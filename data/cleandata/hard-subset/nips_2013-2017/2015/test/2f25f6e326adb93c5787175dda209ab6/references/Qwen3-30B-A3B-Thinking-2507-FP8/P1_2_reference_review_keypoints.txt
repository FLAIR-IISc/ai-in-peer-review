- Problem: Likelihood-based DPP inference is bottlenecked by costly spectral decomposition of the kernel operator L for large/continuous domains.  
- Approach: Proposes nonspectral likelihood bounds using pseudo-/inducing inputs (inspired by sparse Gaussian processes) to avoid spectral decomposition.  
- Theory: Derives theoretical nonspectral bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs (Propositions 1 & 2) based on a determinant inequality.  
- Applications: Applies bounds to variational inference and MCMC, enabling likelihood-based inference for larger datasets and continuous domains.  

Strengths:
- Major practical contribution: removes spectral decomposition bottleneck, making DPP inference scalable.  
- Solid theoretical foundation with nontrivial determinant-based proofs and extension to continuous DPPs.  
- Strong empirical validation on synthetic and real (diabetic neuropathy) datasets, showing convergence with more pseudo-inputs.  
- Clear, well-structured writing and consistent notation.  
- Honest acknowledgement of interpretability trade-off when parametrizing L instead of K.  

Weaknesses:
- Lacks direct runtime/accuracy comparison with prior method [7] on the same datasets.  
- Insufficient exploration of solutions for the interpretability loss from parametrizing L.  
- Limited discussion of how broadly the bounds apply to general DPPs beyond the specific cases treated.  
- Missing detailed analysis of computational trade-offs between number of pseudo-inputs m and inference time/accuracy.  

Evaluation:
- Quality: Technically sound with solid theoretical and experimental support.  
- Clarity: Clearly written and accessible to readers familiar with DPPs/Gaussian processes.  
- Originality: Novel application of nonspectral bounds to DPPs, especially continuous case.  
- Significance: Makes likelihood-based DPP inference feasible for larger and continuous domains, enabling broader applications.  

Recommendation:
- Overall recommendation: Accept (valuable contribution with minor addressable weaknesses).  
- Pros cited: solves major computational bottleneck, novel theory, strong experiments, clear presentation.  
- Cons cited: limited direct comparison to prior work, interpretability and m-vs-time trade-off not fully addressed.  

Suggested improvements:
- Add direct runtime/accuracy comparison with [7] on same datasets.  
- Discuss potential methods to recover or approximate interpretability (e.g., approximating K from L).  
- Provide detailed analysis of computational trade-offs between m and inference time/accuracy.  
- Expand discussion on limitations and applicability of the bounds to more general DPP settings.
