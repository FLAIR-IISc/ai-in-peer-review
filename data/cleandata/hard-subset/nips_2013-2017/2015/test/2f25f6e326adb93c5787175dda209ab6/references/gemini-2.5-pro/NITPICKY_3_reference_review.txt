### Summary

This paper introduces a novel method for likelihood-based inference in determinantal point processes (DPPs) that circumvents the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix L. The main contribution is a set of non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the determinant term `det(L + I)` that appears in the DPP likelihood. These bounds are derived using a low-rank approximation of the kernel matrix based on a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points," an idea explicitly inspired by variational sparse Gaussian processes (e.g., Titsias, 2009). The authors present these bounds for both DPPs on finite sets and on continuous domains. They then demonstrate how these bounds can be integrated into two standard inference paradigms: approximate inference via a variational lower bound and asymptotically exact inference via a Metropolis-Hastings (MH) sampler with retrospective sampling. The methods are evaluated on a synthetic 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP and a real-world spatial statistics dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Contribution:** The primary contribution—providing non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds for the DPP likelihood—is a significant step forward. It offers a compelling alternative to methods that rely on partial eigendecomposition (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013), especially in the continuous domain where spectral properties are often analytically intractable. This work makes DPP inference more broadly applicable.

2.  **Methodological Generality:** The framework is thoughtfully developed for both finite and continuous DPPs, demonstrating the versatility of the core idea. The connection to the well-established literature on sparse Gaussian processes provides a strong theoretical foundation.

3.  **Flexible Inference Framework:** The paper shows how the proposed bounds can be used for both a fast, approximate variational method and a more computationally intensive but exact MCMC sampler. The optimization of the pseudo-input locations `Z` to tighten the bounds is a crucial and powerful component of the proposed framework.

4.  **Empirical Demonstration:** The experiments, particularly on the diabetic neuropathy dataset, successfully demonstrate the practical utility of the method. The convergence of the likelihood bounds and parameter estimates as the number of inducing points `m` increases (Figure 3) provides convincing evidence for the approach's validity. The authors are also commendably transparent about the failure of the variational method on their synthetic example.

### Weaknesses

Despite the strong core idea, the paper suffers from a substantial number of technical inaccuracies, presentation flaws, and experimental gaps that undermine its overall quality.

1.  **Major Technical Flaw in Variational Lower Bound:** There appears to be a critical sign error in the formulation of the variational lower bound `F(θ,Z)` in Equation (6). According to Proposition 1, `1/det(L + I) ≥ det(Q + I) * exp(-tr(L-Q))`. Taking the logarithm, this implies `-log det(L + I) ≥ log det(Q + I) - tr(L-Q)`. The lower bound on the full log-likelihood `ℓ(θ)` should therefore be `∑ log det(L_{Y_t}) - T * ( -log det(Q+I) + tr(L-Q) )`. However, Equation (6) presents the final two terms as `- T log det(Q + I) + T tr(L-Q)`. Given that `L-Q` is positive semi-definite, `tr(L-Q) ≥ 0`, so the proposed formulation incorrectly *adds* a positive term instead of subtracting it, making it an invalid lower bound. This error casts serious doubt on the correctness of the variational inference results presented.

2.  **Lack of Careful Presentation:** The manuscript is replete with minor but cumulative errors that suggest a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   **Typos:** In Section 2.1, the expansion of `detK{xi,xj}` contains `K(yi,yi)` which should clearly be `K(xj,xj)`.
    *   **Incorrect Internal Referencing:** The paper repeatedly refers to equation numbers that do not exist or are incorrect. For example, Section 3.2 refers to bounds in "(13)" twice, Section 4.1 refers to "(15)", and Section 4.2 refers to "(16)". These seem to be placeholders from a different draft and make the text difficult to follow.
    *   **Inconsistent Citation Style:** Citations are not formatted correctly. Many appear as bracketed numbers (e.g., `[1]`, `[5]`, `[10]`) rather than proper citations (e.g., `[16, Chapter 7.3]`).
    *   **Imprecise Definitions:** Equation (1) defines the probability of a subset `A` as `det(K_A)`, which is characteristic of an L-ensemble rather than a proper DPP, for which a normalizing constant `det(I+K)` is required. The notation `P(A ⊂ Y)` is also ambiguous; `P(Y=A)` would be standard.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Analysis:**
    *   The discrepancy in the performance of variational inference—failing on the synthetic dataset but apparently succeeding on the real-world one—is not adequately explained. The authors attribute the failure to identifiability issues but do not discuss why these issues were not prohibitive in the second experiment. This leaves the reader wondering about the reliability and applicability of the VI approach.
    *   The paper claims advantages over the spectral approach of Affandi et al. [7] (e.g., avoiding storage of the `n x n` matrix `L`, applicability to continuous domains). However, a direct empirical comparison of runtime and accuracy on a shared task is conspicuously absent. Such a comparison is essential to substantiate the claimed practical benefits.
    *   The requirement that the integral `Ψ` be analytically tractable for the continuous case is a significant practical limitation. This is mentioned but its impact is not fully discussed. It restricts the choice of kernels and base measures to a few specific pairings (e.g., Gaussian-Gaussian).

### Decision

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the central idea of using non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds for DPP inference is excellent and potentially of high impact, the current manuscript is not ready for publication. A major technical error in the derivation of the variational lower bound, combined with numerous presentational flaws and an incomplete experimental evaluation, prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper reads as a premature draft rather than a finished piece of work. The core contribution is strong enough that I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the paper, correct the technical and presentational errors, and strengthen the experimental section for a future submission.
