This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the evaluation of the term `det(L + I)`, which typically requires a full spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix `L`. The authors propose novel, non-spectral bounds for this determinant, applicable to DPPs on both finite and continuous domains. The core idea is inspired by sparse approximations in Gaussian processes (GPs), using a set of "inducing" or "pseudo-inputs" `Z` to form a low-rank approximation of the kernel operator. These bounds are then integrated into two standard inference paradigms: a fast, approximate variational inference (VI) scheme using the lower bound, and an asymptotically exact Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm that uses both bounds in a retrospective sampling framework. The proposed methods are validated on a synthetic toy problem with a known ground truth and applied to a real-world dataset of nerve fiber locations, successfully reproducing and extending results from prior work.

This work builds upon a line of research aiming to make DPPs more practical for machine learning applications. Previous work, such as Affandi et al. (2014) [7], also used bounds for MCMC inference but relied on *spectral* bounds, requiring the computation of the first `m` eigenvalues via methods like power iteration. The key distinction and novelty of the present work is that its bounds are entirely non-spectral, avoiding any eigenvalue computation. The approach of using inducing points to form a low-rank approximation `Q` of a kernel matrix `L` is a cornerstone of the sparse GP literature (e.g., Titsias, 2009 [11]), and the authors make this connection explicit. The originality here lies in porting this powerful idea to the DPP setting and deriving the corresponding bounds on the determinant term, using a known matrix inequality from Seiler & Simon (1975) [14].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-understood and highly significant bottleneck in the practical application of DPPs. By providing a method that sidesteps expensive `O(n^3)` eigendecompositions, this work has the potential to scale DPP inference to much larger problems. The non-spectral nature of the bounds is a genuine advance over previous approximation schemes.

2.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The proposed method is technically solid. The connection to the sparse GP framework is elegant and well-motivated. The derivation of the bounds, while relying on an existing inequality, is correctly applied to the DPP likelihood. The paper is comprehensive, providing a unified treatment for both the finite and continuous cases, which strengthens the contribution.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It begins by clearly motivating the problem, introduces the necessary background, and then systematically builds its contribution, first for the simpler finite case and then extending to the continuous setting. The distinction between the proposed method and prior work is made very clear. The experimental section is also well-structured and the results are presented lucidly.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed to demonstrate the utility and correctness of the method.
    *   The 1D toy experiment is an excellent choice for validation, as the exact likelihood is computable, allowing for a direct comparison between the proposed retrospective MCMC and an ideal MCMC sampler. The results convincingly show that the method works correctly.
    *   The honest reporting of the VI method's failure on this problem due to parameter identifiability is a major strength. It highlights a known issue with the `L`-parametrization of DPPs and rightly suggests that the full posterior exploration of MCMC is valuable in such cases.
    *   The application to the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrates practical utility on a real-world problem. The convergence of the parameter estimates and the likelihood bounds as the number of inducing points `m` increases provides strong evidence for the robustness of the VI approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Practical Cost of Optimizing Inducing Inputs:** The performance of the bounds relies on the quality of the inducing inputs `Z`. For the VI approach, jointly optimizing `Z` and the kernel hyperparameters `θ` is standard. However, for the MCMC method, the paper states that `m` can be increased and `Z` re-optimized whenever the bounds are not tight enough to make an acceptance decision. This re-optimization within an MCMC step could be computationally very expensive, potentially negating some of the benefits of avoiding the eigendecomposition. A more detailed discussion of the practical trade-offs and computational cost of this adaptive scheme would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Inherited Limitation of `L`-parametrization:** The paper explicitly and correctly states that it focuses on the computational problem and not the interpretability problem arising from parametrizing the kernel `L` instead of the more natural kernel `K`. While this is a fair scoping of the work, it remains a significant practical limitation. The toy experiment clearly shows how this can lead to unidentifiable parameters (e.g., `κ`). This is not a flaw of the proposed method itself, but a broader issue that users of this method will face.

3.  **Novelty of the Core Inequality:** The core mathematical tool, the determinant inequality from [14], is not new. The main contribution is its novel application in this context. While this is a perfectly valid and strong contribution, the framing could be slightly more explicit about this being a novel application of an existing tool combined with ideas from another field (sparse GPs).

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and well-known computational bottleneck in DPP inference.
*   The proposed non-spectral approach is a significant and novel improvement over previous spectral approximation methods.
*   The method is technically sound, well-motivated by an elegant connection to sparse GPs.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough, providing both validation against a ground-truth and a compelling real-world application.
*   The authors are intellectually honest about the limitations of their approach (e.g., the `L` vs. `K` issue).

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The practical cost of optimizing the inducing inputs within the MCMC sampler is not fully analyzed.
*   The work inherits the significant practical problem of poor parameter interpretability/identifiability from the `L`-parametrization framework.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a clear, significant, and well-executed contribution to the literature on probabilistic modeling. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. The proposed non-spectral bounds are a valuable new tool for the DPP community, and the paper does an excellent job of demonstrating their utility. This work is highly likely to be built upon by future research and used by practitioners seeking to apply DPPs to larger datasets. I strongly recommend acceptance.
