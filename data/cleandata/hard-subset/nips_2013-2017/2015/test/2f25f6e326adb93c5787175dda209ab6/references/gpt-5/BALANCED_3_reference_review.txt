Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a core bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): evaluating det(I+L), which typically requires a spectral decomposition. The authors propose nonspectral, computable bounds on this determinant by introducing inducing (pseudo-)inputs Z and forming a low-rank approximation Q = LYZ L−1Z LZY (finite case) or its continuous analogue via Ψij = ∫ L(zi, x)L(x, zj)dµ(x). Using determinant inequalities (Seiler & Simon, 1975), they derive upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) that avoid eigendecompositions. They show how to use these bounds in two inference paradigms: (i) a variational lower bound that can be maximized jointly over kernel parameters and pseudo-inputs, and (ii) a retrospective MH sampler that refines the bounds by increasing m and reoptimizing Z until an acceptance decision is possible. They extend the bounds to continuous DPPs under trace-class assumptions and when Ψ is tractable (e.g., Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure). Experiments include a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian toy example with known spectrum and a real spatial dataset (epidermal nerve fibers) demonstrating convergence of estimates with m and agreement with prior findings (Affandi et al., 2014; Waller et al., 2011).

The work is well-positioned relative to existing DPP learning methods that rely on eigendecompositions (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014; Gillenwater et al., 2012; Mariet & Sra, 2015) and to Nyström approximations (Affandi et al., 2013). The inducing-point construction and the variational lower bound echo sparse GP methodology (Titsias, 2009), and the continuous-case treatment connects to numerical Fredholm determinant literature (Bornemann, 2010).

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable idea: reuse inducing-variable machinery to bound DPP normalizers without spectral knowledge.
- Works for both finite and continuous domains; the continuous case is nontrivial and useful in spatial statistics.
- The retrospective MH construction makes exact Bayesian inference possible in principle without eigenvalues, provided bounds can be tightened.
- Computational profile is favorable when m ≪ n (O(n m^2)), and the numerator determinants det(LYt) remain tractable.
- Empirical evidence shows bound tightening with increasing m and coherent parameter recovery where identifiable.

Weaknesses
- Tightness and convergence of the bounds as m increases are only argued heuristically; no formal guarantees (rates or conditions) are provided for finite or continuous settings.
- Practical scope of the continuous-case bounds is limited to settings where Ψ is tractable; this is discussed, but experiments focus on the Gaussian-Gaussian case.
- Empirical evaluation is modest: a 1D toy and one real dataset; no comparison to spectral-bound MH or other learning methods on medium-scale problems where they are still feasible.
- Optimization of pseudo-inputs Z is crucial but underdeveloped: no analysis of initialization, optimization stability, or sensitivity; CMA-ES is used in places and gradient methods mentioned, but details and derivatives are not provided.
- Identifiability issues from directly parameterizing L are observed (κ), but the paper does not propose remedies (e.g., priors/constraints or alternative parameterizations).
- Lacks a thorough runtime/memory comparison against power iteration–based methods, fixed-point/EM approaches, or Nyström baselines in finite domains.

Quality
- The main propositions are plausible and well-motivated via Seiler–Simon determinant inequalities; finite- and continuous-case constructions mirror established variational GP bounds. Assuming the supplementary proofs are correct, the technical core is sound.
- The retrospective MH procedure is a principled application of bounding-based accept/reject with refinement; however, guarantees that the bounds can be tightened sufficiently (as m → ∞, optimized Z) are not formalized.
- Experimental validation supports correctness but is not comprehensive; more quantitative comparisons and larger-scale studies would bolster the claims.

Clarity
- Generally clear exposition, with a helpful progression from finite to continuous cases and clear statement of assumptions (trace-class, computability of Ψ).
- Some notation could be streamlined (e.g., consistent use of subscripts; occasional typos like “1det”), and explicit gradients with respect to Z, θ for the variational bound would aid reproducibility.
- The relationship to Nyström approximations is acknowledged but could be clarified further (e.g., when Z ⊂ Y vs. Z ⊄ Y).

Originality
- Applying determinant inequalities with inducing-point approximations to bound DPP normalizers appears novel in the DPP literature. The connection to sparse GP variational bounds is known, but the adaptation to DPP likelihoods (finite and continuous) and their use in retrospective MH is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
- Avoiding eigendecompositions opens doors to learning DPP models in settings where spectra are unavailable or too costly, particularly in continuous domains. This is practically important and likely to be adopted by both ML and spatial statistics communities.
- The lack of stronger theoretical guarantees and limited empirical breadth temper the immediate impact, but the idea is promising and could spur follow-up work.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and practically relevant nonspectral bounds for DPP inference.
- Extension to continuous DPPs is valuable.
- Bridges two influential literatures (DPPs and sparse GPs).
- Enables exact MH without spectra via retrospective sampling.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experiments and comparisons; no medium-scale benchmarks against spectral methods where feasible.
- No formal convergence/tightness guarantees as m increases; reliance on heuristic optimization of Z.
- Practical constraints in continuous case (computability of Ψ) and unresolved identifiability issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical results on bound convergence as m grows (e.g., conditions under which F(θ, Z) → `(θ), rates with fill distance of Z).
- Detail and analyze optimization of Z (initialization strategies, gradients, complexity, effect on tightness). Consider greedy/covering strategies or DPP-based selection of Z to encourage spread.
- Include comparisons on moderate n where spectral MH/EM/fixed-point methods are still viable; report wall-clock, memory, and accuracy (log-likelihood, parameter recovery).
- Explore additional datasets (finite and continuous) and ablations (sensitivity to m, Z size/schedule in retrospective MH).
- Discuss remedies for identifiability (priors, reparameterizations) and provide guidance on parameter interpretability when using L.
- Release code and implementation details.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents a neat and potentially impactful idea with solid intuition and some theoretical underpinnings, but the empirical evaluation and theoretical tightness guarantees are currently too limited for NeurIPS. Strengthening the experimental comparisons and adding convergence analysis would likely elevate this to a strong submission.
