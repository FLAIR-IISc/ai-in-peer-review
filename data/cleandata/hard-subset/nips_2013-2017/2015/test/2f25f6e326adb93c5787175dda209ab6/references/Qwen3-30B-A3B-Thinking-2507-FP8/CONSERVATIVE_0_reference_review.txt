

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper proposes "nonspectral bounds" on the likelihood of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) that avoid spectral decomposition. The authors claim to address the computational bottleneck in DPP inference by deriving bounds on the determinant in the likelihood that don't require spectral knowledge. They extend variational inference and MCMC methods using these bounds, first for finite sets and then for continuous domains.

The main contribution appears to be a straightforward extension of variational inference techniques from Gaussian processes (specifically [11]) to DPPs. The authors acknowledge this inspiration in Section 2.2, noting they "draw inspiration from bounds used for variational inference of GPs." The mathematical derivation of the bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 relies on a standard determinant inequality [14], which has been previously applied in other contexts. The paper's central claim of providing "nonspectral bounds" is therefore not sufficiently novel for NIPS, as it represents a direct application of existing GP techniques rather than a new methodological contribution specific to DPPs.

The paper's most serious weakness is the lack of convincing experimental validation against established baselines. The authors reference [7] as the most relevant prior work addressing spectral decomposition in DPPs, but they provide no direct comparison with this method. In the toy experiment (Section 5.1), they acknowledge their variational approach yields "poorly consistent results" across runs, with "relative errors for α and γ go up to 100%." They then switch to MCMC, but the results don't show clear advantages over spectral-based methods. For the real dataset (Section 5.2), they test different numbers of inducing points but don't compare with the spectral decomposition approach from [7], making it impossible to assess whether their method offers practical improvements.

The paper's theoretical claims are also questionable. The authors claim their bounds are "cheap-to-evaluate," but they don't provide computational complexity comparisons with existing methods. The O(nm²) complexity they cite is standard for inducing point methods in GP literature, not a new computational advantage. More importantly, the paper doesn't address the fundamental issue they acknowledge in Section 2: parametrizing L instead of K sacrifices interpretability. The authors admit "the number of points and the repulsiveness of the points in Y do not decouple as nicely" when using L, but they don't provide evidence that this tradeoff is worth the claimed computational benefits.

The experiments are insufficient to validate the paper's claims. The toy example is too simple (1D Gaussian-Gaussian) and relies on having known spectral decomposition for validation - which contradicts their main claim of "without spectral knowledge." The real dataset (diabetic neuropathy) is small (only 7 samples total) and doesn't demonstrate scalability or practical advantages over existing methods. The paper's assertion that "the overall optimization of the variational lower bound is relatively fast" is unconvincing without direct comparisons to the spectral decomposition approach.

The paper also fails to address a critical question: why would anyone use these bounds instead of simply computing the spectral decomposition when it's available? The authors claim spectral decomposition is "rarely available in practice," but provide no evidence for this assertion. For many DPP applications, spectral decomposition is either available or can be computed efficiently for moderate-sized problems. The paper doesn't demonstrate a compelling use case where spectral decomposition is truly intractable.

The paper has several technical issues. The title claims "inference without spectral knowledge," but the method still requires choosing inducing points (pseudo-inputs), which represents knowledge about the data structure. The paper doesn't provide guidance on selecting the number of inducing points m, which is crucial for practical performance. The variational inference results are described as "poorly consistent" across runs, undermining the paper's main claim of providing a robust inference method.

In summary, this paper is a direct extension of existing GP techniques to DPPs without providing sufficient evidence of novelty, theoretical contribution, or practical advantages over established methods. The experimental validation is insufficient, the comparison with relevant baselines is missing, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the claimed computational benefits outweigh the loss of interpretability from parametrizing L instead of K.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Clear mathematical derivation of bounds
- Well-organized presentation
- Demonstrates application to a real dataset

**Con Against Acceptance Arguments:**
- Lacks clear novelty (direct extension from GP literature)
- Insufficient experimental validation against relevant baselines
- Poorly justified choice of parametrizing L instead of K
- No convincing evidence of practical advantages over existing methods
- Toy experiments don't demonstrate the claimed benefits for real-world applications

This paper does not present a sufficiently novel or validated contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors have taken a known technique from Gaussian process literature and applied it to DPPs without demonstrating that the technique provides meaningful advantages for DPP inference. The paper would benefit from more thorough experimental comparisons with the most relevant baseline methods (particularly [7]) and a clearer demonstration of why these bounds are practically useful where existing methods fail.
