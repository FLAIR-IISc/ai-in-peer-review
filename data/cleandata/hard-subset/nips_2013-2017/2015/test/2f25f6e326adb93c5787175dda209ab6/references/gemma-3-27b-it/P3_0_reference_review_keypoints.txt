- Summary: Proposes non-spectral bounds on DPP likelihoods to avoid expensive eigen decompositions and integrates them into variational and MCMC inference for discrete and continuous DPPs, validated on synthetic and real data.

- Strength: Addresses a core bottleneck by removing the need for spectral decomposition, enabling scalable DPP inference.

- Strength: Derives novel, mathematically sound non-spectral likelihood bounds inspired by GP variational techniques.

- Strength: Method applies to both discrete and continuous DPPs, increasing practical applicability.

- Strength: Empirical evaluation is thorough, with synthetic MCMC accuracy checks and a real diabetic neuropathy application.

- Strength: Builds on and clearly connects to existing literature in DPPs, Gaussian Processes, and variational inference.

- Strength: Paper is well-written and organized, clearly presenting problem, methods, and results.

- Weakness: Kernel L parameterization can lead to parameter identifiability issues (e.g., κ), reducing interpretability of learned parameters.

- Weakness: Optimizing pseudo-inputs adds computational cost; trade-offs versus spectral methods and scaling behavior need deeper analysis.

- Weakness: Limited empirical comparison to other existing DPP inference methods, including spectral approximations.

- Weakness: Theoretical intuition for when the bounds are tight and why they work could be developed further despite existing determinant-inequality proofs.

- Overall Assessment: Strong, significant contribution that effectively addresses a key practical limitation in DPP inference, with acknowledged but nonfatal limitations.

- Argument for acceptance: Solves a critical inference bottleneck and provides novel bounds that work in practice on synthetic and real datasets.

- Argument for acceptance: Broad applicability to discrete and continuous settings and clear, solid presentation.

- Argument against acceptance: Parameter identifiability concerns with L parameterization.

- Argument against acceptance: Added computational burden from pseudo-input optimization.

- Argument against acceptance: Insufficient comparative evaluation against alternative inference methods.

- Recommendation: Strong Accept — the paper’s strengths and potential impact outweigh its limitations.
