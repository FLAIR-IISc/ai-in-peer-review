## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a significant practical bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the computational cost of spectral decomposition, particularly for large datasets or continuous domains. The authors propose and analyze bounds on the DPP likelihood that *avoid* explicit spectral decomposition. They leverage ideas from variational inference for Gaussian Processes to derive these bounds, adapting them for both finite and continuous DPPs. The paper demonstrates the efficacy of these bounds within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, validating them with both a synthetic 1D Gaussian example and a real-world dataset of nerve fiber patterns.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on a substantial body of work concerning DPPs, properly citing foundational papers [1, 2, 3, 4, 5] and more recent applications [6, 7, 8, 9]. It acknowledges the trade-offs between parameterizing the kernel K vs. L [3, 7] and positions itself firmly within the latter, tractable camp.  The key innovation lies in moving beyond the spectral bounds proposed in [7], which still require iterative power methods.  The connection to variational inference in GPs [10, 11] is clearly articulated, and the extension to the continuous case builds on related operator theory [18, 19]. The authors accurately situate their contribution as providing a practical alternative when spectral methods are intractable.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Core Practical Issue:** The avoidance of spectral decomposition is a crucial step towards making DPP inference scalable and applicable to real-world problems. This is a significant contribution.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:**  The derivation of the bounds in Propositions 1 & 2 appears rigorous, relying on established results from determinant inequalities [14]. The extension to continuous domains is a notable achievement.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the mathematical notation is handled appropriately.  The decision to initially focus on the finite case and then extend to continuous domains is helpful for reader understanding.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, while not exhaustive, are convincing. The 1D Gaussian example effectively demonstrates the correctness of the MCMC implementation. The application to the diabetic neuropathy dataset shows the method's promise in a more complex scenario.
* **Connections to Existing Methods:** The clear articulation of the link between these bounds and variational sparse GPs is insightful.  The retrospective sampling trick within MCMC [17] is appropriately referenced and applied.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Identifiability:** The experiments, particularly the 1D Gaussian case, reveal a difficulty in identifying some parameters (specifically κ) when using the L-parametrization. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t fully explore the implications or potential solutions. Further discussion on the limitations of direct L-parametrization would be valuable.  
* **Computational Cost of Pseudo-Input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition is a win, the optimization over pseudo-inputs (Z) during both variational inference and MCMC introduces a new computational burden. The paper doesn’t provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of this optimization relative to spectral methods for moderately sized problems.  A more thorough complexity analysis would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The real-world application is somewhat limited. While demonstrating the method's feasibility, it doesn't provide a particularly compelling comparison against existing DPP inference techniques (e.g., those in [7]) on a comparable dataset.  A more direct comparison would strengthen the claim of practical benefit.
* **Discussion of Assumptions:** The continuous DPP case relies on assumptions about the kernel L and base measure µ, specifically concerning Mercer’s theorem and trace-class operators. While mentioned, a slightly more detailed discussion of these assumptions and their implications for real-world applications would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Solves a critical practical problem in DPP inference, namely the computational cost of spectral decomposition.
* Presents a theoretically sound solution with well-derived bounds.
* Demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed approach through both synthetic and real-world experiments.
* Contributes a valuable addition to the toolbox of DPP inference methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Parameter identifiability concerns when using the L-parametrization.
* The computational cost of pseudo-input optimization needs further investigation.
* Limited scope of experimental validation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a significant challenge in DPP inference. The proposed bounds provide a practical alternative to spectral methods and are supported by solid theoretical analysis and empirical validation. While some weaknesses remain, they are acknowledged by the authors and do not detract significantly from the overall contribution.  I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and recommend **acceptance**.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**Confidence:** 8/10.  A slight concern about the parameter identifiability and limited scope of experiments prevents a higher confidence score.
