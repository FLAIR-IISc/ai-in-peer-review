- Addresses the computational bottleneck in DPP likelihood inference by removing the need for spectral decomposition of operator L.
- Proposes novel nonspectral bounds on the DPP likelihood that avoid eigenvalue computations.
- Derives bounds using determinant inequalities and draws inspiration from variational inference for Gaussian processes.
- Extends the nonspectral approach from finite sets to continuous domains, providing a unified framework.
- Theoretical contributions (Propositions 1 and 2) are sound, properly referenced, and proven in the supplement.
- Experimental validation includes synthetic Gaussian examples (compared to exact spectral methods) and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset.
- Authors acknowledge and discuss the interpretability trade-off of parametrizing L instead of K.
- Writing and organization are clear and accessible, with helpful roadmap and adequate supplementary details for reproduction.
- Novelty: first nonspectral bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs without eigenvalue computations.
- Significance: enables scalable DPP inference, broadening applicability to large-scale and spatial statistics problems.
- Experimental results show comparable performance to spectral-based methods.
- Main weakness: lacks a detailed runtime comparison with existing spectral methods (e.g., [7]).
- Variational inference results in Section 5.1 are described as "poorly consistent" and need clearer explanation and contrast with MCMC.
- Limited discussion on how to mitigate interpretability issues from parametrizing L.
- Recommendation: accept — high-quality, significant contribution with minor addressable weaknesses.
- Suggested improvements: add runtime comparison table vs. spectral methods, expand discussion of interpretability trade-offs and mitigation, and clarify/contrast variational vs. MCMC results.
