

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

## Summary
This paper proposes nonspectral bounds on the likelihood of determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoid the computationally expensive spectral decomposition required in standard likelihood-based inference. The authors derive bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs using "pseudo-inputs" (inducing points), which they incorporate into variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods. The approach draws inspiration from variational sparse Gaussian processes and claims to provide a computationally efficient alternative to existing methods like those in [7], which require power iteration to compute eigenvalues.

## Strengths
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to DPP inference. The bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 are derived using a nontrivial determinant inequality, and the extension to continuous domains is well-executed. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrates the practical applicability of the approach. The authors are careful to acknowledge the interpretability trade-offs of parametrizing L directly instead of K, and they provide useful insights about the behavior of pseudo-inputs.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

### Lack of Comparative Evidence
The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of rigorous comparison to existing methods. While the authors acknowledge [7] as using spectral bounds, they fail to demonstrate how their nonspectral approach offers meaningful advantages in terms of computational efficiency, accuracy, or scalability. The paper does not provide:
1. Runtime comparisons with spectral methods
2. Accuracy comparisons using the same dataset and evaluation metrics
3. Analysis of how the quality of the bounds depends on the number of pseudo-inputs

Without these comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed method represents a meaningful improvement over existing approaches. The claim that their method "does not require any eigendecomposition" is misleading, as the pseudo-inputs effectively serve as an alternative to spectral decomposition, just like the eigenvalues in [7].

### Questionable Parametrization Choice
The authors make a questionable choice to parametrize the kernel L directly rather than K, which they acknowledge makes interpretation more difficult. The experiments in Section 5.1 demonstrate this problem clearly: the parameter κ (related to the base measure) is poorly identified and varies across runs, while the other parameters show high variability. This undermines the paper's claim that their approach provides a practical alternative to existing methods.

### Limited Novelty
The core technique (using pseudo-inputs to approximate determinants) is directly borrowed from variational sparse Gaussian processes [11], with minimal adaptation for DPPs. The extension to continuous domains is a natural progression but not particularly novel. The paper fails to clearly articulate how this work differs from or improves upon the existing literature on DPP inference, especially [7] which already addresses the spectral decomposition bottleneck.

### Insufficient Validation
The experimental section lacks proper validation against established baselines. The diabetic neuropathy dataset experiments show convergence of the overdispersion measures as the number of inducing variables increases, but this is expected behavior for any approximation method. The paper does not compare against the state-of-the-art DPP inference methods to demonstrate that their approach provides better results or is more efficient.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality**: The theoretical development is sound, but the experimental validation is insufficient. The paper does not provide the necessary evidence to support its claims about the practical advantages of the proposed method.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good explanations of the technical concepts. The connection to Gaussian processes is well-articulated.

**Originality**: The approach is not substantially novel. It's a straightforward adaptation of existing variational techniques from Gaussian processes to DPPs, with minimal theoretical innovation. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how this work differs from or improves upon previous contributions.

**Significance**: The proposed method does not represent a significant advance in the field. The computational bottleneck addressed is already handled by existing methods, and the paper fails to demonstrate that their approach provides meaningful improvements in practice. The choice to parametrize L directly rather than K also limits the practical significance of the work.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents technically sound mathematics, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed method offers meaningful advantages over existing approaches. The lack of rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art methods, the questionable parametrization choice, and the limited novelty of the core technique undermine the paper's contribution. The field of DPP inference is already well-served by existing methods, and this work does not convincingly demonstrate that it advances the state of the art.

The authors should either:
1. Provide comprehensive comparisons with existing methods to demonstrate clear advantages, or
2. Reconsider their parametrization choice to make the method more interpretable and practical

Without these critical improvements, the paper does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS. The field would benefit more from a thorough comparison of existing methods rather than a minor extension of variational techniques from Gaussian processes to DPPs.
