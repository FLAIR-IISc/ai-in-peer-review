- Paper addresses the bottleneck of computing det(I+L) for DPPs by proposing nonspectral, inducing-point-based bounds that avoid eigen decompositions.  
- For finite domains the authors define a low-rank surrogate Q using m pseudo-inputs Z and prove bounds det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ det(L+I) ≤ det(Q+I).  
- They extend the construction to continuous DPPs (Fredholm determinants) with analogous bounds involving LZ and Ψ (Ψij = ∫L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x)).  
- Two inference methods are instantiated: a variational lower bound (O(n m^2) per eval) and a retrospective MH sampler that refines Z on-the-fly to make exact acceptance decisions.  
- Continuous-case contribution is nontrivial and practical for kernels/base measures where Ψ is analytic (e.g., Gaussian–Gaussian).  
- Conceptual novelty: first nonspectral, inducing-point determinant bounds for finite and continuous DPPs with clear links to sparse GP variational methods.  
- Computational benefit: avoids eigen computations and scales without storing full L, with intuition that Q is a Nyström-like surrogate.  
- Retrospective MH is exact (modulo randomness) given sufficiently tight bounds and matches ideal MH in toy experiments.  
- Empirically, pseudo-inputs self-organize and variational estimates stabilize as m increases; bounds tighten with m in experiments.  
- Theoretical gap: no formal convergence/rate guarantees that bounds tighten as m→∞ or under nested Z, only informal arguments.  
- Limited experimental scope: no large-scale discrete benchmarks or runtime/accuracy comparisons to spectral methods, fixed-point MLE, or log-det approximations.  
- Variational instability observed (parameter non-identifiability) with inconsistent runs; paper lacks guidance on when VI is reliable or on reparameterizations/prior fixes.  
- Continuous-case practicality depends on computing Ψ; unclear how quadrature/Monte Carlo approximations affect MH correctness and bound tightness.  
- Missing related work discussion on scalable log-det approximations (Hutchinson/Lanczos) that are relevant baselines for finite DPPs.  
- Implementation details are sketched but insufficient: gradients w.r.t. Z and θ, numerical stability/invertibility of LZ, and Z initialization strategies need elaboration for reproducibility.  
- Presentation is overall clear and organized, but notation/assumption statements could be more explicit and minor typos exist.  
- Originality is strong: novel combination of Seiler–Simon determinant inequality with GP variational machinery applied to DPP likelihoods.  
- Significance is high for enabling DPP inference without spectra, especially in continuous spatial settings, but wider adoption needs stronger empirical comparisons and guidance on m/Z.  
- Suggested improvements: provide theoretical convergence statements, add large-scale discrete experiments vs. baselines, discuss log-det approximation methods, and include implementation/gradient details and guidance on computing Ψ.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, general nonspectral bounds with practical inference methods and likely to inspire follow-up work.  
- Rejection arguments: limited empirical validation, missing theoretical guarantees on tightness and Z selection, and observed VI instability raise robustness concerns.
