## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in parametric inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors propose and analyze bounds on the DPP likelihood that *do not* rely on spectral decomposition, offering a practical alternative for large-scale or continuous DPPs where such decomposition is intractable. They demonstrate the use of these bounds within Variational Inference (VI) and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks, with both theoretical analysis and experimental validation on synthetic and real-world datasets.  The work builds upon existing techniques from Gaussian Processes (GPs) and extends them to the DPP setting.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant practical issue:** The spectral decomposition requirement is a major limitation of many DPP inference methods. This paper directly tackles that problem.
* **Sound theoretical foundation:** The bounds are derived rigorously, leveraging existing results on determinants. The extension to continuous DPPs is a valuable contribution.
* **Practical applicability:** The bounds allow for the use of standard VI and MCMC techniques without the computational burden of eigendecomposition.
* **Comprehensive evaluation:** The authors provide experimental results on both a synthetic example (where a ground truth exists for comparison) and a real-world dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.  The comparison to existing methods (specifically [7]) is helpful.
* **Clear writing:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details. The motivation is clearly stated, and the authors appropriately flag sections that can be skipped for readers unfamiliar with operator theory.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Interpretability:** The authors acknowledge, and the experiments confirm, that directly parametrizing the kernel *L* (rather than *K*) leads to loss of interpretability of the inferred parameters. While the focus is on tractability, this is a significant drawback.
* **Computational cost of Pseudo-input Optimization:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, the optimization of the pseudo-inputs *Z* within the VI and MCMC frameworks adds a computational cost. The paper demonstrates it’s manageable, but doesn't fully explore the trade-offs in detail, especially as dimensionality increases.
* **Limited comparison to alternative approximations:** The main comparison is to the spectral bounds in [7].  While relevant, it would be beneficial to compare against other non-spectral DPP approximation techniques, if any exist.
* **Continuous Case Caveats:** The continuous case relies on assumptions about the kernel and measure (specifically, satisfying conditions for Mercer’s theorem or similar). These assumptions are acknowledged but could be discussed with more nuance regarding their practical implications.



**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations appear correct, and the experimental validation supports the claims. The complete analysis of the bounds and their application to inference algorithms demonstrates a complete piece of work. The authors are upfront about the limitations of their approach.  **Strong.**
* **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written. The organization is logical, and the authors provide sufficient background information for readers to understand the key concepts.  **Strong.**
* **Originality:** While building on existing techniques (e.g., sparse GP approximations), the application of these ideas to DPP inference without spectral decomposition is novel. The extension to continuous DPPs further adds to the originality. **Good.**
* **Significance:**  The ability to perform scalable inference for DPPs without spectral decomposition is a significant advancement. This could enable the application of DPPs to a wider range of problems and datasets. The demonstrated results on a real-world dataset suggest practical impact. **Good.**

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

* Solves a critical practical bottleneck in DPP inference.
* Provides a theoretically sound and well-supported method.
* Demonstrates effectiveness through experiments.
* Clear and well-written paper.

**Con Acceptance:**

* Loss of parameter interpretability.
* Additional computational cost associated with pseudo-input optimization.
* Limited comparison to other approximation methods.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The authors address a significant practical challenge in DPP inference with a theoretically sound and well-validated approach. While the loss of parameter interpretability and the additional computational cost of pseudo-input optimization are drawbacks, the benefits of avoiding spectral decomposition outweigh these concerns, particularly for large-scale problems. The paper is a valuable contribution to the field and is likely to be of interest to a broad audience within NIPS.
