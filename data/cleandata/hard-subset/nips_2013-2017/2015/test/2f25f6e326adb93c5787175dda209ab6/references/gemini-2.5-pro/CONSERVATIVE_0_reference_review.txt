**Summary**

This paper addresses the computational challenge of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), specifically the evaluation of the `det(L + I)` term, which is typically intractable for large or continuous domains. The authors propose novel, non-spectral bounds on this determinant, drawing inspiration from the use of inducing variables in sparse Gaussian Processes (GPs). They derive a lower and an upper bound on the likelihood for both finite and continuous DPPs. These bounds are constructed using a low-rank approximation of the kernel matrix `L` based on a small set of "pseudo-inputs." The paper then demonstrates how these bounds can be incorporated into two standard inference schemes: a variational inference (VI) approach that maximizes the lower bound, and a Metropolis-Hastings (MH) MCMC algorithm that uses both bounds for exact-to-the-model inference via retrospective sampling. The methods are evaluated on a synthetic 1D dataset and a real-world spatial point process dataset of nerve fibers.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a significant problem:** The computational cost of the DPP likelihood, particularly the determinant of the kernel matrix, is a major barrier to its application in large-scale settings. Proposing an inference method that avoids direct eigendecomposition is a well-motivated and important research direction.

2.  **Methodologically sound connection to established techniques:** The core idea of using inducing points to form a low-rank approximation is a well-understood and successful technique in the Gaussian Process literature. The authors make a clear and technically sound connection, adapting this idea to the DPP context. The extension of the bounds to the continuous operator case is a non-trivial and valuable technical contribution.

3.  **Flexibility of the proposed framework:** The paper demonstrates that the derived bounds are versatile enough to be used in both an approximate VI framework and an asymptotically exact MCMC framework. This showcases the general utility of the core contribution.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Comparison to Existing Methods:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the absence of a direct and rigorous comparison against the primary baseline method it aims to improve upon. The introduction specifically critiques the spectral-bound approach of Affandi et al. [7], which also uses MCMC, for its reliance on power iteration methods. However, the experiments do not provide any empirical evidence to support the claim that the proposed non-spectral approach is superior. A convincing evaluation would require a direct comparison of wall-clock time, bound tightness, and posterior accuracy against the spectral method on a shared task. Without this, the practical significance of the proposed method remains unsubstantiated.

2.  **Inconsistent Experimental Narrative:** The paper presents two inference methods (VI and MCMC) but evaluates them in a disconnected manner. In the synthetic experiment, the VI method is reported to yield "poorly consistent results," leading the authors to use the more robust MCMC approach. However, in the real-world diabetic neuropathy experiment, they revert to using only the VI method. This is concerning. Why was the MCMC method, shown to be more reliable in the first experiment, not applied to the more complex real-world data? This choice undermines the confidence in the results presented for the neuropathy dataset. The claim that the variational method is a "cheap-but-approximate" alternative is not fully convincing when it fails on a simple toy problem.

3.  **Limited Novelty:** The core technical contribution is the application of the inducing variable trick from sparse GPs to the DPP likelihood. While this is a clever and useful adaptation, it is an incremental step rather than a fundamental breakthrough. The key determinant inequality used in the proof is from a much older paper (Seiler & Simon, 1975). The work primarily consists of combining existing ideas from different fields.

4.  **Practicalities of Pseudo-Input Optimization are Under-discussed:** The performance of the bounds is critically dependent on the choice of the pseudo-inputs `Z`. The paper states that these are optimized jointly with the kernel parameters but provides little detail on the challenges of this nested optimization. This is a non-convex optimization problem, and its cost, stability, and sensitivity to initialization are important practical concerns that are not adequately addressed. The observation that the optimized pseudo-inputs form neat grids is interesting but anecdotal.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The motivation is clear, and the mathematical derivations for the finite case are easy to follow. Section 4 on the continuous case is necessarily more technical but is well-signposted for readers who may wish to skip it.

**Recommendation: Borderline Reject**

This paper presents a methodologically sound and interesting idea for scalable DPP inference. The connection to sparse GPs is elegant and the technical work to derive the bounds is solid. However, the contribution is significantly weakened by insufficient and inconsistent experimental validation. The claims of superiority over existing spectral methods are not backed by empirical evidence, which is a critical omission for a paper focused on computational efficiency. The failure of the VI method in the toy experiment followed by its exclusive use in the real-data experiment casts doubt on the robustness and reliability of the proposed approximate inference scheme.

While the core idea has merit, the work in its current state feels incomplete. To be acceptable for NIPS, the paper would need a substantially revised experimental section that includes:
*   A direct, quantitative comparison against the spectral-bound MCMC method of Affandi et al. [7].
*   An application of the proposed MCMC method to the real-world dataset to provide a more reliable analysis than the VI results.
*   A more thorough discussion of the computational costs and practical challenges of optimizing the pseudo-inputs.

As it stands, the paper presents a promising approach but fails to provide the rigorous evidence needed to convince a skeptical reader of its practical advantages over established methods.
