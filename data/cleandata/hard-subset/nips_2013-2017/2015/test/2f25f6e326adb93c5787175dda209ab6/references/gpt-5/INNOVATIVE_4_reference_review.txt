Summary
This paper tackles a central bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs): the need for spectral decompositions of the L-operator (or large log-determinants). The authors propose non-spectral, computable upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) in both finite and continuous settings, built from low-rank, inducing-point approximations of L inspired by variational sparse GPs (Titsias, 2009) and a determinant inequality (Seiler & Simon, 1975). For finite domains, they define Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY via m pseudo-inputs Z and prove bounds on 1/det(I+L), yielding a tractable variational lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood and enabling retrospective Metropolis–Hastings (MH) with adaptive tightening of bounds by increasing m and optimizing Z. They extend the theory to continuous DPPs via Fredholm determinants, deriving analogous bounds that only require computing Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj) dµ(x). Experiments include (i) a 1D Gaussian–Gaussian setting where ground-truth spectrum and Fredholm determinant are available, validating the MH scheme, and (ii) a real 2D dataset of epidermal nerve fibers, showing that variational training with increasing m converges to stable overdispersion measures that separate clinical classes.

Relation to prior work
Previous DPP learning methods typically rely on eigendecompositions (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML; Gillenwater et al.), or require power iterations to approximate leading eigenvalues for MH. Nystrom approximations have been explored for scalability, but most pipelines still hinge on spectral information. The present work eliminates spectral computations entirely, reusing inducing-variable machinery from sparse GPs and a classical determinant inequality to craft likelihood bounds applicable in both finite and continuous cases. The continuous extension via Fredholm determinants and closed-form Ψ under Gaussian–Gaussian setups is particularly noteworthy.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual contribution: A clean, non-spectral route to DPP likelihoods that unifies finite and continuous settings. The inducing-point framing plus determinant inequalities is original in the DPP context and opens a new direction distinct from spectral approximations.
- Practicality: Complexity O(n m^2) (finite case) with m ≪ n, avoiding O(n^3) eigendecompositions and full n×n storage. For continuous domains, the Fredholm determinant bounds reduce to small m×m objects provided Ψ is computable.
- Algorithms: Both a variational lower bound for fast training and a retrospective MH scheme for exact inference (in the limit), with a principled mechanism to adapt m and Z until an accept/reject decision is certifiable.
- Empirical validation: The 1D Gaussian–Gaussian experiment convincingly shows MH with the proposed bounds matches an “ideal” MH using the true Fredholm determinant. On the neuropathy dataset, the variational procedure yields stable overdispersion measures with increasing m, reproducing known class separation.
- Honest discussion: The paper is transparent about identifiability issues (e.g., κ) when parametrizing L directly and about when variational training may be unreliable.

Weaknesses
- Experimental scope and comparisons: No large-scale finite-domain experiments (e.g., item selection/recommendation) where n is large and the approach could shine, nor runtime/comparison against spectral MCMC or Nyström-based baselines beyond informal complexity claims. A head-to-head with [7] (power-iteration MH) would strengthen the case.
- Theoretical tightness and guidance: While bounds tighten with larger m and optimized Z, the paper provides limited theory about rates/tightness as a function of m or design of Z. Practical guidelines for choosing m, initialization of Z, and stopping criteria would help.
- Continuous case generality: The approach requires Ψ to be tractable; beyond the Gaussian–Gaussian case, guidance on numerical quadrature (e.g., Bornemann 2010, cited) and its effect on bias/variance is brief. A small study showing robustness with numerical Ψ would broaden applicability.
- Algorithmic details for MH: Retrospective MH involves re-optimizing Z at each step to tighten bounds; the computational burden and convergence diagnostics (acceptance rates vs m growth, wall-clock per step) are not quantified.
- Clarity/Presentation: Some equation numbering and footnotes are off; Section 4 contains a few broken sentences and typos. Including more intuition for the determinant inequality and a short proof sketch in the main text would improve readability.

Quality
Technically sound and grounded on established inequalities. The finite and continuous bounds are well-motivated; the variational objective resembles the Titsias bound adapted to DPPs. Empirical evidence is positive but somewhat limited in breadth and comparative evaluation.

Clarity
Generally clear, but could use polish: fix equation/footnote glitches; add intuition and a brief proof sketch for Propositions 1–2; clarify assumptions (e.g., invertibility/conditioning of LZ, trace-class conditions for L, and when (co)variance integrals Ψ exist).

Originality
High. Non-spectral determinant bounds tailored to DPP likelihoods with inducing variables appear new, especially the continuous extension via Fredholm determinants.

Significance
Potentially high. If adopted, this can materially broaden the applicability of DPPs in settings where spectral decompositions are infeasible (large finite sets, continuous domains), and it provides a principled bridge between sparse GP techniques and DPP inference.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide theoretical guarantees or empirical diagnostics on how bound gaps shrink with m and optimized Z? Any connection to posterior variance reduction in the GP analogy?
- How costly is re-optimizing Z per MH step in practice? Could amortization or warm starts be used?
- For continuous settings without closed-form Ψ, what quadrature schemes worked best, and how do they affect the bounds’ validity?
- Any regularization strategies for ill-conditioned LZ?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a large-scale finite-domain experiment with comparisons to spectral methods (e.g., [7]) and Nyström-style baselines, reporting accuracy vs runtime/memory.
- Provide practical heuristics for choosing/increasing m and initializing Z (e.g., k-means++, space-filling).
- Include a short proof sketch and intuition for the determinant inequality in the main text.
- Discuss reparameterizations or priors to address identifiability (e.g., handling κ), and report sensitivity analyses.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a bold, novel contribution that circumvents a core limitation in DPP inference with a principled and broadly applicable idea. While the experimental section could be stronger and the presentation needs polish, the conceptual advance and potential impact justify acceptance.
