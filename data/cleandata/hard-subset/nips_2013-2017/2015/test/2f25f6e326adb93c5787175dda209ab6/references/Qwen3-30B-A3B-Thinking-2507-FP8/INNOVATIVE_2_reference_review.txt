

# Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper presents a novel approach to likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that eliminates the need for spectral knowledge, a major computational bottleneck in existing methods. The authors develop nonspectral bounds on the likelihood that do not require the spectral decomposition of the operator L, which is typically required for DPP inference. These bounds leverage "pseudo-inputs" (inducing points) that can be optimized to tighten the bounds, enabling efficient variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods.

The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by recognizing that the spectral decomposition requirement in DPP inference is not necessary. Previous work, such as [7], required power iteration methods to approximate the spectral decomposition, which is computationally expensive and limited to finite domains. By contrast, the proposed bounds generalize to both finite and continuous DPPs, providing a more versatile inference framework. The theoretical foundation (Propositions 1 and 2) builds on nontrivial determinant inequalities [14] and connects naturally to variational sparse Gaussian processes [11], demonstrating a clever cross-pollination of ideas between two related fields.

The paper's strengths lie in its novelty and clear demonstration of the method's utility. The experiments on both a toy Gaussian continuous DPP and a real diabetic neuropathy dataset effectively validate the approach. The retrospective MCMC method successfully recovers parameters for the continuous case where variational inference struggled with identifiability issues, and the variational inference method scales efficiently with the number of pseudo-inputs. The paper's clear connection to existing work and well-structured presentation make it accessible to a broad audience.

The significance of this work is substantial. DPPs have gained traction as modeling tools for repulsive phenomena across machine learning, statistics, and physics, but their practical adoption has been limited by computational challenges. By removing the spectral decomposition requirement, this paper opens the door to more scalable DPP inference, potentially enabling broader application in areas like search engine diversity modeling [7], forest spatial analysis [4], and news summarization [3].

While the paper is strong in novelty and significance, it has a few limitations. The identifiability issues with directly parametrizing L (as shown in Section 5.1) remain unresolved, though the authors acknowledge this limitation. The paper doesn't deeply explore the trade-offs between the number of pseudo-inputs and computational efficiency, though the experiments suggest the method scales well with m. The connection to the kernel K (rather than L) remains a theoretical gap, as the authors note in Section 6.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS values. It tackles a fundamental limitation in a growing area (DPPs) with a simple yet powerful insight that avoids a major computational hurdle. The authors demonstrate the practical utility of their method through well-designed experiments, and the theoretical contribution is both clean and generalizable.

The work is technically sound, with clear theoretical foundations and strong experimental validation. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to readers familiar with Gaussian processes and point processes. While the method has some limitations, they are acknowledged by the authors, and the core contribution—nonspectral bounds for DPP inference—is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance.

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel approach to DPP inference that eliminates spectral decomposition requirement
- Solves a significant practical bottleneck in DPP applications
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear connections to Gaussian processes
- Effective experimental validation on both toy and real datasets
- Clear presentation and well-motivated contribution

**Con Acceptance:**
- Identifiability issues with parametrizing L directly remain unaddressed
- Limited exploration of the trade-off between pseudo-input count and computational efficiency
- Theoretical connection to kernel K is not fully resolved

This paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling with DPPs. The authors have identified and solved a key computational bottleneck that has limited the practical application of DPPs, and they've done so with a simple, elegant approach that builds on existing ideas in a novel way. For a venue that values bold, novel contributions over incremental methodological improvements, this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance.
