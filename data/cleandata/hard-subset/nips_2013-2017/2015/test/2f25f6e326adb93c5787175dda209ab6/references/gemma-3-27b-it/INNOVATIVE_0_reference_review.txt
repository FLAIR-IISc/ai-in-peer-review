This paper tackles a significant practical issue in applying Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): avoiding the computationally expensive spectral decomposition required for likelihood evaluation. The core idea – leveraging bounds inspired by variational Gaussian processes to sidestep this decomposition – is genuinely novel and addresses a bottleneck that limits the scalability of DPP inference. The authors clearly articulate the problem and connect it to existing work, both in DPPs and related fields like GPs. The extension to continuous DPPs is a nice addition, broadening the applicability of their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed approach of utilizing determinant bounds for likelihood-free DPP inference is a significant departure from standard methods and directly addresses a key computational limitation.
* **Practicality:** Avoiding spectral decomposition makes DPP inference feasible in settings where it was previously intractable (e.g., large datasets, continuous domains with complex kernels).
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The use of existing bounds from GP literature and the careful derivation of analogous bounds for DPPs provide a solid theoretical basis.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The authors effectively explain the mathematical details and motivate their choices.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experiments, while relatively simple, demonstrate the feasibility and correctness of the proposed MCMC method on a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian example, and the variational approach on a relevant real-world dataset.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Identifiability Concerns:** The experiments reveal issues with parameter identifiability, specifically with the scaling parameter κ in the Gaussian case. This isn’t a fatal flaw, but it highlights a potential limitation of directly parametrizing the kernel L.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments, especially the real-world application, could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing DPP inference methods (even those that *do* require spectral decomposition, to quantify the trade-offs).
* **Computational Cost of Bounds:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, the proposed method introduces the need to optimize over pseudo-inputs (Z). The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of this optimization compared to spectral decomposition for smaller problem instances would be valuable.  The runtime reported for the diabetic neuropathy dataset is a good start, but more analysis of scaling with problem size is needed.
* **Minor Clarification Needed:** The connection between the continuous and discrete bounds, and the assumptions required for the continuous case, could be more explicitly stated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a significant problem in DPP inference.
* The theoretical contributions are sound, and the experimental validation provides initial evidence of effectiveness.
* The potential impact is high – this work could unlock the application of DPPs to a much wider range of problems.
* The authors are clearly aware of the limitations of their work and discuss them honestly.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The identifiability issues observed in the experiments raise some concerns about the practical utility of the method.
* The experimental validation is somewhat limited, and a more thorough comparison with existing approaches would strengthen the paper.
* The computational cost of optimizing the pseudo-inputs could be a bottleneck for very large datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and potentially impactful approach to DPP inference. While there are some limitations, the originality and significance of the work outweigh these concerns. I’m willing to overlook the relatively limited experimental validation and the identifiability issue, given the boldness of the approach. The paper pushes the boundaries of DPP modeling and provides a valuable contribution to the field. 

**Recommendation:** Accept. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the comments regarding parameter identifiability and a more thorough analysis of computational costs. A discussion of potential strategies for selecting or initializing the pseudo-inputs would also be beneficial.
