- Proposes nonspectral bounds for the intractable DPP normalizing constant, avoiding spectral decomposition for finite and continuous DPPs.  
- Uses "pseudo-inputs" inspired by sparse Gaussian processes to derive bounds enabling variational inference and MCMC without spectral knowledge.  
- Theoretical contribution: novel bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 that remove the spectral bottleneck in DPP inference.  
- Practical contribution: enables more efficient likelihood-based inference for larger DPP datasets.  
- Experiments on synthetic and real data (diabetic neuropathy) show variational convergence with more inducing variables and consistent MCMC results.  
- Well-motivated problem and clear connection to prior work that relied on expensive spectral approximations.  
- Strengths: clear theoretical development, thorough experimental validation, and practical applicability.  
- Weakness: parametrizing L instead of K reduces interpretability, and the paper under-discusses practical implications of this trade-off.  
- Weakness: limited empirical comparison with prior spectral methods (e.g., [7]); lacks direct wall-clock time and accuracy comparisons on the same datasets.  
- Writing and organization are good, though the finite vs. continuous case connection could be made more explicit.  
- Supplementary material contains rigorous proofs, but main paper could better summarize key proof insights for non-expert readers.  
- Originality: novel nonspectral approach distinct from prior spectral-bound methods, with a well-explained link to sparse GP literature.  
- Significance: addresses a major computational bottleneck, making DPPs more usable in large-scale ML applications (image search, recommender systems, spatial stats).  
- Recommendation: technically sound and empirically convincing; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to add comparative experiments and deeper interpretability discussion.
