Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) when the spectrum of the L-operator is unavailable or expensive to compute. The key idea is to bound the normalizing constant det(I+L) (finite case: det(L+I); continuous case: Fredholm determinant) using low-rank approximations induced by a set of pseudo-inputs Z, without relying on eigendecompositions. For finite domains, the work proposes bounds of the form det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I), where Q = LYZ L_Z^{-1} L_ZY, inspired by variational sparse GP ideas [11] and a determinant inequality [14]. The approach extends to continuous DPPs via analogous bounds in terms of L_Z and Ψ = ∫ L_Zx L_xZ dµ(x), enabling inference without spectral knowledge of L. These bounds are used to build (a) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood that can be optimized jointly over kernel parameters and Z, and (b) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that refines the bounds adaptively.

This complements prior ML work on DPP parameter learning that either parametrizes K with explicit spectral constraints or parametrizes L but still relies on eigen-information for the normalizer (e.g., [7] using spectral truncations and power iterations), Nyström approaches for finite DPPs [13], and EM/fixed-point methods for discrete DPPs [8, 9]. The use of inducing-point style bounds for DPP likelihoods and the extension to continuous DPPs without spectral decompositions appear novel. The continuous formulation connects to Fredholm determinant numerics [23] but avoids quadrature over eigenvalues, instead leveraging kernel integrals Ψ.

Quality:
- The bounding strategy is mathematically plausible and grounded in [14]. The finite-domain bounds mirror the structure of the Titsias variational GP bound [11], with a trace correction tightening the lower bound.
- The computational claims are reasonable: evaluating the lower bound requires O(n m^2) time via LYZ and LZY multiplications and determinants of m×m matrices; numerator terms det(L_Yt) depend on |Y_t| and are standard in DPP learning.
- The extension to continuous DPPs is well-motivated. When Ψ can be computed in closed form (e.g., Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures), the method is practical.
- Experimental validation includes a controlled 1D Gaussian/Gaussian case where ground-truth spectrum and Fredholm determinants are available, and a real 2D spatial dataset (diabetic neuropathy), demonstrating both MCMC and variational use. Results show coherent behavior as m increases and agreement with “ideal” MH in the toy case.

Clarity:
The presentation is generally clear and well-organized. The separation between finite and continuous settings is helpful. Some details (e.g., gradients w.r.t. Z, numerical stability of L_Z inversion, complexity breakdowns for all terms) are deferred or briefly discussed; adding implementation notes would further improve reproducibility. Minor typographical issues exist but do not obstruct comprehension.

Originality:
The nonspectral determinant bounds tailored to DPP likelihoods, and their deployment for both variational inference and retrospective MH, appear original relative to [7, 8, 9, 13]. The continuous-domain extension with computable Ψ for common kernels/base measures is a notable contribution.

Significance:
DPPs remain important for modeling diversity/repulsion in both discrete and spatial settings. Removing dependence on eigen-computations makes likelihood-based inference more widely applicable, especially in continuous domains and large finite domains where power iterations are slow or inapplicable. The ability to trade off accuracy and cost via m and Z optimization is practically valuable. The paper should be of interest to both the DPP and approximate inference communities.

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple, general, and computationally attractive bounds that avoid spectral information.
- Unified treatment of finite and continuous DPPs, with clear parallels to sparse GP methodology.
- Practical inference algorithms (variational and MH with retrospective decisions) leveraging the bounds.
- Empirical evidence of correctness (toy case with ground-truth) and utility (real 2D spatial data), with behavior improving as m increases.
- Transparent discussion of L vs K parametrization trade-offs and identifiability issues.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Essential improvements:
- Tightness and diagnostics: Provide more systematic evaluation of bound tightness vs m, beyond qualitative convergence plots. For the finite case, report the gap between the upper and lower bound on log det(L+I) and the resulting impact on parameter estimates. For the continuous case, quantify tightness where ground-truth Fredholm determinants are computable (as in the toy example).
- Comparisons: Include head-to-head runtime and accuracy comparisons with spectral-bound MH from [7] on at least one finite-domain dataset, and with EM/fixed-point methods [8, 9] where applicable. This would clarify regimes where nonspectral bounds are preferable.
- Computational details: Add guidance on optimizing Z (gradient derivations when kernels are differentiable; handling nonconvexity; initialization heuristics such as k-means on estimated intensity, space-filling designs, or greedily minimizing tr(L−Q)). Discuss regularization/jitter for L_Z and numerical stability.

Optional but valuable:
- Scalability: Report scaling experiments for larger n and various m, including memory footprint, and discuss streaming/mini-batch strategies for computing tr(L−Q) in very large finite domains.
- Continuous kernels: Beyond Gaussian/Gaussian, discuss other kernel/base measure pairs where Ψ and tr(L) are tractable (e.g., Matérn with Gaussian base via known convolutions), and provide guidance when only numerical quadrature is available.
- Theoretical insights: Offer conditions under which the bounds converge as m→∞, and if possible, rates in terms of kernel approximation error (e.g., connections to Nyström leverage scores or GP posterior variance integrals).

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Novel, general-purpose bounds for a core bottleneck in DPP inference, applicable in continuous settings where prior methods are limited.
- Solid methodological contribution with clear practical algorithms and encouraging empirical demonstrations.
- Bridges two influential areas (DPPs and sparse GP variational methods) in a technically sound way.

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Limited empirical breadth and head-to-head comparisons with prior DPP learning methods.
- Practical guidance on pseudo-input optimization and stability could be expanded.
- Continuous-domain applicability hinges on computable Ψ; scope beyond Gaussian/Gaussian is not thoroughly explored.

Overall assessment:
A well-motivated and technically sound contribution that removes a key barrier to likelihood-based DPP inference without spectral knowledge. With stronger empirical comparisons and added implementation guidance, the work would be highly impactful. The current version is already a meaningful step forward.
