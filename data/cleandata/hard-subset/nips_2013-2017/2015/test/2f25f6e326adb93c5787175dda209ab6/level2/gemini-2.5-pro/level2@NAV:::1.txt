This paper addresses the key challenge of computationally expensive likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), where the primary bottleneck is the evaluation of the normalizing constant, `det(L + I)`. While prior work relies on costly spectral approximations, this paper introduces novel, non-spectral bounds on the likelihood. Drawing an elegant connection to sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes, the authors use a set of "inducing points" to derive cheap-to-evaluate bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs. These bounds are then leveraged to develop two inference schemes: a fast, approximate method based on variational inference (VI) and an exact MCMC sampler using retrospective sampling.

The primary strength of this work is its originality and significance. The proposed non-spectral bounds provide a principled and computationally efficient alternative for DPP inference, potentially extending the applicability of these models to larger-scale problems. The work is technically sound, clearly written, and the application to both VI and MCMC demonstrates the versatility of the approach. The experiments provide solid validation; the MCMC method is correctly benchmarked on a synthetic problem with a known solution, while the VI approach shows practical utility on a real-world dataset, demonstrating clear separation between data classes.

The paper could be strengthened by a deeper analysis of its limitations. The authors report that the VI method performed poorly on the synthetic experiment ("yields poorly consistent results") but do not investigate the cause of this failure. A discussion on the limitations of the variational approximation—whether the issue is optimization-related or a fundamental property of the bound in certain regimes—would be valuable for practitioners. Furthermore, while the paper adopts the common but less interpretable L-kernel parameterization, the synthetic experiment highlights a resulting parameter non-identifiability issue. A more explicit discussion of this trade-off would be beneficial.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a significant and novel contribution. The proposed method is a valuable addition to the field, making complex DPP models more tractable. I recommend acceptance, encouraging the authors to address the aforementioned limitations in the final version to enhance the paper’s impact.
