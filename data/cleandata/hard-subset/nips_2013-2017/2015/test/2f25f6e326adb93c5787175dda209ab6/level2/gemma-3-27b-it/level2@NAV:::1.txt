## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses the computational challenges of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), specifically the need for spectral decomposition of kernels. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood, for both finite and continuous domains, that circumvent this requirement, enabling efficient variational inference and MCMC methods. The paper is well-motivated, building upon existing work in DPP modeling and addressing a key bottleneck in their application to larger datasets.

The core contribution – the non-spectral likelihood bounds – appears to be a significant step forward. The derivations, based on extending bounds from Gaussian Process literature, are presented with sufficient detail, although some aspects are deferred to the supplementary material. The proposed inference methods (variational and MCMC) are standard and appropriately adapted to utilize the new bounds. The experimental validation on both a toy Gaussian example and a real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset is valuable. The results demonstrate that the proposed MCMC method achieves comparable performance to existing spectral approaches in the simplified setting while avoiding the computationally intensive eigendecomposition. Variational inference is also shown to be effective, providing comparable results to those reported in prior work. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the toy example allows for a direct comparison to “ideal” methods, its simplicity limits the demonstration of practical benefits. The diabetic neuropathy dataset experiment, while valuable, relies on reproducing existing results, rather than showcasing a novel application or significant performance improvement. A more detailed analysis of the computational speedup achieved by avoiding spectral decomposition would be helpful. Additionally, the discussion surrounding the interpretability trade-off of parameterizing L versus K feels somewhat cursory. A deeper exploration of potential strategies to bridge this gap would strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and potentially impactful bounds on DPP likelihoods.
*   Clear presentation and solid theoretical foundations.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness through experiments.
*   Addresses a significant computational bottleneck in DPP inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental validation is somewhat limited in scope.
*   Lack of detailed computational analysis of speedups.
*   Brief discussion of the interpretability trade-off.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP modeling. The proposed bounds offer a promising avenue for scaling up DPP inference to larger datasets and more complex applications. While some areas could be strengthened, the technical soundness and potential impact warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed analysis of computational performance and a more thorough discussion of the interpretability implications of the proposed approach.
