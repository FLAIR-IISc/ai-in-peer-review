- Problem: Efficient likelihood-based parameter inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) is hindered by intractable normalizing constants (det(L+I) or Fredholm determinants).
- Prior work: Spectral approximations (e.g., Affandi et al. 2014) estimate the kernel spectrum but remain computationally expensive.
- Main contribution: Derives novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on the DPP normalizing constant using inducing points/pseudo-inputs.
- Methodic idea: Adapts sparse Gaussian process (Titsias-style) inducing-point approximations to construct low-rank kernel approximations for DPPs.
- Computational benefit: Bounds avoid eigendecompositions and are much cheaper (e.g., O(n m^2) for finite DPPs).
- Scope: Bounds and algorithms are developed for both finite-set and continuous-domain DPPs.
- Inference algorithms: Proposes a fast variational inference (VI) maximizing the lower bound over parameters and inducing locations.
- Exact inference: Presents an asymptotically exact Metropolis–Hastings MCMC with retrospective sampling that tightens bounds adaptively.
- Experiments (synthetic): MCMC matches an ideal sampler on a 1D continuous DPP, validating correctness.
- Experiments (real data): VI produces coherent, useful results on a 2D nerve-fiber dataset, reproducing prior findings.
- Theoretical soundness: Derivations are based on a Seiler & Simon determinant inequality and are mathematically careful.
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written, with effective structure and clear connections to sparse GP literature.
- Originality: The inducing-point, non-spectral bounding approach for DPP likelihoods is novel and distinct from prior spectral methods.
- Significance: Method enables cheaper approximate and exact Bayesian inference for DPPs, likely increasing their practical applicability and scale.
- Strength — Novelty: Introduces a fundamentally new approach to approximating DPP normalizers.
- Strength — Generality: Framework applies to both finite and continuous DPPs.
- Strength — Practicality: Provides concrete VI and MCMC algorithms with clear computational advantages.
- Strength — Accessibility: Clear exposition makes the technical contribution approachable.
- Weakness — Missing direct comparison: Lacks empirical runtime/accuracy comparison to the spectral method of Affandi et al. on real data.
- Weakness — Inducing-point optimization details: More guidance needed on Z initialization, optimizer choice, sensitivity, and selecting m.
- Weakness — L-kernel parameterization: Identifiability issues are noted; paper could discuss mitigation (e.g., priors) within the proposed framework.
- Recommendation: Strongly recommended for acceptance; weaknesses are minor and do not undermine the main contribution.
