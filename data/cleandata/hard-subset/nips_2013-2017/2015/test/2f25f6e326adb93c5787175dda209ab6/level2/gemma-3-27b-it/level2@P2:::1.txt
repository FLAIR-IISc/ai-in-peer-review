This paper addresses the problem of inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) by deriving bounds on the likelihood that avoid the need for spectral decomposition, a common computational bottleneck. The authors provide both finite and continuous domain results, building on existing variational and MCMC inference techniques. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and tackles a practical challenge in applying DPPs to larger datasets.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the growing literature applying DPPs for modeling diversity (e.g., [3, 7, 4]). A key issue in this area is the computational cost of likelihood evaluation, stemming from the need for spectral decomposition. Previous approaches have either parameterized the kernel directly (sacrificing interpretability) or used approximations to the spectrum ([7]). This paper contributes a new approach – deriving bounds on the likelihood that bypass spectral calculations entirely, inspired by techniques from Gaussian process variational inference [11]. They extend these bounds to the continuous domain, providing a more general solution. The connection to related work is well-established, citing key papers in the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivations appear rigorous, with a clear reliance on existing mathematical results [14]. The extension from the finite to the continuous case is also well-justified.
* **Practical Relevance:** Avoiding spectral decomposition is a significant practical contribution. The bounds offer a computationally cheaper alternative to existing inference methods, potentially enabling the application of DPPs to larger problems.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The notation is consistent, and the authors clearly explain the motivation and intuition behind their approach. The organization is logical, building from the finite case to the continuous case.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide experimental results on both a synthetic dataset (demonstrating the correctness of the MCMC implementation) and a real-world dataset (diabetic neuropathy). The results support the effectiveness of their method, and the analysis of the pseudo-inputs is insightful.
* **Connections to Existing Methods:** The authors explicitly link their work to variational inference and MCMC, providing a clear path for implementation and use.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Interpretability Trade-off:** As acknowledged by the authors, parametrizing the kernel *L* rather than *K* sacrifices interpretability. While tractability is gained, understanding the meaning of the learned parameters becomes more difficult. This is a fundamental trade-off, but it's important to emphasize this limitation.
* **Complexity of Bounds:** While avoiding spectral decomposition, the bounds themselves involve computing and optimizing over pseudo-inputs (Z). The computational cost of *this* optimization isn’t fully detailed. While shown to be efficient in experiments, a theoretical analysis of the computational complexity of optimizing over *Z* would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison:**  While comparisons to [7] are made, a more thorough comparison against other DPP inference methods (e.g., non-parametric approaches [8, 9]) would strengthen the paper. Showing performance tradeoffs (speed vs. accuracy) would be particularly helpful.
* **Minor Clarification Needed:**  In Section 4.2, the statement "This is the continuous equivalent to (4)" is a little vague.  While the authors mention the Fredholm determinant, a more explicit connection to the inequalities in Proposition 1 would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant practical challenge in DPP inference – the computational cost of spectral decomposition.
* The proposed bounds offer a computationally cheaper alternative without sacrificing theoretical soundness.
* The empirical results support the effectiveness of the method on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and connects to relevant prior work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The interpretability of the parameters is reduced by directly parametrizing kernel *L*.
* The computational complexity of optimizing over the pseudo-inputs *Z* isn’t fully analyzed.
* The comparison to other DPP inference methods is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs. The proposed bounds provide a practical solution to a significant computational bottleneck, and the empirical results are encouraging. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the computational complexity of optimizing over the pseudo-inputs and adding a more thorough comparison to alternative inference methods. Addressing the minor clarification needed regarding the connection between Propositions 1 and 2 would also be beneficial.
