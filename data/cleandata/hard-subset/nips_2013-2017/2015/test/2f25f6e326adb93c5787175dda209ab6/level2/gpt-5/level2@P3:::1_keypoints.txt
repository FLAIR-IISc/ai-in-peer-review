- Proposes nonspectral bounds on the DPP normalizer det(I+L) using low-rank inducing points Z, avoiding eigendecompositions.
- Finite-domain bounds: det(Q+I) e^{-tr(L−Q)} ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I) with Q induced by pseudo-inputs, inspired by variational sparse GPs and a determinant inequality.
- Extends the bounds to continuous DPPs via L_Z and Ψ = ∫ L_Zx L_xZ dµ(x), enabling likelihood inference without spectral knowledge.
- Uses the bounds to build (a) a variational lower bound optimized over kernel parameters and Z, and (b) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings scheme that refines bounds adaptively.
- Complements prior DPP learning work that relies on spectral info or Nyström; the inducing-point bound idea and continuous extension appear novel.
- Bounding strategy is mathematically plausible and grounded in prior inequality results; lower bound structure mirrors Titsias variational GP with a trace correction.
- Computational cost claims are reasonable: lower-bound evaluation costs O(n m^2) and m×m determinants; likelihood numerator terms are standard.
- Continuous extension is practical when Ψ and tr(L) are available in closed form (e.g., Gaussian kernel with Gaussian base measure).
- Experiments include a controlled 1D Gaussian/Gaussian toy with ground-truth and a real 2D spatial dataset, showing coherent behavior as m increases.
- Presentation is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful separation of finite and continuous settings.
- Some implementation details (gradients w.r.t. Z, numerical stability of L_Z inversion, full complexity breakdown) are deferred and would aid reproducibility.
- The nonspectral determinant bounds and their use for variational inference and retrospective MH are original contributions.
- Removing eigen-dependence broadens applicability of likelihood-based DPP inference, especially for continuous and large finite domains.
- Strengths: simple, general, computationally attractive bounds; unified finite/continuous treatment; practical variational and MH algorithms; empirical validation.
- Essential improvement: report systematic evaluations of bound tightness vs m, including upper/lower log-det gaps and impact on parameter estimates.
- Essential improvement: include runtime and accuracy comparisons with spectral-bound MH [7] and EM/fixed-point methods [8,9] on at least one finite dataset.
- Essential improvement: add guidance on optimizing Z (gradient formulas, initialization heuristics, handling nonconvexity) and numerical stabilization/jitter for L_Z.
- Optional: report scalability experiments for larger n and various m, including memory usage and potential streaming/mini-batch approaches for tr(L−Q).
- Optional: discuss other kernel/base-measure pairs where Ψ and tr(L) are tractable and guidance when numerical quadrature is needed.
- Optional/theoretical: provide conditions and possible convergence rates as m→∞, relating to Nyström/GP approximation errors or leverage scores.
- Pro-acceptance: novel, general-purpose nonspectral bounds addressing a core bottleneck in DPP inference, with solid methodology and promising empirical results.
- Con-acceptance: limited empirical breadth and head-to-head comparisons; need more practical guidance on pseudo-input optimization and stability.
- Overall: technically sound and meaningful contribution that removes a key barrier to DPP likelihood inference; would be more impactful with stronger empirical comparisons and implementation guidance.
