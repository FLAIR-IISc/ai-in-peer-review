

This paper addresses the problem of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a class of point processes that model repulsiveness between points. The main challenge in DPP inference is the intractable normalizing constant in the likelihood, which involves evaluating a large determinant for finite sets or a Fredholm determinant for continuous domains. Previous approaches (e.g., [7]) require spectral decomposition of the kernel matrix/operator, which is computationally expensive and limits scalability.

The authors propose a novel approach to bypass the spectral decomposition by deriving bounds on the likelihood that do not depend on the spectrum of the kernel. Their key insight is to use "pseudo-inputs" (inducing variables) to create cheap-to-evaluate bounds, inspired by similar techniques used in Gaussian process inference. The authors prove two main bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) for finite and continuous domains, respectively, and demonstrate how these bounds can be used for variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a technically sound solution to a well-known challenge in DPP inference. The bounds are derived from a nontrivial determinant inequality, and the proofs are rigorous (though relegated to supplementary material).
2. The authors clearly explain the connection to Gaussian process inference, making the contribution accessible to readers familiar with that literature.
3. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrates the practical utility of the approach. The toy experiment with a known spectral decomposition allows for validation against "ideal" likelihood-based methods, while the diabetic neuropathy dataset shows applicability to real-world problems.
4. The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly the interpretability issues when parametrizing the kernel L directly instead of K, which is a thoughtful consideration of the trade-offs.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison to existing methods in terms of computational complexity. While the authors claim their approach is cheaper than spectral-based methods, a more explicit complexity analysis would strengthen the contribution.
2. The motivation for using the kernel L instead of K is somewhat brief. While the authors acknowledge the interpretability trade-off, a more detailed discussion of why this is the standard practice in machine learning would help contextualize the work.

Originality:
The core contribution of deriving spectral-free bounds for DPP likelihoods is novel. While the authors draw inspiration from GP inference, their extension to DPPs is not trivial, and the bounds are specifically tailored to the structure of DPP likelihoods. The extension to continuous domains is also a significant contribution that has not been addressed in previous work.

Significance:
This work has the potential to significantly improve the practical applicability of DPPs in machine learning. By avoiding the need for spectral decomposition, the proposed methods make likelihood-based inference feasible for larger datasets, which is a major limitation in current DPP applications. The methods could be directly applied to many existing DPP applications in news summarization, search engines, and spatial statistics.

Clarity:
The paper is well-written and organized. The authors provide clear definitions of DPPs, explain the challenges in inference, and present their solution in a logical sequence. The connection to Gaussian process inference is well-explained, making the paper accessible to a broader audience. The experimental section is thorough and well-motivated.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field. The technical approach is sound, the experimental validation is convincing, and the methods address a real limitation in current DPP inference. The paper is well-motivated and makes a clear contribution to the literature.

Recommendation: Accept

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel approach to DPP inference that avoids spectral decomposition
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with practical validation
- Clear connection to related work in Gaussian process inference
- Demonstrated applicability to real-world problems
- Well-written and well-organized

Arguments against acceptance:
- Could provide more detailed computational complexity comparison
- Might benefit from a deeper discussion of the interpretability trade-off when using L instead of K

This paper represents a significant step forward in making DPPs more practical for real-world applications, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
