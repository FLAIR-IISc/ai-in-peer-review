**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the significant computational challenge of likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The key difficulty lies in computing the normalizing constant of the DPP likelihood, which takes the form of a large determinant (`det(L+I)` or a Fredholm determinant `det(I+L)`). Previous approaches have tackled this by approximating the spectrum of the kernel matrix/operator L, which is computationally expensive and often restricted to DPPs on finite domains.

The main contribution of this work is the derivation of novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this determinant term. These bounds are applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs. The approach is inspired by sparse approximations in Gaussian Processes, introducing a set of "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" to construct a low-rank approximation to the kernel. Based on these bounds, the paper proposes two inference schemes: (1) a fast, approximate variational inference method that maximizes a lower bound on the log-likelihood, and (2) an asymptotically exact Metropolis-Hastings MCMC algorithm that uses the bounds for acceptance decisions via retrospective sampling. The methods are validated on a synthetic 1D problem where the exact likelihood is computable, and on a real-world 2D spatial point process dataset of diabetic neuropathy.

**Critique**

This is a well-written and timely paper that makes a valuable contribution to making DPPs more practical and scalable. The proposed non-spectral bounds represent a significant and original step forward from previous spectrum-based approximation methods.

**Quality:**
The work is technically solid and presents a complete contribution, from the theoretical derivation of the bounds to their practical application in both variational and MCMC frameworks. The experimental evaluation is well-designed. The use of a synthetic "Gaussian-Gaussian" model, where the ground truth is known, provides a strong sanity check for the MCMC method. The application to the diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrates the practical utility of the variational approach on a real problem and confirms findings from previous studies.

One major point requires clarification regarding the variational lower bound in Equation (6). The bound on the log-likelihood is given as `F(θ,Z) = ... - T log det(Q + I) + T tr(L-Q)`. The derivation relies on Proposition 1, which in turn cites an inequality from Seiler & Simon (1975). This inequality, `det(I+A) <= det(I+PAP)exp(Tr(A-PAP))`, suggests that `log det(I+L) <= log det(I+Q) + tr(L-Q)`. If this is the case, the lower bound on the log-likelihood term `-log det(I+L)` should be `-log det(I+Q) - tr(L-Q)`, implying a negative sign for the trace term in Equation (6). This seems to be a critical discrepancy. A clarification of this derivation, perhaps due to properties of the `(L-Q)` matrix or a different inequality being used, is essential for validating the technical correctness of the variational inference section.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The problem is well-motivated, and the proposed solution is presented logically, starting with the simpler finite case before moving to the continuous domain. The connection to sparse Gaussian Processes is helpful context for readers familiar with that literature.

Suggestions for minor improvements:
-   In Section 4, a sentence or two in the main text providing intuition on how the `Psi` matrix in the continuous case (Eq. 11) is the analogue of the `Q` matrix in the finite case (Eq. 4) would strengthen the connection between the two sections.
-   In Figure 1, the text refers to a green line for the prior distribution, but this is missing from the figure's legend.

**Originality:**
The central idea of using inducing points to create non-spectral bounds for DPP inference is novel and insightful. While the machinery of inducing points and variational bounds is borrowed from the Gaussian Process literature (e.g., Titsias, 2009), its application to the determinantal structure of DPPs is a new and non-trivial contribution. This work presents a clear conceptual advance over the existing spectral approximation techniques for DPPs (e.g., Affandi et al., 2014).

**Significance:**
The potential impact of this work is high. The computational cost of inference has been a major barrier to the widespread adoption of DPPs, especially for large-scale problems. The proposed methods, particularly the fast variational approach, could significantly lower this barrier, enabling the use of DPPs in a wider range of applications. The unified treatment of both finite and continuous domains is another significant strength. The paper honestly discusses the inconsistency of the variational method on the toy problem, which correctly highlights that the more expensive MCMC approach is still necessary in some cases. This is a valuable observation for practitioners. A brief discussion on what might cause this inconsistency (e.g., local optima, looseness of the bound) and how one might diagnose it in practice would further increase the paper's impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel, computationally efficient, and non-spectral method for DPP inference.
2.  The proposed bounds and inference schemes apply to both finite and continuous DPPs.
3.  The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and technically strong (pending clarification on the point below).
4.  The work has the potential to significantly increase the practical applicability of DPPs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  There is a potential technical error in the sign of the trace term in the variational lower bound (Equation 6). This is a critical point that must be addressed. Without a satisfactory explanation, the correctness of the variational method is in question.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper with a significant contribution. The primary concern is the potential sign error in the variational lower bound. Assuming this can be clarified as either a typo or a valid derivation that was not immediately apparent, the paper would be a clear accept. The MCMC part of the contribution is sound regardless. Given the overall quality and potential impact, this work is recommended for acceptance, contingent on a satisfactory author response clarifying the derivation of Equation (6).
