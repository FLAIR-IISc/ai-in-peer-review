

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The main contribution is deriving cheap-to-evaluate nonspectral bounds on the likelihood of DPPs for both finite and continuous domains, which avoids the computationally expensive spectral decompositions required by previous approaches. The authors demonstrate how these bounds enable variational inference and MCMC methods for DPP parameter estimation without requiring spectral knowledge.

The paper makes a strong technical contribution by providing rigorous theoretical bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) that generalize sparse GP approximations to DPPs. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets (diabetic neuropathy) demonstrates the practical utility of their approach, showing convergence of inference results as the number of inducing variables increases. The comparison with ideal likelihood-based methods in the toy experiment provides convincing evidence of correctness.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured exposition that carefully distinguishes between finite and continuous cases. The connection to sparse Gaussian process methods is well-motivated and helpful for readers familiar with that literature. The authors demonstrate strong field knowledge through comprehensive references to DPP literature and appropriate citations of prior work (e.g., Affandi et al. 2014).

The novelty is significant: previous work on DPP inference either required spectral decompositions (which are intractable for large domains) or used approximate methods that sacrifice accuracy. By providing bounds that avoid spectral knowledge entirely, this work enables more scalable DPP inference while maintaining theoretical guarantees.

A minor limitation is that the paper acknowledges the interpretability trade-off when parametrizing L instead of K, which is a well-known issue in the DPP literature. The variational approach showed inconsistent results across runs in the toy experiment, suggesting MCMC may be preferable in some cases - an insight the authors appropriately highlight.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that addresses a fundamental challenge in DPP inference with practical significance for the machine learning community. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the paper is well-written. The authors have made a clear, significant improvement over existing approaches, and their bounds provide a valuable tool for practitioners working with DPPs. I recommend acceptance for NIPS.
