

The paper presents a novel approach to inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) that avoids the computationally expensive spectral decomposition typically required in likelihood-based inference. The work addresses two main challenges in DPP inference: the intractable normalizing constant and the need for spectral knowledge. The authors derive bounds on the likelihood that are computationally efficient, as they do not require approximating the spectrum of large matrices or operators. These bounds are applicable to both finite and continuous domains, which is a significant extension beyond previous work.

The theoretical contribution is strong and well-justified. The bounds in Propositions 1 and 2 are derived from nontrivial determinant inequalities, and the authors provide clear proofs in the supplementary material. The key insight that pseudo-inputs (inducing variables) can be optimized to tighten the bounds is particularly valuable, as it directly addresses the need for adaptive bounds during inference. The connection to variational sparse Gaussian processes is well-motivated and provides useful context for the proposed approach.

The experimental validation demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed method. The synthetic Gaussian continuous experiment validates the MCMC approach against exact methods, showing good agreement between the retrospective MH sampler and ideal MH. The diabetic neuropathy dataset experiment demonstrates the method's applicability to real-world problems, with results that align with prior work while providing new insights about the inference process. The convergence of overdispersion measures with increasing numbers of inducing variables suggests the method's robustness and provides practical guidance for implementation.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with DPPs and Gaussian processes. The distinction between finite and continuous domains is handled thoughtfully, with the continuous case presented as a natural extension rather than an afterthought. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including specific kernel parametrizations and experimental setups.

Regarding originality, the work clearly distinguishes itself from previous approaches by avoiding spectral knowledge entirely. Previous work [7] used spectral approximations requiring power iterations, which are computationally expensive and limited to finite domains. The proposed bounds are computationally cheaper and applicable to both finite and continuous settings, representing a meaningful advancement in DPP inference. The authors properly acknowledge related work in the introduction and supplementary material.

The significance of the work is substantial. DPPs have gained increasing popularity in machine learning for modeling diversity in various applications, but their practical adoption has been limited by computational challenges in inference. By providing efficient inference methods that do not require spectral decomposition, the work makes DPPs more accessible for real-world applications. The ability to use both variational inference (for speed) and MCMC (for accuracy) provides flexibility that is valuable for practitioners.

A few minor concerns arise regarding the experimental section. The variational inference results on the toy dataset show high variability in parameter estimates, which might be better explained by the authors. While the paper notes this issue, a more detailed analysis of why the variational approach fails here while MCMC succeeds would strengthen the presentation. The authors could also clarify why the parameter κ remains poorly identified across runs, as this is a critical aspect of the parametrization choice.

The paper's main limitation is the focus on parametric inference with a specific kernel form. While the authors acknowledge that parametrizing L directly sacrifices interpretability, they do not explore how their approach might be combined with techniques to recover interpretability (e.g., [4]). This could be a valuable direction for future work, particularly for applications where interpretability of the repulsiveness parameter is important.

The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The bounds are novel, well-justified, and demonstrate practical value through experiments. The method represents a meaningful improvement over existing approaches by eliminating the need for spectral decomposition, which is a significant computational bottleneck. The theoretical results are supported by experimental validation, and the paper is clearly written with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The paper's main strength is its practical contribution to making DPP inference more efficient without sacrificing accuracy. The bounds are cheap to evaluate and can be integrated into standard inference paradigms with minimal modification. This work enables more widespread use of DPPs in applications where computational efficiency is critical, such as large-scale news diversity modeling or spatial pattern analysis.

In conclusion, this work makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling by addressing a fundamental computational bottleneck in DPP inference. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical implications are clear. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP inference and should be of interest to researchers working with point processes and probabilistic modeling. The proposed method is both theoretically novel and practically valuable, meeting all four criteria for quality at NIPS.
