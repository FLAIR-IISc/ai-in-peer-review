- Proposes a novel DPP inference method that avoids costly spectral decomposition.
- Targets two core DPP inference challenges: intractable normalizing constant and reliance on spectral knowledge.
- Derives computationally efficient likelihood bounds that do not require approximating spectra.
- Bounds apply to both finite and continuous domains, extending prior work.
- Theoretical contributions are strong, based on nontrivial determinant inequalities with clear proofs.
- Shows pseudo-inputs (inducing variables) can be optimized to tighten bounds, improving adaptivity.
- Connects method to variational sparse Gaussian processes, providing useful context and motivation.
- Experiments validate practicality: synthetic Gaussian case shows agreement between retrospective MH and exact MH.
- Real-world diabetic neuropathy dataset demonstrates applicability and insights into the inference process.
- Overdispersion measures converge with more inducing variables, indicating robustness and implementation guidance.
- Paper is well-written, organized, and provides sufficient experimental/kernel detail for reproducibility.
- Originality: distinguishes itself by eliminating spectral requirements, improving over prior spectral-approximation methods.
- Significance: makes DPPs more practical for large-scale applications by enabling efficient variational and MCMC inference.
- Minor concerns: high variability in variational parameter estimates and persistent poor identification of parameter κ need more analysis.
- Main limitation: focus on parametric kernel inference and lack of exploration of methods to recover interpretability of L.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, practically valuable, and advances the state of the art in DPP inference.
