## Review of "Inference for determinantal point processes without spectral knowledge"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of performing likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decomposition, a common bottleneck in existing methods. The authors propose novel bounds on the DPP likelihood, both for finite and continuous domains, enabling the use of variational inference and MCMC methods without explicit eigenvalue calculations. This is a significant contribution, as it expands the applicability of DPPs to larger and more complex datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of current approaches and positioning the work within the broader context of DPP modeling and inference. The technical development of the bounds, inspired by variational Gaussian processes, is sound and presented with sufficient detail. The extension to continuous domains is a valuable addition, demonstrating the generality of the proposed approach. The experimental validation, including a synthetic example and a real-world dataset (diabetic neuropathy), provides evidence of the method's effectiveness. The results on the neuropathy dataset align with previous findings and showcase the ability to discern differences between patient groups.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the computational cost of evaluating the bounds themselves. While avoiding spectral decomposition is a major advantage, the cost of optimizing the pseudo-inputs (Z) within the variational and MCMC frameworks needs further clarification. The authors mention the optimization process but don’t provide detailed runtime comparisons. Additionally, the connection between the parameters of the kernel L and the interpretability of the model, a point raised in the introduction, could be explored more deeply. While the authors acknowledge this trade-off, a more nuanced discussion of its implications would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant practical limitation of DPP inference.
*   Provides novel and theoretically sound bounds on the likelihood.
*   Demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach through experiments.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational cost of bound evaluation (optimization of Z) needs further analysis.
*   Discussion of the interpretability trade-off could be more in-depth.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP modeling. The proposed bounds offer a practical solution to a challenging problem, and the experimental results are encouraging. While some aspects could be further elaborated, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above.
