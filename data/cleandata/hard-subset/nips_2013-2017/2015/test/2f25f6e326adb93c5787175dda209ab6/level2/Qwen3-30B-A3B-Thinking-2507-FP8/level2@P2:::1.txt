

This paper proposes a novel approach to likelihood-based inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) by deriving bounds on the intractable normalizing constant without requiring spectral decomposition. The main contribution is the derivation of nonspectral bounds for both finite and continuous DPPs, which enable more efficient variational inference and MCMC methods.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in DPP inference: the need for spectral decomposition when evaluating the likelihood. The authors correctly identify that prior work [7] relies on spectral approximations that are computationally expensive, especially for large datasets. Their approach of using "pseudo-inputs" to derive bounds without spectral knowledge is theoretically sound and builds on existing work on sparse Gaussian processes.

The paper's strengths are its clear theoretical contributions, thorough experimental validation, and practical applicability. The bounds derived in Propositions 1 and 2 are novel and avoid the spectral bottleneck that has limited prior DPP inference methods. The experimental section demonstrates the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world data (the diabetic neuropathy dataset), showing that the variational approach converges with increasing number of inducing variables and that MCMC inference yields consistent results with ideal methods.

The paper's main weaknesses relate to the interpretability trade-off and limited comparison with prior methods. While the authors acknowledge that parametrizing L instead of K sacrifices interpretability (Section 1), they don't adequately address how this affects practical applications. The paper would be stronger if it provided a more detailed discussion of when parametrizing L is acceptable versus when the interpretability of K would be essential.

The experimental comparison with existing methods is limited. While the paper references [7] as the main prior work using spectral bounds, it doesn't directly compare computational efficiency or inference quality between the proposed method and [7] on the same datasets. A quantitative comparison of wall-clock time and inference accuracy would strengthen the empirical claims.

The paper is well-written and logically organized, though the connection between the finite and continuous cases could be made more explicit. The supplementary material provides necessary proofs, but the main paper could briefly summarize the key insights from these proofs to aid readers less familiar with determinant inequalities.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a clear contribution to the DPP literature. While [7] uses spectral bounds for DPP inference, this paper's nonspectral approach is novel and avoids the computational bottlenecks of spectral methods. The connection to sparse Gaussian processes (Section 3.1) is well-explained and strengthens the theoretical foundation.

The significance of this work is substantial. DPPs have gained popularity in machine learning for modeling diversity (e.g., [3, 6, 7]), but their practical application has been limited by computational challenges in inference. This paper directly addresses that limitation, making DPPs more accessible for real-world applications with larger datasets. The method could be particularly valuable for the growing body of work applying DPPs to image search, recommendation systems, and spatial statistics.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs in the supplementary material. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of parametrizing L instead of K, though they could expand on this trade-off.

For improvement, the authors should:
1. Include a direct comparison with [7] on computational efficiency and inference quality
2. Provide more discussion about when parametrizing L is acceptable versus when K would be preferred
3. Elaborate on the relationship between the number of pseudo-inputs and the tightness of the bounds
4. Clarify the connection between the finite and continuous cases more explicitly

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the DPP literature with clear theoretical and practical value. The paper addresses a significant bottleneck in DPP inference and provides a practical solution that could enable broader application of DPPs in machine learning. The work is well-executed, theoretically sound, and empirically validated, making it a valuable addition to the NIPS literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the comparative experimental evaluation and interpretability discussion.
