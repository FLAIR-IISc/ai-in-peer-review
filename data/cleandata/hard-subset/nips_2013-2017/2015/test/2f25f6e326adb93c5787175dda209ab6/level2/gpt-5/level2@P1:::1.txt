Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) when spectral information of the kernel operator is unavailable or impractical to compute. The authors parameterize the L-ensemble kernel (common in ML practice) and derive nonspectral upper and lower bounds on the normalizing determinant term det(I+L) for both finite ground sets and continuous domains. In the finite case, they introduce a low-rank proxy Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY based on m inducing points Z and establish bounds det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(I+L) ≤ 1/det(Q+I). In the continuous case, they provide analogous Fredholm determinant bounds in terms of LZ and Ψij = ∫ L(zi, x)L(x, zj) dµ(x), under trace-class assumptions.

These bounds enable (i) a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood that is cheap to evaluate (O(n m^2) and reduced memory), and (ii) an exact Metropolis–Hastings scheme using retrospective acceptance tests where bounds can be tightened by increasing m and optimizing Z. The approach draws inspiration from variational sparse Gaussian processes (Titsias, AISTATS 2009) and leverages determinant inequalities (Seiler & Simon, PNAS 1975). Compared to prior DPP inference methods that require eigenspectra or Nyström-type approximations (e.g., Affandi et al., AISTATS/ICML; Gillenwater et al., NIPS; Mariet & Sra, NIPS), the contribution is to avoid spectral computations altogether and to handle continuous domains without discretization.

Experiments include: (1) a 1D Gaussian-Gaussian DPP where exact spectrum and Fredholm determinant are known, validating the MH sampler and illustrating identifiability issues for certain parameters under the L-parameterization; and (2) a real 2D spatial dataset (diabetic neuropathy), showing that the variational approach yields consistent overdispersion estimates as m increases and separates two clinical classes, with reasonable runtimes.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: clean, nonspectral determinant bounds applicable to both finite and continuous DPPs, extending ideas from sparse GPs to DPP inference.
- Practicality: avoids large matrix eigendecompositions and full n×n storage; uses only n×m cross-covariances and m×m matrices.
- Generality: continuous-domain treatment via Fredholm determinant bounds with computable Ψ for Gaussian kernels with Gaussian base measures.
- Inference toolkit: both a variational objective and an exact MH scheme via retrospective acceptance; clear connection to tightening bounds by optimizing inducing points.
- Empirical validation: synthetic example with known ground truth validates the MH sampler; real-data study reproduces prior qualitative findings and shows convergence in m.
- Clarity about limitations of L-parameterization (identifiability) and when MCMC may be preferable to variational methods.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Tightness and behavior of bounds: although empirical convergence with increasing m is shown, there is no theoretical characterization of the rate at which the gap between bounds shrinks, nor guidance on choosing m or optimizing Z beyond heuristics.
- Limited empirical scope: experiments are relatively small scale; there is no runtime/accuracy comparison to spectral methods (e.g., partial eigensolvers) or to recent scalable DPP learning approaches on large finite ground sets.
- Continuous-case generality: while Gaussian/Gaussian kernels allow closed-form Ψ, guidance is limited for other kernels/base measures (computability, numerical stability, complexity).
- Variational method stability: the paper shows cases where variational optimization struggles (parameter identifiability), but does not provide diagnostics or regularization strategies (e.g., priors, reparameterizations) to mitigate these issues.
- Theoretical conditions: the continuous-domain assumptions (trace-class, eq. (9)) are stated, but practical checks or sufficient conditions for common ML setups could be elaborated. Convergence of bounds as m→∞ is only informally motivated.
- Computational details: the cost statement O(n m^2) assumes efficient computation of tr(L) and reuse across multiple realizations; more explicit complexity accounting (including costs of optimizing Z and increasing m in MH) would strengthen the case.

Quality:
Technically sound overall. The finite- and continuous-domain bounds are clearly stated and connected to a known determinant inequality; using them within VI and MH is standard and correct. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement; including key proof sketches in the main text would improve accessibility. The empirical validation supports correctness, though broader benchmarks would bolster claims of efficiency.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized, with intuitive explanations and explicit links to sparse GPs. A few notational inconsistencies (e.g., switching between Y1 and Yt) could be cleaned up. The role of m and Z is well motivated; further practical guidance for selecting/initializing Z would be helpful.

Originality:
High. To my knowledge, nonspectral determinant bounds of this form for DPP likelihoods—especially in continuous domains—have not been presented in prior ML work. The adaptation of inducing-point machinery from GPs to DPP normalization is a neat and nontrivial idea.

Significance:
Moderate to high. DPPs are widely used in ML and statistics; removing spectral requirements broadens applicability (large finite ground sets, continuous domains). The method is likely to be adopted by practitioners, especially for continuous DPPs where spectral tools are unavailable.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Can you provide theoretical monotonicity/tightness results in m and Z (e.g., that the bounds are nondecreasing/nonincreasing as m increases), or conditions under which both bounds converge to the truth?
- Practical guidance for Z: initialization strategies, optimization schedules, and whether sharing Z across multiple realizations Yt is beneficial.
- For continuous domains beyond Gaussian/Gaussian, what kernels/base measures yield tractable Ψ? Could numerical quadrature (e.g., Bornemann 2010) be combined with your bounds?
- Can you report wall-clock comparisons vs. spectral-bound MH (Affandi et al.) on finite problems and show memory savings?
- Could reparameterizations (e.g., separating intensity from repulsion) or priors improve identifiability when using L?

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, general, and practically useful bounds avoiding spectral computations.
- Unified treatment of finite and continuous DPPs.
- Enables both variational and exact MCMC inference.
- Clear empirical validation and reproducibility of known qualitative findings.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited experimental breadth and comparative evaluation.
- Lack of theoretical guarantees on bound tightness/rates and guidance for choosing m.
- Variational instability not fully addressed; reliance on MCMC may be costly without benchmarks.

Overall recommendation:
Lean accept. The contribution is original and addresses an important bottleneck in DPP inference with a well-motivated, implementable method. Strengthening experiments and adding theoretical insights on bound behavior would further improve the paper.
