- Paper derives computable nonspectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) for finite and continuous DPPs using inducing (pseudo) inputs, inspired by sparse GP variational bounds.  
- Uses these bounds to enable variational inference and exact (retrospective) Metropolis–Hastings without eigenvalue computations.  
- Extends bounds to Fredholm determinants under trace-class assumptions for continuous domains.  
- Finite-domain bound reduces complexity to O(n m^2) and retrospective MH construction is theoretically sound.  
- Experiments validate MH correctness in a 1D continuous Gaussian–Gaussian toy and show practical optimization behavior on a neuropathy dataset.  
- Theoretical derivations are grounded (Seiler–Simon determinant inequalities) and carefully handled.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited: no large-scale discrete DPP experiments and few baselines.  
- No systematic diagnostics of bound tightness versus spectral methods or as a function of m and Z optimization.  
- Clarity is good overall; proofs and some details are deferred to the supplement.  
- Would benefit from pseudocode for retrospective MH with adaptive m and discussion of regularization when LZ is ill-conditioned.  
- Novel contribution: applying inducing-variable determinant bounds to DPP likelihoods, especially in continuous domains without spectra.  
- Significance: removes a key spectral bottleneck and could broaden practical DPP inference if validated at scale.  
- Open concerns about bound tightness, robustness in high dimensions, and computational/stability costs for Ψ integrals beyond Gaussian cases.  
- Suggested comparisons: spectral truncation (Affandi et al.), Nyström methods, and larger finite-problem benchmarks.  
- Recommend diagnostics/guidance for choosing m and optimizing Z, exploration of k-DPPs and parameter identifiability, and code release for reproducibility.  
- Pros: novel nonspectral bounds, solid theory, practical variational and MH algorithms, clear GP connections.  
- Cons: limited empirical scope, unclear tightness in challenging regimes, sparse practical guidance on Z/m selection and stability.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept.
