Summary and relation to prior work: The paper tackles likelihood-based inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without relying on spectral decompositions. The main claims are: (1) derivation of computable, nonspectral upper and lower bounds on det(I+L) for both finite and continuous DPPs using inducing inputs (pseudo-inputs), inspired by sparse GP variational bounds (Titsias, AISTATS’09); (2) use of these bounds to enable variational inference and exact (retrospective) Metropolis–Hastings without eigenvalue computations; (3) empirical validation on a toy continuous Gaussian–Gaussian setting (with known spectrum) and a real neuropathy dataset. This connects to prior NIPS work on DPP learning and algorithms that leverage spectra or fixed-point/EM methods (e.g., Gillenwater et al., Mariet & Sra, Zou & Adams), and to spectral-bounding approaches for DPPs (Affandi et al., ICML/AISTATS), as well as numerical Fredholm determinant work (Bornemann).

Quality: The bounds are mathematically grounded (via Seiler–Simon determinant inequalities) and carefully extended to Fredholm determinants under trace-class assumptions. The finite-domain bound reduces complexity to O(nm^2), and the retrospective MH construction is sound. Experiments convincingly verify MH correctness in the 1D case and demonstrate practical optimization behavior. However, empirical evaluation is limited: no large-scale discrete DPPs, few baselines, and no systematic tightness diagnostics versus spectral methods.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized, with clear exposition of K vs. L, likelihood issues, and algorithmic uses of the bounds. Some details are deferred to the supplement (proofs, continuous-case equivalences). It would help to provide pseudocode for the retrospective MH with adaptive m and to discuss conditions/regularization when LZ is ill-conditioned.

Originality: Extending inducing-variable style determinant bounds to DPP likelihoods, and especially to continuous domains avoiding spectral information, appears novel. The bridging of GP variational ideas with DPP inference is an interesting and nontrivial contribution.

Significance and usefulness: The approach removes a key bottleneck in DPP inference and broadens applicability to continuous domains. If broadly validated, it could be adopted by practitioners who avoid spectral computations. Current evidence suggests promise but leaves open how tight/robust the bounds are in high dimensions and at scale.

Limitations and constructive suggestions:
- Provide comparative studies with spectral truncation (Affandi et al.) and Nyström methods on larger finite problems.
- Include diagnostics of bound tightness (gap vs. m, effect of Z optimization) and guidance for choosing m.
- Discuss computational cost and stability of Ψ integrals beyond Gaussian–Gaussian settings; consider quadrature-based approximations (cf. Bornemann).
- Explore k-DPPs and parameter identifiability when parametrizing L; consider priors or reparameterizations mitigating κ non-identifiability.
- Release code for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, general nonspectral bounds (finite and continuous).
- Solid theory; practical algorithms (variational and MH).
- Clear connections to GP variational methods.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited empirical scope and comparisons.
- Unclear bound tightness in challenging regimes.
- Practical guidance on Z/m selection and stability is sparse.

Overall: Weak accept.
